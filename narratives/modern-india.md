# Modern India — From Crown Rule to a Civilizational Republic in 168 Years

**Goal:** Narrative for modern India (1858–present), the period from the British Crown's takeover of the East India Company's domains through Gandhi's nonviolent revolution, the trauma of Partition, the Nehruvian republic, the long Cold War interlude, the 1991 economic opening, the IT boom, and the rise of a Hindu-nationalist political center that now governs a country of 1.4 billion. Modern India is a paradox in motion. It is simultaneously the world's largest democracy, the world's most populous nation, a nuclear-armed regional power, a major space-faring state, and a society that still contains caste violence, severe regional inequality, and an active argument with itself over what kind of country it ought to be. This book walks through the actual sequence — the colonial period, the freedom struggle, the founding of the republic, the long decades of slow growth and short emergencies, the opening and the boom, and the current civilizational reorientation — without flattening any of it into a single story. The story is not over. The book ends in 2026, and the chapters that come next are being written now.

**Status:** First draft, 15 chapters.

A note on names and transliteration: Indian personal names are given in the order their bearers used (Jawaharlal Nehru, Indira Gandhi, B. R. Ambedkar, A. P. J. Abdul Kalam). Place names use modern official forms — Mumbai (formerly Bombay), Chennai (formerly Madras), Kolkata (formerly Calcutta), Bengaluru (still commonly Bangalore), Varanasi (also Banaras and Kashi) — with the older form noted on first use where it appears in the historical record. Sanskrit and Hindi terms are given in the most common English-language transliteration; macrons and diacritics are omitted for readability.

A note on "first" claims: this is a modern, densely documented period, so most "firsts" here are well-attested. "First non-European literature Nobel" (Tagore, 1913), "first chairman of the drafting committee of an independent country's constitution to also be a member of a previously untouchable caste" (Ambedkar, 1947), "first space agency from outside the Soviet–American–European bloc to put a probe in lunar orbit" (ISRO with Chandrayaan-1, 2008), "first country to land on the lunar south pole" (India with Chandrayaan-3, 2023), and "world's most populous nation" (India surpassed China in 2023) are all genuine firsts in the strict modern sense.

A note on the politics: this is a contested period. Indian historiography has been actively re-litigated for the last twenty years — over Partition, over Nehru's economic choices, over the Emergency, over the Babri demolition, over the BJP's rise, over Modi's record. Where the historical evidence supports a particular reading, the text takes a position. Where evidence is mixed or contested, the text says so. Calling the Emergency a "controlled authoritarian interlude" is not the same as approving of it; calling the Babri demolition a "criminal mob action" is not the same as opposing Hindu reform. The aim is to describe what happened. The reader is welcome to disagree with the interpretive frame, which is the basic posture this book takes toward all of its periods.

# Chapter 1 — The Raj Consolidates

The previous TL ended in 1206 with Qutb-ud-din Aibak founding the Delhi Sultanate (Delhi Sultanate Ch 1). Five and a half centuries of Indo-Islamic rule followed — the Khalji and Tughlaq sultans, the Bahmani south, the imperial Mughals, the Maratha confederacy that displaced them — and then, beginning in the 18th century, a slow takeover by a chartered private corporation operating out of London, the East India Company. By 1857 the Company governed roughly two-thirds of the subcontinent directly, collected taxes, ran courts, fielded an army of around 300,000 sepoys (Indian soldiers in Company employ), maintained its own civil service, and shipped opium to China and cotton to Lancashire. The remaining third was governed by princely states under Company "subsidiary alliances" — treaties under which an Indian ruler accepted a British Resident at his court, gave up his right to make foreign policy, paid for a British-officered subsidiary force stationed in his territory, and was guaranteed his throne against rivals. The system was profitable, exploitative, racially structured, and unstable in ways the Company itself did not fully understand.

In May 1857, sepoys of the Bengal Army stationed at Meerut, fifty miles northeast of Delhi, mutinied over the issue of greased cartridges for the new Enfield rifle. The cartridges' paper coverings were rumored to be greased with cow tallow and pig lard — religiously offensive to Hindu and Muslim soldiers respectively — and the rumor was probably true for at least some batches manufactured at the Dum Dum arsenal near Calcutta. The mutiny spread along the Gangetic plain. Mutinous sepoys marched on Delhi, declared the elderly Mughal emperor Bahadur Shah Zafar — by then a pensioner of the Company, living in the Red Fort under tight supervision — to be the legitimate sovereign of a restored Mughal empire, and held the city against British counterattack for four months. Rebellions broke out at Cawnpore (modern Kanpur), Lucknow, Jhansi, and across central India. The rebellion was not a single coordinated rising; it was a series of local revolts that took fire from a common spark. It was not a national independence movement in any modern sense; the mutineers had multiple incompatible goals (Mughal restoration, princely autonomy, religious orthodoxy, mutual self-protection against punishment) and made no attempt to coordinate beyond their immediate regions. But it was the largest armed challenge to British rule in India in the 19th century, and it nearly succeeded.

The Company's recovery took fourteen months and was extraordinarily violent. British and loyal Indian forces relieved the besieged garrison at Lucknow in November 1857 and again in March 1858; they retook Delhi in September 1857 after a six-day assault on the city walls; they hunted down the leaders of the central Indian rebellion — including the Rani of Jhansi, who died fighting in 1858 — through 1858 and into 1859. Reprisals were systematic. Captured mutineers were tied to the muzzles of cannons and blown apart in mass executions intended to deny the dead a recognizable corpse for funeral rites. Entire villages along the path of retaking were burned. Bahadur Shah Zafar was tried for treason against the British Crown — a legal fiction, since he was not a British subject — convicted, and exiled to Rangoon, where he died in 1862; his sons were shot. The total death toll from the rebellion and its suppression has never been precisely counted, but estimates range from several hundred thousand to over a million.

What is now called the **Sepoy Mutiny** in British historiography, the **First War of Indian Independence** in Indian nationalist historiography, and increasingly the **Indian Rebellion of 1857** in academic literature, ended the East India Company. The Company had ruled in the name of a charter granted by Parliament; its bankruptcy in 1858 — political bankruptcy, since the rebellion had exposed the Company's military and administrative failure — gave Parliament the political opening to take over directly. The **Government of India Act 1858** transferred Company territories to the Crown, abolished the Company's Court of Directors, and created the office of Secretary of State for India in the British cabinet. Queen Victoria became Empress of India. Crown rule had begun.

---

The system the Crown inherited was a hybrid empire of two parts. **Direct British India** covered roughly two-thirds of the subcontinent — the Gangetic plain, Bengal, the Punjab, the western coast around Bombay, the southern coast around Madras — and was governed through a hierarchy of British district officers under provincial governors under a Viceroy in Calcutta (later Delhi). **Princely India** covered the remaining third — over five hundred semi-independent states ranging from Hyderabad (larger than France, with a Muslim ruling dynasty over a majority-Hindu population) and Kashmir (a Hindu Dogra dynasty over a majority-Muslim population) down to two-room "states" whose entire territory consisted of a single zamindar's estate. The princes ran their internal affairs subject to British "paramountcy" — the Crown's right to intervene in matters of succession, foreign policy, or what the Crown considered misgovernment.

The British administered direct India through the **Indian Civil Service**, the small, elite British (and increasingly Indian) bureaucracy that ran the country at the district level. The ICS was recruited through competitive examination held in London — the first such Indian could sit for the exam was Satyendranath Tagore, brother of Rabindranath, in 1863 — and trained in British universities and at the staff college at Haileybury. At its peak in the late 19th century, the ICS had roughly a thousand officers governing a population of around 300 million. Each district officer was responsible for revenue collection, magistracy, police supervision, and famine relief over an area the size of an English county and a population of one to three million people. The ratio of administrators to administered was, by any modern standard, absurdly low; British district officers were forced to delegate most actual work to Indian subordinates — the **clerks, accountants, and revenue settlement officers** who actually ran the day-to-day administration — and to operate through the existing village headmen and local revenue collectors who had run the same functions under the Mughals and the Company. The system worked because most Indians had limited contact with the British directly. A villager in 1880 might never see a European in person; what they encountered was Indian subordinates carrying out a system the British had defined.

The colonial state's three main functions were extraction, internal order, and (later) selective development. **Land revenue** was the largest source of imperial income through the late 19th century — the assessed value of agricultural production, collected in cash. Different parts of India had different revenue systems inherited from earlier regimes: the **Permanent Settlement** (1793) in Bengal had given hereditary land rights to zamindars (the previous revenue collectors) in exchange for fixed-rate revenue, which became increasingly favorable to them as inflation eroded the real value; the **ryotwari system** in Madras and Bombay assessed individual cultivators directly; the **mahalwari system** in north India assessed village communities collectively. The Permanent Settlement is in retrospect the most consequential — it created a class of large landlords (zamindars) in Bengal and Bihar whose revenue burdens grew lighter while their tenants' burdens grew heavier, producing the long-running peasant indebtedness and tenancy crisis that defined 20th-century rural Bengal.

The second function was **internal order**, maintained through a layered police system, an Indian Army (reorganized after 1857 to mix recruits from different regions and religious communities specifically to prevent another sepoy-style coordinated mutiny), and the colonial legal system. The Indian Penal Code of 1860, drafted by the historian and politician Thomas Macaulay (who had earlier served on the Company's governing council), codified the criminal law of India along English lines and remained the governing code, with successive amendments, until 2023, when the Modi government replaced it with the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita.

The third function, **development**, was selective and emerged most clearly under Viceroy Lord Dalhousie (1848–56) and later Lord Curzon (1899–1905). **Railways** were the centerpiece. India had no railways in 1850; by 1900 it had 25,000 miles of track, the fourth-largest network in the world. The railways were built by private British companies under government guarantees of 5% returns — a structure that placed all the financial risk on the Indian treasury while privatizing the profits — and were designed primarily to serve British strategic and commercial interests: connecting the cotton fields of central India to the export ports of Bombay, connecting the wheat-growing Punjab to the coastal ports, and giving the army the ability to move troops rapidly to any rebellious region. The military design rationale was explicit. After 1857, no garrison was placed more than three days' rail journey from any major city. The railways were Indian-built and Indian-operated at the foot-soldier level, with British supervisors and British engineering management. They were also, by accident, transformative. The railway network of 1900 stitched together a subcontinent that had previously been a patchwork of regional economies, made internal travel possible at a previously unimaginable scale, and created the physical infrastructure for the pan-Indian nationalism that would emerge in the next generation. A Bengali babu in 1885 could travel to a Congress meeting in Bombay in four days instead of two months. This mattered.

---

The **Great Famines** of the late Victorian period were the dark counterpoint. The famine of 1876–78 killed somewhere between five and ten million people across the Deccan and Madras presidency, mostly in territories the British had directly ruled for over a century. The famine of 1896–97 killed perhaps another five million. The famine of 1899–1900 killed at least a million more. The pattern across all three was the same: a monsoon failure produced regional crop shortfalls; the colonial government, committed to free-market orthodoxy, refused to interfere with grain markets; grain merchants exported food away from famine-stricken regions toward more solvent markets elsewhere in India and abroad; relief works were established but at wages below subsistence; the dead were predominantly the rural landless and the urban poor.

The political economist Mike Davis, in his 2001 book *Late Victorian Holocausts*, made the case that these famines were structurally produced by colonial policy rather than by natural shortfall. The evidence is substantial. India had had famines throughout its history, but pre-colonial famines were typically responded to with state-organized grain stockpiles, emergency tax relief, and movement of grain between regions — interventions the Mughal and Maratha states had practiced as routine governance. The Victorian colonial state, ideologically committed to laissez-faire and to balanced budgets in the Indian exchequer, treated famine relief as a charitable burden to be minimized rather than as a sovereign obligation. The contrast is sharpened by the fact that India in the late 19th century was a net food exporter — wheat, rice, and millet flowed out of India to Britain and to other parts of the empire while millions starved inside the country. The number of Indians who died in the Victorian famines is comparable to the entire population of Australia at the time. The political and moral cost of this history would shape Indian nationalism, and Indian economic policy after independence, for the next century.

---

The intellectual and cultural revolution running parallel to colonial extraction was the **Bengal Renaissance** — a wave of reform, literary innovation, and self-conscious modernization centered on the Bengali bhadralok (the educated middle and professional class) from roughly 1820 through 1900. The reformers were Hindu by background, educated in English, and located mostly in Calcutta (the colonial capital until 1911). They argued for the abolition of sati (the immolation of widows on their husbands' funeral pyres, banned by the Company in 1829 after a long campaign by the reformer Raja Ram Mohan Roy), for widow remarriage, for women's education, for the reform of the Hindu joint family, and for what they considered a return to a rational, Vedanta-based Hinduism stripped of the elaborate ritualism the reformers blamed on later interpolations.

The most internationally consequential figure of the late Bengal Renaissance was the poet, novelist, essayist, composer, painter, educationalist, and political activist **Rabindranath Tagore** (1861–1941). Tagore came from a wealthy Brahmo Samaj family — the Brahmo Samaj being a 19th-century Hindu reform movement that combined Vedanta-derived monotheism with social reformism — and produced over the course of a sixty-year career a body of work that has no precise Western parallel. He wrote in Bengali, which limited his international readership during his lifetime, but his English self-translations of his poetry collection *Gitanjali* won him the **Tagore Nobel Prize in Literature in 1913** — the first non-European to receive any Nobel Prize, and a moment of enormous symbolic importance for Indian self-conception. The award radically changed Indian intellectual life. It demonstrated, in the most legible-to-Europe way possible, that Indian culture was capable of producing work of universally recognized literary value at exactly the moment when the colonial framework was insisting Indian culture was second-rate. Gandhi (Chapter 3) would call Tagore the "Gurudev" (great teacher) and acknowledge his moral authority even where they disagreed; Tagore would in turn coin Gandhi's title "Mahatma" (great soul). Tagore wrote the national anthems of two future countries — *Jana Gana Mana* for India and *Amar Sonar Bangla* for Bangladesh — making him the only person in history to author two different sovereign states' national anthems.

Tagore's politics were complicated. He renounced his British knighthood after the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (Chapter 2). He founded a university, Visva-Bharati, at Santiniketan in rural Bengal, dedicated to the integration of Indian and global learning. He opposed both narrow Indian nationalism and aggressive Hindu majoritarianism; he was an early and consistent critic of Mussolini and of fascism; he traveled the world giving lectures on what he considered a universal humanism. The position he occupied — internationalist, anti-colonial, anti-nationalist, religious but not sectarian, modernist but rooted — was idiosyncratic and influential, and many of the figures the rest of this book will introduce defined themselves partly in conversation with him.

---

The political vehicle that would carry the freedom struggle, the **Indian National Congress**, was founded at Bombay in December 1885, twenty-seven years after Crown rule began. The first session was attended by 72 delegates — mostly English-educated lawyers, journalists, and landowners from the major presidencies — convened by a retired Scottish civil servant, **Allan Octavian Hume**, with the encouragement of the then-Viceroy Lord Dufferin. The original Congress was a remarkably modest body. Its founding objects were the "training of public men" and the "amicable adjustment of differences" between Indians and the British government. Its early demands were limited: more Indians admitted to the ICS, Indian representation on imperial legislative councils, equal treatment of Indians and Europeans under law, freer trade. The early Congress assumed it was operating within the British constitutional framework and that loyalty to the Crown was compatible with reform demands. It would take twenty years before this premise was seriously questioned.

The transition began with the **Partition of Bengal** — but that, and the radicalization of the Congress, belongs in the next chapter.

# Chapter 2 — The Congress Awakens

The Congress that A. O. Hume convened in 1885 was a debating society. The Congress that walked out of the Amritsar session in December 1919 was a mass-mobilizing political party demanding self-rule. Between those two states lay thirty-four years of education in the limits of British constitutionalism — and the consistent demonstration, by the colonial state itself, that loyalty would not be rewarded with reform.

Two figures define the early Congress. **Gopal Krishna Gokhale** (1866–1915), a Maharashtrian economist and educator, was the leader of the Congress "moderates" — the faction that believed in petition, debate, and gradual reform within the British constitutional framework. The moderates' theory of change was that the British, being a constitutional people, would eventually be persuaded by sustained reasonable argument to extend full citizenship rights to their Indian subjects. Gokhale's *Servants of India Society*, founded in 1905, trained young Indians for public service on the explicit moderate model: nonviolent, constitutional, evidence-based. His student, more famously, would be Gandhi (Chapter 3), who called Gokhale his "political guru" and adopted from him the discipline of methodical fact-finding before action.

**Bal Gangadhar Tilak** (1856–1920), also Maharashtrian, was the leader of the "extremists" (the British term; the faction called itself "nationalist" or "radical"). Tilak rejected the moderate premise. The British, in his view, were not a constitutional people in their imperial role; they were a foreign occupation, responsive only to pressure. His tactics were mass mobilization, public festivals (he revived the Ganesh Chaturthi public processions in 1894 as occasions for political assembly that the British could not easily ban), the boycott of British goods, and incitement to civil resistance. He gave the movement its slogan — "Swaraj is my birthright, and I shall have it" — and was twice imprisoned for sedition, including a six-year sentence to Mandalay (1908–14) for editorials in his Marathi newspaper *Kesari* that the colonial state read as incitement to political assassination. He was sixty when he came back to politics, and he was already dying.

The moderate–extremist split came to a head at the **Surat Congress** in December 1907, where the two factions came to literal blows on the conference floor over the question of who would chair the session and whether the recently-adopted Swadeshi resolution (Chapter below) would be carried forward. Chairs were thrown. The session collapsed. The Congress split — the moderates continuing as the official party, the extremists meeting separately — and remained split until 1916. The British colonial state read the split as a vindication of its strategy of cultivating Indian moderates while marginalizing Indian radicals. The actual lesson was different. The split demonstrated that a single political vehicle could not contain the range of Indian demands; what came next would require organizing forms more flexible than parliamentary debating societies.

---

The proximate cause of the radicalization was **Lord Curzon's** viceroyalty (1899–1905). Curzon was the most consequential and most-disliked Viceroy of the late Raj — a young Etonian aristocrat who treated India as a personal project, threw himself into administrative detail with enormous energy, restored archaeological sites (he funded the early conservation work on the Taj Mahal and on Mughal architecture more broadly), expanded irrigation, and made consistently provocative decisions in matters of education and political symbolism. His most consequential single act was the **Partition of Bengal** in October 1905.

The proposal, as Curzon framed it, was an administrative convenience. Bengal Presidency in 1905 was an enormous administrative unit — including all of modern West Bengal, modern Bangladesh, modern Bihar, modern Jharkhand, modern Odisha, parts of modern Assam, a total population of around 78 million people. The territory was, Curzon argued, simply too large to administer efficiently from a single capital. The partition divided it into two new provinces: **East Bengal and Assam** (with a Muslim majority, capital at Dhaka) and **West Bengal** (with a Hindu majority, capital at Calcutta).

The Bengali public read the partition very differently. Calcutta was the political and intellectual capital of Indian nationalism; the Bengali bhadralok was the largest English-educated middle class in India, and the largest single source of Congress membership; the Bengali press was the most influential vernacular press in India. Partitioning Bengal along a religious line, splitting the educated Hindu Bengali population between the two new provinces and creating a new Muslim-majority unit, was — in the nationalist reading — a deliberate attempt by the colonial state to weaken Indian political organization by breaking up its single most coordinated regional bloc and by introducing a religious-administrative principle that would handicap future cross-communal alliance.

Both readings were partially correct. Curzon and the India Office did want administrative efficiency. They also wanted to weaken Bengali nationalism, and the documentary record (released decades later) shows they understood the religious dimension perfectly well. The partition was both administrative and political, and the Bengali nationalist reading was substantively correct about its political intent.

The reaction was the **Swadeshi Movement** (1905–08) — the first sustained mass mobilization of the Indian nationalist period and the template for everything Gandhi would do later. *Swadeshi* (svah-DAY-shee) literally meant "of one's own country." The movement called for the boycott of British manufactured goods — Manchester cotton textiles in particular — and for the patronage of Indian-made substitutes; the burning of imported cloth in public bonfires; the establishment of swadeshi industries and educational institutions outside the colonial system; the use of vernacular language in education and public life. *Bonkim Chandra Chatterjee*'s 1882 patriotic song *Vande Mataram* — "I bow to thee, Mother" — was adopted as the movement's anthem and would become, after independence, India's national song (the national *anthem* is Tagore's *Jana Gana Mana*; the *song* is *Vande Mataram*).

The movement was, by the standards of late-Victorian colonial politics, enormous. Bengali cloth markets where British textiles had dominated for decades emptied out within months. Swadeshi industries — small-scale and often inefficient, but symbolically charged — sprang up across the province. Bengali university students adopted *khadi* (homespun cotton, the term Gandhi would later make universal) as a uniform. Schools, libraries, and cultural societies operating outside the colonial education system proliferated. The movement also produced its dark side: a small but consequential turn toward violent revolutionary activity. The **Anushilan Samiti** (founded 1902) and the **Jugantar group** (founded 1906) trained young Bengalis in bomb-making and assassination on the model of late-19th-century European anarchist groups. The first British official to be assassinated by Indian revolutionaries — District Magistrate Douglas Kingsford, targeted at Muzaffarpur in April 1908, though the bomb killed two English women instead — initiated a phase of revolutionary terrorism that would persist on the margins of the nationalist movement for the next forty years.

Curzon was recalled in 1905, partly over the political fallout from the partition (and partly over an unrelated quarrel with the British commander-in-chief in India). The partition was reversed in 1911 by a successor Viceroy — Hardinge — who simultaneously announced the move of the imperial capital from Calcutta to a newly designed city to be built south of the existing town of Delhi, in part to remove the British administration from the politically inflamed Bengali context. **New Delhi** would be built between 1911 and 1931 by the architects Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker; the **New Delhi inaugurated in 1931** marked the formal completion of the new capital. But the political damage of 1905 was already done. The Swadeshi movement had demonstrated that mass mobilization could shake the colonial state. The reversal of the partition had demonstrated that the colonial state could be made to back down. The next generation of nationalists would absorb both lessons.

---

The First World War transformed the situation. The Government of India committed 1.5 million Indian soldiers and laborers to the war — fighting in France, in the Middle East, in East Africa, in Mesopotamia, on the Western Front at Ypres and Neuve Chapelle. India provided more soldiers to the war than Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa combined. Indian taxpayers paid for the deployment. Indian agricultural production was diverted to feed the British army. Indian industry — what little of it existed — was reorganized for war production, with the result that the war years produced a brief boom in Indian manufacturing as British imports collapsed.

The political price of this contribution was understood by Indian leaders to be significant. The Congress, the Muslim League (founded 1906, originally as a body to lobby the British for separate Muslim political representation), Tilak (released from Mandalay in 1914 and returning to active politics), and Gandhi (returning from South Africa in January 1915, having spent twenty-one years there refining the techniques that would define the next phase) all supported the war effort on the explicit understanding that constitutional advance would follow British victory. The **Lucknow Pact** of December 1916 — negotiated between Congress and the Muslim League at simultaneous sessions in the city, with Tilak and Jinnah as central figures (the same Muhammad Ali Jinnah who would later lead the Pakistan movement was at this point a Congress moderate and a Muslim League progressive simultaneously) — committed both organizations to a joint demand for self-government within the British Empire, with reserved seats for Muslims in the new legislatures. It was the high-water mark of Hindu-Muslim political cooperation in the late Raj.

The British response, when it came after the war, was the **Government of India Act 1919** — the **Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms** (after Edwin Montagu, Secretary of State for India, and Lord Chelmsford, the Viceroy). The reforms introduced **dyarchy** at the provincial level — splitting administration into "reserved" subjects (police, justice, finance) retained by British governors and "transferred" subjects (education, public health, agriculture) handed to Indian ministers responsible to elected provincial legislatures. At the central level, the changes were smaller: an expanded Imperial Legislative Council with a partly elected Indian majority, but with the Viceroy retaining override powers on all significant matters. The franchise was extended — about 10 million Indians (perhaps 3% of the adult population) could vote in the 1920 elections — and Indian ministers were given control of specific functions for the first time.

Congress moderates accepted the reforms as a step forward. Tilak rejected them as inadequate. And then, before the Congress could metabolize the political question of whether to participate in the new system, the Punjab erupted.

---

The **Rowlatt Act** of March 1919 extended wartime emergency powers — preventive detention without trial, in-camera trials by special tribunals — into peacetime. The act was a response to revolutionary terrorism (the Ghadar conspiracy of 1914–15, in which Indian revolutionaries based in California had attempted to land arms shipments on the western coast for an anti-British rising; the Bengali revolutionary groups still active around Calcutta) but its sweeping language could be applied to any political activity the government chose to criminalize. Gandhi, in his first major political intervention since returning to India, called for a nationwide *hartal* (general strike) in protest. The strike was widely observed across northern India. In the Punjab — a province already tense from wartime grievances over conscription, food prices, and inflation — the protest tipped into riots, particularly at Amritsar, where two Congress leaders, Saifuddin Kitchlew and Satyapal, were arrested and several Europeans were killed in the resulting violence. The British provincial government imposed martial law.

On April 13, 1919 — the Punjabi festival of Baisakhi, when crowds traditionally gathered in Amritsar to celebrate the new year and to visit the Golden Temple — somewhere between 10,000 and 20,000 people, mostly rural pilgrims unaware that martial law was in force, gathered at the **Jallianwala Bagh**, an enclosed public garden surrounded by walls about ten feet high, with a single narrow entrance and exit. **Brigadier General Reginald Dyer** arrived at the entrance with 90 soldiers, two armored cars (which he was unable to bring through the entrance), and a clear order in his mind to disperse the crowd by force without warning. He blocked the exit. He gave the order to fire. His soldiers — Indians and Gurkhas — fired 1,650 rounds of rifle ammunition into the crowd over roughly ten minutes, aiming at the densest concentrations of people and at those attempting to scale the walls. They stopped when ammunition ran low.

The official British death toll was 379. The Congress count, which is now generally accepted as closer to accurate, was at least 1,000 dead and three times that wounded. The **Jallianwala Bagh massacre** of April 13, 1919 was, by orders of magnitude, the worst single act of state violence the British had committed in India since 1857. The military court of inquiry — the Hunter Commission — censured Dyer for his actions and removed him from command. But the British Parliament's reaction was telling. The House of Lords passed a motion of approval for Dyer's conduct. A British public subscription raised £26,000 for him — the equivalent of perhaps £1.5 million in 2026 currency — to compensate him for his "ill-treatment" by the official Hunter inquiry. Dyer retired to England as a hero of the imperial right. He died in 1927 with no expression of regret.

Rabindranath Tagore renounced his British knighthood in a public letter to the Viceroy four weeks after the massacre. The Hunter Commission's findings, and the British reaction to them, ended forever the credibility of the moderate Congress argument that the British would respond to reason. After April 1919, the question for Indian nationalism was not whether to break with the colonial system but how. The next chapter is about the man who answered that question.

# Chapter 3 — Gandhi's Method

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi (1869–1948) — by 1919 he was forty-nine years old, a London-trained lawyer who had spent the previous twenty-one years in South Africa, and a public figure principally known for having invented and refined a method of political resistance he called **satyagraha**. He returned to India in January 1915 at Gokhale's invitation, with Gokhale's parting instruction that he should spend a year traveling the country in third-class railway carriages before involving himself in national politics. He largely followed the instruction. By the time Jallianwala Bagh forced him into the center of the Congress in 1919, he had assembled a method — and a self-presentation — unlike anything the moderate Congress had previously contemplated.

The South African years matter for understanding what happened next. Gandhi had arrived in Durban in 1893 as a young lawyer hired to handle a commercial dispute for an Indian merchant. He encountered, in colonial Natal and then in the Transvaal, the early architecture of the racial regime that would later become apartheid: the head tax on Indian indentured laborers, the requirement that Indians register and carry identity cards, the prohibition on Indian movement between provinces, the disenfranchisement of Indian voters. Gandhi's first political action was a refusal to be ejected from a first-class railway carriage at Pietermaritzburg in 1893, having paid for the ticket and refused to move to a third-class car because of his race; he spent the night on the platform. The episode became, in his later self-presentation, the moment that committed him to political resistance. He spent the next two decades organizing the Indian community in South Africa — first by petition and legal argument, then by mass civil disobedience — culminating in the Satyagraha campaign of 1906–14 against the Transvaal Asiatic Registration Act. The campaign won partial concessions in 1914 from the Union of South Africa government under General Jan Smuts. Gandhi went home.

He brought with him three things he had not had in 1893: a method, an identity, and a wardrobe. The **method** he called *satyagraha* (sat-YAH-grah-hah, literally "truth-force" or "soul-force") — a name he had coined specifically to distinguish his approach from "passive resistance," which he considered too defensive and too dependent on the moral weakness of the opponent. Satyagraha rested on five principles: that the resister's truth must be morally compelling, not merely instrumental; that the resistance must be nonviolent in deed, word, and intention; that the resister must be willing to suffer the consequences of the action without retaliation; that the goal was the conversion of the opponent, not their defeat; and that the campaign must be disciplined, with carefully chosen issues and forms of action, not spontaneous outbursts.

The method was not invented from nothing. It drew on three streams: Hindu ahimsa (nonviolence as a religious obligation, prominent in Jainism and in the Bhagavad Gita's contemplation of moral action); Tolstoy's late Christian anarchism (Gandhi and Tolstoy exchanged letters in 1909–10, the year of Tolstoy's death, on the question of nonviolent resistance to state authority); and the practical experience of the Indian community in South Africa, where the available political tools were limited and violent resistance would have meant immediate annihilation. The combination was distinctive. No one before Gandhi had argued that mass nonviolent civil disobedience could be a political program for an entire population's liberation rather than a personal ethical commitment or a tactical concession to military weakness.

The **identity** was that of an Indian peasant. Gandhi wore Indian peasant clothing — by 1921 he had reduced his daily wardrobe to a single hand-spun cotton dhoti (loincloth) and a shawl. He traveled third class on Indian trains. He ate the simplest possible diet — rice, vegetables, fruit, milk, no meat, no alcohol, no spices. He lived in *ashrams* (residential communities organized around shared work and spiritual discipline) — first at Sabarmati near Ahmedabad (founded 1915) and later at Sevagram near Wardha (founded 1936). He practiced fasting as a political and spiritual discipline. The self-presentation was theatrical, intentional, and effective. The Congress moderates had been speaking the language of British liberalism; Gandhi was speaking a language British liberalism could not fully process. Winston Churchill, then a backbench opponent of Indian self-government, famously sneered in 1931 at the spectacle of "a seditious Middle Temple lawyer, now posing as a fakir of a type well known in the East, striding half-naked up the steps of the Viceregal palace" to negotiate as an equal with the representative of the King-Emperor.

The **wardrobe** mattered more than it seems. The hand-spun cotton — *khadi* — was both a symbol and an industrial policy. The colonial economic order rested in significant part on Indian raw cotton being exported to Lancashire, woven into cloth by industrial machinery, and re-exported to India for sale, with the value-added captured by British industry. Gandhi's spinning-wheel campaign — *khadi* production by hand, using the *charkha* (small portable spinning wheel) that became the emblem of Indian nationalism — was a deliberate attempt to short-circuit that loop. Every yard of hand-spun cloth was a yard of Lancashire cotton not sold. The campaign was economically marginal at scale, but the symbolism was central. The *charkha* would later appear on the Indian National Congress flag (1931) and remains on the Indian state emblem today.

---

Gandhi's first Indian campaigns were small and local. The **Champaran agitation** of 1917 — a campaign in support of Bihar's indigo tenant farmers against the synthetic-indigo-driven collapse of their forced cultivation contracts — was Gandhi's first encounter with rural Indian poverty and his first demonstration that the method developed in South Africa could work on Indian conditions. He won concessions from the Bihar government and learned, in the process, that he could deploy disciplined nonviolence among illiterate peasant populations who had never heard of Tolstoy. The **Kheda agitation** of 1918, in Gujarat, was a similar campaign on similar lines — tenant farmers against revenue collection during a famine year — and again succeeded. Both were local. Neither was a national political program. But they established Gandhi's working methods: live among the people you are organizing, fast publicly when negotiations stall, refuse to leave until the dispute is resolved, accept whatever consequences the colonial state imposes.

The first national-scale campaign came after Jallianwala Bagh. The **Non-Cooperation Movement** of 1920–22 was Gandhi's response to the British government's refusal to discipline General Dyer and to the broader recognition, after the Rowlatt Act and the Punjab violence, that constitutional cooperation with the colonial state had no future. The program was sweeping: Indians were to withdraw from government schools and colleges, from the law courts, from the new dyarchical legislatures, from the British honors system, from all forms of British employment, and from British goods. The program also bound itself to the **Khilafat Movement** — a parallel Indian Muslim campaign in support of the Ottoman Caliphate, which the post-WWI Allies were about to dismantle and which Indian Muslims regarded as the spiritual head of the global Muslim community. The Khilafat–Non-Cooperation alliance was strategically extraordinary. It produced, for the first and only sustained time in colonial Indian history, a unified Hindu-Muslim mass political movement under a single command structure.

The Congress under Gandhi's direction in 1920 was a different body from the Congress of 1885. Membership was opened to virtually anyone for a token annual subscription of four annas (a quarter of a rupee). Regional committees were organized down to the village level. The movement could call for the boycott of an election or the closure of a town and have the order implemented across hundreds of cities. Total Congress membership reached two million by 1921. The Congress had become a mass organization.

The movement lasted twenty-two months. It was unilaterally called off by Gandhi in February 1922 after the **Chauri Chaura** incident — a police-station-burning by a Non-Cooperation crowd in eastern Uttar Pradesh that killed twenty-two policemen. Gandhi argued that the violence proved India was not yet ready for nonviolent independence. The decision was bitterly contested within the Congress. Subhas Chandra Bose, a young Bengali nationalist who would become Gandhi's most consequential intra-Congress opponent (Chapter 4), considered the decision a catastrophic abdication. Jawaharlal Nehru — Motilal Nehru's son, then a young Allahabad lawyer entering Congress politics — described in his autobiography the feeling that the movement had been "snatched away" at its peak. Gandhi was imprisoned a month later, tried for sedition over articles he had written in his weekly *Young India*, and sentenced to six years. He served two before being released on medical grounds in 1924. The Khilafat alliance, which had partly held together because of the Non-Cooperation campaign, collapsed; Hindu-Muslim political alignment never fully recovered.

---

The second great satyagraha was the **Salt March** of 1930, eight years later, and was probably Gandhi's tactical masterpiece. The British colonial government held a salt monopoly: all salt sold in India had to be produced or licensed by the government, and a tax was levied on each pound. The salt tax was modest in revenue terms but politically significant — it taxed a necessity of life, fell most heavily on the poorest, and required the rural Indian population to pay the government for the right to consume something that washed up daily on the coast.

Gandhi chose the salt monopoly as the issue for his second major national campaign because it had three properties: it was nationally legible to everyone (every Indian household used salt and paid the tax); it was symbolically perfect (a foreign government taxing the natural produce of the sea); and it could be defied physically by simply walking to the coast and picking up salt. The action was not abstract. The defiance could be performed by individuals.

The march itself was theatrically engineered. Gandhi left Sabarmati Ashram on March 12, 1930, with 78 chosen volunteers, on a 240-mile walk to the coast at Dandi. The route, the pace (about 12 miles a day), the stops in villages along the way for speeches and prayer meetings, the daily press updates, the photographs and newsreel footage transmitted around the world — all of it was carefully calibrated to maximize attention while remaining absolutely nonviolent and absolutely legal until the act of salt-picking at the end. The arrival at Dandi on April 5 and Gandhi's ceremonial bending to pick up natural salt from the beach the next morning was the most photographed moment of the Indian freedom struggle.

Mass salt-defiance broke out across India in the following weeks. Hundreds of thousands of Indians made or sold illegal salt; the government arrested tens of thousands. The poet Sarojini Naidu led a follow-up satyagraha at the Dharasana salt works (in May 1930) where over 2,000 satyagrahis were systematically beaten by police lathis (long wooden batons) — an event covered in extraordinary detail by United Press correspondent Webb Miller, whose dispatch was the first sustained, English-language, eyewitness account of British colonial police violence to reach mass American newspaper readers. American public opinion shifted. The 1930s American left was already disposed against European colonialism; the Salt March made British India a specific and visible target.

Gandhi was arrested in May 1930 and held without trial under the Bengal Regulation of 1818. He was released in January 1931 for the **First Round Table Conference** in London, the first of three conferences held between the British government and Indian political representatives to discuss the constitutional future. The conferences were inconclusive. They produced the **Government of India Act 1935** (Chapter 4), the largest single piece of colonial constitutional legislation, which would frame Indian politics until 1947 — but they did not produce independence, which was the only thing the Congress now wanted. Gandhi's negotiations with successive Viceroys (Irwin in 1931, Linlithgow later) settled various intermediate disputes — temporary truces, the suspension of various satyagrahas, the release of political prisoners — without resolving the underlying question.

By 1934, Gandhi had withdrawn formally from the Indian National Congress, declaring that the Congress had drifted from his program of village reconstruction and constructive work, while remaining its presiding moral authority. The campaign for *swaraj* (self-rule) continued. The cast of leaders who would carry it through the final phase — Nehru, Patel, Bose, Maulana Azad, the rising provincial figures who would become independent India's first state-level politicians — was already in place. The next chapter is about the seventeen years from the Salt March to the Mountbatten Plan, during which the question shifted from whether the British would leave to who would inherit what they left behind.

# Chapter 4 — The Path to Freedom

The seventeen years from the Salt March to independence were the most politically dense in colonial Indian history. They contained four substantial constitutional revisions, two world wars, the rise of an alternative armed Indian liberation movement, a famine that killed perhaps three million people, the breakdown of the Hindu-Muslim political alliance the Congress had assumed it could rebuild, and finally the rushed dismantlement of the Raj under a Labour government that had run out of money and Asia-strategy.

The **Government of India Act 1935** is where to start. The Act was the largest single piece of British colonial legislation ever passed — 321 sections and ten schedules, over 1,200 pages — and was the framework within which Indian politics operated from 1937 through the formal handover. The Act created an All-India Federation that combined British India with the princely states (though the federation provisions never actually came into effect because too few princely states acceded); restructured British India as eleven provinces with their own elected legislatures, Indian cabinets, and substantially expanded franchises (around 35 million voters, roughly 14% of the adult population); abolished dyarchy and gave full provincial autonomy to elected Indian governments in the provinces (subject to gubernatorial "emergency" override powers); and left central government — defense, foreign affairs, communications — entirely in British hands, governed by a Viceroy whose authority was now constitutional rather than purely executive.

The 1937 provincial elections, the first held under the new Act, produced **Congress provincial ministries** in seven of the eleven provinces. The ministries — in Bombay, Madras, the United Provinces, Central Provinces, Bihar, Orissa, and the North-West Frontier Province — held office for twenty-eight months before resigning in October 1939 over the question of whether India had been entered into the war without consultation. They were the Congress's first sustained experience of executive government. Several future cabinet ministers of independent India served in them; the administrative habits formed in 1937–39 — provincial planning departments, public-works programs, rural development schemes, language policy choices in education — shaped what the postwar Indian state would look like. Vallabhbhai Patel, who would later be independent India's first home minister and the architect of the integration of the princely states (Chapter 5), ran the Congress provincial governments as a kind of de facto coalition leader. Jawaharlal Nehru did not hold provincial office; he stayed at the national party level, traveling, writing, organizing.

The Congress's main electoral competition in 1937 was the **Muslim League** — by then under the leadership of **Muhammad Ali Jinnah**, who had returned to active Indian politics in 1934 after a decade in London. The 1937 results were a disaster for the League: in Muslim-majority provinces it won pluralities rather than majorities, and the Congress refused (controversially, in retrospect) to share provincial cabinet positions with League representatives in the United Provinces where a coalition might have been mathematically possible. The League's reading of the results was that the Congress was now in effect a Hindu party, however much it claimed otherwise, and that Muslim political interests required a separate organizational vehicle.

This conclusion was the basis for the **Lahore Resolution** of March 1940 — the so-called "Pakistan Resolution," though the word *Pakistan* (coined by the Cambridge student Choudhry Rahmat Ali in 1933 as an acronym for **P**unjab–**A**fghania–**K**ashmir–**S**indh–Baluchis**tan**) did not appear in the resolution's text. The Lahore Resolution called for "Independent States" — plural — in the Muslim-majority regions of British India. What this would have meant in practical constitutional terms was deliberately left vague. Whether Jinnah in 1940 actually wanted a separate sovereign Muslim state, or whether he was using the demand as a bargaining lever to extract better terms for Muslims within a federated India, is a question Indian and Pakistani historiography has been arguing about for eighty years. The probable answer is that Jinnah's position changed between 1940 and 1947, that he was open to a federated solution in the early 1940s and committed to partition only after the late-war negotiations failed, but that this is a difficult question to settle definitively because Jinnah was a careful lawyer who did not write down his strategic thinking.

---

The Second World War transformed the political landscape. The Viceroy Lord Linlithgow declared India at war with Germany in September 1939 without consulting any Indian political body. The Congress demanded an immediate transfer of power as the price for cooperation; Linlithgow refused; the Congress provincial ministries resigned in October. India spent the rest of the war under direct British rule, with Congress leadership periodically imprisoned and the Muslim League gradually becoming the official British interlocutor for "Indian opinion."

The British **Cripps Mission** of March 1942 — led by the Labour Party's Sir Stafford Cripps, sent to India under wartime pressure from Roosevelt and the British dominions to negotiate Indian cooperation — offered eventual dominion status after the war, with the right of provinces to opt out of the post-war Indian union (an early signal of acceptance of the partition principle). The Congress rejected the offer as deferring real power to an undefined future moment while demanding immediate war cooperation. Gandhi described the offer as "a post-dated cheque on a failing bank." The Mission failed.

In August 1942, with Japanese forces advancing through Burma and Burma–India border attacks underway, the Congress passed the **Quit India Resolution** at Bombay. The resolution demanded immediate British withdrawal and authorized a mass nonviolent rebellion. Gandhi gave the famous "do or die" speech to the All India Congress Committee — "we shall either free India or die in the attempt; we shall not live to see the perpetuation of our slavery." Within twenty-four hours, the British arrested Gandhi, Nehru, Patel, Maulana Azad, and the entire senior Congress leadership. They would not be released until June 1945.

The **Quit India Movement** that followed was the most violent of the major Congress satyagrahas, despite Gandhi's nonviolent framing — partly because the Congress leadership was imprisoned and could not enforce discipline, and partly because the issue (immediate independence from a wartime occupying power) was more existential than any previous one. Telegraph lines were cut, police stations attacked, railway tracks sabotaged, government officials assaulted. The British response was overwhelming: 60,000 arrests in the first months, the deployment of British and Indian army troops against Indian civilians, the establishment of parallel underground Congress committees that operated for the rest of the war. The movement was suppressed by late 1943. The independence question was deferred to the post-war negotiation.

---

The most striking single phenomenon of the war years was the alternative liberation strategy pursued by **Subhas Chandra Bose**. Bose (1897–1945), Bengali, Cambridge-educated, civil-service trained, was the Congress's most charismatic and most operationally aggressive figure of the 1930s. He had been Congress president twice, in 1938 and 1939, before being forced out of the party leadership by Gandhi's faction over the question of whether to make common cause with the British in the war or to seek alliance with Britain's enemies. Bose's view was that the British Empire's wartime weakness was a strategic opportunity that Indian nationalists could not afford to miss; Gandhi's view was that allying with Nazi Germany or Imperial Japan would betray the moral basis of the freedom struggle and would produce, after the war, a worse occupation by a different power.

Bose, ousted, fled India in January 1941 under house arrest at Calcutta, escaped through Afghanistan to the Soviet Union (then a non-belligerent against Germany), and was passed via Berlin and Tokyo to a Japanese-occupied Southeast Asia. From the Andaman Islands and Singapore he reorganized the **Indian National Army (INA)** — *Azad Hind Fauj* — built originally by other Indian officers from the Japanese-captured Indian prisoners of British forces in Malaya. The INA fought under Japanese command in the 1944 Imphal–Kohima campaign — the Japanese attempt to invade British India through the northeastern hills, which failed disastrously and is generally regarded as one of the decisive land battles of the Pacific War. Bose's eastern army crossed the Indian border at Manipur, planted the Indian tricolor on Indian soil, and was destroyed by British counter-offensives over the following months. Bose himself died on August 18, 1945 in a plane crash in Taiwan while en route to Japanese-occupied Manchuria. The plane crash, the death, and the body's cremation in Tokyo have been the subject of conspiracy theories in Indian political culture for eighty years; there is no serious evidence for any alternative narrative, but the political utility of Bose-as-mystery has kept the speculation alive.

The Bose phenomenon mattered politically in 1945–46 in a way the Congress had not anticipated. The British had assumed that **the INA trials** — court-martials of former INA officers for treason against the Crown, held at the Red Fort in Delhi from November 1945 — would be a quiet legal exercise to reassert imperial discipline. Instead, the trials became a massive cause célèbre. Indian nationalist lawyers — Bhulabhai Desai, Tej Bahadur Sapru, Jawaharlal Nehru (returning to a law career briefly), and others — defended the INA officers as patriots; popular sentiment across India sided with the defendants; the Royal Indian Navy mutinied at Bombay in February 1946 in part over the INA trials; the British government commuted the death sentences and quietly dropped further prosecutions. The trials demonstrated that the British could no longer rely on the Indian Army to enforce British authority against Indian political mobilization. The strategic calculus shifted.

---

The **Bengal Famine of 1943** was the war's other defining Indian event. The famine killed somewhere between two and three million people in Bengal between summer 1943 and early 1944. The causal chain has been the subject of extensive historical analysis (notably Amartya Sen's *Poverty and Famines*, 1981, which made the famine a foundational case in modern famine economics). The proximate causes were a cyclone that destroyed Bengal's autumn 1942 rice crop; the Japanese conquest of Burma, which cut off rice imports that normally supplied 15% of Bengali consumption; and the wartime "denial policy" under which the colonial government had stripped coastal Bengal of boats and grain stocks to prevent Japanese use after invasion. The structural cause was the inability or unwillingness of the colonial government to mobilize a wartime food rationing and distribution system that could have prevented mass starvation in the affected areas. The colonial wartime cabinet (Linlithgow as Viceroy, succeeded by Wavell in October 1943) and the war cabinet in London under Churchill made a series of choices that the historical record now suggests were criminally negligent: continued export of rice from India during the famine peak; refusal to divert shipping from non-essential military uses to bring relief grain; and the dismissal of Bengali demands for emergency aid as exaggerated. Churchill's personal contempt for Indian political demands was a documented factor.

The famine reshaped Bengali politics permanently. Bengal had been the Congress's strongest province in 1937; by 1946 it was the Muslim League's stronghold. The famine had hit the rural Muslim cultivator population hardest, and the League's wartime provincial ministry under H. S. Suhrawardy had handled relief somewhat better than the British and Congress alternatives. The League also benefited from a more general pattern: where the Congress was disabled by the wartime imprisonment of its leadership, the League had cooperated with the war effort, kept its provincial machinery intact, and emerged in 1945–46 with a much stronger political infrastructure than its 1937 results would have predicted.

---

The post-war settlement came under the Labour government elected in July 1945. Clement Attlee's cabinet — committed to decolonization in principle, broke after six years of total war, facing American pressure to withdraw from imperial commitments — sent the **Cabinet Mission** of March–July 1946 to negotiate the constitutional form of an Indian transfer of power. The Mission proposed a three-tier federation: provinces grouped into clusters (one Muslim-majority, one Hindu-majority, one mixed), grouped into a federal union with limited central powers. The Congress and the League both formally accepted the plan in summer 1946, then both immediately repudiated parts of it; the negotiation collapsed by autumn. Jinnah called for a **Direct Action Day** on August 16, 1946 to demonstrate Muslim political resolve. The day became the **Great Calcutta Killings** — a four-day communal riot in which approximately 4,000 people died and over 100,000 were rendered homeless, primarily in Hindu–Muslim attacks across central Calcutta neighborhoods, with both communities involved in atrocities. Calcutta in August 1946 was the dress rehearsal for what would happen across northern India eleven months later.

The plan that would actually settle the question — partition, with the Punjab and Bengal divided along religious-demographic lines, the rest of British India transferred as a single Indian Union, the princely states left to choose between the two new dominions — was announced by the new Viceroy, **Lord Louis Mountbatten**, in June 1947, with handover to occur on August 15, 1947. The acceleration was extraordinary. The Cabinet Mission had assumed a multi-year transition. Mountbatten compressed the schedule to ten weeks. The reasons were partly his own assessment that further delay would produce uncontrollable violence, partly the British government's recognition that it could no longer fund or staff the administration through a long transition, and partly the increasing communal violence in Bengal, Punjab, and the United Provinces, which suggested that the longer the British waited the worse the eventual handover would be.

The accelerated schedule was a catastrophe. The next chapter is what happened in the ten weeks between Mountbatten's plan and the formal independence of the two new dominions.

---

# Chapter 5 — Partition

Cyril Radcliffe arrived in India on July 8, 1947. He had never been to India before. He spoke no Indian language. He had no specialist knowledge of South Asian geography, demography, religion, or history. His career to that point had been at the English bar, where he was widely considered one of the leading commercial litigators of his generation. He was forty-seven years old and had been chosen for his ignorance: the British government wanted a commissioner whose lack of prior Indian connections would make his determinations defensible as impartial. He was given five weeks — five weeks — to draw the lines that would divide the Punjab and Bengal between the new dominions of **India gains independence** and Pakistan.

The work was done in a single small office in the Viceroy's residence in New Delhi, using out-of-date census tables, district-by-district religious-demographic data of variable reliability, and the recommendations of the two **Boundary Commissions** (one for Punjab, one for Bengal) whose Indian and Pakistani members had deadlocked on most contested districts, leaving Radcliffe as chairman to make the determinative calls. The Punjab map was the hardest: in the central Punjab, in districts where Sikh, Hindu, and Muslim populations were thoroughly intermixed and where the Sikh Khalsa institutions (especially the Golden Temple at Amritsar) and the headworks of the canal-irrigation system were spread across what would become both sides of the border, no division could be drawn that did not produce massive minorities on the wrong side of the line. Radcliffe's eventual line ran through villages — sometimes through individual fields, leaving farmers' homes in one country and their crops in another. The Bengal map produced a smaller but equally arbitrary line dividing Calcutta from the eastern jute-growing districts that had supplied its mills.

The lines were finalized on August 12, 1947. They were not published until August 17, two days after independence. The reason for the delay was Mountbatten's calculated political decision: announcing the actual borders before the formal handover would have pre-empted any final political negotiation and might have triggered immediate fighting between the two states' new armies. The cost of the delay was that the population transfers — millions of people moving between regions assigned to the "wrong" state — happened largely in ignorance of where the actual borders were, with the resulting confusion compounding the violence.

**India gains independence** on August 15, 1947. Pakistan formally became independent on August 14, one day earlier, so that Mountbatten could attend both ceremonies in his capacity as the last Viceroy of British India and the first Governor-General of independent India. The flag ceremonies in Karachi and New Delhi were attended by jubilant crowds. Nehru's "Tryst with Destiny" speech to the Constituent Assembly at midnight August 14–15 — "long years ago, we made a tryst with destiny, and now the time comes when we shall redeem our pledge" — has remained the defining rhetorical moment of Indian independence, but the speech was being delivered in Delhi at the same hour the killing was at peak intensity in the Punjab.

---

The **Partition of India** killed between half a million and two million people in the three months around independence. The lower estimate is approximately the British government's contemporaneous figure; the upper estimate is from later Indian and Pakistani academic studies that incorporated previously unreported village-level data and corpses recovered later from disused wells, canals, and railway sidings. The actual number is unknowable. The number of displaced persons is better attested: roughly 15 million people crossed the new borders in 1947–48 — Muslims moving from India to West and East Pakistan, Hindus and Sikhs moving from West Pakistan into India, Hindus moving from East Pakistan into West Bengal. It was the largest forced migration in human history outside of war or famine.

The violence had patterns. In the divided Punjab, the worst killings were Sikh-on-Muslim and Muslim-on-Sikh, with the Sikh *jathas* (armed bands originally formed for self-defense against Muslim violence in West Punjab) systematically attacking Muslim refugee columns and Muslim villages in the east, while Muslim *Khaksar* and tribal-irregular groups in West Punjab attacked Hindu and Sikh refugee columns and villages on their side. In the United Provinces and Bihar, Hindu-on-Muslim violence predominated. In Bengal, the killings were lower in intensity than in the Punjab — partly because Gandhi conducted a months-long personal fast in Noakhali district from October 1946 onward, exerting enormous moral pressure on local Hindu and Muslim leaders to restrain their followers, and partly because the Bengali rural fabric, intermixed as it was, made the kind of clean-sweep ethnic violence the Punjab saw harder to organize. The Sindh experience was different again — relatively low-violence, but with the gradual expulsion of Sindh's substantial Hindu trader and professional population eastward into India.

Refugee columns moved by the train, by oxcart, by foot, in some places stretching dozens of miles. The trains were attacked. **"Ghost trains"** — trains arriving at Lahore from Amritsar or at Amritsar from Lahore with no living passengers, only the corpses of refugees murdered en route — became a defining image. Both the Indian and Pakistani governments later attempted to track the death tolls on specific trains; the records are partial and inconsistent, but multiple "ghost train" incidents are documented in contemporary newspaper accounts and in the recently declassified British military records. Rape was systematic and large-scale on all sides: estimates range from 75,000 to over 100,000 women abducted across the new border, many forcibly converted and remarried in the receiving community, with state-organized recovery missions on both sides continuing into the 1950s.

The receiving cities — Delhi, Calcutta, Karachi, Lahore — absorbed staggering refugee populations. Delhi's pre-Partition population of about 900,000 grew to over 1.4 million within a year, with the new arrivals (mostly Hindus and Sikhs from the West Punjab) clustering in refugee camps and informal settlements that would become permanent neighborhoods. Karachi's population doubled in a year. Lahore lost its Hindu and Sikh middle class and gained a Muslim refugee population from the east. The demographic and economic fabric of all four cities was reshaped within months.

---

The **integration of the princely states** ran in parallel to the Partition violence and was led by Vallabhbhai Patel as independent India's first Deputy Prime Minister and Home Minister, working with V. P. Menon, an Indian civil servant whose long career in the Viceroy's secretariat had given him deep personal relationships with most of the princely households. Of the 565 princely states, Patel and Menon secured 562 accessions to the Indian Union or to Pakistan between June and August 1947, working through a combination of personal pressure, financial inducements (privy purses paid to the former rulers in exchange for their renunciation of sovereignty), and the implicit threat that states attempting to remain independent would face political and economic isolation. The three holdouts — **Junagadh**, **Hyderabad**, and **Kashmir** — each required different solutions.

Junagadh, a Muslim-ruled coastal state on the Kathiawar peninsula in modern Gujarat, with a majority-Hindu population, attempted to accede to Pakistan in August 1947. The local population revolted. The state's Nawab fled to Karachi. The Indian government took over administration and conducted a plebiscite in February 1948 that confirmed accession to India.

Hyderabad, a Muslim-ruled state of 17 million people in the southern Deccan, with a majority-Hindu population and a fanatic Muslim paramilitary force (the Razakars) terrorizing the Hindu majority, attempted to remain independent. After a year of negotiation and escalating Razakar violence, the Indian Army conducted **Operation Polo** in September 1948 — a five-day military operation that occupied the entire state and dissolved the Nizam's government. The operation killed somewhere between 30,000 and 200,000 people, most in the Razakar-led communal violence that the Indian Army occupation suppressed; later inquiries (notably the Sundarlal Committee report, suppressed for decades and only fully released in 2013) confirmed substantial atrocities by both Razakar forces and the Indian Army during and after the operation. Hyderabad joined the Indian Union.

**Kashmir** was different. The Maharaja Hari Singh — Hindu, ruling a majority-Muslim population (roughly 77% Muslim, 20% Hindu, 3% Sikh in 1941) — wanted independence rather than accession to either dominion. In October 1947, tribal Pashtun irregulars from the Pakistani side of the new border invaded Kashmir, ostensibly without Pakistani government coordination (the actual extent of Pakistani military support has been the subject of contested historical analysis for seventy-seven years; the documentary evidence suggests that Pakistani government coordination was real, but the public framing was deliberately ambiguous). The tribal invaders reached the outskirts of Srinagar. Hari Singh signed the **Instrument of Accession** to India on October 26, 1947 in exchange for Indian military intervention. Indian Army units were airlifted to Srinagar the next day. The First Kashmir War ran for thirteen months and was settled by UN-brokered ceasefire in January 1949. The ceasefire line left roughly two-thirds of the former princely state under Indian administration (the Kashmir Valley, Jammu, Ladakh) and one-third under Pakistani administration (later called Azad Kashmir and the Northern Areas). The dispute over the legal and political status of the territory has framed Indo-Pakistani relations ever since.

---

**Gandhi's assassination** completed the founding sequence. On January 30, 1948, in the garden of Birla House in Delhi where Gandhi was conducting his daily prayer meeting, **Nathuram Godse** — a former member of the Hindu Mahasabha and the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh, with a longer record of involvement in Hindu nationalist politics than the immediate news coverage acknowledged — approached Gandhi, touched his feet in respect, drew a Beretta pistol from his waist, and fired three rounds into Gandhi's chest at point-blank range. Gandhi died within minutes.

Godse's stated motive in his trial was that Gandhi had been pro-Muslim. The specific grievance was Gandhi's January 1948 fast — the second of his major late-life fasts in Delhi — to compel the Indian government to release 550 million rupees of Pakistan's share of the partition assets, which the Indian cabinet had been withholding in protest of Pakistani conduct in Kashmir. Patel had been opposed to the release; Nehru and Gandhi had insisted on it as a matter of honoring the partition settlement. Gandhi's fast had forced the cabinet to relent. For Godse and the Hindu nationalist circle around him, the fast was final proof that Gandhi had structurally sacrificed Hindu and Indian interests to Muslim and Pakistani ones across decades of negotiation, and that his moral authority had to be ended by violence.

The assassination shocked the country. The funeral procession from Birla House through Delhi was attended by perhaps two million people. Nehru's radio broadcast from the night of the assassination — "the light has gone out of our lives, and there is darkness everywhere... the light that has illumined this country for these many years will illumine this country for many more years" — was the rhetorical moment in which independent India formally consecrated Gandhi as the *Father of the Nation*. The RSS was banned (the ban was lifted in 1949 on the RSS's commitment to remain a cultural rather than political organization, a commitment whose subsequent honoring has been contested across all of independent Indian history). Godse and his co-conspirator Narayan Apte were hanged at Ambala Jail on November 15, 1949. The full conspiracy — the extent of involvement by the Hindu Mahasabha, by V. D. Savarkar (the older Hindu nationalist theorist, tried and acquitted), and by Pune-based militant networks more broadly — has been the subject of further inquiries (the **Kapur Commission** of 1965–70) that documented additional networks of complicity without producing further criminal convictions. The political question of who, exactly, was responsible for Gandhi's death beyond the two convicted assassins is one Indian historiography is still working through.

The Republic, when it was formally inaugurated two years later, would carry both the memory of the freedom struggle and the trauma of how its first months had ended. The constitution Nehru and Ambedkar would draft, and the political settlement they would impose, was conditioned by both.

---

# Chapter 6 — Nehru's Republic

The Constituent Assembly that drafted India's constitution had been elected in 1946 under the limited franchise of the late Raj. After Partition, the assembly's remaining members — 299 representatives drawn from the provinces and the acceded princely states — sat in Delhi from August 1947 through November 1949 to produce the founding document. The chairman of the drafting committee was **B. R. Ambedkar** (1891–1956), the most consequential figure of independent India that the West has typically heard the least about.

Ambedkar was born to a Mahar family — a Dalit caste community in Maharashtra, in the system of social ranks that the colonial census had begun calling "untouchable" — and was the first member of his community to receive higher education at international levels. He took degrees at Bombay University, at Columbia University in New York (PhD in economics, 1927), and at the London School of Economics, with a parallel call to the English bar at Gray's Inn. He spent the 1920s and 1930s as the leading political voice for Dalit (literally "broken" or "crushed," the term Dalits prefer for their own community) emancipation, and the leading critic of Gandhi's framing of caste reform as a problem of Hindu spiritual reform rather than a problem of legal and economic structure. Ambedkar and Gandhi clashed publicly across the 1930s — most famously over the **Poona Pact** of 1932, where Gandhi's fast forced Ambedkar to give up his demand for separate Dalit electorates in exchange for a system of reserved seats within general constituencies — and the disagreement was substantive and unresolved. Ambedkar's later view, written down in *Annihilation of Caste* (1936) and in his unfinished history *The Buddha and His Dhamma* (1957, published posthumously), was that the Hindu social order was so structurally hostile to Dalit dignity that Dalits could only achieve full citizenship by formally leaving Hinduism. He converted to Buddhism with around half a million Dalit followers in October 1956, two months before his death.

That Ambedkar — a Dalit, a public Gandhi-critic, a man who would convert out of Hinduism — was placed in charge of drafting the new Indian constitution was Nehru's decision, supported by Patel. The decision is a case study in what the early Nehruvian state was. The new republic was claiming to be a constitutional democracy with full equality before the law; placing a Dalit at the center of the founding document made the claim politically and symbolically real. Ambedkar's authority over the constitution's text is also analytically real: the document's emphasis on individual rights and equality before the law, its abolition of untouchability (Article 17), its empowerment of the federal state to pursue affirmative-action programs (reservations) for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, and its careful balance of federal and provincial powers, reflect Ambedkar's specific intellectual project.

The **Indian Constitution adopted in 1950**, formally enacted on January 26, 1950 (Republic Day, chosen to commemorate the 1930 *Purna Swaraj* declaration of complete independence by the Congress), is the longest written constitution of any sovereign nation, then or now — 395 articles and 8 schedules in the original 1950 document (since expanded to over 470 articles and 12 schedules by numerous amendments). It established India as a "sovereign democratic republic" (the word "socialist" was added by the 42nd Amendment in 1976; the word "secular" was added the same year); created a parliamentary system on the British Westminster model with a President as head of state and a Prime Minister as head of government; created an independent judiciary with the Supreme Court at its apex; reserved seats in legislatures for Scheduled Castes (Dalits) and Scheduled Tribes (Adivasis, India's indigenous-forest-dwelling communities); created a federal structure with substantial state-level autonomy; protected individual fundamental rights through judicial enforcement; and provided non-justiciable but politically authoritative Directive Principles of State Policy guiding the government toward economic redistribution, secular education, and welfare provision.

The constitution did several things that are easy to miss from the West. It abolished untouchability in a way that European constitutions had never had cause to do. It established universal adult suffrage — every Indian over 21 (later 18, by amendment) regardless of literacy, property, gender, or caste — at a moment when much of Europe and the United States had not yet extended full voting rights to all of their adult citizens. It made Hindi (in the Devanagari script) the official language of the Union government — but added a transitional period during which English would continue as a working language, and explicitly enshrined linguistic diversity in the Eighth Schedule, which listed (originally) 14 recognized languages (now 22). It contained no established religion. It guaranteed the right to "freely profess, practice and propagate" religion (Article 25, where the word *propagate* was contested by some Hindu members because it implied a right to proselytize). The constitution was, in 1950, the most ambitious document of universal citizenship rights anywhere in the world.

---

The first **Nehru becomes first Prime Minister** in 1947 and held the office continuously until his death in May 1964. The seventeen years of his prime-ministership define what Indian historians call the Nehruvian Era and what the country still partly lives inside.

The Nehruvian project rested on four pillars. The first was **secular democracy** — the principle that the Indian state would treat all religions with equal respect, would not establish any religion, and would not allow religious considerations to drive state policy. The principle was tested almost immediately by Partition's communal violence and by the Hindu nationalist agitation around Gandhi's death; Nehru's specific contribution was to insist, repeatedly and at political cost, that the Indian state could not be a Hindu state, that the 35 million Muslims who had remained in India were full citizens with full rights, and that any political tendency that sought to make India a religious state would be met with the full weight of constitutional law. This commitment was not popular with every Congress colleague (Patel's instincts on Muslim and Pakistani questions were notably harder than Nehru's), and it was bitterly opposed by the RSS and the Hindu Mahasabha. The settlement Nehru imposed — that India would be a constitutionally secular state — held substantially until 1991 and is still the formal basis of Indian constitutional law, even as the political center has moved sharply away from it.

The second pillar was **planned economic development**. The **First Five-Year Plan launched in 1951** committed India to a Soviet-influenced model of state-led industrialization, with the public sector taking the "commanding heights" of heavy industry, agriculture as a private but state-coordinated sector, and consumer industries left to private capital under heavy regulation. The Second Plan (1956–61), drafted under the chairmanship of the Indian Statistical Institute's P. C. Mahalanobis, accelerated the heavy-industry emphasis: steel mills at Bhilai (Soviet collaboration), Rourkela (German), and Durgapur (British) became the symbols of the era; the **First IIT established in 1951** at Kharagpur (followed by Bombay 1958, Madras 1959, Kanpur 1959, Delhi 1961) created the engineering-education infrastructure that would later staff both Indian industry and, much later, Silicon Valley; large dam projects (Bhakra Nangal, Hirakud, the Damodar Valley Corporation) physically reshaped the Indian agricultural landscape.

The performance was mixed. India's annual GDP growth from 1950 to 1980 averaged about 3.5% — a rate the economist Raj Krishna would later call the **"Hindu rate of growth"** in a phrase that has been resented in Indian discourse ever since (Krishna himself was using it descriptively, not pejoratively, but the phrase has often been deployed to suggest some kind of cultural inability to grow faster). Population growth in the same period was about 2%, so per capita growth was barely 1.5%. India remained one of the poorest countries in the world by per capita income. But the period also produced the institutional foundations of a modernizing economy: a steel industry, an engineering workforce, a defense-research apparatus, a higher-education infrastructure, a banking system. The argument over whether Nehru's economic choices were correct given the constraints of the time has been a central element of Indian political economy since 1991 (Chapter 11). The retrospective consensus is now that the planning model was too rigid and that the import-substitution framework had locked in inefficiencies that compounded; the more careful version of the same argument is that the alternatives available in 1951 — full market opening to a global economy still dominated by colonial trade patterns, or a Communist-style command economy — would have been worse, and that Nehru chose the least-bad option available to him.

The third pillar was **non-alignment** in foreign policy. India would not join NATO. India would not join the Warsaw Pact. India would not allow foreign military bases on Indian soil. India would maintain working diplomatic relations with both blocs. India would lead, with Tito's Yugoslavia and Nasser's Egypt, the construction of a third pole of post-colonial states — the **Non-Aligned Movement**, formally constituted at Belgrade in 1961. The doctrine was widely admired in the developing world and consistently mistrusted in Washington, where John Foster Dulles's State Department considered neutrality between the West and the Soviet Union a form of soft hostility to the West. The doctrine produced one major strategic catastrophe (Chapter below, China 1962) and one substantial diplomatic success (the moral leadership India exercised through the 1950s on issues of decolonization and nuclear disarmament). Whether the trade was net positive depends on how one values the two outcomes.

The fourth pillar was **the integration of the princely states into modern republican structures**. Patel's work in 1947–48 had legally integrated the states; the harder work was administrative integration — converting heterogeneous princely administrations, with their own civil services and judicial systems and revenue codes, into the standardized Indian system. The **States Reorganisation Act 1956**, after a long agitation in southern India for redrawing internal state boundaries on linguistic lines, reorganized the country into fourteen states and six union territories, with boundaries that broadly followed linguistic divisions (Andhra Pradesh for Telugu speakers, Tamil Nadu for Tamil speakers, Kerala for Malayalam speakers, Karnataka for Kannada speakers, Maharashtra and Gujarat eventually split in 1960). The reorganization is now generally recognized as a major piece of state-building: it gave each major linguistic community a state in which their language was the official medium of government, education, and administration, defused linguistic separatism that had been a serious threat in the early 1950s, and produced the federal structure that has held the country together since.

---

The **1962 Sino-Indian War** broke Nehru. India and the People's Republic of China had recognized each other in 1950, with the slogan *Hindi-Chini bhai-bhai* — "Indians and Chinese are brothers" — defining the Nehruvian framing of bilateral relations. The slogan obscured a serious territorial dispute. The eastern border between India and Chinese-occupied Tibet — the **McMahon Line** drawn at the 1914 Simla Convention between British India, Tibet, and China — had never been accepted by any government in Beijing. The western border — the **Aksai Chin** plateau, an empty high-altitude region claimed by India as part of Ladakh and by China as part of Xinjiang — had also never been bilaterally agreed. China was, by the late 1950s, building a road across Aksai Chin connecting Tibet to Xinjiang. Indian forward patrols had begun encountering Chinese road-construction crews and military posts in territory India considered its own.

The **Forward Policy** of 1961 — Nehru and Defence Minister Krishna Menon's response to the territorial dispute — instructed Indian troops to establish forward posts inside disputed territory, including some posts behind Chinese positions, on the theory that physical Indian presence would deter Chinese advance without provoking actual conflict because (as Indian planners assumed) the Chinese would not risk a wider war over the issue. The assessment was wrong. On October 20, 1962, the People's Liberation Army launched coordinated attacks across both the western (Ladakh) and eastern (NEFA, the North-East Frontier Agency, now Arunachal Pradesh) fronts. Indian forward positions were overrun within days. The PLA advanced 90 miles into Indian-claimed territory in the east before unilaterally declaring a ceasefire on November 21 and withdrawing to positions north of the McMahon Line — keeping all the Aksai Chin territory it claimed in the west, and demonstrating it could have advanced much further in the east if it had chosen.

The military defeat was complete. Roughly 1,400 Indian soldiers were killed in action; 1,047 were wounded; over 3,000 were captured. The captured included two brigade commanders and the disgraced corps commander General B. M. Kaul, who had personally directed the Forward Policy implementation. The political damage to Nehru was profound. The Defence Minister Krishna Menon was forced out. The intelligence and military leadership Nehru had defended for fifteen years was discredited. The non-alignment doctrine had failed in its first major test: India had received military aid from the United States and Britain during the crisis (airlifted small arms, ammunition, and air-defense equipment) but no strategic guarantees, and the Soviet Union — Nehru's primary diplomatic partner — had been technically neutral but in practice closer to Beijing than to Delhi. Indian public confidence in the Nehruvian project never fully recovered.

Nehru himself never fully recovered. He suffered a stroke in January 1964 and died of a second stroke on May 27, 1964. He had been Prime Minister for seventeen years. His daughter Indira had been managing his political appointments and his household for years; his colleague Lal Bahadur Shastri would succeed him as PM. The next chapter is what they did.

---

# Chapter 7 — Shastri and Indira's Rise

The Congress was unprepared for Nehru's death. There was no designated successor. The party leadership reverted to a process — the **Kamaraj Plan** institutional arrangements left over from 1963, named after the Tamil party chief Kumaraswami Kamaraj — that had originally been designed to bring younger leaders into the party but now functioned as a mechanism for collective selection of a Prime Minister. The party elders met in the days after Nehru's death and produced a consensus candidate: **Lal Bahadur Shastri** (1904–66), a small, soft-spoken UP politician who had served as Home Minister under Nehru and who was, importantly, acceptable to all of the major Congress factions because he did not threaten any of them. Shastri was sworn in on June 9, 1964.

Shastri's nineteen months in office surprised everyone. The man whose chief political asset had been his inoffensiveness turned out to have a serious independent program. The **Green Revolution begins in 1965**, the foundational shift in Indian agriculture from the recurrent-famine subsistence model to one of food self-sufficiency, was launched under Shastri in collaboration with the agriculture minister C. Subramaniam, the Indian Council of Agricultural Research, and (consequentially) the American foundations and US Agency for International Development. The program imported and locally adapted the high-yielding wheat varieties developed at the **International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center** in Mexico by Norman Borlaug, combined them with chemical fertilizers, irrigated water, and pesticides, and concentrated the package geographically on the Punjab and parts of Haryana where the irrigation infrastructure and cultivator base could absorb it. The first wheat harvest under the new package, in 1968, yielded 17 million tons against a previous best of 12 million; by 1971 India had achieved approximate food self-sufficiency in cereals. The package was unevenly distributed (rice followed wheat by about a decade; cotton and pulses lagged longer; the eastern states with their water-logged soils and tenant-farming systems got less benefit than the Punjab and Haryana), and its long-term environmental costs (groundwater depletion, soil exhaustion, pesticide accumulation, agricultural debt and farmer suicides) would become defining issues by the 2000s. But the short-run achievement was that the recurrent late-colonial famines of the 1870s–1940s — including the 1943 Bengal famine that had killed three million people — became, by the 1970s, a historical category rather than a recurring risk. The Indian state could feed its population. This had not been true since at least the 1860s.

The other defining event of Shastri's premiership was the **Second Indo-Pakistani War** of August–September 1965. The war began with Pakistani Operation Gibraltar — an infiltration of Pakistani-trained irregulars into Kashmir, on the theory that they would catalyze a Kashmiri uprising against Indian administration — that failed in its immediate goal but escalated to full-scale conventional war between the two armies along the entire Punjab–Rajasthan border. The fighting lasted seventeen days. Both sides claimed victory; the actual military result was roughly a stalemate, with India holding more captured Pakistani territory than vice versa at the ceasefire. The war was settled diplomatically at the **Tashkent Conference** in January 1966, brokered by the Soviet Union (the United States and China were both ambivalent or hostile to mediation), where Shastri and the Pakistani President Ayub Khan signed an agreement returning all captured territory and agreeing to settle Kashmir bilaterally rather than through war. The agreement is now widely considered to have been unfavorable to India given the military situation, but it ended the war.

Shastri died of a heart attack in Tashkent on the night of January 10–11, 1966, hours after signing the agreement. He had been Prime Minister for nineteen months. The Indian political class again confronted the question of who would lead the country, and again chose by Congress-elder consensus. The Congress's regional bosses — known as the **Syndicate**, a group including K. Kamaraj (Tamil Nadu), S. Nijalingappa (Karnataka), Atulya Ghosh (West Bengal), and S. K. Patil (Maharashtra) — selected Nehru's daughter **Indira Gandhi** as the next Prime Minister on the explicit understanding that she would be a pliant figure they could control. She was forty-eight, had served briefly as Information and Broadcasting Minister under Shastri, and had no independent political base. She had been chosen because she was Nehru's daughter and because the Syndicate believed she could be managed.

This proved to be one of the great political miscalculations of post-independence India. **Indira Gandhi becomes PM in 1966** and would hold the office, with one three-year interruption, for fifteen of the next eighteen years. She would also reshape the Congress in her own image, dismantle the Syndicate, and produce the most consequential single political career in modern Indian history.

---

Indira's first term — January 1966 to March 1971 — was a period of consolidation against the party establishment that had elected her. The Congress lost ground heavily in the **1967 General Election**, finishing with 283 of 520 Lok Sabha (lower house) seats — its narrowest national majority ever and its first major loss of state-level governments. Non-Congress coalitions took power in nine of the seventeen Indian states. The Syndicate read the 1967 results as evidence that Indira was electorally weak and that the party needed to return to collective leadership. Indira read the results as evidence that the Syndicate's old-style patronage politics had run out of legitimacy and that she needed to build her own political base independently of the elders.

She did so through populist programs. The **Bank Nationalization** of July 1969 — converting the fourteen largest private commercial banks into state-owned institutions in a single overnight ordinance — transferred control of the largest piece of Indian financial capital from private (mostly Bombay industrial) families to the central government and the public sector unions. It was massively popular. The bank-employee unions had been agitating for nationalization for years; the rural-cooperative banks and the smaller industrialists had wanted credit access the private banks had restricted; the Indian left was unanimous in supporting it. The Syndicate had opposed it. The split it produced in the Congress was decisive: in November 1969 the Syndicate expelled Indira from the Congress, and Indira responded by reorganizing her followers as **Congress (R)** — Requisition Congress, claiming continuity with the original organization — while the Syndicate continued as **Congress (O)** — Organisation Congress. The split functioned as a referendum within the party: Congress (R), Indira's faction, retained the larger share of Congress MPs and won the political battle. Indira ran the country as a minority government with support from the Communist parties from 1969 to 1971.

The **Abolition of Privy Purses** in 1971 was the second major populist move. The 1947 settlement with the princely states had committed the Indian government to pay the former rulers annual "privy purses" — pensions calibrated to their former state revenues — as compensation for renouncing sovereignty. The pensions had been guaranteed by the constitution. Indira's government, with the support of a two-thirds Lok Sabha majority following the snap **1971 General Election** (which she called early and won decisively on a single-issue *"Garibi Hatao"* — "Remove Poverty" — campaign, taking 352 of 518 Lok Sabha seats), passed the 26th Constitutional Amendment abolishing the privy purses entirely. The former princes lost their guaranteed pensions and their formal recognition as princes. The political class of the former princely states never recovered as a political force, and the symbolic break with the late-colonial settlement was complete.

---

The defining war of the Indira era came at the end of 1971. **East Pakistan** had been part of the Pakistani state since 1947 but was politically subordinated to West Pakistan: West Pakistani officials staffed the East Pakistani administration, West Pakistani military officers held all senior commands, Urdu (a West Pakistani language) was imposed as official medium in a Bengali-speaking province, and the substantial revenue surplus from East Pakistani jute exports flowed west. The Pakistani **1970 General Election** — the first conducted under universal franchise in a unified Pakistan — produced an absolute majority for the East Pakistani **Awami League** under **Sheikh Mujibur Rahman**, on a program of substantial East Pakistani autonomy. The West Pakistani military government under General Yahya Khan refused to allow Mujibur Rahman to form a government. On March 25, 1971, the Pakistani Army launched **Operation Searchlight** — a coordinated military campaign across East Pakistan to suppress the Awami League and Bengali nationalist political organization. The operation killed somewhere between several hundred thousand and three million Bengalis over the following nine months, displaced ten million refugees into the Indian state of West Bengal, and committed atrocities (the **Bangladesh genocide**) that the U.S. State Department's Dhaka consul Archer Blood documented in real time and that the Nixon administration suppressed for Cold War political reasons.

India's strategic problem was acute. Ten million East Pakistani refugees were a humanitarian and economic catastrophe for the eastern Indian states, which lacked the infrastructure to support the population. The Awami League was operating a government-in-exile in Calcutta. The Indian government recognized **Mukti Bahini** — the Bengali armed resistance forces — and provided training, weapons, and logistical support from Indian territory. India also signed the **Indo-Soviet Treaty of Peace, Friendship and Cooperation** in August 1971 — a strategic shift away from non-alignment, providing for "mutual consultation" in event of attack — that explicitly counterbalanced the U.S.-Pakistani strategic alignment under Nixon and Kissinger and provided the Soviet veto cover for what India was about to do.

Pakistan attacked first. Pakistani Air Force preemptive strikes on Indian airfields on December 3, 1971 gave India the pretext for full-scale military intervention. The **Bangladesh Liberation War** lasted thirteen days. Indian forces under General Sam Manekshaw — with the Mukti Bahini operating in coordination — advanced from three sides into East Pakistan, isolated the Pakistani garrison, and accepted the surrender of 93,000 Pakistani troops at Dhaka on December 16, 1971. It was the largest mass surrender of a regular army since the Second World War. East Pakistan became the independent state of Bangladesh. The new country recognized Sheikh Mujibur Rahman as its first prime minister.

The 1971 war was Indira Gandhi's strategic high-water mark. The opposition leader Atal Bihari Vajpayee — later himself Prime Minister (Chapter 11) — was widely reported to have hailed her in Parliament as **"Durga"**, the warrior goddess (Vajpayee in later years disputed having used the exact word, but the epithet stuck to the moment). Her domestic political authority was, for a brief moment, beyond serious challenge. The **Smiling Buddha nuclear test** of May 18, 1974, in which India detonated a 12-kiloton fission device underground at Pokhran in the Rajasthan desert, was the next confirmation. The test made India the first country outside the original five nuclear powers (US, USSR, UK, France, China) to demonstrate nuclear weapons capability. The Indian government called it a **"peaceful nuclear explosion"** in a fiction that fooled no one — the test triggered Pakistani crash nuclear-weapons development and international sanctions but also definitively established India as a major regional military power.

The crown ought to have rested easy. Instead, within twelve months, Indira would face the worst political crisis of her career — and would meet it by suspending Indian democracy. The next chapter is the Emergency.

# Chapter 8 — The Emergency

The political turn that produced the Emergency began with an electoral case. In 1971 Indira Gandhi had won her Rae Bareli Lok Sabha seat against the socialist veteran Raj Narain by a massive margin. Raj Narain filed an election petition in the Allahabad High Court alleging multiple corrupt practices: that the District Magistrate had been used for campaign work, that government officials had built election platforms, that distinct election-law violations had occurred. The petition seemed quixotic; the violations alleged were minor and procedural. The case ground through the courts for four years.

On June 12, 1975, Justice Jagmohan Lal Sinha of the Allahabad High Court ruled in Raj Narain's favor on two narrow technical grounds, finding Indira Gandhi guilty of electoral malpractice, voiding her 1971 election, and barring her from holding elected office for six years. The judgment was stayed for twenty days to allow appeal. It was the first time in Indian history that a sitting Prime Minister had been found guilty of an offense by an Indian court.

The political opposition saw the judgment as the moment to force Indira's resignation. The **Jayaprakash Narayan** movement — JP, the elderly Gandhian socialist who had been agitating for what he called "**Total Revolution**" against the Congress since 1973, drawing on the rural distress, urban unemployment, and middle-class anger over corruption that had built up across the early-1970s economic downturn — called for a mass rally at Delhi's Ramlila Maidan on June 25 and for an indefinite gherao (encirclement) of the Prime Minister's residence. JP also called on the police and the army to refuse "illegal" orders from the Indira government. The call was, by any constitutional standard, an extraordinary one — JP was effectively calling for the breakdown of governmental authority — and it was made by a figure whose Gandhian moral authority gave the call weight that no ordinary political appeal could have carried.

On the night of June 25–26, 1975, the **Emergency declared in 1975** was proclaimed under Article 352 of the Constitution — the provision allowing emergency suspension of fundamental rights in cases of "internal disturbance." The proclamation, signed by President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed at 11:45 PM on Indira Gandhi's recommendation, made effective without prior cabinet discussion, was the most consequential constitutional act of independent Indian history outside of the founding itself.

What the Emergency did, over the next twenty-one months, was to suspend the operative parts of Indian democracy. The opposition leadership — JP, Morarji Desai (former finance minister and rival), Atal Bihari Vajpayee, L. K. Advani, virtually every senior opposition politician across every party — was arrested in the first hours and held under the **Maintenance of Internal Security Act** (MISA), which allowed detention without trial. Press censorship was imposed within days, with prior-restraint instructions sent to every newspaper editor in the country requiring submission of front pages and editorials to government censors before publication. Three constitutional amendments — the 38th, 39th, and 42nd — were passed by a now-purely-Congress Parliament, restricting judicial review of emergency proclamations, retroactively legalizing the 1971 election, and granting the central government sweeping new powers over states, civil liberties, and election rules. Judges who had ruled against the government were transferred to remote benches. Civil-service officers who had spoken inconveniently were removed. The fundamental-rights enforcement mechanism that the Constitution had built was disabled.

The **Sanjay Gandhi** factor made the Emergency something more than a constitutional power-grab. Sanjay (1946–80) was Indira's younger son, twenty-eight in 1975, with no formal political position but with extensive informal authority through his mother's confidence. He had been running a parastatal small-car project (the Maruti project, eventually delivered as the Maruti Suzuki small car of the 1980s) that became a model of Emergency-era corruption: contracts handed to Sanjay-favored firms, regulatory shortcuts cleared by ministerial fiat, government banks pressured to lend on terms commercial banks would not have accepted. Sanjay also directed two campaigns that defined Emergency-period popular memory of the regime more than any policy decision did. The first was **Slum Demolition** — particularly the Turkman Gate demolitions in Old Delhi, where over 300,000 residents of the historic city's working-class neighborhoods were forcibly displaced from their homes to make way for what the Sanjay-affiliated Delhi Development Authority described as urban beautification. Police killed at least twenty-five protesters in the Turkman Gate firing of April 1976; the actual death toll across the wider Delhi demolitions has never been credibly counted. The second was **forced sterilization** — a coercive family-planning campaign that performed an estimated eight million sterilizations between 1976 and 1977, with administrative quotas imposed on government employees that produced widespread coercion (especially of poor men) and at least 1,800 documented deaths from infected post-surgical wounds in poorly equipped rural sterilization camps. The two programs together represented the kind of state behavior that the Indian middle class had assumed was reserved for authoritarian regimes elsewhere.

The Emergency had defenders, then and now. Indira's argument was that the country had been on the edge of breakdown, that JP's call for army and police disobedience was a serious institutional threat, that some democratic functioning had to be suspended temporarily so that the underlying constitutional order could be preserved. Some elements of this argument have force. The pre-Emergency political situation had been genuinely dangerous: industrial strikes had paralyzed major cities, the Naxalite insurgency in Bengal and Bihar was active, university students had been agitating against the government for years, and JP's specific call for police disobedience had no precedent in Indian constitutional history. The Emergency also coincided, by coincidence or by design, with a period of partial economic recovery: inflation came down from 28% in 1974 to single digits in 1975–76, agricultural production was strong, the trains ran on time (a phrase that became famously associated with Emergency apologetics). But the case for the Emergency rests heavily on the policy outcomes of its first six months; the case against it rests on the constitutional damage of its full twenty-one-month duration, the personal political abuses of the Sanjay period, the suppression of civil liberties, and the demonstration that the Indian constitutional system had no built-in safeguards against an executive willing to use Article 352 as broadly as Indira used it.

---

The Emergency ended in a way that surprised everyone. In January 1977, Indira Gandhi announced an election — the **March 1977 General Election** — on the apparent assumption that the country had accepted the Emergency framework, that the opposition leadership was demoralized after eighteen months in detention, and that the Congress would win a working majority. Why she made this assessment is still unclear; the most common interpretation is that her intelligence services had given her overoptimistic reports and that she had genuinely lost touch with public sentiment after months of staged news in censored media. She released the opposition leaders from detention, scheduled the election for late March, and lifted most of the Emergency restrictions in the campaign period.

The opposition leaders, freed from detention but still under restrictions, met in Delhi in January 1977 and formed the **Janata Party** — a four-way merger of the old Congress (O), the Bharatiya Lok Dal (Charan Singh's farmer-based party), the Jana Sangh (the Hindu nationalist precursor party that included Vajpayee and Advani), and the Socialist Party (JP's network). The merger was extraordinary: ideologically, the Janata combined Hindu nationalists, Gandhian socialists, North Indian rural conservatives, and elder Congress dissidents in a single electoral vehicle. The unifying program was anti-Emergency. The campaign was conducted in an unusually compressed period — about ten weeks — with the Janata's senior figures (Desai, JP himself in advisory capacity though not as a candidate, Vajpayee, Charan Singh) campaigning across the Hindi belt and into the south. The Congress entered the campaign with all the institutional advantages of incumbency but without an effective response to the Emergency-period record.

The result was a political earthquake. The Janata Party won 295 of 542 Lok Sabha seats, the Congress was reduced to 153 (its worst result ever), and Indira Gandhi personally lost her own Rae Bareli seat to Raj Narain — the same opponent whose 1975 election petition had triggered the entire chain of events. Sanjay Gandhi lost his Amethi seat. The Congress was wiped out across northern India, retaining seats only in the south and parts of Maharashtra. The Janata's victory was the first defeat of the Indian National Congress in any general election since independence — thirty years of unbroken Congress rule had ended in a single vote.

**Morarji Desai** was sworn in as Prime Minister on March 24, 1977. The Janata government would last twenty-eight months — a complicated period covered in the next chapter — before fracturing along the original ideological lines and producing the conditions for Indira's return. But the basic point about Indian democracy had been made and would be remembered: the electorate had punished the Emergency at the ballot box, peacefully, in a process the deposed government had itself organized. The Indian democratic system had survived its first authoritarian interlude.

---

# Chapter 9 — Indira Returns

The Janata government was a coalition that had defeated the Emergency but had no plan for what came next. Morarji Desai (81 years old when he took office) was Prime Minister; Charan Singh (the Lok Dal leader, an UP farmer-politician with deep agrarian-caste roots) was Deputy Prime Minister and Home Minister; Atal Bihari Vajpayee (the Jana Sangh's most respected figure) was External Affairs Minister. The cabinet's ideological range was vast and the unifying program — anti-Emergency — was a one-time issue that did not generate ongoing legislative direction. Within months, the cabinet was openly feuding. Charan Singh pressed for agricultural-loan write-offs and farmer subsidies. The Jana Sangh wing pressed for Hindu cultural symbols in state programs. The old Congress (O) wing pressed for moderation and continuity. JP, by 1978, was effectively withdrawn from active politics (he died in October 1979). The government continued to function bureaucratically but lost political cohesion across 1978.

The Janata's most consequential single act was the **44th Constitutional Amendment** of 1978 — a careful rewriting of the emergency provisions that the Indira government had abused. The amendment narrowed the constitutional grounds for emergency declaration (replacing "internal disturbance" with "armed rebellion"), required cabinet recommendation in writing for the President to declare emergency, restored full judicial review of fundamental rights during emergencies, made the right to life and personal liberty (Article 21) non-suspendable, and reversed most of the 42nd Amendment's pro-executive provisions. The 44th Amendment was the legal-constitutional response to the Emergency, and it has held: no subsequent Indian government has invoked the Article 352 national emergency provisions, and the procedural safeguards the 44th Amendment introduced have functioned as intended.

But the Janata could not hold its own coalition. In July 1979, Charan Singh withdrew his Lok Dal contingent from the government over Desai's refusal to push for harder action on Sanjay Gandhi's pending criminal cases. Desai resigned. The Congress (R) — Indira's faction, now reconstituted under the new name **Congress (I)** for "Indira" — agreed to support a minority Charan Singh government, then withdrew that support before Charan Singh had survived a single Parliament confidence vote. Charan Singh became the only Prime Minister of India never to have faced the Lok Sabha. Parliament was dissolved. The **1980 General Election** in January 1980 returned Indira to power with 353 of 529 Lok Sabha seats — a clearer majority than she had ever previously won. The Janata's electoral mandate had collapsed in three years.

The Congress's argument in 1980 was simple: the Janata could not govern. The two and a half years of Janata rule had produced unstable cabinets, unimplemented promises, and ineffective administration. Indira returned on the implicit promise that the Congress could at least manage the bureaucracy. The argument was not, in 1980, an argument about whether the Emergency had been justified — that had been settled in 1977 — but about whether the alternative was workable. The voters' verdict in 1980 was: no.

---

Indira's second term ran from January 1980 to her assassination in October 1984. The dominant issue was the Punjab.

The **Punjab crisis** had been building since the 1970s. The Akali Dal — the regional Sikh political party — had pressed since the **Anandpur Sahib Resolution** of 1973 for greater autonomy: a renegotiated federal settlement that would give the Punjab control over its own rivers (water-rights disputes with neighboring Haryana and Rajasthan were central), greater fiscal autonomy, and recognition of Punjabi-language and Sikh-religious symbols in state policy. The demands were politically substantive but not separatist. In response, however, Indira's Congress government had encouraged the rise of **Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale** — a young, charismatic Sikh fundamentalist preacher whom Congress strategists initially saw as a useful instrument for splitting the Akali political base. Bhindranwale and the radical Sikh political-religious organization Damdami Taksal began advocating for Khalistan — an independent Sikh sovereign state — and conducting targeted assassinations of Hindu and Sikh political opponents. By 1982 Bhindranwale had taken up residence inside the **Golden Temple complex at Amritsar** — the holiest site in Sikhism — and was operating as the de facto authority over the temple complex while continuing to direct militant activity from inside it. By 1984 the security situation in the Punjab had deteriorated to multiple political killings per week, with the Indian state having lost effective authority over substantial parts of the province.

**Operation Blue Star** was Indira's response. Between June 1 and 8, 1984, the Indian Army conducted a full-scale assault on the Golden Temple complex to remove Bhindranwale and his armed followers. The operation involved tank fire on the **Akal Takht** — the Sikh political-spiritual seat — major damage to the Golden Temple's surrounding structures, and the deaths of over 500 people including Bhindranwale, around 80 Indian Army soldiers, and (the actual disputed number) several hundred to several thousand civilians who had been inside the complex at the time of the assault, many of them Sikh pilgrims who had no involvement in militant activity. The military objective was achieved: Bhindranwale was dead, the temple complex was retaken. The political consequence was a wound in the Sikh community that has not closed. The Golden Temple was the religious center of every Sikh on Earth, the equivalent in religious terms of a military assault on the Vatican or the Kaaba; the Akal Takht's reconstruction was rejected by the Sikh community for years afterward because the original Indian-government rebuild had been politically tainted. The Sikh political consensus, even among Sikhs who had been horrified by Bhindranwale's actual conduct, hardened against the Congress.

On October 31, 1984, two of Indira Gandhi's Sikh bodyguards — **Beant Singh** and **Satwant Singh** — shot her on her way from her residence at 1 Safdarjung Road to a scheduled interview with the British actor Peter Ustinov at her office. Beant Singh fired three rounds from a service revolver; Satwant Singh emptied a Sten submachine gun magazine. Indira died on the way to the hospital. Beant Singh was killed in the immediate gunfight with other guards; Satwant Singh and a third co-conspirator (Kehar Singh, a civil-service officer) were captured, tried, and hanged in January 1989. The assassination was direct revenge for Operation Blue Star.

What followed was worse. The **1984 anti-Sikh pogrom** in Delhi and several northern Indian cities — November 1–3, 1984 — killed at least 3,000 Sikhs (the official Indian government figure; later inquiries suggested 8,000 or more). The killings were not spontaneous riots; they were organized by Congress politicians and local strongmen, with police complicity. Voter lists were used to identify Sikh households. Petrol and rubber tires (for the burning of victims, a method the killers had used in earlier communal violence) were distributed by trucks bearing Congress political insignia. The Delhi police, under direct central-government authority, were instructed to stand down. The killings stopped on November 3 only when the army was finally deployed to restore order. The political class that had organized the pogrom included Congress figures who would go on to substantial subsequent political careers, including Jagdish Tytler and Sajjan Kumar; the legal accountability process for those killings, eventually producing a conviction of Sajjan Kumar in 2018, took thirty-four years.

The pogrom changed Indian politics in ways that are still working themselves out. The Congress, which under Nehru had been the institutional guardian of secular tolerance, had organized communal mass violence at the center of the Indian state. The fiction of Congress secular credibility never fully recovered. The Sikh community's grievance with the Indian state hardened into a thirty-year alienation that produced a violent Khalistani militancy in the Punjab through the late 1980s, suppressed eventually by a brutal counterinsurgency under the Punjab Police chief K. P. S. Gill. The Hindu-majoritarian critique of the Congress — that the party preached secularism in principle but practiced communalism in pursuit of votes — gained an empirical anchor that the BJP would use across the next thirty years.

---

**Rajiv Gandhi** — Indira's elder son, an Indian Airlines pilot with limited political experience, who had been drawn into politics only after Sanjay Gandhi's death in a small-plane crash in June 1980 — was sworn in as Prime Minister within hours of his mother's death. The Congress called the **December 1984 General Election** less than two months after the assassination and won 414 of 542 Lok Sabha seats — the largest single-party majority in any Indian election before or since, driven entirely by sympathy voting. Rajiv was forty when he took office. He had been described variously as a "computer raja" — meaning to suggest both his interest in technological modernization and his distance from old Congress politics — and he committed his first months to a modernization agenda: telecommunications upgrades that began the conversion from analog landlines to electronic exchanges (the work of his confidant **Sam Pitroda** and the new Centre for Development of Telematics), the **New Education Policy 1986** that expanded the role of the Navodaya Vidyalaya residential schools and shifted curriculum priorities, an end to the import-substitution restrictions on consumer electronics that would later be the cultural foundation of the IT industry.

Then came the **Shah Bano case**. In April 1985, the Supreme Court ruled that Mohammed Ahmed Khan, an Indore lawyer, was required to pay continuing maintenance to his divorced wife Shah Bano under section 125 of the Criminal Procedure Code. The ruling was a routine application of secular Indian criminal law. It became politically charged because the parties were Muslim and because Muslim personal law (the *Sharia*-derived family law that applied to Muslim Indians in matters of marriage, divorce, and inheritance) does not require post-divorce maintenance beyond the *iddat* period (three menstrual cycles). Conservative Muslim political organizations protested that the Supreme Court was effectively imposing secular law on Muslim religious matters. The Rajiv government, in a major political miscalculation, passed the **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act** in May 1986 to legislatively reverse the Supreme Court ruling and restore the conservative interpretation of Muslim personal law. The episode was politically catastrophic. Indian feminists denounced the legislative reversal as the state subordinating women's rights to conservative religious leadership; Hindu nationalist organizations denounced it as an example of Congress "appeasement" of Muslims; secular liberals denounced it as a violation of the principle of equal citizenship. The argument that the Congress practiced two different standards for Muslim and Hindu religious matters became, after Shah Bano, the central rhetorical lever the BJP would use to dismantle Congress secular credibility across the next decade.

The same period brought the **Babri Masjid** controversy to the center of Indian politics. In February 1986, a district court in Faizabad — under a Congress-aligned district administration — ordered the unlocking of a small disputed mosque in Ayodhya (the **Babri Masjid**, traditionally identified as having been built in 1528 by a general of the Mughal emperor Babur). The mosque had been the subject of a property dispute since 1949, when Hindu activists had placed idols of the deity Ram inside the structure overnight and the district magistrate had locked it shut to prevent further communal disturbance. The 1986 unlocking allowed Hindu worship inside the mosque, was politically welcomed by the Hindu nationalist Vishwa Hindu Parishad (VHP), and triggered Muslim political mobilization against the perceived state assistance to Hindu religious claims. Both sides understood that the symbolic stakes were higher than the actual building: the Babri Masjid stood on what Hindu activists claimed was the birthplace of the deity Ram, and the construction of a Ram temple at the site would have civilizational meaning. The VHP began organizing nationwide *Ram Shila Pujan* — brick-consecration ceremonies in which villages contributed bricks for the eventual Ram temple — across 1989. The Babri site was at the center of Indian political contention for the next six years, culminating in the demolition (Chapter 11).

---

The Rajiv government's other defining crisis was the **Indian Peace Keeping Force** intervention in Sri Lanka. The Sri Lankan civil war between the Sinhalese-majority government and the Tamil-minority Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) had been escalating since 1983. The Tamil insurgency had political support from the Tamil-speaking Indian state of Tamil Nadu (where successive state governments had quietly provided sanctuary and training to Tamil militant groups) and direct support from Indian intelligence (RAW). In July 1987 Rajiv signed the **Indo-Sri Lanka Accord** with Sri Lankan President Jayewardene, under which India would deploy a peace-keeping force to the Tamil-majority north and east of Sri Lanka to disarm the LTTE and oversee a political settlement granting Tamil regional autonomy. The deployment turned catastrophic. The LTTE refused to disarm. The IPKF — committed initially as a 6,000-strong peacekeeping force — escalated to over 80,000 troops fighting an insurgency in unfamiliar terrain against the very militants Indian intelligence had previously supported. Approximately 1,200 IPKF soldiers were killed before the force was withdrawn in March 1990. The deployment was a strategic and political failure that defined Rajiv's defense legacy and would, in 1991, return to kill him: an LTTE suicide bomber detonated a vest at a campaign rally near Madras on May 21, 1991, killing Rajiv and fourteen others. He was forty-six.

The **Bofors scandal** — corruption allegations around a 1986 contract under which the Swedish defense firm Bofors AB had supplied 410 howitzer guns to the Indian Army, with kickbacks paid to Indian political intermediaries — broke in Swedish radio reporting in April 1987. The Indian opposition (the V. P. Singh-led faction within the Congress that had been forced out, the Janata-successor parties, the left, and the BJP) made Bofors the centerpiece of the 1989 campaign. The actual evidence against Rajiv was always limited — direct kickbacks to senior Congress figures including possibly Rajiv himself were alleged but never proved in court — but the broader perception of corruption was politically lethal. The 1989 General Election reduced the Congress to 197 seats, costing it the parliamentary majority and producing a National Front coalition government under V. P. Singh.

The **Bhopal disaster** — the December 1984 industrial accident at the Union Carbide India pesticide plant in Bhopal, in which approximately 40 tons of methyl isocyanate gas was released into the air around the plant, killing an estimated 3,000 people in the immediate aftermath and another 15,000 to 25,000 over subsequent decades from injury and exposure — sits across this period as a separate but illustrative event. The disaster was the worst single industrial accident in human history. The legal and political response was a thirty-year sequence of compromises: a 1989 out-of-court settlement under which Union Carbide paid US$470 million (about US$870 per affected person) in exchange for criminal-charge dismissal, a settlement now widely regarded as inadequate; the company's eventual sale to Dow Chemical in 2001 without resolution of the residual environmental contamination at the site; the prolonged Indian inability to extradite the Union Carbide CEO Warren Anderson to face manslaughter charges before his death in 2014. Bhopal illustrated everything about the period — a developing-country industrial economy operating dangerous facilities without effective safety regulation; a state apparatus unable to negotiate adequate compensation against transnational corporate resources; a legal system unable to assign accountability for catastrophic damage. The site was finally remediated in the late 2010s. Survivors are still receiving partial compensation in 2026.

# Chapter 10 — Liberalization

The 1989 General Election produced a hung Parliament. V. P. Singh — the former Congress finance minister who had broken with Rajiv over Bofors, joined the new Janata Dal, and unified the anti-Congress opposition outside the Hindu nationalist BJP — became Prime Minister at the head of a National Front coalition with outside support from the BJP on one side and the Left on the other. The arithmetic was unstable. The coalition would last eleven months.

Two issues defined V. P. Singh's brief premiership. The first was the **Mandal Commission report**. The Mandal Commission had been set up in 1979 under the Janata government to study the conditions of India's **Other Backward Classes (OBCs)** — the broad category of mostly agricultural, mostly low-status (though not Dalit) Hindu castes that had historically been excluded from elite professions and government employment but had not been covered by the Scheduled Caste/Scheduled Tribe reservations system. The commission, chaired by B. P. Mandal, had reported in 1980 recommending that 27% of central-government jobs be reserved for OBCs in addition to the existing 22.5% reserved for SCs and STs — bringing total reservations to just under 50%. The Indira and Rajiv governments had shelved the report. V. P. Singh's government announced in August 1990 that it would implement the Mandal recommendations.

The political effects were enormous. The OBC vote — perhaps 40% of the total Indian electorate — had not been a coherent political bloc; the Mandal implementation made it one. The new OBC political parties that emerged across the 1990s — the Samajwadi Party in UP, the Rashtriya Janata Dal in Bihar, the Janata Dal (Secular) in Karnataka, the DMK in Tamil Nadu — would dominate state-level politics in the Hindi belt and substantially in the south for the next two decades. The Congress lost its OBC vote across this period, and the BJP, which initially opposed the Mandal report on the grounds that it would split the Hindu vote, eventually adapted by building cross-caste Hindu coalitions of its own. The Indian political landscape that resulted — caste-based regional parties controlling the Hindi-belt states, the Congress confined increasingly to coastal and southern states, the BJP organizing Hindu cross-caste coalitions at the national level — was set up directly by the 1990 Mandal decision.

The middle-class and upper-caste reaction was immediate and intense. Anti-Mandal protests broke out across northern Indian universities; the most striking single image was that of Rajiv Goswami, a Delhi University student who set himself on fire in protest on September 19, 1990, beginning a wave of self-immolations that killed at least 159 people across the autumn of 1990. The protests revealed the depth of upper-caste anxiety about reservations — a politics that the BJP would later mobilize but that V. P. Singh's coalition could not contain.

The second defining issue was the BJP's **Ram Janmabhoomi movement**. The VHP, in September 1990, organized a **Rath Yatra** — a chariot procession across northern India from Somnath in Gujarat to Ayodhya in UP — led by the BJP president **L. K. Advani**, calling for the construction of a Ram temple at the disputed Ayodhya site. The Rath Yatra was carefully theatrical: Advani traveled in a converted Toyota truck disguised as a chariot, gave speeches in towns along the route, and was accompanied by VHP, RSS, and BJP cadres. Communal violence broke out at multiple stops along the route. In Bihar, the state government under Lalu Prasad Yadav arrested Advani on October 23, 1990. The BJP withdrew its support from V. P. Singh's government over the arrest. The coalition collapsed. Chandra Shekhar, a former socialist who broke from the Janata Dal, served as Prime Minister for four months at the head of a rump government with Congress outside support, before the Congress also withdrew support and Parliament was dissolved in March 1991.

---

The **1991 General Election** was conducted in two phases — May 20 and (after Rajiv Gandhi's assassination by LTTE suicide bomber on May 21 in the middle of campaigning) June 12 and 15 — and returned the Congress as the largest single party with 232 of 545 Lok Sabha seats. **P. V. Narasimha Rao** — a Telugu Brahmin from Andhra Pradesh, former external affairs minister and home minister under both Indira and Rajiv, considered by political observers to be an ineffectual elder statesman — was sworn in as Prime Minister on June 21, 1991. He was seventy years old. He had no organic base in the Congress family or in any regional faction. He had been chosen because he was acceptable to everyone and threatening to no one. He would, like Indira before him, surprise the people who chose him.

Rao inherited a financial crisis. The Indian rupee was overvalued; the trade deficit was widening; the Gulf War had spiked oil prices and disrupted remittance flows from Indian workers in the Persian Gulf; the Soviet Union (India's largest trading partner under preferential bilateral arrangements) was collapsing, which would shortly remove a major component of the Indian export and import structure. Foreign exchange reserves had fallen to about three weeks of import cover by mid-1991. The Reserve Bank of India had been forced, the previous month, to physically airlift 47 tons of gold reserves to London to be pledged as collateral for emergency Bank of England and IMF lending. The Indian economy was about to default.

Rao's first major appointment was the **Economic liberalization begins in 1991** under the new Finance Minister **Manmohan Singh** — an Oxford-trained economist with thirty years of senior bureaucratic experience (RBI Governor, Planning Commission deputy chairman) but no political experience. The combination of Rao's political authority and Manmohan's technical credibility was, in retrospect, the most important Indian political partnership since Nehru-Patel. The July 1991 budget — presented by Manmohan to Parliament on July 24 — opened the Indian economy in ways the country had not seen since independence:

- The rupee was devalued by 24% over the first two weeks of the new government.
- The complex industrial licensing system — the so-called **License Raj** under which any new factory or capacity expansion required prior government permission, a system that had created decades of administrative obstruction and corruption — was abolished except in a small set of specifically restricted industries.
- Foreign direct investment limits were raised from 40% to 51% in most industries, and the Foreign Investment Promotion Board was created to fast-track approvals.
- Import licensing was substantially dismantled; import tariffs began a fifteen-year decline from peaks of over 200% to single digits.
- Capital-market reforms allowed foreign institutional investors to buy Indian listed equities directly.
- The public-sector monopoly in core industries (steel, telecommunications, banking) was retained in name but eroded in practice through partial-privatization and competition with new private entrants.

The reforms were politically possible because they had no domestic political champions and because the alternative — outright default and IMF-mandated structural adjustment — was worse than anything the reforms imposed. Manmohan was a technical figure presenting technical solutions to a fiscal crisis. The Congress's left wing, led by Arjun Singh, opposed the reforms but had no alternative. The BJP, the Janata-successor parties, and the Left parties opposed individual measures but did not have the parliamentary numbers to block them. The Indian capitalist class, which had operated under the License Raj as a small protected oligopoly, was initially divided — Bombay industrial families like the Bajaj and Birla groups had built their positions under the old regime and feared open competition, while a new generation of entrepreneurs (Sunil Mittal at Bharti, Dhirubhai Ambani at Reliance, the founders of Infosys and Wipro at Bangalore) welcomed the opening. The IT sector, in particular, was about to discover that the new framework was an export-led boom waiting to happen.

The structural results of the 1991 reforms unfolded over the next thirty years. India's GDP growth rate accelerated from the 3.5% Hindu-rate average of the prior decades to 6–7% for the 1990s and to peaks of 8–9% in the 2003–10 period. Foreign exchange reserves recovered from three weeks of cover to over $600 billion by the mid-2020s. The Indian middle class — defined by economists variously, but at the broadest definition adding several hundred million people between 1991 and 2025 — was created by the opening of consumer markets. Poverty rates fell substantially across this period, though inequality also widened. The Indian economy of 2026 — fifth-largest in the world by nominal GDP, third-largest by purchasing-power parity — was set up by what Manmohan Singh did in 1991–92. The political class never gave him formal credit; the reforms were defended after the fact by every subsequent Indian government regardless of party.

---

The Rao government was politically defined, however, less by the economic reforms than by what happened on December 6, 1992. The **Babri Masjid demolished** action that day was the culmination of the VHP–BJP campaign that had been organizing since the 1989 Ram Shila Pujan. A rally of approximately 150,000 *kar sevaks* (volunteers) had gathered at Ayodhya, ostensibly for a symbolic *kar seva* (volunteer service) at the disputed site. The BJP leadership (Advani, Murli Manohar Joshi, Uma Bharti) was present. The Uttar Pradesh state government — then under BJP control, with **Kalyan Singh** as Chief Minister — had given the Supreme Court a sworn assurance that the rally would not damage the structure. The central Rao government, which had legal authority to deploy central paramilitary forces in the state to protect the structure, had chosen not to do so on the grounds that the state government's assurance should be respected.

The kar sevaks began climbing the mosque structure at midday. The senior BJP leaders, present on the rally platform a few hundred yards from the structure, did not order the crowd to stop. By late afternoon the three domes of the 464-year-old mosque had been pulled down brick by brick. The structure was reduced to rubble. The BJP leaders' speeches and gestures during the demolition were televised. **L. K. Advani** later said he wept when the demolition began. **Atal Bihari Vajpayee**, who was not present at Ayodhya that day, expressed regret. **Uma Bharti** and others were photographed celebrating on the platform. The Liberhan Commission, established later by Rao to investigate the demolition, took seventeen years to report (it submitted its findings in 2009) and concluded that the demolition had been planned and that the BJP and VHP leaders bore primary responsibility. None of the criminal cases produced convictions of senior BJP leaders; the principal accused were acquitted in September 2020 by a special CBI court that ruled the demolition had been spontaneous despite the documentary record.

The demolition triggered the worst Hindu-Muslim communal violence since Partition. In Bombay (Mumbai by 1995), riots in December 1992 and January 1993 killed at least 900 people — predominantly Muslim, in attacks that the Srikrishna Commission later documented as substantially organized by the Shiv Sena political party in coordination with local police. The **1993 Bombay bombings** of March 12, 1993 — coordinated explosions at twelve locations across Bombay including the Stock Exchange, the Air India building, and major hotels, killing 257 people — were carried out by an organized-crime network (Dawood Ibrahim's D-Company) operating from Karachi with the cooperation of Pakistani intelligence in retaliation for the Bombay riots. The bombings were the first international terror attack of major scale against Indian civilian targets and the first systematic use of Pakistani-territory-based organized crime as a vector for political violence — a pattern that would define Indo-Pakistani relations for the next two decades.

---

The Rao government completed its full five-year term (1991–96) — the first non-Nehru-Gandhi government in Indian history to do so — and then lost the 1996 General Election. The Congress had been damaged by the Babri demolition (which Rao had failed to prevent), by the Bombay riots and bombings (which had taken place under partial Congress administrative responsibility), by the Bofors-era corruption legacy, and by the general perception that the economic reforms had benefited urban elites while leaving rural India behind. The party fell to 140 seats; the BJP emerged as the largest single party with 161 seats but lacked the alliance partners to form a government. The next chapter is the coalition decade and the BJP's slow rise to a working national majority.

---

# Chapter 11 — Coalition Decade and NDA

The 1996 to 1998 period was the chaotic high-water mark of coalition politics. Between May 1996 and October 1999, India had four Prime Ministers — Vajpayee (briefly), H. D. Deve Gowda, Inder Kumar Gujral, and Vajpayee again — and three general elections. The institutional cause was that no single party had the seats to form a government; the political cause was that the BJP was building toward an electoral majority but had not yet assembled the alliance partners to translate plurality into governing coalition. The BJP's eventual breakthrough came through the **National Democratic Alliance (NDA)** — a formal coalition organized by Vajpayee in 1998, including the BJP plus a long list of regional parties (the AIADMK in Tamil Nadu, the Trinamool Congress in West Bengal, the Telugu Desam Party in Andhra, the Akali Dal in Punjab, the Shiv Sena in Maharashtra) that gave the BJP working majorities in the 1998 and 1999 elections. The NDA's distinctive innovation was that the BJP set aside its most ideologically charged issues — the Ram temple, the abrogation of Article 370 in Kashmir, the Uniform Civil Code — in exchange for governing-coalition support from regional parties whose voters were not Hindu nationalist. The NDA's "common minimum program" was a moderate economic-reform-and-good-governance agenda that the regional parties could accept and that allowed the BJP to learn the institutional habits of national government.

The Vajpayee government that took office in October 1999 — and held until May 2004 — was the BJP's first sustained experience of central executive authority. **Atal Bihari Vajpayee** (1924–2018), the Prime Minister, was an unusual figure in his own party: a poet, a multi-decade parliamentarian (he had first been elected to the Lok Sabha in 1957), a public figure whose actual ideological commitments were softer than his party affiliation suggested. His political success rested on his ability to project moderate consensus while presiding over an explicitly Hindu nationalist organizational machine. The decade of his ascendancy — 1996 to 2004 — was when the BJP became a normal Indian political party.

The defining strategic event of the Vajpayee period was the **Pokhran-II nuclear tests** of May 11 and 13, 1998. India conducted five underground nuclear tests at the Pokhran range in Rajasthan — the first detonations since the 1974 Smiling Buddha test — including one claimed thermonuclear device (the actual yield of which has been disputed by external technical assessments, with most foreign analysts concluding the yield was below the Indian government's claimed 45 kilotons). The tests were conducted in tight secrecy: American satellite surveillance had been deliberately deceived by Indian operations security; the United States learned of the tests only when the underground detonations were detected by global seismographic networks; the immediate international response was sweeping sanctions from the United States, Japan, and Germany. India's strategic rationale was both regional (Pakistan was known to be near nuclear-weapons capability and would test its own devices two weeks later, on May 28, 1998 at Chagai Hills in Baluchistan) and historical (the 1974 test had been a single-device demonstration that did not produce reliable weapons capability; the 1998 tests aimed at producing a deployable nuclear arsenal). The Indo-Pakistani strategic balance became formally nuclear in May 1998. The international sanctions imposed on India were largely lifted within three years as the Bush administration repositioned India as a strategic partner against China.

The **Kargil War** of May–July 1999 was the first armed Indo-Pakistani confrontation under nuclear conditions and a model of what subsequent Indo-Pakistani military crises would look like. Pakistani Northern Light Infantry troops, disguised as Kashmiri irregular insurgents (the "Kargil intruders" framing was maintained by the Pakistani government for years afterward despite eventual acknowledgment of regular Pakistani Army involvement), infiltrated across the Line of Control in the high-altitude Kargil district of Jammu and Kashmir and occupied unmanned Indian military posts at altitudes of 14,000–18,000 feet. India responded with the largest Indian Army mobilization since 1971 and with combat air operations by the Indian Air Force. The Indian counter-offensive, conducted in extraordinarily difficult mountain conditions over ten weeks, retook the captured peaks at substantial cost (527 Indian Army personnel killed in action). The international response — particularly American pressure on Pakistan, under the Clinton administration's Pakistan-specific channels — pushed Pakistan to withdraw. The Pakistani military's domestic political position was substantially damaged by the operation's failure; General Pervez Musharraf, the principal Pakistani officer behind the Kargil plan, would use that domestic damage to launch a coup against the elected Pakistani civilian government in October 1999. India's response to Kargil — measured retaliation contained within the operational area, no escalation across the international border — set the pattern for how nuclear-era Indo-Pakistani crises would be managed.

The Vajpayee government also produced the most ambitious infrastructure-investment program of any post-independence Indian government to that point. The **Golden Quadrilateral** — a 5,846-kilometer four-lane (later six-lane) national highway network connecting Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, and Kolkata, with feeder spurs to most of the major Indian cities — was conceived and substantially built between 1999 and 2006. The **Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana** (PMGSY), the rural roads program launched in 2000, paved or graded over 750,000 kilometers of rural roads across the next twenty-five years and substantially closed the rural-urban physical infrastructure gap. The combined road program was the largest single physical-infrastructure investment of the Indian state since the colonial-era railways. Its electoral consequences were direct: rural voters reached by the new roads were more likely to access urban markets, hospitals, schools, and jobs, and were more likely to vote for the party that built the roads.

The Vajpayee government also presided over the **IT boom and Bangalore rises** that defined Indian economic perception abroad. The 1990s opening had created the regulatory space; the late-1990s Y2K crisis — the global panic over the 2000 calendar rollover and the need to remediate decades of two-digit-year computer code — gave Indian software services firms (Tata Consultancy Services, Infosys, Wipro, HCL Technologies) their first large-scale Western contracts. Bangalore (Bengaluru, by then increasingly using its Kannada name), Hyderabad, Pune, and Chennai became the centers of an export-oriented software-services industry that grew from approximately $5 billion in 2000 to $150 billion by 2020. The IT industry transformed the Indian middle class: educated young Indians could enter a global economy directly from their hometowns at wages that were Western-comparable in nominal terms (and far higher in purchasing-power terms within India). The "back office of the world" framing was real economically and shaped Indian public-policy debate across the 2000s.

The Vajpayee government lost the 2004 General Election. The "**India Shining**" campaign — built around the economic-reform success — failed to translate to the rural voter, who had not experienced the IT boom directly and who had been hit hard by the 2002 Gujarat communal violence (Chapter below). The Congress under the new leadership of **Sonia Gandhi** — Rajiv's Italian-born widow, who had taken over the Congress in 1998 after a long political withdrawal — won 145 seats, the BJP took 138, and the **United Progressive Alliance** (UPA) — a Congress-led coalition with support from the Left parties and an array of regional partners — assembled the working majority. Sonia Gandhi declined the prime-ministership in the face of organized BJP campaigning around her foreign birth and named **Manmohan Singh** as Prime Minister instead. The architect of the 1991 reforms became Prime Minister in his own right at age seventy-two.

---

The 2002 Gujarat communal violence requires its own paragraph here because of its later consequences. On February 27, 2002, a train carriage at Godhra station in Gujarat caught fire, killing 59 Hindu pilgrims returning from Ayodhya. The cause of the fire was disputed at the time and remains contested: a state inquiry concluded it was deliberately set by Muslim mob action; an independent inquiry concluded the cause was likely accidental. Regardless of cause, the response was anti-Muslim communal violence across Gujarat over the next three months that killed at least 1,000 people (the Indian government figure; later inquiries suggest 2,000 or more), the vast majority Muslim, with attacks documented in over a dozen Gujarat cities and with credible evidence of police and state-administration complicity. The Gujarat Chief Minister at the time was **Narendra Modi**, in his first stint as Chief Minister (he had been appointed in October 2001 to that position by the BJP). Modi's role in either organizing, condoning, or failing to suppress the violence was the subject of multiple inquiries across the next decade and is still actively contested. A Special Investigation Team appointed by the Supreme Court in 2008 concluded in 2012 that there was no prosecutable evidence against Modi personally for the violence. The US government revoked Modi's visa in 2005 over the 2002 violence; the visa was restored in 2014 when he became Prime Minister. The 2002 violence shaped Modi's political identity for fifteen years — for his critics, indelibly tying him to Hindu nationalist communal violence; for his supporters, identifying him as a strong leader who had imposed order on a chaotic state and as a figure the Indian left would attempt to discredit no matter what. The Indian Supreme Court closed the principal pending case against Modi in 2022. The 2002 violence is now legally inert but politically active.

# Chapter 12 — Manmohan's UPA

The UPA's first term (May 2004 to May 2009) was probably the most successful Indian government of the post-1991 period. The combination of Sonia Gandhi as party leader (with effective coordination authority over the Congress organizational machinery and the coalition partners) and Manmohan Singh as Prime Minister (with technical competence and personal probity unmatched by any post-Nehru predecessor) produced a remarkably coherent governing program.

The defining policy of UPA-1 was the **Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act** (MGNREGA), passed in 2005 and operational from 2006. The program legally guaranteed up to 100 days of paid manual labor per year per rural household, at the agricultural minimum wage, on locally proposed public-works projects. The scheme was the largest workfare program in human history by enrollment (roughly 250 million workers registered at peak; 50–100 million working in any given year), the largest such program by spending (around 0.5% of GDP at peak), and a substantial achievement in rural welfare guarantee. The economic-development debate around MGNREGA has been continuous since: critics argue it has reduced agricultural productivity by pulling labor out of farms and has crowded out productive private investment; defenders argue it has provided rural consumption floor in drought years, has reduced distress migration, and has built (often imperfectly) durable rural infrastructure (canal cleaning, watershed regeneration, village road maintenance). The program is, in 2026, still operating; no government has dared dismantle it.

The second defining policy was the **Right to Information Act** of 2005 — a sweeping freedom-of-information law that required all government bodies at central, state, and local levels to respond to citizen information requests within 30 days, with limited exemptions for national security and personal privacy. The RTI Act became a foundational instrument of Indian civil society activism, citizen accountability, and journalism. The volume of RTI requests filed annually grew from a few thousand in 2006 to over six million by the mid-2010s, and the law substantially changed the operating habits of the Indian bureaucracy by introducing systematic external scrutiny of administrative decision-making. The cost was real: RTI activists became targets of organized political and bureaucratic retaliation, with at least 100 RTI activists killed in the decade after the Act's passage. The Act's effectiveness was eroded in later years by court rulings and government amendments that narrowed its scope. But in 2005 it was a major piece of democratic deepening.

The third was the **US nuclear deal** — the **Indo-US Civil Nuclear Agreement** of October 2008 — under which the United States, after sustained negotiation across Manmohan's first term, agreed to lift its post-1974 sanctions on nuclear-related trade with India and to support India's exception from the Nuclear Suppliers Group export-control regime. The deal acknowledged India's existing nuclear-weapons status outside the Non-Proliferation Treaty framework and gave India access to the international civilian nuclear market while not requiring India to sign the NPT or surrender any weapons capability. The agreement was a major diplomatic shift: the United States, under Bush and then Obama administrations, was effectively choosing India over Pakistan as its primary South Asian strategic partner, and Indian-American relations entered a phase of substantive convergence that would shape both countries' foreign-policy positioning for the next two decades. The agreement was nearly fatal to UPA-1 domestically — the Left parties, which had been providing outside support to the coalition since 2004, withdrew over the deal in July 2008, and the Manmohan government survived a confidence motion only by a few votes (and with significant evidence of cash-for-votes inducement, which later produced criminal investigations of multiple parliamentarians). The deal was nonetheless ratified.

The **Mumbai terror attacks** of November 26–29, 2008 — the **26/11** attacks, the most deadly single terror operation against Indian civilians in decades — were the worst single security failure of the Manmohan period. Ten Lashkar-e-Taiba terrorists infiltrated by sea from Karachi to the Mumbai waterfront on the evening of November 26 and conducted coordinated attacks at multiple targets: the Chhatrapati Shivaji Terminus railway station, the Taj Mahal Palace Hotel, the Trident-Oberoi Hotel, the Leopold Café, the Cama Hospital, and the Nariman House Jewish community center. The siege lasted 60 hours. 166 people were killed (including six Americans and several Israeli, British, and Canadian nationals); the only captured terrorist, Ajmal Kasab, was tried in India, convicted, and executed in 2012. The Pakistani intelligence service's coordination of the attacks was extensively documented by the captured terrorist's own statements, by intercepted Lashkar-Pakistan handler communications, and by external intelligence-service intelligence sharing. India's response was diplomatic rather than military — the Manmohan government chose not to retaliate militarily under the assessment that Indo-Pakistani nuclear deterrence made conventional escalation too dangerous, a calculation that the BJP opposition criticized at the time and that would become a point of comparison with Modi's later Balakot strikes (Chapter 13). The 26/11 attacks crystallized Indian public sentiment around Pakistani-sponsored terrorism and reduced public tolerance for the Manmohan-era policy of dialogue with Pakistan.

UPA-1 won re-election in May 2009 with an even stronger mandate — the Congress alone took 206 seats, the UPA coalition reached 262, and Manmohan was sworn in for a second term. The 2008 financial crisis had been navigated with relative success (Indian growth slowed from 9% to 5% but did not contract, and the public-sector banking system absorbed the global shock through partial state recapitalization rather than systemic failure). The Indian economy was growing at over 8% annually. Per capita income had doubled in the eight years since the IT-driven acceleration began. The political class read the 2009 result as endorsement of the UPA model.

---

UPA-2 (May 2009 to May 2014) is the period during which the Manmohan settlement collapsed. The collapse had three drivers: corruption, economic deceleration, and the rise of organized anti-corruption mobilization that the Congress could not absorb.

The **2G spectrum scandal** broke in November 2010, when the Comptroller and Auditor General reported that the Telecommunications Minister A. Raja (a DMK politician in the UPA coalition) had allocated 2G mobile-telephony spectrum licenses in 2008 at substantially below-market prices, with the alleged loss to the exchequer being approximately Rs 1.76 lakh crore (about $39 billion at then-exchange rates). The number was politically devastating. The scale was unimaginable. The allocations had been concentrated among a small group of telecom firms, most of whom resold their licenses at multi-billion-dollar profits within months. The Supreme Court in 2012 cancelled all 122 licenses allocated under the 2008 process. Raja was tried, eventually acquitted in 2017 by a special court that found insufficient prosecutable evidence (a verdict the Congress later used to claim vindication and that the BJP described as judicial failure), but the political damage to UPA-2 was permanent.

The **Commonwealth Games scandal** broke in October 2010 around the New Delhi Commonwealth Games of that year — multiple investigations documented contract irregularities, cost overruns, and quality failures across the games' infrastructure. The Games Organising Committee chairman Suresh Kalmadi was arrested in 2011 and tried over several years; the case was eventually closed in 2024 without conviction. The political impact again preceded the legal outcome by many years: the Games had been a high-visibility national event held at a moment when the Congress was claiming managerial competence, and the corruption findings undermined that claim.

The **Coalgate scandal** of 2012 documented similar allocation-pricing issues in the assignment of coal-mining blocks to private firms during 2005–11, with CAG-estimated losses ranging from $33 billion to $200 billion depending on methodology. The Supreme Court in 2014 cancelled all 204 coal-block allocations made under the contested process. Multiple senior Congress figures (though notably not Manmohan Singh personally) were charged in associated cases; the legal proceedings have continued for over a decade.

The **Anna Hazare** anti-corruption movement of 2011–12 was the political vehicle that translated the scandals into electoral consequence. Hazare — a 73-year-old former Indian Army soldier turned Gandhian rural activist from Maharashtra, with a long history of local good-governance campaigns — began a hunger strike in April 2011 at Delhi's Jantar Mantar demanding the passage of a strong **Lokpal Bill** that would create an independent anti-corruption ombudsman with prosecutorial powers over the central executive. The movement attracted enormous middle-class urban support, particularly through social media and through television-news coverage that gave Hazare round-the-clock attention. A second Hazare fast in August 2011 at the Ramlila Maidan drew sustained crowds. The Congress government's responses — alternately conciliatory and dismissive — failed to manage the movement's political momentum. The Lokpal Bill was eventually passed in 2013 in a substantially weakened form that satisfied few activists. But the political consequences of the Hazare movement were larger than the legislation. The movement produced two new political vehicles: **Arvind Kejriwal**, who had been Hazare's chief organizational deputy, broke with Hazare in 2012 and founded the **Aam Aadmi Party** (AAP) — a Delhi-based anti-corruption populist party that would win the Delhi state election in 2013 and 2015 and would become a substantial regional force; and the **Bharatiya Janata Party** under its new leadership, which adopted the anti-corruption framing as its own and positioned itself as the natural alternative to a discredited UPA.

The economy was also slowing. Indian GDP growth fell from 9% in 2010 to 4.7% in 2013. The rupee fell sharply against the dollar in mid-2013, hitting historical lows. The deceleration combined supply-side factors (deferred infrastructure investment, regulatory uncertainty around the scandals' fallout) with global factors (the slowdown in Chinese demand for Indian commodity exports, the US Federal Reserve's signaling of monetary tightening that triggered emerging-market capital outflows). Inflation remained stubbornly above 10%, putting pressure on real wages. The Indian middle class — the constituency that had benefited most from the 1991–2010 growth boom — was experiencing for the first time the sense that the high-growth period might be over.

The BJP organized for 2014. **Narendra Modi** had been re-elected Chief Minister of Gujarat three times (2002, 2007, 2012) on a development-focused political platform that had cultivated a national-level reputation as an effective administrator. He had also rebuilt his international position, partly through systematic outreach to Indian-American business networks and to Hong Kong–Singapore–London Indian diaspora communities. In September 2013, the BJP formally named Modi as its prime-ministerial candidate for the upcoming general election — over the resistance of senior party figure L. K. Advani, who had assumed the candidacy would go to him. The campaign that followed was the most expensive and most data-intensive Indian election campaign to that point: heavy use of holographic 3D projections that allowed Modi to "appear" simultaneously at multiple rallies, sophisticated micro-targeted social-media outreach, sustained branding around the "Gujarat model" of economic development, and a personal cult of Modi as a decisive alternative to the perceived drift of the Manmohan years. The Congress was unable to articulate a coherent response. Rahul Gandhi, Sonia's son and the heir apparent, ran a hesitant campaign that did not address the corruption charges directly.

The **2014 General Election** result was the first single-party Lok Sabha majority since 1984. The BJP took 282 seats — its first absolute majority in any general election. The Congress fell to 44 seats — its worst result ever and the lowest seat-count of any major Indian party in a general election. The NDA total was 336 seats out of 543. **Narendra Modi becomes PM** on May 26, 2014. The Manmohan settlement had ended. The next decade was the BJP's.

# Chapter 13 — Modi I

Modi's first term (2014–2019) was the most ambitious legislative and administrative period of any Indian government since Nehru's first decade. Whether the ambitions translated into commensurate outcomes is, eight to ten years on, a contested judgment. The most consistent pattern is that Modi's first term consisted of large, dramatic interventions whose actual results often fell short of the framings.

The most dramatic single act was the **demonetization of banknotes** on November 8, 2016. Modi addressed the nation at 8:15 PM on a recorded television broadcast and announced that, effective midnight that same evening, the 500-rupee and 1,000-rupee notes — comprising approximately 86% of the cash currency in circulation by value — were no longer legal tender. The notes had to be deposited at banks for replacement with new notes (a redesigned 500-rupee note and a brand-new 2,000-rupee denomination) within fifty days. The stated rationale was the elimination of "black money" — undeclared cash holdings — and the disruption of counterfeit-currency networks (some allegedly Pakistan-sourced) and terrorism financing.

The implementation was chaotic. The Reserve Bank of India had not stockpiled adequate replacement notes; ATMs across the country recalibrated for the new currency at variable rates; rural and small-town banks were overwhelmed by depositor demand; cash-economy small businesses and informal-sector workers (the substantial majority of Indian economic activity, including most agricultural labor and most retail) lost their working capital overnight. The estimated economic cost of the disruption was 1–2 percentage points of GDP growth in 2017. The stated benefits were not realized: the RBI's eventual count, in 2018, showed that 99.3% of the demonetized notes had been redeposited at banks, meaning that virtually no black money had been destroyed and that the principal hoarders had successfully laundered their holdings through proxy depositors or through cash-to-cash conversion networks. Counter-arguments — that demonetization had increased digital payments adoption (which is documented: UPI digital-payments volume grew from negligible levels in 2016 to over 10 billion monthly transactions by 2023), that it had broadened the tax base (also partially documented), and that the political signal of decisive action against the wealthy had electoral benefit — are real but harder to weigh against the immediate disruption costs.

The **Goods and Services Tax (GST)**, implemented July 1, 2017, was the second major intervention. The GST replaced a long-accumulated tangle of central excise duties, state value-added taxes, service taxes, and a dozen smaller levies with a single nationally-harmonized indirect-tax system across all Indian states. The reform had been in legislative development since the Vajpayee period and required a constitutional amendment to allocate taxation authority between the Union and the states; both the Vajpayee and Manmohan governments had failed to assemble the political consensus required. The Modi government negotiated the deal with state governments by guaranteeing revenue protection (with the Centre making up any state-level shortfalls for the first five years) and by setting up the GST Council as a federal cooperative body where state finance ministers vote on rate changes. The implementation phase was again chaotic — small businesses struggled with compliance requirements, the GST Network IT infrastructure had bottlenecks, multiple rate tiers complicated reporting — but the long-term effect was a substantial simplification of the Indian indirect-tax system and the removal of internal trade barriers between Indian states.

The third broad initiative was the **Swachh Bharat Abhiyan** (Clean India Mission) — a public-sanitation program launched in October 2014 with the stated goal of constructing toilets for every Indian household by 2019 to end open defecation. The program built approximately 110 million toilets at central and state expense across five years. The construction was real (though the quality and the sustained-use rate of the built toilets is contested — multiple later assessments found that many constructed toilets were not used, that water-supply provisions for the toilets were inadequate, and that the behavioral-change campaign had only partial reach). The political and symbolic value of the program — the Prime Minister of India personally identifying with the dignity of poor Indians' sanitation — was substantial regardless of the implementation gaps. The Swachh Bharat framing also extended to a broader urban-cleanliness campaign that produced visibly different urban environments in BJP-governed states across the late 2010s.

The fourth was the **Jan Dhan Yojana** financial-inclusion program of 2014, which opened bank accounts for over 500 million previously unbanked Indians by 2020, and the related **Aadhaar** biometric identity system — which had been launched under the Manmohan government in 2010 but which the Modi government dramatically expanded and operationalized for delivering government benefits. The combination of Jan Dhan + Aadhaar + mobile-phone-number registration (the so-called **JAM trinity**) created the digital-identity and digital-payments infrastructure for the next phase of Indian state-citizen interaction. Welfare payments, agricultural subsidies, COVID relief payments, and pension transfers all migrated to the new infrastructure across the late 2010s. The Aadhaar system has been controversial — civil-liberties critics have raised concerns about state surveillance and database security, and the Supreme Court in 2018 narrowed the permissible uses of Aadhaar verification — but the basic infrastructure became a permanent fixture of the Indian state.

---

The defense and foreign-policy story of Modi I was structured around two episodes with Pakistan and a broader strategic reorientation.

The **Uri attack** of September 18, 2016 — a militant attack on an Indian Army camp at Uri in Jammu and Kashmir that killed 19 Indian soldiers — produced India's first formally acknowledged cross-border "surgical strike" operation. Indian Special Forces units crossed the Line of Control on the night of September 28–29, 2016 and attacked alleged militant launch pads inside Pakistan-administered Kashmir. The operation's actual effects — whether it destroyed significant militant infrastructure, how many militants were killed — has been contested by Pakistani denials and by external analysts who have questioned the Indian government's specific claims. But the political message — that India would now respond to Pakistan-based terror attacks with explicit retaliation rather than the Manmohan-era policy of strategic restraint — was the point.

The **Pulwama–Balakot** crisis of February 2019 was the larger test. On February 14, 2019, a Jaish-e-Mohammed suicide bomber detonated an explosive-laden vehicle alongside a Central Reserve Police Force convoy at Pulwama in Kashmir, killing 40 paramilitary personnel. India responded on February 26 with **Indian Air Force airstrikes at Balakot**, a Jaish-e-Mohammed training facility located inside Pakistan's Khyber-Pakhtunkhwa province — the first Indian air strikes inside Pakistani territory since 1971. Pakistan retaliated with attempted strikes the next day inside Indian-administered Kashmir; in the resulting aerial engagement, an Indian MiG-21 was shot down over Pakistan and its pilot Abhinandan Varthaman was captured (and released two days later after diplomatic pressure). The actual battle-damage assessment of the Balakot strikes has been contested — Indian claims of substantial militant deaths were not supported by external satellite imagery analysis — but the political message was again the point: India would strike inside Pakistani territory in response to terror attacks, and the Indo-Pakistani strategic equation now included formal Indian air operations beyond the Line of Control. The Balakot strikes were also conducted ten weeks before the 2019 General Election; whatever their military effect, the political effect on Indian public sentiment was significant.

The broader strategic reorientation was toward a closer Indian alignment with the United States, Japan, and Australia — the **Quadrilateral Security Dialogue** (Quad), which had originated under Vajpayee and Manmohan but had been quiescent through the early 2010s. The Modi government revived the Quad in 2017, expanded annual Indo-American and Indo-Japanese defense exercises substantially, and signed major U.S. defense-cooperation agreements (LEMOA, COMCASA, BECA) that integrated Indian military capabilities with American logistics, communications, and intelligence networks. The strategic logic was the rising threat from China — particularly after the 2017 Doklam standoff (a 73-day Indo-Chinese military standoff at a tri-junction with Bhutan in the eastern Himalayas, ended by mutual disengagement) — and the recognition that India's southeastern and northern strategic position required American security underwriting that Indian non-alignment had previously precluded.

---

The 2019 General Election was a referendum on Modi's first term, and the result was a stronger BJP majority than 2014. The BJP took 303 seats (the NDA reached 353); the Congress recovered marginally to 52 seats. Modi was sworn in for a second term with an even stronger mandate and the political authority to pursue the BJP's core ideological program, which the coalition constraints of NDA-1 had partially blunted. The next chapter is what he did with that authority.

# Chapter 14 — Modi II

Modi II (2019–2024) was the period during which the BJP delivered its core Hindu-nationalist agenda after thirty years of organizing. The three signature acts of the second term — the abrogation of Article 370 in Kashmir, the Citizenship Amendment Act, and the construction of the Ram temple at Ayodhya — completed the legislative-and-symbolic program the BJP had been promising since the 1990s.

The **abrogation of Article 370** on August 5, 2019, was the boldest single act of the Modi period. Article 370 of the Indian Constitution had granted Jammu and Kashmir special autonomous status since 1949 — separate state constitution, separate flag, restrictions on land purchase by non-Kashmiri residents, separate central-government legislative authority. The article had been the basis of the 1947 accession terms and had become, in BJP framing, a permanent obstacle to Kashmiri integration with the Indian Union and a special privilege for Muslim-majority Kashmir that the rest of India did not enjoy. The Modi government's procedure for abrogation was constitutionally aggressive: a presidential order on August 5 changed Article 370's operative provisions through a reinterpretation of "Constituent Assembly" (the original Kashmir Constituent Assembly having dissolved in 1957) as the Jammu and Kashmir State Legislative Assembly (which was under President's Rule and not in session). A parallel **Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act** divided the former state into two Union Territories — Jammu and Kashmir, and Ladakh — converting the territory from a state under President's Rule to a centrally-administered territory with substantially reduced autonomy. The constitutional legality of the abrogation was challenged in the Supreme Court; the court's 2023 ruling upheld the abrogation by 5–0 majority while raising questions about specific procedural aspects.

The implementation was managed with extraordinary security. The internet was shut down in the Kashmir Valley starting August 4 and was not fully restored for over a year (the longest internet shutdown ever imposed by a democratic government). All major Kashmiri political leaders, including three former state Chief Ministers, were placed under preventive detention; many were held for months without trial. Substantial paramilitary deployments locked down Kashmir Valley cities and prevented public protests. The international response was muted: Pakistan protested loudly but had no leverage; the United States and the United Kingdom expressed mild concern but did not push for reversal; China protested the Ladakh component (which included Aksai Chin, claimed by China) and would later use the protest as justification for the 2020 Galwan border clashes (below). The Indian public was overwhelmingly supportive: opinion polls showed support for the abrogation at over 70% across all major Indian states except Kashmir itself.

The Kashmiri political consensus after 2019 was that the abrogation was a unilateral imposition that delegitimized the constitutional basis of the 1947 accession. The integrationist counter-argument was that the special status had been a fiction since 1953 (when Sheikh Abdullah's pro-autonomy state government had been dismissed) and that the constitutional reorganization had simply formalized what had been the actual federal arrangement for decades. The political question — whether the abrogation will permanently shift Kashmir into the normal Indian federal framework or whether it will produce, over time, a longer-running political crisis — is, in 2026, still being settled.

The **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA)** of December 2019 was the second core ideological measure of Modi II. The act provided expedited Indian citizenship for non-Muslim refugees from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who had entered India before December 31, 2014. The list of eligible religions — Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, Christians — explicitly excluded Muslims. The act, in conjunction with the proposed **National Register of Citizens (NRC)** — a citizenship-verification process that had been implemented in Assam and was being discussed for nationwide rollout — produced widespread opposition: Muslim political organizations argued that the combination of CAA's religious-criterion citizenship pathway and NRC's documentation-based citizenship verification was specifically designed to render undocumented Muslims stateless while protecting undocumented non-Muslims. Mass protests broke out across the country between December 2019 and March 2020, most prominently at the **Shaheen Bagh** sit-in in southeast Delhi (a months-long peaceful protest led primarily by Muslim women that became the defining symbol of CAA opposition). Communal violence in northeast Delhi in February 2020 killed 53 people — predominantly Muslim, in attacks where Delhi Police complicity has been alleged by multiple inquiries — and the protests broadly dissipated under the impact of the COVID lockdown that began in March 2020.

The **Ram Temple consecrated in Ayodhya** on January 22, 2024 completed the third leg of the program. The construction of the temple at the disputed Ayodhya site — on land where the Babri Masjid had stood until December 1992 — had been authorized by the Supreme Court's November 2019 ruling resolving the land-title dispute. The court awarded the disputed site to the Ram temple trust while allotting an alternative five-acre parcel elsewhere in Ayodhya for a new mosque. The temple's construction began in 2020 and the *pran pratishtha* (consecration of the deity inside the new temple) was conducted on January 22, 2024 in a ceremony attended by Modi personally. The opposition political parties, the Shankaracharyas (the senior Hindu religious authorities), and the Indian liberal commentariat were divided on whether to attend; most stayed away on the grounds that the ceremony was being conducted while construction was incomplete and that the political framing of the event subordinated religious to political authority. The consecration was nonetheless a defining moment in 21st-century Indian political-religious history: it completed the symbolic project that the VHP had launched in 1986, vindicated the BJP's long-running political bet on Hindu cultural mobilization, and demonstrated the BJP's ability to deliver substantive cultural outcomes that previous Congress governments had been unwilling or unable to attempt.

---

The non-ideological story of Modi II was dominated by COVID, the farmers' protests, and the slow-motion strategic confrontation with China.

The **COVID-19 pandemic** struck India in March 2020. The Modi government imposed one of the world's most stringent national lockdowns on March 25, 2020 — a 21-day complete shutdown of economic activity announced with four hours' notice and subsequently extended for two months. The implementation produced a humanitarian crisis: tens of millions of urban migrant workers, suddenly unemployed and unable to access transport home, were stranded in cities without food, shelter, or medical care; many died on the long walks home; the economic disruption produced India's first GDP contraction since 1979 (an estimated –6.6% in fiscal 2020–21). The Indian vaccine rollout, beginning in January 2021, was eventually the world's largest by total doses administered (over 2.2 billion doses by 2022); the **Delta variant wave** of April–June 2021 produced the worst public-health disaster of the period, with India's official COVID death toll reaching approximately 470,000 but with academic excess-death studies estimating actual mortality at 3–5 million. The Delta wave overwhelmed Indian hospital infrastructure across multiple states; the images of crematoria operating at full capacity around the clock and of family members of COVID patients searching for oxygen cylinders defined the worst weeks of 2021.

The political consequences of COVID were mixed. The Modi government's initial response was widely supported (the lockdown framing as decisive national action met its political moment) but the Delta wave damaged its competence reputation, particularly in states (including Uttar Pradesh) where hospital infrastructure failed under load. The post-pandemic economic recovery was, by global standards, strong: Indian GDP growth recovered to 8.7% in fiscal 2021–22 and stayed above 7% through 2023–24. The Indian economy in 2024 was the world's fifth largest by nominal GDP, having overtaken the United Kingdom in 2022.

The **2020–21 farmers' protests** were the largest sustained protest movement against the Modi government. In September 2020, the government passed three agricultural reform laws that opened the agricultural produce market to private buyers, allowed contract farming with private corporations, and removed price controls on essential commodities. The laws were technically defensible from a market-economy standpoint and had been recommended by multiple economist commissions for two decades. The political execution was poor. Farmers' organizations from Punjab, Haryana, and Western UP — predominantly Sikh and Jat Hindu small-landowners benefiting from the existing **Minimum Support Price** (MSP) system and the regulated *mandi* (market yard) infrastructure — read the laws as designed to dismantle the price-support and market-access systems that protected them from corporate buyer concentration. Farmers' unions organized a sustained encampment at the Delhi border, principally at the Singhu and Tikri checkpoints, beginning November 2020. The encampments persisted through one of the coldest North Indian winters in decades and through the Delta COVID wave. The protests included Republic Day 2021 violence at Red Fort, organized by a fringe of the movement, that produced criminal cases and political division within the protest leadership. After fourteen months of standoff, Modi announced on November 19, 2021 that the three laws would be repealed, conceding the protesters' core demand. The repeal was the largest single Modi-government policy reversal of the second term and a substantial demonstration of organized rural-Indian political capacity.

The **Galwan Valley clash** of June 15, 2020 — a hand-to-hand combat engagement between Indian and Chinese troops in the high-altitude Galwan Valley of Ladakh that killed 20 Indian soldiers and an undisclosed number of Chinese troops (later Chinese acknowledgments and external estimates suggest at least 40) — was the first lethal Indo-Chinese border engagement since 1975. The clash followed weeks of Chinese forward deployments into multiple sectors of the disputed Ladakh frontier and produced an ongoing standoff that, six years later, has not fully resolved. The Modi government's response was a substantial Indian military reinforcement of the eastern Ladakh region, combined with a public-relations effort to minimize the political damage from the casualties. India's response also included the banning of dozens of Chinese mobile applications (notably TikTok), increased tariffs on Chinese imports, and broader regulatory restrictions on Chinese FDI in Indian companies. The Indo-Chinese relationship after Galwan has been worse than at any point since the 1962 war.

The **Chandrayaan-3 lands on Moon** mission of August 23, 2023 was the most successful single Indian scientific achievement of the period. The Indian Space Research Organisation's robotic lunar lander touched down at 70°S, 32°E on the lunar surface — the first soft landing on the lunar south pole region by any space agency, and the first Indian soft landing on the Moon after the 2019 Chandrayaan-2 mission's lander had failed during descent. The mission was completed at a total cost of approximately $75 million — less than the production budget of several Hollywood space films — and demonstrated the cost-effectiveness of the Indian space program (also visible in earlier missions: the 2008 **Chandrayaan-1 lunar mission**, India's first lunar probe; the 2014 **Mangalyaan reaches Mars** mission, the first Asian Mars mission and the first to succeed on first attempt by any space agency; and the parallel scientific programs across these missions). The political framing of Chandrayaan-3 by the Modi government — the lunar surface as Indian sovereign reach, the technical achievement as evidence of broader civilizational capability — was successful: the Indian public received the achievement as national vindication.

---

Modi's **G20 presidency** of 2023 was the diplomatic high-water mark of the second term. India hosted the G20 summit in Delhi in September 2023 with extensive coordination across multiple national capitals and multiple sub-national working groups, produced the Delhi Declaration (a consensus document despite Russia-Ukraine war tensions), and used the presidency to substantially elevate India's diplomatic profile as a leader of the global South. The summit's branding around "*Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam*" (the Sanskrit phrase meaning "the world is one family") was a successful piece of cultural-diplomatic positioning. India's diplomatic relationships across the period of the G20 presidency were strong with the United States (which had stepped up arms transfers and intelligence cooperation), with the European Union (concluding major trade negotiations), with the Gulf monarchies (energy and labor migration), and with Russia (oil purchases sustained despite Western sanctions). The simultaneous tensions — with China across the disputed border, with Canada over the Trudeau government's accusations of Indian involvement in the killing of a Khalistani activist on Canadian soil, with the United States over the alleged Indian government plot to assassinate the New York–based Khalistani lawyer Gurpatwant Singh Pannun — sat alongside the diplomatic successes without disrupting them.

The **2024 General Election** was held in seven phases between April and June 2024 and produced a result the BJP had not expected. The party's seats fell from 303 to 240 — a substantial loss that left the BJP short of an outright Lok Sabha majority and dependent on its NDA partners (particularly the Telugu Desam Party in Andhra Pradesh and the Janata Dal United in Bihar) to form the government. The opposition INDIA bloc — a Congress-led coalition that had assembled across 2023 — improved substantially, with the Congress doubling to 99 seats and the bloc total reaching 234. The 2024 result was the third Modi mandate but the first one to require coalition management rather than majority governance, and it has substantially changed the operating conditions of the second-decade BJP.

# Chapter 15 — The Republic at Seventy-Seven

The Modi 3.0 government — formed in June 2024 with the BJP at 240 Lok Sabha seats and the NDA coalition at 293 — operates under coalition constraints that the BJP had not faced since 1999. The early signals of the third term suggest the government will be more moderate on the ideological program than Modi II had been: the major signature legislative items have been deferred, the cabinet portfolio distribution has been more accommodating to coalition partners than the second-term cabinet, and the BJP's organizational machinery is partially focused on a generational transition (Modi is 75 in 2025; the question of who succeeds him at the next election in 2029 is unresolved within the party).

Three structural conditions will define the next decade of Indian politics regardless of who is in charge.

The first is the **demographic dividend**. India in 2026 has approximately 1.43 billion people, having surpassed China in 2023 to become the world's most populous nation. The Indian median age is 28 (compared to 39 in China, 39 in the United States, 49 in Japan). India will add another 200 million people to its working-age population by 2050. This demographic structure is an enormous economic opportunity — a young workforce can drive growth and innovation if the workforce is educated and employed — and an enormous economic and political risk if it is not. India's labor-force participation rate is, by global standards, low (about 56% overall, with female participation around 33%); the formal employment rate is even lower. The Indian state's most important medium-term question is whether it can generate sufficient employment for the demographic wave that is currently in school. Manufacturing growth has been below targets; agricultural productivity has plateaued; the IT and services sectors have absorbed a substantial share of the educated middle-class workforce but not enough to absorb the broader labor pool. The political economy of the next twenty years will be heavily shaped by whether India can produce 12–15 million new jobs per year for the next twenty years; the current pace is closer to 7–8 million.

The second is the **China question**. The Indo-Chinese strategic competition is the defining external pressure on Indian foreign and security policy. The Galwan-era border standoff has not fully resolved; substantial Chinese forward deployments persist along the Line of Actual Control; Chinese infrastructure construction in Tibet and Xinjiang continues to alter the disputed-border equation. The broader question is whether India can sustainably operate as a strategic counterweight to China across the Indo-Pacific region. The Quad partnership with the United States, Japan, and Australia is one element; bilateral defense cooperation with France, the United Kingdom, Israel, and South Korea is another; the building of indigenous defense-industry capacity (the **Atmanirbhar Bharat** — "self-reliant India" — initiative that the Modi government has emphasized since 2020) is a third. Whether the combination is sufficient to manage Chinese strategic pressure is the question of the next two decades. The economic-coupling question — India's continued dependence on Chinese imports for several critical industrial sectors, particularly pharmaceuticals, electronics, and rare earths — is parallel and unresolved.

The third is the **diaspora superpower** dimension. The Indian diaspora is, in 2026, approximately 32 million people, including 5 million in the United States, 4 million in the Gulf monarchies, 3 million in the UK and Europe, and substantial communities in Canada, Australia, Singapore, and East Africa. The diaspora's economic, political, and cultural influence on contemporary India has grown dramatically since the 1991 opening — both as a financial flow (remittances exceeded $125 billion in 2023, the largest annual remittance receipt of any country in history) and as a political asset (Indian diaspora communities have substantial influence on host-country relationships with India, particularly in the United States, where Indian-Americans now hold senior positions across multiple branches of government, the corporate sector, and academia). The "civilizational" framing that the Modi government has emphasized — India not as a post-colonial state but as a continuous civilization with global reach — is at least partly underwritten by the diaspora reality. The diaspora is also a vehicle for the export of Indian politics back to India: the funding base for both the BJP's Hindu nationalist organizations and for the Indian liberal commentariat's institutional infrastructure includes substantial overseas-Indian components. India in 2026 is no longer a country whose political life can be cleanly separated from the political life of its citizens and former citizens abroad.

---

The question of what India is becoming, in 2026, is genuinely open. The country has, in the eighty years since independence, accomplished things that very few comparably sized post-colonial states have achieved. It has held competitive multiparty elections at every five-year interval since 1952, including the 2024 election that reduced the ruling party's majority — a transfer of partial political authority by ballot rather than by coup. It has produced a nuclear-armed military capable of regional deterrence and a space program capable of lunar and Martian missions. It has lifted hundreds of millions of people out of absolute poverty, though several hundred million remain poor by any reasonable definition. It has produced cultural and intellectual exports — films, software, academic and literary work, religious and philosophical traditions in revival — that have substantially shaped global culture far beyond Indian borders.

It has also produced things that the founding generation of the republic would not have endorsed. The constitutional secularism Nehru defended has been substantially eroded by the BJP's reframing of Indian identity around majoritarian Hindu cultural symbols. The press freedom that was the strongest argument against the Emergency has been eroded by a combination of corporate capture of the major media houses, regulatory pressure on independent outlets, and the criminal prosecution of journalists under various national-security statutes. The judicial independence that survived the Emergency has been substantially worn down by post-retirement appointments of judges to executive sinecures, by the executive's increasing influence over Supreme Court appointment processes, and by selective non-enforcement of court rulings the government finds inconvenient. The civil-society space that the Right to Information Act expanded has been substantially narrowed by the use of foreign-contribution regulation, money-laundering investigation, and direct administrative pressure against NGOs perceived as politically inconvenient. The independent fiscal and statistical institutions (CAG, NSSO, RBI) have been substantially restructured under political direction, with documented effects on the reliability of official Indian economic and social-indicator data.

Whether the net trajectory of these changes is — in the framing of Indian liberal critics — the slow conversion of a constitutional democracy into a "Hindu Rashtra" majoritarian state, or — in the framing of Indian conservative defenders — the long-overdue correction of a Nehruvian secularism that had subordinated the country's actual cultural majority to its post-colonial elite, is the basic interpretive question of contemporary Indian politics. The empirical evidence is rich and the disagreement is real. The book takes no position on what comes next; the chapters that come next are being written by Indian voters, by Indian institutional actors, by the Indian diaspora, and by external pressures the country has only partial ability to control.

What the book does take a position on is the basic claim. India in 2026 — at seventy-seven years from independence, at 168 years from the formal establishment of Crown rule, at over five thousand years from the first urban civilizations of the Indus Valley (Indus Valley Ch 1) — is a continuous historical project that has, against substantial odds, survived. The next chapter is not yet written. The country goes on.

# The Byzantine Empire — The Empire That Wouldn't Die

**Goal:** Narrative for the fifth and final TL in the Greco-Roman chain, picking up where Ancient Rome ends. Covers 330 CE (the founding of Constantinople) through 1453 (the fall to Mehmed II) — 1,123 years, the longest single civilization in this app. The central thesis: there was no Byzantine Empire. There was just *Rome*, refusing to die. The people we call Byzantines called themselves *Romaioi* — Romans — speaking Greek, worshipping Christ, ruling from a city that started its life as a Greek colony, and outlasting the western half of their empire by a full millennium. They were the bridge between antiquity and the modern world. When they finally fell, their scholars carried Plato to Florence, their religion settled in Moscow, and their imperial idea was inherited by sultans who kept Hagia Sophia standing as a mosque.

**Status:** First draft, 12 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — The Empire That Wouldn't Die

There is no Byzantine Empire.

Or rather — there was, and they did not know it. Nobody in this entire 1,123-year story ever called themselves a Byzantine. They called themselves **Romaioi** (roh-MY-oy, Greek for "Romans"). They called their state *Basileia ton Romaion*, "the Empire of the Romans." Their emperors styled themselves *Imperator*, *Augustus*, *Basileus* — Roman titles, sometimes in Latin and sometimes in Greek translation. When their last emperor died fighting on the walls in 1453, he was, in his own mind and in the minds of his soldiers, the Roman emperor in Roman Constantinople defending the Roman Empire from a foreign invader.

The word "Byzantine" was invented by a German scholar named Hieronymus Wolf in 1557 — a hundred and four years after the empire was already gone. Wolf needed a label that distinguished the Greek-speaking medieval state from the Latin-speaking ancient one his own readers identified with, and he reached back to **Byzantion** (BIZ-an-tee-on), the small Greek colony that had stood on the site of Constantinople before Constantine arrived. The name stuck for the same reason a lot of historiographic conventions stick: it was useful. It gave western European writers a way to talk about an empire they wanted to think of as decadent, schismatic, and Other — separate from "real" Rome, which they preferred to imagine as the ancestor of the West.

Throw the label out and the picture changes. There was no fall of Rome in 476 CE (Ancient Rome Ch 10). There was a fall of the *western half* of the Roman Empire. The eastern half — older, richer, more populous, with better fortifications, better tax revenues, and a far more impregnable capital — kept going. It kept going for almost exactly a thousand years after Romulus Augustulus handed his crown to Odoacer. During those thousand years, it called itself Rome. It used Roman law. It maintained Roman administrative titles. It traced its emperors in unbroken succession back to Augustus. And it watched, with a mixture of bafflement and amusement, as the barbarian kingdoms of western Europe slowly forgot how Rome had worked and started reinventing it badly.

This narrative will use "Byzantine" anyway, because that is the word the reader knows. But every time you see it, mentally translate it as "Roman." The people in this story were not the descendants of the Romans. They were the Romans. They just happened to live in a Greek-speaking eastern Mediterranean and to have stopped wearing togas around the time you stop wearing one to your job.

A note before we go further: dates in this narrative are CE (Common Era, the secular equivalent of AD) unless otherwise noted. The Byzantine state itself dated events from "the creation of the world" — Anno Mundi — using a chronology that placed the creation in 5509 BCE, which means a Byzantine reading this in 1453 would have called it the year 6961. We will spare ourselves that.

---

The story starts with one man, one battle, and one decision.

The man was **Constantine I** (later "the Great"), born around 272 in modern Serbia, son of a Roman officer who rose to become one of the four co-emperors of the **Tetrarchy** (the four-emperor power-sharing system that **Diocletian** had set up in 293 to manage an empire that had become too large for one ruler — Ancient Rome Ch 8). The Tetrarchy worked while Diocletian was personally enforcing it and collapsed the moment he retired in 305. Constantine spent the next eighteen years fighting his way through a series of civil wars against rival claimants. The decisive moment came on October 28, 312, at the **Battle of the Milvian Bridge** outside Rome, where Constantine destroyed the army of his rival **Maxentius** and took the western half of the empire.

Constantine claimed that on the eve of the battle he had seen a Christian symbol in the sky — the **Chi-Rho** (KY-roh, the first two letters of "Christ" in Greek, superimposed) — accompanied by the words *In hoc signo vinces*, "in this sign you shall conquer." Whether the vision happened, whether Constantine actually believed it happened, or whether the whole story was retrofitted by Christian biographers afterward is one of those questions the surviving evidence cannot settle. What is not in doubt is that the next year, in 313, Constantine and his eastern colleague **Licinius** (lih-SIN-ee-us) issued the **Edict of Milan**, which legalized Christianity across the empire and ordered the return of confiscated church property. After three centuries of being a persecuted underground sect, Christianity was suddenly the favored religion of the Roman emperor. The pattern was an old one in the ancient world: a ruler builds legitimacy by liberating a previously persecuted monotheistic community and casting himself as that community's divinely chosen patron. Cyrus the Great had done this for the exiled Jews 850 years earlier, releasing them from Babylonian captivity and inscribing his benevolence on the Cyrus Cylinder (Persian Empire Ch 1). Constantine's Edict of Milan was the same move at the same scale — the Roman emperor declaring himself the protector of a persecuted monotheist faith, and reaping the political reward.

Then in 324, Constantine defeated Licinius and reunited the entire empire under one ruler for the first time in a generation. And then, in 325, he did something that would shape religion in Europe for the next sixteen hundred years: he convened the **First Council of Nicaea** (nye-SEE-uh, a town in northwestern Anatolia, modern İznik in Turkey), the first ecumenical (empire-wide) gathering of Christian bishops, summoning over 300 bishops from across the empire to settle a doctrinal dispute that was tearing the new state religion apart. The dispute was about the nature of Christ — specifically, whether the Son was co-eternal with the Father (the position of the bishop **Athanasius** of Alexandria) or whether the Son had been created by the Father at some point and was therefore subordinate (the position of an Alexandrian priest named **Arius**, whose followers were called **Arians**). The council sided with Athanasius and produced the **Nicene Creed** — the statement of faith still recited every Sunday in Catholic and Orthodox churches today, which begins "We believe in one God, the Father almighty… and in one Lord Jesus Christ… begotten not made, of one substance with the Father."

Most readers raised in a Christian context will recognize the creed and assume the dispute was a minor theological argument. It was not. The Arian controversy nearly destroyed the eastern half of the empire over the next century. Emperors picked sides. Bishops were exiled, restored, exiled again. Mobs rioted in the streets of Antioch and Alexandria over the placement of a single Greek letter (the difference between *homoousios*, "of the same substance," and *homoiousios*, "of like substance," is one iota — literally the Greek letter that gives English "iota" — and the most famous theological controversy of the fourth century turned on whether Christ was *homo*-ousios or *homoi*-ousios with the Father). The Greek-speaking East cared about theology in a way the Latin-speaking West did not, and the cost of that caring would become a recurring theme. Constantine's son **Constantius II** (con-STAN-tee-us, ruled 337–361) was an Arian who reversed his father's pro-Nicene policy and persecuted Athanasius mercilessly — the iota war ran for fifty years before Nicene Christianity finally won permanently.

---

The decision that made Byzantium possible came in 324, the same year Constantine reunited the empire. He decided to build a new capital.

The site he chose was a small fishing town called Byzantion — a Greek colony founded in 657 BCE on a triangular peninsula on the European side of the **Bosporus**, the narrow strait connecting the Sea of Marmara to the Black Sea. The location was, in retrospect, the most defensible major-city site in the Mediterranean world. Three sides of the peninsula are surrounded by water (the Bosporus to the east, the Sea of Marmara to the south, an inlet called the **Golden Horn** to the north). Only the western side, facing the Thracian plain, is overland — and the western side is short enough that a single line of walls can seal it off. The Bosporus is also the gateway between two seas (the Black and the Mediterranean) and two continents (Europe and Asia), meaning whoever controls Byzantion controls the most important trade chokepoint between northern grain, Russian fur, Anatolian metal, and Mediterranean shipping.

Constantine renamed it **Constantinople** ("Constantine's city") and dedicated it on May 11, 330. Or rather: he formally rededicated it as "New Rome" — the official name on the inaugural inscription was *Nova Roma*, and the city was given a Senate, free grain distributions, seven hills (the geography was kindly cooperative), and every major Roman institution copied or transplanted from the original. The intent was not to abandon Rome. The intent was to give the empire a second capital, equal in dignity to the first, but better positioned to rule the wealthier eastern provinces. Within a few decades the practical reality of imperial administration would shift the center of gravity east, and within a few centuries the western capital would be a memory.

Constantine also began the urban program that would eventually make Constantinople the largest city in the Christian world. He built the **Hippodrome** — a U-shaped chariot-racing arena modeled on the Circus Maximus in Rome, capable of seating up to 100,000 spectators. (We will come back to the Hippodrome in nearly every chapter, because it is impossible to overstate how central the racing track was to Byzantine politics. Imagine if every American presidential candidate had to formally appear before the crowd at the Super Bowl, and if the Super Bowl crowd routinely rioted, deposed presidents, and lynched generals.) He laid out broad colonnaded avenues, a great central forum, and the foundations of an imperial palace complex that would grow over the next eight centuries into a labyrinth of throne rooms, chapels, gardens, and ceremonial chambers covering a quarter of the peninsula.

He also began stripping the Mediterranean world of its art treasures to fill the new capital. Pagan statues from Athens, Delphi, Ephesus, and Rome itself were carted to Constantinople and erected as decorations in the Hippodrome and the forums. The most famous — a bronze column of three intertwined serpents, originally cast in 479 BCE from melted-down Persian armor as a thank-offering after the Greek victory at **Plataea** (Persian Empire Ch 3) — was uprooted from Delphi and stood in the spina of the Hippodrome. Its lower section is still there. Modern Istanbul, walking past it, has not always known what it is looking at.

Constantine died in 337. He was baptized only on his deathbed, by an Arian bishop, which has caused some consternation for later Catholic and Orthodox traditions that prefer their first Christian emperor to have been clearly orthodox. The Eastern Orthodox Church canonized him as a saint anyway, calling him *Isapostolos* — "Equal-to-the-Apostles" — for his role in legalizing the faith and convening Nicaea. His mother **Helena**, who had made a pilgrimage to Jerusalem in 326–328 and reportedly discovered the **True Cross** of the Crucifixion buried under a pagan temple on Golgotha, was canonized too. The True Cross would become the most important relic in the empire and a recurring character in the chapters ahead.

---

The fourth century was a long, slow argument about whether the empire was really Christian.

Constantine had legalized Christianity but not made it the state religion. His sons mostly favored the Arian faction. His nephew **Julian** (called "**the Apostate**" by Christian writers, ruled 361–363) was the last pagan emperor — a thoughtful, philosophically trained ruler who had been raised as a Christian, hated it, converted privately to traditional Greco-Roman polytheism, and on taking the throne tried to roll back Constantine's revolution. Julian restored animal sacrifice, reopened pagan temples, withdrew Christian privileges, and wrote bitter polemics against the church. He also tried to revive the Jerusalem Temple — partly out of philosophical sympathy with Judaism, partly because rebuilding the Temple would have falsified Jesus's prophecy that not one stone would be left on another. Julian's attempt was abandoned when an earthquake (or, depending on the source, a fire) destroyed the construction site. He died less than three years into his reign, struck by a spear during a poorly-planned invasion of Sasanian Persia (Persian Empire Ch 9). His last words, according to the hostile Christian tradition, were "*Vicisti, Galilaee*" — "You have conquered, Galilean." This line is almost certainly a later invention, but it captured what Julian's failure meant: paganism's last imperial champion was dead, and there would be no second attempt.

The pagan rollback ran out of steam, and seventeen years after Julian's death, on February 27, 380, Emperor **Theodosius I** issued the **Edict of Thessalonica**, which made **Nicene Christianity** — the version that had won at Nicaea fifty-five years earlier — the official state religion of the Roman Empire. Pagan sacrifice was banned in 391. The Olympic Games, which had run continuously every four years since 776 BCE, were abolished in 393 (or possibly 426; the exact date is debated). The temples were closed, looted, or rededicated as churches. The Library of Alexandria's pagan elements (what was left of them) were dispersed. A thousand-year religious civilization, the entire intellectual and ritual world of classical antiquity, was officially dissolved in a single generation.

Theodosius was the last emperor to rule both halves of the empire. When he died in 395, the empire was split between his sons: **Arcadius** (ar-KAY-dee-us) took the East with its capital at Constantinople, **Honorius** (hoh-NOR-ee-us) took the West with its capital first at Milan and then (after 402) at Ravenna. The split was not new — the empire had been divided into eastern and western administrative halves for a century, and it would technically remain a single state in legal theory for centuries afterward. But after 395, no single person would ever rule both halves again. The two courts grew apart. The two churches grew apart. The two languages grew apart, as Latin retreated in the East and Greek became the daily working language of administration.

This is the conventional date — 395 — for the start of the Byzantine Empire, even though no Byzantine ever knew that. They thought they were just Romans whose empire happened, for administrative reasons, to have its operational capital in the east.

---

What kept the East alive while the West collapsed was a combination of geography, economy, and one extraordinary piece of architecture.

The geography is the simpler half. The Eastern provinces were richer. **Egypt** alone produced enough grain to feed Constantinople and pay a substantial chunk of the imperial budget. **Syria** and **Anatolia** had bigger populations, more cities, and better-developed agriculture than anywhere in the West outside Italy. The major eastern frontiers (the lower Danube and the upper Euphrates) were shorter than the western ones (the Rhine, the upper Danube, the British coast, the African coast). When Goths and Huns and Vandals swept across Europe in the fifth century, they were attacking a western state that had to defend a longer perimeter with a smaller revenue base. The same migrations broke against the eastern frontier and were either deflected — onto the West, where they could do worse damage — or absorbed.

The economy followed from the geography. Constantinople sat on a major trade hub and could collect customs duties from anyone shipping through the Bosporus. The eastern Mediterranean's network of cities — Alexandria, Antioch, Ephesus, Thessalonica, Caesarea — generated a tax base unmatched anywhere in the West. The empire could afford a professional standing army. It could afford a professional bureaucracy that did not need to be staffed by aristocratic amateurs. It could afford an enormous engineering project to seal off its capital from any imaginable land assault.

That engineering project was the **Theodosian Walls**.

Constantine's original wall had become inadequate as the city grew. So in 408–413, during the long reign of Theodosius's grandson **Theodosius II** (ruled 408–450 — child emperor for most of his reign, dominated first by his sister Pulcheria and then by his pious wife Aelia Eudocia), the imperial regent **Anthemios** ordered a new fortification line drawn farther west, sealing off the entire landward approach to the city. The Theodosian Walls were not a single wall. They were a layered defense system: a wide ditch (filled with water from cross-walls when needed), an outer wall (about 30 feet or 9 meters tall), a terrace, a higher inner wall (about 40 feet or 12 meters tall and 16 feet or 5 meters thick), and 96 towers spaced at regular intervals so that any approach to one tower was within bowshot of two others. The whole system stretched roughly four miles (6.5 km) across the peninsula, sealing the city off from the Thracian plain to the west.

The Theodosian Walls would not be breached by a foreign army for over a thousand years. They survived sieges by Avars (a Eurasian steppe confederation) in 626, Arabs in 674–678 and 717–718, Bulgars repeatedly, Rus' in 860, and a coalition of European Crusaders in 1203–1204 (and even the Crusaders in the end did not breach the land walls — they got in via the sea walls along the Golden Horn). Constantinople did not fall to a land assault until 1453, and even then only because the attackers had something the previous attackers had not had: gunpowder cannons. For more than a millennium, the Theodosian Walls were the most formidable defensive system in the world. They are the single most important reason the eastern half of the Roman Empire outlived the western half by a thousand years.

---

The fifth century did its worst to the empire and the empire absorbed it.

In 410, Visigoths under Alaric sacked Rome (Ancient Rome Ch 10). The shock was psychological, not strategic. Constantinople was unaffected. In 451 the **Hunnic** confederation under Attila ravaged the Balkans and extracted enormous tributes from Constantinople before turning west and attacking Gaul. Eastern emperors paid Attila off rather than fight him on his own terms — a recurring Byzantine tactic that western moralists have always found contemptible and that Byzantine accountants always found cheaper than war. The same year, Emperor **Marcian** (ruled 450–457, an old soldier who had married Theodosius II's pious sister Pulcheria after her brother's death and become emperor through that marriage) refused to continue paying Attila tribute and convened the **Council of Chalcedon** (kal-SEH-don, a town across the Bosporus from Constantinople, modern Kadıköy in Istanbul).

Chalcedon was the fourth ecumenical council, and it was supposed to settle a different theological war that had succeeded the Arian controversy. The argument now was not about whether Christ was divine — Nicaea had settled that. The argument was about *how* Christ was divine and human at the same time. Two wings emerged. The **Monophysites** (moh-NOF-ih-syts, "single-nature" Christians, dominant in Egypt and Syria) held that Christ had one composite divine-human nature. The **Dyophysites** (dy-OF-ih-syts, "two-nature" Christians, dominant in Rome and Constantinople) held that Christ had two distinct natures, divine and human, united in one person. Chalcedon sided with the Dyophysites and produced the **Chalcedonian Definition** that is still the official doctrine of the Catholic, Orthodox, and most Protestant churches: Christ in two natures, "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation."

The losers refused to accept the verdict. Egypt and Syria — the Monophysite heartlands — broke off into their own churches, which still exist today: the **Coptic Orthodox Church** of Egypt, the **Syriac Orthodox Church**, the **Armenian Apostolic Church**, the **Ethiopian Orthodox Church**. The schism was permanent. Sixteen hundred years later, these churches still reject Chalcedon. And the political consequence — that the empire's two richest provinces, Egypt and Syria, would never again feel fully loyal to the Constantinopolitan church or state — would matter enormously when the Arabs arrived in the seventh century. The locals welcomed them. Why fight for an empire that had spent two centuries persecuting your bishops?

The Henotikon, an attempt by Emperor **Zeno** (ruled 474–491) in 482 to paper over the Chalcedonian split with a vague compromise edict, made things worse: it triggered the **Acacian Schism** (ah-KAY-shun, named after the Patriarch Acacius) — the first formal break between the churches of Rome and Constantinople, lasting from 484 to 519. The Acacian Schism was eventually patched up. The pattern it set — Rome and Constantinople drifting apart on doctrinal questions, then reconciling, then drifting apart again — would repeat for the next six centuries until the breach became permanent in 1054 (Chapter 7).

Meanwhile, in the west, the empire was disintegrating. **Vandals** took North Africa in 439, cutting off the western grain supply. **Visigoths** took Spain and southern Gaul. **Franks** moved into northern Gaul. **Anglo-Saxons** crossed into Britain. In 476, the Germanic general **Odoacer** deposed the last western emperor, the unfortunate teenager **Romulus Augustulus**, and sent the imperial regalia east to Constantinople with a message: there was no need for a separate western emperor any longer. Odoacer would rule Italy as a king, nominally under the eastern emperor's authority. The eastern court accepted the regalia, recognized the new arrangement, and went on with business.

To Byzantines, 476 was not a fall. It was an administrative consolidation. The empire was again a single state with a single emperor in Constantinople, and the western provinces were under barbarian governors who would eventually, presumably, be brought back into proper Roman administration. The fact that this view was politically convenient does not mean it was wrong, exactly. It does mean that the *next* emperor capable of seizing on it would try to make it real.

That emperor was Justinian.

# Chapter 2 — Justinian's Reconquest

Justinian was an unlikely candidate to rule the world.

His uncle **Justin I** (ruled 518–527) was an illiterate Thracian peasant who had walked into Constantinople in his youth as a pig farmer's son looking for work in the imperial guard. By the early sixth century he had risen, through a combination of competence, ruthlessness, and the kind of luck that catches every veteran soldier who outlives his rivals, to command the **Excubitors** — the elite palace guard regiment. When Emperor **Anastasius I** (ruled 491–518) died without an heir in 518, Justin was the man with troops in the room, and he became emperor by acclamation. He was around 68 years old, semi-literate (according to Procopius's hostile *Secret History* — the only source for the detail — he had to sign documents using a stencil with the word *legi*, "I have read," cut into it for him to trace; no friendlier source confirms this and it may be polemic), and entirely uninterested in the kind of sophisticated cultural projects his nephew would pursue. What Justin had was good political instincts and a brilliant nephew.

The nephew was **Petrus Sabbatius**, born around 482 in a village in modern North Macedonia, brought to Constantinople as a teenager, and educated at his uncle's expense in law, theology, history, and Greek philosophy. By the time Justin took the throne, Petrus — now using the regnal name **Justinian** — was already running the actual administration. When Justin died in 527, Justinian formally inherited a throne he had effectively held for years.

He was brilliant, exhausting, and obsessed. He worked through most nights. The historian **Procopius of Caesarea** (proh-KOH-pee-us, c. 500–570) called him "the sleepless emperor." Procopius was the greatest Greek historian of the period, and served as legal secretary to Justinian's leading general — about whom much more shortly. His ambition was simple and fixed: to restore the Roman Empire to its full territorial extent, reconquer the lost western provinces, codify Roman law, build the greatest church in Christendom, and fix the Chalcedonian schism. He achieved most of these goals, broke the empire's economy doing it, and left successors who would spend two centuries cleaning up the consequences.

---

Justinian's reign begins not with a battle but with a wedding.

In the early 520s, while still co-emperor with his uncle, Justinian fell in love with a woman named **Theodora** (theh-oh-DOR-uh). The complete biography of Theodora is unrecoverable because the only detailed source for her early life is Procopius's **Secret History** (the *Anekdota*, written around 550 but not published in his lifetime — a manuscript that savaged Justinian, Theodora, and the general Belisarius in unsparing prose, surviving by chance in a single Vatican copy that was rediscovered in the seventeenth century). Procopius reports that Theodora was the daughter of a bear-keeper at the Hippodrome, that she became an actress and possibly a prostitute, that she traveled to Egypt and back, that she was famous on the Constantinopolitan stage for an obscene mime routine involving trained geese and Leda. How much of this is true and how much is the disgruntled invective of a man who had to write panegyrics to the imperial couple by day and was venting in private by night is impossible to know. What is clear is that Theodora came from outside the aristocracy — that she was, by the standards of the imperial court, scandalously low-born — and that Justinian had to get the law against patrician-actress marriages changed before he could marry her in 525.

Theodora was crowned co-empress when Justinian took the throne in 527 — not the consort of an emperor but an empress in her own right, with her own court, her own seal, her own diplomatic correspondence. She was central to Justinian's government for the next twenty-one years, until her death from cancer in 548. She protected Monophysite bishops despite her husband's official Chalcedonian policy. She funded a monastery for former prostitutes. She pushed through legislation expanding women's rights — making it harder to divorce a wife without cause, criminalizing forced prostitution, giving women fuller property rights. Whether or not Procopius's biographical details are accurate, the policy record is unambiguous: she used imperial power to protect women in ways no previous Roman empress had.

The single moment that made Theodora a legend came in January 532, in the worst political crisis of Justinian's reign.

The **Nika riots** began as a dispute between the two great chariot-racing factions — the **Blues** and the **Greens**. The **factions** were not just sports teams. They were para-political organizations with their own organized militias, their own neighborhoods, and their own quasi-religious affiliations: Blues were Chalcedonian and aristocratic, Greens were Monophysite and populist. On January 10, 532, the city prefect attempted to hang seven faction members for murder. Two of them — one Blue, one Green — survived the gallows when the rope broke and were sheltered by sympathetic monks. Their factions, normally hostile, united to demand pardons. When Justinian temporized, the unified factions began rioting, sweeping through the city with the chant "*Nika!*" — "Conquer!" — that gave the riot its name.

Within days, half the city center was on fire. The original Hagia Sophia (a smaller wooden-roofed basilica, the second of the name; the first had burned in 404) was destroyed. The Senate House burned. Senators caught up in the chaos elected an alternative emperor — **Hypatius**, a nephew of the late Anastasius — and acclaimed him in the Hippodrome. Justinian, holed up in the imperial palace with Theodora, his generals, and his closest advisers, prepared to flee by ship.

Theodora stopped him. The exact words Procopius records may not be verbatim — these speeches almost never are — but the substance is well attested: she told the council that exile was worse than death, that the imperial purple was a fine shroud, and that she would not run. The line Procopius gives her — "*Royalty is a good burial-shroud*" — is one of the most quoted in Byzantine history. Justinian stayed.

His generals **Belisarius** and **Mundus** then led an armed force into the Hippodrome through the imperial passages, while the eunuch **Narses** (NAR-seez, a court chamberlain who would prove to be one of the most extraordinary military commanders of the age — his story comes back) bribed key Blues to abandon the rioters. The trapped crowd in the Hippodrome was massacred. Procopius reports 30,000 dead, though the number is suspect by ancient standards. Hypatius was executed. The riots ended. Justinian's throne was secure for the next thirty-three years, though every emperor in Constantinople for the next thousand years would remember that the Hippodrome could turn into a deathtrap for an unpopular ruler in a single afternoon.

The destroyed city center now needed to be rebuilt. Justinian seized the opportunity.

---

He started with the law.

By the sixth century, Roman law was a thousand years of accumulated mess — overlapping statutes, contradictory rulings, opinions of long-dead jurists treated as binding precedent, imperial edicts that nobody had bothered to reconcile with each other. Practical lawyers operated by tradition and connection rather than by any orderly code. Justinian commissioned a panel of legal scholars under the jurist **Tribonian** (trih-BOH-nee-an) to fix this once and for all. The result, completed in stages between 529 and 534, was the **Corpus Juris Civilis** — "the Body of Civil Law" — a four-part compilation that has shaped almost every legal system in continental Europe.

The four parts: the **Codex** (a streamlined collection of imperial edicts, replacing all earlier compilations); the **Digest** or **Pandects** (a fifty-book compilation of the writings of the great Roman jurists, condensed and edited to remove contradictions); the **Institutes** (an introductory textbook for first-year law students, still readable, still in print); and the **Novels** (Latin *Novellae*, the new laws Justinian and his successors issued after the original codification was complete).

The cultural impact of the Corpus is hard to overstate. After the Latin West rediscovered the Digest at Bologna around 1070, it became the foundational text for the medieval revival of Roman law — taught in every European law school, cited in every continental court, ancestor of the legal codes of France, Germany, Spain, Italy, the Netherlands, Quebec, Louisiana, Latin America, and Japan. Most modern legal systems outside the English-speaking common-law tradition descend, more or less directly, from a project a Constantinopolitan emperor commissioned to clean up his clerks' filing system. The Corpus is also where the Latin tags every English-speaking lawyer learns come from: *res ipsa loquitur*, *nemo iudex in causa sua*, *audi alteram partem*. Justinian's legal staff did their work in Latin, the language of Roman law, even as the empire around them was switching to Greek for everything else. The Latin text is part of the time capsule.

---

He continued with architecture.

Five days after the Nika riots ended, Justinian ordered the construction of a new **Hagia Sophia** (HAH-yah soh-FEE-ah, "Holy Wisdom" — the cathedral was dedicated not to a saint but to one of the divine attributes of Christ, who is the Wisdom of God in the Greek theological tradition). The architects were two mathematicians — **Anthemius of Tralles** and **Isidore of Miletus** — chosen for their command of geometry rather than their building experience. The construction took five years and ten months. Up to ten thousand workers labored in shifts. Marble was shipped in from quarries across the empire (purple porphyry from Egypt, green marble from Thessaly, golden marble from Numidia). The completed building was dedicated on December 27, 537. According to a legend that may even be true, Justinian walked into the empty cathedral after the ceremony and exclaimed "*Solomon, I have surpassed thee!*" — a comparison to the Jerusalem Temple that became the most quoted line of his reign.

Hagia Sophia is a work of architectural daring that no Roman engineer had ever attempted at this scale. The central feature is a **dome** — but not the kind of dome the Romans had built before, which always sat on a circular drum (like the Pantheon in Rome, Ancient Rome Ch 7). Hagia Sophia's dome sits on a *square* base, which means the architects had to solve a geometric problem: how do you transition from four walls to one circle? Their solution — possibly invented for this building, possibly borrowed from earlier Persian or Syrian experiments — was the **pendentive** (PEN-den-tiv): a triangular concave surface that fills the corner between two walls and curves up into the dome above. The four pendentives smoothly transfer the weight of a circular dome onto a square set of supporting piers. This invention made it possible to put a dome on top of any rectangular space, and it became the standard feature of every later Byzantine, Ottoman, and Russian Orthodox church, from the Süleymaniye in Istanbul to St. Peter's in Rome to St. Basil's in Moscow.

The dome itself spans 102 feet (31 meters) and rises 180 feet (55 meters) above the floor — at the time, the largest enclosed interior space in the world, a record it held for nearly a thousand years until the completion of Seville Cathedral in the early sixteenth century. The original dome partially collapsed in an earthquake in 558 and was rebuilt — slightly higher and steeper — by Isidore the Younger, the original architect's nephew. The interior was clad in marble revetments and gold mosaics that, lit by hundreds of windows around the dome's base, gave the impression that the dome was floating on a ring of light. The Byzantine writer **Procopius**, in his *Buildings* (a panegyric to Justinian's construction projects, the opposite-mood book to his *Secret History*), wrote that the dome appeared "not to rest on solid masonry but to cover the space with its golden dome suspended from heaven."

When the Ottomans took Constantinople in 1453, they kept Hagia Sophia standing and converted it to a mosque, adding minarets and Islamic calligraphic medallions but leaving the structure essentially intact. When the Turkish Republic secularized it in 1934, it became a museum. In 2020 it was reconverted to a mosque. It has been continuously roofed and continuously functional for fifteen hundred years. Almost no other major building in the world can say that.

---

While Hagia Sophia was rising, the empire was also rebuilding the **Hagia Eirene** (eer-EE-nee, "Holy Peace") — another church burned in the Nika riots, dedicated to a different divine attribute, completed in 548. Hagia Eirene survives today as one of the two oldest substantially original Byzantine churches in Istanbul. Justinian's reconstruction of his capital was not just a political flex; it was a wholesale rewriting of the city's monumental skyline, on a scale and to a quality that would not be matched in Europe for nearly a millennium.

And then he turned to the West.

---

In 533, Justinian sent a small army across the Mediterranean to North Africa. Its commander was **Belisarius** (bel-ih-SAR-ee-us, c. 500–565), a young general from Thrace who had already distinguished himself in the eastern wars and during the Nika riots. The target was the **Vandal Kingdom**, the Germanic state that had ruled North Africa since Gaiseric's conquests in the 430s (Ancient Rome Ch 10). The Vandals were Arian Christians who had spent a century persecuting their Catholic subjects, taxing them heavily, and getting fat on the wealth of the African provinces. They were also, by the 530s, militarily soft — a small ruling caste of Germanic warriors whose grandsons preferred chariot races and country villas to actual fighting.

Belisarius landed with about 15,000 men, marched on Carthage, and won two pitched battles in three months. The Vandal king Gelimer fled into the Atlas Mountains and surrendered the following year. The entire Vandal kingdom — North Africa, the western Mediterranean islands (Corsica, Sardinia, the Balearics) — was Roman again. Belisarius returned to Constantinople and was given a triumph — the first triumph since Augustus's day (Ancient Rome Ch 6) for a non-imperial general — riding a chariot to the Hippodrome at the head of his troops with the captured Gelimer walking before him. The North African campaign had taken less than a year.

Justinian, encouraged, ordered Italy next.

This was a much harder target. The **Ostrogothic Kingdom** (OS-troh-goth-ic, "Eastern Goths") had ruled Italy since 493 under their king **Theodoric the Great**, who had governed competently, kept the Roman institutions running, employed Roman bureaucrats (including the philosopher **Boethius**, beh-OH-thee-us, who wrote the *Consolation of Philosophy* in prison while awaiting execution by Theodoric for treason — Boethius's book is one of the most important works of medieval philosophy and one of the last great Latin texts of antiquity), and kept his own Gothic warriors out of the locals' way. By the 530s Theodoric was dead, his successors were weaker, and Justinian saw an opportunity.

Belisarius landed in Sicily in 535, took the island in months, crossed to the mainland, and captured **Naples** and then **Rome itself** in late 536. He held Rome through a year-long Gothic siege in 537–538 — a brilliant defensive campaign in which a much smaller Roman force pinned the Goths against the Aurelian Walls and starved them into withdrawal. He then advanced north and took **Ravenna**, the Ostrogothic capital, in 540, capturing the king Vitiges and most of his court. It looked like the reconquest was done.

It wasn't. The Goths, having lost a king, immediately raised another — **Totila**, a brilliant cavalry commander who reorganized Gothic resistance and turned what should have been a mopping-up operation into a twelve-year nightmare. Belisarius was recalled to Constantinople and sent east to fight the Persians (the Sasanians under **Khosrow I** had taken advantage of the western distraction to invade Syria in 540, sacking Antioch and dragging its population into Mesopotamia — a Sasanian-Roman war that would consume the empire's eastern attention for the next decade). Justinian, paranoid about Belisarius's popularity and possible imperial ambitions, kept his most capable general on a short leash for years afterward, depriving him of the resources he needed to actually finish either war.

The Italian war ground on. By the late 540s Totila had recovered most of Italy, including Rome itself (which he took, lost, and took again). Belisarius was eventually returned to Italy with a tiny force and told to manage. He did what he could. By 552, after Belisarius had again been recalled, Justinian sent the elderly eunuch **Narses** — the same court chamberlain who had bribed the Blues during the Nika riots — to finish the job. Narses was around 75 years old, had no formal military training, and turned out to be one of the most gifted generals of late antiquity. He destroyed Totila's army at the **Battle of Taginae** in 552 and the Gothic remnant under Totila's successor at the **Battle of Mons Lactarius** in 553. The Ostrogothic Kingdom was extinguished. Italy was Roman again.

The reconquest had taken nineteen years instead of the projected one or two. It had cost more than the empire could afford. And the Italy that Narses delivered to Justinian was a smoking ruin. Rome itself had been depopulated — its population, reportedly half a million in the early imperial period, had crashed below 50,000 by the mid-sixth century, with whole neighborhoods reverting to pasture inside the Aurelian Walls. The senate had ceased to meet. The aqueducts had been cut by besiegers and never properly repaired. The cultural life that had survived the Gothic kingdoms collapsed under the weight of the war that was supposed to restore it.

Justinian also seized the southeastern corner of **Spain** from the Visigoths in 554, creating the province of **Spania** — a strip of coast from Cartagena to Cadiz that Byzantium would hold for about seventy years before the Visigoths recovered it. Briefly, the Mediterranean was again a Roman lake. Briefly.

---

The bill came due in two installments.

The first was the **Plague of Justinian**, which arrived in Constantinople in 541 and killed an estimated quarter of the eastern Mediterranean's population in successive waves over the next two centuries. The disease — almost certainly **bubonic plague** caused by *Yersinia pestis*, the same pathogen as the later Black Death — apparently originated in central Asia or the Horn of Africa, traveled along the grain ships from Egypt to Constantinople, and from there spread to every Mediterranean port. Procopius, who lived through the first wave, wrote a clinical description in his *Wars* that medical historians still recognize: bubonic swellings in the groin and armpits, sudden fevers, vomiting blood, hallucinations, death within days. He estimated 10,000 deaths a day at the peak in Constantinople. Modern estimates put the total mortality of the first wave at 25–40% of the city's population, and the cumulative effect of the subsequent waves at around 50% of the eastern Mediterranean's population over two centuries.

The economic damage was permanent. Tax revenues collapsed. Cities shrank. The army could no longer be filled with native recruits and increasingly relied on barbarian foederati. Justinian himself caught plague in 542 and recovered. The empire as a whole did not.

The second installment was the **Three Chapters Controversy** of 553. The "three chapters" were three sets of theological writings — by Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrrhus, and Ibas of Edessa, three Syrian bishops dead more than a century — that the Monophysites considered tainted with Nestorianism (the doctrine, condemned in 431, that Christ's divine and human natures were almost two separate persons). Justinian, still trying to patch the Chalcedonian schism that was alienating Egypt and Syria, convened the **Fifth Ecumenical Council** at Constantinople and pressured it into condemning these three long-dead theologians — a gesture meant to placate the Monophysites without formally repudiating Chalcedon. The gesture failed in both directions. The Monophysites were not satisfied (they wanted Chalcedon repudiated outright). The western church was furious (it considered the condemnation of long-dead theologians a violation of due process). Pope **Vigilius** was effectively kidnapped to Constantinople and bullied into signing off, and his successors spent decades trying to walk it back. The schism between Constantinople and Rome was not formally renewed, but the Three Chapters mess deepened the western perception that the Eastern emperors meddled in church matters in ways no proper Christian ruler should.

---

One last small story from Justinian's reign deserves mention because of the chain of consequences it set off.

Around 552 — the same year Narses was finishing off the Ostrogoths — two **Nestorian Christian monks** arrived in Constantinople from the East. Nestorians (NES-tor-ee-anz) were yet another wing of Christianity, distinct from Chalcedonians and Monophysites. Condemned at the Council of Ephesus in 431, they had spread eastward into Sasanian Persia and beyond. They had been living in **China** — Procopius calls the source country *Serinda*, "the country of the Seres," meaning the silk-producing land — and they reported that they could break the Chinese monopoly on silk production. China had kept the secret of silk for over two thousand years (Ancient China Ch 5; the silkworm and the mulberry-leaf process were a state secret enforced by capital punishment). The monks claimed they could smuggle the eggs out and start production inside the empire.

Justinian financed them. They returned with silkworm eggs concealed in the hollow chambers of bamboo walking sticks. The eggs were brought to Constantinople, hatched, and bred. Within a generation Byzantine **silk production** was a state-controlled industry, eventually centered on Thebes in Greece and Constantinople itself. The Byzantine state imposed a strict monopoly: silk dyed in imperial purple could only be made in the imperial workshops and could only be worn by the emperor and senior officials. (This is also where the modern sense of "royal purple" comes from: the dye, a deep red-purple known as **Tyrian purple**, was extracted from the murex sea-snail in a process so labor-intensive that an ounce of dye required tens of thousands of snails. The Byzantines preserved the Phoenician craft (Phoenicia Ch 4) and made it the marker of imperial dignity for nine hundred years.)

The Chinese silk monopoly was broken. The Silk Road shifted. The Byzantines became the silk merchants of the Mediterranean for the next five hundred years. And it all happened because two anonymous monks walked into the imperial palace in 552 with hollow walking sticks and a proposition. The Byzantines had a gift for these moments — for converting a chance arrival into a state industry, for taking an institution they did not invent and running it better than the inventors had. Silk is a small story by the standards of Justinian's reign. It is also a Justinianic story in miniature: Roman ambition meeting Greek cleverness, financed by an emperor who could not stop pulling at every thread he encountered.

---

Justinian died in 565, after thirty-eight years on the throne. He had reconquered North Africa, Italy, and southern Spain. He had codified the law. He had built Hagia Sophia. He had survived a riot that should have killed him and a plague that did kill half his subjects. He had also bankrupted the treasury, bled the army, and left behind territorial commitments his successors could not meet. Within a generation, the Lombards (yet another Germanic people) would invade Italy and seize most of what Belisarius and Narses had reconquered. Within a century, the Sasanian Persians and then the Arabs would strip Egypt and Syria from the empire entirely. Most of what Justinian achieved did not last.

But Hagia Sophia is still there. The Corpus Juris Civilis is still there. The pendentive dome is still there in every major Byzantine, Ottoman, and Russian church. The silk industry he founded survived as long as the empire did. He overreached spectacularly, and even what he overreached for left foundations the empire would build on for nine more centuries.

When his successor **Justin II** (his nephew, ruled 565–578) opened the imperial treasury after his uncle's death, he reportedly found it empty. The bill for restoring the Roman Empire had come due, and Justinian's heirs were going to pay it.

---

# Chapter 3 — The Heraclian Convulsion

The fifty years after Justinian's death were an exercise in trying to hold on.

His successors — **Justin II** (565–578), **Tiberius II Constantine** (578–582), **Maurice** (mor-EES, 582–602) — were competent men handed a fiscally exhausted empire and an overextended frontier. They lost most of Italy to the Lombards (lom-BARDS, a Germanic people who invaded in 568 — within four years they held everything north of Rome and Ravenna, and within a century the only Byzantine holdouts in Italy were the Exarchate of Ravenna, the south, and Sicily). They lost ground to the **Avars** and **Slavs** in the Balkans. They held the eastern frontier against the Sasanians at enormous cost.

Maurice, the longest-reigning of the three, was a soldier-emperor of real ability. He intervened successfully in a Sasanian civil war in 591, restoring the legitimate king **Khosrow II** to the Persian throne in exchange for territorial concessions in Mesopotamia and Armenia — a diplomatic coup that gave Byzantium two decades of peace on the eastern frontier. He wrote a military manual called the **Strategikon**, a practical handbook on how to run a Roman army that survives and is still readable. He also tried to fix the empire's fiscal problem the only way that mathematically worked: cutting army pay.

This was a mistake. In 602, the Danube army mutinied over a winter campaign Maurice had ordered, refused to obey a recall, marched on Constantinople, and acclaimed a centurion named **Phocas** (FOH-kus) emperor. Maurice, his wife, and his five sons were captured and executed — the sons forced to watch their younger brothers die before their own turn came. It was the first violent overthrow of a Byzantine emperor in two centuries, and it began a pattern of palace-coup instability that would haunt the empire for the next four hundred years.

Phocas was a disaster. He had no political experience and no clear constituency. He purged the senatorial aristocracy. He let the Danube frontier collapse. He provoked Khosrow II — who had been a personal friend of Maurice and had sworn vengeance for his murder — into invading the eastern provinces in 603. Within five years the Sasanians were across the Euphrates. Within a decade they had taken Antioch (611), Damascus (613), and Jerusalem (614, sacking it and carrying off the **True Cross** that Helena had reportedly discovered three centuries earlier — Chapter 1). By 619 the Sasanians had completed the conquest of Egypt. The empire was reduced to Anatolia, the southern Balkans, and a handful of Italian holdings, and even those were under continuous Avar and Slav pressure.

In 610, before the worst of these losses, the Exarch of Carthage's son **Heraclius** (heh-rah-KLEE-us) sailed east with the African fleet, took Constantinople, and overthrew Phocas. The mob lynched the deposed emperor on the steps of the imperial palace. Heraclius was acclaimed in the Hippodrome and inherited an empire that was, by any objective accounting, finished.

He did not act like it.

---

Heraclius's first move was to switch the empire's official language from Latin to Greek.

This sounds like a footnote and was actually one of the most consequential administrative decisions in the empire's history. The Eastern Roman Empire had been functionally Greek-speaking for centuries — Greek was the everyday language of the streets of Constantinople, of the merchants of Antioch, of the theologians of Alexandria, of the army's enlisted ranks. But the official language of the imperial chancery, of the law, of the highest court rituals had remained Latin, the language Constantine had brought with him in 330. Heraclius, around 620, formalized what had already been true on the ground: the empire was a Greek state. Imperial documents were now to be issued in Greek. The emperor's title shifted from *Imperator* to *Basileus* (BASS-ih-lyoos, the Greek word for "king," previously used only of foreign monarchs but now claimed by the Roman emperor as his own). The legal codes would still be written in Latin (Justinian had codified them less than a century earlier, and the Latin texts were not going to be retranslated en masse), but everything new was in Greek.

This is the line modern historians sometimes use to mark the actual transition from "Roman Empire" to "Byzantine Empire." The Byzantines themselves did not see it that way, but it was a real change — by the eighth century, knowledge of Latin in Constantinople was a specialist skill, the way knowledge of classical Greek is in modern English-speaking universities. The two halves of Christendom were now also two halves of language, and theological disputes were increasingly carried out by people who could not read each other's documents in the original.

---

In 622, twelve years into his reign, Heraclius did the thing that nobody had expected him to be able to do.

He counterattacked.

The Sasanian war by this point had been running for nearly two decades. The Persians held everything from Armenia to the Egyptian desert. They had besieged Constantinople in 626 (in coordination with an Avar attack from the north — the city was saved by the Theodosian Walls, by Greek fire on the Persian fleet across the Bosporus, and by a Marian intercession that Constantinopolitans credited to a citywide procession of icons, which gave the **Marian veneration** central to Eastern Orthodoxy a particular boost). Heraclius's response was unconventional. Rather than defend the eastern provinces in detail — which the empire could no longer afford — he embarked on a series of audacious counter-offensives, taking the war directly into the Persian heartland.

He sailed from Constantinople with an army in 622, landed in northwestern Anatolia, and crossed into Armenia. Over the next six years he maneuvered through the Caucasus, Azerbaijan, and Mesopotamia, alternately threatening Khosrow's western capital at **Ctesiphon** (TES-ih-fon, on the Tigris near modern Baghdad — Persian Empire Ch 9) and burning Sasanian shrines. He allied with the **Western Turks** (a steppe confederation — a tactical innovation that Byzantine and Sasanian rulers had previously disdained) and with the **Khazars**. He recovered Armenia. He pushed into Azerbaijan and burned the great Zoroastrian fire-temple at **Adur Gushnasp**, retaliating for the Persian sack of Jerusalem.

The decisive moment came on December 12, 627, at the **Battle of Nineveh** — fought, in a coincidence that no Byzantine could have missed, at the same site as the great Mesopotamian capital that the Medes and Babylonians had destroyed twelve hundred years earlier (Mesopotamia Ch 12). Heraclius personally led the Roman cavalry, killed three Persian commanders in single combat (according to the Byzantine sources — these accounts are exactly as reliable as you would expect), and routed the main Sasanian field army. Khosrow II was overthrown by his own nobles a few months later, executed, and replaced by his son, who promptly sued for peace. The Sasanians evacuated all the conquered provinces, including Egypt. The True Cross was returned.

In March 630, Heraclius rode into Jerusalem in the most theatrical religious pageant of late antiquity, personally carrying the recovered cross of the Crucifixion to the **Church of the Holy Sepulchre** — the church Constantine's mother Helena had built three centuries earlier on the supposed site of the Resurrection. The Byzantine sources describe him entering the city barefoot, in a hair shirt, surrounded by chanting clergy. The triumph was the high point of his reign and one of the most celebrated moments in Byzantine history, commemorated annually thereafter as the Feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross — a feast still observed in the Eastern Orthodox calendar.

It was also, in retrospect, the moment of maximum overreach. The empire had won a twenty-five-year war, recovered all its territory, restored its most precious relic, and humiliated its eternal rival. Both Byzantium and Sasanian Persia were exhausted by the effort. Their armies were depleted. Their treasuries were empty. Their populations were demoralized by decades of war and plague (the **Plague of Justinian** had returned in additional waves throughout the early seventh century). The two great empires of the ancient world had spent their reserves fighting each other, and neither had any cushion left for what came next.

What came next arrived from Arabia.

---

Six years after Heraclius's triumph in Jerusalem, an Arab army defeated the main Roman field army at the **Battle of Yarmouk** (yar-MOOK, August 636 — fought along the Yarmouk River near the modern Syria-Jordan border). The Arab commander **Khalid ibn al-Walid** (KAH-lid IH-bin al-wah-LEED, the most gifted general the early Arab armies produced — possibly the most gifted general the seventh century produced anywhere) lured the Roman force into a battle in difficult terrain, exploited a sandstorm, and shattered an army that on paper outnumbered his perhaps two to one. The Roman commander, an Armenian eunuch named **Vahan**, was killed or captured. The defeat ended Roman power in Syria. Within two years the Arabs had taken Damascus (a second time, this time permanently), Antioch (whose fall ended six centuries of Roman rule in the city Augustus had governed personally), and Jerusalem (which surrendered to the Caliph **Umar** in 637, the holy city changing hands for the second time in a generation).

Egypt fell next. The Arab general **Amr ibn al-As** invaded in 639 with a small force, took **Babylon Fortress** at the apex of the Nile delta in 641, took **Alexandria** itself in 642, and within a few more years had completed the conquest of the empire's wealthiest province. The Coptic Christians of Egypt, who had been Monophysites under heavy Constantinopolitan pressure for two centuries (Chapter 1), did not resist the Arab takeover with any enthusiasm. Their bishops negotiated terms. The Greek-speaking ruling class largely fled. The Arabic-speaking Muslim minority who took over taxation and high administration found that the existing Coptic-speaking bureaucracy continued to function smoothly under new management. Egypt would never again be Roman.

The pattern was the same in Syria. The local population, Monophysite or Jewish or unaligned, had no particular reason to fight for an empire that had spent the past two centuries persecuting their preferred theology and taxing them mercilessly. The Arab invaders offered lower taxes and freedom of worship for non-Muslims who paid the **jizya** (a head tax on non-Muslim subjects, lower than the Byzantine taxes the same populations had been paying). They were welcomed in many places. The Christological hairsplitting that had dominated imperial politics for three centuries had finally produced its political consequence: the empire had alienated half its tax base, and when an outside power offered better terms, the alienated half went over.

By the time Heraclius died in 641 — broken in mind and body, his last years marked by erratic behavior, religious experimentation, and an incestuous second marriage to his niece Martina — the empire had lost everything he had recovered. The True Cross was evacuated to Constantinople before Jerusalem fell again. The recovered provinces were gone. The empire was reduced to Anatolia, parts of the southern Balkans, the Italian holdings, and the great city itself.

But Heraclius had bought the empire something more important than territory. He had bought it a reorganization.

---

The **theme system** (THEEM-uh, from Greek *thema*, meaning "a placement" but used in Byzantine administration for both a military district and the army stationed in it) is the institutional adaptation that allowed the empire to survive what would otherwise have been a fatal loss of territory and revenue.

The traditional Roman administrative system separated military and civilian power. A province had a civilian governor for taxes and law and a military commander (a *dux*, the word that gives English "duke") for the army. The army was a salaried professional force, paid in cash from central revenues, equipped by central workshops, deployed wherever needed.

The seventh-century crisis broke that system. Tax revenues from Egypt and Syria were gone. The empire could not pay a salaried professional army out of what was left. So it improvised.

The **themes** were large military districts in Anatolia, each commanded by a **strategos** (strah-tay-GOSS, "general"), who was simultaneously the military commander and the civilian governor of his district. The soldiers stationed in each theme — instead of being paid in cash they could not receive — were granted **military lands** (*stratiotika ktemata*) in exchange for hereditary military service. A soldier got a farm. His son inherited the farm and the military obligation. The army became a force of citizen-soldier farmers who supplied their own equipment, fed themselves from their land, and reported to the strategos when called up. The state paid only a small cash supplement and provided weapons in time of war.

The system began informally under Heraclius — historians used to date it precisely to 640, but the current scholarly consensus is that it evolved gradually from emergency expedients in the 620s and 630s and was not really systematized until Constans II's reign in the mid-seventh century. By the end of the seventh century there were **four themes** covering Anatolia: the Anatolikon (the largest, in central Anatolia), the Armeniakon (in the northeast), the Opsikion (around Constantinople), and the Thrakesion (along the Aegean coast). Each was simultaneously a province, an army, and a tax-collecting unit.

The theme system did three things at once. It saved the army, by paying it in land instead of cash. It saved Anatolia, by giving the local peasantry a stake in defending it. And it produced, eventually, a new aristocracy — the strategoi and their officer corps, who would gradually consolidate large estates from the theme lands and become the **military aristocracy** that would dominate Byzantine politics from the eighth century onward.

It also did something less measurable. It made the empire smaller and tougher. The empire that emerged from the seventh-century crisis had given up its ambition to control the southern Mediterranean. It was now a Greek-speaking, Anatolia-centered state with its capital at Constantinople and its religious identity defined by Chalcedonian Orthodoxy. The Monophysite provinces were gone. The Latin West was a different country. The empire had finally become what posterity would call Byzantium — and it would survive in this form for another eight hundred years.

---

A small theological epilogue closes the chapter.

Heraclius and his successors made one last desperate attempt to mend the Chalcedonian schism that had cost them Egypt and Syria. The compromise was called **Monothelitism** (mon-oh-THEL-it-iz-um, "single-will-ism") — the doctrine that Christ had two natures (preserving Chalcedon) but a single will (the unity that the Monophysites wanted). It was an elegant attempt to thread a needle, and like Zeno's Henotikon two centuries earlier, it satisfied no one. Rome rejected it. The Monophysites had already been lost to the Arab conquests and no longer cared what Constantinople taught. The Greek east was divided.

The fight was carried by an unlikely figure: **Maximos the Confessor** (mak-SEE-mos, c. 580–662), a former imperial secretary turned monk who became the leading theologian of his age. Maximos rejected Monothelitism, was tried for treason in Constantinople in 654, refused to recant, was tortured (his tongue and right hand were cut off — these being the instruments of his offense, since he had spoken and written against imperial policy), and exiled to the Caucasus, where he died in 662. Nineteen years later, the **Sixth Ecumenical Council** of 680–681 formally repudiated Monothelitism, vindicated Maximos posthumously, and even **anathematized a sitting pope** — Honorius I, who had endorsed Monothelitism in correspondence — in a ruling that would haunt later Catholic theology of papal infallibility.

The lesson of the seventh century, written in the bodies of Maximos and the lost provinces, was that Byzantine theology was no longer a tool for managing the empire's eastern populations. The eastern populations were gone. Theology was now a question of how the empire's *remaining* population — Greek, Chalcedonian, Anatolian — defined itself. The answer would shape everything that followed.


---

# Chapter 4 — The Iconoclast Century

The empire that emerged from the seventh century was a smaller, harder, more religious thing than the one that had entered it. The lost southern provinces had been the empire's wealthiest tax base and its most heterodox population. What remained — Anatolia, Thrace, Greece, the Italian holdings — was overwhelmingly Greek-speaking, Chalcedonian, and accustomed to siege.

It was also under continuous attack. The **Umayyad Caliphate** (oo-MY-ad, the first hereditary Arab dynasty, ruling from Damascus from 661 onward), having absorbed Syria, Egypt, Persia, and most of North Africa within thirty years of Muhammad's death in 632, now considered Byzantium the next obvious conquest. Two great sieges of Constantinople defined the late seventh and early eighth centuries.

The **First Arab Siege of Constantinople** ran from 674 to 678 — a four-year naval blockade in which Umayyad fleets repeatedly attempted to force the Bosporus. They were defeated by an invention that would protect the empire for the next four hundred years: **Greek fire**.

Greek fire was the most feared weapon in the medieval Mediterranean. The traditional account credits its invention to a Syrian Greek refugee named **Kallinikos** of Heliopolis, who arrived in Constantinople around 672 carrying the secret. The exact composition was a state secret so closely guarded that no ancient source preserves the formula in full — it was lost when the empire fell, and the recipes circulated in modern histories are scholarly guesses rather than documented fact. The leading reconstructions propose some combination of crude petroleum (skimmed from natural seeps in the Caucasus), pine resin, sulfur, and quicklime, heated in a copper container and pumped through a bronze siphon onto enemy ships. The mixture ignited on contact with seawater (the quicklime reaction), continued burning on the surface of the water, and could not be extinguished by ordinary means. Imagine a flamethrower mounted on a warship, projecting a stream of burning liquid that floated on the sea and stuck to whatever it touched. The Arab fleet of 678 was destroyed by it. The fleet of 717–718 was destroyed by it. Russian fleets in 941 were destroyed by it. The Greek fire monopoly was a strategic asset comparable to any nuclear deterrent of the modern age, and the Byzantines guarded it accordingly: the formula was held by a single family, the manufacturing was done in a single workshop in the imperial palace, and the operators were sworn to secrecy on pain of death.

The **Second Arab Siege**, in 717–718, was the empire's near-death experience.

The Umayyad caliph Sulayman sent his brother Maslama with an army of perhaps 80,000 men and a fleet of 1,800 ships to take Constantinople once and for all. The Byzantine emperor at the moment of crisis was **Leo III** (called "the Isaurian," from the rugged southeastern Anatolian region of Isauria where his family came from — though "Isaurian" may be a later Byzantine simplification, and his family appears to have come originally from northern Syria). Leo III had taken the throne in March 717 in a palace coup against the previous emperor, who had panicked at the approaching Arab armies. Leo had less than five months to prepare his city.

The Theodosian Walls did most of the work. The Arabs could not breach them and could not storm them. The siege ground on through the unusually severe winter of 717–718, during which the Arab army camped in tents on the Thracian plain froze and starved by the thousand. A Bulgarian army arrived from the north — bribed by Leo to attack the Arab rear — and inflicted further losses. The Arab supply fleet, manned partly by Egyptian Christian conscripts who deserted to the Byzantines on contact, was destroyed by Greek fire in successive engagements. By August 718 the surviving Arab forces lifted the siege and retreated. Of the 1,800 ships that had sailed against Constantinople, only five returned to the Caliphate. The siege was the largest pre-modern military operation ever launched against the city, and it had ended in catastrophic Arab defeat.

The historical consequence is hard to overstate. If Constantinople had fallen in 718, the Arab armies would have had a clear path into Europe via the Balkans, and the entire trajectory of European history might have looked different. As it happened, the failure of the second siege effectively halted Arab expansion into Europe by sea. The Caliphate would never again attempt an assault on Constantinople. Fourteen years later, on the other side of the Mediterranean, the Frankish ruler **Charles Martel** would defeat an Umayyad raid in southern France at the **Battle of Tours**, which is the moment European textbooks usually credit for stopping the Arab advance into western Europe. Tours mattered, but Constantinople in 718 mattered more.

---

Leo III, having saved the empire, decided to break it.

In 726 — the date is approximate; the sources are Iconoclast-hostile and the chronology is contested — Leo issued an edict against the use of religious images in Christian worship. Specifically, he ordered the removal of the famous icon of Christ above the Chalke Gate of the imperial palace and its replacement with a plain cross. This single act started a controversy that would consume the empire for the next 117 years and that would permanently alter the relationship between the Byzantine state and the Roman papacy.

To understand why, you need to understand what icons were in eighth-century Christian practice.

An **icon** (from Greek *eikon*, "image") was a painted image of Christ, the Virgin Mary, or a saint, used as an aid to prayer and veneration. Icons had been a central feature of Eastern Christian piety for centuries — every Byzantine church was full of them, every Byzantine home had its private icons, every Byzantine peasant had a personal relationship with the painted figure of his or her favorite saint. People kissed them, lit candles before them, addressed prayers to them. Some icons were believed to be miraculous (the *Hodegetria*, an image of the Virgin reportedly painted from life by Saint Luke himself, was carried in procession around Constantinople's walls during the 626 Avar siege and was credited with the city's deliverance). Some were even believed to have appeared without human hands (the *acheiropoieta*, "not-made-by-hands," like the Image of Edessa, a cloth that supposedly bore the imprint of Christ's face).

The theological argument against icons drew on the Old Testament prohibition of graven images (Exodus 20:4) and the Islamic example: Islam's stark prohibition of religious images had given the Caliphate an austere visual culture that some Byzantines openly admired. The argument was also political. Leo III believed — or argued — that the empire's recent military disasters were divine punishment for the idolatrous worship of images. Stripping the empire of icons would restore divine favor and military success. There was a class element too: the icon-veneration tradition was strongest among ordinary people and the urban populace, while the iconoclast movement found its core support among the Anatolian military aristocracy and the army itself, both of which had been shaped by long contact with Islamic neighbors.

The argument *for* icons drew on the Incarnation: Christ had become a human being with a face, and to forbid the depiction of that face was to deny the reality of his humanity. The leading defender was **John of Damascus** (yo-HAN of dah-MASS-kus, c. 676–749), who is one of the most important figures of the period and one of the great ironies of the iconoclast controversy. John was a Christian priest and theologian whose family served as administrators for the Umayyad caliph in Damascus — meaning that the most influential defense of Byzantine icon veneration was written by a Christian living safely under Muslim rule, beyond the reach of the Byzantine emperor he was theologically opposing. John's *Three Treatises on the Divine Images* laid out the careful theological distinction that would eventually win: a distinction between **veneration** (*proskynesis*, the honor due to a saint or to Christ depicted in an icon) and **worship** (*latreia*, the absolute adoration due to God alone). One could legitimately venerate the image of Christ, John argued, because the honor passed through the image to its prototype. One could not worship the image, because the image was not God. This distinction is still official Catholic and Orthodox doctrine.

---

Leo III's son **Constantine V** (ruled 741–775, called "Kopronymos" — KOP-roh-NEE-mos, "name of dung," by his enemies, who claimed he had defecated in the baptismal font during his christening; this is a polemical slur, almost certainly invented by his iconodule biographers) was a more militant iconoclast than his father. He hated monks — monks were the strongest icon defenders, and the great monasteries were the major centers of icon production. He persecuted them brutally. He convened the **Council of Hieria** (hye-ER-ee-uh) in 754, which assembled 338 bishops and condemned icon veneration as idolatry. The council declared itself the Seventh Ecumenical Council, claiming to settle the question definitively.

It did not. The papacy — Pope Stephen II in 753 — had already broken with Constantinople over the iconoclast question, an act that had enormous political consequences. Stephen could no longer rely on the Byzantine emperor for protection against the Lombards in Italy. He turned instead to the **Franks**, traveling personally to the court of **Pepin the Short** in 754 and securing a Frankish military alliance. Pepin invaded Italy, defeated the Lombards, and donated the conquered territory in central Italy to the papacy, creating the **Papal States** that would form the temporal power of the Pope for the next eleven centuries.

Pepin's son **Charlemagne** would extend this arrangement. On Christmas Day 800, Pope Leo III (a different Leo III — not the iconoclast emperor, but the contemporary Pope, a confusion the careful reader will need to track for the next sentence) crowned Charlemagne **Imperator Romanorum** in Saint Peter's Basilica, creating the **Holy Roman Empire** as a Latin Christian counterweight to Constantinople. The Byzantine reaction was outrage: the Romans had a Roman emperor, in Constantinople, and there was only one of him. The Frankish empire, in Byzantine eyes, was a usurpation. But the practical reality was that the western half of Christendom now had its own emperor, its own pope-emperor relationship, and its own institutional independence from Constantinople. The schism of 1054 (Chapter 7) was already implicit in 800.

The iconoclast controversy, in other words, did not just split the Byzantine church. It split Christendom into the eastern and western halves that have remained divided ever since.

---

The first iconoclast wave was reversed in 787 by an unlikely figure: **Empress Irene** (eye-REE-nee, formally Irene of Athens, c. 752–803).

Irene had married Constantine V's son Leo IV in 769. When Leo IV died in 780, she became regent for their nine-year-old son Constantine VI. She was a committed iconodule (icon-supporter), and within seven years she had engineered the reversal of imperial policy. The **Second Council of Nicaea** in 787 — actually convened twice, after a first attempt in 786 was broken up by iconoclast soldiers — restored icon veneration on the basis of John of Damascus's distinction between veneration and worship. The Council is recognized as the **Seventh Ecumenical Council** by both the Catholic and Orthodox churches today, replacing Hieria 754 in the official sequence.

Constantine VI grew up under his mother's domination, attempted to assert his independence, was repeatedly outmaneuvered by Irene, and was finally arrested, blinded, and deposed by his mother in 797 — in the same room of the Sacred Palace where she had given birth to him twenty-six years earlier. Irene then ruled in her own right as **Basileus** (the masculine form — she insisted on the male title, signing imperial documents as "Irene, the great basileus and autocrator of the Romans") from 797 to 802. She is the first woman to rule the Byzantine Empire as sole monarch — a status the western papacy treated as so anomalous that Pope Leo III used the alleged "vacancy" of the imperial throne (because no emperor was a woman, in the western view) as part of the justification for crowning Charlemagne in 800.

Irene's reign ended in a coup in 802. She was exiled to Lesbos, where she died the following year. The Eastern Orthodox Church, which had benefited from her restoration of icons, canonized her as a saint despite her having blinded her own son. Saints in the Byzantine tradition are not required to be morally unblemished. They are required to have done the right thing on a sufficiently important question.

---

The second iconoclast wave began in 815 under Emperor **Leo V the Armenian** (ruled 813–820, an iconoclast military emperor of Armenian origin who restored iconoclasm at the **Synod of Constantinople** in 815). It continued under his successors **Michael II** and **Theophilos** (theh-OH-fih-los, ruled 829–842) — Theophilos being one of the most attractive personalities of the iconoclast period: a learned, aesthetically sophisticated emperor who hated icons but loved court ceremony, mathematics, and Arabic-influenced palace architecture. He commissioned the scholar **Leo the Mathematician** (c. 790–870) to design **mechanical automata** for the imperial throne room — golden lions that roared and lashed their tails when foreign envoys approached, mechanical birds that sang from a golden tree, a throne that rose toward the ceiling when the emperor was seated upon it. These were not just luxuries; they were a Byzantine version of statecraft, designed to stun ambassadors with the technological superiority of the empire and to make foreign visitors uncertain whether they were being received by a man or a god. Bishop Liutprand of Cremona, visiting Constantinople in 949, described them in detail and was duly impressed; the Byzantine writer Constantine VII Porphyrogennetos (Chapter 5) recorded their use as part of imperial protocol. Leo the Mathematician also designed an optical telegraph system that could transmit messages from the eastern frontier to Constantinople in under an hour using synchronized fire-beacons. The iconoclast court was many things, but it was not unsophisticated.

The chief intellectual defender of icons during the second iconoclast wave was **Theodore the Studite** (THEE-oh-dor, 759–826), abbot of the **Stoudios Monastery** in Constantinople and one of the great monastic reformers of the medieval world. Theodore wrote prolifically against iconoclasm, was repeatedly imprisoned and exiled for his opposition, and rebuilt the Stoudios Monastery into the most influential religious community in the empire. The **Stoudite Rule** he composed for his monks — strict, communal, intellectually serious — became the template for monasticism throughout the Byzantine and Slavic worlds, and many of the monasteries of Mount Athos still follow it in modified form today.

Theophilos died in 842, leaving his three-year-old son Michael III on the throne. The regent was Theophilos's widow **Empress Theodora** (a different Theodora — not Justinian's wife, who had died three centuries earlier; the Byzantines reused names mercilessly, and a reader needs to track each separately). This second Theodora had been a secret iconodule throughout her husband's reign — she reportedly hid her icons from Theophilos behind a mirror — and on his death she immediately set about restoring icon veneration. The formal restoration came on the first Sunday of Lent, March 11, 843, in a great procession in Constantinople that has been celebrated ever since as the **Triumph of Orthodoxy**, the **Sunday of Orthodoxy**. It is still the major liturgical feast of the first Sunday of Lent in every Eastern Orthodox church, marking the date the iconoclast crisis ended and the icon-rich worship of contemporary Orthodoxy was definitively established.

---

Why did the iconoclast controversy matter? Why did the Byzantine state spend more than a century tearing itself apart over the question of whether painted images of saints could be venerated?

Three answers, none of them complete on its own.

First, the controversy was about the relationship between **divinity and matter**. The iconoclasts believed that the divine could not be represented in matter without compromising its transcendence. The iconodules believed that the Incarnation — God taking on human flesh — had sanctified matter and made the depiction of Christ not just permissible but theologically required. The argument was, at its core, about whether God could be touched.

Second, the controversy was about the **balance of power between the state and the church**. The iconoclast emperors were trying to assert imperial authority over religious doctrine — to make the emperor's theological judgment binding on the church. The iconodules, including monks like Theodore the Studite and theologians like John of Damascus, were defending the church's independent authority on matters of doctrine. The iconodule victory was, among other things, a victory for the principle that the emperor did not have unilateral theological authority. The institutional consequences — a more independent patriarchate, a stronger monastic movement, a more distinct division between sacred and secular spheres — were lasting.

Third, and most consequentially, the controversy split Christendom permanently. The iconoclast period drove the papacy into Frankish arms, created the Holy Roman Empire as a rival institution, and accelerated the linguistic, theological, and institutional divergence between the Greek East and Latin West. The Byzantines emerged from the iconoclast period with a richer, more developed icon-centered piety than they had entered it with — and with a Latin neighbor who increasingly considered them schismatic foreigners rather than fellow Romans.

The empire that came out of 843 was, however, ready for its golden age.

---

# Chapter 5 — The Macedonian Renaissance

The years between 843 and 1025 are the closest thing to a golden age that the Byzantine Empire ever experienced after Justinian. The icons were restored. The Arab armies had been pushed back. The frontier was stabilizing. The economy was recovering. And the imperial throne was occupied, more or less continuously, by a remarkable succession of capable rulers from a single dynasty that had taken its name from the Macedonian theme of northern Greece (modern Greek Macedonia, not the ancient Macedonia of Alexander the Great, though the geographic overlap was considerable).

The dynasty's founder was, in some respects, an unlikely candidate. **Basil I** (called "the Macedonian," ruled 867–886) was an Armenian peasant who had been brought to Constantinople as a teenager, found work as a stable groom, and risen — through his startling physical strength, his good looks, and his willingness to do dirty work — to become the trusted companion of Emperor **Michael III** (the same Michael who had been a child during the icon restoration of 843, now grown into a reckless young man with a serious drinking problem and a habit for Hippodrome chariot racing). Basil murdered the emperor's chief minister in 866 with imperial connivance, was adopted by Michael as co-emperor, and then, in September 867, murdered Michael himself in his bedchamber. The legitimate Macedonian dynasty thus began with a regicide. Byzantine sources written under his descendants worked hard to soften this — Basil was, after all, the founder of the longest-lasting and most successful dynasty in Byzantine history — but the basic facts were impossible to obscure.

What Basil and his successors then did with the throne was extraordinary.

---

The first major project of the Macedonian period was the **Photian Schism** — and "schism" here means not a permanent split but a sharp confrontation between Constantinople and Rome that prefigured everything that would come in 1054.

**Photius** (FOH-tee-us, c. 810–893) was one of the most learned men of the ninth century — a scholar, philosopher, and lay civil servant who became Patriarch of Constantinople in 858 despite never having taken holy orders before his elevation (he was rushed through six holy orders in six days, a procedural irregularity Rome refused to accept). His *Bibliotheca* — a collection of summaries and critiques of 279 ancient books, many of them now lost — is one of the great works of Byzantine scholarship and a major source for our knowledge of vanished classical literature. His patriarchate was twice contested by Rome (which preferred his rival Ignatius), and the resulting **Photian Schism** of 863–867 — a mutual exchange of excommunications between Pope Nicholas I and Patriarch Photius — rehearsed every argument that would later be deployed in 1054: papal supremacy versus conciliar authority, the *Filioque* clause in the Nicene Creed (a Latin addition stating that the Holy Spirit proceeded "from the Father *and the Son*," which the East considered a heretical alteration of the original creed), the use of unleavened versus leavened bread in the Eucharist, clerical celibacy versus clerical marriage. The schism was patched up. The grievances were not.

Photius's tenure also overlapped with one of the most consequential missionary projects in European history. In 862, the **Slavic** prince Rastislav of **Great Moravia** (a Slavic state in central Europe, in what is now the Czech Republic and Slovakia) sent a request to the Byzantine emperor for missionaries who could preach Christianity to his people in their own language. Photius assigned two brothers from Thessalonica — **Cyril** (originally named Constantine, c. 826–869) and **Methodius** (c. 815–885) — to the mission. Cyril, a brilliant philologist, invented an entirely new alphabet for the Slavic language: the **Glagolitic script** (gla-goh-LIT-ik, named from the Old Slavic word *glagol*, "word"). The script was specifically engineered to capture the sounds of Slavic, which had no native written tradition.

The Glagolitic alphabet was eventually superseded by a simpler script developed by Cyril's disciples in the late ninth century — the **Cyrillic alphabet** (named after Cyril, though he did not create it personally), which adapted Greek letters with additional symbols for distinctively Slavic sounds. Cyrillic became the script of Bulgarian, Serbian, Macedonian, Russian, Ukrainian, Belarusian, and a long list of smaller languages. The brothers also translated the Bible and the Orthodox liturgy into **Old Church Slavonic** — a Slavic literary language that became the Latin of the Orthodox Slavic world, used in churches from Ohrid to Moscow for the next thousand years.

This was a Byzantine cultural achievement on the scale of the Hagia Sophia. Rome had insisted on Latin as the only legitimate language for liturgy in western Christendom, with disastrous consequences for the long-term retention of converted peoples (English, German, French, Polish, and Hungarian Christians all worshipped in a language they did not understand for the next thousand years). Constantinople, by contrast, had translated its religion into the language of its converts. The Slavic peoples received Christianity in their own tongue, with their own alphabet, with their own translated scriptures. The result was that Slavic Orthodoxy took root much more deeply than Latin Christianity ever did in northern Europe — and that the Slavic world became, by the eleventh century, a culturally autonomous Byzantine satellite extending from the Adriatic to the Volga.

---

The Macedonian dynasty also benefited from a steady recovery of military strength.

**Constantine VII Porphyrogennetos** (kon-stan-TEEN, "Born-in-the-Purple," c. 905–959) was Basil I's grandson and one of the most attractive personalities to occupy the Byzantine throne. He spent most of his life as a co-emperor pushed aside by stronger men — most notably the admiral **Romanos I Lekapenos** (roh-MAHN-os, lek-ah-PEH-nos), who muscled his way into power as senior emperor from 920 to 944, married his daughter to Constantine, and effectively ran the empire while Constantine remained a figurehead. Romanos I was a competent emperor in his own right. He successfully fought the Bulgarians, brought the empire's relations with neighboring states onto a more stable footing, and issued land legislation protecting small landholders against the encroachment of large aristocratic estates — the so-called *dynatoi*, "the powerful ones." But Constantine VII outlasted him, finally taking sole power after Romanos was deposed by his own sons in 944 and dying in obscurity.

What Constantine VII did with his decades of forced leisure was write. Constantinople under his patronage became one of the great scholarly centers of the medieval world. He commissioned a series of encyclopedic compilations on imperial administration, court ceremony, military strategy, agriculture, and history. Three of these survive and are among the most important sources we have for tenth-century Byzantium.

The **De Administrando Imperio** ("On the Administration of the Empire," composed around 952) is a confidential manual on geopolitics, written by Constantine for his son Romanos II's instruction. It contains ethnographic descriptions of every neighbor — Pechenegs, Khazars, Magyars, Rus', Bulgarians, Serbs, Croats, Arabs, Lombards, Franks — with practical advice on how to play them off against each other. The book is the closest thing the medieval world produced to a modern intelligence briefing, and it includes details about peoples like the early Rus' that survive nowhere else.

The **De Ceremoniis** ("On Ceremonies") describes the imperial court rituals in obsessive detail: which day of the year requires which color of imperial garment, how foreign envoys are to be received, what music is played at which moment of an imperial banquet, how the throne is to be approached. Most readers find this book intolerably dull. But it is a unique window into the theatrical machinery of Byzantine power, the way the empire used spectacle to overawe both subjects and foreigners.

The **De Thematibus** is a less successful work — a description of the empire's military themes that is now considered partly inaccurate — but it preserves administrative information that would otherwise be lost. Together, these three books are the foundational documents of what historians call the **Macedonian Renaissance**: a deliberate, state-sponsored revival of classical learning in tenth-century Byzantium, parallel to and a century earlier than the more famous Carolingian Renaissance in the Latin West (the Frankish revival of classical scholarship under Charlemagne and his successors).

The Macedonian Renaissance was not just official scholarship. It produced the **Souda** (SOO-duh) — a massive 30,000-entry Greek-language encyclopedia compiled around 980, organized in alphabetical order, covering history, biography, literature, philosophy, philology, geography, and grammar. The Souda is the single most important source for our knowledge of ancient Greek literature, because it preserves quotations and biographical details from hundreds of works that have not otherwise survived — an enormous fraction of what we know about pre-Christian Greek civilization comes through the Souda. It produced **Photius's Bibliotheca**, mentioned earlier. It produced manuscripts of classical Greek literature copied in the new **minuscule** script (a flowing cursive form of Greek writing, more compact and faster to write than the older majuscule script, that became standard in the ninth century and dramatically increased the rate at which classical texts could be transmitted). Most of the Greek classical texts that survive into the modern world reach us through Macedonian-era copies. The Athens of Plato is preserved by the Constantinople of Constantine VII.

---

The military recovery accelerated under Constantine VII's successors.

**Nikephoros II Phokas** (nih-KEH-foh-ros FO-kus, ruled 963–969) was a brilliant general from the Anatolian military aristocracy who had already reconquered **Crete** from the Arabs in 961 — a campaign that was the empire's most important military success in two centuries. Crete had been Arab since 824 and had been used as a base for Aegean piracy that had devastated Byzantine coastal trade for over a century. Nikephoros's reconquest restored Byzantine naval supremacy in the eastern Mediterranean. Two years later he took the throne in a coup and turned his attention east.

In 969, Nikephoros recaptured **Antioch** — one of the great cities of late antiquity, the seat of one of the five original Christian patriarchates, lost to the Arabs in 638. Its reconquest was the symbolic culmination of three centuries of Byzantine military recovery. He pushed the eastern frontier deep into Syria, recovering territory that had been Arab since the seventh century. He was assassinated later that year in a palace coup organized by his wife **Theophano** and her lover **John I Tzimiskes** (TSIM-iss-keez), who succeeded him as emperor and continued the offensive — pushing into Mesopotamia and reportedly threatening Jerusalem itself before being recalled by an unrelated crisis. Tzimiskes was probably poisoned by his own minister in 976, opening the way for the man whose name dominates the rest of the Macedonian period.

**Basil II** (ruled 976–1025) is the great warrior emperor of medieval Byzantium. Tall, plain-faced, austere, never married, ruling for forty-nine years, he transformed the empire into the dominant power of the eastern Mediterranean. Procopius wrote about Justinian. Anna Komnene would write about her father Alexios (Chapter 8). Nobody wrote a comparable hagiography of Basil II, partly because he was personally so unappealing — he had no court life, no literary interests, no taste for ceremony, no interest in being praised. He is described as wearing dyed-purple robes only in battle and ordinary military gear in the palace. He probably preferred this. Basil II is the only Byzantine emperor for whom the documentary record consists almost entirely of his administrative and military acts, with his personality emerging only by inference.

His first decades were spent fighting an internal civil war against the Anatolian military aristocracy, which had assumed it could control the boy emperor and was disabused of this assumption violently. By the late 980s Basil had crushed the rebels and asserted personal control. He then turned to the **Bulgarians**.

The **First Bulgarian Empire** had emerged on the lower Danube in the late seventh century when the Turkic-speaking Bulgars (a steppe people from the Pontic region, originally Turkic-speakers but rapidly Slavicized) crossed the Danube and conquered the local Slavic populations. Under successive rulers it had become the major rival to Byzantium in the Balkans. Tsar Symeon the Great (ruled 893–927) had been Constantine VII's principal antagonist, repeatedly defeating Byzantine armies and nearly capturing Constantinople itself. Under Symeon's successors the empire had fragmented but remained dangerous. Under Tsar **Samuel** (SAM-yoo-el, ruled 997–1014) it had reconsolidated and was again threatening Byzantine control of Greece.

Basil II decided to destroy it.

The Bulgarian war ran from roughly 1000 to 1018, eighteen years of grinding mountain warfare in the rugged terrain of modern North Macedonia, Bulgaria, Albania, and Serbia. Basil's signature tactic was relentless persistence — annual campaigns, year after year, never resting on a victory, never accepting a partial settlement. The decisive engagement came on July 29, 1014, at the **Battle of Kleidion** (KLY-dee-on, the "Pass of the Keys" in Bulgarian Klyuch, in what is now southwestern Bulgaria). Basil's army surrounded a Bulgarian force of about 15,000 men in a narrow mountain pass and captured nearly all of them.

What he did next made his name. According to the Byzantine chroniclers — and these accounts are detailed enough and contemporary enough to be substantially credible, though the specific number is debated — Basil ordered all 15,000 prisoners blinded, leaving one man in every hundred with a single eye to lead the others home. The 99% who were fully blinded were tied together in groups of a hundred, each led by a one-eyed guide, and sent back to Tsar Samuel. Samuel reportedly suffered a stroke and died two days after seeing his army return in this condition. The sobriquet **Boulgaroktonos** — "Bulgar-Slayer" — was attached to Basil II afterward, though it was not used in his lifetime; the earliest attestation is about a century later.

Some modern historians have argued the blinding story is exaggerated or invented. The Byzantines did practice mass blinding as a punitive measure — it was a standard penalty for treason and for usurpers (less terminal than execution, but it disqualified a man from being a candidate for the throne, since the empire required physically perfect rulers). Whether the Kleidion blinding was on the scale tradition records is not certain. What is certain is that the Bulgarian Empire collapsed within four years of the battle. By 1018 the entire Bulgarian state had been absorbed into Byzantium. The Balkans were Byzantine again, for the first time in four centuries.

---

Two other achievements of Basil II's reign deserve mention.

The first was the **Christianization of Kievan Rus'** in 988. The Rus' (ROOS, an early medieval Slavic-Norse confederation centered on the Dnieper River, the ancestor of modern Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus) had been raiding Byzantine territory periodically since the ninth century. The most spectacular raid had been in 860, when a Rus' fleet had appeared in the Bosporus and threatened Constantinople itself before withdrawing. The raid of 907, led by Prince **Oleg** of Kyiv, had been more strategic — it ended with a treaty granting Rus' merchants advantageous trading terms in Constantinople, terms that built up a substantial Rus' merchant community in the Byzantine capital and that required regular diplomatic management for the next century. But the Rus' as a whole remained pagan, and their relationship to Byzantium remained transactional.

In 988, Prince **Vladimir** of Kyiv (VLAH-dih-meer, c. 958–1015), who had reached out to Basil II for marriage to the emperor's sister **Anna Porphyrogennetos** in exchange for military aid against an internal Byzantine rebellion, agreed to convert to Orthodox Christianity as a condition of the marriage. The Rus' chronicle the *Primary Chronicle* records that Vladimir had previously sent envoys to investigate the world's religions — the envoys reported that Islam was unacceptable because it forbade pork and wine ("drinking is the joy of the Rus'," Vladimir is said to have declared, "we cannot exist without that pleasure"); that western Christianity was unimpressive ("we beheld no glory there"); and that the liturgy at Hagia Sophia in Constantinople was so beautiful that "we knew not whether we were in heaven or on earth." The story is almost certainly stylized, but the political logic is real: Byzantine Christianity offered Vladimir more than Latin Christianity did — translated scriptures, autonomous metropolitan organization, the prestige of marriage to the imperial family, ongoing diplomatic and economic ties. He chose Orthodoxy, mass-baptized his population in the Dnieper, and brought the entire Rus' state into the Constantinopolitan religious and cultural sphere.

The Christianization of Rus' was the single most consequential religious transition in Eastern European history. It locked Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus into Orthodox Christianity rather than Latin Christianity, gave them Cyrillic rather than Latin script, oriented their high culture toward Greek rather than Roman models, and laid the foundation for what would later become the Russian claim to be the **Third Rome** (Chapter 12). When the Russian state began calling Moscow the Third Rome in the late fifteenth century, it was claiming an inheritance that had been assembled, deliberately and patiently, by Constantinople over the previous five hundred years.

As part of the marriage agreement, Vladimir sent Basil II a force of **6,000 Norse-Rus warriors** — heavily armed axe-wielding northmen who became the elite imperial bodyguard known as the **Varangian Guard** (VAR-an-jee-an, from the Old Norse *varingjar*, "sworn companions"). The Varangians were not just decoration. They were the imperial last line of defense, fanatically loyal to whoever currently held the throne (their loyalty was to the office, not the man — they switched smoothly from one emperor to the next, even when the new emperor had murdered the old one). They were also a recruitment magnet for adventurers from across the northern world: after the Norman Conquest of England in 1066, large numbers of Anglo-Saxons fleeing the new Norman regime traveled to Constantinople and enrolled in the Varangian Guard, which by the late eleventh century was substantially Anglo-Saxon. The Varangians served the empire for over four centuries, and the famous Norwegian king Harald Hardrada served in the Guard before returning to Scandinavia and dying at the Battle of Stamford Bridge in 1066.

The second achievement was that Basil II left the empire fiscally healthy — the only Byzantine emperor between Anastasius I and the Fall who can plausibly claim that distinction. He had filled the treasury through systematic taxation, military success, and an aggressive policy of resuming aristocratic land grants that had been used to deprive the small landholders of the theme system. When he died in December 1025, the empire stretched from southern Italy to Armenia, the borders were secure, the army was strong, and the central treasury held enough gold to sustain a major war. There would never again be a moment when so much had been so consolidated.

His successors threw it away in fifty years.

---

# Chapter 6 — Ceremony, Silk, and the Hippodrome

This chapter is a pause. Six hundred years have gone by — Constantine to Basil II — and the political narrative will continue to grind forward in the next chapter. But before it does, there is a question worth asking. How did the machinery of Byzantine civilization keep running through six centuries of plague, civil war, foreign invasion, and theological crisis? Emperors fell, dynasties rotated, provinces were lost — but the silk weavers kept weaving, the imperial workshops kept minting gold, the patriarchs kept performing the liturgy, the bureaucrats kept collecting customs duties on the Bosporus. Something more durable than any single emperor was holding it all together. The argument of this chapter is that the empire's daily institutions — its court, its currency, its commerce, its monasteries, its ceremony — were the durable thing, and that the political crises we have been tracking played out on top of a substrate that the crises did not reach.

What did it mean to wake up in tenth-century Constantinople? How did a Byzantine eat, dress, work, fight, pray, and die?

The empire was, throughout its existence, the most urban, most literate, most ceremonial society in the Christian world. Constantinople in 1000 had perhaps 400,000 to 500,000 inhabitants — by far the largest city in Europe (Paris had perhaps 20,000 in the same period; London perhaps 15,000) and second in the world only to Baghdad, the Abbasid capital, which was at this point larger than Constantinople by a wide margin and was the only city in Eurasia that surpassed it. (Tang Chang'an had been comparable in size before its sack in 904; by 1000 the eastern Eurasian primacy had passed to the cities of Song China.) The next chapter will continue the political story. This chapter will linger inside the city for a moment.

---

Start with the **emperor**.

The Byzantine emperor was, in theory, the absolute monarch of a Christian empire ordained by God. In practice, his power was constantly contested — by the senate, the church, the army, the great aristocratic families, the urban populace, and the chariot factions of the Hippodrome. The Byzantine state had no orderly mechanism for imperial succession. An emperor reigned until he died, was deposed, was murdered, or abdicated. Of the eighty-eight emperors between 330 and 1453, perhaps a third died violently — assassinated, blinded and deposed, killed in battle, or executed after a coup. The throne was, in this sense, a far more dangerous job than the papacy or the caliphate.

What the emperor wore was prescribed by ceremony to a degree no modern monarch matches. The full imperial regalia included the *loros* (a long jeweled stole wound around the body in a complex figure-eight pattern), the *skaramangion* (a long brocaded silk tunic), the *tzangia* (purple boots — only the emperor could wear purple shoes), the *stemma* (an arched crown set with precious stones, with pendant strings of pearls hanging beside the face). Variations of these garments were prescribed for every day of the liturgical and civil year, and the emperor's wardrobe was one of the major operating expenses of the imperial household.

What the emperor did was equally choreographed. The **De Ceremoniis** describes his daily schedule in painful detail: when he rose, when he prayed, which icons he venerated and in what order, when he received foreign ambassadors, when he dined, when he attended church, when he went to the Hippodrome, when he processed through the city. Almost every public action was a ritual.

The most famous ritual was the **proskynesis** (pros-kih-NEE-sis, "prostration") — the act of full prostration before the emperor, with the supplicant lying flat on the floor, forehead touching the marble. Foreign envoys were required to perform proskynesis before being received in audience. The Western European bishop **Liutprand of Cremona** (a tenth-century Lombard diplomat who served as ambassador to Byzantium and wrote two memoirs of his visits) described his reception by Emperor Constantine VII in 949: he prostrated himself three times before the throne; the throne, by the previously-mentioned mechanism designed by Leo the Mathematician, then rose toward the ceiling, with the emperor wearing different robes when it descended again. Mechanical golden lions roared. Mechanical birds sang. A choir of eunuchs chanted hymns of praise. Liutprand was, despite his determined western contempt for the Byzantines, impressed.

This was theater, but it was theater with a purpose. The empire was perpetually outnumbered, outflanked, and overstretched. Spectacle compensated for what military force could not always deliver. Ambassadors who had been overawed in the throne room were less likely to recommend war on their return home. Subjects who had seen the emperor in his terrifying glory were more likely to obey his administrators in their unglamorous offices.

---

Around the emperor was a court of extraordinary complexity, and at the center of it was a class of officials unique to Byzantium: the **eunuchs**.

Eunuchs in the Byzantine court were not slaves. They were not prisoners. Many came from respectable families. Some were castrated as children specifically to qualify them for high office. Others were made eunuchs as adults, voluntarily, to enter the imperial bureaucracy. The empire had perhaps a dozen senior offices reserved exclusively for eunuchs — the **parakoimomenos** (the emperor's chamberlain, an enormously powerful office), the **protovestiarios** (in charge of the imperial wardrobe and treasury), the **praepositus sacri cubiculi** (master of the sacred bedchamber). These were not minor offices. The parakoimomenos in the late ninth century could effectively run the empire while the emperor slept. The eunuch general **Narses** (Chapter 2) had defeated the Ostrogoths. The eunuch **Joseph Bringas** had run the empire under two emperors in the mid-tenth century.

Why eunuchs? The Byzantine answer was practical: eunuchs could not father children, which meant they could not start dynasties, which meant they could be trusted with positions of personal access to the emperor and his family without fear that they would seize the throne for their descendants. Eunuchs were also legally barred from becoming emperor themselves, which meant their loyalty was structurally directed toward the current ruling family rather than toward their own line. The eunuch class was, in this sense, an institutional answer to the problem of dynastic succession: a class of high-functioning officials whose interests were aligned with the throne's stability rather than with their own perpetuation.

The Byzantines were also more comfortable with the third-gender quality of eunuchs than later Western Europeans would be. Court eunuchs occupied a recognized social role with its own dignity, its own dress code, its own career path. They were not seen as failed men or as objects of pity. They were seen as a specialized administrative caste, and they were treated as such.

This is a feature of Byzantine society that has often been treated as scandalous or grotesque by later writers. It is more usefully understood as one of several institutional adaptations the empire developed to manage its peculiar combination of absolute monarchy, dynastic instability, and large bureaucratic state.

---

The other distinctive Byzantine institution at the heart of city life was the **Hippodrome**.

The Hippodrome was a chariot-racing arena built by Septimius Severus in the late second century and expanded by Constantine. By the tenth century it sat along the eastern flank of the Great Palace, in the heart of the imperial city. Its U-shape, modeled on the Circus Maximus in Rome, was about 1,400 feet long (430 meters) and 380 feet wide (115 meters), with a central spina decorated with monuments looted from across the Mediterranean — the Serpent Column from Delphi (Chapter 1), an Egyptian obelisk dedicated by Theodosius I, a bronze statue of Hercules (later destroyed by the Crusaders in 1204).

Races were the central public entertainment of Constantinople — eight to twenty-four chariot races on race days, four chariots per race, with charioteers (mostly slaves or freedmen, often famous, sometimes wealthy) competing on behalf of the four traditional racing factions: the **Blues**, the **Greens**, the **Reds**, and the **Whites** (the Reds and Whites had withered to insignificance by Justinian's day; from the sixth century onward only the Blues and Greens really mattered). The factions had organized fan clubs, dressed in faction colors, controlled neighborhoods, ran organized claques in the Hippodrome itself, and functioned as semi-official political institutions. The Blues drew their support disproportionately from the aristocracy and the Chalcedonian establishment; the Greens drew theirs from the urban populace and the Monophysite-leaning artisan classes (a connection that lingered even after the Monophysite question had been resolved by the Arab conquests).

The emperor attended major races in person, seated in the **kathisma** — an imperial box connected by a private staircase to the Great Palace, allowing him to enter and leave without exposing himself to the crowd. From the kathisma he could be acclaimed by the populace, heckled by the populace, or — in extreme cases like the Nika riots of 532 (Chapter 2) — directly threatened by the populace. The Hippodrome was where Byzantine emperors learned what their subjects thought of them, and where Byzantine subjects learned which of their wishes the emperor was prepared to grant.

Successful charioteers were celebrities on the order of modern athletes. **Porphyrios** of the Greens, the most famous Byzantine charioteer (active in the late fifth and early sixth centuries), had statues erected in the Hippodrome and bronze monuments in his honor that survived into the early modern period. His fans — and the rival fans of his Blue counterparts — produced elaborate carved monuments documenting their respective heroes' victories. We know more about Porphyrios than we do about most Byzantine emperors of his century.

---

Outside the imperial palace and the Hippodrome, the city was a working metropolis on a scale Western Europe had not seen since classical Rome.

Constantinople's harbors — the Neorion and the Theodosian Harbor on the Sea of Marmara, the Prosphorion and the Boukoleon on the Golden Horn — handled thousands of merchant ships a year. The customs duties from Bosporus traffic were one of the empire's largest single revenue streams. Goods came through from everywhere: Russian fur and amber from the Black Sea ports, Indian pepper and Chinese silk via the Red Sea and Egypt, North African grain (until 642), Italian timber, Danubian salt fish, English tin, Arab perfumes and incense.

The city's manufacturing base was concentrated in the silk industry. Justinian's smuggled silkworms (Chapter 2) had matured by the tenth century into a state-controlled production system based around imperial workshops in Constantinople and provincial centers in Greece (Thebes, Corinth, Patras). The state monopolized the production of certain silk grades — particularly the deep-purple "*blattai*" reserved for imperial use, dyed with murex purple in a process inherited from the Phoenicians (Phoenicia Ch 4). Lower grades were produced for export and for the domestic market under tight regulatory supervision. The **Book of the Eparch** — a tenth-century regulatory manual for the city's guilds, surviving in a single Geneva manuscript — describes the rules in detail: who could buy raw silk, who could weave it, who could sell finished garments, what prices could be charged, what penalties applied to violations. The picture is of a remarkably regulated commercial economy, far more formalized than anything in contemporary Western Europe.

The other major industry was **gold**. The **bezant** — the western European name for the Byzantine gold solidus, a coin originally introduced by Constantine and standardized at 4.5 grams of nearly pure gold — was the international reserve currency of the Mediterranean for over seven centuries. Foreign rulers from Britain to India hoarded bezants as a store of value. The bezant's stability — maintained, with one important exception we will come to in Chapter 7, from the fourth through the eleventh century — was a major source of Byzantine soft power. A ruler who could pay in bezants could buy mercenaries, allies, treaties, and intelligence anywhere in the known world.

**Anastasius I**'s currency reform in 498 had also restored the bronze coinage, creating clearly denominated **follis** coins for daily transactions. The Byzantine monetary system was, by medieval standards, sophisticated — a tri-metallic currency (gold for international and high-value transactions, silver for medium transactions, bronze for daily small transactions) with consistent denominations and reliable purity. Western Europeans who came east on diplomatic missions or pilgrimages were routinely astonished by the wealth of Constantinople — by the casualness with which gold was used, by the marble and the gilding and the silk and the perfumed water and the orchestra of musicians during dinner.

---

Education in Constantinople ran on two tracks.

The **University of Constantinople** had been founded in 425 by Theodosius II — a cluster of imperially endowed chairs in Greek and Latin grammar, rhetoric, philosophy, and law, with about 31 chairs in total. It functioned more or less continuously from the fifth century to the fifteenth, though its prestige and funding fluctuated. In the ninth century, the scholar **Leo the Mathematician** taught at the Magnaura School in the imperial palace, training a generation of officials and clergy in geometry, music theory, and astronomy. In the eleventh century, **Michael Psellos** (Chapter 7) reorganized higher education around philosophy, with himself as **hypatos ton philosophon** ("consul of the philosophers") at its head. The University was never a degree-granting institution in the modern sense, but it produced trained civil servants, lawyers, physicians, and clergymen for the empire's institutions.

The other track was the **monastery**. Byzantine monasteries were not just religious communities; they were schools, hospitals, libraries, scriptoria, agricultural enterprises, and pension homes for retired aristocrats. The **Stoudios Monastery** in Constantinople, reformed by Theodore the Studite (Chapter 4), was the most influential, but there were hundreds of others scattered across the empire. The greatest concentration was on **Mount Athos** (AH-thos, a remote peninsula in northern Greece dedicated to monastic settlement from 963 onward, when **Athanasios the Athonite** founded the Great Lavra). Mount Athos remains today the largest surviving Eastern Orthodox monastic community, a self-governing peninsula of twenty monasteries occupying about 130 square miles (330 km²) — and women have been forbidden from entering since 1046, an interdiction that is still in force.

Monasteries copied manuscripts, preserved liturgical practice, trained clergy, and provided alternative careers for men and women who did not wish to or could not marry. They were also enormously wealthy, accumulating lands and endowments over centuries until they became major economic actors in their own right. The Byzantine state had a complicated relationship with monastic wealth — sometimes confiscating it (Constantine V during the iconoclast period was particularly aggressive about this), sometimes endowing it lavishly (almost every emperor founded at least one monastery as part of his personal patronage). By the eleventh century, the largest Constantinopolitan and Athonite monasteries were among the most economically significant institutions in the empire.

---

Religious life in Byzantium was the constant background hum of every other aspect of life.

The Orthodox liturgical year structured the calendar — every day was a saint's day, every week had its fasts (Wednesdays and Fridays, plus the four extended fasting periods around Christmas, Easter, the apostles' feast, and the Dormition). Every Christian Byzantine was supposed to fast for roughly half the year. Animal flesh, dairy products, and (during the strictest periods) olive oil were forbidden during fasts, with elaborate exceptions for the sick, the pregnant, the very young, and travelers. The combined effect was a national diet built around bread, vegetables, legumes, fish, and seafood, with meat and dairy as celebratory foods rather than daily staples.

The Hagia Sophia was the centerpiece. The full **Divine Liturgy** of John Chrysostom — the standard Byzantine eucharistic service, with chanted hymns, processions of clergy in elaborate vestments, clouds of incense, and a continuous antiphonal exchange between priests and choir — took roughly two hours. On major feast days the service expanded to include vigils, processions through the city, and ceremonial acts involving the emperor. The visual environment was overwhelming: gold mosaics, marble columns, hundreds of oil lamps, banners, colored silks. Byzantine writers describe walking into Hagia Sophia as walking into a vision of heaven — the building was deliberately designed to overwhelm the senses, to make the worshipper feel that the boundary between earthly and divine had become thin.

For ordinary Byzantines, the most important religious activity was probably the veneration of personal **icons**. Every household had its private icon corner. Travelers carried small icons in their luggage. Soldiers carried icons into battle. Mothers gave birth with icons in the room. Dying Byzantines kissed icons in their last moments. Icons were not, in the post-iconoclast theological consensus, worshipped — they were venerated as windows onto the saints they depicted, as channels through which the divine reached into the human world. After the iconoclast crisis, this distinction was understood and cherished. Byzantium had decided, definitively, that the divine could be touched, and that touching it through the medium of paint and wood was a normal part of being a Christian.

---

The empire would face fresh disasters in the next chapter — a fiscal collapse, a battlefield catastrophe, and a final formal break with Rome. But its institutions, its city, its theology, its court, its commerce, its monasteries, and its faith had reached a coherent maturity by the early eleventh century. What followed would gradually erode these things, but they were not lost. Even in the empire's worst moments, the silk weavers of Thebes were still weaving silk. The monks of Stoudios were still copying manuscripts. The icon painters of Constantinople were still producing the small wooden panels that would survive long after the empire fell, and that today fill the world's great museums. Byzantium produced a civilization whose physical artifacts you can hold in your hand. That civilization was already in place by the year 1000.

# Chapter 7 — The Slow Crack

Basil II died on December 15, 1025, in the imperial palace, planning a campaign to reconquer Sicily from the Arabs. The campaign never sailed. Within fifty years his consolidated empire had fragmented, its currency was halved, its army was a wreck, and the most prestigious title in Christendom had been transferred from the Roman emperor in Constantinople to whoever was fighting his way to the top of a chaotic court.

This chapter is about the rapid deterioration that turned the strongest medieval state in Europe into a wounded empire begging for outside help by 1071. It is also about the formal break between the Greek and Latin churches that took place in the middle of that deterioration, in 1054 — an event whose long-term consequences would dwarf its immediate impact.

---

The deterioration was not military at first. It was fiscal.

The Macedonian dynasty had run the empire on a strict gold standard. The **nomisma** (no-MIZ-mah, the Greek term for the gold solidus, also called the bezant in the West) had been minted at 4.5 grams of essentially pure gold for over six hundred years — an extraordinary monetary stability with almost no parallel in pre-modern history. Then, around 1030, the gold content of the coin began to slip. By the 1040s it was about 90% pure. By the 1050s, 80%. By the 1060s, 70%. By the 1070s, around 30%. The currency that had been the international reserve of the Mediterranean world was now visibly debased, lighter than its old weight, redder in color from the increased copper alloy, less trusted in foreign markets.

Why did the empire debase its currency? The simple answer is that it was running out of money. The aristocratic families who had been kept in check by Basil II were now back in control of the imperial court — their fortunes were vast, but the imperial treasury depended on tax revenues from peasant landholders, and the aristocrats had spent the post-Basil years buying up peasant lands, converting tax-paying smallholdings into tax-exempt aristocratic estates worked by tied tenants. Tax revenue collapsed. The army, the bureaucracy, and the court still needed to be paid. Successive emperors filled the gap by reducing the gold content of the coin and using the saved metal to pay their bills. This worked in the short term and was disastrous in the long term: the debasement broke confidence in the currency, accelerated inflation, hollowed out the army (whose soldiers' pay no longer purchased what it used to), and undermined the empire's standing in international trade.

The aristocratic resurgence had a name: the **dynatoi** (dih-NAH-toy, "the powerful ones"), the great landed families of Anatolia and the European provinces — the **Phokas**, **Skleros**, **Doukas**, **Komnenos**, **Argyros** dynasties that supplied generals, governors, and emperors for the next two centuries. These families were rich, well-connected, militarily competent, and politically dangerous. Basil II had spent his life suppressing them. His successors were either incapable of doing so (because they were not soldiers) or unwilling (because they had married into the same families).

---

The political instability was theatrical. Between 1025 and 1081, the empire was ruled by a parade of inadequate emperors, most of whom came to the throne by marriage to the same elderly empress: **Zoe Porphyrogennita** (ZOH-ee, c. 978–1050), Basil II's niece and the legitimate heir to the Macedonian dynasty.

Zoe was 50 years old when she first married. She married three times in succession (Romanos III Argyros, who probably murdered her first husband; Michael IV the Paphlagonian, who definitely had Romanos III drowned in his bath; Constantine IX Monomachos, after Michael IV's death), with brief interludes in which she ruled alongside her sister Theodora as joint empresses (the only time in Byzantine history that two women ruled simultaneously in their own right). Each husband took the throne by virtue of marriage to her. The third, Constantine IX, was a feckless aesthete who funded the magnificent monastery church of **Hosios Loukas** in Boeotia (whose mosaics from this period are among the finest surviving Byzantine works) but spent his reign losing border provinces, allowing the army to decay, and presiding over a court culture obsessed with eunuch favorites, theological speculation, and elaborate festivals.

Constantine IX's reign also produced the empire's most influential intellectual: **Michael Psellos** (PSEL-os, 1018–1078). Psellos was a scholar, philosopher, and palace insider whose **Chronographia** is one of the great Byzantine literary works — a memoir of the emperors he served, written in luminous Greek prose, full of psychological observation and dry court gossip. Psellos was also the chief proponent of a Byzantine revival of Platonic philosophy, an enthusiasm that would worry his more orthodox contemporaries (Plato's pagan metaphysics and Christian theology had been awkwardly co-existing for centuries; Psellos pushed the Plato side harder than was conventional). His student **John Italos** would be tried for heresy in the next century for taking the Platonic revival further still. The intellectual ferment of the late Macedonian period was real and impressive. It just did not translate into political competence.

---

While the court drifted, two formal breaks occurred that would shape European history for a thousand years.

The first was the **Great Schism** of 1054.

The breach between the Roman papacy and the Patriarchate of Constantinople had been building for centuries. The **Photian Schism** of the 860s (Chapter 5) had rehearsed the major arguments. The iconoclast period had driven the papacy into Frankish arms (Chapter 4). The **Filioque** clause — the Latin addition to the Nicene Creed stating that the Holy Spirit proceeded "from the Father *and the Son*" — had been adopted in Frankish liturgy in the eighth century, condemned by Photius in the ninth, and was by the eleventh century the universal practice of the Latin church and a standing grievance of the Greek church (which considered any addition to the original creed of 381 a heretical alteration). The use of unleavened bread (azymes) in the Latin Eucharist, the Latin requirement of clerical celibacy, the Latin claim of papal supremacy over the universal church — these were the standing differences, and they were getting harder to paper over.

In 1054 the Patriarch of Constantinople, **Michael Cerularius** (sair-oo-LAH-ree-us), and the papal legate **Cardinal Humbert of Silva Candida** got into a shouting match over these issues during a diplomatic visit. The argument escalated. On July 16, 1054, Humbert marched into Hagia Sophia in the middle of the Divine Liturgy and laid a **bull of excommunication** against Cerularius and his clergy on the high altar. Cerularius excommunicated Humbert in return. Both men, in strict canonical terms, had exceeded their authority — Humbert was acting on a papal commission whose pope (Leo IX) had died three months earlier, while Cerularius's counter-excommunication was a personal act rather than a synodal one. Neither anathema was formally aimed at the entire opposing church.

But the symbolic impact was enormous. The Latin and Greek churches had been formally severing communion in the persons of their respective leaders, in the most prestigious church in Christendom, in the middle of the central rite of Christian worship. Whatever the legal niceties, the schism was now public, theatrical, and irreversible. Within a generation, the two churches had stopped recognizing each other's sacraments. By 1100, the Greek and Latin churches had become two distinct Christian communions — communions that have remained divided for nearly a thousand years, despite multiple attempts at reunion (most notably the Council of Florence in 1439, which we will reach in Chapter 11).

The mutual excommunications of 1054 were formally lifted by Pope Paul VI and Patriarch Athenagoras in 1965, in a Vatican II-era gesture of reconciliation. The actual schism is still in place. Catholics and Orthodox can today attend each other's services with mutual respect, but they cannot share communion, and the underlying theological and ecclesiological differences remain unresolved.

---

The second break came on August 26, 1071, on a high plain near the town of **Manzikert** (MAN-zih-kert) in eastern Anatolia (modern Malazgirt in eastern Turkey).

The opposing forces were the army of Emperor **Romanos IV Diogenes** (ruled 1068–1071, the latest soldier-emperor pulled in to fix the empire's military problems) and a large Seljuk Turkic force commanded personally by Sultan **Alp Arslan** (alp ar-SLAN, "Heroic Lion," ruler of the Great Seljuk Empire). The **Seljuks** (SEL-jooks) were a Turkic dynasty originating from Central Asia who had moved into the Iranian world in the early eleventh century, converted to Sunni Islam, and conquered most of Iran, Mesopotamia, and Syria within a generation. By the 1060s they were raiding deep into Byzantine Anatolia. Romanos IV's campaign of 1071 was supposed to push them back.

It went catastrophically wrong. The two armies were comparable in size — perhaps 40,000 men each, though estimates vary. Romanos's army was a mixed force of regular Byzantine themes, Norman mercenaries, Pecheneg auxiliaries, and Armenian troops — a coalition with mutually suspicious commanders. Alp Arslan's force was a more cohesive Turkic cavalry army, fighting on terrain it knew well. The battle began with conventional maneuvering. Then, at the moment of decision, Romanos's reserve corps under the command of **Andronikos Doukas** (a member of the rival aristocratic faction) deliberately withdrew from the field rather than support the emperor. The withdrawal turned the battle into a rout. Romanos's main army was surrounded and destroyed. Romanos himself was captured — the first Roman emperor since Valerian eight centuries earlier (Persian Empire Ch 8) to be taken alive on a battlefield.

Alp Arslan treated his captive surprisingly well. There is a famous (probably apocryphal) exchange between sultan and emperor after the battle: Alp Arslan asks Romanos what he would have done if their positions had been reversed, and Romanos answers, "I would have killed you, or paraded you through the streets of Constantinople." Alp Arslan replies that his own punishment is harsher: "I will release you." The release was strategic — Alp Arslan wanted the empire weakened by a returning emperor whose authority was contested, not destroyed by a power vacuum. He extracted a peace treaty involving territorial concessions and tribute, and let Romanos go.

The aftermath proved Alp Arslan's calculation correct. While Romanos was traveling back, the Doukas faction in Constantinople deposed him, blinded him, and installed their own candidate **Michael VII Doukas** as emperor. The civil war that followed lasted years. The peace treaty Romanos had signed with Alp Arslan was repudiated. Anatolian provincial governors stopped sending tax revenues to the central government. The frontier collapsed.

Within a decade of Manzikert, **Turkic nomads** had spread across the entire Anatolian plateau. The **Sultanate of Rum** (ROOM, "the Sultanate of Rome") was established at Iznik (the old Nicaea) by 1077, a hundred miles from Constantinople. It was a Turkic state ruled by the **Seljuks of Rum**, a junior branch of the Great Seljuk Empire; they took the name "Rum" because they had moved into territory whose inhabitants were *Romaioi*, Romans. The cities of central Anatolia — Caesarea, Iconium, Sebasteia, Melitene — fell one by one. The peasant population, the manpower base of the theme system, was uprooted, scattered, or killed. The **Turkmen** nomads who arrived behind the Seljuk armies converted Anatolia from a Greek-speaking, Christian, settled-agrarian region into a Turkish-speaking, Muslim, partly-pastoral one over the next two centuries. The transformation was not complete; substantial Greek Christian populations remained until the twentieth century. But the demographic balance had shifted in a way that the empire could never reverse.

Manzikert is the moment that, in retrospect, ended the medieval Byzantine state and began the long Byzantine decline. It was not the worst military disaster in absolute terms — the loss of Egypt and Syria in the 630s and 640s had been worse in territorial terms. But Egypt and Syria had been peripheral provinces dominated by Monophysite populations indifferent to imperial fortunes. Anatolia was the empire's heartland. Losing Anatolia was losing the manpower base, the recruiting ground, the agricultural base, and the strategic depth that had allowed Byzantium to weather the seventh-century catastrophe. After Manzikert, the empire was a Balkan state with a few coastal Anatolian footholds. It was not a Roman empire of the eastern Mediterranean any longer. It was something smaller, more European, and on a long downward trajectory.

---

The chaos of the 1070s ended in 1081, when a young general from one of the great Anatolian aristocratic families seized the throne and put a partial brake on the slide. His name was **Alexios Komnenos**, he was 24 years old, and he is the subject of the next chapter.

---

# Chapter 8 — The Komnenian Restoration

Alexios I Komnenos took the throne on April 4, 1081, in a coup so quick that the soldiers he had bribed to admit him to the city celebrated by sacking the wealthy quarters of Constantinople for three days. He was a member of the Komnenos family, an Anatolian military aristocratic dynasty that had already produced one short-reigning emperor (Isaac I, 1057–1059) before being pushed aside. Alexios's mother **Anna Dalassene** was a politically ferocious woman who had spent the previous decade planning her sons' rise to power. By the early 1080s Alexios had outmaneuvered his rivals, married into the Doukas family (the rival aristocratic clan, securing political reconciliation through marriage to **Irene Doukaina**), and assembled the resources to seize the throne.

The empire he inherited was in extremis. Most of Anatolia was under Turkic occupation. **Robert Guiscard** (gee-SKAR), the Norman duke of southern Italy, had landed an invasion force in the Balkans and was advancing toward Thessalonica. The **Pechenegs** (PEH-cheh-negz, a Turkic nomadic confederation) were raiding across the Danube. The treasury was empty. The army that had fought at Manzikert no longer existed in any meaningful form. Foreign coin had largely replaced the debased nomisma in domestic transactions, a humiliation for an empire that had supplied the Mediterranean's reserve currency for seven centuries.

Alexios spent the next thirty-seven years — until his death in 1118 — putting the empire back together. He did this by combining diplomacy, military improvisation, fiscal reform, and a willingness to make distasteful deals with the foreigners his predecessors had alienated. The Komnenian Restoration is not a recovery of imperial greatness. The empire that emerged would never again be a major Mediterranean power. But it would survive, and its survival was Alexios's achievement.

---

His first move was monetary. In 1092, after a decade of preparation, Alexios introduced a new gold coin, the **hyperpyron** (hee-PEER-puh-ron, "super-refined") — a 4.5-gram coin with about 87% gold purity, replacing the catastrophically debased nomisma. The hyperpyron would not be quite as pure as the old solidus, but it was reliable enough to restore confidence in Byzantine money. It became the new international currency of the eastern Mediterranean, holding its value for the next two centuries (it would be debased again under Alexios's later Komnenian successors and finally collapse in the thirteenth century, but for the moment it bought Alexios time).

His second move was diplomatic. In 1082, before the hyperpyron was issued, Alexios made the bargain that would haunt Byzantine commerce for the next century. He needed naval help against the Norman invasion. The only naval power with the capacity to provide it was **Venice** — a small Adriatic city-state that had been a Byzantine vassal for centuries but had grown wealthy and independent through trade. Alexios offered the Venetians a deal: in exchange for naval support, Venice would receive sweeping commercial privileges throughout the empire — exemption from most Byzantine customs duties, dedicated quarters in Constantinople and other major ports, the right to trade freely in territories where their Byzantine competitors had to pay full tariffs. The arrangement was enshrined in a **Chrysobull** (KRY-soh-buhl, "golden bull," an imperial document sealed with a gold seal) issued in 1082.

The Chrysobull of 1082 was the death warrant of Byzantine commercial independence. The Venetians took the privileges and turned Constantinople into a Venetian-dominated commercial hub. Within a generation Italian merchants — Venetians, Pisans (granted comparable privileges in 1111 by Alexios's son John II), and Genoese (granted privileges in 1155) — controlled the carrying trade, the long-distance commerce, and most of the high-value commodity trade of the eastern Mediterranean. Byzantine merchants, paying full tariffs at home while their Italian competitors paid none, were squeezed out of their own markets. The empire's commercial economy gradually became a tributary of the Italian maritime republics. The fall of Constantinople in 1204 (Chapter 9) and the ultimate fall of 1453 (Chapter 11) were both carried out by states whose commercial dominance had been handed to them by the Komnenoi.

But in 1082 Alexios needed ships, and the Venetians had ships, and the long-term consequences were a problem for whoever came next.

---

His third move was the appeal that produced the First Crusade.

By 1095, with the Norman threat handled and the eastern frontier stabilized, Alexios decided to attempt a partial reconquest of Anatolia. He needed manpower. The empire's domestic recruiting base was inadequate — Anatolia, the traditional source of theme troops, was under Turkic occupation. Alexios sent a diplomatic mission to **Pope Urban II** in March 1095, asking for western mercenary contingents to assist in a campaign against the Seljuks.

What Alexios got back was something he had not asked for. Urban II, at the **Council of Clermont** in November 1095, transformed Alexios's mercenary request into a call for a general Christian war of liberation — to recover not just Anatolia but the **Holy Land** itself, including Jerusalem, which had been under Muslim rule since 638. Urban offered participants a **plenary indulgence** (full remission of the temporal penalties for sin). He framed the campaign as a pilgrimage with weapons. He called on every Christian capable of bearing arms to "take the cross." The response was overwhelming — far larger than anything Alexios had imagined.

In 1096, three successive waves of crusader contingents converged on Constantinople. The **People's Crusade** under Peter the Hermit was the first — a disorganized mass of perhaps 40,000 untrained pilgrims who arrived at Constantinople in summer 1096, were ferried across the Bosporus by a relieved Alexios, and were promptly massacred by the Seljuks within weeks of crossing. The **Princes' Crusade** that followed was more serious — armies under **Godfrey of Bouillon**, **Bohemond of Taranto** (the son of Alexios's old enemy Robert Guiscard, which was awkward), **Raymond of Toulouse**, and **Hugh of Vermandois**. Each leader and his army arrived separately and was received by Alexios in elaborate ceremony, fed at imperial expense, and required to swear an oath of fealty to the Byzantine emperor — promising to return any conquered territory that had previously been Byzantine.

The crusader leaders found Constantinople bewildering. The wealth astonished them. The court ceremony was alien. The Greek language was incomprehensible. Alexios's daughter **Anna Komnene** — born in the imperial purple chamber in 1083, age 13 at the time of the First Crusade — described the crusader princes in unsparing terms: greedy, treacherous, easily provoked, suspicious of every Byzantine kindness. She would later write the great history of her father's reign, the **Alexiad**. Anna's *Alexiad*, completed around 1148, is one of the great works of medieval historiography and the first major historical work known to have been written by a woman in the European tradition. It is also a one-sided document — Anna detested the crusaders and never lost an opportunity to record their faults — but it is a near-contemporary account of one of the most consequential moments in medieval history, and it has been a primary source for crusade historians ever since.

The crusader armies crossed into Anatolia in 1097. They besieged and took **Nicaea** (the old Byzantine city, now a Seljuk capital), which Alexios then claimed as a Byzantine recovery — to crusader fury, since they had expected to plunder the city. They marched across the Anatolian plateau, suffering terribly from heat, thirst, and Seljuk harassment. They besieged **Antioch** for nine months in 1097–1098, took it after horrific casualties, and then, instead of returning the city to Byzantine control as their oaths required, kept it for themselves — installing Bohemond of Taranto as Prince of Antioch. Alexios was not amused. He considered the crusader betrayal of their oath a permanent grievance that justified Byzantine non-cooperation in subsequent crusades. The crusaders considered Alexios's failure to march to their relief at Antioch a permanent grievance that justified their later treatment of Byzantium. Both sides were partly right. Both sides spent the next century and a half nursing the grudge.

In July 1099, the surviving crusader forces took Jerusalem and massacred most of the Muslim and Jewish population. The **Kingdom of Jerusalem** was established. The other crusader states — the **County of Edessa**, the **Principality of Antioch**, the **County of Tripoli** — were carved out from former Byzantine and Arab territory in the Levant and northern Mesopotamia. The crusaders had achieved their stated goal. They had also created a network of Latin Christian states in territory the Byzantines considered legitimately their own — and they had set up the structural conflicts that would, over the next century, drive Byzantium and the Latin West into outright war.

---

Alexios died in 1118. His son **John II Komnenos** (ruled 1118–1143, called **Kalojohannes**, "John the Good") is one of the most quietly impressive Byzantine emperors. He is also one of the least studied, because he had no Anna Komnene daughter writing his biography and because his reign produced no spectacular failures or successes — just steady, methodical extension of the empire's recovery. He campaigned almost continuously: against the Pechenegs (whom he destroyed at the Battle of Beroia in 1122, ending Pecheneg power forever), against the Hungarians, against the Serbs, against the Seljuks of Rum, against the crusader states in Cilicia. He recovered more Byzantine territory in Anatolia, restored Byzantine suzerainty over the Principality of Antioch, and pushed Byzantine forces into the Holy Land itself. He was on a campaign in Cilicia in 1143 when he was wounded by a poisoned arrow — accidentally, in his own quiver, while hunting boar — and died of the resulting infection. The accident was so improbable that contemporaries suspected assassination, but no plausible plot has ever been documented.

His son **Manuel I Komnenos** (ruled 1143–1180) was a more theatrical version of his father — a charismatic, multilingual, aesthetically sophisticated emperor who pursued an ambitious foreign policy on multiple fronts. Manuel's reign was the high-water mark of Komnenian recovery. He intervened in Italian politics, attempting to manipulate the German emperor Frederick Barbarossa and the papacy. He maintained close diplomatic relations with the crusader states. He patronized Latin court culture, inviting Western knights to Constantinopolitan tournaments — a move that scandalized conservative Byzantines who considered jousting an unseemly western barbarism. He married a Western princess (Maria of Antioch, of the crusader Antiochene house). He married his children to Western royals. The Constantinopolitan court of the 1170s was the most cosmopolitan it had been since Justinian's day.

It was also overstretched. Manuel's ambitious foreign policy required a level of military and diplomatic activity the empire could no longer easily sustain. The decisive setback came on September 17, 1176, at the **Battle of Myriokephalon** (mee-ree-oh-keh-fah-LON, "Place of a Thousand Heads," a mountain pass in southwestern Anatolia). Manuel had assembled a massive expedition to retake the Anatolian interior from the Seljuks of Rum. The force was perhaps the largest Byzantine army to take the field since Manzikert. It was ambushed in the pass by Sultan **Kilij Arslan II** of Rum, suffered heavy casualties, and was forced to retreat in disarray. Manuel survived and successfully extracted the surviving force, but the campaign's strategic objective was dead. The dream of recovering the Anatolian interior — the dream that had animated Byzantine military policy since Manzikert a century earlier — was abandoned. Manuel reportedly compared his defeat to Manzikert in his subsequent communications with the western powers; he was not exaggerating. After Myriokephalon, Anatolia was permanently Turkic. Byzantium would never again seriously attempt to reclaim it.

---

Manuel died in 1180, leaving the throne to an eleven-year-old son, **Alexios II**. The regency was managed by Manuel's western widow Maria of Antioch and her favorite Alexios Komnenos (a different Alexios from the boy emperor — a cousin), and it provoked widespread Greek resentment of Latin court influence. In 1182 a coup brought to the throne the most colorful and most disastrous of the Komnenian emperors: **Andronikos I Komnenos** (ruled 1183–1185), a cousin of Manuel I, a man who had spent most of his life in spectacular adventures across the medieval world. He had escaped prison, hidden among Turks, romanced the wife of the Antiochene prince, fathered children by his own niece, contracted a Western dynastic marriage for his teenaged son — and so on for sixty-five years. He had finally returned to Constantinople in his old age determined to take the throne.

Andronikos's coup was bloody. He arranged the murder of the regent Alexios. He had Maria of Antioch strangled. He married the eleven-year-old Alexios II's twelve-year-old French widow Agnes of France (forty-five years his junior). He then strangled Alexios II personally. Once on the throne, he turned on the wealthy Constantinopolitan elite, executing aristocrats, confiscating estates, and pursuing fiscal reforms that would have helped the imperial treasury if they had not been carried out with such public cruelty that they made him universally hated.

The single most consequential act of his reign came before his coup. In 1182, during the regency unrest, the population of Constantinople had risen up and **massacred the Latin merchant community** — perhaps 60,000 Italians (mostly Pisans and Genoese), killed or sold as slaves over the course of several days, with Latin priests singled out for particular cruelty. The massacre was a delayed reaction to a century of resentment over Italian commercial privileges. It was also a permanent stain on Byzantine relations with the Italian republics, who would not forget. The pogrom of 1182 is one of the immediate causes of the Fourth Crusade two decades later.

Andronikos's reign ended in September 1185 when a Norman invasion fleet from Sicily took Thessalonica (with great brutality, sacking Byzantium's second city in retaliation for the 1182 massacre), and the Constantinopolitan populace, already terrorized by Andronikos's purges, rose against him. He was caught fleeing the city, dragged back, and lynched in the Hippodrome over the course of three days — his eyes gouged out, his right hand cut off, his teeth pulled, his skin peeled, his body hacked apart by the same crowd that had cheered his coup three years earlier. He was the last Komnenian emperor.

His death is sometimes cited as the moment the Byzantine state lost its final reservoir of legitimacy. The dynasty that had restored the empire after Manzikert had ended in mob justice. The aristocracy was leaderless. The Latin west was furious. The next twenty years of Byzantine history would be a blur of weak emperors from the **Angelos** dynasty (Isaac II, Alexios III), losing territory, losing money, losing competence, until the climactic disaster of 1204.

---

# Chapter 9 — The Catastrophe of 1204

In April 1204, Christian crusaders from western Europe stormed the walls of the largest Christian city in the world, sacked it for three days, and destroyed in a single week more of the surviving heritage of classical antiquity than any other event between the burning of the Library of Alexandria and the Reformation. They carried off mountains of gold and silver, looted churches, melted down bronze statues for coinage, raped nuns in their convents, and installed a Latin emperor on the throne of the Romans. The city would not be Greek again for fifty-seven years, and it would never again be what it had been.

The Fourth Crusade is one of the strangest events in medieval history. It started as a campaign to liberate Jerusalem and ended as a campaign to install a Catholic emperor in Constantinople. It was carried out by Christians against Christians, in violation of explicit papal prohibition, with the Latin and Greek churches in nominal communion at the time. It happened because of a chain of contingencies, miscalculations, and outright frauds, with each step seeming locally rational and the cumulative outcome catastrophic for everyone involved.

Here is how it happened.

---

In 1198 the new Pope **Innocent III** called for a Fourth Crusade to recover Jerusalem (which had been retaken by the Muslim sultan **Saladin** in 1187, in a campaign that destroyed the original Kingdom of Jerusalem and gave rise to the Third Crusade — the unsuccessful one with Richard the Lionheart and Frederick Barbarossa). The strategic plan was to attack Egypt first, since Egypt was the wealthy core of the Ayyubid sultanate and a victory there would force Ayyubid concessions in the Holy Land. Egypt could only be reached by sea. The crusaders, mostly French nobles led by **Boniface of Montferrat**, contracted with **Venice** to provide transport ships for the expedition. The contract — negotiated by the doge **Enrico Dandolo** in 1201 — committed the crusaders to pay 85,000 silver marks for ships sufficient to transport 33,500 men and 4,500 horses.

The crusaders did not show up in the numbers they had promised. When the expedition assembled at Venice in summer 1202, perhaps a third of the projected force was present. The crusaders could not pay the contracted price for the ships they had requested. Dandolo, who was 90 years old and blind but politically formidable, presented the crusaders with an ultimatum: they could not leave Venice without paying, but he would reduce the debt if they helped Venice retake the Adriatic city of **Zara** (modern Zadar in Croatia), which had recently rebelled against Venetian control and was now under the protection of the King of Hungary — a Catholic king who had himself taken the cross.

The crusaders agreed. The Pope was furious — attacking a Catholic city under crusader protection was an explicit violation of crusade vows — but his condemnation arrived too late to prevent the storming of Zara in November 1202. Innocent III excommunicated the entire crusade and then, when the practical implications dawned on him, conditionally lifted the excommunication for everyone except the Venetians (who he held primarily responsible). The crusade was now operationally compromised but technically still in progress.

While the crusaders wintered at Zara, a Byzantine prince showed up offering them a deal.

**Alexios IV Angelos** was the son of the deposed and blinded Byzantine emperor **Isaac II Angelos** (who had been overthrown by his brother Alexios III in 1195). The young Alexios had escaped to the West and was now offering the crusaders 200,000 silver marks, 10,000 troops for the Egyptian campaign, the submission of the Greek church to Rome, and a permanent garrison of 500 Byzantine troops to maintain the Latin Kingdom of Jerusalem — all in exchange for crusader assistance in restoring his father (and himself as junior co-emperor) to the Byzantine throne. The deal was structurally absurd — Alexios had no resources to make any of those payments, and his proposed coup against Alexios III was politically delusional — but it solved the crusaders' immediate cash flow problem, and Boniface of Montferrat had personal ties to the Angelos family that made the proposal seem more credible than it should have. The crusader leaders accepted.

In June 1203 the crusader fleet arrived off Constantinople. The city was still the largest in Christendom, ringed by the Theodosian Walls (which had repelled every assault for eight centuries) and the sea walls along the Golden Horn and the Sea of Marmara. The Byzantine navy was a wreck — Andronikos I had let it decay decades earlier, and successive emperors had relied on Italian fleets for everything important — but the city was still apparently impregnable. **Alexios III**, the reigning emperor, mismanaged the crisis comprehensively. He refused to negotiate. He failed to defend the harbor chain across the Golden Horn (the Venetian fleet broke through and entered the inner harbor). When the crusaders launched their first major assault in July 1203, his army did not engage. Within weeks Alexios III had panicked, taken what gold he could grab, and fled the city. The blinded Isaac II was retrieved from his prison and reinstated. His son Alexios IV was crowned as co-emperor.

Now Alexios IV had to pay what he had promised. He could not. The Constantinopolitan treasury did not contain 200,000 marks. Greek attempts to raise the money — including melting down church plate, levying special taxes, and forced loans from the aristocracy — produced perhaps half the contracted amount and unleashed widespread popular fury. Alexios IV's government became increasingly unpopular as winter set in. The crusader army camped outside the city demanded payment. Riots broke out in the city. In late January 1204 a Greek nobleman named **Alexios Doukas Mourtzouphlos** ("Bushy-Eyebrows") seized the throne, strangled Alexios IV, and let Isaac II die of fright. The new **Alexios V Mourtzouphlos** repudiated the agreement with the crusaders.

---

The crusader leaders held a council in March 1204. They resolved that since the Greeks had broken their agreement, the only remaining option was to take Constantinople by storm and install a Latin emperor. The deal among the crusader leaders divided the future spoils in advance: a Latin emperor would be elected by an electoral committee, three-quarters of the loot would be split between the Venetians and the French crusaders to settle the contract, and the territory of the empire would be partitioned among the leading crusaders.

The assault began on April 9, 1204. The crusaders did not attack the land walls — those had never been breached and were probably not breachable. They attacked the sea walls along the Golden Horn, where Venetian ships armed with siege ladders could pull alongside the wall towers. The first day's assault failed. The crusaders regrouped, renewed the attack on April 12, and broke through near the **Petria Gate**. Alexios V Mourtzouphlos fled the city. By nightfall on April 12, organized Greek resistance had collapsed. The crusaders entered Constantinople.

What followed was three days of systematic looting that contemporaries — including the Greek bishop and historian **Niketas Choniates** (nih-KEE-tas khon-ee-AH-teez), an eyewitness — described in horrified detail. The great churches were stripped of their gold and silver liturgical implements. Tombs were opened and their occupants' jewelry torn from their bodies. The bronze monuments of the Hippodrome — including the famous Lysippus Hercules, the Heracles by Lysippos — were melted down for coinage. The four bronze horses that had decorated the spina were carted off to Venice, where they still stand atop the Basilica of San Marco (the originals are now indoors; replicas are visible from St. Mark's Square). Hagia Sophia was looted of its silver iconostasis, its gold lamps, its ceremonial vestments. A prostitute was placed on the Patriarch's throne and made to sing obscene songs. Niketas Choniates fled the city in disguise and recorded what he had seen, as a primary source whose horror is, even nine centuries later, undimmed.

The damage was permanent. The accumulated wealth and artistic patrimony of nine centuries of Byzantine civilization was largely destroyed in three days. What was not melted down was carried off as relics and treasure to western European cathedrals, where some of it survives — the Crown of Thorns now in Notre-Dame in Paris, fragments of the True Cross in Limburg Cathedral, the Pala d'Oro altarpiece in St. Mark's Venice, all of these were Constantinopolitan loot. The Byzantine state lost its capital, its dynasty, its treasury, and its cultural inheritance simultaneously.

The crusaders elected **Baldwin of Flanders** as the new **Latin Emperor of Constantinople**. The **Latin Empire** would last from 1204 to 1261. It controlled Constantinople and its hinterland, parts of Greece, and a network of Italian-administered ports. The crusader contingents seized lordships across the former Byzantine territory: the **Duchy of Athens** (under the de la Roche family), the **Principality of Achaea** in the Peloponnese (under the Villehardouin family), the **Triarchy of Negroponte** (Euboea, divided among Venetian families). Venice itself took the lion's share — Crete, Modon and Coron in the Peloponnese, the Cycladic islands, and a quarter of Constantinople itself, including Hagia Sophia (which was converted to a Latin Catholic cathedral). The empire that Justinian had imagined as the universal Christian state was now a patchwork of Frankish lordships and Italian colonies.

---

Greek resistance reformed in three exile states.

The **Empire of Nicaea** was established in 1204 by **Theodore I Laskaris** (las-KAR-is, son-in-law of Alexios III), centered on Nicaea in northwestern Anatolia. Theodore I and his son-in-law and successor **John III Doukas Vatatzes** (vah-TAT-zeez, ruled 1221–1254) made Nicaea the most successful of the exile states. John III was a sober, methodical ruler who built up agricultural self-sufficiency (he reportedly issued chickens and grain to peasant villages to encourage food production), invested in domestic textile industries, restored a competent army, and gradually pushed the Latin Empire out of Asia Minor. Under his rule Nicaea became the de facto Byzantine state — recognized by most of the Orthodox world as the legitimate successor, with the Patriarchate of Constantinople reestablished in Nicaea and continuing to consecrate Greek bishops as if from the original see.

The **Empire of Trebizond** (TREB-ih-zond, on the Black Sea coast of northeastern Anatolia) was founded in 1204 by Alexios I Megas Komnenos and David Komnenos, grandsons of Andronikos I, with Georgian backing. Trebizond was a smaller state with a maritime focus, ruled by the **Grand Komnenoi** dynasty for the next two and a half centuries — outlasting Constantinople by eight years before falling to Mehmed II in 1461. Trebizond's culture was distinctively its own, a Byzantine fragment that had developed its own court traditions, its own historiography, and its own foreign policy.

The **Despotate of Epirus** (eh-PYE-rus, in northwestern Greece and southern Albania) was founded by **Michael I Komnenos Doukas**, an illegitimate cousin of Alexios III. Epirus had a brief moment of greatness under **Theodore Komnenos Doukas** (ruled 1215–1230), who recovered Thessalonica from the Latin Empire and crowned himself emperor — claiming to be the legitimate Byzantine ruler in opposition to the Nicaeans. Theodore's claim collapsed when he was captured by the Bulgarians at the **Battle of Klokotnitsa** in 1230, after which Epirus became a regional power without imperial pretensions.

---

The recovery of Constantinople was carried out by **Michael VIII Palaiologos** (pah-lay-oh-LOH-gos, ruled 1259–1282 as Byzantine emperor, 1261 onward in Constantinople). Michael was an Anatolian aristocrat from the Palaiologos family who had served as a general under John III Vatatzes and had risen to be regent for the boy emperor John IV Laskaris in 1258. In December 1258 Michael arranged his own elevation as senior co-emperor; in 1261 he completed the political transition by having the eleven-year-old John IV blinded and shut away in a monastery.

The reconquest of Constantinople itself was almost an accident. On July 25, 1261, a Nicaean force under the general **Alexios Strategopoulos** had been sent on a small reconnaissance mission near Constantinople. Strategopoulos discovered through local contacts that the city's Latin garrison had been mostly evacuated to attack a Venetian rival in the Black Sea, leaving the walls almost undefended. He led his small force — perhaps 800 men — through a postern gate at night, took the city without significant resistance, and welcomed Michael VIII to the Byzantine capital fifteen days later, on August 15, 1261. The Latin Emperor Baldwin II fled to western Europe to spend the rest of his life trying to organize a counter-crusade that never materialized.

The recovered capital was a wreck. Constantinople in 1261 was a city of perhaps 35,000 inhabitants — down from at least 400,000 before 1204. Whole neighborhoods were depopulated. Churches were in ruins. The aqueducts had been sabotaged. The harbor walls were collapsed. Hagia Sophia, when Michael VIII entered it for his triumphal coronation, was a stripped Catholic cathedral missing most of its gold mosaics, its silver iconostasis, its furniture, and its liturgical vessels.

Michael VIII spent the rest of his reign — twenty-one more years — trying to consolidate the recovered empire and prevent western European powers from organizing a second crusade to retake Constantinople. The most dangerous threat came from **Charles of Anjou**, the brother of King Louis IX of France, who had been installed as King of Sicily in 1266 and who spent the 1270s assembling an alliance to invade Byzantium. Michael's diplomatic response was layered. He concluded the **Union of Lyons** in 1274 — a formal reunion of the Greek and Latin churches under papal supremacy, opposed by virtually the entire Greek clergy and population, but useful as a tool for keeping the Pope from blessing Charles's invasion. He funded a Sicilian rebellion against Charles in 1282 — the **Sicilian Vespers**, a successful uprising that destroyed Charles's invasion plans by removing the staging base. The Union of Lyons was repudiated by Michael's son Andronikos II immediately after Michael's death, but the diplomatic gambit had worked: Constantinople had survived the immediate post-1261 crisis.

But the empire that Michael VIII left to his son in 1282 was a shadow. It controlled Constantinople, parts of western Anatolia, parts of Thrace, and the southern Greek mainland. It had no functioning navy. Its army was substantially mercenary. Its treasury depended on Italian merchant presence for tax revenue. The Anatolian frontier was crumbling — by the 1280s, Turkic emirates were emerging across the old Nicaean territories, and one of them, an obscure principality on the northwestern Anatolian frontier ruled by a chieftain named **Osman**, would by the next generation be calling itself the **Ottoman Beylik**.

The Byzantine restoration after 1261 had recovered the ceremonial form of empire. It had not recovered the substance.

---

# Chapter 10 — Palaiologan Twilight

The last two centuries of the Byzantine Empire are the slowest collapse in medieval history. After the recovery of 1261, the empire kept getting smaller, weaker, and poorer. Its emperors traveled across Europe as supplicants. Its generals lost battles to opponents whose grandfathers had been raiding herders. Its theologians wrote brilliant works while the territories they wrote in were incrementally swallowed up by Ottoman expansion. And yet — this is the strange thing about the Palaiologan period — Byzantine high culture experienced one last efflorescence in these decades, producing some of the most original Greek writers, painters, and theologians since late antiquity. The empire was dying creatively at its peak.

This chapter covers the period from Michael VIII's death in 1282 to the eve of the Ottoman siege of 1422 — about a hundred and forty years of contraction, civil war, theological controversy, and a precarious diplomatic dance between the Latin West, the Slavic Balkans, and the rising Turkic powers of Anatolia.

---

Michael VIII's son **Andronikos II Palaiologos** (ruled 1282–1328) was a learned, pious, fiscally cautious emperor who happened to be in charge during one of the worst possible moments in Byzantine history. He inherited a state with a ceremonial empire and a real budget shortfall. His response was to cut spending. The single most consequential cut was the dismantling of the Byzantine navy — Andronikos II saved money by laying up the imperial fleet and contracting maritime defense to **Genoese** mercenaries. The decision had immediate consequences and long-term ones. The immediate consequence was that the Genoese, paid to protect Byzantine shipping, used their position to dominate Black Sea and Aegean commerce — establishing a fortified colony at **Galata** (the district of Constantinople across the Golden Horn from the imperial city, which became a Genoese walled enclave with its own laws, its own taxes, and its own fleet, all explicitly outside Byzantine sovereignty). The long-term consequence was that Byzantium permanently lost the capacity to defend itself by sea. Every Byzantine emperor after Andronikos II had to ask Italians for naval help.

The other major Andronikos II disaster was the **Catalan Company**. In 1303, with the Anatolian frontier collapsing under Turkic pressure, Andronikos hired a force of about 6,500 Catalan and Aragonese mercenaries — the **Almogavars**, veterans of the Sicilian Vespers wars, commanded by the unstable **Roger de Flor** — to push back the Turks. The Catalans were spectacularly effective in their first campaign, breaking Turkic siege lines and recovering territory. They were also impossible to control. They demanded enormous wages, plundered the Byzantine countryside they were supposed to be liberating, and got into murderous quarrels with their Byzantine sponsors. In April 1305, Andronikos's son and co-emperor Michael IX arranged the assassination of Roger de Flor at a banquet. The Catalans took this as license for open war. They sacked Thrace and Macedonia for the next four years — the so-called **Catalan Vengeance** — and finally moved south, seized the **Duchy of Athens** from its Frankish ruler in 1311 at the **Battle of Halmyros**, and ruled it as the **Catalan Duchy of Athens** for the next seventy-seven years. The empire had succeeded in pushing one foreign occupier off its territory only by hiring another and then losing control of him completely.

---

Then came the civil wars — and this is where the empire signed its eventual death warrant. The single most consequential decision of the entire Palaiologan period was made by a Byzantine claimant during one of these civil wars, when John Kantakouzenos invited the Ottomans across the strait into Europe to fight on his behalf. The Ottomans came, won the war, and then refused to leave. Every detail of the genealogical complexity that follows is in service of one outcome: a Byzantine emperor handed the Turks their permanent European foothold, and the empire never recovered from it. Track who is fighting whom if you can; if you can't, just hold onto that single fact.

Andronikos II's grandson **Andronikos III** rebelled against him in 1321, kicking off seven years of intermittent civil war that destroyed what was left of imperial finances and dragged Bulgarian and Serbian armies into Byzantine domestic politics. Andronikos III finally won in 1328, reigned competently until 1341, and then died at age 45, leaving the throne to his nine-year-old son **John V Palaiologos** under the regency of John's mother **Anna of Savoy** and the senior nobleman **John Kantakouzenos**.

The regency arrangement collapsed within months. Kantakouzenos and the regency council fell out, and the dispute escalated into the **Byzantine Civil War of 1341–1347** — a six-year conflict that did the empire more damage than any external war since 1204. The civil war was simultaneously a dynastic dispute (regency vs. Kantakouzenos), a class conflict (rural aristocracy vs. urban populist movements, with the **Zealot** revolutionary government taking control of Thessalonica from 1342–1349 in a remarkable experiment in popular government), and a theological dispute (about which more in a moment). The fighting drew in Bulgarians, Serbs, and — most consequentially — **Ottomans**.

It was during this civil war, in 1346, that Kantakouzenos invited **Ottoman** mercenaries from the emir Orhan to fight on his behalf. Kantakouzenos married his daughter Theodora to Orhan as part of the alliance. The Ottoman troops crossed into Europe to support Kantakouzenos's faction — the first Ottoman armies to fight on European soil. They returned to Anatolia after their initial campaigns, but the precedent had been set. In 1352 Kantakouzenos called them back for a renewed civil war. This time they did not fully leave. In 1354 a major earthquake destroyed the walls of the Byzantine fortress at **Gallipoli** on the European side of the Dardanelles. Orhan's forces, conveniently nearby, occupied the ruined fortress and refused to give it back. The Ottomans had their permanent foothold in Europe, and they had it because a Byzantine emperor had hired them, married into them, and called them across the strait twice. The empire had handed its eventual conquerors a bridgehead.

The civil war also destroyed the empire economically. The currency was debased again and again. The army was in tatters. The Italian merchant communities — Genoese in Galata, Venetian in their own enclave — extracted more privileges in exchange for emergency loans. By the time Kantakouzenos finally took the throne as **John VI** in 1347 (and then graciously stepped aside in 1354 to retire as a monk and write his own apologetic history of the war), the empire he had won was not worth winning.

The same year John VI took the throne, the **Black Death** arrived in Constantinople. The Black Death was the second great plague pandemic — the same disease (*Yersinia pestis*, bubonic plague) that had caused the Plague of Justinian eight centuries earlier (Chapter 2). The Constantinopolitan outbreak of 1347 killed between a third and half of the city's surviving population. The empire's already collapsed tax base contracted further. Repeated plague outbreaks over the following decades kept the population suppressed. The Black Death was the worst demographic catastrophe in Byzantine history.

---

The civil war was tangled with one of the most distinctive theological controversies in late medieval Christianity: **Hesychasm** (HESS-ih-kazm).

Hesychasm was a contemplative monastic practice — derived from the Greek *hesychia*, "stillness" — that had been developing on Mount Athos for centuries. The practice involved silent prayer (the **Jesus Prayer**: "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me"), repeated continuously, often coordinated with breathing. Advanced practitioners reported that this practice could produce visions of an **uncreated divine light** — the same light that the disciples Peter, James, and John had seen at Christ's Transfiguration on Mount Tabor, according to Hesychast theology. The light was not a physical phenomenon nor a metaphor; it was, the Hesychasts argued, a direct experience of the **uncreated energies** of God.

This claim provoked a storm. A Calabrian Greek monk named **Barlaam** (BAR-lahm) attacked Hesychasm as superstitious and theologically incoherent — God's essence is unknowable to created beings, Barlaam argued, and any claim to direct experience of God's light was either a hallucination or heresy. The Hesychast response came from **Gregory Palamas** (PAL-uh-mass, c. 1296–1359), a Mount Athos monk who would become Archbishop of Thessalonica. Palamas developed a sophisticated distinction between God's **essence** (*ousia*, fully transcendent and unknowable) and God's **energies** (*energeia*, the activities and operations through which God interacts with creation, knowable to humans through grace). The uncreated light was an energy, not the essence — and direct experience of the uncreated energies was therefore both possible and theologically orthodox.

The Hesychast controversy ran through the 1330s and 1340s, intersected with the civil war (Kantakouzenos was a Hesychast supporter, the regency tended Barlaamite), and was finally settled in Palamas's favor at a series of councils at Constantinople in 1341, 1347, and 1351. Palamism became official Eastern Orthodox doctrine. Palamas himself was canonized in 1368, less than a decade after his death.

The intellectual stakes here are larger than they might appear. Palamism resolved one of the deepest tensions in Christian theology — between God's transcendence and God's accessibility — in a way that would shape all subsequent Eastern Orthodox spirituality. The Western Catholic and Protestant traditions did not adopt the essence-energies distinction, and it remains one of the defining theological differences between Eastern and Western Christianity. The contemplative Hesychast tradition Palamas defended also became the dominant spiritual practice in Russian, Romanian, and Greek monasticism, producing a continuous lineage of spiritual writers — most famously the eighteenth-century compilation called the **Philokalia** — that remains the heart of Orthodox spiritual practice today.

That all of this happened during the worst political collapse in Byzantine history is one of the strangest features of the Palaiologan period. The empire was dying. The theology being produced inside it was as ambitious and as influential as anything in the Christian tradition.

---

Outside the theological sphere, the empire's political deterioration accelerated.

In 1331 the Anatolian city of **Nicaea** — the empire's wartime capital from 1204 to 1261, the city of the First and Second Ecumenical Councils — fell to Sultan **Orhan** of the Ottomans. The fall of Nicaea was a profound symbolic blow. The empire that had taken its name from the city for half a century had now lost it permanently to the Turks. Andronikos III had attempted to relieve the city in 1329 at the **Battle of Pelekanon** (peh-leh-kah-NON, on the southern shore of the Sea of Marmara) and had been defeated by Orhan personally. Pelekanon ended any realistic Byzantine hope of reconquering northwestern Anatolia. By 1340 the Ottomans had taken **Nicomedia** (NI-koh-MEE-dee-ah, the old eastern capital under Diocletian, the city where Constantine had died — modern İzmit) and most of the Bithynian coast. The empire's Anatolian provinces were reduced to a handful of coastal footholds.

The other major external threat was the **Serbian Empire** under **Stefan Dušan** (DOO-shan, ruled 1331–1355), a brilliant Serbian king who exploited the Byzantine civil war to expand Serbian rule across most of the Balkans — taking Macedonia, Albania, Epirus, and parts of Thessaly, and crowning himself "Emperor of the Serbs and Greeks" in 1346. Dušan's empire briefly rivaled Byzantium itself in extent and was, by the 1340s, the major power of the southern Balkans. He died unexpectedly in 1355 at age 47, possibly poisoned, and his empire fragmented within a few years — but his reign had permanently broken Byzantine control of the southern Balkans, and the Serbian successor states that emerged from his death were weak enough that they could not effectively resist Ottoman expansion either.

In 1369 the Ottomans took **Adrianople** (modern Edirne in European Turkey) and made it their new capital. Adrianople had been a Byzantine city for over a thousand years — site of the disastrous Roman defeat by the Visigoths in 378 (Ancient Rome Ch 9), of the major Bulgarian victory over Krum's army in 813, of countless other Byzantine military campaigns. Its loss meant that the Ottomans now controlled the entirety of Thrace except for Constantinople itself. The empire was reduced to its capital, a few coastal cities (Thessalonica, briefly recovered for the empire in the 1370s), and a few exclaves in Greece (the **Despotate of the Morea**, in the Peloponnese, ruled by Palaiologan princes). Constantinople was a Byzantine island in an Ottoman sea.

In 1389 the Ottoman victory at the **Battle of Kosovo** over the Serbs eliminated Byzantium's last potential Balkan ally. The Bulgarian state had already been destroyed (Tarnovo fell to the Ottomans in 1393). The Byzantine emperor became, in formal terms, an **Ottoman vassal** — required to pay tribute, provide troops for Ottoman campaigns, and travel to the sultan's court when summoned. This was the most humiliating arrangement in nine centuries of Byzantine sovereignty, and it was the price of Constantinople's survival.

---

A small intellectual aside before this chapter ends. The Palaiologan period produced, despite its political miseries, an extraordinary cultural renaissance.

**Demetrios Kydones** (kih-DOH-neez, c. 1324–1398) was an imperial minister and translator who rendered Thomas Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* into Greek — opening Latin scholastic theology to Byzantine readers and provoking a generation of Greek engagement with Western philosophy. The mosaics and frescoes of the **Chora Church** in Constantinople (now the Kariye Mosque, restored to mosque status in 2020) were completed in the early fourteenth century under the patronage of Theodore Metochites and represent the artistic peak of Byzantine icon painting — sophisticated, emotionally intense, technically brilliant. The historian **John Kantakouzenos**, after his abdication, wrote a four-volume history of his own civil war that is one of the most readable late Byzantine works. The philosopher **Nikephoros Gregoras** wrote a contemporary Roman History covering 1204–1359 that, despite being a defeated Barlaamite polemic, contains essential historical information available nowhere else.

And in the 1390s, in the Despotate of the Morea, the most original Byzantine philosopher of the late Middle Ages began his career: **Gemistos Plethon** (PLEH-thon, c. 1355–1452). Plethon was a Platonist who pushed the Byzantine engagement with classical philosophy further than anyone before him — eventually proposing, late in life, a return to a reformed version of Greek polytheism as the basis for a renewed Hellenic civilization. His more conventional contemporaries considered him a crypto-pagan. His later influence on Italian Renaissance Platonism — through his appearance at the Council of Florence in 1439 and his impact on the young Cosimo de' Medici, who would found the Florentine Platonic Academy on his account — would be enormous (Chapter 12). Plethon is one of the strangest figures in Byzantine intellectual history, and one of the most consequential bridges between the Byzantine and Renaissance Italian worlds.

---

The empire's last reprieve came from an unexpected direction. On July 28, 1402, at the **Battle of Ankara** in central Anatolia, the Ottoman sultan **Bayezid I** ("the Thunderbolt") was decisively defeated by **Tamerlane** — **Timur** (TEE-moor, c. 1336–1405), the Turkic conqueror who had built a vast empire stretching from Anatolia to India and was now sweeping through the Anatolian heartland. Bayezid was captured and died in captivity the following year. The Ottoman state fragmented into a civil war among Bayezid's sons that ran from 1402 to 1413, with each son claiming the sultanate from a different city.

For Byzantium, Tamerlane's victory was an unexpected gift. The pressure on Constantinople collapsed. The Byzantines, who had been on the verge of being besieged in 1402 — Bayezid had been preparing the assault — were suddenly safe for the first time in two generations. Emperor **Manuel II Palaiologos** (ruled 1391–1425), who had spent the years 1399–1402 traveling through western Europe (Venice, Paris, London) trying to drum up military support against the Ottomans (and being received with respectful sympathy but no concrete aid), returned to a city that had been spared by an entirely external event. Manuel II's tour is itself a striking piece of Byzantine history — the Roman emperor traveling as an honored guest through cities that had once been Roman provinces but were now sovereign states whose rulers he had to flatter for favors. He spent Christmas 1400 at the court of Henry IV in London. He was painted in his imperial robes by an English manuscript illustrator. He met scholars across Italy and France. He returned having extracted nothing concrete but having impressed the Latin West with the dignity and erudition of the dying empire.

Tamerlane died in 1405. The Ottoman civil war ended in 1413 with the victory of **Mehmed I** (1413–1421), who reconsolidated the Ottoman state. By 1422 his son **Murad II** had besieged Constantinople for the first time. The siege failed — the Theodosian Walls held, as they always had — but it was the first time in nearly a century that the Ottomans had directly assaulted the imperial city. The reprieve was over. The next siege would be the final one.

---

# Chapter 11 — The Last Hundred Years and the Fall

The empire's last half-century is the most thoroughly documented period of its history. Eyewitness accounts of the final siege of 1453 survive in remarkable abundance — Greek chronicles, Italian merchants' diaries, Ottoman annals, Spanish military reports — and the events have been picked over by historians for five and a half centuries. What follows is the conventional outline, briefly extended where the documentary record is particularly rich.

The fall of Constantinople is one of those events where the actors knew, at the time, that they were participating in a historic catastrophe. The contemporaries who wrote about it described it as the end of an age. They were not exaggerating. Whether you take 1453 as the symbolic end of the Middle Ages, the real end of the Roman Empire, the start of the Renaissance, the start of the Age of Exploration (the closure of the eastern Mediterranean to European Christian shipping was a major motivation for Iberian exploration of the Atlantic in the late fifteenth century), or simply the disappearance of a particular medieval Christian civilization that had been continuously present since 330 CE — every framing has its merits, and they are all true at once.

This chapter is the story of how it happened.

---

Manuel II's son **John VIII Palaiologos** (ruled 1425–1448) inherited a diminished and frightened empire. The 1422 siege had failed, but Murad II had subsequently taken Thessalonica (in 1430, after a long Venetian-administered defense) and had reduced Byzantine territory to Constantinople, the Despotate of the Morea, and a few coastal exclaves. John VIII spent his reign on a single strategic gamble: securing western military aid by submitting the Greek church to Roman papal authority.

This was the **Council of Florence**, held intermittently between 1438 and 1445 (originally convened at Ferrara, then moved to Florence due to a plague outbreak). It was the most ambitious church council since Chalcedon nearly a thousand years earlier. The Greek delegation included Emperor John VIII himself, Patriarch Joseph II of Constantinople, the leading Byzantine theologians (including **Bessarion** of Trebizond, who would become the most consequential Greek scholar in Renaissance Italy, and **Mark of Ephesus**, who would lead the resistance to union), and the elderly Plethon, who attended as a court philosopher and used his time in Florence to give lectures on Plato that would profoundly influence Cosimo de' Medici.

The theological negotiations were conducted in good faith. The Greeks and Latins discussed the **Filioque** clause, papal supremacy, purgatory, the use of unleavened bread in the Eucharist, and several smaller points of difference. They reached a series of compromises that the Greek delegation signed at the formal **Decree of Union** on July 6, 1439. The decree affirmed papal supremacy ("the Roman pontiff is the successor of Peter, prince of the apostles, and true vicar of Christ"), accepted the Filioque as an orthodox formulation in either creed, and acknowledged the Catholic doctrine of purgatory.

Mark of Ephesus refused to sign. The Greek populace, when the delegation returned to Constantinople, refused to accept the union. The leading anti-unionist theologian **George Scholarios** (later Patriarch Gennadios II) preached against it. The famous quip — attributed (probably apocryphally) to the Byzantine grand admiral **Loukas Notaras** — captured popular sentiment: "Better the Sultan's turban in the city than the Cardinal's mitre." The Greek clergy, except for the bishops who had personally signed in Florence, refused to commemorate the Pope in their liturgies. The Union of Florence became dead letter within a few years of being signed.

The price the Greeks were supposed to receive was western military aid. They received some — Pope Eugene IV organized a crusade in 1443–1444 led by the Polish-Hungarian king **Vladislav III** and the Hungarian general **John Hunyadi**. The crusade made initial progress in the Balkans but was destroyed by the Ottomans at the **Battle of Varna** on November 10, 1444 — Vladislav was killed, the crusader army shattered, and any prospect of large-scale European military intervention against the Ottomans evaporated. The Union had been signed without producing the promised aid. The Greeks had compromised their religious autonomy for nothing. The unionist cause was now hated by the Greek population while the empire remained as exposed as before.

---

Murad II died in February 1451. He was succeeded by his nineteen-year-old son **Mehmed II** — the man who would take Constantinople.

Mehmed II is one of the most studied conquerors in medieval history. He was extraordinarily intelligent, extensively educated (fluent in Turkish, Arabic, Persian, Greek, and Hebrew, with serious interests in Greek philosophy, geography, and military engineering), ruthless, ambitious, and from the moment of his accession committed to one specific goal: the conquest of Constantinople. His grandfather Bayezid I had besieged it. His father Murad II had besieged it. His own first reign — he had briefly held the throne as a teenager from 1444–1446 before being pushed aside by his father — had ended in part because he had been considered too inexperienced to attempt the siege. Mehmed had returned to the throne determined to finish what his ancestors had started.

His preparations took two years. He built a fortress called **Rumeli Hisarı** (roo-MEH-lee hee-SAH-ruh, "the European Castle") on the European side of the Bosporus in 1452, opposite an existing Ottoman fortress on the Anatolian side, sealing the strait between the Black Sea and Constantinople — meaning that no grain could reach the city from the Black Sea ports without Ottoman permission. He commissioned a Hungarian engineer named **Orban** to cast the largest cannons the world had ever seen — bronze monsters firing stone balls weighing up to 1,200 pounds, requiring teams of sixty oxen to move them. The largest, called the **Basilica**, had a 27-foot barrel and could throw a stone ball nearly a mile. Orban had originally offered his services to the Byzantines, who could not pay his price; he had then approached Mehmed, who could.

Mehmed assembled an army of perhaps 80,000 to 100,000 men. He assembled a fleet of perhaps 100 to 150 ships. He had the new gunpowder weapons. He had time — the Hungarians, the Venetians, and the Pope were all distracted by their own concerns and would not provide major aid. The defenders inside Constantinople numbered perhaps 7,000 men, including 700 Genoese mercenaries under **Giovanni Giustiniani Longo**, a Genoese soldier of the Lomellini family who had volunteered to come and fight — bringing his own troops at his own expense — in what he correctly understood as the great Christian cause of the age. Other Italian volunteers swelled the total. The Byzantine citizen militia could perhaps add a few thousand more. The Theodosian Walls and the Golden Horn chain were the empire's last assets.

The emperor was **Constantine XI Palaiologos** (ruled 1449–1453), John VIII's brother, who had been Despot of the Morea before being summoned to Constantinople on his brother's death. Constantine XI was an experienced soldier, a personally brave man, and — perhaps most importantly — a recognizable figure of dignity in a moment that demanded one. He had inherited an impossible task. He performed it with a kind of doomed integrity that has made him the most legendary of Byzantine emperors.

---

The siege began on April 6, 1453.

For fifty-three days the Ottoman army and fleet pressed against the city. The cannons hammered the Theodosian Walls at multiple points, opening breaches that the defenders desperately repaired with rubble, earth, and bundled timber overnight, only to have the same breaches opened again the next day. The Ottoman fleet, blocked by the Golden Horn chain (a great iron chain stretched across the harbor mouth from Galata to the imperial city, supported by floating logs), tried to force the entrance and was beaten back by Greek fire and superior Christian seamanship. In an extraordinary feat of engineering on the night of April 22, Mehmed's engineers built a greased wooden ramp over the hill behind Galata and dragged seventy ships overland into the upper Golden Horn, neutralizing the chain and adding a new front to the siege. The defenders had to spread their already-thin manpower thinner.

The pivotal events of the siege are well-documented in eyewitness accounts:

**Nicolò Barbaro**, a Venetian surgeon and ship's officer who kept a daily diary of the siege, recorded the cannon fire, the failed assaults, the morale of the defenders, the desertions, the flares of hope and despair. His account is dry, practical, and detailed.

**George Sphrantzes** (sfrant-ZEEZ), Constantine XI's secretary and lifelong friend, survived the fall and wrote the *Memoirs* — a Byzantine chronicle of the last decades that includes intimate details of life inside the besieged palace. Sphrantzes was an unusually reliable witness; his account of the emperor's last days has been the basis for every subsequent treatment.

**Leonard of Chios**, a Latin archbishop present in the city during the siege, wrote a letter to Pope Nicholas V immediately after escaping that summarized the fall in considerable tactical detail. Leonard was hostile to the Greek defenders (he considered them inadequately committed to the union of 1439) and his account is biased, but it is contemporary.

**Tursun Beg**, a young Ottoman official who was present, wrote a Turkish chronicle of Mehmed's reign that includes a sympathetic Turkish-side account of the siege.

These sources are largely consistent on the major facts. Constantinople was running out of men, ammunition, food, and morale. The walls were breaking down faster than they could be repaired. The Genoese in Galata maintained an officially neutral stance — declining to fight on either side openly — while clandestinely passing information and supplies to both sides, an approach that infuriated everyone involved. The Italian volunteers and the Greek defenders fought together with surprising solidarity given the deep unionist-anti-unionist tensions of the previous decades. Constantine XI personally rotated through the threatened sections of the wall, encouraging the defenders, sleeping in armor, eating what his men ate. The siege had become an endurance contest that the Byzantines were slowly losing.

---

The final assault came on the night of May 28–29, 1453.

Mehmed had been planning a single concentrated attack to overwhelm the exhausted defenders. The assault began about an hour before dawn on the 29th. The first wave of irregular Ottoman troops — the *bashi-bazouks*, expendable forces sent in to wear down the defense — was thrown against the walls and largely destroyed. The second wave of Anatolian regulars achieved a partial breach but was repelled by Giustiniani's Genoese counterattack at the **Saint Romanus Gate** — the section of the walls where the most concentrated cannon damage had occurred and which had become the primary point of pressure.

Then the **Janissaries** went in. The Janissaries (yan-iss-AIR-eez) were the elite Ottoman infantry corps and Mehmed's personal guard — his last and best assault force. They were recruited through the *devshirme* system: Christian boys from Balkan villages were taken from their families, converted to Islam, given specialized military training, and made to serve as the sultan's personal slave-soldiers. The system produced extraordinarily disciplined and fanatically loyal troops. Led by the sultan in person, they pressed through the breach, encountered exhausted defenders, and broke them.

The decisive moment, according to the eyewitness accounts, was the wounding of Giovanni Giustiniani Longo. Hit by an Ottoman bullet in the chest, Giustiniani gave the order for his Genoese troops to evacuate him to the inner walls — a decision that, in the chaos of the assault, was taken by other troops as a general retreat. The Greek defenders, seeing the Italians fall back, broke also. Constantine XI rode to the breach personally to try to rally them, was unable to hold the line, and — according to legend, which is consistent with several eyewitness accounts though no one survived who actually saw it — threw off his imperial purple robes and rushed into the press of Ottoman troops to die fighting as a common soldier. His body was never definitely identified. There have been periodic later "discoveries" of imperial remains that have all been disputed. The most likely truth is that Constantine XI was killed in the breach, his body stripped and lost in the general slaughter.

The Ottomans poured into the city. Resistance collapsed. The **Hagia Sophia**, full of refugees praying through the night, was broken open. The men were killed or taken as slaves; the women and children sold. Mehmed had ordered three days of plunder by custom but cut it short to one day on the evening of May 29 itself — his troops had become uncontrollable, and he wanted to preserve as much of the city as possible for his new capital. He rode to the Hagia Sophia in the late afternoon and ordered the building consecrated as a mosque. The first Islamic prayers in the cathedral were performed on Friday, June 1, 1453.

The Byzantine Empire was over.

---

A few details from the immediate aftermath, since they came up in earlier chapters and deserve closing:

The grand admiral **Loukas Notaras**, the leading Byzantine official who survived the siege, was initially treated honorably by Mehmed and offered a position in Ottoman service. He refused. He was executed several days later, along with his sons, after a series of incidents whose exact nature is disputed.

**Bessarion**, who had signed the Florence Union and converted to Catholicism, settled in Italy as a cardinal, founded an important academy of Greek studies at his home in Rome, and donated his great library of Greek manuscripts to Venice — the **Biblioteca Marciana**, which still holds thousands of Byzantine texts that would otherwise have been lost when Constantinople fell.

**George Scholarios**, the leading anti-unionist, was named **Patriarch Gennadios II** by Mehmed himself a few months after the conquest. Mehmed wanted a stable Greek Orthodox church in his new capital and considered the unionist faction discredited; the anti-unionist Scholarios was the obvious candidate to head the surviving Christian institutions under Ottoman rule.

**Trebizond**, the last remaining Byzantine successor state, fell to Mehmed in 1461. Its ruler, **David Komnenos**, was executed with most of his family. The line of the Grand Komnenoi was extinguished.

The **Despotate of the Morea**, ruled by Constantine XI's brothers Demetrios and Thomas Palaiologos, fell to the Ottomans in 1460. Thomas escaped to Italy with his children. His daughter **Zoe Palaiologina** (later renamed Sophia) eventually traveled north and married **Grand Duke Ivan III of Moscow** in 1472 — a marriage that gave the Russian princes a credible claim to the Byzantine inheritance and that supplied them with the Byzantine double-headed eagle as the symbol of the Russian state, which is still on the Russian flag today.

The empire was gone. The story of what came after — the cultural, religious, and political afterlife of Byzantium — is the subject of the final chapter.

# Chapter 12 — The Inheritance

When something dies after eleven hundred and twenty-three years, it does not disappear cleanly. The Byzantine Empire's physical territory was erased from political maps in May 1453. Its institutions, its texts, its art, its religion, its political ideas, and a substantial fraction of its population continued to exist — in Ottoman territory under new management, in Italy as Greek refugees, in Russia and the Slavic Orthodox world as the senior partner in a shared faith, in Western Europe as a recovered classical inheritance newly available to scholars who had never been able to read it.

This chapter is about the afterlife. It is shorter than the others because most of it has been alluded to throughout the book — every chapter has been pointing at the empire's eventual disappearance and its eventual influence on the world that followed. What this chapter does is collect those threads into one place. Byzantium did not so much fall as scatter. The fragments are still around us.

---

Start with the **Greek scholars in Italy**.

The fall of Constantinople accelerated a migration that had been happening for decades. From the Council of Florence in 1438–1439 onward, Byzantine scholars had been traveling to Italy in increasing numbers — sometimes for diplomatic missions, sometimes to teach Greek to Italian humanists, sometimes as religious refugees fleeing Ottoman expansion. After 1453 the trickle became a flood. Scholars who had survived the siege escaped to Italy with whatever manuscripts they could carry. Byzantine families that had cultivated Greek scholarship for generations relocated to Florence, Rome, Venice, Padua, Ferrara.

The cumulative impact on Italian culture was enormous. Italian humanists had been recovering Latin classical literature for two centuries — that was the **Renaissance** as it had existed up to 1450. But Greek had been functionally inaccessible. Plato existed in fragmentary Latin paraphrases. Aristotle existed in Latin translations from Arabic translations from Greek originals. The major works of Greek philosophy, drama, history, poetry, and theology had not been read in the Latin West since the early Middle Ages. The Byzantine refugees brought the originals, knew the language fluently, and were willing to teach.

The figures involved are well-known to historians of the Renaissance. **Manuel Chrysoloras** (kris-oh-LOH-ras, c. 1355–1415) had taught Greek in Florence as early as 1397 and trained the first generation of Italian Greek scholars. **Bessarion** (Chapter 11), now Cardinal Bessarion, established a Greek manuscript collection in Rome that became the largest in the West and donated it to Venice on his death — over six hundred manuscripts, the foundation of the modern Biblioteca Marciana, preserving texts that would otherwise have been lost. **Gemistos Plethon**, attending the Council of Florence as an old man, gave lectures on Plato that so impressed Cosimo de' Medici that Cosimo founded the **Florentine Platonic Academy** under **Marsilio Ficino**, which translated Plato's complete works into Latin for the first time and produced the Renaissance Neoplatonism that would shape European philosophy for the next two centuries. **John Argyropoulos** (ar-jeer-OH-poo-los) taught Greek at the universities of Florence and Rome. **Demetrius Chalcondyles** edited the first printed edition of Homer in 1488. **Aldus Manutius**, the Venetian printer, employed Cretan Greek refugees to produce the **Aldine** editions of the Greek classics in the 1490s and 1500s — the books that gave the rest of Europe its first reliable access to Plato, Aristotle, Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes, Thucydides, and the other foundational Greek texts.

The standard claim that the fall of Constantinople "caused" the Renaissance is too strong — the Italian Renaissance had its own deep roots, and the recovery of Greek was already underway before 1453. But the Byzantine refugees of the 1450s and 1460s accelerated and shaped that recovery. They brought the manuscripts, the linguistic competence, and the philosophical traditions that turned the Italian Renaissance into a serious engagement with Greek thought rather than a mostly-Latin enterprise. The Byzantine scholars who fled the dying empire were the indispensable middlemen of one of the largest cultural transmissions in European history.

---

The second major inheritance was **religious**.

The Eastern Orthodox Church survived the fall. Mehmed II, with characteristic political acumen, recognized that the Greek population of his new capital and his Balkan territories needed continued ecclesiastical leadership. He appointed George Scholarios as **Patriarch Gennadios II** in 1454 and granted him civil authority over the Christian populations of the empire — an arrangement called the **Rum millet** (the "Roman community," using *Rum* in its Ottoman sense as a designation for the Greek Orthodox Christians). Under the Ottoman millet system, the Patriarch of Constantinople became the head of an autonomous Christian community within the Ottoman state, with jurisdiction over religious affairs, family law, education, and most internal matters of the Greek population. The Patriarchate continued to function in Constantinople — operating from new premises since the Hagia Sophia was now a mosque — and it continued to function as the senior see of the Eastern Orthodox communion.

This Ottoman-era patriarchate was the institutional vehicle through which Greek Orthodox identity, language, and culture survived the centuries between the fall and the Greek War of Independence in 1821. The Patriarchate was politically constrained, financially squeezed, and periodically harassed by Ottoman administration, but it kept functioning. It maintained the liturgical traditions, the canonical texts, the educational institutions, and the historical memory of Byzantium under conditions where independent Greek political existence was impossible. When modern Greek nationalism emerged in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, it drew its sense of continuity directly from the Patriarchate — the institutional bridge between the Byzantine past and a modern Greek future.

The patriarchates of **Antioch**, **Alexandria**, and **Jerusalem** — the other ancient Eastern Orthodox sees — had continued under Muslim rule since the seventh century and continued under Ottoman rule afterward. They formed, with Constantinople, the four ancient Eastern patriarchates of the Greek-speaking Orthodox world. Their existence and continuity meant that Eastern Orthodoxy — distinct from both Roman Catholicism and Protestant traditions — survived as a major Christian communion through the post-Byzantine centuries.

---

The third inheritance was **Russian**.

The marriage of **Zoe Palaiologina** to Grand Duke **Ivan III of Moscow** in 1472 (Chapter 11) gave the Muscovite princes a credible claim to the Byzantine succession at the moment when Russian territorial expansion and political consolidation were beginning in earnest. Ivan III adopted the **double-headed eagle** as the symbol of the Russian state. He took to using imperial Byzantine ceremonial protocols at his court. His grandson Ivan IV (Ivan the Terrible) took the title **Tsar** — the Russian form of *Caesar* — in 1547 in a self-coronation explicitly modeled on Byzantine imperial ritual. The **Patriarchate of Moscow** was established in 1589, with Constantinople's reluctant approval, formalizing Russia's claim to be an autocephalous (independent) Orthodox church.

The doctrine that Moscow was the **Third Rome** — first formulated by the monk Philotheus of Pskov in a letter to Grand Duke Vasily III around 1510 — became central to Russian political-religious identity. The argument went: the first Rome had fallen to barbarians and heresy in the fifth century. The second Rome (Constantinople) had fallen to Turks because of the Greek capitulation to Catholicism at the Council of Florence. The third Rome was Moscow, the last surviving seat of true Orthodox Christianity, the rightful heir of the imperial Christian tradition. "And a fourth there shall not be." The argument shaped Russian political and religious self-understanding from the sixteenth century onward, and it surfaced repeatedly through the imperial period — every time a Tsar claimed the right to intervene in Balkan or Middle Eastern politics on behalf of Orthodox Christians, the Third Rome argument was somewhere in the background.

The Russian Orthodox Church — the largest of the modern Orthodox churches — is, in this sense, the institutional descendant of Constantinople. Its liturgical practice, its theology, its monastic tradition (much of it transplanted from Mount Athos via the medieval Russian monasteries), its iconography, and its self-understanding all derive directly from Byzantium. The same is true, in different proportions, of the Romanian, Bulgarian, Serbian, Ukrainian, and Georgian Orthodox churches. Roughly a quarter of a billion Christians in the world today belong to churches that trace their religious inheritance directly to medieval Constantinople.

The fourth inheritance was **Ottoman**.

This is the inheritance Western Europeans have been most resistant to acknowledging, but it is real and substantial. Mehmed II was an admirer of Byzantine institutions. He preserved the Patriarchate, the millet system, the city's basic administrative geography, and many of its trade relationships. He kept Hagia Sophia standing rather than demolishing it. He commissioned the historian **Critobulus of Imbros** to write a history of his own conquests in classical Greek, modeled on Thucydides. He patronized Italian Renaissance painters (the famous portrait of Mehmed by Gentile Bellini, made on commission in 1480, was the first realistic European portrait of an Ottoman sultan). He saw himself, plausibly, as the legitimate heir of the Roman imperial tradition — taking the title **Kayser-i Rûm** ("Caesar of Rome") and treating Constantinople as the natural seat of his expanding empire.

The Ottoman state that grew out of Mehmed's conquests retained many Byzantine features. The administrative geography — the sanjaks and provinces — followed Byzantine themes in their broad outlines. The agricultural taxation system inherited Byzantine surveys and assessment practices. The Greek Orthodox clergy continued to perform functions for the Greek population that Byzantine clergy had performed before. The Ottoman court ceremony, while heavily influenced by Persian and Turkic precedents, also borrowed from Byzantine protocols — particularly in the elaborate reception of foreign ambassadors, where the Ottoman ceremony of the elevated throne and the silent corps of officials owed something to the Byzantine machinery of intimidation. The classical Ottoman style of mosque architecture — exemplified by the works of **Mimar Sinan** (mee-MAR see-NAHN) in the sixteenth century — was a direct descendant of Hagia Sophia, with its central dome on pendentives, its half-domes on the four sides, and its ring of windows lighting the interior. The Süleymaniye Mosque (1557) and the Selimiye Mosque in Edirne (1574) are not just Hagia Sophia's children; they are arguably its best children, more rationally laid out, structurally more sophisticated, more elegantly proportioned. Sinan was working in a tradition Anthemius and Isidore had founded ten centuries earlier.

The Greek-speaking population of Anatolia and the Balkans continued to live in Ottoman territory, often in the same villages and cities they had inhabited before the conquest, often speaking the same Greek dialects, often worshipping in the same churches under the new millet arrangement. The Ottoman conquest was not a population replacement. It was a change in management, with substantial continuity at the local level. By the time the Ottoman Empire itself collapsed in the early twentieth century, the Greek population of Anatolia had been there continuously for nearly three thousand years — through Hittites (Hittite Empire Ch 8), Phrygians, Lydians, Persians, Alexander, the Hellenistic kingdoms, Romans, Byzantines, Seljuks, and Ottomans. The Greek-Turkish population exchange of 1923 — when 1.2 million Greeks were expelled from Anatolia and 400,000 Turks from Greece — was the final, brutal end of that continuity.

The fifth inheritance was **legal**.

Justinian's **Corpus Juris Civilis** — the codification of Roman law that had been completed in 534 (Chapter 2) — became the foundation of European civil law in a process that began in the late eleventh century and continued through the early modern period. The rediscovery of the *Digest* at Bologna around 1070 launched the medieval revival of Roman law. The Bologna law school became the major center of legal education in medieval Europe. Italian civilians (lawyers trained in the Justinianic tradition) taught at every European university in the late Middle Ages. The codified legal systems of France, Germany, Spain, Italy, the Netherlands, Quebec, Louisiana, Latin America, and Japan all derive — through complex chains of transmission — from Justinian's commission.

The English-speaking common law tradition is the major exception to this pattern, and even it has been substantially influenced by Roman law concepts (much of the law of contracts, trusts, and equity has Roman roots, and several common-law jurists trained on the continent imported Justinianic principles directly). Most of the world's legal systems today — every system except the common law and the various Islamic, Confucian, and traditional African and Asian systems — descend from a project initiated by an emperor in Constantinople fifteen hundred years ago. The empire is gone. The law it codified is the operational legal system of about half the planet.

The sixth and most diffuse inheritance is **iconographic**.

Western European Christian art, particularly Roman Catholic art, drew constantly on Byzantine models from the early Middle Ages onward. The development of devotional images of Christ, the Virgin, and the saints in Western Europe followed Byzantine iconographic conventions for centuries — the seated Theotokos and Child, the Crucifixion, the Last Supper, the Pantocrator, the standard saint poses, the gold backgrounds. Italian altarpieces of the late medieval and early Renaissance period (Cimabue, Duccio, the early Giotto) are deeply indebted to Byzantine icons. The transition from "Italo-Byzantine" to specifically Italian Renaissance painting — beginning with Giotto in the early fourteenth century — was a transformation of an existing Byzantine pictorial tradition rather than its replacement.

The iconographic traditions of the Eastern Orthodox world remained closer to their Byzantine origins. The icons painted in seventeenth-century Russia or twenty-first-century Greece follow recognizably the same conventions as the icons painted in twelfth-century Constantinople. The painters worked from manuals derived from Byzantine treatises. They used the same gold backgrounds, the same gesture vocabulary, the same color symbolism. Eastern Orthodox liturgical visual culture is a continuous tradition stretching from Justinian's Constantinople to today.

This is the inheritance most casual visitors notice without realizing it. Anyone who walks into a Greek or Russian Orthodox church in the modern world, anywhere in the world, sees a visual environment that was set by Byzantine artists more than a thousand years ago. The aesthetic survives the death of the political state that produced it.

---

What did Byzantium leave us?

A city that is still standing. A mosque-cathedral that is still operational. A book of laws that became the law of half the world. A church that is still holding services in a language that is now the academic specialty of a few thousand scholars but was once the everyday language of one of the great civilizations of human history. A diplomatic tradition that taught Western Europe how to conduct business with foreigners. A monastic tradition that survives unchanged on Mount Athos and in countless other Orthodox monasteries from the Aegean to Alaska. A textual inheritance — Plato, Aristotle, Sophocles, the New Testament, the Greek Fathers, the Souda — without which the European intellectual tradition would be unrecognizable. An iconographic vocabulary that still defines what religious art looks like for hundreds of millions of people. A claim about the relationship between divinity and matter — the Incarnation, defended at Nicaea, defended at Chalcedon, defended at the Triumph of Orthodoxy in 843, defended at the Hesychast councils in the fourteenth century — that has shaped Christian theology for two thousand years.

There is also, finally, an idea. Byzantium spent eleven centuries demonstrating that a multilingual, multiethnic, multidoctrinal Christian state was possible — that Roman institutions, Greek language, and Christian religion could be combined into a single durable polity governed by law and tied together by a shared imperial identity that did not depend on a single ethnic group or a single linguistic community. The empire absorbed Slavs, Armenians, Goths, Bulgars, Norse, Anglo-Saxons, Arabs, Jews, and dozens of other peoples into its administration, its army, its church, and its cities. It did not always do this gracefully. It often did it badly. But it did it, for an extraordinarily long time, and the institutional template it left behind — that universal Christian empire could be a real political form, not just a theoretical aspiration — shaped the imperial ambitions of the Holy Roman Empire, the Russian Empire, the Habsburg Monarchy, and (in inverted form) every modern European nation-state that has had to define itself against the Byzantine model.

The empire fell on May 29, 1453. The civilization is still here, scattered across Greek monasteries and Russian cathedrals and Italian libraries and Turkish mosques and the legal codes of two-thirds of the world. The Byzantines, who were nothing if not stubborn, would probably consider that survival a partial victory. The empire that had refused to die for eleven centuries had finally been killed. But it had not stopped being inherited.

The Romans who never stopped calling themselves Romans had won the only kind of victory their long defeat allowed them. Their world was over. Its inheritance was permanent.

# Rashidun & Umayyad — Islam's Century of Conquest

**Goal:** Narrative for the founding of Islam and its first imperial century — 632 to 750 CE — covering the Rashidun Caliphate (the four "rightly-guided" successors of Muhammad) and the Umayyad Caliphate (the first hereditary Muslim dynasty, ruling from Damascus). In 118 years, a religion founded by a single man in Mecca became the official creed of an empire stretching from the Atlantic coast of Morocco to the Indus River — bigger by territory than Rome at its peak, than Achaemenid Persia under Darius (Persian Empire Ch 2), than Han China under Emperor Wu (Han Dynasty Ch 3). The conquest was rapid; the conversion was slow. The borders this period drew on the map of the Middle East and North Africa are essentially the borders today, and the religion this period spread is the second-largest in the world.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

A note before starting. This is the founding moment of Islam and Islamic political history, and the early sources are not neutral. Most of what we know about the period 600–750 comes from Arabic chronicles compiled in the ninth and tenth centuries — meaning that the events were written down between one and three centuries after they happened, by authors who were themselves Sunni or Shia partisans of one faction or another. The traditional story is reasonably reliable on the major outlines (the conquests, the caliphal succession, the architectural projects) and much less reliable on the dialogue, the moral characterizations of individual caliphs, and the foundational theological disputes — exactly the parts that mattered most to the chroniclers' own sectarian arguments. Where the modern scholarly consensus diverges from the traditional account, this narrative tries to flag it. Where the sources contradict each other, this narrative says so. The result is a story with a few more "the surviving evidence suggests" markers than usual; the alternative would be to pretend the ninth-century Sunni or Shia version of events is what actually happened.

A second note, on names. Arabic transliteration into English has no fixed standard. The same person can appear as Mu'awiya, Muawiya, Muawiyah, or Mu'awiyah in different books. This narrative uses the most common American English forms, with pronunciation guides on first use. Where a name has both an Arabic and an English form (Caliph Umar / Omar; the Quran / the Koran), the more common Arabic form is used.

A note on "first" claims. Almost every "first" in this narrative — first hereditary Muslim dynasty, first Islamic gold coin, first congregational mosque with a domed prayer hall, first systematic translation of Greek texts into Arabic — means "first surviving evidence of." Earlier examples may have existed and been lost. The Umayyad century happens to be very well documented architecturally (the buildings are still standing) and very poorly documented administratively (the original Umayyad archives perished when the Abbasids took Damascus). This shapes what we can claim with confidence and what we cannot.

A note on what this is not. This is not a survey of medieval Islamic civilization. The intellectual flowering that the West knows as the "Islamic Golden Age" — al-Khwarizmi's algebra, Ibn Sina's medicine, the House of Wisdom in Baghdad — happens **after** the Umayyad period, under the Abbasids who overthrew them. That story is told in [Islamic Golden Age](#) Ch 1 onward, picking up exactly where this narrative ends with the Battle of the Zab in 750. The narrative you are reading is the founding moment: the conquest, the early religion, and the political-administrative scaffolding that the Abbasids would inherit.

# Chapter 1 — Pre-Islamic Arabia

To understand how a religion founded in Mecca in 610 conquered an empire by 720, you have to understand the country it started in.

The **Arabian Peninsula** is roughly a million square miles of mostly desert, about a third the size of the continental United States — bigger than Texas, Alaska, and California put together. Most of that land is uninhabitable for sustained agriculture. Rainfall is low to nonexistent, summer temperatures routinely exceed 110°F, and the few groundwater sources are unreliable. The peninsula's interior is a vast sand sea called the **Empty Quarter** (the **Rub' al-Khali**, ROOB al-KHAH-lee), the largest continuous body of sand on Earth, where almost no one lives even today. Around the edges of this central wasteland, narrow inhabitable bands run along the Red Sea coast, the Persian Gulf coast, and the Yemeni highlands in the south.

The pre-Islamic Arabian population — perhaps two to three million people in the early seventh century, though estimates vary widely — was concentrated in these edge zones. Most lived as **Bedouin** (BED-oo-in, from Arabic *badawi*, "desert-dweller") nomads, herding camels, sheep, and goats across seasonal ranges, moving between water sources, raiding rival tribes when conditions demanded. A minority lived in agricultural oases and small commercial towns. A few lived in the genuinely urban settlements that mattered for what came next: **Mecca** (MEK-ah) and **Yathrib** (YATH-rib, later renamed Medina) in the western highlands called the **Hijaz** (hi-JAHZ); **San'a** (SAN-ah) and **Najran** in the Yemeni south; **al-Hira** (al-HEE-rah) on the Iraqi frontier.

The Bedouin tribal society was the matrix from which Islam emerged, and it had a few features the reader needs to picture. Authority ran through tribes and clans, not through territorial states. A man's first loyalty was to his tribe, his second to his clan within the tribe, his third to his immediate family within the clan. Crime was avenged by the victim's kin under the principle of **blood-vengeance** — a killing called for a counter-killing, sustained over generations if necessary, and the threat of perpetual feud was what kept casual violence in check. Hospitality to strangers and protection of guests were absolute obligations, almost sacred. Wealth was measured in camels and slaves rather than in land or money. There was no Bedouin state, no Bedouin king, no Bedouin standing army, no Bedouin written law — and no Bedouin tradition of obeying anyone outside one's own tribal hierarchy.

This is the social material that Muhammad and the Rashidun caliphs would have to organize into an empire. The job was harder than just persuading individuals to convert. It meant persuading whole tribes to accept the political authority of someone outside their tribal hierarchy — and then keeping them persuaded after the original loyalty bond (to Muhammad personally) had died with him. The Ridda Wars of 632 (Chapter 3) were what happened when the persuasion temporarily collapsed.

---

The most consequential settlement in pre-Islamic Arabia was **Mecca**.

Mecca sits in a narrow valley in the western highlands of the Hijaz, about 45 miles inland from the Red Sea coast. It is hot, dry, and unsuited to agriculture. What it had — and the only reason it existed as a town — was the **Zamzam well** (a freshwater source in an otherwise waterless valley) and the **Kaaba** (KAH-bah, "the cube"), a roughly cube-shaped stone shrine that pre-Islamic Arabs treated as the most important sanctuary on the peninsula.

The Kaaba in 610 was a polytheistic shrine. Inside and around it were arrayed roughly 360 idols, representing the gods of various Arabian tribes — a deliberately inclusive arrangement that made Mecca a kind of religious common ground for tribes that otherwise lived under permanent feud. The annual pilgrimage to the Kaaba (the **hajj**, which Islam later reorganized but did not invent — the pilgrimage already existed in pre-Islamic form) brought tens of thousands of pilgrims to Mecca during four sacred months in which all tribal warfare was suspended by general consent. The sacred-month truce let the pilgrims travel safely and the merchants of Mecca conduct the most lucrative trading season of the year.

The Kaaba was also said, in pre-Islamic Arab tradition, to have been built by **Abraham** and his son **Ishmael** — the same Abraham who is the founding patriarch of Judaism and Christianity. Whether the Abraham-and-Ishmael story is genuine pre-Islamic tradition or was retrofitted onto Mecca by the Quranic revelation is debated by modern scholars, but the story was certainly in circulation by Muhammad's lifetime and gave the Kaaba an Abrahamic monotheistic genealogy alongside its polytheistic operating reality. Muhammad's own theological claim was that he was restoring the original Abrahamic monotheism that the Kaaba had been built to honor before the polytheistic idols got added on. This made his religion a reform, not an innovation — at least in the way he framed it.

The tribe that controlled Mecca was the **Quraysh** (koo-RAYSH), and they had organized themselves around two functions. They administered the Kaaba sanctuary — providing food, water, and lodging for pilgrims, maintaining the sacred-month truce, mediating between visiting tribes. And they ran the long-distance caravan trade that the Kaaba's pilgrim traffic made possible. Two great Quraysh-organized caravans left Mecca each year: the **summer caravan to Syria** (carrying Yemeni spices, Arabian leather, slaves, and gold north to the Roman cities of Damascus and Gaza), and the **winter caravan to Yemen** (carrying Mediterranean textiles, metals, and luxuries south). The caravans are mentioned by name in the Quran — Chapter 106 ("Quraysh") opens by invoking exactly this seasonal trade pattern as evidence of God's favor toward the tribe.

The Quraysh had subdivided themselves into several **clans** (sub-tribal groupings), each with its own elder council and its own piece of the Mecca operation. Two clans matter for the rest of this story: the **Banu Hashim** (BAH-noo HAH-shim), Muhammad's clan, and the **Banu Umayya** (BAH-noo oo-MAY-yah), the rival clan that would eventually found the Umayyad dynasty after Muhammad's death. The Banu Umayya were richer and more politically dominant in pre-Islamic Mecca than the Banu Hashim. Muhammad's revelations would temporarily reverse the relationship; the Umayyads would permanently re-reverse it sixty years later (Chapter 5).

---

The other Hijazi town that mattered was **Yathrib**, about 210 miles north of Mecca through difficult desert terrain.

Yathrib was an oasis town, fed by groundwater in a way Mecca was not, with significant date-palm cultivation and a more agricultural economy than the trader-dominated Quraysh capital. Its population in the early seventh century included two main Arab tribal groupings — the **Aws** and the **Khazraj** — who had been engaged in a long, bitter feud, and three substantial **Jewish tribes** (the **Banu Qaynuqa**, **Banu Nadir**, and **Banu Qurayza**), which made Yathrib one of the most religiously and ethnically mixed settlements on the peninsula.

The Yathribi Jews were an old community whose origins are not fully clear. Tradition held they had migrated from Palestine after the Roman destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE; the surviving evidence is consistent with that timing but does not confirm it. By Muhammad's lifetime they were thoroughly Arabized — speaking Arabic, organized as Arab-style tribes, intermarried with their Arab neighbors, conducting their religious life in Hebrew but their daily life in the language of the surrounding population. They had brought monotheistic religious ideas into the Hijaz that almost certainly influenced Muhammad's revelations. After the Hijra in 622 (Chapter 2), Muhammad's relationship with the Jewish tribes of Yathrib — initially cooperative under the Constitution of Medina, later catastrophically violent — would shape the early Muslim community's self-definition vis-à-vis the older monotheistic religions.

Yathrib in 622 was, additionally, exhausted by the Aws-Khazraj feud, which had recently killed off so many of the older tribal leaders that the surviving notables were actively looking for an outside arbiter to break the cycle. This is the practical reason Muhammad was invited to Yathrib in 622 — not because the Yathribis had become Muslims (most of them had not yet) but because he was an outsider with a reputation for honesty who could potentially adjudicate the feud. The arbitration job was the leverage Muhammad would use to convert the city.

---

Beyond the Hijaz, two great empires loomed over Arabia: the **Sasanian Persian Empire** to the northeast (Persian Empire Ch 9–10) and the **Byzantine Empire** to the northwest (Byzantine Empire Ch 1–3).

By 600 CE both empires were exhausted. Sasanian Persia and Byzantium had been at war off and on since the third century, and the most recent round (602–628 CE) was the most destructive yet — the great Roman-Persian war Heraclius eventually won at the Battle of Nineveh in 627 (Persian Empire Ch 10). Both empires had spent their reserves fighting each other. Both had alienated their religious minorities. Both had bled their tax base dry. When the Arab armies erupted out of the desert in the 630s (Chapter 3), they hit two empires that had just spent twenty-six years exhausting each other.

The empires also each had an Arab buffer kingdom on the desert edge, designed to manage the unsettled tribes and project state power into the peninsula:

The **Lakhmids** (LAKH-mids), centered at the city of **al-Hira** in southern Iraq, were the Arab clients of Sasanian Persia. Largely **Christian** by the sixth century — specifically Nestorian Christian, the same wing of the eastern church the Sasanians sheltered (Persian Empire Ch 9) — the Lakhmids managed the desert frontier for the Persians, escorted caravans, raided rival tribes when ordered to, and provided trained Arab warriors to the Sasanian army. The dynasty was deposed by the Sasanians around 602 in a misguided attempt to bring the buffer territory under direct rule. The decision left the Persian-Arab frontier without an experienced manager precisely at the moment when the Arabs across that frontier were about to become a serious military problem.

The **Ghassanids** (GHAS-sah-nids), centered in the Hauran region of southern Syria, were the Arab clients of Byzantine Rome. Like the Lakhmids they were largely Christian — but **Monophysite** Christian, the wing condemned at Chalcedon in 451 (Byzantine Empire Ch 1) — and their relationship with their Constantinopolitan patrons was correspondingly tense. The Ghassanids managed the Roman desert frontier, escorted caravans, fought the Lakhmids on behalf of Constantinople, and provided trained Arab cavalry to the Roman field armies. Like the Lakhmids, they fell apart in the early seventh century when imperial mismanagement deposed their last great king.

The fall of the buffer kingdoms is one of the underrated structural factors in the Arab conquests. Both Sasanian Iraq and Byzantine Syria entered the 630s without functional Arab clients on their desert frontiers, which meant that the early Arab raids hit imperial territories that had no early-warning system, no friendly Arab cavalry to harass invaders, and no institutional memory of how to fight in the desert. The Arabs walked through holes that had been there for a generation.

---

A note on what pre-Islamic Arabs actually believed.

The standard term for the period before Islam, in Islamic historiography, is the **Jahiliyya** (jah-hi-LEE-yah, "the time of ignorance"). This is a polemical term coined by the early Muslim community to disparage what came before; it implies that pre-Islamic religion was barbaric darkness lit only by the Quranic revelation. Modern historians of religion are more cautious. Pre-Islamic Arabian religion was a varied mix of:

**Tribal polytheism** — each tribe with its own gods, often associated with sacred trees, stones, or springs. The most widely worshipped pre-Islamic Arab deities were **Allah** (already a generic Arab name for the supreme creator god, though most pre-Islamic Arabs treated him as one god among many rather than the only god), **al-Lat**, **al-Uzza**, and **Manat** (three goddesses sometimes called the "daughters of Allah" in pre-Islamic ritual — these are the figures behind the contested "Satanic Verses" episode in the Quranic tradition). Each tribe had its own pantheon.

**Christianity** — significant in the Yemeni south (where the Aksumite kingdom of Ethiopia had briefly conquered the country in the 520s), in the northern client kingdoms (the Lakhmids and Ghassanids), and in scattered urban communities elsewhere. Estimates of the Christian population of pre-Islamic Arabia range widely, but it was not negligible.

**Judaism** — a substantial community in Yathrib and the surrounding Hijazi oases, smaller communities in Yemen (where one pre-Islamic king, **Dhu Nuwas**, had converted to Judaism around 510 and persecuted his Christian subjects, prompting the Aksumite intervention).

**Hanifs** — a small, scattered group of pre-Islamic Arab monotheists who had rejected polytheism but did not formally convert to either Christianity or Judaism. Islamic tradition treats Muhammad's grandfather Abd al-Muttalib and several other Meccan elders as informal Hanifs who were already drifting toward the kind of monotheism Muhammad would formalize.

**Magic, divination, sacred trees, sacred stones, sacred animals, and a wide range of locally specific ritual** — the same kind of folk religion that surrounded the formal religions of every contemporary culture.

In other words, pre-Islamic Arabia was religiously diverse. The Quranic narrative, and the later Muslim historiographic tradition, simplified the picture for polemical reasons. The reality was messier and more interesting.

---

Cross-civ check: the early seventh century in Arabia overlaps with the **Tang Dynasty** in China (founded 618, the great consolidator of Sui-era institutions and one of the most powerful states in world history at the time), the **Gupta Empire's** final disintegration in northern India (Gupta Empire Ch 7–8), the **Heraclian recovery** of Byzantium after the catastrophic Sasanian war (Byzantine Empire Ch 3), the **Sasanian** Persian Empire's last decade before destruction (Persian Empire Ch 10), and the **Kingdom of Aksum** in Ethiopia. Pre-Islamic Arabia was also in active commercial and diplomatic contact with **Aksum** — the Ethiopian kingdom that had occupied Yemen briefly in the early sixth century and that would later shelter the first Muslim refugees from Meccan persecution under its Christian king, the **Negus**.

The world Muhammad was born into in 570 was a world of tired empires, bleeding frontiers, and a peninsula at the geographic center of the late antique trade system. Almost nothing about that world looks like a launching pad for a new religion that would conquer half of it within a century. The peninsula's role had always been to supply spices, gold, slaves, and Bedouin mercenaries to the great states around it; it had never produced an empire of its own. The Quranic revelation and the Rashidun-era political genius would change this in two generations.

# Chapter 2 — Muhammad

Almost everything we know about Muhammad's life comes from sources written between 100 and 250 years after his death. The two most important early biographies — Ibn Ishaq's *Sirat Rasul Allah* ("Life of the Messenger of God"), composed around 770 CE, and Ibn Hisham's later edition of Ibn Ishaq, around 830 CE — are the foundation of the traditional account. The Quran itself, while preserved with extraordinary textual fidelity (Chapter 3), gives almost no narrative biography of its prophet; it presupposes a reader who already knows the story.

Modern Western scholarship on the historical Muhammad has been arguing for fifty years about how much of the traditional biography is reliable. The maximalist view (still dominant in Muslim scholarship and well represented in Western academia) holds that the major outlines of the traditional account — birth in Mecca, marriage to Khadija, first revelation at Hira, Hijra to Medina, conquest of Mecca, death in 632 — are reliably attested even if individual incidents and dialogue are embellished. The minimalist view (a much smaller school, associated with scholars like John Wansbrough and Patricia Crone, particularly in the 1970s and 1980s) held that the traditional account is so thoroughly retrofitted by ninth-century Sunni-Shia sectarian disputes that very little of it can be confidently treated as history. The current center of gravity is closer to the maximalist view than to the minimalist one, but with more caution about specific dates and dialogue than was typical in older popular accounts.

This narrative follows the standard maximalist account. The reader should keep in mind that the level of certainty for "Muhammad fled Mecca in 622" is much higher than for "the angel Gabriel said exactly the following words to Muhammad on the night of the first revelation."

---

**Muhammad ibn Abdullah** was born around **570 CE** at Mecca, into the Banu Hashim clan of the Quraysh (Chapter 1).

His early life was unsentimental. His father Abdullah had died on a trading journey a few months before Muhammad was born. His mother Amina died when he was about six. His paternal grandfather Abd al-Muttalib, who had taken him in, died when he was about eight. From age eight onward Muhammad was raised by his uncle **Abu Talib** (ah-BOO TAH-lib), the new head of the Banu Hashim, who treated him with affection but had limited resources to spare. Muhammad grew up materially modest and socially marginal — a Hashimite cousin in the wealthier branches of the Quraysh trader establishment, a young man with a reputation for honesty (his nickname *al-Amin*, "the trustworthy") but not for wealth or political clout.

He worked, like most young Quraysh men of his generation, in the caravan trade. By his early twenties he had risen far enough to be employed as the agent of a wealthy widowed merchant named **Khadija bint Khuwaylid** (kha-DEE-jah, fifteen or so years his senior — the exact age difference is disputed in the sources). Khadija evidently liked what she saw of his work, because around **595 CE** she proposed marriage to him. He accepted. The marriage produced four daughters who survived to adulthood — including **Fatima** (FAH-tih-mah, the youngest, who would later marry Ali and become the matriarch of the Shia imamate lineage) — and two sons who died in infancy. The lack of a surviving son was one of the structural problems that would shape the post-Muhammad succession crisis (Chapter 3).

For the next fifteen years Muhammad lived as a successful Meccan merchant. The early sources describe him as a settled family man, financially secure for the first time in his life, increasingly drawn to private religious meditation. He had a habit of withdrawing to a cave on Mount Hira, north of Mecca, for solitary prayer and reflection. The retreats grew longer over time. They culminated, in **610 CE**, in the event that Islamic tradition treats as the founding moment of the religion.

---

Muhammad was about forty years old, alone in the cave at **Hira** (HEE-rah), when he had what he later described as an overwhelming visionary experience. According to the traditional account, the angel **Gabriel** (Arabic *Jibril*) appeared to him and commanded him to recite. The first revealed verses are preserved as Chapter 96 of the Quran, beginning **"Iqra'! Bismi rabbika alladhi khalaq"** — "Recite! In the name of your Lord who created."

Muhammad returned home shaken, convinced he had either gone mad or seen something genuinely supernatural. Khadija reassured him, took him to her cousin **Waraqa ibn Nawfal** (a Hanif monotheist who had reportedly studied Christian scripture), and Waraqa told Muhammad that what he had experienced was the same kind of prophetic call that had come to **Moses** centuries earlier. From this point onward the revelations recurred. Muhammad would receive verses periodically — sometimes in dramatic visionary states, sometimes more quietly — for the next twenty-two years, until his death in 632. The accumulated revelations, eventually compiled and standardized by Caliph Uthman around 650 (Chapter 3), are the **Quran**.

A note on the Quran. The text is about 78,000 Arabic words long, divided into 114 chapters (called **suras**) of widely varying length. The chapters are arranged not chronologically but roughly by length, longest first — which makes the text difficult to read as a biography of Muhammad's spiritual development, since the early Meccan revelations (mostly short, vivid, eschatological) are at the end of the book and the later Medinan revelations (longer, more legalistic, more political) are at the beginning. The Quran is not a story; it is a series of divine addresses, sometimes in narrative form (retellings of stories from the Hebrew Bible and the Christian gospels, often in compressed and theologically distinctive form), sometimes in legal form, sometimes in lyrical praise of God, sometimes in direct response to specific events in Muhammad's life. The Arabic prose is famously beautiful — the Quran's claim of inimitability (*i'jaz*) rests on its language, which any Muslim believer holds to be impossible to match. Translations cannot fully convey what the Arabic does.

A note on whether Muhammad was illiterate. Islamic tradition has emphasized Muhammad's illiteracy because it strengthens the miracle of the Quran — a man who could not read or write produced a text of unmatched literary beauty. Modern scholars are divided. The Arabic word *ummi*, applied to Muhammad in the Quran (e.g. 7:157), can mean "illiterate" but it can also mean "Gentile" (a non-Jew, someone outside the People of the Book). A Meccan caravan trader in his forties would be unusual to have zero literacy in a society that did some written commercial accounting. The traditional interpretation may be theologically motivated rather than historical. The current scholarly view tends toward "the question is genuinely unresolved" rather than a confident answer either way.

---

Muhammad's preaching at Mecca over the next dozen years (roughly 610–622) was strict monotheism, with three core demands: the worship of one God only, social-economic justice (especially toward orphans, widows, and the poor), and acceptance of Muhammad as God's messenger. The early Meccan suras of the Quran are dominated by vivid eschatological imagery — the Last Day, divine judgment, the rewards of paradise, the punishments of hell — and by ethical injunctions that put the early Muslim community at odds with Quraysh commercial values.

The Quraysh leadership did not, at first, take Muhammad seriously. The Hashimite cousin claiming to be a prophet was an embarrassment but not a threat. As his small community of converts grew, the Quraysh attitude hardened. Muhammad's monotheism was not just personally offensive to the polytheistic Meccan establishment — it was an existential threat to the Kaaba's commercial role as a multi-tribal sanctuary, since strict monotheism would eventually require dismantling the 360 idols and ending the polytheistic pilgrimage that fed the Meccan economy. By the late 610s the Quraysh were applying pressure: a social and economic boycott against the Banu Hashim, public mockery of Muhammad's preaching, beatings and isolated killings of vulnerable converts (including the slave **Bilal**, who later became the first muezzin — the caller to prayer — of the Muslim community).

Two events near the end of Muhammad's Meccan period shaped what came next.

In **619 CE** — what later Muslim tradition called the **Year of Sorrow** — both Khadija (Muhammad's wife of twenty-four years and his most important emotional support) and his uncle Abu Talib (his clan protector and political shield against Quraysh aggression) died within months of each other. The deaths left Muhammad both personally devastated and politically exposed. With Abu Talib gone, the Banu Hashim leadership passed to another uncle, **Abu Lahab**, who was unsympathetic to Muhammad's mission and refused to extend clan protection. Muhammad was now an unprotected man in a hostile city.

In **620 or 621**, a delegation of pilgrims from **Yathrib** met Muhammad outside Mecca during the pilgrimage season. They had heard of his preaching, were intrigued, and were also dealing with their own urgent problem: the Aws-Khazraj feud (Chapter 1) had exhausted both tribes, and the Yathribi notables were looking for an outside arbiter who could mediate disputes the tribal elders had failed to resolve. Negotiations followed across the next two pilgrimage seasons. At the second meeting, in 622, the Yathribi delegation — by then including substantial converts from both the Aws and the Khazraj — formally invited Muhammad to settle in their city, accept the role of arbitrator, and bring his Meccan followers with him.

The invitation became one of the most consequential decisions in religious history.

---

In **622 CE**, Muhammad and his small community of Meccan followers — perhaps a hundred adults plus families — left Mecca in stages and migrated to Yathrib. The journey is called the **Hijra** (HIJ-rah, "the migration"). Muhammad himself left Mecca last, traveling with his close companion **Abu Bakr** (ah-BOO BAKR — Khadija's father, but more importantly the most senior of the early converts and a future caliph). They evaded a Quraysh pursuit, hid in a cave for three days while search parties passed nearby (the cave incident is one of the foundational miracle stories of the early tradition — a spider's web at the entrance allegedly persuaded the searchers no one had entered), and reached Yathrib by an indirect route.

Yathrib was renamed **Medina** (literally "the City," shortened from *Madinat al-Nabi*, "City of the Prophet"). The Hijra is the foundational political event of Islam — so foundational that the Islamic calendar starts not with Muhammad's birth (around 570), not with the first revelation (610), not with his death (632), but with the year of the Hijra. Year 1 AH ("After the Hijra") begins on July 16, 622 in the Western calendar. Every Islamic date from then to now is reckoned from the founding of the first Muslim political community at Medina. (The choice is theologically significant: Islam dates its calendar not from the prophet's life but from the establishment of the *umma*, the community. This is one of several places where early Islam differs structurally from early Christianity, which dates its calendar from the birth of its founder.)

At Medina, Muhammad's role expanded from religious teacher to political leader. He drafted what later sources call the **Constitution of Medina** — a treaty among the various Muslim, Jewish, and Arab tribal groupings of the city that established Muhammad as the arbitrator of disputes, codified mutual defense obligations, and recognized the religious autonomy of the Jewish tribes within the new political framework. The text survives in Ibn Ishaq's biography. It is one of the earliest written constitutions in any society, and it reads — even allowing for likely later editing — as a remarkably pragmatic document. Muhammad was not yet trying to convert the Yathribi Jews; he was trying to organize a viable city-state out of warring tribal factions.

The political community at Medina came to be called the **Umma** (OOM-mah) — a word that originally meant something like "the people" but which the Quranic revelation began to use specifically for the community of Muslim believers. The Umma is one of the load-bearing concepts of Islamic political thought: the community of Muslims is, in principle, a single body that transcends tribal, ethnic, and (eventually) national boundaries. The concept worked well at the scale of seventh-century Medina. It would become harder to operationalize when the Umma grew to include tens of millions of people across thousands of miles (Chapter 5).

---

The relationship between Medina and Mecca did not stabilize. The Quraysh continued to view Muhammad's community as a security threat — particularly because the Medinan Muslims, having lost their property when they emigrated, began conducting raids on Meccan caravans to support themselves. The raids escalated. In **624** the Quraysh sent a relief force to protect a major caravan returning from Syria. Muhammad's much smaller force ambushed the relief force at the wells of **Badr**, in the Hijaz between Mecca and Medina, on March 13, 624. The Muslim force of perhaps 313 men defeated a Quraysh army of perhaps 950 (the numbers in the early sources are suspicious in their precision, but the rough scale is reliable). Several leading Quraysh notables were killed. The Muslim losses were small.

The **Battle of Badr** was the founding military event of Islam. The early Muslim community treated it as direct evidence of divine support — Quran 8 ("The Spoils of War") was revealed in its aftermath and explicitly addresses the battle. Strategically Badr was less decisive than the early sources make it sound (the Quraysh remained the dominant military power in the Hijaz for several years afterward), but psychologically it transformed the Muslim community's confidence. They had won a battle they were not supposed to be able to win.

A year later, in **625**, the Quraysh returned with a larger force — perhaps 3,000 men — and met the Muslims at **Mount Uhud**, just outside Medina. The Battle of Uhud started well for the Muslims (the Quraysh charge was repulsed by Muslim archers) and then went badly when the archers abandoned their position to seize the loot left behind by the retreating Quraysh. The Quraysh cavalry counterattacked into the gap, the Muslim formation collapsed, Muhammad himself was wounded by a thrown stone, and rumors briefly circulated that he had been killed. The Muslim force broke and retreated to fortified positions on Mount Uhud, where the Quraysh failed to press the assault and eventually withdrew.

Uhud is the founding cautionary battle of the Islamic tradition. The lesson, drawn from it by the Quranic revelation in Chapter 3 (al-Imran, verses 121–155), was that even the prophet could lose, that discipline mattered, and that the community had to be prepared for setbacks as well as victories. The early Islamic tradition was not triumphalist about military success. It treated victories as divine favor and defeats as divine warning, a pattern that — combined with the Bedouin warrior culture's practical skill — produced an unusually adaptable military mindset.

In **627** the Quraysh tried a third time, with a much larger coalition force (perhaps 10,000 men) that besieged Medina. This is the **Battle of the Trench** (or **Battle of the Confederates**), so named because the Muslims, on the advice of the Persian convert **Salman al-Farisi**, dug a defensive trench across the only approach to Medina that cavalry could use. The trench (a Persian military innovation, novel to the Arabs) immobilized the Quraysh army. After two weeks the besiegers ran out of supplies and morale and went home. The Muslim community then turned on the Jewish **Banu Qurayza** tribe, which had negotiated with the Quraysh during the siege, executed the adult men, and enslaved the women and children. The mass execution — variously estimated at 600 to 900 men — is one of the most morally difficult episodes in the early Muslim community's history, and Muslim and non-Muslim historiographies have read it differently for thirteen centuries.

After the Trench, the Quraysh stopped attacking. The military momentum had shifted to Medina.

---

In **630**, after a Quraysh treaty violation (the killing of some Muslim allies by a Meccan-allied tribe), Muhammad assembled an army of about 10,000 — a coalition of Medinan Muslims and converted Bedouin tribes — and marched on Mecca itself.

The Quraysh leadership, calculating that resistance was hopeless, capitulated without significant fighting. Muhammad entered Mecca, declared a general amnesty for nearly all his former opponents (including **Abu Sufyan**, the Umayyad-clan leader who had organized most of the anti-Muhammad campaigns and whose son Mu'awiya would later found the Umayyad caliphate — Chapter 5), and went directly to the Kaaba. He smashed the 360 idols inside and around the shrine. He performed the first explicitly Islamic version of the pilgrimage ritual. He rededicated the Kaaba to monotheistic worship, recovering its supposed Abrahamic identity from underneath the polytheism (Chapter 1).

The **conquest of Mecca** in 630 is the political event that fixed Mecca's permanent status as the holiest city of Islam. Every Muslim has, since 630, faced Mecca during the five daily prayers (the **qibla**, the direction of prayer). Every Muslim with the financial and physical means is required at least once in life to perform the **hajj** — the major pilgrimage to Mecca during the sacred month of Dhu al-Hijja, with the rituals Muhammad himself performed in 630 and during his later "Farewell Pilgrimage" of 632. Mecca's holiness predated Islam (the Kaaba had been a Hijazi pilgrimage destination for centuries), but Islamic Mecca is Muhammad's reorganization of pre-Islamic Mecca around monotheistic principles.

The conquest of Mecca consolidated Muhammad's political position throughout Arabia. Tribes that had been on the fence began converting in numbers. By the **Farewell Pilgrimage** in early 632, Muhammad commanded the allegiance of most of the Arabian Peninsula, either through direct Islamic conversion or through political treaties with tribes that accepted Muslim political supremacy without yet accepting full conversion. The exact extent of Islamic political reach in 632 is debated by modern scholars — the traditional account exaggerates it, the minimalist account understates it, the truth is somewhere in between — but in any case, Muhammad's last year saw delegations from across the peninsula traveling to Medina to pledge allegiance.

---

In **June 632**, Muhammad fell ill at Medina with what the early sources describe as a fever, headaches, and progressive weakness. He died on **8 June 632** in the apartment of his youngest wife **Aisha** (AH-ee-shah, daughter of Abu Bakr), her head reportedly cradled in her arms. He was somewhere between sixty and sixty-three years old — the precise birth year being uncertain. He was buried in Aisha's apartment, beneath the floor where he died. The site became part of the Mosque of the Prophet in Medina; it remains the second-holiest pilgrimage site in Sunni Islam after the Kaaba at Mecca.

He had not designated a successor.

This is the structural fact that produced the entire subsequent history of Islamic political conflict. Muhammad had had no surviving son. He had not, on the Sunni reading of the surviving evidence, ever explicitly designated any other man as his successor — neither Abu Bakr, nor Umar, nor his cousin and son-in-law Ali. (The Shia reading is different and is covered in Chapter 4.) He had instructed the community in religious matters, but he had left no clear instructions about who should lead the community in political matters after his death.

The succession dispute began within hours of Muhammad's death and has not been fully resolved in the thirteen centuries since.

---

Cross-civ check: Muhammad's lifetime (570–632) overlaps with the **Heraclian recovery** of Byzantium under Emperor Heraclius (Byzantine Empire Ch 3), the **last great Sasanian war** under Khosrow II (Persian Empire Ch 10) and the Sasanian collapse that followed, the founding of the **Tang Dynasty** in China in 618 (Tang-Song China — the dynasty that would become the cultural superpower of the seventh-century world, while Muhammad was preaching at Mecca and his successors were conquering Persia), the **Asuka period** in Japan (when Buddhism was being formally adopted as state religion), and the late **Visigothic kingdom** in Spain (the kingdom Tariq ibn Ziyad would destroy in 711 — Chapter 6; what Islamic Iberia became over the following eight centuries is its own narrative, al-Andalus). The Hijra in 622 is contemporary with Heraclius's first counter-offensive against the Sasanians. The conquest of Mecca in 630 is contemporary with Heraclius's triumphant return of the True Cross to Jerusalem (Byzantine Empire Ch 3). Within six years, the Arab armies pouring out of Muhammad's reorganized Arabia would take from Heraclius everything he had just recovered (Chapter 3).

# Chapter 3 — The Rashidun Caliphs

The four caliphs who ruled the Muslim community from Muhammad's death in 632 to the assassination of Ali in 661 are called, in Sunni tradition, the **Rashidun** (rah-shee-DOON, "the rightly-guided"). They are: **Abu Bakr** (632–634), **Umar ibn al-Khattab** (634–644), **Uthman ibn Affan** (644–656), and **Ali ibn Abi Talib** (656–661). Each was chosen by some form of community consensus rather than dynastic succession. Each ruled the original community at Medina, then increasingly the rapidly expanding empire that the conquests were producing. Three of the four were assassinated.

The Sunni veneration of all four caliphs as legitimate is one of the founding markers of Sunni identity. The Shia view, which rejects the legitimacy of the first three caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman) and recognizes only Ali, is the founding marker of Shia identity. This chapter narrates the standard Sunni chronology while flagging the Shia counter-narrative where it diverges.

---

The succession argument began the day Muhammad died.

A faction of the Medinan Muslims — the **Ansar** (ahn-SAR, "the helpers," meaning the Yathribis who had hosted the Hijra and converted in Medina rather than Mecca) — gathered at a meeting hall called the **Saqifa** of the Banu Sa'ida and began discussing who should succeed Muhammad. A faction of the Meccan Muslims — the **Muhajirun** (moo-hah-jih-ROON, "the emigrants," the Quraysh who had migrated from Mecca with Muhammad in 622) — heard about the meeting and rushed to it. The two groups argued. The Ansar leadership proposed sharing the leadership role between an Ansar candidate and a Muhajirun candidate. The Muhajirun rejected the compromise; they argued that the new Muslim community had to be led by the Quraysh (the tribe to which Muhammad had belonged) for political and theological reasons. The argument was tense but not violent. By the end of the meeting, the Muhajirun candidate **Abu Bakr** — Muhammad's father-in-law and one of the very earliest converts — had received pledges of allegiance from a majority of those present.

This is the Sunni version. The traditional Shia counter-version holds that Muhammad had explicitly designated **Ali ibn Abi Talib** — his cousin and son-in-law (the husband of Fatima, Muhammad's daughter) — as his successor, in a public declaration at **Ghadir Khumm** (gha-DEER KHOOM, an oasis on the route from Mecca to Medina) during the Farewell Pilgrimage in 632, only a few months before his death. The Shia hold that Abu Bakr's selection at the Saqifa was an opportunistic coup conducted in Ali's absence (Ali was busy preparing Muhammad's body for burial) by a Quraysh faction unwilling to see the caliphate go to Muhammad's bloodline. The Sunni reply is that Muhammad's words at Ghadir Khumm — "He of whom I am the master, of him Ali is also the master" — were a personal endorsement of Ali's character rather than a formal political designation. The two readings produce two different histories of the early Islamic period and two different theologies of religious-political authority that have shaped Muslim communities ever since.

A clean Sunni-Shia rule of thumb for reading what follows: Sunnis consider the Rashidun caliphate the proper continuation of Muhammad's mission, with all four caliphs legitimate. Shias consider the first three caliphs usurpers and only Ali legitimate. The split is theological, not just political — it shapes how Sunnis and Shias understand religious authority itself. (Chapter 4 unpacks the theology more fully.)

---

**Abu Bakr** (ruled 632–634) was elected caliph at the Saqifa and took the title **Khalifat Rasul Allah** (kha-LEE-faht rah-SOOL al-LAH, "Successor of the Messenger of God"). The Arabic word *khalifa* — "successor," the source of the English "caliph" — became the title of the new office.

His two-year reign was almost entirely consumed by the **Ridda Wars** (RID-dah, "the apostasy"). After Muhammad's death, much of Arabia attempted to secede from the Muslim federation — most tribes treating their allegiance to Muhammad as a personal pact that died with him. Several tribal leaders proclaimed themselves prophets in their own right, in deliberate imitation of Muhammad's career. The most significant was **Musaylima** (moo-SAY-lim-ah), of the Banu Hanifa tribe in central Arabia, who attracted a large following and posed a genuine military threat to Medina.

Abu Bakr's response was to refuse any negotiation with the seceding tribes. He declared that Muhammad's political authority was not personal but institutional — the Umma was a permanent commitment, not a contract that ended with the prophet's death — and that any tribe attempting to leave was in religious as well as political revolt. He then dispatched the most gifted military commander of the early community, **Khalid ibn al-Walid** (KAH-lid IH-bin al-wah-LEED), to put down the revolts.

Khalid did. Across 632 and 633, he led campaigns through central and eastern Arabia, defeating one tribal coalition after another. The decisive battle against Musaylima at **Yamama** in late 632 was unusually bloody; it killed Musaylima but also killed a number of the Muslim companions who had memorized the Quran (the *huffaz*), which is what prompted Abu Bakr to commission the first systematic written collection of Quranic verses (the project that would later be completed under Uthman). By the end of 633 the Ridda Wars were essentially over and Arabia was solidly Muslim.

Abu Bakr died of natural causes in August 634, having ruled for about two years and three months. Before his death, he had personally nominated his successor — Umar ibn al-Khattab — and the leading Companions (the surviving men who had known Muhammad personally) accepted the nomination. The Sunni view treats this as an acceptable form of caliphal succession. The Shia view treats it as compounding the original error.

---

**Umar ibn al-Khattab** (ruled 634–644) was the second caliph and, by most reckonings, the most consequential. His ten-year reign saw the most spectacular phase of the Arab conquests: the destruction of the Sasanian Empire, the loss of Syria and Egypt by the Byzantines, and the construction of the administrative scaffolding that the caliphate would use for the next several centuries.

Umar himself was a hard man. The early sources describe him as physically imposing, austere in personal habit, terrifying when angered, scrupulously honest, and obsessed with administrative competence. He took the title **Amir al-Mu'minin** (ah-MEER al-moo-mih-NEEN, "Commander of the Faithful"), which would become the standard caliphal title for the next thirteen centuries. He lived modestly, slept on a mat, wore patched clothes, and carried out his responsibilities personally. He toured his expanding empire on horseback and conducted business in person with conquered governors. He was assassinated in 644 by a Persian slave named **Abu Lu'lu'a** (ah-BOO LOO-loo-ah), who stabbed him while he was leading the dawn prayer at the Mosque of the Prophet in Medina. The slave's motive was apparently personal grievance over taxation. The killing left no trail of conspiracy and no obvious political beneficiary; it was just one man's grudge that ended one of the most successful reigns in Muslim history.

Under Umar the conquests began.

---

The **Arab conquests** of 634 to 651 are one of the most remarkable military expansions in human history — comparable in compressed scale only to Alexander's campaigns or to the later Mongol explosion under Genghis Khan (Mongol Empire Ch 2–3). Within seventeen years, Arab armies took Syria, Palestine, Egypt, all of Mesopotamia, and the entire Iranian plateau from two empires that had been the dominant Eurasian powers for four centuries.

The pattern was set in **636**, in two battles three months apart.

In August 636, the Arab commander Khalid ibn al-Walid (transferred from the Iraqi frontier specifically for the campaign) faced the main Byzantine field army along the **Yarmouk River**, on what is now the Syria-Jordan border. The Byzantine force had perhaps 40,000 to 80,000 men under the Armenian eunuch general **Vahan**; the Arab force was smaller, perhaps 24,000 to 40,000 (the numbers in the Arabic and Greek sources vary widely). Khalid maneuvered the Byzantines into difficult terrain along the steep ravines of the Yarmouk gorge, exploited a sandstorm that blew into the Byzantine lines on the sixth day of fighting, and shattered the Roman army. Vahan was killed or captured. The defeat ended Roman power in Syria. Within two years the Arabs had taken Damascus (occupied a second time, this time permanently), Antioch (the great Roman provincial capital that Augustus had governed personally), and Jerusalem (which surrendered in 637 to Caliph Umar in person, on terms negotiated with the Patriarch Sophronius — the holy city changing hands in a deliberately peaceful handover).

Three months after Yarmouk, in November 636, a separate Arab army under **Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas** (SAHD IH-bin AH-bee wak-KAHS) fought the main Sasanian field force under **Rostam Farrokhzad** at the **Battle of al-Qadisiyyah** (kah-dih-SEE-yah) somewhere in southern Iraq. The exact site is disputed; the date is firm. Each side fielded perhaps 30,000 to 60,000 troops. The battle lasted four days, with each day given a name in the Arabic sources (the Day of Concussion, the Day of Battle, the Night of Fury, the Day of Decision). On the final day, a sandstorm blew into the Sasanian lines, the war elephants panicked, and the Persian formation broke. Rostam was killed. The Sasanian field army was destroyed. The Persian capital at **Ctesiphon** fell almost immediately afterward (Persian Empire Ch 10). The Arabs captured the royal treasury, the throne room, and the famous **Spring of Khosrow carpet** — a 95-foot-long silk-and-jewel weaving depicting a Persian garden, which the conquerors immediately cut into pieces and distributed to the troops as plunder.

The Persian collapse continued for six more years. The **Battle of Nihavand** in 642 destroyed the last major Sasanian field army in the Zagros mountains; the Arabs called it the **Victory of Victories**. The last Sasanian king, **Yazdegerd III** (yaz-deh-GERD), fled east through the Iranian plateau, attempting to rally resistance, and was finally murdered at **Merv** in 651 — reportedly by a local miller who killed him for his clothes and jewelry (Persian Empire Ch 10). With Yazdegerd's death the Sasanian Empire ended after 427 years.

The Egyptian conquest was a separate campaign, conducted by **Amr ibn al-As** (AHMR IH-bin al-AHS) starting in 639. Amr crossed the Sinai with about 4,000 troops, took the Roman fortress of **Babylon-in-Egypt** (a fortress at the apex of the Nile delta near modern Cairo, not the Babylon in Iraq) in 641, and captured **Alexandria** itself in 642. The Coptic Christian population of Egypt — who had been suffering under Constantinopolitan religious persecution for two centuries (Byzantine Empire Ch 1) — did not resist with any enthusiasm. Their bishops negotiated terms. The Greek-speaking ruling class largely fled. Egypt has been an Arabic-speaking Muslim country ever since.

---

The conquests succeeded for several reasons that are worth listing because the popular narrative ("Islam spread by the sword") is a partial truth that misses the structural picture.

First, the Arab armies hit two empires that had just spent twenty-six years exhausting each other (Persian Empire Ch 10, Byzantine Empire Ch 3). Both Sasanian Persia and Heraclian Byzantium were broke, depopulated, and militarily depleted in the 630s. The Arabs caught them at the worst possible moment.

Second, the local populations of the conquered provinces — Coptic Christians in Egypt, Monophysite Christians in Syria, Nestorian Christians in Iraq, Jews everywhere — were alienated from their imperial overlords by decades of Constantinopolitan or Sasanian religious persecution. The Arab terms for non-Muslim subjects (the **dhimmi** arrangement: protection of life, property, and worship in exchange for the **jizya** head tax and acceptance of Muslim political supremacy — Chapter 7) were better in most cases than what the locals had been getting from their previous rulers. Egypt and Syria were not "conquered" in the sense of being held down by force against unified popular resistance; they were liberated, in many places, from one ruling class by another.

Third, the Arab armies were small but tactically extraordinary. They were highly mobile — almost entirely cavalry, fast camel-mounted infantry, no slow siege trains — which let them choose their battles and disengage when conditions were unfavorable. They were extremely well led (Khalid ibn al-Walid, in particular, is in the discussion for the most gifted commander of the seventh century, anywhere). They were religiously motivated to a degree that surprised contemporary observers — Arab warriors fought believing that death in battle was a direct route to paradise, which produced the kind of unit cohesion most armies struggle to manufacture. And they were strategically patient — willing to take cities through long sieges, willing to negotiate surrenders rather than insist on military glory, willing to settle conquered territories under Arab garrisons rather than retreating after victory.

Fourth — this is important and underappreciated — the conquest was rapid but the conversion was slow, sometimes very slow. The Arab armies did not enter conquered cities and demand mass conversion. They demanded political submission and the jizya tax. They left religious life largely alone. Muslim conversion was a gradual process that took centuries, driven by social-economic incentives (lower taxes, eligibility for Arab status, marriage prospects) rather than by direct coercion. Egypt was probably not majority Muslim until the tenth or eleventh century. Iran was not majority Muslim until the ninth or tenth. The popular shorthand "Islam was spread by the sword" mistakes the conquest for the conversion. The conquest was indeed largely military; the conversion was largely social and economic, conducted over many generations.

This matters for understanding what the Umayyad and early Abbasid caliphates actually were. They were Arab military elites ruling over majority-Christian populations in Syria, majority-Coptic populations in Egypt, majority-Zoroastrian populations in Iran, and majority-Jewish populations in some pockets of Iraq. The slow conversion of these populations to Islam over the following centuries is the deeper story; the lightning Arab conquest is just the political prelude.

---

Umar's other major contribution was administrative.

He created the **diwan** (dee-WAHN) in about 638 — a written register of Arab Muslim warriors entitled to a share of the conquest revenues, organized by tribe and seniority. The diwan formalized the army's relationship to the state: warriors received cash stipends from the conquered provinces' tax revenues, with payments scaled by closeness to Muhammad and by service record. The system worked while the conquests kept producing new revenue. It would break later when the conquests slowed and the converting non-Arab population started demanding equal stipends (Chapter 8).

He also founded the **garrison cities** — major new urban settlements, deliberately separated from the existing populations of conquered provinces, where Arab warriors and their families lived and from which Arab governors administered the surrounding countryside. The most important were **Kufa** (KOO-fah, in central Iraq, founded 638), **Basra** (BAHS-rah, in southern Iraq near the Persian Gulf, founded 638), and **Fustat** (foos-TAHT, in Egypt south of modern Cairo, founded 641 by Amr ibn al-As after the Egyptian conquest). Kairouan (kair-WAHN, in modern Tunisia) was added later in the 670s under Mu'awiya. These garrison cities grew rapidly into major urban centers, became the political fault lines of every later Iraqi or Egyptian rebellion against the caliphate, and produced the early scholarly traditions in Quran recitation, hadith collection, and jurisprudence (Chapter 7) that would eventually grow into the great Islamic intellectual schools.

Umar refused to let his Arab warriors permanently settle the conquered countryside. He kept them concentrated in the garrison cities, where they could be mobilized for further campaigns and could be prevented from "going native" — assimilating into the Coptic-Christian or Persian-Zoroastrian local population. The policy worked for two generations and broke down by the third, as Arab settlers married locally, learned local languages, and began to merge into the broader population.

He also institutionalized the Islamic calendar, dating from the Hijra (Chapter 2). The first official year 1 AH was retroactively set as 622 CE, and Umar's chancery began dating documents this way around 638. The Islamic year is purely lunar (twelve months of 29 or 30 days, totaling roughly 354 days), so it drifts about 11 days earlier each solar year — meaning that Islamic dates do not align cleanly with solar seasons or with the Western calendar over long stretches. Modern Muslim countries usually use both the Islamic and the Gregorian calendars in parallel.

---

**Uthman ibn Affan** (OOTH-mahn IH-bin ah-FAHN, ruled 644–656) was the third caliph. He had been chosen by a small electoral council Umar had appointed before his assassination — a council of six senior Companions, including Ali, Talha, al-Zubayr, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, and Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, with Uthman emerging as the consensus candidate.

Uthman was a member of the **Banu Umayya**, the wealthy Quraysh clan that would later found the Umayyad dynasty (Chapter 5). He was Muhammad's son-in-law (married first to Muhammad's daughter Ruqayya, then after her death to her sister Umm Kulthum), a very early convert, and one of the most important financial backers of the Medinan community during its lean early years. He was also old (in his late sixties at his accession), unassertive in personal style, and disposed to trust the family connections that would eventually destroy him.

His twelve-year reign began well. The conquests continued — Cyprus was taken in 649, the **Battle of the Masts** in 655 broke Byzantine naval power in the eastern Mediterranean, Khorasan in eastern Iran was consolidated. Most consequentially, around 650, Uthman commissioned the **canonical text of the Quran**.

The need for canonization had been visible since Abu Bakr's reign. The Quran had originally been preserved through oral memorization (by the *huffaz*, the professional reciters) and through scattered written manuscripts on palm leaves, parchment scraps, animal bones, and stones. As the Muslim community spread across the conquered provinces, regional variations in the recitation began to appear — different cities had inherited slightly different versions of certain verses from the Companions who had carried the text to them. Uthman, alarmed by reports that his armies in Armenia and Azerbaijan were arguing about which recitation was correct, commissioned a small editorial commission led by **Zayd ibn Thabit** (one of Muhammad's original scribes who had also overseen Abu Bakr's earlier collection) to produce a single authoritative text. The commission consolidated the existing collected materials, resolved variants by reference to the Quraysh dialect (Muhammad's own dialect — the criterion privileged the Meccan reading where it differed from regional ones), and produced about five master copies that were sent to the major garrison cities.

Uthman then ordered all variant copies destroyed.

The destruction order is the foundational act of Quranic textual canonization. From 650 onward, every Quran was supposed to be a copy of the Uthmanic text. Modern scholarship (including the comparative analysis of the very early Sana'a manuscript discovered in 1972) has confirmed that the Uthmanic codex was, in textual terms, remarkably well-preserved — the variants between the earliest surviving Qurans are minor enough that the canonical text we have today is, to a high degree of certainty, the text Uthman authorized. This is unusual among major religious texts, which typically show much greater textual drift over comparable periods. The Uthmanic codification is one of the most consequential editorial decisions in the history of religion, and one of the very rare ones whose textual outcome we can verify.

The codification was not universally welcomed. Some senior Companions had their own personal Quranic codices that differed in minor ways from the Uthmanic version (the **Ibn Mas'ud** codex from Kufa was the most famous case), and they resented being told to destroy them. Others objected on principle to what looked like Uthman over-centralizing religious authority in his own family.

The complaints accumulated. Uthman, against the advice of Ali and other senior Companions, had been steadily appointing his Umayyad relatives to provincial governorships — most notably his cousin **Mu'awiya** as governor of Syria, and his foster-brother **Marwan** as his personal chief of staff in Medina. The Umayyad governors enriched themselves and their clients. Tax revenues from the provinces increasingly flowed through Umayyad pockets before reaching the central treasury. The non-Umayyad Companions, who had borne the burden of the conquests, felt frozen out.

In 656, a delegation of soldier-rebels arrived at Medina from the garrison city of **Fustat** in Egypt, ostensibly to present grievances against the Egyptian governor (an Umayyad relative of Uthman). After confused negotiations, the delegation laid siege to Uthman's house in Medina for several weeks. Ali and other senior Companions tried to mediate, with limited success. On 17 June 656, the rebels broke into Uthman's house and murdered the eighty-two-year-old caliph as he was reading his copy of the Quran — a death that, for shock value alone, would shape every Muslim political imagination for centuries.

The murder of a caliph by Muslims who claimed to be acting in the name of justice was the founding catastrophe of the Umma. It had to be understood, theologically as well as politically, by every faction in the community that came after.

---

**Ali ibn Abi Talib** (AH-lee IH-bin AH-bee TAH-lib, ruled 656–661) was the fourth caliph. He was Muhammad's cousin (the son of Muhammad's uncle Abu Talib, who had raised the orphan prophet — Chapter 2), Muhammad's son-in-law (the husband of Fatima), and the father of Muhammad's only male grandchildren who survived to adulthood, **Hasan** and **Husayn**.

He had been one of the very earliest converts (depending on the source, the second or third overall, after Khadija and possibly Abu Bakr). He had been Muhammad's most trusted political and military lieutenant during the Medinan years. The Shia tradition holds, as Chapter 2 noted, that Muhammad had explicitly designated Ali as his successor at Ghadir Khumm; the Sunni tradition rejects this, but acknowledges that Ali was a leading Companion of unique closeness to Muhammad.

Ali had been bypassed for the caliphate three times — in favor of Abu Bakr in 632, in favor of Umar in 634, in favor of Uthman in 644. His election in 656 — by a coalition of the Egyptian rebels, Medinan Muslims who had supported Uthman until his murder, and senior Companions like Ali himself — made him caliph at last. He was about fifty-six years old.

His five-year reign was disastrous. The basic problem was that Ali's accession had been preceded by Uthman's assassination, and significant portions of the Muslim community held Ali at least morally responsible for not having protected the previous caliph more vigorously. Two prominent Companions, **Talha** and **al-Zubayr** (both members of the original electoral council that had chosen Uthman), and Muhammad's widow **Aisha** (Abu Bakr's daughter, who had hated Uthman in life but now demanded vengeance for his murder), raised an army at Mecca and marched to Iraq to challenge Ali. Ali met them at the **Battle of the Camel** outside Basra in November 656 — the battle was named for Aisha's camel-borne litter, which became the focal point of the fighting. Ali won. Talha and al-Zubayr were killed. Aisha was captured, treated with respect, and escorted back to Medina, where she lived in dignified retirement for another two decades.

The Battle of the Camel was the **first time Muslims fought Muslims in pitched battle**. The shock was enormous. The Muslim community had been at war with various Quraysh and Bedouin opponents for two decades, but it had never before turned its weapons on itself.

The First Fitna had only just begun. Mu'awiya, the Umayyad governor of Syria (the same Mu'awiya whom Uthman had appointed and who had done very well out of Uthman's nepotism), refused to recognize Ali's caliphate and demanded vengeance for his cousin Uthman's murder. Ali assembled an Iraqi army and marched on Syria. The two forces met at **Siffin** (sif-FEEN), on the upper Euphrates near the modern Syria-Turkey border, in summer 657. After several days of inconclusive fighting, Mu'awiya's troops famously raised pages of the Quran on their lance tips and called for arbitration of the dispute by reference to scripture. Ali — against the advice of his more militant supporters — accepted. The two sides withdrew. Arbitrators were appointed to decide who should be caliph.

The arbitration disaster broke Ali's coalition. His more militant supporters considered the acceptance of arbitration a betrayal — the question of caliphate, they argued, could not be decided by human deliberation, only by the sword and divine judgment. They split off, declaring both Ali and Mu'awiya unfit to rule. They became the **Kharijites** (KHAH-rij-ites, "those who go out"), the first sectarian breakaway in Islamic history. The Kharijites went on to develop a distinctive theology — they held that any Muslim who committed a major sin had ceased to be a Muslim, and that the caliphate should go to the most pious individual regardless of lineage or tribal affiliation. (A small Kharijite community survives today in the **Ibadi** Muslims of Oman, North Africa, and Zanzibar; modern Ibadism is theologically much more moderate than the original seventh-century Kharijites, but the genealogy is direct.)

Ali fought the Kharijites at the **Battle of Nahrawan** in 658, defeated them militarily, and accumulated a generation of bitter enemies. The arbitration itself produced no usable result — the arbitrators failed to reach an agreement and the question of caliphate remained unresolved. Mu'awiya kept his Syrian governorship. Ali kept his Iraqi capital at Kufa. The two Muslim states existed in tense coexistence for the next three years.

In **661**, a Kharijite named **Ibn Muljam** assassinated Ali at the great mosque in Kufa, stabbing him with a poisoned blade as he led the dawn prayer. Ali died two days later. He was about sixty-one years old. He was buried at a site outside Kufa that became, over the following centuries, the city of **Najaf** — the burial place of the First Imam and one of the holiest pilgrimage sites in Shia Islam.

---

The Rashidun caliphate ended with Ali's death in 661. His elder son **Hasan** was briefly proclaimed caliph by the Iraqi army at Kufa, but he had no taste for civil war. After a few months of negotiation, Hasan formally abdicated to Mu'awiya in exchange for a substantial pension and the promise that the caliphate would revert to the descendants of Ali after Mu'awiya's death. Hasan retired to Medina, where he died (probably poisoned, almost certainly by his wife at Mu'awiya's instigation, though the surviving evidence is contested) around 670.

The promise of caliphal reversion was not kept. Mu'awiya designated his own son Yazid as successor (Chapter 5), turning the caliphate into a hereditary Umayyad office. The dispute that this would shortly produce — between Mu'awiya's son Yazid and Ali's younger son Husayn — is the subject of the next chapter.

The Sunni judgment on the Rashidun period is that all four caliphs were rightly-guided successors of Muhammad, that the conquests they ran were divinely favored, and that the catastrophe at the end (Uthman's assassination, the First Fitna, Ali's death) was a tragedy that did not invalidate what had come before. The Shia judgment is that the first three caliphs were usurpers, that Ali was the legitimate caliph from 632 onward but had been wrongly bypassed, and that the entire Sunni narrative of the Rashidun period is a piece of Quraysh propaganda designed to hide the truth.

The reader can see that two thousand years of subsequent Muslim history had to be lived inside one of these two readings.

The mid-seventh century is the moment when the late-antique world system that had been organized around Rome-Persia-China-India began to reorganize: Persia was destroyed by the Arabs, half of Rome was taken by the Arabs, India was fragmenting, only China remained intact and continued to grow. The political geography that emerged from this period was the early-medieval one, dominated in the western half by the Caliphate, in the eastern half by Tang China.

# Chapter 4 — The Sunni-Shia Split

This chapter is about an event that happened in October 680 and a religious division it produced that is still organizing political life across the Middle East in 2026. The event is the killing of **Husayn ibn Ali**, Muhammad's grandson, at a place called **Karbala** (KAR-bah-lah) in southern Iraq. The killing — the most important single event in Shia Islam — was the moment when an essentially political dispute about succession (Chapter 3) crystallized into a permanent religious division about authority, legitimacy, and the meaning of suffering itself.

This chapter will address the Karbala event itself, the formation of distinct Shia identity in its aftermath, the eventual subdivisions within Shia Islam (Twelver, Ismaili, Zaydi), and the substantive theological differences that the split produced. The chapter is somewhat out of chronological order — Karbala happens twenty years into the Umayyad caliphate, before the architectural and conquest projects of the next chapter — but the importance of the event is great enough that it deserves its own treatment, and the chapters that follow will need to refer back to the Sunni-Shia framework this one establishes.

A note before starting: this chapter handles religiously sensitive material on which Sunni and Shia Muslims disagree fundamentally. The narrative tries to present each tradition's view fairly, while also noting where modern historical scholarship considers one or the other claim to be uncertain. The author is not a Muslim of either tradition. Where the two traditions agree, the chapter treats their joint testimony as solid evidence. Where they disagree, the chapter reports the disagreement.

---

To understand Karbala, you have to understand what had happened in the twenty years between Ali's assassination in 661 and Husayn's death in 680.

After Ali's killing, Mu'awiya became the undisputed caliph (Chapter 3). He moved the capital from Medina (where Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali had ruled) to **Damascus**, his old governor's seat in Syria. He ran the caliphate for nineteen years (661–680), competently and at considerable distance from the original Medinan-Iraqi center of Muslim political life. His major innovation was administrative continuity with Byzantine Syria — he kept the Greek-speaking Roman bureaucracy in place, used the existing Byzantine tax registers, and ran the conquered Roman cities essentially the way Constantinople had run them. This made his caliphate functional but also estranged it from the more religiously inflected Iraqi Muslim communities at Kufa and Basra (where Ali had ruled and where Ali's sons Hasan and Husayn still lived).

Mu'awiya's most consequential decision was to designate his son **Yazid** as his successor before his own death. This converted the caliphate from an elected office (with the Rashidun pattern of community consensus) into a **hereditary monarchy** — an Umayyad family business. The decision was deeply unpopular among many Companions and their descendants. Designating one's son as successor was a Byzantine and Sasanian practice; it was not how Abu Bakr or Umar or even Uthman had been chosen. The traditional Arab tribal practice was for tribal succession to go to the most able adult male of the ruling clan, not necessarily the predecessor's son. Mu'awiya was bringing imperial-style hereditary succession into Islamic governance for the first time.

He gathered pledges of allegiance to Yazid from the major political constituencies of the empire while he was still alive — partly through persuasion, partly through bribery, partly through veiled threats. By the time Mu'awiya died in April 680, most of the empire had formally accepted Yazid as the next caliph. Two prominent figures had not: **Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr** (the son of Ali's old ally al-Zubayr, who would shortly raise his own counter-caliphate at Mecca, prolonging the Second Fitna into the 690s — Chapter 5) and **Husayn ibn Ali**, Ali's surviving son, who from his home at Medina refused to swear allegiance to Yazid.

---

**Husayn ibn Ali** (hoo-SAYN, born 626 in Medina) was Muhammad's grandson — the second son of Ali and Fatima, the younger brother of Hasan. He was the one of Muhammad's grandchildren that the Prophet had reportedly held particular affection for, and the early Muslim community accorded him significant deference on that basis. By 680 he was about fifty-four years old, settled at Medina with his family, an experienced political figure who had stayed quiet during his older brother Hasan's brief caliphate and the long reign of Mu'awiya. He had not made trouble. But he had also never sworn allegiance to Yazid, and Yazid's accession in April 680 forced him to declare himself.

He left Medina, secretly, in the spring of 680, taking his immediate family and a handful of supporters. He traveled to Mecca, where he hoped to be safe in the sacred city and could weigh his options. While at Mecca, he received letters from Shia partisans in **Kufa** — Ali's old capital, where significant pro-Alid sentiment had survived Mu'awiya's twenty years of Damascus-based rule — inviting him to come to Iraq, lead an uprising against Yazid, and reclaim the caliphate for Muhammad's bloodline.

Husayn deliberated. The political case for going to Kufa was that Iraqi pro-Alid sentiment, properly mobilized, could produce the army he would need to challenge Yazid. The political case against was that Mu'awiya had ruled Iraq for two decades through a series of competent, ruthless governors, and that the Iraqi Shia network was probably less capable of delivering on its promises than the letters were claiming. The case for going was that Husayn was Muhammad's grandson and that staying quiet would have been a betrayal of the family's claim. The case against was that going might end in personal disaster.

He decided to go.

He sent his cousin **Muslim ibn Aqil** ahead to Kufa as an advance representative, to gather formal pledges of allegiance from the Iraqi Shia and prepare the ground. Muslim arrived, was warmly received, gathered perhaps 18,000 written pledges — and then Yazid's appointed governor of Kufa, the formidable **Ubaydullah ibn Ziyad** (oo-bay-DOO-lah IH-bin zee-YAHD), arrived in the city, arrested the prominent Shia leaders, executed Muslim ibn Aqil and a handful of other organizers, and froze the Iraqi Shia network through fear. By the time Husayn himself approached Iraq with his small family entourage in early October 680, the city that had pledged him 18,000 supporters had been intimidated into silence. None of the Kufan Shia rode out to meet him.

Ubaydullah dispatched a force to intercept Husayn. The two parties met at a desolate spot in the Iraqi desert called **Karbala**, about 50 miles southwest of Kufa, in early October 680. Ubaydullah's force, perhaps 4,000 men under the commander Umar ibn Sa'd, surrounded Husayn's party of perhaps 70 men plus their families and cut them off from the Euphrates River, denying them water in the desert heat.

The siege lasted about a week. Negotiations failed. Husayn refused to swear allegiance to Yazid; Ubaydullah refused to let him leave without the oath. On **10 October 680** — the **tenth day of the month of Muharram**, the date now known as **Ashura** (ah-SHOO-rah) — Husayn and his small band were attacked. Husayn's male relatives and supporters were killed one by one as they made individual stands. Husayn's half-brother **Abbas ibn Ali** — the son of Ali by another wife after Fatima's death — was killed trying to bring water to the women and children. Husayn's nephew **Qasim ibn Hasan** was killed. Husayn's eighteen-year-old son **Ali al-Akbar** was killed. Husayn's infant son was hit by an arrow as Husayn held him in his arms.

Husayn himself was killed last, after fighting alone among the bodies of his family. The Umayyad force decapitated his corpse, trampled his body under their horses, and stripped the dead. The women — including Husayn's surviving sister **Zaynab** and his only surviving son **Ali Zayn al-Abidin** (ah-LEE ZAYN al-AH-bih-din, who had been too sick to fight and was therefore spared) — were taken captive and brought to Yazid's court at Damascus, with Husayn's severed head carried before them as a trophy.

The killing of the Prophet's grandson, the destruction of his family in front of him, the desecration of his corpse, the parade of his severed head through the streets of Damascus, the captive women of the Prophet's household displayed at the Umayyad court — all of this happened. The basic events are not in dispute between Sunni and Shia tradition; the Sunni sources confirm the events with perhaps less hagiographic embellishment but with substantively the same outline. What is disputed is the meaning.

---

The Shia interpretation of Karbala, formed over the following century, was that **Husayn had been martyred** in the truest sense — that he had gone to certain death rather than swear allegiance to a corrupt usurper, that his suffering was a redemptive act for the Muslim community, that the Umayyads who killed him had thereby revealed themselves as illegitimate, and that the obligation of subsequent generations of Shia Muslims was to mourn Husayn, remember Karbala, and resist injustice in his name.

The annual commemoration of Husayn's killing — observed during the first ten days of the month of Muharram and culminating in **Ashura** itself — became the central ritual of Shia religious life. Shia Muslims gather in **husayniyya** (special communal halls dedicated to Husayn's memory) to listen to recitations of the Karbala narrative, weep openly, beat their chests rhythmically in time with the recitation, and process through the streets in funeral parades for an event that happened thirteen and a half centuries ago. In some Shia communities (especially in South Asia and Iraq), the more intense forms of mourning include self-flagellation with chains or short blades — a controversial practice within Shia Islam itself, defended by some senior clerics and discouraged by others.

The deeper theological move was to elevate Husayn's killing into a structural principle of Shia thought: that the rightful religious-political authority of Muhammad's bloodline had been violently suppressed by usurper caliphs, that the suppression continued through the Umayyad and (later) the Abbasid dynasties, and that the obligation of Shia Muslims was to wait — through possibly very long historical periods of injustice — for the eventual return of legitimate rule under the Imams (the lineage of Muhammad's bloodline through Ali, Hasan, Husayn, and their descendants). The waiting itself became a form of pious resistance.

The Sunni interpretation of Karbala was different. Most Sunni sources mourn the death of the Prophet's grandson and condemn Yazid for allowing it. But the Sunni tradition does not make Karbala a structural principle of religious authority. It treats Husayn's killing as a tragic political failure — an avoidable catastrophe that Yazid handled badly — without using it to delegitimize the broader Umayyad caliphate or the Sunni framework of caliphal authority. Sunni religious life therefore does not commemorate Ashura the way Shia life does; the day is observed in Sunni tradition primarily as an optional fast (associated with Moses's deliverance from Pharaoh, a much older tradition) rather than as a mourning for Husayn.

The cumulative effect of the Karbala memory was to give Shia Muslims a religious-emotional vocabulary that has remained politically powerful for thirteen centuries. **"Every day is Ashura, every land is Karbala"** is one of the most-quoted slogans of modern Shia political mobilization — invoked in 1979 during the Iranian Revolution against the Shah, in 1980–88 during the Iran-Iraq War, in the 2000s by Hezbollah in Lebanon and the Mahdi Army in Iraq. Karbala is not a museum-piece historical event for Shia Muslims; it is a living reference point that shapes contemporary politics. Understanding why modern Iran behaves the way it does, why Lebanese Hezbollah operates the way it does, why Iraqi Shia politics has the particular character it does — none of this is fully intelligible without Karbala.

---

Beyond the Karbala memory itself, the Sunni-Shia split produced substantive theological differences that have widened over the centuries. Five differences are most important.

**First: religious authority.** Sunnis hold that religious authority after Muhammad rests with the consensus of qualified scholars (the *ulama*) interpreting the Quran and the hadith. There is no divinely designated successor to Muhammad and no clergy with sacramental powers; the *ulama* are simply learned men whose authority derives from training and recognition. Shias hold that religious authority rests with the **Imam** — the divinely designated descendant of Ali through Husayn, who possesses unique spiritual knowledge passed down through the bloodline. After the disappearance of the Twelfth Imam in the late ninth century (in Twelver Shia doctrine — see below), religious authority passes to the senior **mujtahids** (independent scholarly reasoners), but the underlying structure is hierarchical and the mujtahids derive their legitimacy from the Imam's authority, not just from scholarly consensus.

**Second: the imamate.** Sunnis recognize no special spiritual authority in any bloodline after Muhammad. Shias hold that twelve (or, in other Shia traditions, seven or five) divinely designated **Imams** descended from Ali through Husayn possess unique spiritual knowledge and the right to lead the community. The first Imam was Ali himself. The second was Hasan. The third was Husayn. The lineage continues through Husayn's surviving son Ali Zayn al-Abidin (the only male child to survive Karbala — Chapter 4), and so on. (See the lineage subsection below for the major variants.)

**Third: legal sources.** Sunnis recognize four main sources of Islamic law — the Quran, the hadith, the consensus of the community, and analogical reasoning. The four classical Sunni legal schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) developed in the ninth and tenth centuries and represent slightly different methodologies for applying these sources. Shias recognize Quran, hadith (particularly from the Imams as well as from Muhammad), consensus, and *aql* (reason). The Shia legal tradition (the **Ja'fari** school, named for the Sixth Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq, who codified much of the early Shia legal tradition) developed alongside the Sunni schools but emphasized the Imams' rulings as a continuing source of guidance.

**Fourth: the Mahdi.** Both Sunni and Shia traditions await the appearance of the **Mahdi** (al-MAH-dee, "the rightly-guided one"), an apocalyptic figure who will appear before the end of time to establish justice on earth. But the two traditions understand the Mahdi differently. In Sunni eschatology, the Mahdi will be a future descendant of Muhammad, born and growing up at some unspecified later time, who will rule briefly before the end of the world. In Twelver Shia eschatology, the Mahdi is **Muhammad al-Mahdi** — the Twelfth Imam, who was already born around 869, went into **occultation** (a state of hidden existence) in 874, and is alive but hidden until the appointed time of his return. The difference is enormous: Sunnis are waiting for someone who has not yet been born, while Twelver Shias are waiting for someone who is already alive but hidden.

**Fifth: the role of mourning and intercession.** Shia religious practice gives a much larger place to mourning rituals (especially around Husayn and the Imams), to pilgrimage to the tombs of the Imams (Karbala, Najaf, Mashhad, Kazimiyah, Samarra), and to intercessory prayer through the Imams as spiritual mediators. Sunni practice is generally more austere, with mosque-centered congregational prayer as the dominant ritual mode and intercession discouraged in many traditions (particularly the more reformist Sunni currents like Salafism and Wahhabism). The visible "look" of Shia and Sunni religious life — the public processions, the funeral parades, the elaborate shrines — differs in substantial ways that go back to the Karbala-shaped formation of Shia distinct identity.

---

Within Shia Islam, the imamate lineage produced three main subdivisions, all of which agree on the legitimacy of the first four Imams (Ali, Hasan, Husayn, and Husayn's son Ali Zayn al-Abidin) but diverge on later succession.

**Twelver Shias** (the largest branch today, roughly 85% of all Shias, the official religion of Iran since the Safavid conversion of 1501 — Safavid Persia Ch 1–2) recognize twelve Imams, ending with the Twelfth Imam Muhammad al-Mahdi, who went into occultation in 874. Twelver Shia clerical authority is organized around the **marja-i taqlid** ("source of emulation") system, in which senior living mujtahids issue binding rulings that ordinary Shia must follow.

**Ismaili Shias** (sometimes called "Seveners") recognize the same imamate up through the Sixth Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq (who died in 765), but split with the Twelvers over the next succession. Ismailis hold that Ja'far's elder son **Isma'il** was the rightful Seventh Imam (rather than his younger brother Musa, who is the Twelver Seventh Imam). The Ismaili line continued through Isma'il's descendants and produced, eventually, the **Fatimid Caliphate** that ruled Egypt from 909 to 1171 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6). Modern Ismailis are a smaller but globally distributed community; the **Aga Khan**, the hereditary leader of the Nizari Ismaili branch, is one of the most prominent contemporary Muslim spiritual figures.

**Zaydi Shias** (the smallest of the three main branches, concentrated today in Yemen) recognize a different fifth Imam — **Zayd ibn Ali** (the grandson of Husayn, who led an unsuccessful revolt against the Umayyads in 740 and was killed) rather than Muhammad al-Baqir, who is the fifth Imam in both Twelver and Ismaili traditions. Zaydi religious thought is in some ways closer to Sunni Islam than to other Shia traditions, particularly on the question of who can be Imam (Zaydis hold that any qualified descendant of Hasan or Husayn can rise to the imamate by leading a successful revolt against tyranny, rather than the lineage being divinely fixed).

The subdivisions matter for modern politics. Iranian Shia Islam is Twelver. The Lebanese Hezbollah is Twelver. The Iraqi Shia majority is mostly Twelver. The Houthi movement in Yemen is Zaydi. The Aga Khan's followers are Ismaili. The conflicts among these communities, and between them and Sunni neighbors, draw on subdivisions that go back to the eighth-century lineage disputes about who was the legitimate Imam after a particular ancestor died.

---

A thirteen-century Shia-Sunni history in compressed form. The Umayyad century (this narrative's main subject) was a period of Umayyad-Sunni hegemony with a Shia opposition organized around the descendants of Ali. The Abbasid Revolution in 750 (Chapter 8) initially benefited Shia partisans (the Abbasids had used Alid loyalty as part of their revolutionary coalition) but quickly turned against them — the Abbasids were also Sunnis and had no intention of yielding power to Ali's bloodline. Shia movements proliferated in opposition to the Abbasids: the Fatimid Caliphate in Egypt (909), the Buyid amirs who took de facto control of Abbasid Baghdad in 945, the Ismaili Assassins in Iran in the eleventh century. The Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 ended the Abbasid caliphate (Mongol Empire Ch 5) and opened a period in which Sunni-Shia conflict was less politically structured. The major reorganization came with the Safavid conversion of Iran in 1501 (Safavid Persia Ch 1–2), which made Iran a permanent Twelver Shia state and crystallized the modern Sunni-Shia geopolitical divide that runs through the contemporary Middle East.

The thread from Karbala in 680 to the Iran-Saudi rivalry in 2026 is real and continuous. It is not the only thread in modern Middle Eastern history, but it is one of the major ones, and any account of the region that ignores it is missing structural information.

---

Cross-civ check: the Karbala killing in 680 happened during the **Tang Dynasty** in China (Empress Wu's reign, 690–705 CE), the late **Heraclian** period in Byzantium (Justinian II's reign, 685–711 — Byzantine Empire Ch 3, just before the Iconoclast Century), the rise of the **Bulgarian Empire** in the Balkans, and the **Pala Empire's** predecessors in northeastern India. The closest contemporary analogue to the Karbala-shaped religious-political division is the **Christological controversies** in early Christianity (Byzantine Empire Ch 1) — the disputes over Christ's nature that produced the Chalcedonian, Monophysite, and Nestorian traditions and that gave Christianity its lasting denominational structure. Both Islam and Christianity, in their first centuries, produced foundational schisms whose theological substance was real but whose political driver was a contested succession. The Christian schisms produced the Orthodox-Catholic-Oriental divide; the Muslim schism produced Sunni-Shia. Both divisions are still operative in 2026.

# Chapter 5 — Damascus

The Umayyad Caliphate, founded by **Mu'awiya I** (moo-AH-wee-ah) in 661 after Ali's assassination and Hasan's abdication, ran from a capital that no Rashidun caliph had ever lived in: **Damascus**, the old Roman provincial capital of Syria. This chapter is about the period from Mu'awiya's accession in 661 through the reign of **Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan** (AHB-d al-MAH-lik), the great organizer of the early Umayyad state, who ruled from 685 to 705. Across these forty-four years, the caliphate transformed from a Medinan-Iraqi Arab tribal federation into a centralized empire whose administrative practice owed more to Byzantium than to Arabia.

---

Mu'awiya, the first Umayyad caliph, was the son of **Abu Sufyan**, the Quraysh-Umayyad chief who had organized most of Mecca's military campaigns against the early Muslim community at Medina (Chapter 2). Mu'awiya himself had been a late convert — his family submitted only after the conquest of Mecca in 630, when resistance was hopeless — and the early Companions had always treated him as a man of the second tier. His rise to power was a comeback story for the Umayyad clan that had originally opposed Muhammad and now ended up running Muhammad's empire.

Mu'awiya had been governor of **Syria** for twenty years before becoming caliph. He had been appointed by Caliph Umar around 640, after his elder brother Yazid had died, and confirmed by Uthman during the Umayyad-favoring nepotism that contributed to Uthman's downfall (Chapter 3). His twenty years in Damascus had transformed him from a marginal Umayyad cousin into the most experienced provincial administrator in the empire. He understood the inherited Byzantine bureaucracy, knew the Greek-speaking civil servants who ran it, and had built the **Arab navy** in the late 640s and 650s — using Egyptian and Syrian shipwrights and Coptic crews to construct fleets that defeated the Byzantines at the **Battle of the Masts** in 655 and made the Mediterranean an Arab-contested sea for the first time. (Without Mu'awiya's navy, the conquest of Spain in 711 and the Siege of Constantinople in 717 — Chapter 6 — would not have been physically possible.)

When Mu'awiya inherited the caliphate in 661, he kept his capital at Damascus rather than returning to Medina. The decision was deeply consequential. Damascus was a Roman city — colonnaded streets, monumental public buildings, a functioning Byzantine administrative apparatus, a Greek-speaking civil service. Medina was a small oasis town with primitive infrastructure and limited administrative depth. Running the empire from Damascus meant running it through inherited Byzantine institutions; running it from Medina would have meant building a new administrative system from scratch. Mu'awiya chose the institutional shortcut.

The choice imported Byzantine practice into Islamic governance in ways that would shape the Umayyad caliphate for the rest of its existence. The Umayyads ran a chancery on Byzantine lines, used Greek as the working language of administration in the western provinces (and Pahlavi, the Sasanian administrative language, in the eastern provinces) until Abd al-Malik's Arabization reform of 695, employed Greek-speaking Christian civil servants from the late Roman bureaucratic class (the most famous being the Christian theologian **John of Damascus**, who served as a senior Umayyad financial official before becoming the major theological defender of Byzantine icon-veneration in the 740s — Byzantine Empire Ch 4), and structured their court ceremonies on a Byzantine model.

This continuity was practical, but it was also a source of legitimacy concerns within the Muslim community. The early Companions in Medina considered the Umayyads suspiciously close to Roman imperial style — they wore silk, they sat on thrones, they were addressed in elaborate Persian-Roman court honorifics, they hunted with falcons and built palaces in the desert with Roman-style mosaics and baths. The accusation that the Umayyads had become "Arab kings" rather than Muslim caliphs would dog the dynasty until its fall.

---

Mu'awiya's reign (661–680) was marked by stability, consolidation, and the gradual but unmistakable transformation of the caliphate into a hereditary monarchy.

The conquests slowed. The Arab armies stabilized the frontiers without dramatic new acquisitions, except for the gradual conquest of North Africa westward from Egypt (Tripolitania, then Ifriqiya, eventually reaching the Atlantic by the 700s) and the unsuccessful First Arab Siege of Constantinople of 674–678 (a four-year naval blockade that the Byzantines defeated using Greek fire — Byzantine Empire Ch 4). Mu'awiya invested heavily in the navy, in administrative organization, and in negotiating internal political stability. He intermarried Arab clans and Byzantine-Christian elite families. He kept the leading Companions and their descendants paid, employed, and politically managed.

His most consequential domestic decision was nominating his son **Yazid** as successor (Chapter 4). The hereditary succession transformed the caliphate from an elected office into a family business and produced the Karbala crisis of 680. Mu'awiya himself had managed the political fallout of Husayn's killing well enough — he died unexpectedly in April 680, just before the worst of the Yazid succession crisis broke open — but the structural change he had introduced was permanent. Every subsequent Islamic dynasty, Sunni or Shia, would be hereditary. The Rashidun pattern of caliphal election by community consensus was over.

---

Yazid I (ruled 680–683) lasted three years. His reign saw the killing of Husayn at Karbala (Chapter 4) and the start of the **Second Fitna** — the second major Muslim civil war, this one centered on the rival caliphate of **Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr** in Mecca.

Ibn al-Zubayr (the son of Ali's old ally al-Zubayr) had refused, like Husayn, to swear allegiance to Yazid. Unlike Husayn, he had remained at Mecca rather than venturing to Iraq, sheltered behind the sanctity of the holy city and his own personal political reputation. After Husayn's death, ibn al-Zubayr declared himself caliph, gathered support across the Hijaz and parts of Iraq, and ran a counter-government from Mecca for the next twelve years. At the height of his control, the Hijaz, much of Iraq, southern Arabia, and parts of Egypt recognized ibn al-Zubayr rather than the Umayyads.

Yazid's response was unusually brutal. He sent an army to besiege Medina in 683 (the **Battle of al-Harrah**), which sacked the city and killed many of the Companions' descendants. He then sent a force toward Mecca to besiege ibn al-Zubayr; the siege was conducted with catapults bombarding the holy city, and the **Kaaba itself was damaged** by stone projectiles and burned. The bombardment of Mecca by an Umayyad army was the kind of theological catastrophe the dynasty would never live down. Yazid died unexpectedly later in 683, his son Mu'awiya II reigned briefly and died in the same year, and the Umayyad dynasty fragmented temporarily — different Umayyad branches recognizing different candidates in different provinces.

Order was restored by **Marwan I** (a senior Umayyad cousin who had been Uthman's chief of staff in 656), who rallied the family and was acknowledged caliph in late 684, though he died only a year later. His son **Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan** inherited the caliphate in 685.

---

**Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan** (ruled 685–705) was the second great organizer of the Umayyad state, after Mu'awiya. His twenty-year reign accomplished the institutional transformation that turned the caliphate into a recognizably Islamic empire.

His first task was to win the Second Fitna. In 692, Abd al-Malik dispatched his most ruthless general, **al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf** (al-HAJ-jaj), to deal with ibn al-Zubayr at Mecca. Al-Hajjaj besieged the city, bombarded the Kaaba a second time (also damaging it), killed ibn al-Zubayr in battle in October 692, and crucified his corpse on the gates of Mecca. The Hijaz was reincorporated into the Umayyad caliphate. Al-Hajjaj was rewarded with the governorship of Iraq, where he ruled with notorious severity until his death in 714 — the Umayyad enforcer-in-chief whose name is still synonymous in Arabic-language historical memory with effective but cruel administration.

With the Second Fitna won, Abd al-Malik began the structural reforms.

---

The most consequential reform was **Arabization**.

Until Abd al-Malik, the Umayyad bureaucracy had run in inherited languages — **Greek** in Syria and Egypt (the Byzantine administrative language), **Pahlavi** (Middle Persian) in Iraq and Iran (the Sasanian administrative language). The civil servants were the inherited Christian and Zoroastrian/Manichaean professionals from the conquered states, working in their own languages on documents written in their own scripts. The Arab Muslim ruling class was a thin layer on top of a continuing Roman-Persian administrative apparatus.

Around **695**, Abd al-Malik decreed that the bureaucracy must shift to **Arabic** as its working language. The decision required creating a whole new class of Arabic-literate administrators, training them in tax accounting and law and chancery practice, and gradually displacing the inherited Greek-and-Pahlavi-speaking professionals. The transition took a generation. By the time of the Abbasid Revolution in 750, Arabic was the universal administrative language of the caliphate from Spain to Sind. The shift locked Arabic in as the language of the Middle East.

The cumulative effect of Arabization was enormous. Within two centuries, Arabic had displaced Aramaic (the previous lingua franca of the Levant for over a thousand years — Phoenicia Ch 7, Ancient Israel Ch 6), much of Coptic in Egypt (which survived only in Christian liturgy), and substantial portions of Pahlavi/Middle Persian as the everyday language of the Mesopotamian populations (Persian, however, would survive in Iran proper as a vibrant literary and administrative language and would re-emerge in Persianized Islamic form under the Abbasids — Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). The linguistic map of the modern Middle East — Arabic from Morocco to Iraq, Persian in Iran, Turkish in Anatolia (after a later Turkic conquest) — was set in place by the Umayyad-era language policies and the slow demographic processes they enabled.

The reform required, as a side project, **reforming the Arabic script itself**. Pre-Umayyad Arabic was written without dots distinguishing similar-looking consonants and without short-vowel marks fixing pronunciation. This had been adequate for a small community of native Arabic speakers who could supply the missing information from context. It was inadequate for a non-native scribal class learning Arabic for administrative purposes, and it was particularly inadequate for the standardized Quran text Uthman had codified (Chapter 3), where any pronunciation ambiguity was theologically intolerable. Under Abd al-Malik and al-Hajjaj, scholars associated with the early grammar school at Basra developed the diacritical and vowel-mark systems that are still used in Arabic today. The reformed script let Arabic be written precisely enough for administrative use across an empire of non-native speakers.

---

Abd al-Malik's second major reform was **monetary**.

The Umayyad coinage in 685 was a hybrid. Gold coins were Byzantine **solidi**, struck in Constantinople or in Arab imitation, depicting the Byzantine emperor. Silver coins were Sasanian **drachms**, depicting the last Sasanian shahs. The Arab Muslim state was conducting its commerce in money minted by the empires it had destroyed.

In **696**, Abd al-Malik issued the first **purely Islamic gold dinar** — struck at the Damascus mint, weighing about 4.25 grams (the Roman solidus standard), bearing Arabic Quranic inscriptions and no human imagery. The same reform produced an Islamic silver **dirham** (replacing the Sasanian drachm) and a copper **fals** (for petty transactions, replacing the various Byzantine and Sasanian fractional currencies). The new coinage standardized the imperial money supply, signaled Umayyad sovereign legitimacy, and was a deliberate visual statement that the caliphate was no longer a continuation of Roman or Persian rule but its own thing.

The aniconism — the absence of human or animal images on the coins — was theologically significant. Islamic religious tradition was developing, in this period, toward a strict prohibition of representational imagery in religious contexts (a position that would crystallize over the following centuries and would later influence the Byzantine iconoclast movement of the 720s — Byzantine Empire Ch 4). The dinar's aniconic design declared that Umayyad money was Islamic money in a way the Byzantine and Sasanian coins it replaced had not been.

The Umayyad gold dinar became the standard currency of the Mediterranean and the Middle East for the next four centuries, gradually replacing both Roman and Sasanian coinage in the regions the caliphate controlled and circulating widely outside it as well. Vikings raided Frankish territories partly to acquire silver dirhams (Viking Age Ch 5). Indian traders accepted Umayyad and early Abbasid coins. The dinar was the gold standard of medieval Eurasian commerce.

---

Abd al-Malik's third major reform was **architectural**, and it deserves its own chapter (Chapter 7) — but the foundational project began under him: the **Dome of the Rock** in Jerusalem, completed in 691.

The Dome was the first major Umayyad building project, the first major piece of Islamic monumental architecture, and the first piece of physical infrastructure designed to make a public statement about the religion's place in the world. It was built on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem — the platform that had once held the Jewish Temple destroyed by the Romans in 70 CE, and that had been left in ruins for over six hundred years afterward (Christianity having abandoned the site in favor of the Holy Sepulchre church across the city). Abd al-Malik chose to claim the abandoned Temple Mount as Islam's Jerusalem property, building a magnificent shrine over the rock from which Muhammad was said to have ascended on his Night Journey. The project's full architectural significance is the subject of Chapter 7.

The political motivation was multiple. The Dome was a statement against Christian Jerusalem (the Holy Sepulchre church across the city). It was a statement against Mecca (where ibn al-Zubayr's counter-caliphate had controlled the Kaaba during much of the Second Fitna, and where Abd al-Malik may briefly have considered redirecting pilgrimage to Jerusalem instead — though the actual extent of this policy is contested in the sources). It was a statement of Umayyad religious legitimacy, claiming Jerusalem's holy geography for the dynasty's project.

It also worked aesthetically. The Dome of the Rock has been continuously standing since 691, with periodic refurbishments, and it remains one of the most beautiful pieces of architecture in the world.

---

The Umayyad state that emerged from Abd al-Malik's reign (in place by 705, when his son al-Walid I succeeded him) had a recognizable shape. It was an Arab Muslim military elite running an empire that stretched from Spain to Sind. It used Arabic as its administrative language, gold dinars and silver dirhams as its currency, monumental Islamic architecture as its public face, and the dhimmi system to govern its overwhelmingly non-Muslim subject populations (Chapter 7).

The political structure was hereditary monarchy with Damascus as the capital, an Umayyad family running the state, governors of the major provinces (Syria, Egypt, Iraq, Khorasan, Spain, North Africa) appointed from the family or from trusted clients. The Arab tribal federation that had won the conquests had been largely transformed into a professional military caste paid in cash from the conquered populations' tax revenues — the **diwan** system inherited from Umar but increasingly bureaucratized.

The structural problem the dynasty had not solved, and would not solve, was the **mawali** issue (Chapter 8) — the growing population of non-Arab Muslim converts who paid the same taxes as non-Muslims and were excluded from the diwan stipend system. Conversion to Islam had been, in the early conquests, almost incidental to the political-military project. But by the late Umayyad period the mawali population was large and growing — Persian-speaking Iraqis and Iranians, Coptic Egyptians, Berber North Africans — and the Arab privilege built into the original Rashidun fiscal system was an increasingly visible injustice.

The Umayyads tried, in fits and starts, to address the issue. **Umar II** (ruled 717–720), the most personally pious caliph of the dynasty, briefly equalized the tax system in favor of recent converts. The reform was honest, popular among non-Arabs, and gutted Umayyad revenues. His successors quietly reversed it. The structural contradiction between an Arab-privilege fiscal system and a religion that proclaimed all believers equal was the slow-burning fuse that would eventually detonate the Abbasid Revolution (Chapter 8).

---

Cross-civ check: the early Umayyad period (661–705) overlaps with the **Tang Dynasty** at its height (Empress Wu's reign 690–705 — exactly contemporary with Abd al-Malik), the **Heraclian convulsion** in Byzantium and the loss of southern provinces (Byzantine Empire Ch 3), the **Pala dynasty's** predecessors in Bengal, the Mayan **Late Classic** period in Mesoamerica (Maya Civilization Ch 6), and the early **Visigothic** kingdom in Spain (the kingdom Tariq ibn Ziyad would shortly destroy in 711 — Chapter 6). The architectural ambition of the Dome of the Rock in 691 is contemporary with the building of major Tang Buddhist temples and pagodas in Chang'an — both empires investing imperial wealth in monumental religious architecture as a statement of dynastic legitimacy. The administrative consolidation under Abd al-Malik is contemporary with the institutional consolidation of the Tang civil service — both states moving from improvised post-conquest governance toward systematic bureaucratic professionalism. The two empires were the eastern and western anchors of late seventh-century Eurasian state power.

# Chapter 6 — The Conquests West and East

The Umayyad Caliphate under Abd al-Malik's son **al-Walid I** (ruled 705–715) and his successors hit its territorial peak. In a single decade — roughly 705 to 715 — Arab armies conquered Spain (711), Sind (711), and Transoxiana (709–715), more than doubling the empire's geographic reach. The conquests were extraordinary in scale, in simultaneity, and in the ease with which a relatively small Arab force could topple existing states from Iberia to Central Asia.

This chapter is about how the Umayyad conquests reached their maximum extent — and also about the decisive failure (the 717–718 Siege of Constantinople) that defined the empire's eastern and northern limits for the remainder of its existence.

---

Spain was conquered by accident.

In 711, a small Berber-Arab raiding party under the Berber commander **Tariq ibn Ziyad** (TAH-rik IH-bin zee-YAHD) crossed the strait between Morocco and Spain. Tariq was the freedman client of the Umayyad governor of North Africa, **Musa ibn Nusayr**, and his expedition was probably authorized as a reconnaissance raid rather than a conquest. He landed at the rocky promontory now called **Gibraltar** (a Spanish corruption of the Arabic *Jabal Tariq*, "Tariq's Mountain") with about 7,000 men, mostly Berbers from the recently conquered Maghreb, and marched north into the disintegrating **Visigothic Kingdom**.

The Visigoths had ruled Spain since the late fifth century, when Germanic tribes had taken the peninsula from the collapsing Western Roman Empire (Ancient Rome Ch 10). Their kingdom in 711 was a fragile elective monarchy, weakened by recent succession crises and ongoing factional warfare among Visigothic nobles. King **Roderic** had taken the throne the previous year in disputed circumstances. The internal opposition was substantial.

In July 711, Tariq met Roderic and the main Visigothic field army at the **Battle of Guadalete** (gwah-dah-LEH-teh), in southern Spain. The battle was decisive — the Visigothic right wing, commanded by the family of Roderic's predecessor and bitter enemy, may have defected mid-fight (the surviving sources are not unanimous on this point). Roderic was killed. The Visigothic state collapsed almost instantly. Within a year, Tariq and his commanders had taken Toledo, Mérida, Córdoba, and most of the major Iberian cities. By 718 the entire peninsula was under Muslim rule, except for a small Christian holdout in the Asturian mountains of the far north.

The conquest of Spain initiated **781 years** of Muslim presence on the Iberian Peninsula, ending only with the fall of Granada to the Catholic Monarchs in 1492. The Muslim civilization of medieval Spain — called **al-Andalus** in Arabic — would become one of the great cultural centers of the medieval Mediterranean, producing the Great Mosque of Córdoba, the Alhambra of Granada, the philosophical and scientific work of Ibn Sina (in the east) and Ibn Rushd (in the west), and the convivencia of Christians, Muslims, and Jews living together in al-Andalus that has been romanticized but was also genuinely productive (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6–7). The 711 conquest is the founding moment of all of this.

The conquest of Spain also produced the strange afterlife of the Umayyad dynasty in al-Andalus. The Umayyad survivor **Abd al-Rahman I** would flee the Abbasid massacre of 750 (Chapter 8) and reach Spain as the only Umayyad prince still alive, founding an independent **Emirate of Córdoba** in 756 that would continue under his descendants for nearly three centuries. The Umayyad caliphate ended in the east; in the far west, in al-Andalus, the dynasty kept going for a long time after.

---

Sind was conquered in the same year.

In 711, the seventeen-year-old Umayyad general **Muhammad ibn al-Qasim** (moo-HAM-mad IH-bin al-KAH-sim) led an expedition through Baluchistan into Sind — the lower Indus Valley, modern southeastern Pakistan. The campaign was punitive in origin, prompted by Sindhi pirates' attacks on Arab shipping in the Persian Gulf, but it expanded into a full conquest. Muhammad ibn al-Qasim defeated the Hindu **Raja Dahir** at the **Battle of Aror**, took the major cities of Multan and Brahmanabad, and brought Sind under Umayyad control by 713.

Sind became the easternmost Umayyad province and the first foothold of Islam on the Indian subcontinent. The Muslim presence in northwestern India would expand and contract over the following centuries, becoming politically dominant under the **Delhi Sultanate** in the thirteenth century and the **Mughal Empire** in the sixteenth (medieval-india, mughal-empire — both not yet shipped). The 711 conquest of Sind is the founding moment of Islam in South Asia.

Muhammad ibn al-Qasim himself had a less successful afterlife. He was recalled to Damascus in 715 after the death of his patron, the governor al-Hajjaj, and was tortured to death by the new caliph **Sulayman** (sool-eye-MAHN) in a court intrigue. He was twenty years old.

---

The Central Asian conquest was conducted by **Qutayba ibn Muslim** (koo-TAY-ba IH-bin MOOS-lim), the Umayyad governor of Khorasan from 705 to 715. Qutayba's campaigns extended Umayyad control across the Oxus River into **Transoxiana** — the region containing modern Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, southern Kazakhstan, and parts of Turkmenistan, called by the Arabs *Ma wara' an-Nahr* ("That Which Lies Beyond the River").

Qutayba took **Bukhara** in 709, **Samarkand** in 712, and pushed the Umayyad frontier to the Syr Darya River and the foothills of the Tian Shan mountains by 715. The conquest brought into the Islamic world some of the great trading cities of the Silk Road — Bukhara and Samarkand had been Sogdian Iranian-language commercial centers for over a millennium, with thriving Buddhist, Manichaean, Nestorian Christian, and Zoroastrian communities. The Umayyad conquest converted the local population gradually over the following two centuries into one of the great heartlands of Islamic civilization. By the ninth century, Bukhara and Samarkand were major centers of Persian-Islamic high culture; the Persian-language poet **Ferdowsi** (who wrote the Shahnameh epic — Persian Empire Ch 9) and the philosopher-physician **Ibn Sina** (Avicenna) were both products of this Central Asian Islamic culture (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4).

Qutayba was killed in 715, in a mutiny by his own troops over his political maneuvering during the Umayyad caliphal succession crisis. His career, like Muhammad ibn al-Qasim's, ended badly — but the territory he had conquered stayed Umayyad and then Abbasid, and the Islamic civilization that grew up there would shape the cultural history of Iran and Central Asia for the next thirteen centuries.

---

Three simultaneous conquests in 711 — Spain, Sind, Transoxiana, all in essentially the same year — are remarkable. They reflect the enormous projection power of the Umayyad state at its peak, the high quality of Arab military organization, and the structural fragility of the states the Umayyads were attacking. Iberia had a divided Visigothic monarchy. Sind had a small Hindu kingdom outclassed by Arab military technology. Transoxiana had Sogdian city-states with no central political authority. None of the targets had the institutional depth to resist sustained Arab pressure.

The major Umayyad failure was at the one target that did have institutional depth: **Constantinople**.

---

The 717–718 **Second Arab Siege of Constantinople** is one of the most consequential battles in history. It is also far less well-known in the Western canon than it should be — partly because the failure of the siege is harder to celebrate than the dramatic Christian victories at Tours (732) and Lepanto (1571), partly because the Greek-language Byzantine sources documenting it have been less central to Western historical memory than the Latin sources for parallel events.

The strategic logic of the campaign was clear. By 717, the Umayyad caliphate had absorbed most of the eastern Mediterranean coast — Syria, Egypt, North Africa, Sicily was being raided regularly. Spain had just been added. The Byzantine Empire was reduced to Anatolia, the southern Balkans, and the Italian holdings in Sicily and southern Italy. Capturing Constantinople would deliver the Byzantine capital, the theme system that defended Anatolia (Byzantine Empire Ch 3), the Balkan provinces, and a clear path into Europe via the Danube and the Alpine passes. The same strategic considerations that had made the First Arab Siege of 674–678 attractive applied with even more force in 717.

The Umayyad caliph **Sulayman** sent his brother **Maslama** (mahs-LAH-mah) with what was, by the standards of the period, a massive force — perhaps **80,000 troops** and **1,800 ships**, in the largest pre-modern military operation ever launched against Constantinople. The siege began in August 717.

It went badly from the start.

The Byzantine emperor **Leo III** had taken the throne in March 717 in a palace coup and had used the five months before the siege to prepare. The **Theodosian Walls** of Constantinople (Byzantine Empire Ch 1) — the most formidable defensive system in the medieval world — held against everything the Arab army could throw at them. The Arab fleet was destroyed in successive engagements by Byzantine **Greek fire** (the medieval flamethrower-equivalent that had broken the First Arab Siege in 678 — Byzantine Empire Ch 4). The unusually severe winter of 717–718 froze the Thracian plain where the Arab army was camped, killing perhaps tens of thousands of men by exposure and starvation. A Bulgarian army arrived from the north, bribed by Leo to attack the Arab rear, and inflicted further losses. The Arab supply fleet, manned partly by Egyptian Christian conscripts, suffered mass desertions when sailors switched sides on contact with Byzantine forces.

By August 718, exactly one year after the siege began, Maslama lifted it and retreated. Of the **1,800 ships** that had sailed against Constantinople, only **five returned to the Caliphate**. The Arab field army that had walked east into Anatolia returned to Syria depleted and demoralized. The greatest single military operation the Umayyad state ever attempted had ended in catastrophic failure.

The historical consequences are hard to overstate. If Constantinople had fallen in 718, the Umayyad armies would have had a clear path into Europe via the Balkans and the Hungarian plain. There would have been no Byzantine Empire to anchor Christian eastern Europe for the next 700 years. There would have been no eastern Christian buffer to absorb Mongol pressure in the thirteenth century. The trajectory of European history would have been fundamentally different. As it happened, the failure at Constantinople in 718 effectively halted Arab expansion into Europe by sea. The Caliphate would never again attempt a serious assault on the Byzantine capital.

The defeat also contributed to internal Umayyad legitimacy problems. After 718, the Umayyads could no longer credibly promise the conquests that had justified their rule — and the Arab military elite that had grown wealthy from those conquests had to readjust to a caliphate whose expansion was over. The internal tensions this created were one of the slow-burning structural causes of the Abbasid Revolution thirty years later (Chapter 8).

The contrast with the Western European narrative is worth flagging. Western European textbooks have traditionally credited the **Battle of Tours** in 732 — when the Frankish ruler **Charles Martel** defeated an Umayyad raid in central France — with stopping the Arab advance into Europe. Tours mattered, but its strategic significance has often been overstated. The raid Charles Martel defeated was probably a probe rather than a serious invasion; the Umayyad force was perhaps 20,000 men, not the 400,000 some early modern Western histories claimed; and the long-term impact was modest. The **Siege of Constantinople in 717–718** was much more consequential. The Arab expansion into Europe was halted in the Balkans by Greek fire, not in Aquitaine by Frankish infantry. The fact that the Western narrative tradition celebrates Tours rather than Constantinople reflects the West's preference for crediting Latin Christian heroes over Greek Christian ones — an old pattern in European historical memory that can be observed in many other cases as well.

---

After 720, Umayyad expansion essentially stopped. The frontiers were stabilized — Constantinople in the northwest, the Pyrenees in the west, the Tian Shan in the east, the Indus in the southeast. Some incremental gains continued (the conquest of Sicily would begin in 827 under the Aghlabid emirs in North Africa, well after the Umayyad period; raids into southern France would continue into the 730s; Caucasus campaigns against the Khazars persisted through the 730s and 740s). But the great explosion of conquest that had begun in 634 with Khalid ibn al-Walid in Syria had run its course by 720.

The empire that remained covered roughly **5,000 miles east-to-west** — about the distance from London to Beijing — and it was held together by a thin Arab military elite ruling overwhelmingly non-Arab, mostly non-Muslim populations. The challenges of running this empire, rather than expanding it, would dominate the next thirty years and would eventually destroy the Umayyad dynasty.

---

Cross-civ check: the Umayyad expansion peak (705–720) overlaps with the **Tang Dynasty** under Empress Wu and Emperor Xuanzong (the Tang would collapse partially in the An Lushan Rebellion of 755–763, just after the Abbasid Revolution destroyed the Umayyads — both great early-medieval empires entering structural crisis at almost the same time), the **early Iconoclast** Byzantium under Leo III (Byzantine Empire Ch 4 — Leo III is the same emperor who saved Constantinople in 718), the late **Visigothic** kingdom destroyed in 711, and the early **Pala** dynasty in northern India. The fact that the Tang and Umayyad armies almost met in Central Asia in this period — both empires expanding into the same general area, separated only by the Tian Shan and the Pamirs — would produce, a generation later, the **Battle of Talas** in 751, where an Abbasid-led army defeated a Tang force on the Talas River in modern Kyrgyzstan. Talas was after the Umayyad period (it happened a year after the Battle of the Zab — Chapter 8), but it was the natural outcome of the simultaneous Tang and Umayyad expansions of the early eighth century. The two expanding empires hit each other on the Talas, the Tang lost, and Central Asia was Islamicized rather than Sinicized for the next thousand years (Tang-Song China — not yet shipped).

# Chapter 7 — Architecture and the Religion

The Umayyad Caliphate built things. It also developed a religion. The two activities were intertwined — the major architectural projects of the dynasty were religious buildings, and the major theological developments of the period were shaped by the building program. This chapter is about both: the early Islamic monumental architecture (the Dome of the Rock, the Great Mosque of Damascus, the Aqsa Mosque) and the early Islamic religious tradition (the hadith, the early sharia, the dhimmi system, the early theological controversies that would later produce the great Mu'tazila debate in the Abbasid period).

---

The **Dome of the Rock** — completed by **Abd al-Malik** in 691, on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem — is the oldest surviving piece of Islamic monumental architecture and one of the most beautiful buildings in the world.

The site's history is layered. The **Temple Mount** had been the platform of the Jewish Second Temple, destroyed by the Romans during the Jewish-Roman War in 70 CE (Ancient Israel Ch 8). The Romans had built a Temple of Jupiter on the site after suppressing the Bar Kokhba revolt in 135. Under the Christian emperors (after Constantine), the Jupiter temple was abandoned and the Temple Mount left in ruins — the Christians of late antique Jerusalem had concentrated their religious building on the Holy Sepulchre church across the city, which Constantine's mother Helena had built around the supposed site of the Resurrection (Byzantine Empire Ch 3). The Temple Mount was a deliberately abandoned sacred site for over six centuries before the Umayyad conquest.

Abd al-Malik chose to claim the abandoned Temple Mount for Islam. The theological motivation was the **Night Journey** narrative — the early Islamic tradition that Muhammad had been taken miraculously from Mecca to Jerusalem one night, ascended from a rock on the Temple Mount through the heavens to meet earlier prophets and God, and returned to Mecca by dawn. The rock itself became, in Islamic tradition, the sacred point from which Muhammad's ascension had taken place. The Dome of the Rock was built directly over this rock as a shrine — a covering for the sacred site, not a congregational mosque (the slightly later **al-Aqsa Mosque** at the southern end of the same platform served the congregational function).

The architecture was a deliberate piece of theological communication. The dome itself — a large gilded hemispherical wooden dome originally covered in gold leaf, with the modern gold coating dating to a 1990s restoration — sat on an octagonal arcade that visually tied the dome to the geometry of the rock below. The interior was decorated with elaborate mosaics in the late Roman-Byzantine style, with calligraphic Arabic inscriptions running around the inside arcade (the earliest surviving monumental Arabic inscriptions, and the oldest surviving Quranic text in any form). The inscriptions were polemical — they include extended passages denying the divinity of Christ and the doctrine of the Trinity, addressed quite specifically to the Christian visitors who would inevitably come to see the new building. This was an Islamic statement set down in Christian Jerusalem, with the Christians as the explicit theological audience.

Why the octagonal plan? The eight-sided arcade has been interpreted as a deliberate echo of the eight-sided plans used in Christian baptisteries and martyria of late antiquity (the **Church of the Holy Sepulchre** itself had originally featured an octagonal rotunda over the supposed tomb of Christ). The Umayyads were using a familiar Christian architectural vocabulary — central plan, octagonal arcade, gilded dome, Roman-Byzantine mosaics — to make a building that would compete with the Christian holy sites for visual and religious authority. The form was Roman; the message was Islamic.

Why the materials? The mosaics include depictions of crowns, jewels, and trees — luxury imagery in Roman-Byzantine convention. Some scholars have read these as Sasanian and Byzantine royal regalia, displayed as trophies of Islamic conquest over the two empires whose treasuries had funded the building. The polychrome marble panels lining the interior were spolia, taken from Roman buildings across the eastern Mediterranean. The visual statement of the Dome was: this is the wealth of the conquered empires, redirected to the service of the new religion.

The Dome of the Rock has been continuously standing since 691, with repeated restorations. It is the oldest surviving major work of Islamic architecture and one of the earliest examples of any monumental architecture from the medieval Mediterranean to survive intact. The reason it survives is that it was kept in continuous religious use through every change of regime — Umayyad, Abbasid, Fatimid, Crusader (who briefly converted it to a Christian church called the Templum Domini in the twelfth century), Ayyubid, Mamluk, Ottoman, and modern. Religious continuity is the best preservation strategy in architectural history.

---

The **Great Mosque of Damascus** — completed by **al-Walid I** in 715 — is the second great Umayyad architectural project and a different kind of building: a congregational mosque, designed for the daily and weekly communal prayer of an entire urban population.

The site's history was even more layered than the Temple Mount's. There had been a temple to the **Aramaean storm god Hadad** on the site since at least the Iron Age (Ancient Israel Ch 6 — Hadad is the same god Aramean kings worshipped). Under Roman occupation it became a temple of **Jupiter**. After the Christianization of the Roman Empire, the Jupiter temple was converted into a basilica dedicated to **John the Baptist** (whose head was reputedly buried in the eastern courtyard, a relic Christians had honored for several centuries before the Arab conquest). The early Umayyad conquerors had initially shared the cathedral with the Christian community of Damascus — Muslims praying in the eastern half, Christians worshipping in the western half — for about seventy years.

Around 706, al-Walid I bought out the Christian congregation (paying them substantial compensation and authorizing the construction of new churches elsewhere in the city), demolished the basilica, and built a purpose-designed congregational mosque on the site. The construction took about a decade. The result was the oldest surviving congregational mosque in the world that retains its original plan, and one of the most influential religious buildings in Islamic architectural history.

The Great Mosque of Damascus is structured around a vast rectangular **courtyard** (the **sahn**) with a covered prayer hall along one side. Three minarets rise from the corners — including one (the Minaret of Jesus, in the southeastern corner) that Islamic eschatological tradition holds will be the place from which Jesus will descend at the end of the world. The walls and the prayer hall were decorated with extraordinary **glass mosaics** in gold, green, and blue, depicting an idealized paradise of cities, palaces, gardens, rivers, and trees — but no human or animal figures (the aniconic prohibition). The mosaics were Byzantine in technical execution (the workshops were probably staffed by Greek mosaicists from Constantinople, hired or sent by the emperor as part of a diplomatic exchange) and Islamic in iconographic content. The image of paradise on the mosque walls was intended to give the worshipper a visual prefiguration of the heavenly garden the Quran promises to the faithful.

The Great Mosque set the architectural template for early Islamic congregational mosques. Its courtyard-and-prayer-hall plan, its mihrab niche indicating the direction of Mecca (the **qibla**), its minbar (preacher's pulpit), its colonnaded portico — these features would become the standard elements of Sunni mosque architecture for the next thirteen centuries. Most significant later mosques across the Islamic world descend, formally if not directly, from the Damascus prototype.

---

The **al-Aqsa Mosque** in Jerusalem — built by al-Walid I around 705, on the southern end of the same Temple Mount platform that holds the Dome of the Rock — is the third major early Umayyad religious building. It is the **congregational mosque** of the Temple Mount complex, while the Dome is the **shrine** over the rock. Together with the Dome, al-Aqsa makes Jerusalem the third-holiest city in Islam after Mecca and Medina.

The al-Aqsa Mosque has been damaged and rebuilt several times across the thirteen centuries of its history (most consequentially by the 749 earthquake that destroyed much of the original Umayyad structure, with rebuilding under the Abbasids producing essentially the building that now stands). The current building preserves the basic Umayyad plan but with substantial later modification.

The phrase **al-Aqsa**, "the farthest [mosque]," comes from the Quran (Chapter 17, verse 1 — the verse describing Muhammad's Night Journey to "the farthest mosque" from the sacred mosque in Mecca). Whether the Quranic phrase originally referred to a building in Jerusalem or to a heavenly mosque is debated by modern scholars; the early Islamic identification of the Quranic "farthest mosque" with the building on the Temple Mount became standard within a few decades of Muhammad's death and was certainly fixed by Abd al-Malik's time. The naming locked in Jerusalem's religious significance for Islam in the Quranic sources themselves, regardless of the original referential intent.

---

The Umayyad architectural program also included the **desert palaces** — a series of royal estates in remote locations across Syria, Jordan, and Iraq that the Umayyad caliphs used as hunting lodges, retreat houses, and venues for less religiously inhibited entertainment than the Damascus court could comfortably support. The most famous are **Qusayr Amra** (in modern Jordan, with extraordinary frescoed bath murals that include nude female bathers, the only surviving Umayyad-era figural painting), **Khirbet al-Mafjar** (near Jericho, with elaborate Roman-style baths and an intact mosaic floor), **Mshatta** (also in Jordan, with carved limestone facades that combine Sasanian, Byzantine, and Hellenistic ornamental traditions), and **Anjar** in Lebanon (the most fully planned of the desert palaces, laid out on a Roman city grid).

The desert palaces are the secret architectural program of the Umayyad caliphate — the buildings that show what the dynasty actually liked, as opposed to what they showed publicly in Damascus and Jerusalem. They are deeply Roman-Byzantine in their planning and decorative vocabulary, full of figural imagery (including erotic imagery), and designed for a courtly lifestyle that was much closer to Sasanian-Byzantine royal practice than to early Medinan Muslim austerity. The desert palaces are some of the strongest evidence for the Companion-class accusation that the Umayyads had become Arab kings rather than Muslim caliphs.

---

Beyond architecture, the Umayyad century saw the development of the **religious infrastructure** — the institutions, practices, and texts that would define what Islam was, as opposed to just what it had been when Muhammad died.

The most consequential intellectual project was the **collection of the hadith** — the reported sayings and actions of Muhammad, transmitted through chains of oral testimony from the Companions to their students. The hadith would, alongside the Quran, become the second source of Islamic religious law and ethics. The systematic compilations of the great collectors (Bukhari, Muslim, and the others whose works became canonical) lay still a century in the future under the Abbasids (Islamic Golden Age Ch 5); but the **source material** — the individual hadith reports, the chains of transmission (the **isnad**) by which each report was traced back to a specific Companion of Muhammad, the early scholarly evaluation of which transmitters were reliable and which were not — was being gathered and circulated under the Umayyads.

The hadith project posed an unusual epistemic problem. The early Muslim community had to assess, transmitter by transmitter, whether a particular report about Muhammad was reliable. The reports being transmitted were anywhere from one to four generations removed from Muhammad's actual lifetime by the late Umayyad period, and the hadith literature was already being heavily contested for political reasons (Sunni and Shia partisans were each circulating reports that supported their preferred caliphal candidates, and the Umayyad state was patronizing reports that favored its own legitimacy). The discipline of **hadith criticism** — the scholarly evaluation of the soundness of each individual report — emerged in this period as a methodology for sorting genuine from forged reports. The modern Western critical scholarship on hadith is, in some ways, simply an extension of methods the medieval Muslim hadith critics had already developed.

A parallel project was the development of **Islamic jurisprudence** (the **fiqh**, FIK-h). Under the late Umayyads, scholars in the garrison cities of **Medina**, **Kufa**, **Basra**, and increasingly **Fustat** in Egypt began formalizing the methods by which religious law (the **sharia**, sha-REE-ah) could be derived from the Quran and the hadith. The two main early schools were the **Hijazi tradition** at Medina (eventually associated with **Malik ibn Anas**, born around 711, founder of the Maliki legal school) and the **Iraqi tradition** at Kufa (eventually associated with **Abu Hanifa**, born around 699, founder of the Hanafi legal school). The four classical Sunni legal schools — Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali — would not crystallize until the early Abbasid period, but the source materials and the methodological approaches were being developed under the Umayyads.

A third project was the early **Islamic theology** (*kalam*). Three theological currents emerged in the Umayyad century, around questions the Quran did not directly settle:

The **Qadariyya** (KAH-dah-ree-yah, from *qadar*, "divine decree") argued that human beings have free will and moral responsibility for their actions, against the strong-predestinarian Umayyad establishment view that everything happens by divine decree. The Qadariyya position was politically dangerous because it implied that the Umayyad caliphs were morally responsible for their decisions and could be held accountable; the establishment view implied that whatever the caliphs did was God's will and could not be questioned.

The **Murji'a** (MURR-jee-ah, from *irja*, "deferral") deferred judgment on whether Muslims who committed major sins were still Muslims. Their position was politically convenient for the Umayyad dynasty: if the Karbala killing of Husayn (Chapter 4) and the bombardment of Mecca (Chapter 5) and various other Umayyad atrocities did not strictly speaking remove the perpetrators from the Muslim community, then the Umayyad caliphs retained their religious legitimacy regardless of their political behavior. The Murji'a were, in effect, the establishment-friendly theological school of the Umayyad period.

The **Mu'tazila** (moo-TAH-zih-lah, "those who withdraw") emerged late in the Umayyad period and would explode in importance under the early Abbasids (Islamic Golden Age Ch 5). The Mu'tazila were rationalist theologians who held that reason, properly applied, could derive the truths of religion independently of (though in conformity with) revelation. They argued for human free will against predestination, for divine justice (which they understood as committing God to act in accordance with rational ethical principles), and for the **createdness of the Quran** (the controversial doctrine that the Quran was God's created speech rather than a coeternal aspect of God's being). The Mu'tazila program would become the official theology of the early Abbasid state under the caliph **al-Ma'mun** in 833 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 2), with violent enforcement against the traditionalist Sunni scholars who opposed it. The seed of the entire later debate was planted under the Umayyads.

---

The most consequential institutional project of the Umayyad century, for the long-term character of the empire, was the **dhimmi system** — the legal framework for governing the empire's non-Muslim subject populations.

The Quran establishes a category of "**People of the Book**" (*ahl al-kitab*, AHL al-kih-TAHB) — religious communities that had received earlier divine revelations through earlier prophets, specifically Christians (whose revelation was the Gospels through Jesus), Jews (whose revelation was the Torah through Moses), and possibly Zoroastrians and Sabians (the categories were variably interpreted). The People of the Book were entitled, under Quranic and prophetic teaching, to live as protected religious minorities under Muslim political authority — preserving their own religious law, their own clergy, their own places of worship — in exchange for accepting Muslim political supremacy and paying the **jizya** (JIZ-yah), a head tax on adult non-Muslim men.

This Quranic framework became, under the Umayyads, the **dhimmi** (DIM-mee) system — a legal category of "protected people" under Muslim governance. The dhimmi:
- Could practice their own religion in private and could maintain existing places of worship (though new construction was generally restricted);
- Were governed in matters of personal status (marriage, inheritance, religious life) by their own religious authorities and laws (the foundation of the much later Ottoman **millet system** — Ottoman Empire Ch 6);
- Paid the jizya head tax and the kharaj land tax (dhimmi taxes were initially higher than Muslim taxes, which is one of the structural problems that produced the Mawali grievance — Chapter 8);
- Were excluded from senior military and political offices reserved for Muslims;
- Were subject to various social-symbolic restrictions of varying intensity (distinctive clothing in some periods, prohibitions on bell-ringing or public Christian processions in some places, restrictions on building height, and so on — the so-called Pact of Umar codified some of these, though its actual provenance is contested).

The dhimmi framework was, by the standards of contemporary Christian European treatment of religious minorities, generous. The Christian and Jewish populations of the Islamic world generally fared substantially better than Muslims (or Jews) in contemporary Christian Europe. The system was not equality — equality was not on offer in any premodern society — but it was a workable framework for plural religious communities to coexist under a single political authority. The framework would continue, with various local adaptations, throughout the Abbasid period (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6), the Fatimid period, the Ayyubid and Mamluk periods, and the Ottoman period (Ottoman Empire Ch 6) — all the way to the late nineteenth century, when European-influenced legal reforms began to phase out the formal dhimmi categories in favor of equal citizenship.

A last note on dhimmi practice. Conversion from dhimmi status to Islam was permitted and over time became attractive, but conversion away from Islam was forbidden — a Muslim who converted to Christianity or Judaism could be punished by death under the apostasy ruling (the *ridda* prohibition that had also justified Abu Bakr's Ridda Wars in 632 — Chapter 3). The asymmetry — easy entry into Islam, prohibited exit from Islam — was one of the structural mechanisms by which the long, slow conversion of the Middle East to Islam was sustained over the following centuries.

---

Cross-civ check: the early Umayyad architectural and religious development overlaps with the **Tang Dynasty's** architectural ambition under Empress Wu (the Tang built their own great Buddhist cave temples and Chang'an pagodas in the same period the Umayyads were building the Dome of the Rock and the Damascus Mosque), the **Iconoclast** reorganization of Byzantine religious life under Leo III after 717 (Byzantine Empire Ch 4 — the Byzantine iconoclast movement was partly inspired by what the Byzantines had observed of Islamic aniconism), the early **Anglo-Saxon** Christian architecture in northern England (Bede's age, the same generation as Abd al-Malik), and the **Pala dynasty's** Buddhist architectural ambition in northeastern India. The Umayyad development of the dhimmi system is roughly contemporary with the **Coptic patriarchate's** internal reorganization in conquered Egypt (the Coptic Church, under Arab rule, became less politically subordinated to Constantinople and more autonomous than it had been in centuries), the **Nestorian** church's eastward expansion into Tang China (the famous **Xi'an Stele** documenting Nestorian Christianity's presence in the Tang capital was erected in 781, just after the Umayyad period), and the development of the Byzantine **theme system** that gave conquered Christian peasant-soldiers a stake in defending the empire (Byzantine Empire Ch 3). Different states, different religions, broadly similar institutional problems: how to govern multi-religious populations under unified political authority, and how to give the religion that legitimized the state a public architectural face.

# Chapter 8 — The Abbasid Revolution

The Umayyad Caliphate was destroyed in three years.

In 747, an obscure freedman named **Abu Muslim** raised a revolutionary banner in **Khorasan**, the easternmost Umayyad province. By 750, the Umayyad army had been defeated at the Battle of the Zab, the last Umayyad caliph had been hunted down in Egypt, and the surviving Umayyad princes had been massacred at a Damascus banquet. The dynasty that had ruled an empire from Spain to Sind for ninety years was extinguished except for one survivor, who escaped and would shortly found a successor state in al-Andalus.

This chapter is about how that happened. It is also about what came next — the **Abbasid Caliphate** that replaced the Umayyad and that would rule the Islamic world for the next five centuries — and about the Umayyad afterlife in Spain. The detailed Abbasid story is the subject of Islamic Golden Age Ch 1 onward; this chapter ends where that one begins.

---

The Umayyad collapse was structurally driven. By the 740s, the dynasty was failing in five separate ways simultaneously, and any one of them might have been survivable but the combination was not.

**Fiscal failure.** The Umayyad fiscal system had been built around Arab privilege: Arab Muslim warriors received cash stipends from the diwan, paid for by the kharaj land tax that fell most heavily on non-Muslim cultivators. The system worked while the Arab Muslim population was small and the conquered population was large and non-Muslim. By the 740s, however, the population mix had shifted. Conversion to Islam was steady (slow, but cumulatively significant after a century of Muslim rule). The mawali — non-Arab Muslim converts — were paying lower taxes (since the religious tax differential favored them) but were also excluded from the diwan stipends that had originally been Arab privileges. The result was a fiscal squeeze: tax revenues from a converting population were declining, but the cost of paying Arab military stipends and garrisoning the imperial frontiers was not. Umar II had attempted in 717–720 to fix the problem by extending diwan privileges to recent mawali converts and equalizing the tax system; the reform had been popular and had nearly bankrupted the state. His successors had reversed it. The structural fiscal problem remained unsolved for the next thirty years.

**Mawali grievance.** The non-Arab Muslim converts — Persians, Egyptians, Berbers, Aramaic-speaking Iraqis, Sogdians from Central Asia — had been told by the religion they had converted to that all Muslims were equal before God. The Umayyad fiscal and political system told them otherwise. Mawali Muslims paid the same taxes as non-Muslims in many cases, were excluded from senior military commands and provincial governorships, were paid lower stipends than Arab Muslims when stipends were paid at all, and were treated socially as second-class citizens in the Arab-dominated garrison cities. The discontent was strongest among the **Persian-speaking Iraqi and Iranian** mawali, who had a particularly long memory of the Sasanian imperial culture they had lost in the 640s and a particularly developed sense of cultural-civilizational superiority over their Arab rulers. This Persian-Iraqi mawali population was the social base from which the Abbasid Revolution recruited its supporters.

**Tribal feuding.** The Umayyad caliphate was nominally an Arab Muslim federation, but in practice the Arab tribes that had carried out the conquests had carried their pre-Islamic feuds into the new state. The two great tribal blocs — the **Qaysi** (north Arabian) and **Kalbi/Yamani** (south Arabian) — had been at intermittent war with each other since long before Islam, and they continued the feud inside the Umayyad army and provincial administration. Different Umayyad caliphs favored different blocs. Different governors organized their subordinates along bloc lines. By the 740s, the tribal feuding had become so destructive of normal military function that the Umayyad army's coherence was visibly degrading — internal feuds between Qaysi and Kalbi commanders cost the dynasty more soldiers than its external wars did.

**Berber and Kharijite revolts in North Africa.** In 740, the Berber populations of the Maghreb — heavily mawali and chronically aggrieved at Arab fiscal exploitation — revolted under Kharijite religious leadership (the Kharijites, the seventh-century breakaway from Ali's coalition, had migrated to North Africa where their austere theology found receptive audiences among the Berbers). The revolt produced major Umayyad military defeats and effectively detached most of the Maghreb west of Egypt from caliphal control by the mid-740s. North Africa would not be reincorporated into a unified caliphate; it would fragment into a series of independent emirates that the Abbasids would also fail to control.

**Caliphal succession chaos.** The competent caliph **Hisham** died in 743 after nineteen years of stable rule. His successor **al-Walid II** lasted a year before being murdered. **Yazid III** lasted six months before dying of plague. **Ibrahim** lasted two months before being deposed. **Marwan II** finally took the throne in late 744 and tried to stabilize the situation, but he had to spend most of the next five years fighting a major Syrian civil war among rival Umayyad factions. The dynasty was tearing itself apart from inside while its frontiers were collapsing.

By 747 the Umayyad state was structurally ready to be overthrown by anyone who could mobilize the accumulated grievances against it. The Abbasid revolutionaries were the people who could.

---

The **Abbasids** were the descendants of **al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib**, the Prophet Muhammad's paternal uncle. They were therefore cousins of the Prophet — close kin, but not from the Hashimite branch that Muhammad himself had belonged to and that Ali (Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law) had inherited. The Abbasids had been politically marginal throughout the Rashidun and most of the Umayyad period; they had spent the 720s and 730s quietly cultivating a revolutionary network in the eastern provinces, presenting themselves as the rightful Muslim leaders against the Umayyad usurpers.

The Abbasid program was a coalition. They appealed to the **Shia** by claiming to be members of the Prophet's family with a legitimate caliphal claim (without, however, formally committing to Shia theology). They appealed to the **mawali** by promising to abolish the Arab privilege system and create a fully equal Muslim community. They appealed to the **Khorasanis** by promising to restore Iranian-Persian dignity within a renewed Islamic framework. They appealed to the **anti-Umayyad Companions' descendants** in the Hijaz by promising to restore the legitimate caliphate to a closer Prophet-relative. They appealed to anyone who wanted change, while remaining vague about what specifically would be done after the Umayyads were gone.

The operational mastermind of the Abbasid network was **Abu Muslim** (ah-BOO MOOS-lim), an obscure freedman of probably Persian origin whose actual identity is one of the great minor mysteries of early Islamic history. The early sources record different versions of his name, parentage, and origin; what is firm is that he appeared in Khorasan in the early 740s as a young man (probably in his twenties) representing the Abbasid family's eastern operation, and that he was extraordinarily effective at building underground political organizations.

Abu Muslim spent several years in Khorasan cultivating the local Arab settlers, the Persian mawali, the disaffected Yamani tribal clans, and various Shia partisans into a unified revolutionary coalition. The coordination was conducted in secrecy under the cover of the *Hashimiyya* movement (a vague pro-Hashimite political tendency that the Abbasids had been steering toward their own claim for years). The key revolutionary symbol — the **black banner** of the Abbasid family, reportedly chosen in deliberate contrast to the white banners the Umayyads used — became the visual marker of the coming revolt.

In **June 747**, Abu Muslim raised the black banner at the village of **Sikadanj** near Merv, with about two thousand armed supporters, and declared open revolt against the Umayyad governor of Khorasan. Within months, his force had grown to perhaps twenty thousand. By 748 the Abbasid army held all of Khorasan and was advancing west into Iran.

(The black banner has had a long afterlife in Islamic political iconography. "A black banner from Khorasan" became a recurring messianic symbol in later Sunni eschatology — invoked, not coincidentally, by various modern jihadist movements that adopted black banners thirteen centuries later. The original was a dynastic logo, not a religious symbol.)

---

The military campaign moved fast. The Khorasani Abbasid army, well-led and high-motivated, defeated successive Umayyad provincial forces across Iran in 748 and 749. By late 749, the Abbasid commander **Qahtaba ibn Shabib** had reached Iraq. The Abbasid family itself emerged from underground — the senior Abbasid leader, **Ibrahim al-Imam**, was captured and killed by Marwan II's agents, but his brother **Abu al-Abbas as-Saffah** (as-saf-FAH, "the Bloodletter" — the epithet was self-chosen and self-celebratory) was acclaimed caliph by the Abbasid army at Kufa in October 749.

The decisive military encounter came on **25 January 750** at the **Great Zab river** in northern Iraq. The Abbasid army from Khorasan met the main Umayyad field army under **Marwan II** in person. Numbers are debated; modern estimates suggest the two armies were roughly comparable in size (perhaps 25,000 to 50,000 men each), though the Abbasid force was qualitatively superior — better led, better motivated, fighting for a cause its troops believed in, while the Umayyad army was a worn-out collection of tribal levies and the surviving regular professional units. The battle was decisive. The Umayyad army broke and was destroyed. Marwan II fled west across Syria.

He did not stop running. The Abbasid pursuit followed him across Syria, into Egypt. Marwan was finally cornered and killed in a small Egyptian village called **Busir** in August 750. He had been the last Umayyad caliph. His death, six months after the Battle of the Zab, ended the Umayyad dynasty as a ruling power.

---

Then came the calculated extermination.

The new caliph Abu al-Abbas as-Saffah understood the Umayyad family's residual political danger. As long as Umayyad princes survived, they would be the natural rallying points for any anti-Abbasid revolt. The Abbasids therefore conducted a systematic campaign to hunt down and kill every member of the extended Umayyad family they could find. The campaign was conducted with calculated theater.

The most famous episode is the **Damascus banquet massacre**. As-Saffah's representative invited the surviving Umayyad princes — perhaps eighty men, the senior surviving members of the family — to a banquet in Damascus, ostensibly to negotiate their surrender and to receive Abbasid pensions in exchange for political quiescence. When the princes had assembled, the Abbasid hosts had them all murdered at table. According to the most lurid version of the story preserved in later sources, the bodies were covered with leather sheets while the new caliph and his court continued eating. The leather-sheets detail is of disputed provenance — it appears in some later Abbasid-era chroniclers and not in others, and the contemporary Arabic sources love a good banquet massacre with suspiciously clean dating. The banquet itself is well attested; the leather-sheets prop is the kind of theatrical embellishment that may or may not have happened. The broader campaign it represents was real, however: the Abbasids hunted down Umayyad family members across the empire over the following years, exhuming their tombs in Damascus, dismantling their genealogical claim, replacing them with their own. As-Saffah's nickname was not metaphor.

One Umayyad escaped.

**Abd al-Rahman I** (ahbd al-RAH-mahn, born 731), a twenty-year-old grandson of the caliph Hisham, fled the massacre. The dramatic version of the escape — which appears in his own court historian's account and was probably partly true — has Abd al-Rahman swimming the Euphrates while Abbasid horsemen shot at him from the bank. After five years on the run, traveling slowly across North Africa under the protection of Berber tribes (his mother had been a Berber concubine, and the family connection helped), he reached **Spain** — the westernmost province of the old Umayyad empire, where the Arab-Berber occupiers were divided into rival factions and where no Umayyad authority had been established.

In **756**, Abd al-Rahman defeated the Abbasid-appointed governor of Spain at the **Battle of al-Musara** outside Córdoba and proclaimed himself emir of an **independent Emirate of Córdoba** that recognized neither Abbasid nor Umayyad authority — except his own. The new Andalusian Umayyad state would last in various forms for another 270 years, becoming a full caliphate in 929 under Abd al-Rahman III and producing one of the most brilliant cultures of the medieval Mediterranean (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6–7). The story of the swimming prince, dictated by Abd al-Rahman to his court historian and probably partly true even allowing for legendary embellishment, became one of the great founding myths of medieval Spanish history. The implication was enormous: the Islamic world was now politically split, with two rival caliphal lineages — Abbasid in the east, Umayyad in the west — that would never be reunited.

---

The new Abbasid Caliphate was, in many ways, the project the Umayyad caliphate had failed to be.

The Abbasids moved their capital from Damascus to **Baghdad**, founded in 762 by the second Abbasid caliph **al-Mansur** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). The shift from Damascus to Baghdad was a shift from a Mediterranean-oriented capital that had inherited Byzantine institutions to an Iraqi-centered capital that drew on Sasanian Persian traditions. The Abbasid administration was Persianized — the great vizier families like the **Barmakids** were Persian converts, the court culture absorbed Sasanian ceremony, the bureaucracy ran on Sasanian-derived practices. The Abbasid state was, structurally, a Persian-Islamic synthesis where the Umayyad state had been an Arab-Byzantine one.

The Abbasids fixed the mawali grievance the Umayyads had failed to fix. Non-Arab Muslims, especially Persians, were given full equality in the new caliphate's military, administrative, and cultural life. The Arab tribal aristocracy that had run the Umayyad state was sidelined in favor of a more meritocratic and more Persian-influenced administrative class. The Persian language re-emerged as the literary and administrative language of the eastern Islamic provinces (Persian Empire Ch 10) — a recovery that would produce the great medieval Persian poetic tradition (Ferdowsi, Rumi, Hafez, Sa'di) over the following centuries.

The Abbasids also patronized the **Translation Movement** that would become the defining cultural project of the early Islamic Golden Age (Islamic Golden Age Ch 2). The systematic translation of Greek philosophical, mathematical, medical, and astronomical texts into Arabic — building on the late Umayyad-era beginnings (Chapter 7) but operating at vastly larger scale under Abbasid patronage — is one of the great intellectual achievements of human history. The texts that the Abbasid translators preserved would, several centuries later, be re-translated from Arabic into Latin and would form the core of the European Renaissance's scientific recovery (Islamic Golden Age Ch 2).

The Abbasids did not, however, become Shia. Despite their use of Alid loyalty as part of their revolutionary coalition, the Abbasids had no intention of yielding the caliphate to the descendants of Ali. The Shia partisans who had supported the revolution were marginalized after the victory. Shia movements would proliferate in opposition to the Abbasids over the following centuries — the Fatimid Caliphate in Egypt (909), the Buyid amirs in Baghdad (945), the Ismaili Assassins in Iran (1090s), and eventually the Safavid revolution in Iran (1501) — but the central Abbasid state remained Sunni.

The Abbasid caliphate would last, in formal terms, until **1258**, when the Mongol army of **Hulagu Khan** sacked Baghdad and killed the last effective Abbasid caliph, ending five centuries of Abbasid rule (Islamic Golden Age Ch 10, Mongol Empire Ch 5). Long before then, the political reality had become one of nominal Abbasid sovereignty over a fragmented array of effectively independent Islamic dynasties. But the cultural-economic synthesis the Abbasids had built — the Arabic-Persian high culture, the translation movement, the institutional infrastructure of medieval Islamic learning — would survive their political collapse and would shape Islamic civilization for centuries afterward.

---

A note on what 750 meant.

The Umayyad century had built the framework. The conquests had taken the territory. The Damascus capital had stabilized the administration. The Dome of the Rock and the Damascus Mosque had given the religion its public architectural face. The Quranic codification under Uthman, the Arabization under Abd al-Malik, the gold dinar, the dhimmi system — all of these were Umayyad accomplishments that the Abbasids inherited and built on.

But the Umayyad century had also been incomplete. The mawali grievance was structural. The tribal feuding was destructive. The Arab-privilege fiscal system was fundamentally incompatible with a religion that proclaimed all believers equal. The dynasty was overthrown not because Islam had failed but because the Umayyads' particular form of Arab-imperial Islam had failed. The Abbasids would build an Islam-imperial state that worked better — for a while — by abandoning the Arab-privilege framework in favor of a more universal Muslim identity.

The cultural-economic Abbasid era — what the Western historiographic tradition calls the **Islamic Golden Age** — begins with the Battle of the Zab in 750 and the founding of Baghdad in 762. It ends with the Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258. Five centuries. The greatest intellectual workshop in the medieval world. Algebra, algorithms, optics, medicine, philosophy, the preservation of the Greek classical tradition that would later seed the European Renaissance.

The narrative that picks up where this one ends is [Islamic Golden Age](#) Chapter 1. The revolution that destroyed the Umayyad dynasty produced the empire that built medieval Islamic civilization. The transition was violent, calculated, and complete. The dynasty died. The civilization began.

---

A final reflection on the Umayyad century. The Rashidun and Umayyad period of 632–750 is the founding moment of Islamic civilization, and its judgments are mixed. The conquest of an empire stretching from the Atlantic to the Indus in 118 years is one of the most remarkable military expansions in human history. The construction of the Dome of the Rock and the Great Mosque of Damascus established Islamic architecture as a tradition of the first rank. The codification of the Quran, the first systematic hadith collection, the early sharia jurisprudence, the development of the dhimmi system — all of these laid the institutional foundations on which medieval Islamic civilization was built.

The period is also morally complicated, in ways the early Muslim community was already aware of and arguing about. The forced incorporation of conquered populations under Arab rule — even if accompanied by relatively generous protection of non-Muslim religious life — was conquest, and the people doing the conquering knew it. The killing of Muhammad's grandson at Karbala was a religious-political crime that the Umayyad dynasty never recovered from morally. The mawali grievance was a structural injustice that the religion's own egalitarian ideals indicted. The Abbasid massacre of the Umayyads at Damascus in 750 was a calculated atrocity that the Abbasids' own revolutionary rhetoric had repeatedly condemned in the dynasty they were replacing. The early Islamic political tradition was already capable, by the late Umayyad period, of recognizing the gap between the religion's professed ideals and the empire's operating practice.

The honest assessment is that the Umayyad century was both: it built one of the most consequential civilizations in human history, and it did so through methods that the religion itself had to keep arguing about for the next thirteen centuries. The arguments are still going. The buildings are still standing. The conversation continues.

---

Cross-civ check: the Abbasid Revolution and the early Abbasid period (747–800) overlap with the **An Lushan Rebellion** in Tang China (755–763 — the catastrophic civil war that nearly destroyed the Tang dynasty and produced massive population losses, contemporary with the Abbasid consolidation), the **Iconoclast Century** in Byzantium (Byzantine Empire Ch 4), the **Carolingian** consolidation in France (Charlemagne would be crowned Roman emperor in 800, the same generation as the Abbasid caliph Harun al-Rashid — Islamic Golden Age Ch 1), the **Pala dynasty's** ascent in northeastern India, and the early **Tibetan Empire** under Songtsen Gampo and his successors. The mid-eighth century is the moment when the early-medieval Eurasian state system reorganizes — the Tang nearly destroyed, the Byzantines reduced to Anatolia, the Umayyads replaced by the Abbasids, the Carolingians replacing the Merovingians in western Europe, the Tibetans rising in Inner Asia. The Umayyad collapse is one element of a larger pattern of mid-century Eurasian political reorganization. The civilization that emerged from the rearrangement — Abbasid Baghdad, Tang Chang'an, Byzantine Constantinople, Carolingian Aachen, the Lhasa-centered Tibetan Empire — would shape the early-medieval world.

The Umayyad century built the Islamic empire. The Abbasid millennium built the Islamic civilization. The transition happened in the seventy-five days between the Battle of the Zab and the founding meeting of the Abbasid council at Kufa — the most consequential change of dynasty in medieval Islamic history, and the moment that separates the founding moment of Islam from everything that came after.

The dynasty died. The religion stayed. The empire was rebuilt under new management. The Iberian survivor founded a parallel state that would last longer than the original. And the conversation about what Islam should be — a debate that started during Muhammad's lifetime and that Karbala had given a particular Sunni-Shia structure — would continue across the next thirteen centuries, into the present day.

The Iran-Saudi rivalry of 2026, the Hezbollah-Israel conflict of the 2010s, the Iran-Iraq war of the 1980s, the Sunni-Shia tensions in modern Iraq, Syria, Yemen, Bahrain, Pakistan: all of these are operating, in part, on theological-political fault lines that were established between 632 and 750. The narrative you have just read is the founding moment of those fault lines. What came after is, in significant ways, an extended argument about what the founding moment actually meant.

The arguments continue.

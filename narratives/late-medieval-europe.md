# Late Medieval Europe — The Long Crisis

**Goal:** Narrative for late medieval Europe, 1300–1500 CE, the two-century crisis between the High Middle Ages and the early modern world. The continent that came out of 1500 was unrecognizable from the one that walked into 1300: a third of the population dead from plague, the universal Church split between rival popes and then humiliated by reform councils, the manorial labor system broken, the longbow and the cannon ending the chivalric military order, the Italian city-states reinventing banking and painting and architecture, Joan of Arc burned at nineteen, Constantinople fallen to Ottoman cannons, Granada surrendered after 781 years of Reconquista, Columbus across the Atlantic, da Gama around Africa, the printing press multiplying texts by an order of magnitude, and the doctrinal seeds of the Reformation already planted by Wycliffe and Hus a century before Luther.

This is the period historians used to call the Waning of the Middle Ages, after the 1919 Dutch classic by Johan Huizinga. The label has fallen out of fashion because it implies decline, and the late Middle Ages were also the cradle of the Renaissance and the Age of Discovery. But "long crisis" remains accurate. People who lived through 1315–1500 lived through a sequence of catastrophes — climate cooling, famine, plague, war, schism, pogrom, peasant revolt, financial collapse — that has no real parallel in the rest of European history before the twentieth century.

A note on names and pronunciation: late medieval Europe is the period when most of the spellings the reader recognizes today were beginning to stabilize. Names will be given in their most familiar English form. Pronunciations are provided for genuinely unfamiliar names. Dates are CE.

A note on "first" claims: the late Middle Ages produce a lot of "firsts" because the surviving documentation thickens dramatically. Almost every "first" claim should be read as "first surviving evidence of." The European witch craze, for example, has many medieval prequels in trial records that have not survived. Print culture has prequels in Korea and China. Oil painting has prequels in earlier Flemish workshops. Where the chapter says "first," it almost always means "first that we can prove."

A note on myths to puncture, briefly: the Black Death was not called the Black Death by anyone who lived through it. Medieval people did not believe the earth was flat; every educated person knew it was a sphere, and the cosmology of Dante's *Divine Comedy* assumes it. The Renaissance was not a sudden break with the Middle Ages; modern scholars see it as a continuation of late-medieval intellectual currents. Joan of Arc was not a peasant in the modern sense — her family were prosperous tenants, not landless laborers. Several other myths are addressed inline as they come up.

# Chapter 1 — The Great Famine and the End of the Boom

The story of late medieval Europe begins not with plague or war but with the weather.

For three centuries before 1300, the climate of Europe was unusually warm. Vineyards grew in southern England. Norse colonists farmed cereals on Greenland (Viking Age Ch 8). Glaciers in the Alps retreated to their highest stands. The growing season lengthened by perhaps two weeks at the latitude of Paris. Agricultural yields rose, the population doubled, and the high medieval boom of cathedrals and universities and Crusades was financed in significant part by the surplus calories of this warm spell. Climate historians call it the **Medieval Warm Period** or, more recently, the **Medieval Climate Anomaly**.

Around 1300 — give or take a few decades — the Medieval Warm Period ended. European summers cooled by roughly 1°C on average, the growing season shortened, glaciers began to advance, and the run of warm wet weather that had supported the high medieval expansion gave way to a colder, more variable, more dangerous regime. The cooling pulse that began around 1300 and persisted for the next five centuries is called the **Little Ice Age** in modern climate history. It was not an ice age in the geological sense. It was a cooling of perhaps 1–2°C in northern hemisphere summers, with much higher year-to-year variability — exactly the kind of climate that breaks medieval agriculture.

Why did the cooling happen? The current scholarly reading involves three overlapping causes: a clustering of major volcanic eruptions in the late thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries (whose sulfate aerosols cooled the upper atmosphere), a dip in solar output called the **Wolf Minimum** between roughly 1280 and 1350, and changes in ocean circulation patterns that are still being worked out. The relative weight of each cause is contested in the climate science literature. What is not contested is the shift itself, which shows up clearly in tree-ring records, glacial moraines, and pollen sequences from across Europe.

The relevant point for human history is that medieval Europe in 1300 was a population pushed up against its agricultural limits. Centuries of high-medieval warmth had supported a population growth that required virtually every plowable acre to be plowed. Marginal land that should never have been farmed — too high, too cold, too thin-soiled — had been brought into cultivation because the warmth made it temporarily viable. When the warmth went away, the marginal land went away with it, and a population that had grown to depend on that marginal yield was suddenly in trouble.

It took fifteen years for the trouble to become catastrophe.

---

The catastrophe arrived in the form of three consecutive failed harvests.

In the spring of 1315, it began to rain across northern Europe. It rained through the planting season. It rained through summer. It rained through what should have been the harvest. Grain rotted in the fields. Hay could not be cured. Fuel could not be dried. The 1315 harvest was the worst on record in living memory. The 1316 harvest was worse: cooler, wetter, even less productive. The 1317 harvest was somewhat better but still inadequate to refill the empty granaries. The cumulative effect across northern Europe — the British Isles, the Low Countries, northern France, the Holy Roman Empire, the western Polish plain — was the **Great Famine** of 1315–1317.

Mortality estimates vary by region, but a credible round figure is that **10% of northern Europe's population** died in those three years from starvation, malnutrition, and the diseases that prey on malnourished bodies. In some northern German cities the death toll appears to have been higher. Cannibalism was reported in chronicles and probably occurred. Parents abandoned children they could not feed. Bands of refugees roamed the countryside, broke into towns to demand bread, and were repulsed by armed citizens. The English wool trade collapsed. The Flemish cloth industry contracted sharply. The price of bread tripled and then tripled again in some cities.

The Great Famine was the largest demographic shock to Europe between the Plague of Justinian in the sixth century and the Black Death thirty years later. It is also one of the most well-documented famines in pre-modern history, partly because the late medieval European bureaucracy had finally developed the literate institutions — town chronicles, manorial accounts, royal correspondence — capable of recording it.

What the Great Famine demonstrated was that the high medieval expansion had peaked. The land could not support the population that had grown to depend on it. Even before plague and war began their work, late medieval Europe was demographically vulnerable in a way that high medieval Europe had not been.

A second blow followed almost immediately. In 1319, a cattle disease — probably **rinderpest** (RIN-der-pest, a viral disease of cattle, related to measles, that produces near-100% mortality in unvaccinated herds) — swept through European herds. The **Great Bovine Pestilence** of 1319–1320 killed perhaps 60% of European cattle and oxen. For an agricultural system that depended on cattle for milk, meat, leather, manure, and (above all) traction power for plowing, the bovine pestilence was a second body-blow. Fields that survived the human famine could not be plowed because the oxen were dead. The agricultural retreat continued through the 1320s and 1330s.

By 1340, on the eve of the Hundred Years War, late medieval Europe had spent a generation contracting. It was a continent already in trouble before plague and war made everything worse.

---

It is worth pausing on what late medieval Europe in 1300 actually looked like, because the chapters ahead will assume the reader can picture it.

The continent had perhaps 75–85 million people, depending on which scholarly estimate you prefer. (For comparison: the Americas in the same period had perhaps 50–60 million; China around 100 million; the Islamic world from Spain to Central Asia perhaps 50 million.) The largest cities were small by modern standards. **Paris** was the biggest at perhaps 200,000 people. **Venice**, **Genoa**, **Florence**, and **Milan** in northern Italy had populations around 100,000 each. **London** was a provincial town of about 80,000. **Constantinople** (Byzantine Empire Ch 10) had collapsed from its tenth-century peak of 400,000 to about 100,000. **Cordoba** and **Seville** in Muslim and recently-Christian Spain were larger than most northern European cities. The population was overwhelmingly rural. Perhaps 90% of Europeans worked the land, lived in villages of 200–500 people, and rarely traveled more than 20 miles from their birthplace in their lifetimes.

The political map was a patchwork. **France** under the **Capetian** dynasty was the largest centralized monarchy, with a population of perhaps 17 million and a sophisticated royal bureaucracy. **England** under the **Plantagenets** was smaller (5 million people) but more administratively unified, with a parliament that already met regularly and a common-law system already a century old. The **Holy Roman Empire** — the loose German-Italian federation that traced its origin to Charlemagne's coronation in 800 (early-medieval Europe) — was politically fragmented, with the emperor elected by seven prince-electors and ruling more in name than in fact. **Italy** was a checkerboard of independent city-states (north and center), the **Papal States** (central), and the **Kingdom of Naples** (south). **Iberia** was four kingdoms — Castile, Aragon, Portugal, Navarre — plus the surviving Muslim emirate of Granada. **Eastern Europe** was a frontier zone of Polish, Hungarian, Lithuanian, and Bohemian kingdoms expanding into Slavic lands. The **Byzantine Empire** held a few footholds in Greece and around the Sea of Marmara (Byzantine Empire Ch 10). **Scandinavia** was a sparsely populated northern world of fishing, timber, and herring trade, increasingly tied to the **Hanseatic League** of north German trading cities.

The universal institutions were the **Catholic Church**, with the papacy in Rome (until 1309) as its administrative head, and the **Latin language**, used by every educated person from Iceland to Sicily. The Church owned roughly a third of the cultivable land in most of western Europe. Almost every village had a parish priest. Every town of any size had a bishop. The seven sacraments — baptism, confirmation, the Eucharist, confession, marriage, holy orders, last rites — structured the life of essentially every Christian European from cradle to grave. The Church taxed (the **tithe**, one-tenth of agricultural income), educated (every cathedral had a school, every university had begun as one), regulated marriage and inheritance, ran most charity, owned most hospitals, sheltered the orphans, and provided the only realistic vertical mobility ladder open to a peasant boy of unusual intelligence.

This is the baseline against which everything in the next 200 years should be measured. The continent that walked into the fourteenth century was Catholic, agricultural, and demographically taut. The continent that walked out of the fifteenth century was different in every one of those dimensions.

---

A few high medieval names show up briefly in this chapter and the next, and the reader should know who they were.

**Dante Alighieri** (1265–1321), the Florentine poet, finished his **Divine Comedy** around 1320 — an epic vernacular poem in Tuscan Italian, structured as a guided tour through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise. The Divine Comedy is the founding masterpiece of Italian literature, the most-read non-Biblical text of late medieval Europe, and the best single demonstration that medieval Europeans knew the earth was a sphere. Dante's universe is built on the Aristotelian-Ptolemaic cosmology that every educated person took for granted: a spherical earth at the center of nested celestial spheres, with Hell underneath Jerusalem, Purgatory on a mountain in the southern hemisphere ocean, and the heavens arrayed above in concentric rotating shells. The flat-earth myth as applied to medieval people is a nineteenth-century invention. Dante's poem is one of many proofs.

**Petrarch** (1304–1374), Francesco Petrarca in Italian, is conventionally identified as the first Renaissance humanist — a label so worn that it can obscure how genuinely strange Petrarch's project was. He spent forty years rediscovering classical Latin manuscripts in monastery libraries (Cicero's lost letters in 1345 was his most famous find), writing love sonnets in Italian to a married woman named Laura, climbing **Mont Ventoux** in Provence in 1336 in what is conventionally called the first self-conscious aesthetic appreciation of nature in European literature, and inventing the cult of the individual genius that defined Renaissance humanism for the next two centuries. Petrarch lived through the Black Death (Laura died in it) and through the Avignon Papacy (he hated it, calling Avignon the "Babylon of the West"). He is the bridge between high medieval scholasticism and what would later be called Renaissance humanism, and we will see his project develop across multiple chapters ahead.

The **Knights Templar**, a military religious order founded in the early twelfth century to protect Crusader pilgrims, were extinguished in this period in spectacularly violent fashion. King **Philip IV** of France, deeply in debt to the Templars and threatened by their independent power, arrested every Templar in his kingdom on Friday October 13, 1307, extracted confessions of heresy and sodomy and idol worship under torture, and pressured the Avignon pope **Clement V** to suppress the order. The Templar grand master **Jacques de Molay** was burned at the stake in 1314, reportedly cursing Philip and Clement from the pyre — both men died within a year. The Templar suppression is the case where the late medieval state demonstrated, in a single coordinated action across an entire kingdom, how powerful royal bureaucracies had become and how vulnerable the universal church was to coordinated secular pressure. It rehearses several of the dynamics — the king-pope confrontation, the use of legal-judicial machinery to destroy political enemies, the choreography of confession and exemplary execution — that the next two centuries would replay in different keys.

The Templar story also gave us **Friday the 13th** as a date of bad luck — the most successful piece of late-medieval superstition still in circulation. (This is one of the few popular Templar legends that probably has a real medieval source.)

Cross-civ check: in 1300, when Europe was tipping into the Little Ice Age, the rest of Eurasia was also in flux. The **Mongol Empire** (Mongol Empire Ch 7) had broken into four khanates, with the **Yuan dynasty** ruling China under Kublai's successors and the **Ilkhanate** ruling Iran. Both the Yuan and the Ilkhanate would collapse before mid-century — the Yuan to the **Ming** founding in 1368 (right in the middle of the Hundred Years War), the Ilkhanate to internal fragmentation in 1335 (which produced the political vacuum the Safavids would eventually fill — Safavid Persia Ch 1). In **India**, the Delhi Sultanate was at its territorial peak under **Alauddin Khalji**, projecting power south into the Deccan for the first time. In **West Africa**, the **Mali Empire** under its founder Sundiata's successors was beginning the trans-Saharan gold trade that would shortly produce the most spectacular royal pilgrimage of the medieval world (Mali Empire Ch 4). In the Americas, the **Aztec** were a small mercenary tribe still wandering the Valley of Mexico — they would not found Tenochtitlan until 1325 (Aztec Empire Ch 1) — and the **Inca** were a small mountain polity around Cuzco that would not begin its imperial expansion until 1438 (Inca Empire Ch 2). The early fourteenth century is a hinge moment in Eurasian history, and the European crisis is one node in a wider system of climate, migration, and disease pressures.

It is also worth flagging, before the next chapter takes over, that the climate stress of 1300 was a Eurasian phenomenon. The same Little Ice Age cooling that produced European famine appears to have stressed Mongol pastoralism on the Eurasian steppe, contributing to the political instability that would eventually break the empire and to the dislocations along the Silk Road that would ferry the plague west.

The plague is the next chapter.

# Chapter 2 — The Black Death

In October 1347, twelve Genoese trading galleys put in at the Sicilian port of **Messina**. The ships had come from **Caffa** (KAH-fah, the modern Crimean city of **Feodosiya**), the major Genoese trading post on the Black Sea. The crews were dying. By the time the ships docked, most of the rowers were dead or dying. Their bodies were covered in painful black swellings — **buboes** (BYOO-bohs) — at the lymph nodes of the neck, armpit, and groin. They coughed blood. Their fingers and toes turned black. They died within days of showing symptoms.

The Messinese harbor authorities, recognizing the symptoms of a plague they had heard rumors about from the east, ordered the ships back out to sea. It was already too late. The disease had jumped from rats to fleas to humans on the docks within hours of landing. Within a week, Messina was beginning to die. Within a month, the disease had spread to mainland Sicily and across the Strait of Messina to Calabria. By spring 1348, it had reached **Pisa**, **Genoa**, **Marseille**, **Venice**, **Florence**, and (via overland trade routes) the cities of central France. By summer 1348, **Paris**. By autumn 1348, **London**. By 1350, **Norway**, **Sweden**, **Iceland**, and **Russia**. In four years it killed roughly **a third of Europe's population**.

This is the **Black Death**, the worst demographic catastrophe in recorded European history.

---

A point of nomenclature first. The phrase "Black Death" is **not what contemporaries called it**. Fourteenth-century European writers called it the **Pestilence**, the **Great Mortality** (Latin *magna mortalitas*), the **Plague**, or sometimes "the death." The earliest known use of the phrase "Black Death" in something close to its modern sense is from a sixteenth-century Latin poem about the 1347–1351 outbreak, but the phrase did not become common in popular history until the nineteenth century, when Scandinavian and German historians popularized it. The "black" probably referred to the metaphor of dread or to the gangrenous black extremities that characterize a minority of plague cases, not to the ordinary appearance of the disease. Most Black Death victims had buboes, fevers, and coughing blood, but were not literally black.

Calling the disease the Black Death is therefore a modern convention. It is so deeply embedded in popular history that abandoning it now is impossible, but the reader should know that it is not how anyone in 1347 talked about what was happening to them.

The disease itself has been definitively identified by modern paleogenomics as **Yersinia pestis** (yer-SIN-ee-ah PES-tiss), the bacterium responsible for **bubonic plague**. DNA from Yersinia pestis has been recovered from Black Death-era mass graves at sites including the East Smithfield cemetery in London (excavated in the 1980s), the Saint-Laurent-de-la-Cabrerisse cemetery in southern France, and dozens of other sites across Europe and the Mediterranean. The 2010s identification of Y. pestis at multiple Black Death sites resolved a long debate among historians (some had argued for anthrax, hemorrhagic fever, or smallpox) by direct genetic evidence. The disease is the same one that causes plague outbreaks today — it has not disappeared, it is treatable with modern antibiotics, and there are still 1,000–2,000 cases per year worldwide, with mortality rates of perhaps 10% in treated cases and 60–90% if untreated.

The vector cycle is well understood. Yersinia pestis lives in a complex ecology of rodents and fleas. The bacterium colonizes the gut of certain flea species (notably **Xenopsylla cheopis**, the **oriental rat flea**), which feed on rodents (notably **Rattus rattus**, the **black rat**). Infected fleas regurgitate bacteria into the rodent's bloodstream when they bite; the rodent dies; the now-hungry fleas jump to the next available warm body, which can be a human. Once a human is infected, the bacterium spreads through the lymphatic system, producing the buboes that gave the disease its medieval name. In a minority of cases, the bacterium reaches the lungs, causing **pneumonic plague**, which can spread directly human-to-human through coughing without any flea intermediary. Pneumonic plague is much more lethal — close to 100% mortality without antibiotics — but spreads less rapidly than the flea-mediated bubonic form, since it requires close personal contact.

The arrival of plague in Europe in 1347 was not the first European plague pandemic. The **Plague of Justinian** in 541 CE (Byzantine Empire Ch 2 mentions it) had been the same disease, hit the eastern Mediterranean about as hard, and continued in waves for two centuries before disappearing for reasons that are still unclear. There are also recent suggestions of plague outbreaks in the Roman world, in Bronze Age Europe, and even in late Neolithic communities — Yersinia pestis DNA has been recovered from skeletons going back several thousand years. What was unprecedented about the 1347 outbreak was the speed and density of its spread, made possible by the integrated trade network that linked the Mediterranean, the Black Sea, and the overland Silk Road.

---

The route into Europe ran through the Mongol-controlled steppes.

The current scholarly reading is that Yersinia pestis was endemic in marmot and ground-squirrel populations in **Central Asia** — probably in what is now Kyrgyzstan, where mass graves at the Issyk-Kul lake region show plague DNA dated to 1338–1339, several years before the European outbreak. From its endemic reservoir, the disease appears to have escaped into the broader rodent populations of the Mongol-controlled trade routes during the political and ecological turbulence of the early fourteenth century — possibly driven by climate stress on steppe rodent populations, possibly by the breakdown of Mongol-imposed peace (the **Pax Mongolica**, Mongol Empire Ch 8) that had previously kept caravan traffic moving through endemic plague zones without incident.

By the early 1340s the disease was spreading along the **Silk Road** westward. It hit the Mongol Khanate of the **Golden Horde** in southern Russia by 1346. In that year, the Golden Horde khan **Jani Beg** was besieging the Genoese trading colony at **Caffa** when plague broke out in his army. According to a single contemporary account by the Italian notary **Gabriele de' Mussi** (writing several years later, possibly without firsthand knowledge), Jani Beg ordered the bodies of his plague-dead soldiers catapulted over the walls into the besieged city — one of the first attested instances of biological warfare. Whether Mussi's account is literally accurate is debated, but the broader story is consistent with the evidence: the plague reached Caffa, the Genoese garrison and merchants caught it, and when the survivors fled westward by ship in 1347 they carried it with them to Constantinople, the Aegean, and the western Mediterranean.

From Caffa onward the route is well-attested. **Constantinople** caught it in summer 1347 — the Byzantine emperor **John VI Kantakouzenos** lost his thirteen-year-old son to it and wrote a clinical account of the symptoms (Byzantine Empire Ch 10). **Messina** in October 1347. **Genoa** and **Venice** by January 1348. **Marseille** by January or February. **Pisa** by March. **Florence** by April. **Paris** by July or August. **London** by autumn. **Norway** in 1349 (carried, according to local tradition, by a single ship that drifted into Bergen with all hands dead). **Iceland** and **Greenland** later. The Norse settlements on Greenland, already in trouble from the Little Ice Age, faded from contact entirely sometime in the fifteenth century — plague is one of several proposed causes (Viking Age Ch 8).

The cumulative European death toll between 1347 and 1351 is estimated by modern historians at roughly **30–50% of the population**, with **a third dead** as the conventional round number. Some regions did much worse. **Florence** lost perhaps 60% of its 100,000 inhabitants; the Florentine writer **Giovanni Boccaccio** lost his stepmother and most of his friends, and used the plague as the framing device for his **Decameron** (deh-KAH-meh-ron) — a hundred bawdy, satirical, and tender stories told by ten young Florentines fleeing the city for a country villa, finished around 1353 and instantly recognized as the founding masterpiece of Italian prose. **Avignon** lost perhaps half its population. Some smaller villages were entirely depopulated. Other regions did somewhat better — **Milan**, partly because it imposed early quarantine measures, lost perhaps 15% — but no significant region escaped untouched.

The plague returned. The 1347–1351 outbreak was the first wave. There were major recurrences in 1361–1362 (the **Pestis Secunda**, "Second Plague," which killed proportionally more children because the survivors of the first wave had partial immunity), 1369, 1374, 1390, and roughly every decade or two for the next three centuries. The cumulative effect kept the European population well below pre-plague levels for nearly two centuries — Europe did not regain its 1340 population until around 1500. Whole villages disappeared. Marginal land returned to forest. The agricultural retreat of the Great Famine became a permanent rural depopulation across much of the continent.

---

The social consequences of the Black Death broke the high medieval order in ways no single act of policy or rebellion could have done.

The most immediate consequence was a **labor shortage**. With a third of the workforce dead, the surviving peasantry suddenly held an enormous bargaining advantage. Wages roughly doubled across western Europe in the decades after 1351 — for agricultural workers, masons, carpenters, weavers, and most other manual trades. Land rents fell. Peasants who had been bound to their manorial lords by hereditary serfdom began to walk away, knowing that any landlord short of labor would take them on. The seigneurial system — under which a peasant family worked the lord's land in exchange for the right to work their own subsistence plot — began to dissolve in western Europe over the next century, though it would persist much longer in the east.

Governments tried to legislate the labor shortage away. In England, the **Statute of Labourers** of 1351 fixed wages at pre-plague levels and criminalized peasants who demanded more or moved between manors. The French crown issued similar ordinances. The Iberian kingdoms passed sumptuary laws restricting what newly-wealthy peasants could wear. Almost everywhere, these laws failed. The economic logic was overwhelming. By 1400, English real wages were the highest they had been for centuries and would be again until perhaps 1700. The peasantry of late medieval England was — for a couple of generations after 1351 — better fed, better clothed, and better housed than at any point before the Industrial Revolution.

This is a paradox worth pausing on. The Black Death killed a third of the continent. The survivors were materially better off. The mechanism is straightforward — fewer people splitting the same agricultural surplus, plus a bargaining advantage in labor markets — but the moral implications are uncomfortable. The standard of living of the medieval European peasantry depended, in significant part, on the demographic catastrophe of the Black Death. This is the same kind of grim irony the next chapter will return to in connection with the Hundred Years War.

The second major consequence was **antisemitic violence** on a scale not previously seen in European history.

The plague was not understood by contemporaries as a bacterial disease (no one understood bacteria until the nineteenth century). It was understood, depending on the audience, as divine punishment for sin, as the result of a corrupt **miasma** (a noxious vapor in the air), or — increasingly, as the death toll mounted — as the work of human enemies who had poisoned the wells. The favored scapegoat was the Jewish community. Jewish communities in much of western and central Europe had been targets of intermittent persecution since the First Crusade (Islamic Golden Age Ch 9), but the Black Death produced an acceleration of pogrom violence that destroyed many of the Rhineland Jewish communities altogether.

The pattern was depressingly consistent: a city would catch the plague, mortality would rise, rumors would spread that local Jews had poisoned the wells, a mob would form, the city authorities would either stand aside or actively participate, and the Jewish community would be murdered or expelled. Strasbourg burned 900 Jews alive on Saint Valentine's Day, February 14, 1349 — before the plague had even reached the city. Mainz, Cologne, Worms, Frankfurt, Speyer, and Basel had their Jewish communities destroyed or expelled within months. **Pope Clement VI**, to his credit, issued papal bulls in 1348 condemning the pogroms, pointing out that Jews were dying of the plague in equal numbers to Christians and could not therefore be the cause, and ordering bishops to protect Jewish communities. The bulls had limited effect outside the Papal States.

The 1348–1349 pogroms permanently shifted the Jewish demographic geography of Europe. Many surviving Jewish communities migrated eastward — to **Poland**, **Lithuania**, **Bohemia**, and **Hungary**, where the Polish king **Casimir III** (1333–1370) and other eastern rulers offered relative protection in exchange for the economic services of Jewish merchants and artisans. The center of Ashkenazi Jewish demographic gravity moved from the Rhineland to the eastern European plains over the next century, where it would remain until the twentieth century. The Black Death pogroms are one of the underexamined hinge moments of European Jewish history.

---

Two other consequences of the plague are worth noting before this chapter closes.

The first is the **Flagellant** movement. In 1348 and 1349, bands of penitents — sometimes thousands strong — moved through central European towns whipping themselves bloody in public processions, arguing that only voluntary self-mortification could appease God's anger and end the pestilence. The Flagellants were a legitimately popular religious movement, drawing recruits from townsmen and peasants, with their own rituals and their own theology of sin and atonement. They were also, by the late 1340s, well outside the Church's institutional control. **Pope Clement VI** condemned the movement in October 1349, and the bishops and secular authorities suppressed it over the next year, but the Flagellant movement is a useful early sign of how late-medieval Christianity was developing forms of popular piety that the institutional Church could no longer easily channel. The Reformation in the sixteenth century is the eventual consequence; movements like the Flagellants are early symptoms.

The second is what historians sometimes call the **late medieval cult of death**. After 1351, European art and culture became preoccupied with mortality in ways earlier medieval art had not been. The genre of the **danse macabre** ("dance of death") — paintings and woodcuts showing skeletal Death dancing with people from every social rank, from pope to peasant — appeared in the late fourteenth and fifteenth centuries and became a major theme of late medieval Europe. **Memento mori** ("remember you must die") devotional images, transi tombs (showing the deceased as a decayed corpse rather than the idealized living figure), the *Ars Moriendi* (the "Art of Dying," a fifteenth-century devotional manual on how to die well), the **Triumph of Death** in Italian fresco painting — all of this is the cultural sediment of a society that had watched a third of itself die in four years and watched plague return generation after generation. People who lived through the Black Death and its echoes did not need to be reminded of mortality in the way modern Western readers do, but they did need to make sense of it. Late-medieval death culture is the result.

---

Cross-civ check: the Black Death was not just a European event. The Yersinia pestis pandemic that originated on the central Asian steppe spread across the entire Eurasian world system. **Egypt** under the Mamluks lost perhaps 30–40% of its population. **Syria** and the Levant were devastated. **Constantinople** lost perhaps half its remnant population. **Anatolia** under the rising Ottoman beyliks (Ottoman Empire Ch 1) was hit hard, though the plague's effect on Ottoman expansion is contested in the literature. **China** had experienced major plague-like epidemics in the 1330s and 1340s — possibly though not certainly the same disease — that contributed to the political instability that brought down the **Yuan Dynasty** in 1368 (Mongol Empire Ch 6, Tang-Song China context). The **Islamic world** generated its own response literature: the Andalusi physician **Ibn al-Khatib** wrote a 1349 treatise arguing for direct human-to-human contagion against the prevailing miasma theory — one of the most modern-sounding pieces of medieval medical thinking on either side of the Mediterranean.

In **West Africa**, the **Mali Empire** under **Mansa Musa**'s successor **Mansa Sulayman** appears not to have been affected (the trans-Saharan trade routes seem not to have been efficient plague vectors, possibly because of the desert's role as a biological filter). In the **Americas**, where Eurasian diseases had not yet reached, the populations of the Aztec frontier and the contemporary Andean kingdoms (Andean Kingdoms Ch 8, Middle Horizon Empires Ch 8) were untouched — they would experience their own demographic catastrophe a century and a half later, when Columbus and Cortés arrived with smallpox and measles. The asymmetric immunological history of Eurasia and the Americas is one of the structural facts of the centuries that followed.

The plague is the single largest event in late medieval European history. Everything in the chapters ahead — the Hundred Years War, the schism, the peasant revolts, the Reformation, the Renaissance, the Age of Discovery — happens in a Europe that has just been culled, and is being culled again every decade or two, and is collectively trying to make sense of what that means.

The Hundred Years War is next.

# Chapter 3 — The Hundred Years War

In the spring of 1337, **Edward III** of England — a tall, handsome, unusually competent young king of twenty-four — formally claimed the throne of France through his mother **Isabella**, sister of the recently-extinguished Capetian line. Within months, an English army had landed in Flanders. Within a decade, English longbowmen had destroyed the flower of French chivalric cavalry at **Crécy**. Within a century, an English king named Henry V had been recognized as heir to the French crown, French civil war had reduced the country to three rival regencies, a teenage girl claiming divine voices had personally led the French army to break the siege of Orléans, and French artillery had ended the war by killing the last English commander on the field at **Castillon**. The whole sequence is conventionally called the **Hundred Years War** — though the name was coined in the nineteenth century, contemporaries did not use it, and the actual fighting spanned 116 years (1337–1453) with several long pauses.

It is the longest sustained inter-state conflict in European history. It is also the war that broke the high medieval military order, shifted the balance of power between cavalry and infantry, professionalized European warfare, and produced the early forms of national identity in both England and France.

---

The dynastic claim was thin.

The Capetian dynasty, which had ruled France since **Hugh Capet** in 987, ran out of male heirs in 1328 with the death of **Charles IV**, the last of the three sons of **Philip IV** (the Templar-suppressing king from Chapter 1). The French succession question became: who inherits when the direct male line dies out? The candidates were Philip IV's nephew **Philip of Valois** (through Philip IV's brother), and Philip IV's grandson **Edward III** of England (through Philip IV's daughter Isabella, who had married Edward II of England). The French nobility, faced with a choice between a French nephew and an English grandson, chose the nephew. Philip of Valois became **Philip VI** in 1328, founding the Valois dynasty that would rule France until 1589. Edward III, then a teenager, accepted the decision at the time. He paid homage to Philip VI for his French duchy of **Aquitaine** in 1329.

By 1337, Edward had changed his mind. The reasons were complex — friction over Aquitaine, French support for the Scots (who were in chronic war with England), French confiscation of Aquitaine in 1337, English reliance on Flemish wool exports that Philip VI was trying to control — but the legal handle was the dynastic claim. Edward unrenounced his earlier homage, declared himself the rightful king of France, quartered the French royal arms with the English on his coat of arms, and went to war.

The French sovereignty claim was the surface issue. The deeper issue was that England had held French territory since the Norman Conquest of 1066 (high-medieval Europe context — William the Conqueror was a French-speaking duke) and the Plantagenet acquisition of Aquitaine through Henry II's marriage to **Eleanor of Aquitaine** in 1152. By the early fourteenth century, the English king ruled a substantial duchy in southwestern France that he held in technical homage to the French crown — a structurally unstable arrangement that produced friction every generation. The Hundred Years War can be understood as the long process by which this anomaly was resolved (badly, expensively, with enormous loss of life on both sides) in favor of a clean separation between an English nation north of the Channel and a French nation south of it.

---

The war happened in three big phases, separated by long pauses.

**The first phase (1337–1360)** was the period of English military shock. Edward III's England had two military advantages the French lacked. The first was the **English longbow**, a six-foot yew-staved bow that could throw a heavy arrow 250 yards (about 230 meters) and pierce most contemporary armor at 100 yards. The longbow was not new — it had been developed in Wales over the previous century — but its systematic deployment as the core of English infantry tactics was a fourteenth-century innovation. Trained from childhood, a competent English longbowman could shoot 10–12 aimed arrows per minute. A force of 5,000 longbowmen could put 50,000 to 60,000 arrows per minute into the air. The cumulative effect on advancing cavalry was devastating: horses panicked, knights were thrown from their saddles, the disordered survivors arrived at the English lines in scattered remnants where dismounted men-at-arms finished them off.

The second English advantage was **dismounted men-at-arms** fighting in disciplined infantry formations rather than charging individually as mounted knights. The English had learned this approach in their long border wars with the Scots; they imported it to France, deploying the men-at-arms behind sharpened stakes and pits to break the French cavalry charge that the longbow had already disordered.

The two innovations together — longbow plus dismounted men-at-arms — were demonstrated catastrophically at **Crécy** on August 26, 1346. A French chivalric host of perhaps 30,000, led by Philip VI personally, charged a smaller English force commanded by Edward III and his sixteen-year-old son Edward of Woodstock (later called the **Black Prince** for his dark armor). The French sent in their **Genoese crossbowmen** first; the longbows outranged and outshot them, breaking their formation. The French knights, charging through their own retreating crossbowmen, ran into the longbow storm in waves. Estimates of French casualties at Crécy run to 4,000–10,000, including 1,500 nobles and the king of Bohemia (who was blind and asked his knights to lead him into the fight, reportedly so he could strike one blow with his sword before dying). English casualties were perhaps 200. The chivalric warfare of the high Middle Ages — armored mounted knight against armored mounted knight, with battlefield outcomes determined by individual valor and the social rank of the combatants — had been demolished in a single afternoon by disciplined infantry firepower.

Edward followed up Crécy by besieging **Calais** for eleven months. The port surrendered in August 1347 (just as the Black Death was beginning to spread west from Sicily). England held Calais as a continental beachhead until 1558.

The Black Death interrupted the war from 1348 through the early 1350s. When it resumed, the Black Prince repeated his father's victory at **Poitiers** on September 19, 1356, where an English-Gascon army a third the size of the French force not only won but captured the French king **John II** alive. With their king prisoner in London (where he was treated as a guest of honor at Edward's court), the French agreed to the **Treaty of Brétigny** in May 1360 — ceding most of southwestern France to England in exchange for Edward renouncing his French throne claim and releasing John. It looked like a permanent settlement. It lasted nine years.

---

**The second phase (1369–1389)** was the French recovery under **Charles V the Wise** and his brilliant general **Bertrand du Guesclin** (du-geh-KLAN). Charles V (king from 1364) was a sickly, scholarly man who hated battle and favored siege warfare, harassment, and the avoidance of pitched engagements. Du Guesclin was a Breton knight of dubious appearance (chronicle accounts describe him as squat and ugly) and unusually shrewd tactical sense. Together, they recovered most of what France had lost at Brétigny over a long, grinding decade of small-unit warfare that never delivered a decisive battle but slowly bled English territory back to French control. By 1380, when both Charles V and du Guesclin died, England held only a coastal strip of Aquitaine and Calais.

The 1370s and 1380s were a period of relative quiet, with both sides distracted by other things. England was distracted by the English Peasants' Revolt of 1381 (Chapter 6) and the political instability of **Richard II**'s minority and erratic adult reign. France was distracted by the death of Charles V and the long minority of his son **Charles VI**, who would eventually descend into chronic mental illness and reduce the country to civil war between rival regency factions.

The French civil war of the early fifteenth century is essential context for the third phase of the Hundred Years War, and it deserves a brief explanation. **Charles VI** of France, who became king as a child in 1380 and did not produce a stable adult reign, suffered from periodic episodes of severe mental illness beginning in 1392. He believed at various points that he was made of glass, that his name was George, that his courtiers were trying to murder him. During his lucid periods he ruled normally; during his mental episodes the regency was contested between two rival noble factions. The **Burgundian** faction, led by Duke **John the Fearless** of Burgundy, was based in the powerful composite state of Burgundy (which included most of the modern Low Countries — Flanders, Brabant, Hainaut). The **Armagnac** faction, led by the Count of Armagnac and rallying around the Dauphin **Charles** (the future Charles VII), represented the southern French nobility loyal to the Valois crown. From 1407 (when John the Fearless had Louis of Orléans assassinated in a Paris street) through 1435 (when Burgundy switched sides), France was wracked by an internal war that ran in parallel with the English invasion. The English gains of the third phase would have been impossible without Burgundian collaboration.

---

**The third phase (1415–1453)** opened with **Henry V** of England, who has had very good Shakespearean press and was indeed a remarkably effective king and battlefield commander. Henry resumed his great-grandfather's claim, invaded Normandy in 1415, and won the most lopsided victory of the entire war at **Agincourt** (AH-zhin-coor or AH-jin-court) on October 25, 1415 — Saint Crispin's Day, the date Shakespeare made famous in *Henry V*.

Agincourt was a re-run of Crécy and Poitiers with one extra horror. Henry's army of perhaps 6,000 (most of them longbowmen) faced a French force of perhaps 20,000–30,000 in a narrow muddy field between two woods. The French cavalry charged through the mud — knee-deep in some places after recent rain — and were shot to pieces by the longbows before reaching the English line. The dismounted French men-at-arms, advancing in heavy plate armor through the same mud, were exhausted before they arrived; those who reached the English line were quickly killed or taken prisoner. The chronicles report several thousand French dead and roughly 1,500–2,000 prisoners taken.

What made Agincourt particularly grim was Henry V's order, partway through the battle, to kill the prisoners — apparently because he feared a French counter-attack and could not spare men to guard them. English men-at-arms reportedly refused the order (they would lose ransom money if the prisoners died) and Henry sent his own archers to do it. Several hundred to perhaps 2,000 captured French nobles were killed in cold blood. The episode is documented in multiple contemporary chronicles. It was controversial at the time and remains so among historians; Shakespeare omits it. It is a useful corrective to the romantic image of late-medieval warfare — Henry V was an excellent king by the standards of his time and was also capable of mass killing of unarmed prisoners when military necessity (or his judgment of military necessity) demanded.

Agincourt opened the way for Henry's systematic conquest of Normandy over the next four years. The **Treaty of Troyes** in May 1420, signed in the wake of further English military success and exploiting Charles VI's incapacity and Burgundian collaboration, was the high-water mark of English continental ambition: Henry married Charles VI's daughter **Catherine of Valois**, was recognized as heir to the French throne, and was effectively appointed regent for the disinherited Dauphin Charles. If Henry had lived, the personal union of England and France might have happened in 1422.

He did not live. Henry died of dysentery in August 1422 at the age of thirty-five, two months before his father-in-law Charles VI. His son **Henry VI** was nine months old. Charles VI's son the Dauphin, now formally disinherited but still claiming the French throne, was nineteen, holed up in a small court at Bourges south of the Loire that contemporaries jokingly called the "Kingdom of Bourges." It was the lowest point French royal authority had reached since Hugh Capet 435 years earlier.

---

This is where Joan of Arc walks into the story.

**Joan of Arc** — **Jeanne d'Arc** in French, **Jehanne** in her own time — was born around 1412 in **Domrémy** (dom-reh-MEE), a village on the Lorraine-Champagne frontier. Her family were prosperous tenants — not landless peasants, as the popular image has it, but moderately well-off villagers with a substantial farmhouse and several head of livestock. She was illiterate, devout, unusually willful, and from the age of about thirteen began to hear what she described as the voices of Saints **Catherine of Alexandria**, **Margaret of Antioch**, and the Archangel **Michael** — telling her, eventually, that she was destined to lead the Dauphin Charles to his coronation at **Reims** Cathedral and drive the English from France.

The first audacious thing she did was demonstrate that anyone listened to her at all. In May 1428, at sixteen, she walked from Domrémy to the nearby town of **Vaucouleurs** and asked the local royalist captain Robert de Baudricourt for an armed escort to take her to the Dauphin. He told her to go home. She came back twice. By February 1429, after she had correctly predicted a French defeat at the **Battle of the Herrings** before news of it reached Vaucouleurs, Baudricourt sent her with a small armed party through 350 miles (560 km) of English-held territory to the Dauphin's court at **Chinon**. She arrived in early March, was examined for orthodoxy by clerics at **Poitiers** for three weeks (they cleared her), and was sent to relieve the besieged city of **Orléans** (or-LAY-ahn) in late April with armor, a horse, a small banner, and a position of effective command of part of the French army.

**Orléans** had been under English siege for seven months and was the strategic key to the lower Loire — if it fell, the road to Bourges was open and the English would probably finish off the disinherited Dauphin's regime within the year. Joan reached Orléans on April 29, 1429. Within nine days the siege was broken: the French sortied repeatedly under her presence, captured or destroyed the English siege fortifications around the city, and forced the English commander Lord Talbot to retreat north on May 8, 1429. The relief of Orléans is the moment that reversed the trajectory of the Hundred Years War. It is reasonable to argue, as many modern historians do, that without Joan it would not have happened — French morale, by the spring of 1429, was so broken that no other commander appears to have been willing to attempt the relief.

What followed was the Loire campaign of June 1429 — a sequence of French victories at Jargeau, Beaugency, and the spectacular open-field victory at **Patay** on June 18, where French heavy cavalry caught the English longbowmen before they could deploy their stakes and rode them down. Patay was the longbow's first major battlefield defeat in eighty years.

Then the coronation. Joan led the Dauphin and the army on a 200-mile (320 km) march through still-Burgundian territory to **Reims Cathedral**, the traditional anointing place of French kings since Clovis (early-medieval Europe). On July 17, 1429, the Dauphin was anointed and crowned as **Charles VII** of France, with Joan standing in armor beside the altar holding her banner. The coronation transformed the political position of the French monarchy: Charles was now the legitimate, anointed king of France in the eyes of every Frenchman who recognized the traditional ritual, and the English Henry VI was a usurper.

What happened to Joan after the coronation is the part of the story that gets less attention. Charles VII, having got what he needed from her, became cooler toward her. Joan's military judgment, by the second half of 1429, was no longer always sound. She insisted on attempting to retake Paris in September; the assault failed and she took a crossbow bolt through the thigh. She was captured by Burgundian forces at Compiègne on May 23, 1430 — Charles VII made no significant effort to ransom her — and sold to the English in November.

Her trial took place at **Rouen** (roo-AHN, in English-held Normandy) over the winter of 1430–31, in front of a tribunal of pro-English French clerics led by Bishop **Pierre Cauchon** of Beauvais. The charges were heresy (her voices), witchcraft, and the cross-dressing she had practiced consistently since leaving Domrémy. The transcripts of the trial survive — they are one of the most extraordinary documents of late medieval Europe, in part because Joan's responses to her interrogators are so consistently sharp. ("Asked whether she knew she was in God's grace, she answered: 'If I am not, may God put me there; and if I am, may God so keep me.'" — a theologically perfect answer that gave the tribunal nothing to convict on.) She was convicted, sentenced to life imprisonment, recanted to escape the stake, then resumed wearing male clothing in prison (probably under coercion, certainly providing the tribunal the pretext to convict her of relapsed heresy), and was burned alive in the marketplace at Rouen on May 30, 1431. She was nineteen.

Her conviction was overturned by a posthumous retrial under Pope Calixtus III in 1456. She was beatified in 1909 and canonized as Saint Joan by Pope Benedict XV in 1920 — half a millennium after her death — partly to consolidate French Catholic support during World War I, when Joan had become a symbol of French national resistance.

The myth of Joan of Arc — the visionary peasant girl who saved France and was burned for it — has been adapted by everyone from Shakespeare (badly) to Mark Twain to Bertolt Brecht to multiple twentieth-century films. The historical Joan is, somewhat improbably, even more interesting than the myth: a teenage girl who, through a combination of astonishing self-confidence, real military competence, the right historical moment, and a story that translated her into the iconography of the French monarchy, genuinely turned the course of one of the longest wars in European history. Almost nothing about her trajectory should have worked. All of it did.

---

The endgame of the Hundred Years War took twenty-two more years.

After Joan, the French gradually recovered the territory the English had won. **Philip the Good** of Burgundy switched sides at the Treaty of Arras in 1435, ending the Burgundian-English alliance and isolating England diplomatically. Charles VII's reformed army — built around the **compagnies d'ordonnance**, professional standing cavalry units paid by the crown rather than by individual lords — slowly retook Normandy in 1449–1450 and Gascony in 1451. The English landed a relief expedition under **John Talbot** to recover Gascony in 1452. The French army, now equipped with the most advanced field artillery in Europe — bronze cannons cast in royal foundries under the direction of the **Bureau brothers**, Jean and Gaspard, who had standardized cannon production for Charles VII's military — caught Talbot's force at **Castillon** outside Bordeaux on July 17, 1453. Talbot's frontal cavalry charge ran straight into massed cannon fire. He was killed, and the last English army on French soil disintegrated. Bordeaux surrendered in October. The Hundred Years War was over.

Castillon is the symmetrical bookend to Crécy. Crécy in 1346 had demonstrated that disciplined infantry could destroy chivalric cavalry. Castillon in 1453 demonstrated that gunpowder artillery could destroy disciplined infantry. The military future of Europe was clearly visible from Castillon: the cannon was now the decisive weapon, and the states that could afford to cast and deploy cannons in quantity (centralized monarchies with bureaucratic tax systems) would dominate the states that could not (feudal aristocracies with private armies).

The same year that French artillery ended the Hundred Years War, Ottoman cannons brought down the walls of Constantinople (Chapter 8). 1453 is one of those years that historians like to designate as a turning point. It really was one.

---

What did the Hundred Years War actually accomplish? At one level, it returned the French and English crowns to roughly the territorial situation of 1337, minus Calais (which England held until 1558). The dynastic claim that started the war was abandoned by both sides as a fiction. The territorial map that finally emerged was not radically different from the map Edward III had inherited.

At a deeper level, the war transformed both kingdoms. France emerged with a much stronger crown — Charles VII's standing army, his royal taxation system (including the *taille* permanent direct tax, made permanent in 1439), his centralized command structure — that would shortly produce the French nation-state of Louis XI and Francis I. England emerged with the opposite: a weakened, bankrupted crown that would shortly collapse into the dynastic civil war called the **Wars of the Roses** (Chapter 8). The war also produced two new senses of national identity. By 1453, men in both kingdoms had been told for a century that they were fighting for "France" or "England" against the foreign other, and the rhetoric stuck. The early forms of French and English nationalism trace to the Hundred Years War in a way they do not trace to anything earlier.

A cross-civ note before the next chapter: while England and France were spending 116 years killing each other, the **Ottoman Empire** was using the same period to consolidate the conquest of the Balkans, defeat the last great Crusade at **Nicopolis** in 1396 (Ottoman Empire Ch 2), survive Tamerlane's near-fatal blow at **Ankara** in 1402 (Ottoman Empire Ch 3), and prepare the conquest of Constantinople in 1453 (Ottoman Empire Ch 4). The **Ming Dynasty** in China was founded in 1368 — right in the middle of the Hundred Years War — and was launching the great voyages of **Zheng He** (1405–1433) at exactly the moment Henry V was conquering Normandy. The **Mali Empire** in West Africa was at its mid-arc peak under Mansa Musa's successors during the early Hundred Years War (Mali Empire Ch 4–5). The **Aztec** Triple Alliance was founded in 1428 (Aztec Empire Ch 2) — one year before Joan of Arc broke the siege of Orléans. The **Inca** Empire's expansion under **Pachacuti** began in 1438 (Inca Empire Ch 2). The **Safavid order** in Iran was militarizing in the late fifteenth century, two generations before its founder Ismail would conquer Tabriz in 1501 (Safavid Persia Ch 1). The fifteenth century is one of the densest moments of imperial formation in human history, and Europe, distracted by its long civil war, was not the central event. It would be, after 1492. Not yet.

# Chapter 4 — The Avignon Papacy and the Western Schism

The single most consequential institutional crisis of late medieval Europe was the long collapse of papal authority that ran from 1309 to 1417. In just over a century, the Catholic Church went from being the unchallenged universal arbiter of Christian Europe — the institution that crowned emperors, deposed kings, and called Crusades — to being the divided, discredited spectacle of two and then three rival popes simultaneously claiming to be Christ's vicar on earth, each excommunicating the others' followers, each absolving the others' sins as null. By 1417, the Church had a single pope again, but the damage to the institution's prestige was permanent, and the consequences would unfold across the next two centuries as the Reformation.

The crisis happened in three overlapping phases: the **Avignon Papacy** (1309–1377, when the popes lived in southern France and were widely seen as French puppets), the **Western Schism** (1378–1417, when there were rival popes), and the **conciliar movement** (the long argument over whether the papacy or a general council of bishops held supreme authority in the Church, decisively decided in the papacy's favor by 1450). All three are essential to understanding why the late medieval Church was institutionally vulnerable to Wycliffe, Hus, and eventually Luther.

---

To understand the Avignon Papacy, you have to understand who **Pope Boniface VIII** was and what happened to him.

Boniface VIII (pope from 1294 to 1303) was the last great medieval pope in the high Gregorian tradition — the tradition that had begun with **Gregory VII** in the eleventh century (high-medieval Europe context) and held that the papacy was the supreme authority in Christendom, above all secular kings. In his most famous bull, *Unam Sanctam* of 1302, Boniface declared that "it is necessary to salvation that every human creature should be subject to the Roman Pontiff." This was as maximal a claim of papal supremacy as any pope ever made. It came at the worst possible time.

The king Boniface picked his fight with was **Philip IV of France** (ruled 1285–1314) — the same king who would shortly destroy the Templars (Chapter 1). Philip was the most ruthless and most administratively competent French king of the thirteenth century. He needed money, was taxing the French clergy without papal consent, and had no patience for Boniface's claim that the French king required papal permission to do anything. The dispute escalated through the late 1290s and early 1300s. Boniface threatened excommunication. Philip's chief minister **Guillaume de Nogaret** organized a small armed force, marched to the papal residence at **Anagni** (ah-NAH-nyee) in central Italy in September 1303, and physically arrested the pope.

Boniface was rescued by the local townspeople after three days, but the elderly pope died a month later — broken, contemporaries said, by the humiliation. The episode at Anagni was the inverse of Henry IV's penance at Canossa in 1077, when an emperor had stood barefoot in the snow for three days waiting for papal forgiveness. By 1303, the king was capable of arresting the pope, and the pope died of it.

Philip IV then arranged the elections of two compliant successors: the brief **Benedict XI** (pope 1303–1304) and the much more consequential **Clement V** (pope 1305–1314). Clement was a Frenchman, a former archbishop of Bordeaux. He was elected under heavy French pressure. Within a year, citing the political instability of central Italy and the difficulty of safe travel to Rome, he relocated the papal court to the southern French city of **Avignon** (ah-veen-YOHN) in 1309. He never left. Neither did the next six popes.

---

The **Avignon Papacy** lasted from 1309 to 1377 — sixty-eight years, seven popes, all of them French. **Petrarch**, who hated Avignon, called it the **Babylonian Captivity** of the Church, drawing the parallel to the Old Testament exile of the Jews in Babylon (Ancient Israel context). The label stuck.

How bad was Avignon? The honest answer is: less bad than the propagandistic accounts suggest, and more bad than the apologetic accounts admit.

The seven Avignon popes were generally competent administrators. They reformed the papal financial system, professionalized the curia (the papal bureaucracy), and developed the elaborate fiscal machinery that funded papal operations. They also lived very well. Avignon was rebuilt around the **Palais des Papes** (the Palace of the Popes), an immense fortified palace that is still one of the largest Gothic buildings in the world. The papal court attracted artists, lawyers, and theologians from across Christendom. Petrarch spent much of his early career there. The musical innovations of the **Ars Nova** (the new compositional style that introduced rhythmic complexity into Western music) flourished at the Avignon court.

What earned Avignon its bad reputation was three things: the perception of French royal capture, the elaborate financial extraction from the rest of Christendom, and the personal venality of some of the popes' courts.

The French capture was real but partial. All seven Avignon popes were Frenchmen. So were the majority of the cardinals they appointed. The papacy was clearly closer to the French crown than to the English, the Imperial, or the Italian crowns, and English chroniclers in particular complained that the Avignon popes blocked English ecclesiastical appointments and favored French diplomatic positions. (The English Statute of Provisors in 1351, which restricted papal appointments to English benefices, is a direct reaction to perceived Avignon favoritism.) But the papacy was not simply a French royal department. The popes were capable of opposing French policy when they chose to; the relationship was structural alignment rather than control.

The financial extraction was the deeper problem. Running the papal court was expensive — the curia, the papal household, the diplomatic corps, the construction of the Palais des Papes. The Avignon popes funded it through a sophisticated system of payments extracted from the rest of Christendom: **annates** (the first year's income from any benefice the pope appointed), **provisions** (papal control over high ecclesiastical appointments, which generated substantial fees), **indulgences** (payments for the remission of sins, which were aggressively marketed), and various other levies. The cumulative effect was that benefices across Europe paid into Avignon a significant fraction of their revenues, and bishops everywhere had powerful financial incentives to keep the curia happy. It was difficult to look at this system from England or Germany or Italy and not conclude that the papacy was running an extraction racket.

The personal venality varied. Some Avignon popes lived in sober monastic style. Others did not. The court, with its lawyers and bankers and ambitious clerics seeking papal favor, attracted the kind of corruption that any wealthy administrative center attracts. Petrarch's polemical descriptions of "the Babylon of the West" with its "stinking, rotten old he-goats" who had reduced the holy seat to a brothel are exaggerated, but there was something to exaggerate.

The cumulative effect over sixty-eight years was that the moral authority of the papacy was significantly reduced. By the 1370s, Italian writers were openly attacking the Avignon Church, and even Avignon-loyal clerics were calling for the popes to return to Rome. **Catherine of Siena** (1347–1380), an extraordinary Dominican mystic and political activist who would later be made a Doctor of the Church, wrote letters to **Pope Gregory XI** that combined spiritual advice with blunt political demands: come back to Rome, restore the papacy to its rightful seat, stop the Babylonian Captivity. Gregory eventually agreed. He returned to Rome in January 1377. He died fourteen months later. The conclave to elect his successor produced the Western Schism.

---

The **Western Schism** (1378–1417) was a thirty-nine-year period in which two and at one point three rival popes simultaneously claimed the throne of Saint Peter. It is the single most damaging episode in the institutional history of the medieval Church.

The mechanism by which it happened is depressingly simple. Gregory XI died in March 1378. The conclave to elect his successor met in Rome amid mob pressure from the Roman population, who demanded an Italian pope after a generation of French ones. The cardinals — most of them French — elected an Italian, the elderly Neapolitan **Urban VI**. Urban turned out to be a difficult, prickly, possibly mentally unbalanced man who immediately began alienating the cardinals who had elected him. Within months, twelve of the French cardinals had withdrawn from Rome, declared Urban's election invalid (because, they now claimed, it had been performed under duress from the Roman mob), and elected a Frenchman, **Clement VII** of Geneva, as the rival pope. Clement returned the papal court to Avignon. Urban remained in Rome.

There was now no procedural way to determine which election was valid. The cardinals had gone through the same procedure in both cases. The same men had voted in both elections. Both popes claimed legitimacy. Both excommunicated each other. The Christian states of Europe lined up by political affiliation: **France** and its allies (Scotland, Castile, Aragon) recognized Clement VII at Avignon. **England** and the **Holy Roman Empire** recognized Urban VI at Rome. **Italy** was split. **Portugal** wavered. **Naples** changed sides. The lay populations of Christian Europe found themselves told that their salvation depended on choosing the correct pope, with no available means of determining which one was correct.

The Schism continued through the deaths of Urban VI and Clement VII and the elections of their successors. Both lines maintained themselves. By the 1390s, it was clear that the dispute would not resolve through mere persistence — both sides could keep electing popes indefinitely. The intellectual current that emerged in response was **conciliarism**: the doctrine that, in cases of disputed papal succession, a general council of bishops representing the whole Church holds the authority to settle the matter, even by deposing the disputed claimants and electing a new pope.

The first attempt at conciliar resolution was the **Council of Pisa** in 1409, which deposed both existing popes and elected a third — **Alexander V**. The two existing popes refused to accept the deposition. There were now three popes. (Alexander V died within a year and was succeeded by **John XXIII**, not to be confused with the twentieth-century pope of the same name. The fifteenth-century John XXIII was a former mercenary captain of disputed personal morality whose name was retroactively erased from the official papal register, which is why a twentieth-century pope could reuse the number.)

The decisive resolution came at the **Council of Constance** (kon-STANCE, in southern Germany) from 1414 to 1418. Constance was the largest church council ever held — some 600 bishops and abbots, plus thousands of theologians, jurists, and political representatives, attended at various points. The council was convened jointly by Holy Roman Emperor **Sigismund of Luxembourg** and the conciliarist John XXIII. Once it convened, it deposed all three rival popes (Roman, Avignonese, and Pisan), and elected a new universally recognized pope: **Martin V**, in November 1417. The Schism was over.

The Council of Constance also did one other thing that would shape the next century of European history: it burned **Jan Hus** alive on July 6, 1415, despite holding a written safe-conduct from the emperor. The Hus episode is significant enough that it gets its own chapter (Chapter 5).

Constance also issued, in its decree *Haec Sancta* of 1415, the most ambitious assertion of conciliarism in the history of the Church: that a general council "lawfully assembled in the Holy Spirit" holds "power immediately from Christ" and that "every person, of whatever rank or office, even the papal," is bound to obey it. This was the closest medieval Christianity ever came to constitutional reform — to placing the papacy under the institutional check of a representative assembly. The Council of **Basel** (1431–1449) tried to extend the conciliarist project further, with *Frequens* requiring regular general councils every ten years.

But the popes won the long argument. Martin V and his successors patiently undermined the conciliarist movement, refused to summon councils, and by the **Concordat of Vienna** of 1448 had effectively re-established papal supremacy over national churches. The conciliarist project — the road not taken in late medieval Catholicism — would have produced a more constitutional, less monarchical Church, and almost certainly would have made the sixteenth-century Reformation either unnecessary or much smaller. The papacy's victory over conciliarism was tactically successful and strategically catastrophic, because it left the Church without an institutional mechanism for reform other than schism.

---

A pause to flag what the Schism era looked like on the ground.

For ordinary parishioners in (say) a Castilian village in 1395, the question was theological with practical consequences. The local priest had been ordained by a bishop appointed by the Avignon pope (who Castile recognized) — but the Roman pope's followers said that the Avignon pope's ordinations were invalid, which meant the local priest's masses were not real masses, which meant the parishioners' confessions and Eucharist were not real either, which meant they were dying in a state of mortal sin. From the Avignon side, the same thing was true in reverse for English and German parishioners. There was no neutral authority to consult. The reasonable answer — which most people, sensibly, settled on — was that this was God's problem to sort out, not theirs, and that following one's own bishop was the best one could do.

The structural effect was a long erosion of the assumption that the universal Church was actually universal. By 1417 there had been three full generations of European Christians who had grown up in a Church with two or three popes. Anti-clerical attitudes had hardened. National churches had grown more administratively independent. The idea that the pope's spiritual authority was self-evident and indivisible had been broken in lay imagination, even after Constance restored a single pope. The institutional damage was permanent.

Cross-civ note: the Western Schism is one of those events with no real parallel in other civilizations, because no other civilization had an institutional church remotely like the Latin Catholic Church. The closest analogies are organizational rather than religious: the Sunni-Shia split in Islam (much earlier and more theologically substantive) or the disputes over imperial succession in Byzantium (Byzantine Empire Ch 9–10), Yuan/Ming China (Ming founding 1368, Tang-Song China context), or the Mongol Khanates after Kublai (Mongol Empire Ch 7). Late medieval European Christianity's combination of an institutional papacy with universal claims, a dispersed European political system, and a literate clerical caste capable of generating principled critiques is a unique configuration. So is the resulting century-long crisis.

# Chapter 5 — Wycliffe, Hus, and the Late-Medieval Reformations

The standard textbook version of the European Reformation places it in 1517, with Martin Luther's Ninety-Five Theses on the church door at Wittenberg. This is true but misleading. The Reformation Luther started had been rehearsed for at least 130 years before he wrote a word. The doctrinal positions Luther took — the supreme authority of scripture, the priesthood of all believers, the rejection of papal authority and clerical celibacy and indulgences and (more cautiously) transubstantiation, the demand for vernacular Bibles — had been developed in detail by **John Wycliffe** in late fourteenth-century England and by **Jan Hus** in early fifteenth-century Bohemia. The Lollards and the Hussites were, in everything but name, the first Protestants. They were suppressed, burned, and crusaded against — but the Hussite armies fought five papal-imperial Crusades to a standstill between 1419 and 1434, and Wycliffe's English translation of the Bible circulated underground for over a century. By the time Luther came along, much of the doctrinal scaffolding was already in place.

This chapter traces the late-medieval Reformations that didn't quite happen, and the one that did.

---

**John Wycliffe** (1320s–1384) was an Oxford theologian, philosopher, and royal advisor. He was, by most contemporary accounts, the most brilliant scholastic philosopher in late fourteenth-century England — the kind of intellectual eminence that had earned him the master's chair at Balliol College and a position as adviser to the **John of Gaunt**, Duke of Lancaster (the third son of Edward III, uncle of Richard II, and the most powerful English nobleman of the 1370s and 1380s).

Wycliffe started as a conventional Oxford academic in the 1360s. He drifted toward radical doctrine in the 1370s. By 1380, he was making claims that no orthodox Catholic theologian had dared make for centuries.

Wycliffe's positions, in compressed form:

The supreme authority in Christian life is the **Bible**, not the pope or the magisterium of the Church. Anything in Christian practice that cannot be justified from scripture is illegitimate. The pope, if he opposes scripture, is not just wrong but — Wycliffe sometimes said — possibly the **Antichrist**.

The Church's enormous wealth was a corruption. Christ and the apostles had lived in poverty. A Church owning a third of England's land and demanding tithes from every parish was unrecognizable as the body Christ founded. Wycliffe argued that secular rulers could rightly seize Church property when the Church fell into mortal sin (a doctrine called **dominion**). This was wildly attractive to John of Gaunt and other English nobles who would have liked to seize Church property; it was also wildly attractive to the king's tax collectors.

**Transubstantiation** — the doctrine, formalized by the Fourth Lateran Council in 1215, that the bread and wine of the Eucharist literally become the body and blood of Christ — was, Wycliffe argued, philosophically incoherent and unscriptural. He proposed instead a doctrine called **remanence**, holding that the bread and wine remain bread and wine while also becoming, in some real but mysterious sense, the body and blood. (Luther's later position was very similar.)

The **clergy** had no special spiritual authority that distinguished them from laypeople. Every baptized Christian was a priest. The institutional hierarchy — pope, cardinals, bishops, priests — was a human accretion, not divinely instituted.

**Indulgences** (the practice of paying the Church to remit punishments due for sin) were straightforwardly corrupt. Confession to a priest was unnecessary; confession to God directly was sufficient. The cult of the **saints** was largely idolatrous. Pilgrimage was largely a waste of time.

The Bible should be available in **English** — and any other vernacular — so that every Christian could read it.

In the late 1370s and early 1380s, Wycliffe and a team of his Oxford followers began translating the Bible into English. The **Wycliffite Bible** (sometimes called the **Lollard Bible**) — translated from the Latin Vulgate, in two versions, the first stiltedly literal and the second more readable — was the first complete English Bible. Around 250 manuscript copies survive, which is an enormous number for a banned text and suggests that hundreds or thousands more once existed. Underground production and circulation continued through the fifteenth century.

The English ecclesiastical authorities and the papal hierarchy were horrified, but for two reasons Wycliffe was never burned. He had powerful political protectors (especially John of Gaunt), and he died of natural causes — a stroke, in his Lutterworth parish in 1384, before the Church had fully organized to suppress him. His followers, called **Lollards** (from a Middle Dutch word for "mumbler," used pejoratively), were less protected. Lollardy was suppressed by a series of English laws beginning with *De heretico comburendo* in 1401 (literally "On the burning of heretics"), the first English statute making heresy a capital offense. Lollard preachers were burned; the movement went underground; it survived in scattered communities through the fifteenth century, providing a ready-made audience for Lutheran ideas when those arrived from Germany in the 1520s.

Wycliffe's bones were a separate problem. Forty-four years after his death, the Council of Constance — the same council that had ended the Western Schism (Chapter 4) — ordered Wycliffe's body exhumed, burned, and the ashes scattered in a stream. The order was carried out in 1428. Posthumous burning was the Church's way of declaring that Wycliffe's heresies were so dangerous that even his corpse could not be allowed to rest in consecrated ground. The exhumation order is one of the more theatrical items in the Council's record.

---

The Wycliffite ideas migrated to Bohemia. **Jan Hus** (yahn HOOSS, sometimes anglicized as "John Huss," 1369–1415) was a Czech preacher and theologian — a chaplain at the Bethlehem Chapel in Prague, where he preached in Czech rather than Latin to large lay audiences, and a master at Charles University in Prague (the first university in central Europe, founded in 1348 by the Holy Roman Emperor Charles IV). Hus was not himself a peasant; he was a university-educated cleric of modest origins. His native town of Husinec gave him his surname (which means "goose" in Czech — a fact Hus himself joked about).

Bohemia in the late fourteenth century was an unusually fertile environment for reformist ideas. The Czech kingdom had several distinctive features: a strong reform-minded preaching tradition going back to **Jan Milíč** of Kroměříž and **Matěj of Janov** in the 1370s, a literate urban population in Prague that had grown rich on silver mining, a Czech-speaking peasantry that resented German and Italian Church appointments, and a royal house (Charles IV and his successors) that had at various points patronized reformist preachers as part of broader political agendas. Wycliffe's writings reached Prague in the 1390s through scholarly networks (Czech students had begun studying at Oxford after the marriage of Richard II of England to Anne of Bohemia in 1382). They were enthusiastically received by the Prague reform faction.

Hus adopted most of Wycliffe's positions: scriptural supremacy, attack on indulgences, attack on clerical wealth, attack on papal authority. He was somewhat more cautious on transubstantiation and on lay communion in both kinds (the doctrine that lay communicants should receive both the bread and the cup at communion, not just the bread — the Latin term *utraquism*, from *sub utraque specie*, "under both kinds"). Hus's preaching at the Bethlehem Chapel drew enormous crowds. By 1410, the archbishop of Prague had condemned him and had Wycliffe's writings publicly burned. By 1411, Hus had been excommunicated. The political tension escalated through 1412–1414, with Hus retreating from Prague to a sympathetic noble's castle but continuing to write and preach.

In 1414, the Holy Roman Emperor **Sigismund** invited Hus to attend the Council of Constance to defend his views. Sigismund issued Hus a written **safe-conduct** guaranteeing his personal safety. Hus accepted the invitation, traveled to Constance, was almost immediately arrested by papal authorities, was held in chains for months, and was tried for heresy. The promised safe-conduct was held to be inapplicable on the grounds that the Council was not bound by promises made to heretics — a piece of casuistry that Sigismund accepted without protest. Hus refused to recant. He was condemned and burned at the stake in Constance on July 6, 1415. His ashes were thrown into the Rhine.

The execution of Jan Hus produced the response in Bohemia that the Council had not anticipated.

---

The **Hussite Wars** (1419–1434) were the first successful military rebellion against papal authority in late medieval Europe. They are also one of the most under-taught episodes in the popular history of the period.

In Prague in 1419, a crowd of Hussite supporters threw seven Catholic city councillors out of the windows of the New Town Hall — the **First Defenestration of Prague** (a tradition of Czech political action that would be repeated in 1483 and most famously in 1618, where it began the Thirty Years War). The defenestration was the trigger. Within months, Bohemia was in revolt. The Czech king (Sigismund's brother Wenceslas IV) died in shock at the news; Sigismund, claiming the crown by inheritance, was rejected by the Bohemian estates as the man who had murdered Hus. By 1420, Bohemia was at war with the empire and the papacy.

The military genius of the Hussite revolt was **Jan Žižka** (yahn ZHEESH-kah, c. 1360–1424), a Czech minor noble who had been blind in one eye since youth and would lose the other to a crossbow wound during the wars (and continued to command from a cart, with his lieutenants describing the battlefield to him). Žižka's tactical innovation was the **war wagon** (Czech *vozová hradba*) — armored ox-drawn wagons fitted with mounted firearms (early **handgonnes** and small cannons), arranged in a defensive square that could march across a battlefield, halt, and become an instant fortress. The wagon-fort tactic absorbed cavalry charges, repelled infantry assaults, and gave the Hussites overwhelming advantage in defensive battles. Žižka also pioneered the systematic use of gunpowder firearms by infantry — Hussite armies fielded large numbers of handgonners and crossbowmen at a time when most European armies still relied on bow-and-pike infantry. The Hussite wagon-fort tactic and gunpowder infantry doctrine would shortly be adopted by other armies (including, two generations later, the Ottomans at Chaldiran — Safavid Persia Ch 3).

Five major **Hussite Crusades** were called by the papacy and Sigismund between 1420 and 1431. All five failed. The Hussite armies, though always smaller than their crusader opponents, won repeatedly through tactical superiority and home-territory defense. The Crusades culminated in the catastrophic crusader defeat at **Domažlice** in 1431, when a 100,000-strong imperial army reportedly broke and fled at the mere sound of the approaching Hussite wagons singing the Hussite battle hymn. After Domažlice, the papacy abandoned the crusade strategy and opened negotiations.

The compromise was reached at the **Council of Basel** (the conciliarist council from Chapter 4) in the **Compactata of Prague** in 1436. The compromise: the Hussites accepted nominal reunion with Rome and the legitimacy of Sigismund as king of Bohemia. The papacy accepted lay communion in both kinds (the cup as well as the bread) for Hussite congregations and the right of Bohemian preachers to preach in Czech. This was a substantial concession — it was the only time the late medieval Catholic Church formally permitted distinctively reformed liturgical practice in any region. The Hussite Compactata held for about fifty years before later popes repudiated them, but during that period Bohemia had a semi-independent national church with reform doctrines, two centuries before the Reformation made that pattern routine.

The Hussite movement also fragmented internally. The moderate **Utraquist** wing accepted the Compactata. The radical **Taborite** wing — based in the south Bohemian town of **Tábor**, where they had built a millenarian community organized around shared property and apocalyptic expectation — was crushed at the Battle of Lipany in 1434, where the moderate Hussites and Catholic Bohemians together defeated the Taborites in a particularly grim civil-war engagement. The surviving Taborite remnants would eventually merge with various reformist communities to produce the **Unity of the Brethren** (the Czech proto-Protestant church that would later become the Moravian Church), which is still extant in the twenty-first century.

---

Why didn't the Hussite Reformation spread? The historical question is genuinely interesting because the Hussite ideas were essentially identical to Lutheran ideas and were defended by armies that had defeated five papal-imperial crusades. Why didn't Bohemia's local reformation become a continent-wide one?

Several factors. The Hussite reformation was strongly Czech-national, with the linguistic boundary between Czech and German doubling as the reformation boundary. Hussite ideas crossed into German lands through scholarly networks but did not produce mass movements among German speakers — partly because German princes had no political incentive to break with Rome at a moment when they were busy consolidating their own power within the imperial framework. The Hussite ideas also lacked the printing press as a mass-distribution mechanism. They were spreading, slowly, by manuscript. When **Gutenberg's** printing press appeared in the 1450s (Chapter 7), the basic Hussite-Wycliffite doctrinal package was already a century old but had not yet reached the audience that would shortly be available. Luther got the Hussite-Wycliffite ideas plus the printing press and produced an avalanche.

There is a famous story — possibly apocryphal — that Luther himself, when accused at the Leipzig Debate of 1519 of espousing Hussite heresies, replied: "I am a Hussite without knowing it." It is at minimum a useful summary of the relationship.

---

Cross-civ note on religious reform movements: the late medieval European Reformations sit in a wider pattern of fourteenth-and-fifteenth-century religious reform. Within Latin Christendom there were also the **Devotio Moderna** (a Dutch and Rhineland devotional movement led by **Geert Groote** and the **Brethren of the Common Life**, producing the bestselling devotional manual *The Imitation of Christ* by **Thomas à Kempis** around 1418), and the various Italian observant movements that reformed the Franciscan and Dominican orders from within.

Outside Latin Christendom: the **Hesychast** movement in late Byzantine Orthodoxy (Byzantine Empire context — peak in the fourteenth century), which produced its own theological controversy over the nature of divine light and the spiritual practice of contemplative prayer. The **Sufi** orders across the Islamic world were entering one of their most generative periods — including the Safaviyya order at Ardabil that would eventually produce the Safavid dynasty (Safavid Persia Ch 1). The **bhakti** devotional movement in late medieval India produced regional reform traditions like Kabir, Mirabai, and the early Sikh community under Guru Nanak (Medieval India Ch 6 — the bhakti chapter). All of these are independent developments that should not be over-assimilated to the European story, but it is striking that the late medieval centuries across Eurasia were a period of unusual religious creativity outside (and sometimes against) established institutional religion.

# Chapter 6 — Peasant Revolts and Urban Uprisings

If the Black Death produced material gain for the peasantry through labor scarcity (Chapter 2), it also produced rising peasant expectations and the political assertiveness that came with them. The half-century after the plague saw three of the largest popular uprisings of medieval Europe: the **Jacquerie** in northern France (1358), the **Ciompi** in Florence (1378), and the **English Peasants' Revolt** (1381). All three were suppressed within weeks. None achieved their immediate political goals. All three are, however, useful indicators of the post-plague political weather, and they share enough common features to suggest a pattern: rural and urban workers who had absorbed the Black Death's lessons about labor scarcity and were no longer willing to accept the social subordination the high medieval order had taken for granted.

This chapter is shorter than the previous ones because the revolts themselves were brief. Their structural significance is larger than their narrative footprint suggests.

---

The **Jacquerie** of 1358 was the largest peasant revolt in French history before 1789. It takes its name from the contemptuous nickname **Jacques Bonhomme** ("Jack Goodfellow") that the French nobility used for peasants — the medieval equivalent of "Joe Sixpack."

The political context was the catastrophe of the Hundred Years War. France in 1358 was reeling from the English victory at Poitiers in 1356 (Chapter 3), where King John II had been captured. The French royal government was paralyzed: the king was a prisoner in London, his teenage son the Dauphin Charles was attempting to govern under enormous pressure, the nobility had lost prestige catastrophically (their famous chivalric host had been routed by a smaller English force), and the Estates General in Paris under **Étienne Marcel** (the merchants' leader) was attempting reforms that bordered on revolution. The countryside was being systematically pillaged by routier mercenary companies — bands of demobilized soldiers who had not been paid and were living off the land. Peasant villages were being burned, raped, and looted on a routine basis, often by the same nobility whose military protection the peasants were notionally paying for.

The revolt began in late May 1358 in the Beauvaisis region north of Paris when a group of peasants killed a knight who had been extorting them. Within days the uprising spread across the Île-de-France, Picardy, and Champagne. Peasants attacked noble manors, burning castles, killing knights and their families, and sometimes engaging in symbolic atrocities — chronicle accounts (mostly written by hostile noble or clerical observers) describe the rape of noblewomen in front of their husbands, the killing of children, the eating of roasted noble flesh. How much of this is accurate and how much is later atrocity-mongering is contested. What is clear is that the violence was directed specifically at the nobility — peasants did not attack their own villages, churches, or commoner neighbors. The grievance was social, not anarchic.

The Jacquerie's leader, the only major figure to emerge from it, was a peasant named **Guillaume Cale** (sometimes called Guillaume Carle), who organized perhaps 5,000–6,000 peasant fighters into a coordinated force in the lower Beauvaisis. Cale attempted to coordinate with the bourgeois reform movement of Étienne Marcel in Paris. The connection never fully worked, but it was enough to terrify the nobility into a coordinated response.

The crushing happened on June 10, 1358. **Charles II of Navarre** ("Charles the Bad," a French nobleman who claimed the French throne and would shortly attempt to seize it during the post-Poitiers chaos) lured Cale to a parley under safe conduct, arrested him, decapitated him on the spot, and then attacked the leaderless peasant army. The peasants, who had no real military training or equipment, were slaughtered. The estimates of casualties run from "thousands" to "20,000" in the next several weeks of nobility revenge across the Beauvaisis. Étienne Marcel was killed in Paris a month later. The Dauphin Charles consolidated his control, eventually became Charles V the Wise (Chapter 3), and the reformist moment passed.

The Jacquerie achieved nothing politically. What it left behind was a collective noble memory of how dangerous the rural population had become, and the corresponding political logic of harsh repression that would shape French peasant policy for the next four centuries.

---

The **Ciompi Revolt** in **Florence** in July 1378 was a different kind of uprising — urban rather than rural, and led by industrial workers rather than peasants.

Florence in 1378 was the financial and textile capital of late medieval Europe. The city's economy was dominated by the wool industry, which employed perhaps a third of the urban population. The political system was structured around guilds (the *arti*), with the seven **major guilds** (*arti maggiori*) — bankers, judges, cloth merchants, silk merchants, doctors, furriers, money-changers — holding effective control of city government. The fourteen **minor guilds** (*arti minori*) of artisans had limited access to power. The ordinary wool workers — the **Ciompi** (CHOM-pee, possibly from a French nickname for spinning workers) — had no guild at all and no political representation.

The trigger was a confrontation between the powerful Albizzi faction and a coalition of moderate political opponents over the *ammonizione* (a system of political proscription that the Albizzi had been using to exclude rivals from office). When the Albizzi-controlled regime fell in late June 1378, the resulting power vacuum gave the unrepresented working class an opening. The Ciompi rose on July 21, 1378. They marched through the city, burned the records of the wool merchants' guild, and demanded their own guild representation, fairer wages, and the right to participate in government. Within days they had effectively seized control of the Signoria (the city government).

The Ciompi government — the only direct rule by industrial workers in any major medieval European city — held power for about six weeks. It established three new guilds for wool workers, dyers, and shirt-makers, giving these previously unrepresented workers political voice. It also confronted the structural problem that had limited every previous worker movement: it could not coordinate with the moderate guilds that had initially supported the broader anti-Albizzi coalition. The major guilds and the surviving Albizzi faction reorganized, and on August 31, 1378, an alliance of guild masters and patrician forces overthrew the Ciompi government and restored oligarchic rule.

The Ciompi guilds were dissolved. Their leaders were exiled, executed, or driven into hiding. The Florentine ruling oligarchy was so traumatized by the experience that it spent the next half-century systematically excluding any possibility of working-class political participation. The eventual settlement was the **Medici** ascendancy of 1434 and after (Chapter 7), which provided the urban poor with patronage, employment, and ceremonial inclusion in exchange for political quiescence.

The Ciompi episode is sometimes called the first proletarian revolution. This is anachronistic but not entirely wrong. The Ciompi were industrial workers in a recognizably modern sense — they did not own their tools (the wool merchants did), they sold their labor for wages, and they organized politically against the conditions of that labor. They were also, in a recognizably modern sense, defeated by the structural disadvantages of working-class organization in a capitalist economy: the masters could afford to wait them out; the workers could not afford to stay out of work; the moderate middle classes who had initially supported them found their economic interests aligning with the masters once the immediate political crisis passed. The Ciompi were the first test of a script that the next five centuries would replay many times.

---

The **English Peasants' Revolt** of 1381 — sometimes called **Wat Tyler's Rebellion** — was the largest popular uprising in medieval England.

The political context was the **Poll Tax** of 1380, the third in three years, levied to fund the dragging Hundred Years War. Unlike the earlier graduated income taxes, the 1380 poll tax was a flat per-capita charge on every adult — which was massively regressive, since a poor laborer paid the same as a wealthy yeoman. The tax was widely evaded; the government's response was to send tax commissioners to enforce collection. The commissioners' aggressive methods (chronicle accounts describe inspectors verifying women's tax status by examining whether they were sexually mature) provoked riots in Essex and Kent in late May 1381. Within a week, the riots had grown into an organized movement.

The leaders were **Wat Tyler**, a Kentish ex-soldier whose background is otherwise obscure, and the radical priest **John Ball**. Ball was a Lollard sympathizer (probably — the doctrinal links to Wycliffe are debated) who had been preaching for years on the equality of all Christians and the wickedness of the social hierarchy. His most famous sermon, preached to the rebels at Blackheath on June 12, 1381, took as its text the rhyming couplet:

> When Adam delved and Eve span,
> Who was then the gentleman?

(*Delved* meant "dug"; *span* meant "spun thread.") The point was theological: all human beings descend from Adam and Eve, who worked the soil and the spinning wheel without any noble class. The class system was therefore a human invention without biblical warrant. Ball's egalitarian preaching reached an audience that was already economically empowered by the post-plague labor scarcity, politically radicalized by the poll tax, and ideologically primed by Lollard doctrine. The combination was explosive.

The rebels marched on London on June 13, 1381. They burned the Savoy Palace (the home of John of Gaunt, who was conveniently away in Scotland), destroyed the Inns of Court (where the legal records of serfdom were kept), opened the prisons, and killed the Archbishop of Canterbury **Simon Sudbury** (who, as Lord Chancellor, had introduced the poll tax) and the royal treasurer **Robert Hales**. The fourteen-year-old King **Richard II**, who had taken refuge in the Tower of London with his mother, met the rebels at **Mile End** on June 14 and at **Smithfield** on June 15. At Smithfield, in negotiations whose chronology and detail are disputed, the Mayor of London **William Walworth** killed Wat Tyler with a dagger — the rebels were on the point of attacking when Richard rode forward and reportedly cried "I will be your captain!", successfully calming the leaderless crowd long enough for the city militia to arrive and disperse them.

Richard II's promises to the rebels at Mile End — the abolition of serfdom, free participation in markets, pardons for the rebels — were honored for about a week, then revoked once the rebellion was clearly broken. Royal forces hunted down the surviving leaders over the following month. **John Ball** was arrested, tried, hanged, drawn, and quartered at St Albans on July 15, 1381. The rebellion was suppressed; the poll tax was abandoned; the formal institution of English serfdom was not abolished but quietly fell into disuse over the next half-century as labor market pressures made it economically untenable.

---

What did the late-medieval peasant revolts achieve? Politically, almost nothing. None of the three major revolts produced lasting institutional change. The Jacquerie was crushed in two weeks. The Ciompi held power for six weeks. The English Peasants' Revolt was suppressed in a month. The participants were killed in large numbers. The political demands were rejected. The class system the revolts attacked persisted essentially unchanged for the rest of the medieval period.

What the revolts did achieve was demonstrate that the post-plague European labor force had developed a political consciousness that the high medieval order had not allowed for. Peasants and urban workers could organize across long distances (the English revolt coordinated Essex, Kent, East Anglia, and parts of the Midlands within days), articulate political programs (egalitarian preaching, demands for guild representation, specific tax grievances), and confront royal and noble authority directly. They could also, when crushed, contribute to a quiet structural transformation in which the formal institutions of serfdom and labor coercion eroded over the following century, not because they were abolished but because they became unenforceable.

The economic logic mattered more than the political demands. By 1500, English serfdom was effectively extinct. French serfdom persisted in some regions but had become much milder. Italian urban guild systems had become more inclusive. Eastern European serfdom — for complex reasons that include the absence of comparable post-plague labor markets and the specific political balance between landlord and peasant — went the other direction, intensifying through the late Middle Ages and into the early modern period in what historians call the **Second Serfdom**. Western European peasants gained material conditions they would not lose; Eastern European peasants lost conditions they would not regain for another four centuries.

Cross-civ note on contemporary peasant uprisings: the late fourteenth century was a period of unusual rural unrest across Eurasia. The **Red Turban Rebellion** in China (1351–1368) — actually not the same Red Turban as the later Qizilbash (Safavid Persia Ch 1), but a millenarian Buddhist movement — overthrew the Yuan dynasty and brought **Zhu Yuanzhang**, a peasant turned warlord, to the imperial throne as the first **Ming** emperor. The Ming founding in 1368 was the largest peasant-revolt success in world history and the only one in the late medieval period that produced a long-term political revolution. The contrast with the European revolts is instructive: the Chinese rebellion succeeded because the imperial system had collapsed under climate, plague, and Mongol misrule; the European rebellions failed because European royal governments, despite the Hundred Years War and the schism, had not collapsed at the same level. Late-medieval European peasants got better wages and quietly buried serfdom; late-medieval Chinese peasants founded a dynasty.

The Italian Renaissance is next.

# Chapter 7 — The Renaissance Threshold

The conventional story of the Italian Renaissance starts in 1401, with the famous competition for the bronze doors of the Florentine Baptistery, or in 1436, with the consecration of Brunelleschi's dome on Florence Cathedral, or in 1453, with the fall of Constantinople and the flood of Greek manuscripts west, or in some other dramatic launch moment. The reality was less dramatic. The Italian fourteenth and fifteenth centuries were a continuous period of artistic, intellectual, financial, and architectural innovation that emerged from late medieval foundations and produced the cultural moment that nineteenth-century historians named the **Renaissance** ("rebirth"). This chapter takes a thematic rather than chronological approach to that emergence — the Italian city-states, the Medici, the early humanists, the architectural and artistic revolutions of the fifteenth century, the Northern Renaissance in oil, and the printing press that would shortly change everything.

A point of definition before we start. "Renaissance" is a term invented by the nineteenth-century Swiss historian **Jacob Burckhardt** in his 1860 book *The Civilization of the Renaissance in Italy*, on the model of language used by Italian fifteenth- and sixteenth-century writers about themselves (Petrarch wrote of a *renovatio*, "renewal," and Ghiberti of a *rinascita*, "rebirth"). The term has been heavily contested by twentieth-century historians: medievalists rightly point out that the "rebirth" framing implies that medieval culture had been dead, which it manifestly had not; that the supposed Renaissance discoveries (perspective, naturalism, classical sources, vernacular literature) were extensions of late medieval developments rather than break-throughs; and that the standard periodization (Italy in the fifteenth century, the rest of Europe in the sixteenth) flattens a much more complex regional and chronological story. We will use the term because it is unavoidable, but the reader should hold in mind that it is a label rather than an explanation, and that "the Renaissance" is more usefully thought of as the late-medieval Italian city-state world entering its mature phase than as a sudden break with what came before.

---

The Italian city-states of the late Middle Ages were the world's largest concentration of urban wealth and political independence outside East Asia.

By 1400, **Italy** was the only region of Europe where political fragmentation and urban concentration combined at scale. Northern and central Italy contained perhaps twenty independent or semi-independent city-states, each with its own government, foreign policy, currency, and military. The most important were the so-called **Five States** that the **Peace of Lodi** in 1454 would formalize as the major powers of the peninsula:

**Venice** was a maritime republic governed by an oligarchy of merchant patricians through a Doge (a ceremonial figurehead with limited real power) and a series of councils of ascending exclusivity. Venice's wealth came from Mediterranean trade — the spice routes from Alexandria, the silk routes through Constantinople and Trebizond, the slave routes from the Black Sea, and the salt and timber trade in the Adriatic. By 1400 Venice was probably the wealthiest city in Europe, with a population of perhaps 100,000 and a maritime empire stretching down the Dalmatian coast and into the Aegean.

**Genoa** was Venice's main rival, a smaller maritime republic on the western Italian coast with similar trading networks but a less stable internal politics. Genoa had founded the trading colony at **Caffa** that brought the Black Death to Europe (Chapter 2) and would later, through one of its mariners — **Christopher Columbus**, born in Genoa in 1451 — accidentally open the Atlantic.

**Milan** was a powerful inland duchy in the Po valley, ruled until 1447 by the **Visconti** dynasty and then by their condottiere (mercenary captain) successor **Francesco Sforza** and his descendants. Milan was the most militarized of the major Italian states, with the most professional army and the most aggressive expansionist foreign policy. The Visconti were also major patrons of late Gothic art and the early universities.

**Florence** was the textile and banking capital of Italy and would become the principal site of Renaissance art and intellectual culture. Florence was nominally a republic governed by elected magistrates from the major guilds, but actual political power was concentrated in a small group of wealthy banking families. After 1434, the **Medici** family ran the city without ever holding formal monarchical title, through their control of the Medici Bank, their patronage networks, and their elaborate manipulation of the constitutional offices. Florence's population in the fifteenth century was around 50,000 — half what it had been before the Black Death — but its economic and cultural weight was disproportionate.

**Naples** was the kingdom in the south of Italy, the only major Italian power that was a monarchy in the conventional sense rather than a city-state. Naples was contested between French (Angevin) and Spanish (Aragonese) dynastic claims through the fifteenth century, with frequent shifts of dynasty.

The **Papal States** in central Italy completed the political picture, although the popes were a special case — they were both Italian princes (with significant territory and revenue) and the universal heads of Latin Christendom (Chapter 4).

The political fragmentation of fifteenth-century Italy was, paradoxically, the precondition for the cultural flowering. Five competing capitals meant five sets of patrons, five courts seeking artistic glory, five sources of commission. The city-state competition produced a building boom, a portrait boom, and an intellectual boom that single-state monarchies elsewhere in Europe (France, England, Castile) did not match. When Italian artists and humanists wanted patronage, they had options — and the patrons knew it, which inflated both their prices and their prestige. The mature Italian Renaissance was structurally the product of competitive city-state patronage, not of any single court's vision.

---

The most important political family of the Italian Renaissance was the **Medici** of Florence.

The Medici started as middling Florentine bankers in the fourteenth century. They were not aristocrats — the Florentine constitution was officially anti-aristocratic — but a successful merchant family who had built up the **Medici Bank** through prudent management, papal banking concessions (a major source of their wealth was acting as bankers for the papal curia, collecting dues from across Christendom and remitting them to Rome with a profitable cut), and shrewd political alliances. The first Medici to translate financial wealth into political power was **Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici** (1360–1429), who consolidated the bank's papal business and accumulated political capital without ever holding formal office.

His son **Cosimo de' Medici** (1389–1464) — Cosimo the Elder, Cosimo *il Vecchio* — completed the transformation. Cosimo inherited the bank in 1429 and built it into the largest bank in Europe. He was briefly exiled by a rival faction in 1433. When he was recalled in 1434, he returned to Florence and proceeded to dominate its politics for the next thirty years through what would today be called "soft power": careful constitutional manipulation, cultivation of friendly magistrates, generous patronage of guilds and neighborhood associations, and avoidance of any formal title that would have made him a target for traditional anti-tyranny rhetoric. Foreign visitors who asked who ruled Florence were told "the Council of Two Hundred." Foreign visitors who knew anything knew it was Cosimo.

Cosimo also patronized the early Italian Renaissance as a major project. He founded the **Platonic Academy** in Florence under **Marsilio Ficino** (who translated Plato's complete dialogues into Latin between 1463 and 1483 — the first such Latin translation, made possible by Ficino's Greek training and Cosimo's funding). He commissioned major works from **Brunelleschi**, **Donatello**, and **Fra Angelico**. He founded the Library of San Marco, the first public library in Europe with cataloged collections open to scholars. He hosted the **Council of Florence** in 1438–1439, the great failed attempt at reunion between the Latin and Greek churches before the fall of Constantinople, paying enormous sums to bring the Byzantine emperor and the Greek delegation to Italy. The Council failed politically (it produced a paper union that was repudiated by most Greeks within a few years), but it succeeded culturally — the Greek scholars who attended brought manuscripts of classical Greek texts that the Italian humanists immediately copied, beginning the great fifteenth-century Italian rediscovery of ancient Greek literature.

Cosimo's grandson **Lorenzo de' Medici** (1449–1492) — **Lorenzo the Magnificent**, *il Magnifico* — completed the Medici cultural project. Lorenzo ruled Florence from 1469 to 1492 with even less formal authority than Cosimo had used, and even more cultural impact. He sponsored **Sandro Botticelli** (the painter of the *Birth of Venus* and the *Primavera*), the young **Michelangelo Buonarroti** (whom Lorenzo housed in his palace and treated essentially as an adopted son from the age of fourteen), the philosopher **Pico della Mirandola** (whose *Oration on the Dignity of Man* is the manifesto of Renaissance humanism), and the Greek-trained poet **Angelo Poliziano**. Lorenzo also let the Medici Bank slide toward bankruptcy through a combination of bad loans (especially to Edward IV of England, who defaulted), distracted management, and political concessions that diverted bank resources to political spending. By the time of Lorenzo's death in 1492, the Medici Bank was effectively insolvent, and the family's political position was sustained by reputation rather than by financial substance. Within two years of Lorenzo's death, the Medici had been driven from Florence (the French invasion of 1494, see below) and the bank had collapsed.

The Medici story is the early-modern model of a family that translated financial wealth into political power and cultural patronage and back again, and ran the cycle for three generations before it broke. Versions of the story would replay in Genoa, Augsburg, Antwerp, Amsterdam, London, and (eventually) New York.

---

The intellectual movement that historians call **Renaissance humanism** was the systematic recovery, study, and emulation of classical Latin (and increasingly Greek) literature, philosophy, and rhetoric.

The pioneer was **Petrarch**, whom we met briefly in Chapter 1. Petrarch and his immediate followers — **Giovanni Boccaccio** (1313–1375) of *Decameron* fame, who was also a serious scholar of Latin literature; **Coluccio Salutati** (1331–1406), the Florentine chancellor who professionalized humanist Latin in government correspondence; **Leonardo Bruni** (c. 1370–1444), Salutati's successor and a major scholar of Greek (he translated Aristotle and Plato into much-improved Latin) — established humanism as a coherent intellectual program by roughly 1400.

The program had several components. Recovery: searching monastery libraries across Europe for surviving classical manuscripts, copying them, and making them available for study. Petrarch's discovery of Cicero's lost *Letters to Atticus* in 1345, and **Poggio Bracciolini's** discovery of Lucretius's *De Rerum Natura* in a German monastery in 1417, are the famous moments. Editing: comparing manuscripts, identifying corruptions in the text, restoring what the original was likely to have said. Imitation: writing Latin in the style of Cicero rather than the simpler Latin of medieval church documents. Translation: producing Latin versions of Greek texts (and, increasingly, vernacular Italian versions of Latin texts) so they could reach wider audiences. Education: training the next generation of humanists in classical languages and rhetoric, eventually producing the **studia humanitatis** curriculum (grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, moral philosophy) that became the educational model for European elites for the next four centuries.

The humanists were not anti-Christian. Most of them were devout Catholics. What they argued was that classical pagan literature contained moral wisdom and rhetorical sophistication that complemented (rather than contradicted) Christian revelation, and that the medieval educational curriculum had unduly neglected this body of work. **Pico della Mirandola** in his *Oration on the Dignity of Man* (1486) tried to argue for an even broader synthesis — combining Christian, Greek, Hebrew, and (he believed) ancient Egyptian wisdom into a single perennial philosophy. The orthodox Church was wary; Pico's *Oration* was condemned by the papacy and Pico himself died young (1494, at thirty-one, of arsenic poisoning — possibly accident, possibly murder). But the broader humanist project flourished and would, within two generations, produce the textual scholarship that Erasmus and others would use to launch the Christian humanist movement of the early sixteenth century, which would feed directly into the Protestant Reformation.

---

The architectural and artistic revolution of the fifteenth century is the part of the Renaissance that the lay reader is most likely to recognize.

The launching figure was **Filippo Brunelleschi** (1377–1446), trained as a goldsmith, who designed the spectacular **dome of Florence Cathedral**. The cathedral itself had been under construction since 1296 — the immense Gothic structure of **Santa Maria del Fiore** designed by Arnolfo di Cambio and continued by successors. The body of the church was finished by 1380. The crossing, however, was open to the sky, with a yawning octagonal hole 138 feet (42 m) across that no one knew how to span. Conventional Gothic vaulting could not handle the span. Roman concrete techniques had been lost. The cathedral committee stalled for forty years.

Brunelleschi won the design competition for the dome in 1418 with a proposal that astonished and offended his contemporaries. He proposed a double-shelled brick dome — an inner load-bearing structure and an outer protective shell, with hidden iron and stone tension chains binding the structure horizontally to prevent it from spreading. Most controversially, he proposed to build the entire dome **without exterior scaffolding** — the medieval method of dome construction, which would have been impossible at this scale because no scaffolding could be cantilevered from a 200-foot-high opening. Instead, he proposed building each course of bricks as a self-supporting catenary curve, with each course locking the previous one in place. He also designed special hoisting machinery (powered by oxen on the ground and ropes through pulleys) to lift bricks, stone, and water to the construction level.

The dome was begun in 1420 and finished in 1436 — sixteen years of construction during which Brunelleschi invented essentially every element of the project. The completed dome was the largest masonry dome built in Europe since the Pantheon (Ancient Rome Ch 7) thirteen centuries earlier. It is still standing in 2026 and still the largest brick dome in the world. The lantern that crowns the structure was added by Brunelleschi's successor between 1446 and 1471. The cathedral was finally consecrated in 1436, the year the dome was finished.

Brunelleschi also reinvented **linear perspective** — the geometric system for representing three-dimensional space on a two-dimensional surface that defined Western painting from the fifteenth century until the early twentieth. His exact priority is debated, but two famous demonstrations around 1413 (Brunelleschi's perspective drawing of the Florentine Baptistery, viewed through a peephole that aligned the painted image with the actual building) are conventionally credited as the launch of the technique. The geometric principles were codified in the 1430s by the architect-theorist **Leon Battista Alberti** (1404–1472) in his treatise *De Pictura* ("On Painting"), the first systematic theoretical text on Renaissance art.

Linear perspective was new in the technical sense — the geometric procedure for drawing accurate perspective had not been formalized before — but the broader interest in spatial naturalism had been developing in Italian painting for at least a century before Brunelleschi. **Giotto di Bondone** (c. 1267–1337), painting in the Arena Chapel in Padua around 1305, had already produced figures with three-dimensional weight and emotional psychological depth that look proto-Renaissance to modern eyes. The Brunelleschi-Alberti perspective system was the codification and extension of trends that were already present in late medieval Italian art.

The artists who applied the new spatial techniques most influentially in the early fifteenth century were the sculptor **Donatello** (1386–1466) and the painter **Masaccio** (1401–1428). Donatello's bronze David (around 1440) was the first freestanding nude statue cast in Europe since classical antiquity. His equestrian Gattamelata in Padua (1453) was the first such monument since the Romans. His expressive marble figures — including the bald, intensely staring **Habakkuk** (the *Zuccone*) for the Florentine cathedral campanile — combined classical naturalism with psychological intensity that was new to European sculpture. Masaccio painted the **Brancacci Chapel** frescoes in Florence around 1424–1427 with rigorous Brunelleschian perspective, modeled chiaroscuro figures, and emotional immediacy that made him, in retrospect, the founder of Renaissance painting. He died at twenty-six, possibly poisoned, with most of his major work still ahead of him. The Brancacci Chapel was treated as an open-air school by Florentine painters for the rest of the century — Michelangelo, in his teens, sketched there.

---

The Italian Renaissance was not the only late-medieval European artistic revolution. The simultaneous **Northern Renaissance** in the **Low Countries** (modern Belgium and the Netherlands) developed independently and produced a different aesthetic, centered on **oil painting**, microscopic naturalistic detail, and the integration of religious and bourgeois subject matter.

The launching figures were the **van Eyck** brothers, **Hubert** and **Jan**. The shared masterpiece, the **Ghent Altarpiece** (formally *The Adoration of the Mystic Lamb*), was completed in 1432. It is a vast multi-panel altarpiece — twelve panels, hinged so they can be opened or closed depending on the liturgical calendar — that combines theological complexity with a visual realism unprecedented in European art. Every leaf on every plant in the central panel is botanically identifiable. The figures have individual personalities visible through facial expression, posture, and eye direction. The **Arnolfini Portrait** (1434), Jan van Eyck's solo masterpiece, is a small (32 × 23-inch) double portrait of a Bruges merchant and (probably) his wife in their bedroom, with a convex mirror on the back wall that reflects the room from the opposite angle. Every surface — the bronze chandelier, the wooden floor, the dog's fur, the orange on the windowsill — is rendered with microscopic specificity. The painting is the founding document of the genre that would, much later, become northern European bourgeois portraiture.

What enabled the Northern Renaissance was the **oil painting technique**. Earlier European painting had used **tempera** — pigment mixed with egg yolk on a gesso ground — which dried quickly, did not blend smoothly, and could not produce the layered translucency of oil. Oil painting (pigment mixed with linseed or walnut oil) had been used for minor purposes in earlier medieval art, but its development as a major fine-art medium was a Flemish innovation of the early fifteenth century. The van Eycks did not invent oil painting in the literal sense, but their refinement and systematic exploitation of the medium transformed what European painters could do. Italian painters adopted oil over the next several decades; by the late fifteenth century, oil on panel and oil on canvas had largely replaced tempera as the standard medium of European painting, and would remain so until the twentieth century.

The Northern Renaissance also produced **Hieronymus Bosch** (c. 1450–1516), the Dutch painter from 's-Hertogenbosch whose hallucinatory triptychs — the **Garden of Earthly Delights** is the famous one, painted around 1490–1510 — combined late medieval moralist iconography with grotesque fantasy and proto-surrealist imagery without parallel in fifteenth-century European art. Bosch's work is genuinely strange and remains genuinely strange; modern art historians have not produced a unified interpretation of what he was actually trying to communicate. The likeliest reading is that he was a deeply orthodox Catholic moralist using the visual vocabulary of medieval marginal illumination (where grotesque hybrids and obscene puns had been a tradition for centuries) to communicate the dangers of sin and the necessity of salvation — but the reader is welcome to draw their own conclusions from staring at the paintings.

The Northern Renaissance had its own architectural and intellectual streams as well — the **late Gothic** flowering in Flemish and Brabantine architecture, the **Devotio Moderna** religious movement (Chapter 5), the early Christian humanism of figures like **Rudolf Agricola** (1444–1485) and the young **Erasmus** (1466–1536). The conventional account that has the Renaissance starting in Italy and migrating north is therefore incomplete; the late fifteenth century had two parallel northern and southern Renaissances that were in dialogue with each other (Italian artists studied Flemish oil painting; Flemish humanists studied Italian classical scholarship) but developed substantially independently.

---

The single most consequential late-medieval European invention was the **printing press**.

The technology of movable-type printing — individual reusable metal letters that could be assembled into pages, inked, pressed against paper, and disassembled for reuse — was developed in **Mainz** in the German Rhineland in the 1440s by **Johannes Gutenberg** (c. 1400–1468) and his collaborators. The first major book produced was Gutenberg's **42-line Bible**, a Latin Vulgate Bible printed around 1455 in an edition of perhaps 180 copies (49 of which survive in 2026, in whole or in part). The 42-line Bible is one of the most beautiful books ever printed. It is also the founding artifact of the modern era.

Why did Gutenberg's invention matter so much? The cost of producing a book collapsed by perhaps a factor of ten. A skilled scribe could produce one large manuscript book in a year of work; a Gutenberg press could produce hundreds in the same period. The labor cost per book fell roughly proportionally. The cumulative effect over the next half-century was that books — and the literacy they enabled — became economically accessible to a much broader population than had ever had access to written texts before.

The numbers are dramatic. By 1480, presses had been set up in 110 European cities. By 1500, in over 200. The total number of books produced in Europe between Gutenberg's first Bible (around 1455) and the end of 1500 — the period of **incunabula**, "swaddling clothes," the first half-century of printing — is estimated at roughly **20 million volumes**. This was more books than had been produced in the previous millennium of European manuscript culture combined. The titles printed were dominated by religious texts (Bibles, devotional works, theology), but rapidly diversified into classical literature, vernacular poetry, technical manuals, news-sheets, and proto-newspapers.

The cultural and political consequences would not become fully visible until the sixteenth century. **Martin Luther** in 1517 would post his Ninety-Five Theses to a church door in Wittenberg; within months, printed copies were circulating in every major German city; within a year, Luther was a continent-wide celebrity. The Protestant Reformation was the first great revolution to use mass-produced printed text as its primary mobilization technology. Without Gutenberg's press, Luther's theses would have stayed local; without printing, the Reformation might well have been another Hussite-style regional movement that survived as an enclave but did not transform the continent. The press is the single technology that distinguishes Luther's potential from Hus's.

It also transformed every other domain of European intellectual life. Classical texts became accessible to scholars and educated readers at a level they had never been before. Vernacular literatures expanded as printers commissioned Italian, French, English, and German texts. Technical knowledge — on shipbuilding, on navigation, on metallurgy, on artillery, on the new astronomy of Copernicus that would arrive in 1543 — circulated at a speed that pre-print Europe had not allowed. The European voyages of discovery that begin in the late fifteenth century (Chapter 8) depended on printed maps, printed sailing manuals, and printed accounts of returning voyagers in ways that would have been inconceivable a generation earlier.

A small caveat on "first" claims: movable-type printing had been independently invented in Korea in 1234 and in China earlier than that. Gutenberg's press was a separate European invention rather than an importation. The Korean and Chinese versions did not produce a comparable cultural revolution, partly because of the much larger character sets required by Korean Hangul (introduced 1443) and Chinese (thousands of characters), partly because of the different political-cultural contexts in which printing operated in East Asia. The European press's revolutionary effect depended on the alphabet (only 26 letters needed in metal type), on the highly competitive multi-state political environment that made it difficult to suppress print, and on the existing literate-but-restricted European elite that was eager for cheaper texts. The press is one of those rare cases where a recurring invention finally produced a transformative effect because the surrounding conditions were finally right.

Cross-civ note: the late-medieval European intellectual and artistic flowering was not a unique global event. The same century saw the **Timurid Renaissance** in Herat (Safavid Persia Ch 1 — Bihzad, Jami, the brilliant illustrated manuscripts), the early **Joseon** Korean intellectual flowering under King Sejong (who personally co-invented the Hangul script in 1443), the late **Ming** flowering in classical learning and the great voyages of **Zheng He** (1405–1433), and a generally creative period in late-medieval Indian Bhakti devotional literature (Medieval India Ch 6). Europe was one node of an unusually generative fifteenth-century moment across Eurasia, not a uniquely creative outlier. What would distinguish Europe in the next century — the global voyages, the Reformation, the printed scientific revolution — depended on a combination of technologies (printing, gunpowder ships, navigational mathematics) and circumstances (political fragmentation, post-plague labor markets, capital concentration) that had not yet fully converged in 1450 but would by 1500. The next chapter is about how that convergence happened.

# Chapter 8 — The End of an Era

In the year 1453 — the same year French artillery ended the Hundred Years War at Castillon (Chapter 3) — Ottoman cannons under Sultan **Mehmed II** breached the **Theodosian Walls** of **Constantinople**, ending the Roman Empire after 1,123 continuous years. The two events happened within seven weeks of each other (Constantinople fell on May 29, Castillon on July 17). Both used artillery as the decisive weapon. Both ended a long-running medieval geopolitical configuration. Both are conventionally dated as the end of the Middle Ages, though as we will see at the end of this chapter, the periodization debate is less settled than the textbooks suggest.

This chapter covers the last half-century of the Middle Ages — Constantinople 1453, the Wars of the Roses in England, the completion of the Reconquista at Granada, Columbus, the Portuguese voyages around Africa, and the long debate over when (if ever) the Middle Ages actually ended.

---

**Constantinople** in 1453 was a husk.

The city that had been the largest in Christendom and one of the largest in the world for most of its first thousand years of existence (Byzantine Empire Ch 6) had been progressively reduced over the centuries. The Fourth Crusade's sack in 1204 (Byzantine Empire Ch 9) had broken the imperial economy. The Ottoman conquests of the surrounding Balkans through the fourteenth century (Byzantine Empire Ch 10, Ottoman Empire Ch 2) had stripped away the last hinterland. The repeated Black Death waves had culled the urban population. By 1453, the city was a small Byzantine remnant of perhaps 35,000 people inside walls built to enclose 400,000, with whole districts depopulated and reverting to gardens and ruined churches. The "Roman Empire" of 1453 consisted of Constantinople itself, a few footholds along the Sea of Marmara, and the **Despotate of the Morea** (the Peloponnese in southern Greece) — total population perhaps 100,000. The empire had been a polity in name only for at least a century.

What kept it alive — to the extent it was alive — were the **Theodosian Walls**, the fifth-century triple-layered land fortifications that had repelled every besieging army for a thousand years (Byzantine Empire Ch 1, Ch 6). The walls were what Constantinople still had. The Ottoman strategy, under their young (twenty-one-year-old) Sultan **Mehmed II**, was to break the walls.

The instrument was **gunpowder artillery**. Mehmed had commissioned a Hungarian renegade named **Orban** to cast bronze cannons of unprecedented size for the siege. The largest, called **Basilica** (or sometimes the **Dardanelles Gun**), was 27 feet long, fired stone balls weighing 1,200 pounds, and required 60 oxen and 400 men to move. Its rate of fire was perhaps three rounds per day — slow enough that defenders could repair the wall between shots by dragging up wood and earth and rubble — but the cumulative damage over weeks of bombardment was beyond what masonry walls had been designed to handle.

Mehmed's army of perhaps 80,000 — including Janissary infantry (Ottoman Empire Ch 6), feudal cavalry, gunpowder artillery, and a fleet — surrounded the city in early April 1453. The defenders were perhaps 7,000, including the **Genoese** mercenary captain **Giovanni Giustiniani** with about 700 mostly Genoese professional soldiers and the last Roman emperor **Constantine XI Palaiologos** with the city's residual military forces. The defenders were outnumbered better than ten to one and were facing the most modern siege artillery in the world.

The siege ran from April 6 to May 29, 1453. The decisive moment was Mehmed's brilliant logistical coup of April 22: when the **Golden Horn** (the inlet harbor north of the city) was sealed by an iron chain that prevented Ottoman ships from entering, Mehmed had 70 of his ships dragged overland on greased logs across the ridge behind the Genoese suburb of **Galata** and launched into the upper Golden Horn from the inland side, opening a new front against the harbor walls and forcing the defenders to spread thin. By late May, the cannons had reduced large sections of the Theodosian Walls to rubble. The defenders rebuilt nightly but were running out of men.

The final assault came at dawn on May 29, 1453. Mehmed sent in three waves: irregular Christian and Muslim auxiliaries first, to wear down the defenders; then Anatolian Turkish regular infantry; then the elite Janissaries. The Janissaries broke through the **Saint Romanus Gate** in the third wave. Giustiniani was wounded and carried from the field; his death (a few days later) effectively ended the defense. Constantine XI was killed in the fighting, in armor, somewhere near the gate — his body was never positively identified, which produced the legend of the **Marble Emperor** that would survive in Greek folk memory until the nineteenth century. The Ottomans sacked the city for three days according to the customary terms of conquest by storm.

Mehmed entered the city on May 29 and rode straight to **Hagia Sophia**, the Christian cathedral that had been the largest in the world for nearly a thousand years (Byzantine Empire Ch 2). He converted it to a mosque on the spot. Constantinople became **Istanbul** (an existing Greek nickname meaning "to the city," which the Ottomans adopted as the official name two centuries later). The Roman Empire — the polity that traced its institutional continuity in unbroken succession from the city Romulus founded in 753 BCE (Ancient Rome Ch 1) — was over. It had lasted 2,206 years.

---

The fall of Constantinople had immediate consequences across Christendom. Greek scholars and clerics fled west, mostly to Italy, carrying manuscripts of classical Greek literature that fed into the Italian humanist movement (Chapter 7). The Ottoman state inherited the Byzantine bureaucratic infrastructure, the **Patriarchate** of Constantinople (which Mehmed pragmatically preserved as a religious authority for his Greek Orthodox subjects within the **millet** system, Ottoman Empire Ch 6), and the prestige of being the new Roman emperor — Mehmed adopted the title **Kayser-i Rum** ("Caesar of Rome"), and his eventual successors would conquer most of the lands the Romans had once held in the eastern Mediterranean (Ottoman Empire Ch 5).

The cultural shock to western Europe was substantial but slower-moving than is sometimes claimed. The "Greek scholars fleeing to Italy" narrative is only partially correct: the most important Greek manuscripts had been brought west in the previous half-century by traveling Italian humanists (especially during the Council of Florence in 1438–1439, Chapter 7) rather than by post-1453 refugees. But the symbolic effect — the loss of the eastern Christian capital, the obvious threat the Ottomans now posed to the rest of Christian Europe, the end of any realistic prospect of crusade-style restoration of the Holy Land — reshaped the European political imagination in ways that took decades to fully manifest.

The Ottoman Empire ascended to the position it would hold for the next four centuries: the dominant land power in eastern Europe and the eastern Mediterranean, the principal external threat to Christian Europe, the arbiter of the Balkans and the Middle East. The next five centuries of European geopolitics would be shaped, in significant part, by the Ottoman question.

---

England, meanwhile, was descending into its own civil war.

The English losing of the Hundred Years War (Chapter 3) had bankrupted the royal treasury, demoralized the nobility, and discredited the Lancastrian regime under the gentle, intermittently-mad **Henry VI** (Henry V's son, who had inherited the throne at nine months in 1422). By the 1450s, with Calais the only English continental possession left, returning veterans of the French wars were arriving home with no employment, no land, and military experience. Aristocratic feuds that had been suppressed during the war broke out across the country. The royal court had become a battleground between rival factions led by the king's wife **Margaret of Anjou** (a French princess whose pro-French sympathies had been controversial since her arrival in 1445) and the king's cousin **Richard, Duke of York** (who claimed a stronger hereditary right to the throne than the Lancastrian line through descent from Edward III's third son).

The **Wars of the Roses** (1455–1485) — named centuries later from the white rose of York and the red rose of Lancaster — was the dynastic civil war that resulted. It is one of the most narratively complex conflicts in English history, with multiple battles, several changes of dynasty, repeated reversals, and a cast of characters that includes some of the most memorable in English medieval history (Richard III being the obvious example). The summary is that the House of York seized the throne in 1461 with **Edward IV**, lost it briefly in 1470–71 to the restored Lancastrian Henry VI, regained it in 1471 (with Henry VI murdered in the Tower of London a few weeks later), held it for the rest of Edward IV's reign and the brief reign of his son **Edward V** (the older of the famous "Princes in the Tower" who disappeared in 1483 and were almost certainly murdered), then transitioned to **Richard III** (Edward IV's brother, who took the throne in 1483 by declaring Edward IV's marriage invalid and the princes therefore illegitimate, then probably arranged their murder).

Richard III ruled for only two years. The Lancastrian claim had passed through a complicated dynastic chain to **Henry Tudor**, a Welsh-descended exile in Brittany whose connection to the royal house was through his mother **Margaret Beaufort** (a great-granddaughter of John of Gaunt through John of Gaunt's eventually-legitimized affair with Katherine Swynford). Henry Tudor invaded England in August 1485 with French and Scottish troops, met Richard III at **Bosworth Field** in Leicestershire on August 22, 1485, and won when Richard's allies (notably the Stanley family) defected at a key moment. Richard was killed in the fighting. He was the last English king to die in personal combat on a battlefield. His body was buried hastily in a Leicester monastery whose location was lost over the centuries — until a 2012 archaeological dig in a Leicester city-council car park rediscovered his bones, complete with the distinctive scoliosis Shakespeare had described, and reburied him in Leicester Cathedral with appropriate ceremony.

Henry Tudor became **Henry VII**, the first king of the **Tudor dynasty** that would rule England for the next 118 years and produce **Henry VIII**, **Elizabeth I**, and the English Reformation. The Wars of the Roses had killed off most of the older medieval English aristocracy and consolidated political power in the crown to a degree no late-medieval English king had achieved. The Tudor era — and the early modern English state — emerged directly from the destruction of the medieval English aristocratic order at Bosworth.

---

In Iberia, the long story of the **Reconquista** finally ended.

The **Reconquista** ("reconquest") was the long Christian recovery of the Iberian peninsula from the Muslim emirates that had ruled most of Iberia after the Arab conquest of 711 CE (Islamic Golden Age Ch 7 — Cordoba and Andalusi history). The recovery had begun in the eighth century in the Asturian highlands, accelerated through the eleventh and twelfth centuries (the conquest of Toledo in 1085, the great Almohad-Christian wars), and slowed through the thirteenth century as the Christian kingdoms — **Castile**, **Aragon**, **Portugal**, **Navarre** — consolidated their gains. By 1300, only the small **Emirate of Granada** in southeastern Iberia remained Muslim, paying tribute to Castile and surviving as a satellite state for the next two centuries.

The dynastic union that finally ended the Reconquista was the marriage in 1469 of **Ferdinand of Aragon** (then heir to the Aragonese throne) and **Isabella of Castile** (then heir to the Castilian throne). The marriage joined the two largest Christian Iberian kingdoms into a personal union that would eventually become **Spain** (the political entity formed by the eventual fusion of the two crowns). Ferdinand and Isabella — known together as the **Catholic Monarchs** (*Reyes Católicos*, a title given them by Pope Alexander VI in 1494) — were exceptionally competent rulers. They reformed the royal administration, asserted royal authority over the nobility (curbing the Castilian *grandes* who had dominated the previous reign), and pursued an aggressive foreign policy.

The **Granada War** (1482–1492) was the final phase of the Reconquista. The Catholic Monarchs invested ten years in systematically reducing Granada's frontier fortresses, exploiting internal divisions in the Granadan ruling Nasrid dynasty, and starving the city itself through siege. **Granada** surrendered on January 2, 1492, with the last Nasrid emir **Boabdil** (Spanish corruption of his Arabic name **Abu Abdallah**) handing over the keys of the **Alhambra palace** in a famous ceremony. (Legend has it that Boabdil's mother **Aisha** told him as he wept on a hilltop overlooking the city: "Do not weep like a woman for what you could not defend like a man." The hilltop is still called *El Suspiro del Moro*, "The Moor's Sigh." The line is almost certainly invented by later chroniclers but has stuck.)

The Reconquista was over. Eight centuries of Christian-Muslim coexistence, conflict, and gradual displacement on the Iberian peninsula had ended with the last Muslim political entity extinguished. The 1492 surrender terms guaranteed the Granadan Muslim population freedom of religion, personal property, and language. Within ten years, those terms had been violated and Iberian Muslims were being forcibly converted; over the next century, the **Moriscos** (descendants of forced converts who continued to practice Islam in secret) would be progressively persecuted and finally expelled in 1609.

The Iberian Jewish population suffered the same fate, faster. The **Alhambra Decree** of March 31, 1492, gave all Jews in Castile and Aragon four months to convert to Christianity or leave. Roughly 40,000 to 100,000 Jews left, many to Portugal (where they were expelled five years later), to North Africa, to the Ottoman Empire (where Sultan Bayezid II welcomed them as economically valuable refugees, allegedly remarking that Ferdinand had impoverished his own kingdom and enriched the Sultan's), to the Italian states, to the Netherlands, and eventually to the New World. The Sephardic Jewish diaspora that resulted is one of the major demographic events of late medieval Europe. The cultural loss to Iberia was immense.

The third enormous event of 1492 was Columbus.

**Christopher Columbus** (Italian: Cristoforo Colombo, Spanish: Cristóbal Colón) was a Genoese mariner born around 1451 who had been trying for years to find a royal sponsor for his proposed voyage west across the Atlantic to reach Asia. His geographical theory was incorrect: he believed (relying on a misinterpretation of Marinus of Tyre's geography and an exaggerated estimate of the size of Asia) that the western route from Iberia to Japan was about 2,500 miles, when the actual distance is roughly 12,000 miles. The Portuguese rejected his proposal in 1485 because their own geographers correctly calculated that the actual distance was much larger than Columbus claimed. The Spanish Catholic Monarchs, after years of indecision, finally accepted his proposal in 1492 — partly out of opportunistic interest, partly because the Granada victory had freed up royal attention, partly because Columbus had reduced his demands.

Columbus sailed from **Palos de la Frontera** on August 3, 1492, with three small ships (the *Santa María*, the *Pinta*, and the *Niña*) and 90 men. He stopped at the Canary Islands for refit, then sailed west into the open Atlantic on September 6. On October 12, 1492, his lookout sighted land — an island in the Bahamas that the indigenous **Lucayans** called **Guanahani** and that Columbus renamed **San Salvador**. The exact island has been debated; the modern consensus identifies it as either Watlings Island or Samana Cay. Columbus and his crews proceeded to explore the Bahamas, Cuba, and Hispaniola over the next three months, encountering the Taíno populations of the Greater Antilles, before returning to Spain in March 1493 with samples of indigenous people, parrots, gold, and reports of vast new lands beyond the Atlantic.

Columbus believed until his death in 1506 that he had reached the eastern fringes of Asia — that the Bahamas were islands off the Japanese coast, that Cuba was a peninsula of mainland China, that Hispaniola was a Japanese island. The realization that he had encountered an entirely new continent unknown to ancient geography came over the next decade, primarily through the voyages of **Amerigo Vespucci** (whose name would, in 1507, be applied to the new continent in a German cartographer's map: *America*). Columbus made four voyages in total, was made governor of Hispaniola, was eventually arrested for misrule and shipped back to Spain in chains, was rehabilitated, and died still believing he had reached Asia.

The political and demographic consequences of Columbus's voyages would unfold over the next century. The **Aztec Empire** would fall to **Cortés** in 1519–1521 (Aztec Empire Ch 8). The **Inca Empire** would fall to **Pizarro** in 1532–1533. Eurasian diseases — smallpox, measles, influenza, eventually plague — would kill roughly 90% of the indigenous population of the Americas over the next century and a half, in the largest human demographic catastrophe ever recorded (worse than the Black Death proportionally and incomparably larger in absolute terms). The **Atlantic slave trade**, beginning with Portuguese coastal slaving in the mid-1400s and accelerating after 1500, would forcibly transport perhaps 12 million Africans to the Americas over the next four centuries. Silver from the **Potosí** mines in Bolivia (discovered 1545) would flow through Manila to China and reshape the global monetary economy. None of this was visible from 1492. All of it followed.

---

The Portuguese were doing the same thing on a different ocean.

While Spain was finishing the Reconquista and funding Columbus, **Portugal** had been systematically working its way down the African coast for most of the fifteenth century, looking for a sea route to the Indian Ocean. The project had been started by **Prince Henry the Navigator** (1394–1460), a younger son of King John I of Portugal, who had organized a sustained program of Atlantic exploration from his base at **Sagres** on the southwestern Portuguese coast beginning around 1418. Henry trained generations of Portuguese pilots and cartographers, financed expeditions down the West African coast, and (by the 1440s) had triggered the trans-Atlantic slave trade by importing the first enslaved African captives to Lisbon for sale.

The progression down the African coast was grinding: every expedition pushed a few hundred miles further than the last, then returned to confirm the route and bring back economic intelligence. Cape Bojador (modern Western Sahara) was passed in 1434. Cape Verde in 1444. The equator in the 1470s. The Congo River in 1482. **Bartolomeu Dias** finally rounded the **Cape of Good Hope** at the southern tip of Africa in 1488, the first European to enter the Indian Ocean by sea. The route to Asia by water around Africa was now demonstrated to be possible.

It took another decade to actually use the route. **Vasco da Gama** sailed from Lisbon in July 1497 with four ships and roughly 170 men, rounded the Cape of Good Hope in November, and reached the Swahili coast of East Africa by early 1498. With the help of an Indian or Arab pilot (the contested attribution; possibly the famous **Ibn Majid**, who would have been an Arab Yemeni navigator, but the identification is disputed), da Gama crossed the Indian Ocean and reached **Calicut** on the **Malabar Coast** of southwestern India on May 20, 1498. It was the first sea voyage from Europe to India and the founding moment of European maritime imperialism in Asia.

The Portuguese moved fast. Within twenty years, they had established trading posts at Goa, Malacca, the Spice Islands, Macao, and Nagasaki — a maritime empire stretching from the West African coast to Japan, built on superior naval technology (heavily armed sailing ships with broadside cannons), strategic chokepoints, and the willingness to use violence. The Indian Ocean trade that had been controlled by Arab, Persian, Indian, and Malay merchants for centuries was rapidly subordinated to Portuguese gun-armed shipping (Mali Empire Ch 6 covers the African end of this; Medieval India context covers the Indian end).

The Spanish-Portuguese division of newly-discovered territories was negotiated in the **Treaty of Tordesillas** of June 1494, which drew a line down the Atlantic about 1,100 miles west of the Cape Verde islands: Portugal got everything east of the line (which would include Brazil, discovered in 1500 by **Pedro Álvares Cabral**), Spain got everything west. The treaty was negotiated under papal mediation; no other European power agreed to it, and within decades the Treaty of Tordesillas was effectively a dead letter as the French, English, and Dutch entered the colonial competition. But it set the linguistic boundary in South America (Portuguese Brazil, Spanish elsewhere) that has lasted to the present.

---

So when did the Middle Ages end?

The answer is: there is no single date, and historians have settled on different ones for different purposes. The major candidates are:

**1453** — the fall of Constantinople and the end of the Hundred Years War. The classic textbook answer, traditional in European historiography since the nineteenth century. It marks the end of the medieval Mediterranean order: the last fragment of the Roman state extinguished, the dynastic war that had dominated Latin Christendom for over a century closed, the Ottoman Empire ascendant, gunpowder confirmed as the decisive military technology.

**1485** — the Battle of Bosworth Field and the Tudor accession. The conventional English answer. It marks the end of the medieval Plantagenet dynasty, the consolidation of Tudor royal authority, and the beginning of the early modern English state.

**1492** — Granada, Columbus, and the Jewish expulsion. A popular modern choice, particularly in Iberian and American historiography. It marks the end of the Reconquista, the beginning of the European encounter with the Americas, and the start of the global Atlantic economy that would define the early modern world.

**1500** — round-number convention. Useful for textbook purposes but not corresponding to any specific event.

**1517** — Luther's Ninety-Five Theses. The conventional Protestant choice. It marks the beginning of the Reformation, which is the single largest religious-political rupture in early modern European history.

**1648** — the Peace of Westphalia. A more recent choice, popular among historians of international relations. It marks the end of the Wars of Religion and the beginning of the modern state-system.

The honest answer is that none of these dates marks a clean break. The Middle Ages did not end on a single day. The features we associate with "medieval" — feudal lord-vassal relations, manorial agriculture, universal Catholic Church, scholastic theology, mounted knightly warfare, Latin as the only learned language — eroded at different rates in different places over the long fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. Some medieval institutions persisted into the eighteenth century (French serfdom in some forms) or even later (the Holy Roman Empire was not finally abolished until 1806, and the Papal States persisted until 1870).

A different framing might be: the Middle Ages ended when the central explanatory institutions of medieval Europe — the universal Church, the manorial system, the scholastic university, the chivalric military order, the small Mediterranean trade economy — were no longer adequate to describe how Europeans actually lived. By that standard, the period of transition runs from roughly 1450 to roughly 1550, with no single year decisive. The Italian Renaissance, the Wars of the Roses, the fall of Constantinople, the Reconquista, Columbus, the Cape Route, the printing press, the Reformation, the Ottoman ascendancy, and the demographic catastrophe of the Americas are all part of the long transition.

The Middle Ages were not simply replaced by something better. The early modern world that emerged from the late-medieval crisis had its own catastrophes — the Wars of Religion, the Atlantic slave trade, the European witch hunts (Chapter 5 mentions the *Malleus Maleficarum* of 1486 — the witch craze proper would be a sixteenth- and seventeenth-century event), the demographic destruction of the Americas, the entrenchment of serfdom in Eastern Europe. The continuities are at least as significant as the breaks. Medieval Europe was not a darkness ended by a Renaissance dawn; it was a complex civilizational order that mutated, under enormous demographic and political pressure, into the early modern world.

What is true is that the European who fell asleep in 1300 and woke up in 1500 would have found very little of his world recognizable. The plague had culled the population by a third and recurred every decade. The Church had been split, reformed, and discredited. The Hundred Years War had broken the chivalric order and produced national identities. The printing press had collapsed the cost of books. Italian cities had reinvented painting, sculpture, banking, and architecture. Constantinople was an Ottoman capital. Iberia was Christian and was sending ships to America. The Wycliffite-Hussite reformations had rehearsed the Lutheran one. The institutions that would dominate the next four centuries — the centralized nation-state, the global maritime empire, the printed book, the joint-stock company, the gunpowder army, the Atlantic slave trade — were all in their early forms. The Middle Ages were over in any sense that mattered. The next chapter of European history was beginning.

---

A final cross-civ pause. The world of 1500 — the world that emerged from the late-medieval European crisis — was the first genuinely **global** world in human history. The Portuguese Indian Ocean trading network linked Lisbon to Nagasaki by sea. The Spanish Atlantic system would shortly link Sevilla to Manila via Mexico. The Ottoman Empire connected the Mediterranean, the Black Sea, the Red Sea, and the Persian Gulf. The Ming and the early **Joseon** Korea were tied into the East Asian world system. The Aztec Triple Alliance dominated central Mexico (Aztec Empire Ch 7), the Inca Empire stretched 3,000 miles down the Andes (Inca Empire Ch 3). The Safavid order in Iran was a generation away from founding the Twelver Shia state that would define Iranian identity (Safavid Persia Ch 1). The Mughal Empire would be founded in India in 1526. The Songhai Empire was reaching its peak in West Africa.

In 1500, for the first time, every major continental population — Europe, Africa, Asia, the Americas, the Pacific (the Polynesian voyaging society, Polynesian Voyagers Ch 8) — was within a few decades of being interconnected. The diseases, technologies, crops, ideas, religions, and military innovations that had previously been regional were about to become global. The next five centuries of human history are the working-out of that interconnection.

The European late Middle Ages are the long period in which the conditions for that interconnection were assembled. The continent walked into 1300 with a Mediterranean orientation, an agricultural economy, and a universal Church. It walked out of 1500 with an Atlantic orientation, a partly-monetized economy, a fractured Church, and gunpowder ships ranging from Lisbon to Calicut. The transformation was ugly, expensive, and largely involuntary. It was also the foundation of the modern world.

The Middle Ages ended because the medieval system could no longer describe what Europeans were doing. The early modern world that replaced it was not better in any obvious moral sense — it produced colonial slavery, religious wars on a scale the medieval Church had never managed, and the demographic destruction of the Americas. But it was different, and the difference is what the late medieval centuries had been slowly producing.

The next civilization in this app to pick up the thread is the early modern world — the Reformation, the Wars of Religion, the global trading empires, the scientific revolution, and everything else that the late Middle Ages had set in motion. The story does not end. It just becomes a different story.

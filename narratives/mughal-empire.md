# The Mughal Empire — How a Refugee Timurid Built the Wealthiest Empire in the World

**Goal:** Narrative for the Mughal Empire (1526–1857 CE), the Persianate Indo-Islamic dynasty that ruled most of the Indian subcontinent for three centuries. Across roughly 330 years, the Mughals founded a state on the back of a single battlefield revolution at Panipat (1526), nearly lost the dynasty in the second generation, recovered under Akbar to govern the Hindu majority through a deliberate Hindu-Muslim synthesis, peaked in the seventeenth century as the wealthiest state on Earth (a quarter of global GDP), built the Taj Mahal, lost it all in a slow eighteenth-century unraveling that ended with a Persian conqueror walking off with the Peacock Throne, and finally watched a chartered British company conquer the country one Bengal-paddy-field-and-bribed-general at a time. The Mughals are the dynasty that gave South Asia the Taj Mahal, the Urdu language, biryani, the world's longest sustained Persianate court culture outside Iran, and the political template that the British inherited and that modern India and Pakistan are still living inside. They are also the inheritance every contemporary South Asian political identity is fighting over: Hindu nationalists who view them as foreign occupiers; secular Indian historians who view them as the matrix of pre-modern Indian state-building; Pakistani historians who view them as the founding ancestors of Indian Islam; British imperial nostalgists who view their decline as the necessary precondition for the British civilizing mission. None of those framings is fully wrong. None is fully right.

# Chapter 1 — Babur from Ferghana

The man who founded the wealthiest empire of the early-modern world spent the first twenty-one years of his life losing.

His name was **Zahir-ud-Din Muhammad Babur** (BAH-bur, "Babur" is a nickname meaning "Tiger" in Turkic). He was born in 1483 in **Andijan** (an-dih-JAHN), a small town in the **Ferghana Valley** — a mountain-walled basin east of Samarkand in modern eastern Uzbekistan and Kyrgyzstan. His father, **Umar Shaykh Mirza**, was one of the late Timurid princes — a fifth-generation descendant of **Timur** (TEE-moor, the Turco-Mongol world-conqueror who had built and lost a vast empire 1370–1405, Timurid Empire Ch 1–2). His mother, **Qutlugh Nigar Khanum**, was a direct Genghisid in the senior Mongol royal line. By the standards of late-fifteenth-century Central Asia, Babur had unimpeachable dynastic credentials. His ancestors were Timur and Genghis Khan. The combination meant nothing if you could not also win battles.

He did not, mostly, win battles. His father died in 1494 in an absurd accident — the family pigeon house at the palace at Akhsi collapsed with him on it, and he fell to his death — and twelve-year-old Babur inherited Ferghana. The next decade of his life was a nearly unbroken series of military catastrophes against his Timurid cousins and the rising **Uzbeks** (the Turko-Mongol tribal confederation pushing south from the steppe under the brilliant general **Muhammad Shaybani Khan**, Timurid Empire Ch 8). Babur captured **Samarkand** at fifteen and lost it within months. He took it again at eighteen and lost it again. He spent extended periods as a hunted fugitive in the mountains, surviving on yogurt and stale bread, sleeping in caves, periodically losing his entire army and then reassembling it from whatever Timurid loyalists he could find.

By 1504, with the Uzbeks consolidating control of all Transoxiana, Babur was twenty-one years old and had nothing left in Central Asia. He had lost his ancestral Ferghana. He had lost Samarkand. His mother had died. Most of his original household troops were dead or had defected. He was, in the literal sense, a refugee prince.

He turned south.

---

In October 1504, with a few hundred personal followers and whatever Timurid stragglers he could collect, Babur crossed the **Hindu Kush** mountains (the major mountain range that separates Central Asia from the Indian subcontinent — the same range Timur had crossed 130 years earlier on his way to sack Delhi, Timurid Empire Ch 4) and seized **Kabul** from a Timurid cousin who was even more incompetent than he was.

Kabul became his base for the next twenty-two years. It was not much of a kingdom. The city sat at the head of a valley in modern Afghanistan, with cold winters and a marginal agricultural hinterland that could feed a few thousand troops at most. But it had three structural advantages that Babur came to understand slowly. It controlled the **Khyber Pass** (the main mountain corridor between Afghanistan and the Punjab plains), which meant it sat astride one of the great trade and invasion routes of Eurasian history. It was defensible — the Hindu Kush walls protected its north flank against the Uzbeks, who never seriously threatened it. And it was a Persian-speaking court city with libraries, gardens, and Sufi shrine networks (the **Naqshbandi** Sufi order had its Central Asian heartland just north of the Hindu Kush, Timurid Empire Ch 8), which meant Babur could keep up the cultural-political performance of a legitimate Timurid prince while he figured out what to do next.

What he eventually figured out was that he was not going to retake Central Asia from the Uzbeks. They were too strong, too well-organized, and they were Genghisids in the senior line — which gave them a legitimacy claim Babur could not match. (His own Genghisid descent was through his mother's line, which was junior to the male-line Genghisid descent the Uzbek royal family carried. Steppe political theory cared about this distinction.) He made one more attempt to recover Samarkand in 1511, with help from a brand-new Iranian regime: the **Safavid** dynasty under **Shah Ismail I** (Safavid Persia Ch 2), who had killed Shaybani at the Battle of Merv the year before and was happy to back another Timurid against the Uzbeks. Babur held Samarkand for a few months. The price of Safavid support was that he had to publicly perform Twelver Shia religious observances in a city that had been Sunni for centuries. The Sunni population of Samarkand turned against him, the Uzbeks counterattacked, and Babur was driven back to Kabul one final time. The Central Asian career was over.

He turned, increasingly, toward the south. India was not a new idea — Timur had sacked Delhi in 1398 and Babur considered himself the legitimate heir to those conquests by ancestral right — but it was now the only direction left.

---

Before we cover the conquest, the gunpowder revolution that made it possible deserves its own paragraph. By the early sixteenth century, three distinct gunpowder-weapons traditions had developed across the Eurasian world. The European tradition (cannons cast in bronze, used primarily for siege warfare and naval combat) was advancing rapidly under the pressure of the Italian Wars and the Habsburg-Valois rivalry. The Ottoman tradition (the **Janissary corps** of professional matchlock infantry, supported by field artillery deployed behind a perimeter of chained-together baggage carts called the **tabur cengi**, Ottoman Empire Ch 3) was the most mature — Janissaries had decided major battles for the Ottomans against the Mamluks (1517) and the Hungarians (1526). And the Chinese tradition (where gunpowder had been invented around the tenth century, Tang and Song China Ch 7), which by the early Ming had developed cannons and matchlocks but had not pushed military reform as aggressively as the Ottomans had.

India had effectively missed all three. The major Indian armies of 1500 — the Delhi Sultanate's Lodi forces, the Hindu Vijayanagara Empire in the south (Medieval India Ch 8), the various provincial sultanates — were still organized around heavy cavalry charges, war elephants on the flanks, and infantry archers behind the front line. This was the same military doctrine that had been state of the art a thousand years earlier under the Guptas (Gupta Empire Ch 4), and it was about to encounter gunpowder for the first time in a significant way.

Babur was lucky in one specific respect: his gun-master, a Turkish artilleryman named **Ustad Ali Quli**, had served in the Ottoman army before taking employment with the Timurids. Ustad Ali Quli understood the tabur cengi tactic in operational detail — how to chain wagons into a defensive line, how to space cannons between the wagons, how to coordinate matchlock fire with cavalry counterattacks at the right moment. He brought the tactical knowledge with him to Kabul and trained Babur's small artillery corps. By 1525, Babur had perhaps a few dozen field cannons, several hundred matchlock musketeers, and a working operational doctrine for using them on a battlefield. None of the Indian armies he was about to fight had any of this.

---

The decisive campaign came in 1525-26.

The **Lodi dynasty**, ruling from Delhi as the last Sultanate dynasty (Delhi Sultanate Ch 7), had been in slow decline for fifty years. **Ibrahim Lodi** (ib-rah-HEEM LOH-dee, ruled 1517-26) had alienated his Afghan nobility through arrogant and capricious treatment — executing senior commanders on suspicion, demoting provincial governors who had been allies of his father, generally behaving like a man who believed his own coronation propaganda. In 1525, the governor of Lahore, **Daulat Khan Lodi**, invited Babur to invade Delhi in alliance with the dissident Lodi nobles. Babur accepted.

He marched his army — perhaps 12,000 to 25,000 men, sources disagree — through the Khyber Pass into Punjab in late 1525. Several smaller Lodi forces were brushed aside on the way. By April 1526, Babur was on the plain of **Panipat** (PAH-nee-put, about 55 miles north of Delhi) with the main Mughal army, facing Ibrahim Lodi's force of perhaps 100,000 men plus a thousand war elephants.

The numerical disparity was eight to one against him. The technological disparity was the other way around.

Babur deployed his army in the perfected tabur cengi: roughly 700 baggage carts chained together along the front, gaps left for cavalry sallies, matchlock musketeers behind the carts, field cannons positioned at intervals along the cart line, cavalry held in reserve in the center and wings, mounted archers further forward to harass the approaching enemy. This was the same formation the Ottomans had used at Chaldiran in 1514 to defeat Shah Ismail's Safavids (Safavid Persia Ch 3), and the same formation Suleiman the Magnificent would use at Mohacs to defeat the Hungarians later in 1526 (Ottoman Empire Ch 5). India had not yet seen it.

Ibrahim Lodi made the standard pre-gunpowder decision. He charged.

---

**The First Battle of Panipat** lasted from sunrise to about midday on April 21, 1526. The Lodi cavalry charged into Babur's wagon-fort line. The matchlock fire from behind the carts decimated the leading ranks. The cannons opened up at point-blank range and shattered the Lodi war elephants — which spooked at the explosions, panicked, and trampled the Lodi infantry behind them. The mounted archers harassed the Lodi flanks while the heavy cavalry was pinned at the cart line. Babur's mounted reserves swept around the broken flanks of the Lodi army and completed the destruction.

Ibrahim Lodi was killed in the fighting. Sources disagree on the precise circumstances — he may have been shot, stabbed, or trampled — but his body was identified after the battle and beheaded, the head reportedly carried back to Babur as proof of victory. About 15,000 of his soldiers died with him. The Lodi dynasty, which had ruled Delhi for seventy-five years, was finished. The Delhi Sultanate, founded by Aibak in 1206 and continuous through five Turkic and Afghan dynasties for three hundred and twenty years (Delhi Sultanate Ch 1-8), was over.

Babur entered Delhi unopposed three days later, on April 27, 1526, and proclaimed himself **Padshah** — literally "master-king," the Persian-Turkic imperial title. The dynasty he founded would be called **Mughal** in European sources (a Persian rendering of "Mongol," chosen to emphasize Babur's combined Timurid-Mongol descent) and **Gurkani** in its own court usage (from the Persian title **Guregen**, "royal son-in-law," that Timur had used to indicate his marriage into the Genghisid line). The same dynasty under three names. We will use Mughal because everyone else does.

---

The conquest was not yet complete. Babur had defeated the Lodi army at Panipat but he had not yet secured north India.

The Hindu **Rajput** kingdoms of Rajasthan — particularly **Mewar**, under the formidable **Rana Sanga** of the **Sisodia** dynasty — had been quietly hoping a fight between the Sultanate and the Mughals would eliminate both, leaving the Rajputs as the regional power. When it became clear that Babur had only replaced one Muslim Sultanate with another, Rana Sanga organized a coalition of Rajput rulers (Mewar, Marwar, Amber, Hadoti, and several smaller principalities) to drive Babur out of north India. Babur met them at the **Battle of Khanwa** in March 1527, with the same wagon-fort and matchlock tactics that had won at Panipat. The result was the same. Rana Sanga's coalition was broken; the Rajputs were militarily neutralized for a generation. Babur conducted one further major campaign in 1529 against Afghan Lodi remnants in the eastern Sultanate territories at the **Battle of Ghaghra**, and consolidated control of north India from the Indus to Bengal.

He died in **Agra** in **December 1530**, age forty-seven, less than five years after Panipat. The deathbed scene is recorded in Mughal chronicles with a detail that may be partly legendary but became central to Mughal dynastic mythology: his eldest son **Humayun** (hoo-MAH-yoon) had fallen seriously ill, the doctors despaired, and Babur reportedly walked three times around Humayun's sickbed praying for the illness to be transferred to him. According to the chroniclers, Humayun recovered and Babur fell ill the same week, dying within months. Whether the prayer-transfer story is true or a later embroidery on a coincidence, Babur did die just as Humayun was recovering, and the story shaped Humayun's lifelong sense of his father as a near-saintly figure.

---

The **Baburnama** is one of the great autobiographies in world literature and deserves its own paragraph.

Babur kept a personal memoir written intermittently from 1494 (when he was eleven, recording his father's death and his own accession to Ferghana) through his Indian conquests in the late 1520s. He wrote in **Chagatai-Turkic** (his native language, the literary form of Turkic that the Timurid poet **Mir Ali Sher Nava'i** had recently established as a serious literary medium). He used a candid, observant, self-critical prose style that has no real parallel in contemporary royal memoirs. The book describes Babur's military campaigns, his political mistakes, his romantic entanglements (he writes about a same-sex attraction in his teenage years with the kind of matter-of-fact description that surprises modern readers expecting sixteenth-century reticence), his depressions, his alcohol consumption (he describes drinking heavily through much of his career, then giving up alcohol in 1527 in a sworn renunciation, then describing the renunciation with a wry awareness that he might break it), his observations of natural history (entire chapters describe the flora, fauna, climate, and geography of every region he passed through, with the precision of a working naturalist), and his reflections on the people he met and the books he read.

The Baburnama's value as a historical source is enormous — one of the most detailed primary accounts of Central Asian and northern Indian politics in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, written by a participant rather than a court chronicler. Its value as a literary work is also enormous. Modern scholars have compared it to the autobiographies of Saint Augustine, Benvenuto Cellini, and (occasionally) Marcus Aurelius. The English translation by Annette Beveridge in 1922 is the older standard reference; Wheeler Thackston's 1996 translation is the modern scholarly text.

What stands out to a reader coming to it cold is how little Babur sounds like a conqueror in the European tradition. He grumbles about the Indian climate (he hated it — too hot, no melons, no pomegranates, the wrong kind of bread). He misses Kabul constantly. He records minor military embarrassments alongside the great victories. He spends pages on the precise grades of melon available in different Ferghana villages. The voice is so warm and self-deprecating that it is genuinely difficult to reconcile with the man who beheaded Ibrahim Lodi at Panipat. Both are him.

---

What Babur left to his heirs was a strategic position rather than a consolidated empire.

He controlled Delhi, Agra, and a corridor of north Indian territory from the Punjab to Bihar. He had defeated the Lodis, the Rajputs, and the eastern Afghans, but none of those defeats had produced lasting submission. The Afghan nobility of the eastern Sultanate territories — Bihar and Bengal — were already plotting against Mughal rule and would shortly produce **Sher Shah Suri**, the most formidable opponent the dynasty would face for the next two centuries (Chapter 2). The Rajputs were militarily neutralized but politically restive. The provincial Sultanates of Gujarat, Malwa, Bengal, and the Deccan were still independent. The peninsular south — the great Hindu **Vijayanagara Empire** at its peak, Medieval India Ch 8 — was beyond any Mughal reach and would remain so until Akbar's expansion fifty years later.

What Babur did leave was the institutional template. The Persianate court culture, the Timurid architectural vocabulary (the four-iwan mosque plan, the chahar bagh quartered garden, the soaring blue-tiled domes that Timurid Herat had perfected, Timurid Empire Ch 7), the gunpowder army, the imperial title (Padshah), the dynastic claim (Timurid-Genghisid), and the personal memoir-writing tradition that his great-grandson Jahangir would continue (Chapter 4). Plus the cultural orientation toward Persian as the language of administration and Chagatai-Turkic as the language of the family. The Mughal Empire was Babur's institutional invention as much as it was a territorial state.

---

Cross-civ check: 1526, the year of Panipat, was an extraordinarily consequential year globally. **Suleiman the Magnificent** of the **Ottoman Empire** defeated Hungary at the **Battle of Mohacs** four months after Panipat (Ottoman Empire Ch 5), opening the way to Ottoman expansion into central Europe. The same year, **Charles V** of the **Holy Roman Empire** captured Pavia and held the Pope hostage at Rome (the Sack of Rome would follow in 1527). **Martin Luther** had been excommunicated five years earlier; the **Protestant Reformation** was visibly fragmenting Western Christianity. **Hernán Cortés** had completed the conquest of the **Aztec Empire** (Aztec Empire Ch 8) five years earlier, in 1521, and **Francisco Pizarro** was about to launch his expedition that would destroy the **Inca Empire** in 1533. The mid-1520s are the global pivot point at which gunpowder weapons, religious reformation, Atlantic trade, and centralized fiscal-military states transformed every major civilization simultaneously. Babur's victory at Panipat is a regional manifestation of the same phenomenon — the moment when a small, well-equipped, disciplined invading army defeated a large pre-gunpowder defending army using imported military technology.

The three great Islamic gunpowder empires of the early-modern Middle East and South Asia — the **Ottomans**, the **Safavids**, and the Mughals — were all founded or transformed within a single forty-year window: the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453 (Ottoman Empire Ch 1) and the eastern campaigns of Selim I 1514-17 (Ottoman Empire Ch 5), Ismail I's Safavid conquest of Iran 1501-10 (Safavid Persia Ch 2), and Babur's Panipat in 1526. The three dynasties are sometimes grouped as "the Islamic gunpowder empires" and the framing is genuinely useful — all three were Turkic-origin dynasties ruling Persianate court cultures, all three relied on imported gunpowder technology, and all three came to define the political-religious geography of the early-modern Islamic world. Their relationships were almost as important as their internal histories. Babur's Mughals were closer to the Safavids than to the Ottomans (the Mughal-Safavid border ran through Kandahar in modern Afghanistan, fought over for the next two centuries; the Mughal-Ottoman border did not exist, since the Safavids sat between them). All three dynasties peaked in the seventeenth century. All three declined together in the eighteenth.

The Mughal story is one of three concurrent versions of the same early-modern Islamic project. The most successful, in terms of wealth produced and territory ruled, was the Mughal version. The most institutionally durable was the Ottoman version, which lasted until 1922. The most religiously revolutionary was the Safavid version, which still defines the Iranian state today. The Mughals split the difference: enormously wealthy, enormously consequential, and ultimately the most fragile of the three.

# Chapter 2 — Humayun's Catastrophe and Recovery

The second Mughal emperor lost the dynasty.

His name was **Humayun** (hoo-MAH-yoon, "the fortunate one," a name that turned out to be ironic), Babur's eldest son, born in 1508 in Kabul, and he inherited the throne in December 1530 at age twenty-two. He held it for ten years, lost it completely to an Afghan general named **Sher Shah Suri**, spent fifteen years in exile in Persia and Kabul, recovered Delhi in 1555, and died six months later when he tripped on the steps of his library carrying an armful of books. The one Mughal emperor whose biography reads like a tragicomedy.

He is also one of the most consequential, despite the disasters, because his fifteen years of Persian exile seeded the Persianate cultural orientation that would define the Mughal court for the next two centuries.

---

Humayun's problems started immediately. Babur had divided the empire among his four sons in the Timurid tradition (each son got an appanage to govern, with the eldest holding the imperial title), which meant Humayun's brothers — **Kamran**, **Askari**, and **Hindal** — controlled large blocks of territory and could and did rebel against him repeatedly. Kamran got Kabul and the Punjab. Askari got Sambhal. Hindal got the southern districts. The brothers spent the 1530s alternating between fighting each other, fighting Humayun, and occasionally fighting jointly against external enemies. The Mughal Empire was, in the first generation after Babur, primarily a family civil war.

The external enemies arrived quickly. The **Sultanate of Gujarat** under **Bahadur Shah** (an unrelated namesake of the much later last Mughal emperor) attacked Mughal Malwa in 1535. Humayun campaigned successfully against him, seized Gujarat briefly, then had to abandon it when Bahadur Shah counterattacked while Humayun was distracted by his brothers. The pattern was characteristic. Humayun could win battles but not consolidate gains, because he kept having to leave half his army behind to deter his brothers.

But the existential threat came from the east. In 1537, an Afghan general from Bihar named **Sher Khan Suri** (later **Sher Shah Suri** when he claimed the imperial title) began consolidating the eastern Afghan Lodi remnants into a serious military force. Sher Shah was perhaps the most able military commander of sixteenth-century India, and he had a personal grievance against the Mughals: his father had served under the Lodis, his career had been disrupted by Babur's conquest, and he viewed the Mughals as foreign Turkic interlopers (the irony being that the Lodi Afghans were also relatively recent foreign conquerors, but a generation longer in residence than the Mughals). Sher Shah took Bihar, then Bengal, then started moving up the Ganges toward Delhi.

Humayun marched east to confront him. The two armies met at **Chausa** on the Ganges in **June 1539**, and Humayun's army was destroyed in a surprise night attack. Humayun himself escaped by swimming the Ganges (a porter helped him stay afloat and was rewarded with the throne for half a day, in one of the more peculiar Mughal anecdotes). The two armies met again at **Kannauj** in **May 1540**, and again Humayun's army was destroyed. This time there was no escape route. Sher Shah's victory at Kannauj was complete; the Mughal dynasty was driven out of India entirely.

---

The fifteen-year Sur interregnum is worth a brief tangent, because Sher Shah Suri's administrative reforms ended up shaping the Mughal Empire that eventually returned.

Sher Shah ruled north India for five years (1540-45) before dying in a gunpowder accident at the siege of **Kalinjar** fort in Bundelkhand. His successors — his son **Islam Shah** and various grandsons — held on for another decade until the Suri Sultanate disintegrated into civil wars between rival heirs. But Sher Shah's five years were administratively brilliant. He restored the **Grand Trunk Road** (the great east-west highway from Bengal to Kabul, with caravanserais at one-day intervals — a road in some form had existed since the Mauryan period, Maurya Empire Ch 4, but Sher Shah's restoration was the most thorough refurbishment in centuries). He systematized the land-revenue collection on the basis of a cadastral survey, fixing the assessment at one-third of the gross produce of each holding in cash. He standardized the silver **rupee** and the copper **dam** as the standard coinage of north India (the rupee would survive, in a direct lineal sense, into the modern Indian and Pakistani currencies of the same name). He built the **Rohtas Fort** in modern Pakistan and the great octagonal **Tomb of Sher Shah Suri** at Sasaram, both architectural masterpieces of pre-Mughal Indo-Islamic style.

Akbar's later administrative system (Chapter 3) would borrow nearly all of this — the road, the revenue assessment, the coinage, the standardized weights and measures. The Sur Sultanate is, in administrative terms, the immediate prequel to the Mughal Empire's classic structure. Sher Shah was, in the long run, more important for what he gave the Mughals than for what he took from them.

---

Where was Humayun during all of this?

After Kannauj in 1540, Humayun fled west toward the Indus, then across the Thar Desert, then south through Sindh, then back north through Rajasthan. The chronicles describe years of misery: small bands of loyal followers, defeats in skirmishes, near-starvation, the death of his wife's first son in infancy, the birth of his second son **Akbar** (AHK-bar) in 1542 in the Sindh desert at the village of **Umarkot** while Humayun was on the run from his own brother Kamran. (The future Akbar the Great was born in a tent at a Hindu vassal's outpost while his refugee father was being chased by his uncle.) By 1543, with all hope of reconquest from Indian soil exhausted, Humayun made the decision that would shape Mughal cultural history: he turned west and crossed into **Safavid Persia**.

He arrived at the Safavid court in 1544 and was received by **Shah Tahmasp I** (Safavid Persia Ch 3), the second Safavid Shah and one of the longest-reigning monarchs in Iranian history. Tahmasp received Humayun with imperial honors — banquets, hunting expeditions, robes of state, formal embraces — and then made his price clear. He would lend Humayun a Safavid army to recover his throne. In exchange, Humayun would publicly profess Twelver Shia Islam, permit Shia symbols on Mughal coinage, and cede the strategic city of **Kandahar** (in modern eastern Afghanistan, the gateway between Persia and India) to the Safavids permanently.

Humayun, who was a hereditary Sunni and not theologically Shia in any meaningful sense, accepted. The chronicles record his acceptance with various degrees of embarrassment — some sources have him tearfully agreeing, some have him cynically pragmatic, some skip the moment entirely. The terms were what they were. Humayun went through whatever public motions Tahmasp required. The Safavid loan of 12,000 troops and a senior commander followed.

---

The recovery campaign took eight years.

Humayun's Safavid-backed army recaptured **Kandahar** in 1545, immediately handed it over to Tahmasp's representative as agreed (and almost as immediately tried to reclaim it, since the actual cession had not been popular at the Mughal household), and then turned east into Afghanistan. Kabul was held by Humayun's brother Kamran, who had been quasi-independent throughout the interregnum and who fought a long and bitter war against Humayun's reentry into the family lands. The campaign for Kabul lasted from 1545 to 1553, with Kamran captured, blinded, and exiled to Mecca in the end. (The blinding of brothers was a Mughal innovation Humayun introduced reluctantly and that his successors would use freely; he reportedly wept when Kamran was blinded but accepted that he could not leave a politically dangerous brother alive and intact.)

By 1555, with Kabul secure and the Suri Sultanate visibly disintegrating into civil war between rival Suri claimants, Humayun was finally in a position to attempt the reconquest of India. He marched east through the Punjab, defeated a Suri army at **Sirhind** in June 1555, and entered **Delhi** in July of the same year. The dynasty was back. Humayun had done it — improbably, after fifteen years of exile — and at age forty-seven was poised to begin the actual rebuilding of the empire his father had founded.

He was emperor for six months.

On **January 24, 1556**, Humayun was descending the steep stairs of his library at Sher Mandal in Delhi (the library Sher Shah Suri had built and Humayun had reclaimed) carrying an armful of books. The muezzin called the prayer; Humayun, devout, paused on the step to kneel; he caught his foot in his robe; he fell down the stone stairs; he hit his head and never regained consciousness. He died three days later. The dynasty's improbable recovery, the fifteen years of exile, the desert-tent birth of Akbar, the Persian alliance, the family civil wars, and the painstaking eight-year reconquest all ended in a fatal trip down a flight of stairs.

The family chronicler **Gulbadan Begum** (Humayun's half-sister) recorded the death with a kind of stunned simplicity: he stumbled, he fell, he died. The British historian Stanley Lane-Poole would write four centuries later, in a sentence that has become famous in Mughal historiography: *"He stumbled out of life as he had stumbled through it."* The judgment is unfair to Humayun, who held the dynasty together against extraordinary odds and recovered the throne after a defeat that would have ended any other dynastic claim, but it captures something true about the texture of his life. He was unlucky in ways that were genuinely his fault and in ways that were not, and the death by stairs is the perfect coda.

---

Humayun's Persian exile shaped the Mughal Empire for the next two centuries, in ways that were not yet visible at his death.

The most direct effect was institutional: a wave of Persian scholars, painters, calligraphers, architects, and administrators followed Humayun back to India in 1555, drawn by his patronage and by the promise of imperial employment. Two of these were the painters **Mir Sayyid Ali** and **Abd al-Samad**, both trained at the Safavid royal atelier in Tabriz under the master **Bihzad** (the dominant Persian miniature painter of the late Timurid and early Safavid courts, Timurid Empire Ch 7). The two of them set up the Mughal painting workshop under Humayun and trained the founding generation of Mughal court painters, including the masters who would work under Akbar (Chapter 3). The greatest visual art tradition of pre-modern India was seeded by two Safavid refugees Humayun brought back.

The second effect was cultural-linguistic. Persian had been the administrative language of the Delhi Sultanate (Delhi Sultanate Ch 6) and would have been the Mughal court language anyway, but Humayun's exile turned Persian from "the language of administration" into "the language of high culture." The Mughal court read Persian poetry, wrote Persian memoirs, commissioned Persian historical chronicles, and conducted formal correspondence in elaborate Persian rhetoric, for the next two and a half centuries. The Mughal-Safavid cultural relationship (geopolitical rivals, cultural cousins) was established by Humayun's fifteen-year residence at Tahmasp's court. Even Aurangzeb, the most religiously orthodox of the Mughals (Chapter 6), wrote his memoirs in Persian and corresponded in Persian rhetoric with the Safavid Shahs.

The third effect was architectural. **Humayun's Tomb** at Delhi — built by Humayun's widow **Hamida Banu Begum** in 1572, designed by the Persian architect **Mirak Mirza Ghiyas**, modeled directly on Timurid royal tomb traditions — was the first great Mughal architectural project and the model for everything that followed. Its red sandstone-and-white marble construction, its central domed chamber, its quartered chahar bagh garden setting, its octagonal floor plan — all of these became the template for Mughal imperial architecture from Humayun's Tomb (1572) through Akbar's Tomb at Sikandra (1613), through the Tomb of Itimad-ud-Daulah at Agra (1628), and finally through the **Taj Mahal** itself (1653, Chapter 5). Without Humayun's Persian exile, there would be no Mughal architectural style as the world recognizes it. The Taj Mahal is, in a real architectural-genealogical sense, the great-great-granddaughter of Humayun's stairs.

---

What Humayun left to Akbar was a small empire with a cultural orientation.

The actual territorial inheritance was modest: Delhi, Agra, parts of the Punjab, and a thin corridor of northern Indian land. The Suri Sultanate's collapse meant most of the eastern Sultanate territories were up for grabs. Most of the provincial Sultanates (Gujarat, Malwa, Bengal, the Deccan Sultanates) were independent. Vijayanagara was at peak in the south. The Mughal portion of India in 1556 was perhaps a quarter of what it would be at Akbar's death in 1605.

But Akbar was thirteen years old in January 1556 when his father died. The dynasty's survival depended on the regency that would now have to govern the empire on his behalf. The next chapter is mostly about how that regency, then the adult Akbar, took the small inheritance Humayun left and built it into the wealthiest empire in the world.

Cross-civ check: Humayun's recovery and death (1555-56) coincided with several globally significant moments. **Charles V** of the **Holy Roman Empire** abdicated in 1556, the year Humayun died, splitting the Habsburg Empire between his son Philip II of Spain and his brother Ferdinand I of Austria. **Suleiman the Magnificent** of the Ottoman Empire was mid-reign; the Ottoman peak was just past (Ottoman Empire Ch 6). Ivan the Terrible of Russia was conquering Kazan (1552) and Astrakhan (1556) — bringing the Russian state to the Caspian for the first time. **Mary I** of England was in the second year of her brief Catholic restoration; the Protestant Reformation was visibly destabilizing northern Europe. The **Ming Dynasty** in China was about to suffer the great earthquake of 1556 in Shaanxi (the deadliest earthquake in recorded human history, perhaps 830,000 deaths). Akbar's accession, in this context, was a small event in a globally turbulent year — but the dynasty he was about to build would, within fifty years, become one of the largest economies on the planet.

# Chapter 3 — Akbar the Great

The thirteen-year-old emperor who succeeded Humayun in 1556 ruled for forty-nine years. He expanded the Mughal Empire from a thin north Indian corridor into a state covering most of the subcontinent. He was illiterate (the historians are confident on this point — Akbar could not read) but ran one of the most sophisticated state administrations in the early-modern world. He abolished the **jizya** poll tax on non-Muslims, married Rajput princesses, founded a syncretic religion that was personal devotion and state propaganda in equal measure, and built **Fatehpur Sikri** — a complete planned royal city that he abandoned after fourteen years because it ran out of water. He is, by consensus of modern scholarship, the most consequential pre-modern Indian ruler since Ashoka eighteen centuries earlier.

He is also one of the more contested figures in modern South Asian history. To Indian secular historians, Akbar is the model of pluralistic governance — the Hindu-Muslim synthesis, the religious tolerance, the integration of the Rajput nobility into Mughal service. To Hindu nationalists, the same record looks different — a foreign Muslim monarch whose tolerance was self-serving political calculation rather than principled pluralism. To Pakistani historians, Akbar is a slightly embarrassing ancestor — too syncretic, too friendly to Hindus, insufficiently Islamic. The historical record genuinely supports all three readings, depending on what you emphasize.

What no one disputes is that Akbar's forty-nine-year reign produced the institutional structure of the Mughal Empire. Everything that came afterward — Jahangir's painting court, Shah Jahan's monumental architecture, Aurangzeb's imperial overreach, the whole eighteenth-century decline — happened inside the political and administrative system Akbar built.

---

**Bairam Khan** (BAY-ram khahn, the Turkmen general who had served Babur and Humayun) was Akbar's regent for the first four years of the reign. He was an able commander and a loyal one — without Bairam Khan, the dynasty would not have survived 1556 — but he was also forty-five years older than Akbar and increasingly reluctant to hand over real power as Akbar grew up.

The first immediate crisis was a Hindu general named **Hemu**.

Hemu had served the Suri Sultans through their decline. In late 1556, while the Mughal court was distracted by Humayun's death and the new regency, Hemu marched on Delhi, defeated the small Mughal garrison there, and proclaimed himself **Vikramaditya** — adopting an ancient Hindu imperial title (the legendary king-name from Gupta-period mythology, Gupta Empire Ch 3) and presenting himself as the restorer of Hindu rule at Delhi after three and a half centuries of Muslim sultans. His month-long reign was the most serious Hindu political claim to Delhi the Sultanate-Mughal era ever produced.

Bairam Khan and the boy Akbar marched south to confront him. The two armies met at **Panipat** on **November 5, 1556** — the same plain where Babur had defeated Ibrahim Lodi thirty years earlier. The **Second Battle of Panipat** was decided when an arrow struck Hemu in the eye while he was leading from the front. He fell unconscious; his army, demoralized, broke and fled. The Mughals captured Hemu, brought him before Akbar, and Bairam Khan offered the boy emperor the symbolic ritual of striking the enemy's neck (a Mughal coming-of-age ceremony of sorts). Akbar, according to the chronicles, refused to strike a wounded man; Bairam Khan then beheaded Hemu himself. The execution was in the standard sixteenth-century pattern; the boy emperor's reluctance was reportedly noted at the time as a sign of unusual character.

By 1560, Akbar was eighteen and ready to govern in his own right. He dismissed Bairam Khan with elaborate honors, sent him on the Hajj pilgrimage, and ruled directly. Bairam Khan never reached Mecca. He was assassinated en route in Gujarat by an Afghan with a personal grudge from a long-ago military campaign. Akbar absorbed Bairam Khan's young son **Abdul Rahim Khan-i-Khanan** into his own household; Abdul Rahim grew up to become one of the great Mughal nobles of Akbar's reign and a celebrated Hindi-Persian bilingual poet.

---

Akbar's first major political innovation was the **Rajput marriage alliance**.

In **1562**, Akbar married a princess of the **Kachhwaha** Rajput dynasty of **Amber** (modern Jaipur in Rajasthan). The bride was the daughter of the Raja of Amber, **Bharmal** — a relatively minor Rajput ruler who had decided that submission to Mughal authority was preferable to resistance. The marriage was the first of many. Over the next four decades, Akbar married princesses from most of the senior Rajput houses (Amber, Bikaner, Jaisalmer, Marwar) and integrated the Rajput princes themselves into the Mughal nobility as senior **mansabdars** (ranked imperial officers). The Rajput rulers retained their own kingdoms, paid tribute and provided military service to the Mughal Emperor, and sent their daughters to the imperial harem. Their sons served as senior Mughal generals — most famously **Raja Man Singh I** of Amber, who commanded Akbar's eastern campaigns and rose to be one of the highest-ranking nobles in the empire.

The political significance of the Rajput alliance was enormous. The Rajputs were the most formidable Hindu military power in north India — they had resisted the Delhi Sultanate for three centuries (Delhi Sultanate Ch 7), and Babur had defeated but not subdued Rana Sanga (Chapter 1). By integrating them as Mughal allies, Akbar transformed his most dangerous potential opponents into his most reliable military lieutenants. The Mughal armies that conquered Gujarat (1573), Bengal (1576), and Sindh (1591) were as Rajput as they were Turkic-Persian.

The cultural significance was equally large. The Akbar-era Mughal court became the first major pre-modern Indian state to systematically integrate Hindu and Muslim elites into a single political class. Hindu princes attended Mughal court ceremonies. Hindu deities appeared in Mughal court paintings. Persian-language Mughal poetry was matched by Hindi-language Bhakti devotional poetry composed and performed at the same court (Akbar's court poet **Tulsidas** wrote the Hindi **Ramcharitmanas**, the most influential vernacular retelling of the Ramayana in north Indian Hinduism). The Hindi-Persian-Turkic-Sanskrit cultural synthesis the Delhi Sultanate had begun (Delhi Sultanate Ch 6) reached its institutional climax under Akbar.

The Hindu-nationalist counter-reading of all this is that Akbar's tolerance was political calculation rather than principled inclusion. The principle is moot. What matters is that the political calculation was systematic, durable, and effective enough to govern a Hindu majority for fifty years through a Hindu-Muslim ruling coalition — which no previous Indian Muslim state had managed at this scale.

---

The **jizya** abolition in **1564** was the single boldest religious-political move of Akbar's reign.

The jizya was the per-capita tax on non-Muslim subjects that Islamic states had collected since the early caliphates. The doctrinal justification was that non-Muslims under Muslim rule were **dhimmis** (protected subjects) who paid the tax in exchange for the state's protection of their religious freedom and exemption from military service. In practice, the jizya was both a revenue source and a status marker — it formally distinguished the Muslim minority (full subjects) from the Hindu majority (subordinate but tolerated). The Delhi Sultanate had collected the jizya throughout its 320 years.

Akbar abolished it. The order went out in 1564, eight years into his reign, while he was still consolidating his rule. The orthodox Sunni clergy (the **ulama**) at Delhi and Agra protested loudly. The decision lost the state a meaningful revenue stream. It also signaled, unmistakably, that the Mughal Empire was redefining its relationship with its non-Muslim subjects. Hindus were no longer dhimmis paying for the privilege of practicing their religion in a Muslim state. They were equal subjects of the Mughal emperor, owing the same revenue obligations and entitled to the same civil status as the Muslim minority.

This was, for sixteenth-century Islamic political theory, radical. No previous major Islamic state had done it on this scale. The orthodox response was that Akbar was not really a Muslim — that he had abandoned Sharia for personal preference. The **Fatawa-i-Alamgiri** that Aurangzeb would commission a century later (Chapter 6) explicitly reversed the abolition; **Aurangzeb reimposed the jizya in 1679** as part of his return to orthodox Sunni state policy. The hundred-fifteen-year window of jizya non-collection (1564-1679) is the institutional core of what later historians have called the "Mughal Hindu-Muslim synthesis." Both its existence and its later reversal are central to understanding Mughal religious policy.

---

**Fatehpur Sikri** is the other great Akbar-era project that requires its own treatment.

In **1571**, Akbar decided to build a new royal capital next to the village of **Sikri**, twenty-three miles southwest of Agra. The site had no obvious strategic or commercial logic — it was inland, away from major trade routes, and lacked a reliable water source. The motivation was personal and religious. Akbar had been having serious problems producing a male heir (his early sons had all died in infancy), and a Sufi saint at Sikri named **Salim Chishti** had reportedly predicted that the empress would bear a healthy son. When she did — the boy who would be **Jahangir**, born in 1569 and named **Salim** for the Sufi saint — Akbar built the new capital partly as a votive offering. The chronicles call it **Fatehpur** ("city of victory") to commemorate the conquest of Gujarat in 1573, but the site is identical with Sikri.

The construction took fourteen years (1571-85) and produced one of the most architecturally significant pre-modern royal cities anywhere. The buildings were red sandstone — the local material — quarried from outcrops on the site itself. The architectural style synthesized Persian, Timurid, and Indian elements: the **Buland Darwaza** (the great triumphal gateway, 175 feet high, the largest gateway in the world at the time), the **Diwan-i-Khas** (the audience hall with its famous central pillar with branching brackets, like a stone tree supporting the emperor's throne), the **Panch Mahal** (the five-story open pavilion overlooking the harem courtyards), the **Tomb of Salim Chishti** (the white-marble shrine of the Sufi saint, set as a jewel in the red-sandstone courtyard of the great mosque), the **Anup Talao** (the central pool where Akbar reportedly heard music sung by Tansen, his court musician). Every element of the architectural vocabulary was synthesizing — Hindu temple bracket-systems applied to Islamic palace buildings, Timurid four-iwan plans redrawn in Indian sandstone, Hindu symbolic geometry framing Persian inscriptional Arabic.

And then Akbar abandoned it. By 1585, the city was fully built, occupied, working — and the water supply had failed. The wells were inadequate, the artificial reservoirs lost too much to evaporation, and the surrounding countryside could not produce enough food to feed the imperial court at the volume Akbar wanted. The capital moved to **Lahore** for the next dozen years (Akbar campaigned in the northwest from there) and then to **Agra**. Fatehpur Sikri was left intact, mostly empty, periodically used as a hunting lodge or a way-station, slowly weathering in the Indian sun for the next four hundred years.

It is now a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most photographed Indian monuments after the Taj Mahal. Its architectural significance lies precisely in its abandonment — because nobody lived in Fatehpur Sikri after 1585, the buildings survived essentially unmodified, and the late-sixteenth-century Mughal architectural vocabulary is preserved there in cleaner form than anywhere else in India.

A note on the architectural choices: every major design decision at Fatehpur Sikri had a purpose. The Buland Darwaza's enormous height was deliberate — it was meant to humble visitors, with an Arabic inscription declaring "the world is a bridge, pass over it but build no houses upon it." The branching central pillar in the Diwan-i-Khas placed the emperor literally above all four corners of the room, with senior nobles seated below him on radiating walkways. The location of the Tomb of Salim Chishti at the center of the great mosque courtyard inverted the usual mosque hierarchy — the saint, not the prayer-niche, was the symbolic center. The synthesis of Hindu and Islamic architectural elements was not decorative; it was a programmatic statement about the empire's identity as a Hindu-Muslim joint sovereignty under a Muslim emperor.

---

The most controversial of Akbar's projects was **Din-i-Ilahi** (deen-ee-ee-LAH-ee), the syncretic religion he founded in **1582**.

The background was the **Ibadat Khana** ("House of Worship"), a building Akbar had constructed at Fatehpur Sikri in **1575** for religious debates. He convened scholars from every major faith available at his court — Sunni clerics, Shia scholars, Hindu pandits, Jain monks, Parsi (Zoroastrian) priests, and Portuguese Jesuits sent up from the Goa colony — and made them argue in his presence on Thursday evenings. The debates were minutes. The participants prepared scholarly papers. Akbar (illiterate, but with an extraordinary memory) listened and questioned. The Jesuit accounts in particular — which survive in detailed letters back to Goa and Rome — describe Akbar as exceptionally intelligent, completely uninterested in conversion to any specific religion, and pointed in his theological questions. He was clearly trying to figure out what he himself believed.

What he eventually settled on was **Din-i-Ilahi**, the "Divine Faith." It was not a mass religion. It had perhaps thirty initiated members, all senior Mughal courtiers, and it never spread outside the palace. Its tenets were a synthesis of monotheism (one God, called by various names — Allah, Brahman, Ahura Mazda — in different traditions), abstinence from beef (Hindu), abstinence from pork (Muslim), reverence for fire and the sun (Zoroastrian), abstinence from violence to small creatures (Jain), and a sun-salutation devotional practice that Akbar performed daily. The initiated members signed loyalty oaths to Akbar personally as the spiritual master of the order. The whole thing was, in its actual operation, a kind of personal cult of the emperor as spiritual head of state.

Modern scholarly opinion is divided on what to make of Din-i-Ilahi. The maximum reading is that Akbar genuinely believed himself to be a syncretic religious teacher and saw the Mughal Empire as a Hindu-Muslim universal monarchy that needed a unifying state religion. The minimum reading is that Din-i-Ilahi was a political device to bind senior nobles to him personally through religious oaths, with the syncretic content as window-dressing. Most scholars now suggest something in between — Akbar was personally interested in the comparative study of religions, did believe in some kind of universal monotheism, and used Din-i-Ilahi simultaneously as personal devotion and as political instrument. The religion died with him in 1605. None of his successors maintained it.

The orthodox Sunni reaction to Din-i-Ilahi was outrage. The most consequential opponent was **Sheikh Ahmad Sirhindi** (1564-1624), a Naqshbandi Sufi reformer who argued that Akbar had crossed the line from Islamic monarch to apostate, and that orthodox Sunni Islam needed to reassert itself against the Mughal court's drift toward syncretism. Sirhindi's letters, the **Maktubat-i-Imam Rabbani**, became the foundational texts of South Asian Sunni revivalism for the next four centuries. Aurangzeb's later religious policy (Chapter 6) is in some respects a Sirhindi-tradition response to the Akbarian syncretism. The intellectual lineage from Sirhindi through Shah Waliullah of Delhi (eighteenth century) and ultimately to twentieth-century South Asian Islamist movements is direct and traceable. Akbar's Din-i-Ilahi inadvertently produced its own institutional opposition.

---

The administrative genius of Akbar's reign was the **mansabdari** system, formalized in **1577**.

Every senior Mughal officer was assigned a numerical rank (from 10 to 10,000) called a **mansab** (literally "rank" or "office"). The rank specified two things: the officer's salary, and the number of cavalry he was required to maintain at his own expense and bring to imperial service when summoned. A mansabdar of 5,000, for example, drew a salary commensurate with that rank and was required to keep 5,000 cavalry ready for service. (The actual cavalry counts were sometimes adjusted — a "5,000 zat / 3,000 sawar" notation meant rank 5,000 in personal status, with an obligation of 3,000 actual horsemen, the difference being administrative gradation.)

The mansabs were paid in two main forms. Cash salaries were drawn directly from the imperial treasury. **Jagirs** (jah-GEERS, land revenue assignments) gave the mansabdar the right to collect the land revenue of a specific district, in lieu of cash, with the obligation to pass on any surplus above his entitled salary back to the imperial treasury. Jagirs were not hereditary — they were reassigned periodically, and a mansabdar might hold one jagir this year and a completely different one next year. This nonhereditary feature was central to mansabdari design: it prevented the formation of regional landed aristocracies that could resist central authority. (The contrast with European feudalism was deliberate; medieval European kings could not reassign their dukes' fiefs the way Mughal emperors could reassign their mansabdars' jagirs. The Mughal system preserved central control much more effectively.)

The mansabdari was layered on top of **Todar Mal's revenue settlement** of **1582** — Akbar's finance minister Raja Todar Mal completed a comprehensive cadastral survey of agricultural land, fixed the standard land-revenue assessment at one-third of the gross produce in cash, and standardized weights, measures, and the silver rupee. The combined system produced a remarkable fiscal-military state. Akbar's empire could deploy more cavalry than any European or Middle Eastern state of the period. It could collect land revenue with precision and consistency that Europe would not match until the seventeenth century. And it tied together a multi-ethnic, multi-religious nobility (Turkic mansabdars, Persian mansabdars, Rajput mansabdars, Afghan mansabdars, occasional Indian Muslim or even Christian mansabdars) into a single chain of imperial command in a way no previous Indian state had managed.

The system survived essentially unchanged through Aurangzeb (Chapter 6) and substantially intact into the eighteenth-century decline (Chapter 7). The British East India Company, when it took over Bengal after Plassey (Chapter 8), inherited the mansabdari machinery and modified it gradually rather than replacing it. The institutional skeleton of the late Mughal state was substantially the institutional skeleton of early British India.

---

The **Akbarnama** is the chronicle of all this, and it deserves a paragraph of its own.

**Abu'l Fazl** (ah-BUL fazl, 1551-1602) was Akbar's court historian, friend, and confidant — the third of three brothers (the others were the courtier Faizi and the poet **Faizi**) who had served Akbar from his early reign. Abu'l Fazl was a Persian-language scholar of extraordinary range, fluent in Sanskrit and Hindi as well as Persian and Arabic, with broad reading in Islamic philosophy and Indian religious texts. Akbar commissioned him to write the official chronicle of the reign, and Abu'l Fazl produced a three-volume work — the **Akbarnama** proper — plus an appendix called the **Ain-i-Akbari**.

The Akbarnama is the standard narrative of Akbar's reign — campaigns, court ceremonies, religious debates, building projects, family events. It is also, in places, a piece of imperial hagiography (Akbar appears as a near-divine ruler whose every decision is wise, every battle inevitable, every policy beneficial). Modern scholars have to read it skeptically as a source. But it is also extraordinarily detailed, by far the most detailed surviving primary account of the reign, and on questions where Abu'l Fazl had no reason to lie (the routine administration, the dates of campaigns, the personnel of the court) it remains the basic factual reference.

The **Ain-i-Akbari** (literally "the institutions of Akbar") is more remarkable. It is a comprehensive administrative survey of the empire — provinces, sub-provinces, districts, populations, revenue assessments, military strengths, prices of standard commodities, costs of imperial workshop products (including the price of every type of paint pigment used in the painting workshop, the wages of painters by skill rank, and a detailed list of mansabdars by name and rank). For modern economic and administrative historians of the early-modern world, the Ain-i-Akbari is one of the most valuable single documents in existence — a detailed statistical snapshot of a major early-modern economy, produced by a contemporary administrator with access to the records.

Abu'l Fazl was assassinated in **1602** by a Rajput bandit named **Vir Singh** Bundela, almost certainly on the orders of Akbar's son and rival **Salim** (the future Jahangir, Chapter 4), who hated Abu'l Fazl as the chronicler-confidant who reinforced Akbar's preference for Salim's deceased elder brother. Akbar mourned Abu'l Fazl publicly and never forgave Salim for the murder, though the formal reconciliation was eventually patched together. The killing was emblematic of the late-Akbar court — the syncretic reign producing internal feuds that the imperial succession would inherit.

---

Akbar died at Agra on **October 27, 1605**. He was sixty-three. He had reigned for forty-nine years. The empire he left behind extended from Kabul in the northwest, across the entire Ganges valley, south through Gujarat and Malwa, into the Deccan as far as Khandesh and northern Berar, and east to the borders of Bengal — perhaps two-thirds of the modern Indian and Pakistani territory. The state revenue was an estimated 99 million rupees per year — by far the largest in the contemporary world, larger than the combined revenues of France, Spain, England, and the Ottoman Empire. The imperial nobility numbered around 1,800 mansabdars, drawn from Turkic, Persian, Afghan, Rajput, and Indian Muslim families in roughly equal proportions. The painting workshop had produced thousands of illustrated manuscripts. The historiographical project had produced the Akbarnama and Ain-i-Akbari. The administrative system had been rationalized and standardized to a degree no previous Indian state had achieved.

His tomb is at **Sikandra**, on the Agra-Delhi road, and it is one of the more architecturally peculiar Mughal monuments — a five-tiered pyramidal structure, mostly red sandstone, with a top-tier marble pavilion containing a false grave (the actual burial chamber is below ground level, the false grave above it, the architectural sleight-of-hand a standard Mughal device). The design represents Akbar's late-reign cultural eclecticism: the lower tiers are Indo-Islamic, the upper pavilion is Hindu-derived, the inscriptions include phrases from Din-i-Ilahi alongside the Quranic verses. It is the most visibly syncretic Mughal imperial tomb. Whether you read that as a fitting tribute to the syncretic emperor or as the worst architectural compromise in Mughal history depends on what you think of the syncretic emperor.

Cross-civ check: Akbar (1556-1605) and **Elizabeth I** of England (1558-1603) were almost exact contemporaries and the comparison is genuinely instructive. Both inherited unstable thrones at young ages (Akbar at 13, Elizabeth at 25). Both ruled long, transformational reigns at the head of states their predecessors had nearly lost. Both managed religious-confessional crises through pragmatic compromise rather than principled commitment (Akbar's syncretism vs. Elizabeth's Anglican via media). Both presided over cultural florescence (the Akbarnama and the Mughal painting workshop vs. Shakespeare and the Elizabethan theatre). Both were charismatic monarchs who built personal cults around themselves while running effective institutional administrations. The economic comparison is lopsided: Akbar's empire's revenue around 1600 was roughly thirty times Elizabeth's. India was, by every available measure of pre-modern wealth, the dominant economy of the early-modern world, with Mughal-controlled north India alone larger than any single European state. Elizabeth's England was, by comparison, a marginal northern European principality. The post-1600 trajectory would invert this enormously, but at the moment of Akbar's death, the wealth gap between the two crowns was as wide as the wealth gap between modern Switzerland and Bangladesh — running, this time, in the Mughal direction.

The other concurrent monarch worth comparing is **Abbas I the Great** of Safavid Persia (1588-1629), who reigned at the same time and on the same model — centralizing administration, religious-political assertion, monumental city-building (Akbar's Fatehpur Sikri vs. Abbas's Isfahan, Safavid Persia Ch 4), absorption of culturally-distinct mercenary populations into the imperial military (Akbar's Rajput marriages vs. Abbas's Caucasian ghulam corps). The Mughal-Safavid relationship was the dominant cultural and political relationship of the early-modern Persianate world. Akbar and Abbas exchanged embassies, gifts, painters, calligraphers, and military theorists. Both empires reached their cultural and economic peaks in roughly the same generation. Both would decline together a century later (Chapter 7, Safavid Persia Ch 7). The "two great Persianate empires" is the right framework for thinking about late-sixteenth-century Mughal India — and Akbar is the figure who built the Mughal half of the equivalence.

# Chapter 4 — Jahangir and Nur Jahan

The fourth Mughal emperor was, by the consensus of every primary source that mentions him, an addict.

His name was **Salim** (sah-LEEM), born in 1569 to Akbar and the Kachhwaha Rajput princess of Amber, named for the Sufi saint **Salim Chishti** of Sikri who had reportedly predicted his birth (Chapter 3). He took the regnal name **Jahangir** (jah-hahn-GEER, "world-seizer") when he succeeded his father in **1605** at age thirty-six. He reigned for twenty-two years, until his death in **1627**. The empire he inherited was, on paper, the largest and wealthiest state on Earth. The empire he ruled was, in practical terms, run for most of his reign by his Persian wife **Nur Jahan** (noor jah-HAHN, "light of the world").

Jahangir is one of the more honest narrators of his own reign. His memoir, the **Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri**, written in Persian, describes his alcohol consumption, his opium addiction, his moods, his pleasure in painting and natural history, his political decisions, and his progressive incapacity for the actual work of governance, with a candor that has no parallel in seventeenth-century royal autobiography. He is also a more interesting cultural figure than the addict-summary suggests — Mughal painting reached its absolute peak under his patronage, the empire he inherited held together better than it had any structural reason to, and his marriage to Nur Jahan produced the most genuinely powerful Mughal empress and one of the more remarkable political partnerships in pre-modern Asian history.

But the addict description is also accurate. He acknowledged it himself.

---

The reign began with a rebellion.

Salim had rebelled against Akbar in 1599-1601, partly because Akbar's clear preference was for Salim's elder brother **Daniyal** as heir, partly because Salim wanted real power before Akbar was ready to delegate it. The rebellion was halfhearted — Salim set up a parallel court at Allahabad and minted his own coins for a few years, then submitted when Akbar moved against him. Akbar forgave him, mostly because Daniyal had died of alcoholism in 1604 (the third of Akbar's three sons to die of alcohol-related causes — the other was **Murad**, who died at thirty in 1599) and Salim was now the only surviving heir.

The succession in 1605 was therefore uncontested but resentful. Akbar's senior nobles distrusted Salim. Salim distrusted Akbar's senior nobles. The first major political event of the reign was the rebellion of Salim's eldest son **Khusrau** in 1606 — the eighteen-year-old grandson, briefly the candidate of an anti-Salim party at court that thought Khusrau would be a more pliable emperor, raised an army and was decisively defeated near Lahore. Jahangir blinded Khusrau as punishment (the first use of blinding-of-rivals as Mughal succession discipline) and held him in confinement for the next sixteen years until Khusrau's eventual murder in 1622, almost certainly arranged by Jahangir's son **Khurram** (the future Shah Jahan, Chapter 5) to clear a rival from the succession.

This is the texture of the Jahangir-era court — succession intrigue, blinding and confinement of rival princes, periodic outbursts of paranoid violence interspersed with lengthy periods of imperial inactivity. The empire kept running because the institutional structure Akbar had built (the mansabdari system, Todar Mal's revenue settlement, the standardized provincial administration, Chapter 3) was robust enough to function with minimal central direction.

---

**Nur Jahan** entered the story in **1611**.

She was born **Mehr-un-Nissa** (meh-roon-NEE-sah, "sun among women") in 1577 in **Kandahar** to a Persian noble family that had migrated to India during Akbar's reign. Her father **Mirza Ghias Beg** rose at Akbar's court to become a senior mansabdar; he received the title **Itimad-ud-Daulah** ("pillar of the state") and served Jahangir as chief revenue minister. Nur Jahan was first married in 1594 to a Persian-born Mughal officer named **Sher Afgan**, who served as governor of Bardhaman in Bengal. The marriage produced one daughter (Ladli Begum, who would later be married into the imperial family for political reasons). In 1607, Sher Afgan was killed in a confrontation with the Mughal governor of Bengal, **Qutb-ud-din Khan Koka**, in circumstances that Mughal sources describe variously and that have been the subject of historical speculation ever since (was Sher Afgan plotting rebellion? Was the killing arranged by Jahangir to free his eye for Mehr-un-Nissa? The honest answer is that the surviving evidence is ambiguous).

Mehr-un-Nissa was widowed at thirty and brought to the imperial harem at Agra as a lady-in-waiting to Jahangir's stepmother. She and Jahangir met in 1611 — accounts vary on the circumstances, but one version has them meeting at a palace bazaar where Jahangir came across her selling textiles. They married in May 1611. She was thirty-four; Jahangir was forty-two. He gave her the title **Nur Mahal** ("light of the palace") and within a few years promoted her to **Nur Jahan** ("light of the world").

What followed was the most powerful position any Mughal empress would ever hold.

---

By **1614**, Nur Jahan was effectively running the empire.

Jahangir's increasing alcohol and opium consumption made him incapable of sustained administrative work. He still presided at court ceremonies, still made the formal decisions, still received ambassadors and dispensed justice in the **jharokha-i-darshan** (the ceremonial window appearance). But the actual flow of decisions — appointment of mansabdars, allocation of jagirs, military planning, diplomatic correspondence — passed through Nur Jahan's apartments. She convened her own council of senior nobles (her father Itimad-ud-Daulah, her brother Asaf Khan, several other Persian-faction officers). She issued imperial orders in her own name through a stamped seal called a **mohr** that Jahangir had granted her. She struck imperial coinage with her name on it — a privilege no other Mughal woman ever received, and an unmistakable signal of her actual sovereign authority. She negotiated military deployments. She arranged dynastic marriages.

The most consequential of those marriages was Khurram (the future Shah Jahan) to Nur Jahan's niece **Mumtaz Mahal** in 1612 (Chapter 5). The marriage initially aligned Nur Jahan and Khurram politically, but the alignment broke down in the 1620s when Nur Jahan tried to secure the succession for her own son-in-law (the marginal prince **Shahryar**, married to Nur Jahan's daughter from her first marriage). The Khurram-vs-Shahryar succession struggle dominated the last years of Jahangir's reign, with Nur Jahan steadily losing influence to Khurram's faction.

The political fact of Nur Jahan's de facto rule is well-documented. She was the only Mughal empress to wield this kind of executive power. The structural reason was Jahangir's incapacity, but the personal reason was Nur Jahan's own competence — by every contemporary account, she was a better administrator than Jahangir had ever been. The English ambassador **Sir Thomas Roe** (who arrived at the Mughal court in 1615 and stayed three years, see below) wrote home that the empress "governs him and wynds him up at her pleasure," with a mixture of bemusement and reluctant respect.

The dramatic high point of Nur Jahan's career came in **1626**, when a rebellious noble named **Mahabat Khan** kidnapped Jahangir on a riverbank during a march from Lahore to Kabul. Nur Jahan, traveling separately with her own contingent, organized an immediate counter-attack. She personally rode an armored elephant into the river to attempt a rescue (the elephant was wounded, the rescue failed), then negotiated for several months until she could engineer Jahangir's release through a combination of force and bribery. The episode is in every contemporary chronicle — a fifty-year-old empress leading an armed rescue of the captured emperor — and it captures something genuinely impressive about Nur Jahan as a political figure.

---

**Mughal painting** reached its peak under Jahangir's patronage, and the achievement deserves its own paragraph.

The painting workshop Akbar had founded under the two refugee Tabriz masters **Mir Sayyid Ali** and **Abd al-Samad** (Chapter 3) had produced thousands of illustrated manuscripts during Akbar's reign — the **Hamzanama** (over 1,400 illustrations of the legendary tales of Hamza ibn Abdul-Muttalib, Chapter 3), the Akbarnama paintings, illustrated Persian translations of Hindu epics. The Akbar-era style was crowded, narrative, polychromatic, with every painting telling a story across multiple figures.

Jahangir's painting was different. He preferred single-figure compositions — naturalistic portraits of individual courtiers, animal and bird studies, landscape studies. The court masters of his reign — **Ustad Mansur** (the great animal-painter), **Bishan Das** (the great portraitist), **Abu'l Hasan** (the favored personal painter, whom Jahangir titled **Nadir-uz-Zaman**, "wonder of the age"), **Manohar Das**, **Govardhan**, several others — produced work that was technically extraordinary by any standard. Mansur's painting of the **dodo** (the extinct flightless bird from Mauritius, brought to the Mughal court via Portuguese traders) is the most accurate surviving image of a living dodo. His painting of a Bengal florican is a study in feather-by-feather realism that anticipates eighteenth-century European natural-history illustration. Bishan Das's portrait of Shah Abbas the Safavid (drawn during a Mughal embassy to Isfahan in 1613, see Safavid Persia Ch 4) is one of the few life-from-life portraits of Abbas that survives.

The Jahangir-era painters worked in a deliberate ethnographic-natural-historical vein because Jahangir himself was a serious natural historian. The Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri contains detailed observations on every region of India, its plants, its animals, its peoples, with the precision of a working naturalist. He measured the size of the imperial menagerie's lion. He noted the reproductive cycle of the saras crane. He recorded the date of every fruit's ripening at the Kashmir summer palace. The Mughal painting workshop served his curiosity. It also served the imperial-political purpose of cataloguing the empire — the painting of a particular Persian noble at the Mughal court, executed as an accurate ethnographic portrait, was simultaneously an artwork and a political-intelligence document.

The peak was probably the years 1615-25, when the workshop combined Mansur's natural-history studies, Bishan Das's portraiture, and a group of younger painters working on smaller-format album leaves with European compositional influence (Jesuit missionaries had brought European prints to the Mughal court in the 1590s, and Mughal painters absorbed European techniques of perspective, modeling, and shading without abandoning the Persian-Indian color sense). The Jahangir albums — illustrated personal anthologies the emperor compiled over decades — are among the most prized objects in modern Islamic art collections.

The mention of European influence is worth pausing on. The Jesuits at the Mughal court (mostly Portuguese and Spanish, sent up from the Goa colony from the 1590s onward) brought prints of European paintings, particularly of Christian religious subjects. Mughal painters studied them carefully. The European technique of three-dimensional shading and atmospheric perspective entered Mughal painting in the early seventeenth century and never left. By the late Jahangir period, Mughal portraits of courtiers and family members combined Persian-style flat color planes with European-style modeling and shadow — a unique synthesis that exists in no other Asian or Middle Eastern painting tradition. Mughal painting is one of the very few early-modern visual cultures that absorbed European technique without becoming derivative.

---

**Sir Thomas Roe**'s embassy is the third major topic of the Jahangir reign.

Roe was a courtier in the service of **James I** of England (the same James I who had commissioned the King James Bible translation in 1611, two years before Roe's mission was conceived). The English **East India Company** (founded 1600) had established its first Indian factory at **Surat** in **1612** after defeating a Portuguese fleet at the Battle of Swally, and the Company directors wanted formal commercial privileges from the Mughal court — the right to trade duty-free, the right to maintain factories at additional ports, protection from Portuguese harassment. James I dispatched Roe in 1615 as the first formal English ambassador to the Mughal court, with instructions to negotiate a treaty.

Roe arrived at Surat in September 1615 and traveled overland to Ajmer, where Jahangir was holding court at the time. He spent the next three years (1615-18) at the imperial court, traveling with it as it moved between Ajmer, Mandu, and Ahmedabad. He kept a detailed journal that survives as the first sustained English-language description of Mughal India — and one of the most valuable European sources on the Jahangir court generally. He described the daily ceremonies of the **jharokha-i-darshan** (the ceremonial window appearance, where the emperor showed himself to subjects each morning), the elaborate court receptions, the food, the weather, the inadequacy of the English diplomatic gifts (Roe had brought a portrait of an English nobleman, several mediocre European prints, a coach, and a large quantity of red wine — none of which much impressed a court accustomed to receiving Safavid manuscripts and Portuguese telescopes).

Roe failed to achieve a formal treaty. The Mughal court, accustomed to viewing all foreigners as petitioners regardless of their actual political standing, was not interested in formal reciprocal treaties with a small island kingdom on the western edge of the world. But Roe did secure verbal commitments and **firmans** (imperial decrees) granting the East India Company commercial privileges at Surat and Ahmedabad. The privileges were enough for the Company to expand its operations significantly over the following decades — additional factories at Madras (1639), Bombay (acquired from the Portuguese as part of Catherine of Braganza's dowry to Charles II in 1668), and Calcutta (1690).

The Roe embassy is one of those moments in early-modern history that look much more important in retrospect than they did at the time. To Jahangir's court, Roe was a marginally interesting curiosity — a tall thin Englishman who drank too much red wine and could be persuaded into the embarrassing diplomatic-protocol mistake of bowing too low. To the East India Company, Roe's three years secured the operational base from which the Company would (a hundred and forty years later, in 1757) take over Bengal at Plassey (Chapter 8). The embassy is the formal beginning of the Anglo-Mughal relationship that would, over six generations, end the Mughal dynasty.

---

The last years of Jahangir's reign were dominated by the succession struggle.

By 1623, Khurram (the future Shah Jahan) had emerged as the most able of Jahangir's sons. He had successfully campaigned in the Deccan against the Nizam Shahi Sultanate of Ahmadnagar (1616-21), demonstrating military competence that none of his brothers could match. Jahangir formally designated him heir-apparent and gave him the title **Shah Jahan** ("king of the world"). But Nur Jahan's preferred candidate was the much weaker **Shahryar**, married to Nur Jahan's own daughter, who would have left actual power in Nur Jahan's hands well past Jahangir's death.

Khurram-Shah Jahan, understanding the threat, rebelled against his father in 1622. The rebellion lasted four years, with Khurram losing battles, retreating to Bengal, then to the Deccan, and eventually being driven into formal exile near the Coromandel Coast. The Mughal armies that fought against him were commanded by **Mahabat Khan** (the same noble who would later kidnap Jahangir in 1626) and by Asaf Khan (Nur Jahan's brother, who was actually Khurram's father-in-law and who switched sides repeatedly during the conflict). The rebellion ended with Khurram's submission and reconciliation in 1626, mostly because Jahangir's health was visibly failing and Asaf Khan had calculated correctly that Khurram would be the eventual winner.

Jahangir died at **Bhimbar** in Kashmir on **October 28, 1627**, age fifty-eight. The cause was almost certainly a combination of decades of alcohol abuse, opium addiction, and the asthma he had suffered from since childhood. Nur Jahan, immediately after his death, attempted to install Shahryar on the throne. Asaf Khan, having already opened communications with Khurram, betrayed his sister, seized Shahryar at Lahore, blinded him, and held the throne open for Khurram's arrival from the Deccan. Shahryar and Khurram's other surviving rival brothers were executed within weeks of Khurram's accession. The brutal succession was the new Mughal pattern that would continue, in even bloodier form, into the Aurangzeb generation (Chapter 6).

Nur Jahan, after losing the succession struggle, retired to Lahore on a generous imperial pension. She lived another eighteen years, designing her father Itimad-ud-Daulah's tomb (the small white-marble jewel-box at Agra that pioneered the marble-and-pietra-dura technique perfected at the Taj Mahal a generation later), composing Persian poetry, and managing the substantial estate Jahangir had left her. She died at Lahore in 1645, age sixty-eight, and was buried in the same tomb-complex as her parents. Her grave is small and unornamented, with a simple inscription she reportedly composed herself: *"On the grave of poor Nur Jahan, neither lamp nor rose. Neither does butterfly's wing burn, nor sing nightingale."* The tone of resigned acceptance is characteristic. So is the rhetorical dignity. Nur Jahan was, to the end, more eloquent than any of the men around her.

---

What Jahangir left to Shah Jahan was, on paper, the empire at peak.

The territorial extent in 1627 was essentially what Akbar had left in 1605, slightly modified — gains in Bengal and minor losses in the Deccan, but the same basic shape. The institutional structure (mansabdari, jagir system, revenue assessment) was working, despite the emperor's incapacity, because Akbar had built it well. The painting workshop was at peak. The Persianate court culture was the most sophisticated in the world. The state revenue had grown to perhaps 120 million rupees per year, an increase over Akbar's 99 million.

But the institutional integrity had been quietly hollowing out. The reign had seen no major administrative reforms (Akbar had built the system; Jahangir had inherited it without modifying it). The Deccan situation had deteriorated — the Nizam Shahi Sultanate of Ahmadnagar had outmaneuvered Mughal forces, and a Hindu general named **Malik Ambar** (an Ethiopian-born Habshi who had risen through the Ahmadnagar military hierarchy to become its de facto regent) had built an effective resistance using guerrilla warfare and revenue-assignment techniques borrowed from the Mughals themselves. The European trading companies (English, Dutch, French) had established footholds along the coasts that would become problems in the next century. The succession crisis had set the precedent that brothers would kill brothers without restraint.

Shah Jahan inherited all of this. The next chapter is mostly about what he chose to do with it — which was, primarily, to build.

Cross-civ check: Jahangir's reign (1605-27) overlapped with the **Thirty Years War** in central Europe (1618-48), one of the most destructive religious-political conflicts in European history. **Galileo Galilei** was conducting his telescope observations and writing his major works (the **Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems** would be published in 1632, just after Jahangir's death, and immediately suppressed by the Inquisition). **Shakespeare** died in 1616. **Cervantes** died in 1616 the same week. **Tokugawa Ieyasu** in Japan won the Battle of Sekigahara in 1600 and founded the Tokugawa Shogunate (Edo Japan Ch 1) in 1603, just before Jahangir's accession. **Ming China** under the Wanli Emperor was sliding toward the dynastic collapse that would come with the Manchu invasion in 1644. The Ottoman Empire under Ahmed I and Murad IV was at the start of its long decline (Ottoman Empire Ch 6). Safavid Persia was at peak under Abbas the Great (Safavid Persia Ch 4). The early seventeenth century was an era of major cultural production across Eurasia — and Jahangir's Mughal court, with its painting workshop and its Persianate scholarship and its silken-textiled imperial wealth, was one of the major centers of that production, even when its emperor was too high on opium to actually run the place.

# Chapter 5 — Shah Jahan and the Peacock Throne

The fifth Mughal emperor built the Taj Mahal, founded a new capital city named after himself, commissioned a gem-encrusted golden throne so valuable it became one of the wonders of the world, and was eventually imprisoned by his own son in a small apartment with a window facing the tomb of the wife the Taj Mahal had been built for. The reign is the architectural climax of the Mughal Empire and the moment when the institutional cracks of the eighteenth-century decline first become visible in retrospect.

His name was **Khurram**, born in 1592 in Lahore to Jahangir and Princess Manmati of Marwar (a Rathore Rajput princess, demonstrating the Akbar-era marriage policy at work, Chapter 3). He took the regnal name **Shah Jahan** (shah jah-HAHN, "king of the world") that Jahangir had granted him for his Deccan campaigns in 1617. He took the throne in **1628**, ruled for thirty years, was deposed by his son **Aurangzeb** in **1658**, and lived another eight years under house arrest in Agra Fort before dying in **1666**.

The thirty years of his actual reign (1628-58) were spent doing two things: campaigning in the Deccan to consolidate Mughal control of central India, and building. The campaigning was indecisive — the Deccan never quite came under stable Mughal control, and the wars consumed enormous resources. The building was definitive. The architectural projects of Shah Jahan's reign produced the canonical visual icons of Mughal civilization. The Taj Mahal alone is, by most measures, the most famous building constructed anywhere in the world during the seventeenth century.

---

The reign opened with the standard Mughal succession purge.

Within weeks of taking the throne in February 1628, Shah Jahan ordered the execution of every male relative who could plausibly claim the throne — his blinded brother Shahryar (executed by strangulation in February 1628), his cousins Daniyal's two surviving sons, his uncle Khusrau's surviving son. The total was at least eight relatives killed within a few months. Mughal succession discipline had hardened from Akbar's relatively restrained 1556 (Hemu killed, the rebellious nephews mostly absorbed) and Jahangir's 1605-27 (Khusrau blinded but kept alive for sixteen years) to Shah Jahan's 1628 (everyone who could conceivably claim the throne killed within months). The pattern would harden further under Aurangzeb (Chapter 6) and would help explain why the post-Aurangzeb succession crises (Chapter 7) were so destructive — by then, the executive machinery for killing potential heirs had been so refined that each succession produced a wholesale purge of the imperial family.

With his rivals dead, Shah Jahan turned to the Deccan. He had spent the late 1610s and early 1620s campaigning there, knew the political-military terrain, and viewed the unfinished Akbar-and-Jahangir-era project of southern conquest as his immediate priority. The campaign was protracted. The **Sultanate of Ahmadnagar** was annexed in **1636**. **Bijapur** and **Golconda** were forced into tributary status in 1636 but remained effectively independent for another fifty years. The Maratha resistance under Shahji Bhonsle (the father of Shivaji, Chapter 6) was ongoing throughout. The Deccan front absorbed Mughal armies, Mughal revenue, and Mughal attention for the entire reign without producing decisive results.

The Persian-Safavid frontier was also active. **Kandahar**, the city Humayun had ceded to Shah Tahmasp in 1545 (Chapter 2), had returned to Mughal control under Akbar and was now contested again with Shah Abbas II (Safavid Persia Ch 7). The city changed hands four times during Shah Jahan's reign — Mughal in 1638, Safavid in 1649 (after Shah Jahan's third son Aurangzeb was defeated by Abbas II in three successive battles), Mughal in attempted recoveries in 1652 and 1653, both of which failed. The Mughal-Safavid rivalry over Kandahar was one of the great expensive unwinnable wars of the seventeenth century.

---

Then there was Mumtaz Mahal.

**Arjumand Banu Begum** (better known by her later title **Mumtaz Mahal**, "the chosen one of the palace") was Nur Jahan's niece — daughter of Asaf Khan, Nur Jahan's brother. She was born in 1593 to a Persian noble family at Agra. She was betrothed to Khurram in 1607, when she was fourteen and he was fifteen, and the marriage took place in 1612 with Nur Jahan's enthusiastic support (the marriage was the political instrument by which Nur Jahan tied Khurram into her own faction at court, Chapter 4). Mumtaz Mahal was Khurram-Shah Jahan's third wife and, by every contemporary account, his only one of any emotional significance — the other two were political marriages with whom he had no recorded sustained relationship.

The marriage produced fourteen children in nineteen years. Seven survived to adulthood, including Shah Jahan's four eventually-feuding sons: **Dara Shukoh** (the Sufi-scholar heir), **Shah Shuja** (the Bengal governor), **Aurangzeb** (the eventual victor of the succession war), and **Murad Bakhsh** (the youngest, briefly Aurangzeb's ally before being killed). The sheer number of pregnancies is itself notable — even by seventeenth-century royal standards, fourteen pregnancies in nineteen years is an extraordinary toll on a body, and modern medical historians have suggested it directly contributed to Mumtaz Mahal's eventual death in childbirth.

She died at **Burhanpur** in the Deccan on **June 17, 1631**, giving birth to her fourteenth child (a daughter, who survived). Shah Jahan was on campaign with her, as was the Mughal custom — the imperial harem traveled with the emperor on long campaigns. He had been married to her for nineteen years. His grief was reportedly extraordinary — he went into formal mourning for two years, refused to attend public ceremonies, gave up music and the imperial atelier's productions, dressed in white (the South Asian color of mourning), and reportedly aged visibly within months of her death.

He emerged from mourning in 1633 with a single dominant project: he would build her a tomb that would be remembered forever.

---

The **Taj Mahal** is the project, and it deserves a sustained treatment.

Construction began in **1632** at Agra, on the south bank of the Yamuna river, on a plot of land that had previously belonged to Jai Singh I of Amber (the Rajput noble in Mughal service). The chief architect is generally identified as **Ustad Ahmad Lahori** (a Mughal court architect whose name appears in the imperial records), with the design probably the product of a team rather than a single individual. The construction took approximately twenty-two years (1632-53), employed a workforce variously estimated at 20,000 to 50,000 (sources disagree), and was financed entirely from imperial revenue rather than from any single province's tax base — a deliberate choice that prevented any one region from being economically devastated by the project.

The white marble came from the **Makrana** quarries in Rajasthan, transported approximately 220 miles by ox-cart to Agra. The semi-precious stones for the inlay work came from across Asia: jade and crystal from Central Asia, lapis lazuli from Afghanistan, sapphires from Sri Lanka, jasper from the Punjab, turquoise from Tibet, amethyst from Persia, malachite from Russia, mother-of-pearl from the Red Sea coast. The total is estimated at twenty-eight different types of stone in the inlay work, fitted with the **pietra dura** technique that the Itimad-ud-Daulah tomb (built 1622-28 under Nur Jahan's supervision, Chapter 4) had pioneered in India.

The architectural design is, in its broad outlines, the standard Mughal garden-tomb plan — central domed mausoleum on a raised plinth, four symmetrical minarets at the corners, formal **chahar bagh** garden quartered by water channels, twin secondary buildings flanking the tomb (a mosque on the west, a guest house on the east, both red sandstone). The plan derives directly from Humayun's Tomb of 1572 (Chapter 2), which derived from Timurid royal-tomb traditions (Timurid Empire Ch 7). What makes the Taj different is the execution: the entire central building is white marble (Humayun's Tomb is mostly red sandstone with marble accents), the pietra dura inlay covers virtually every interior surface, the proportions of the dome are slightly elongated (taller and more pointed than the Humayun-Tomb dome) for visual emphasis, and the water-channel-and-reflecting-pool design is calculated so that the building's reflection appears in the central pool when viewed from the entrance gateway.

Every architectural decision was purposeful. The minarets lean very slightly outward (about three degrees), so that in an earthquake they would fall away from the main building rather than onto it. The central dome is double-shelled — an inner dome at functional ceiling height, an outer dome at the visually-dominant exterior height — to balance the proportions of the interior and exterior spaces. The Quranic inscriptions in elegant calligraphic script around the entrance arches are subtly enlarged toward the top so that they appear visually consistent in size when viewed from the ground. The garden's quadripartite plan (the four-part chahar bagh) symbolically represents the four rivers of Paradise from Islamic eschatology, with the tomb as the throne of God at the center. The whole complex is a programmatic statement of Islamic eschatology applied to imperial mourning, with the technical execution at the limit of what early-modern construction could accomplish.

The building was substantially complete by 1648, with finishing work continuing until about 1653. The total cost is variously estimated at 32 million rupees (a contemporary figure) to 70 million rupees (a higher modern estimate), or roughly a year's full imperial revenue. The opportunity cost — the troops not raised, the famines not relieved, the irrigation works not built — was substantial. Modern historians remain divided on whether the Taj Mahal was a sustainable use of imperial wealth or a self-indulgent monument that economically weakened the empire. Both readings have evidence on their side.

---

The **Italian-architect myth** about the Taj Mahal deserves a paragraph of its own, because it is one of the more persistent pieces of misinformation about the building.

Beginning in the late nineteenth century, several British and Italian writers proposed that the Taj Mahal had been designed by an Italian architect named **Geronimo Veroneo**, who had been at the Mughal court in the relevant period. The argument was based on (a) European architectural elements visible in the building, and (b) a brief mention of Veroneo in the journal of an Italian Augustinian friar named Sebastien Manrique, who passed through Agra in 1640 and reported a conversation with Veroneo in which Veroneo was credited with the Taj's design.

The Veroneo theory has been thoroughly demolished by modern scholarship. The European elements in the building (some of the inlay work, perhaps some of the engineering of the dome) are real but secondary; the basic design is unambiguously Persianate-Timurid in its derivation. Veroneo himself, according to other contemporary records, was a jeweler, not an architect, and the Manrique account is the only source that places him in any architectural role. The actual Mughal court records of the construction, including the **Padshahnama** (the official chronicle of Shah Jahan's reign by **Abdul Hamid Lahori**), name Ustad Ahmad Lahori as the chief architect and identify several other Mughal court architects involved in the project, with no Veroneo credit.

The reason the Veroneo theory was popular in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries is that it served a specific political-cultural agenda — the assumption that a building this beautiful must have been designed by Europeans, since (the implicit logic ran) South Asians could not have produced something this sophisticated independently. The theory was a piece of orientalist projection, not a serious historical claim. Modern scholarship rejects it without ambiguity. The Taj Mahal is a Mughal-Persian-Indian product. The architects were South Asian (Ustad Ahmad Lahori was a Punjabi Persian-trained architect; the rest of the team was a similar mix). The European contribution was modest at most.

---

The **Red Fort of Delhi** and **Shahjahanabad** are the second great Shah Jahan project.

The decision was political. The Mughal capital had been at Agra since Babur's time, with Akbar's brief experiments at Fatehpur Sikri and Lahore (Chapter 3). By the late 1630s, Shah Jahan had decided that Agra was too cramped, too southern, and too associated with previous reigns for what he had in mind. He wanted a new capital city built from scratch, with himself as founder, on the model of Akbar's Fatehpur Sikri but on a much larger scale and at a more central north Indian location.

The site selected was **Delhi**, on the western bank of the Yamuna, north of the existing Sultanate-era Delhi (the various older Delhis — Mehrauli, Tughlaqabad, Firuzabad, Old Delhi proper — sat in scattered ruins to the south, Delhi Sultanate Ch 4 and Ch 8). Construction began in **1639**. The new walled city was called **Shahjahanabad** ("city of Shah Jahan") and was occupied from **1648** onward. It is now Old Delhi.

The city plan was grand. A roughly six-and-a-half-mile defensive wall with twelve gates enclosed the whole. The main commercial street, **Chandni Chowk** ("moonlit square"), ran east-west through the city center, connecting the Red Fort on the eastern wall with the Fatehpuri Mosque on the western wall. Sub-streets and **mahallas** (residential quarters) radiated from Chandni Chowk. The city was designed for a population of perhaps 400,000-500,000 — comparable to contemporary Isfahan (Safavid Persia Ch 4) and substantially larger than any contemporary European city. Water was supplied by an extension of Firuz Shah Tughlaq's fourteenth-century **Western Yamuna Canal** (Delhi Sultanate Ch 4), modified to deliver water to public fountains and the imperial palace.

The **Red Fort** (called **Lal Qila** in Hindi-Urdu) was the imperial palace at the eastern wall of the new city, named for its red-sandstone walls. Construction took ten years (1639-48), with the same architectural team that had been working on the Taj Mahal repurposed for the new project. The fort enclosed approximately 254 acres, with private imperial apartments, public audience halls (the **Diwan-i-Aam**, "hall of public audience," with its red-sandstone and white-marble decoration), private audience halls (the **Diwan-i-Khas**, "hall of private audience," where the Peacock Throne stood), the imperial harem complex (the **Rang Mahal**, "palace of colors," with white-marble walls and inlaid water channels), the **Hammam** (the imperial bathhouse, with marble floors heated by water-pipe systems), and extensive formal gardens. The architectural style was the mature Mughal idiom — red sandstone for the structural and public elements, white marble for the imperial-personal spaces, pietra dura inlay for the most intimate areas.

The Diwan-i-Khas, where the Peacock Throne sat, bore an inscription in Persian that has become famous: *Agar firdaus bar roo-e zameen ast / hameen ast-o, hameen ast-o, hameen ast* — "If there is a paradise on the face of the earth / it is this, it is this, it is this." The inscription is attributed to the court poet **Amir Khusrau** of the Delhi Sultanate (Delhi Sultanate Ch 6) but was probably written by a Mughal courtier in the Khusrau style. Either way, the sentiment captures Shah Jahan's view of his own capital.

---

The **Peacock Throne** is the third project that requires attention.

Shah Jahan commissioned the throne in **1628**, immediately after his accession, with a budget of approximately 100,000 tolas of gold (about 2,300 pounds) and a separate budget for jewels that was reportedly larger than the gold cost. The construction took seven years; the throne was completed in **1635**. The chief jeweler was **Said Gilani**, a Persian master goldsmith who had worked at the Mughal court for decades.

The throne was a rectangular gold platform with a canopy supported by twelve emerald pillars. The canopy was topped by two golden peacocks (hence the name) with bodies and tails set entirely in gems — sapphires, emeralds, rubies, and diamonds. The most famous individual stone was the **Koh-i-Noor** (KOH-ee-NOOR, "Mountain of Light") diamond, which had been in the Mughal collection since Babur acquired it after Panipat in 1526 (the diamond's pre-Mughal history is uncertain — it may have come from the Golconda mines of the Deccan or from much further south). The throne also incorporated the **Timur Ruby** (a Persian-acquired stone with a long inscription history including Timur's name, despite not actually being a ruby — it is a balas spinel) and dozens of other named stones.

The total value, expressed in contemporary terms, was reportedly twice the cost of the Taj Mahal. In modern terms, the throne is essentially impossible to value because the gem prices it commanded depended on royal-prestige markets that no longer exist. What can be said is that it represented a substantial proportion of the Mughal imperial gem treasury — perhaps a fifth to a third of the total imperial collection of precious stones. Shah Jahan's choice to concentrate so much of the imperial wealth in a single moveable object would prove consequential a century later, when Nader Shah's 1739 sack would carry the throne off (Chapter 7).

The Peacock Throne stood in the Diwan-i-Khas at the Red Fort from 1648 (when the Red Fort was occupied) until 1739 (when Nader Shah took it). Mughal court ceremonies revolved around it. Foreign embassies were received in front of it. The succession ceremonies of subsequent emperors were held at it. Aurangzeb was crowned at it in 1658 (Chapter 6). The throne was, for ninety years, the visible symbol of the Mughal Empire, and its loss in 1739 marks the end of the empire as a great power as definitively as any single political event of the eighteenth century.

The throne was disassembled in Persia after Nader Shah's assassination in 1747. Some of the gems, including the Koh-i-Noor and the Timur Ruby, eventually made their way (through Afghan and Sikh possession) to the British Crown after the Anglo-Sikh Wars of the 1840s (Chapter 8), and now sit in the British Crown Jewels at the Tower of London. The Indian, Pakistani, Afghan, and Iranian governments have all, at various times, formally requested the return of the Koh-i-Noor. The British have, so far, declined.

---

By the late 1650s, the Mughal Empire was at peak.

The state revenue had grown to approximately 220 million rupees per year — roughly twice what Akbar had collected. The territorial extent was the largest in two centuries. The architectural projects (Taj Mahal completed 1653, Red Fort completed 1648, Jama Masjid completed 1656, Shahjahanabad occupied 1648) were the visible monuments of imperial wealth. The Persianate court culture had reached a level of refinement that subsequent generations would view as a golden age. Mughal painting was at peak. Mughal music — the synthesis of Persian and Indian classical traditions that had begun under Akbar's court musician Tansen — was producing the formal raga system that defines north Indian classical music today. Mughal cuisine — biryanis, kebabs, naan breads, the entire vocabulary of north Indian Persianate cooking — was being codified at the imperial kitchens.

It looked sustainable. It was not. The structural problems were substantial:

- The Deccan campaigns had absorbed enormous resources without producing decisive results. The Marathas, Bijapur, and Golconda were still substantively independent. Aurangzeb's later attempt to finish the Deccan project would consume his entire reign (Chapter 6).
- The Kandahar wars had consumed enormous resources without producing decisive results. Kandahar was lost permanently in 1649. The Afghan frontier was insecure.
- The architectural and cultural expenditure was enormous. The 220 million rupees of state revenue went largely to (a) the imperial military, (b) the imperial nobility's salaries, (c) the imperial court's expenses, and (d) the building projects. There was little fiscal cushion for crisis response.
- The succession discipline (kill all rival heirs at accession) meant that each succession produced a destructive purge of the imperial family — and the Mughal family was now so large that the purges were getting destructive enough to weaken the dynasty itself.

In **September 1657**, Shah Jahan fell seriously ill. The exact illness is not recorded in detail; he was sixty-five and had been visibly aging for several years. The illness was severe enough that the four sons of Mumtaz Mahal — Dara Shukoh in Delhi, Shah Shuja in Bengal, Aurangzeb in the Deccan, Murad Bakhsh in Gujarat — all assumed Shah Jahan was dying and began maneuvering for the throne. By the time Shah Jahan recovered (which he did, mostly), his four sons were already at war.

The next chapter is mostly about how that war was resolved.

Cross-civ check: Shah Jahan's reign (1628-58) coincided with several major events globally. The **Thirty Years War** (1618-48) ended in central Europe with the Peace of Westphalia, establishing the modern European state system. **Cromwell's Commonwealth** (1649-58) was running England after the execution of Charles I in 1649 (the year Aurangzeb lost Kandahar to the Safavids — small Mughal historical detail; the simultaneous regicide of Charles I in London barely registered at Shah Jahan's court). **Louis XIV** of France was a child king under Mazarin's regency and would not begin his personal rule until 1661, after Shah Jahan was deposed. The **Manchu** conquest of Ming China was completed in 1644, beginning the Qing Dynasty. **Tokugawa Iemitsu** in Japan completed the **sakoku** isolation policy that would seal Japan from European trade for two centuries (Edo Japan Ch 3). **Safavid Persia** under Abbas II was in mid-arc of its long decline (Safavid Persia Ch 7). The world was actively reshaping. Shah Jahan, building marble tombs and gem-encrusted thrones, was largely uninterested in any of it.

# Chapter 6 — Aurangzeb

The sixth Mughal emperor is the most controversial figure in Mughal history.

His name was **Muhi-ud-Din Muhammad** (the formal name) or **Aurangzeb** (OR-ang-zeb, "ornament of the throne," the regnal name he took at his accession), born in 1618 to Shah Jahan and Mumtaz Mahal. He took the throne in **1658** after killing his eldest brother Dara Shukoh and imprisoning his father Shah Jahan. He reigned for forty-nine years, expanded the empire to its greatest territorial extent, reimposed the **jizya** tax on non-Muslim subjects in 1679, spent his last twenty-six years bogged down in the Deccan against the **Marathas**, and died in **1707** at age eighty-eight. The empire he left was visibly fraying. The fraying would accelerate after his death into the eighteenth-century decline (Chapter 7).

The historiographical problem with Aurangzeb is that the evidence supports two starkly different readings.

The first reading — common in Hindu nationalist historiography but with respectable scholarly defenders too — is that Aurangzeb was a religious fanatic who systematically reversed the Akbar-era Hindu-Muslim accommodation, reimposed orthodox Sunni state policy at the cost of the empire's institutional cohesion, destroyed Hindu temples and persecuted Hindu practices, and was the proximate cause of the eighteenth-century Mughal collapse through his combination of religious bigotry and territorial overreach. The Maratha resistance under Shivaji and his successors, on this reading, was Hindu civilization defending itself against Mughal religious aggression.

The second reading — common in the Indian Marxist-secularist tradition associated with **Irfan Habib** and his students, and increasingly in revisionist American scholarship by **Audrey Truschke** and others — is that Aurangzeb was primarily a pragmatic state-builder whose religious-policy moves were instrumental rather than zealot-driven, that his Hindu temple destructions were politically targeted at specific local opponents rather than systematically anti-Hindu, that his employment of Hindu officials and generals at high levels of the imperial administration was actually larger than under any previous Mughal emperor, and that the eighteenth-century decline had structural causes (succession problems, fiscal exhaustion from the Deccan wars, the rise of regional powers) that would have produced collapse regardless of religious policy.

Modern scholarship is, broadly, divided between these readings, with most academic Mughal historians leaning toward the second (state-builder) reading and most popular Indian historiography (especially in English-language journalistic and political writing) defaulting to the first (religious fanatic) reading. The honest answer is that Aurangzeb was both — a personally devout Sunni Muslim who genuinely believed in orthodox Islamic state policy, and a pragmatic political actor who employed Hindus when politically useful and persecuted them when politically useful. The proportions of pragmatism versus zealotry in any given decision are usually impossible to recover from the surviving evidence. The historiographical wars over his legacy have, mostly, been driven by twentieth- and twenty-first-century political needs rather than by the seventeenth-century evidence itself.

What is not disputable is the basic chronology, which is the next several paragraphs.

---

The **Mughal war of succession of 1657-59** was the bloodiest in Mughal history.

When Shah Jahan fell ill in September 1657, his four sons — Dara Shukoh in Delhi as the heir-presumptive, Shah Shuja in Bengal, Aurangzeb in the Deccan, Murad Bakhsh in Gujarat — all assumed the throne was about to come open and began organizing armies. Shah Shuja moved first, marching west from Bengal toward Delhi. He was defeated by Dara Shukoh's army (commanded by Dara's son **Sulaiman Shukoh**) at the Battle of **Bahadurpur** in February 1658.

Aurangzeb and Murad Bakhsh, more strategically, allied. They marched from the Deccan and Gujarat respectively, met at the western Malwa town of **Dipalpur**, combined their armies, and moved north toward Agra. Dara Shukoh, who had been holding the imperial court while his father was incapacitated, marched south to meet them. The two armies met at **Samugarh** (sah-MOO-garh), about eight miles east of Agra, on **May 29, 1658**.

The battle was decided by what looked like Dara Shukoh's bizarre tactical choice. He had fielded the larger and better-equipped army (perhaps 60,000 troops to Aurangzeb-Murad's combined 50,000), with strong artillery and elite cavalry. He committed the artillery first, drove Aurangzeb's center back, and was in position to break the line — when, for reasons modern historians still debate, he dismounted from his elephant in the middle of the battle to ride a horse instead. (Some sources say his elephant was wounded; some say he wanted to be more mobile; some say he wanted to ride into the final charge personally.) The dismount was visible to both armies. From a distance, the empty howdah looked like Dara had been killed. His army began to break in confusion. Aurangzeb counter-attacked. Within an hour the battle was lost. Dara Shukoh fled the field with a small bodyguard.

Aurangzeb entered Agra and seized Shah Jahan in his palace — formal house arrest, in the Musamman Burj of Agra Fort, the white-marble tower with the famous view across the Yamuna to the Taj Mahal. Shah Jahan would live in that confinement for the next eight years, attended by his daughter Jahanara (Shah Jahan's daughter who chose to share his imprisonment voluntarily), watching the tomb of his wife from the window. He died there on January 22, 1666, age seventy-four. He was buried in the Taj Mahal beside Mumtaz Mahal.

Aurangzeb then turned to his brothers. Murad Bakhsh, who had been Aurangzeb's ally at Samugarh, was lured to Aurangzeb's camp at Mathura under the pretense of a celebration banquet, arrested, sent to prison at the fortress of Gwalior, and executed in 1661. Shah Shuja was defeated again in early 1659 at the Battle of **Khajwa**, fled east through Bengal into the kingdom of Arakan in modern Burma, and was killed there in 1661 in obscure circumstances (the king of Arakan had him killed, apparently, after some kind of palace intrigue involving Shah Shuja's daughters).

Dara Shukoh's end was the most dramatic. After Samugarh, he fled west through the Punjab and Sindh, attempting to reach the Persian-Mughal border at Kandahar to seek Safavid support. He was betrayed by an Afghan tribal chief named **Malik Jiwan** (whom Dara had earlier shown personal kindness — Malik Jiwan had been condemned to death years before, and Dara had intervened to save him — this is one of the more striking betrayals in Mughal history) and handed over to Aurangzeb's forces. He was brought back to Delhi in chains, paraded through the streets in a public humiliation that the Italian traveler **Niccolao Manucci** witnessed and described in detail, then tried for apostasy by a panel of Sunni clerics on the basis of his Sufi-Hindu syncretist writings (Chapter 5), convicted, and executed in September 1659. His execution was both political (clearing a rival heir) and religious (publicly establishing Aurangzeb's orthodox Sunni credentials by killing the Sufi-syncretist brother).

---

**Dara Shukoh** deserves his own paragraph because what he represented — the road not taken — was the Mughal Empire's most important alternative path.

Dara Shukoh (born 1615) was Shah Jahan's eldest son, his designated heir for thirty years, and a serious scholar and Sufi mystic. He had been initiated into the **Qadiri Sufi order** in his twenties and developed a deep interest in Hindu philosophy. He learned Sanskrit (most Mughals did not), commissioned translations of fifty Upanishads from Sanskrit into Persian (the **Sirr-i-Akbar**, "the Greatest Secret," completed in 1657), wrote his own comparative-religion treatise **Majma-ul-Bahrain** ("the Confluence of the Two Seas") arguing that Sufi Islamic mysticism and Hindu Vedanta were essentially expressing the same truth in different vocabularies, and patronized Sufi-Hindu joint religious gatherings. His intellectual position was that the Hindu and Muslim mystical traditions were two valid paths to the same divine reality, and that the apparent doctrinal differences were superficial.

The political implication of this view, had Dara succeeded to the throne, would have been enormous. Mughal religious policy would presumably have continued in the Akbarian syncretist direction (Chapter 3) — perhaps with more theological substance than Akbar's somewhat improvised Din-i-Ilahi had offered. The Hindu majority would have remained integrated into the imperial system through religious-ideological inclusion as well as marriage-alliance and military-service inclusion. The orthodox Sunni clergy would have been further marginalized.

Aurangzeb's victory closed off this trajectory. The Sufi-Hindu syncretist tradition that Dara represented continued in other forms (the Bhakti devotional tradition, the Sikh community, regional Sufi-Hindu shrine networks) but lost its imperial sponsorship. The Mughal state under Aurangzeb moved decisively toward orthodox Sunni state-religious policy. The hundred-fifteen-year window of the Akbarian synthesis (1564 jizya abolition to 1679 jizya reimposition) was coming to a close. Whether you read this shift as the empire returning to its proper Islamic foundations (the orthodox Sunni reading) or as the empire abandoning the inclusive policy that had made it sustainable (the Akbarian/Dara reading), the shift itself is undisputed.

The translation of the Upanishads into Persian — Dara's intellectual project — had a remarkable afterlife. The Sirr-i-Akbar was translated into Latin by the French orientalist **Anquetil-Duperron** in 1801-02 and widely read in early-nineteenth-century Europe. The German philosopher **Schopenhauer** read it and called it "the consolation of my life." The Upanishadic ideas in nineteenth-century European philosophy — the influence on Schopenhauer, on Nietzsche, on the early Heidegger — came largely through Dara Shukoh's Persian translation and Anquetil-Duperron's Latin retranslation. The intellectual project Aurangzeb killed in 1659 ended up shaping European thought two centuries later.

---

The first decade of Aurangzeb's reign (1658-68) was, by Mughal standards, a period of relative consolidation.

He was crowned at the Red Fort in Delhi in **June 1659**. He took the regnal title **Alamgir** ("world-conqueror," the formal regnal name; "Aurangzeb" remained the personal name and is what most historical writing uses). He proclaimed a return to orthodox Sunni state policy: the Mughal court would now publicly observe orthodox Sunni rituals, the imperial atelier would no longer produce paintings of human or animal forms (which orthodox Sunni interpretation of Islamic image-prohibition forbade), the imperial musicians would be dismissed (Aurangzeb personally disliked music and considered it an unislamic indulgence), the imperial wardrobe would shift to plain austere costume rather than the gold-and-silk court dress of his father's reign.

The reaction at court was muted. The senior nobility, mostly the same Persian-Turkic-Rajput mansabdars who had served Shah Jahan, accepted the new orthodoxy as a court-fashion adjustment rather than a fundamental change in their employment status. The painting workshop was officially curtailed but unofficially continued (later Aurangzeb-period paintings exist, including portraits of Aurangzeb himself, suggesting the prohibition was honored more in theory than in practice). The musicians dispersed to provincial courts where music continued; the great singer **Nyamat Khan** spent the rest of his career at the Hyderabad court. The Persianate cultural world that Akbar, Jahangir, and Shah Jahan had built continued at Aurangzeb's court in modified form, with the surface Sunni-orthodox formality covering substantially continuing institutional practice.

The political reforms of the period were more substantive. Aurangzeb reorganized the imperial revenue collection, tightened up the mansabdari ranks (which had inflated under Shah Jahan), reformed the **karkhanas** (imperial workshops that produced everything from textiles to weapons for the court and the military). He commissioned the **Fatawa-i-Alamgiri** in **1675** — a comprehensive compilation of Hanafi Sunni jurisprudence, completed by a panel of around five hundred scholars over approximately a decade, that would become the standard Islamic legal reference across South Asia and the Middle East and was still cited by British colonial courts in the nineteenth century. He had the **Badshahi Mosque** built at Lahore (completed 1673), one of the largest mosques in the world at the time, designed as an explicit architectural counterweight to Shah Jahan's Taj Mahal — power, not love, in stone.

---

The most consequential single decision of Aurangzeb's reign was the **reimposition of the jizya** in **1679**.

The jizya — the per-capita tax on non-Muslim subjects — had been abolished by Akbar in 1564 (Chapter 3) as the institutional centerpiece of the Hindu-Muslim accommodation. Its abolition had been enormously controversial among orthodox Sunni clerics for the entire 115-year window of its non-collection. Aurangzeb had been considering reimposition for years and had been pressured by the orthodox ulama to do it; the actual decision came in 1679, twenty-one years into his reign, after the Maratha resistance under Shivaji had visibly hardened (see below).

The reimposition order specified that Hindu subjects would pay a per-capita tax graduated by income (the wealthy paid more, the poor paid less), with exemptions for the very poor, for women, for children, for monks, and for state officials (which exempted senior Hindu mansabdars from the personal humiliation of paying it). The amounts were modest in absolute terms — most subjects paid a few rupees per year — but the political symbolism was enormous. The jizya formally re-established the Hindu-as-dhimmi (protected non-Muslim subject) status that Akbar had abolished. It signaled, unmistakably, that the empire was no longer pretending to be a Hindu-Muslim joint sovereignty.

The political consequences were predictable. Hindu opposition to Mughal authority hardened across north India. The Rajput rulers of Mewar and Marwar, who had been broadly cooperative since the Akbar-era marriage alliances, rebelled within months of the jizya reimposition. The **Rajput War of 1679-81** (sometimes called the **Marwar War**) was the most serious Rajput resistance to Mughal rule since Rana Sanga at Khanwa in 1527. Aurangzeb won the war militarily but did not break the political-symbolic damage to Mughal-Rajput relations, and the Rajput-Mughal alliance never fully recovered.

The deeper consequence was longer-term. The Akbarian synthesis had given the Mughal Empire its largest single institutional advantage over alternative Indian states: it was the only major Indian Islamic state that had genuinely integrated the Hindu majority into its administrative and military elite. Aurangzeb's policy reversal eroded that advantage. By the early eighteenth century, the Marathas, the Sikhs, the Rajputs, and the southern Hindu polities were all in active opposition to Mughal authority. The empire's ability to govern the subcontinent depended on the cooperation of the Hindu majority. Aurangzeb's policy made that cooperation harder.

Whether this policy reversal was the proximate cause of the eighteenth-century decline (the standard reading) or merely the most visible symbolic moment of a decline driven by deeper structural causes (the revisionist reading) remains debated. Both views have evidence.

---

**Shivaji** and the Maratha resistance is the other major topic of the reign.

**Shivaji Bhonsle** (shih-VAH-jee BHON-slay) was born in 1630 to a Maratha family that had served as senior officers in the Bijapur Sultanate of the Deccan. The Marathas were a Marathi-speaking Hindu population concentrated in the western Deccan plateau (modern Maharashtra), historically organized as cavalry-and-fort polities serving variously the Bahmani Sultanate, the Bijapur Sultanate, and the Mughals as mercenary forces. Shivaji's father Shahji Bhonsle had been a senior Bijapur officer; Shivaji grew up in the western Maharashtra hill country with a Hindu religious education from his mother **Jijabai** that emphasized resistance to Muslim rule.

Shivaji built a Maratha state in the western Deccan over the course of the 1650s and 1660s. His method was distinctive — small mobile cavalry forces operating from a network of hill forts (the **Sahyadri** mountain forts of the Western Ghats — Pratapgarh, Sinhagad, Raigad, Torna, several dozen others), conducting rapid raids against Bijapur and Mughal positions, withdrawing to defensible hill positions when superior forces appeared, and gradually consolidating territorial control through a combination of military victories and revenue-collection penetration of the surrounding lowland districts. He destroyed a major Bijapur army at the Battle of **Pratapgarh** in 1659 (where he reportedly killed the Bijapur general **Afzal Khan** with a hidden tiger-claw weapon during a peace negotiation, an episode that became central to Maratha mythology). He raided the Mughal port of **Surat** in 1664, looting an estimated 100 million rupees of merchant goods. He was captured and brought to Aurangzeb's court at Agra in 1666 in a curious diplomatic experiment that ended when Shivaji escaped from house arrest by hiding in a basket of sweets.

The defining moment was Shivaji's **coronation as Chhatrapati** at **Raigad fort** on **June 6, 1674**. The ceremony was a deliberate revival of Hindu sovereign-king ritual that had been dormant since the Vijayanagara period (Medieval India Ch 8) — performed by a Brahmin priest from Varanasi, with traditional Sanskrit Vedic ritual, conferring on Shivaji the title **Chhatrapati** ("lord of the parasol," an old Hindu sovereign title) and explicitly framing him as a Hindu king ruling a Hindu kingdom independent of Mughal authority. The coronation was a direct political-religious challenge to Mughal sovereignty over India.

Shivaji died in 1680 at age fifty, leaving the Maratha state to his son **Sambhaji**. Sambhaji continued his father's resistance against Aurangzeb, was eventually captured by the Mughals in 1689, refused to convert to Islam, and was tortured to death — eyes gouged out, tongue cut out, then quartered, over a period of weeks — on Aurangzeb's orders. The execution radicalized Maratha resistance further. Sambhaji's infant son was held by the Mughals; Sambhaji's brother **Rajaram** continued the fight from southern Maharashtra; Rajaram's widow **Tarabai** continued it after his death; Sambhaji's son **Shahu** eventually emerged as the Maratha sovereign in the early eighteenth century. The Maratha resistance never broke. By the 1690s and 1700s, it had become an unwinnable counter-insurgency that consumed the entire Mughal southern army.

---

The **Deccan Wars** of 1681-1707 are the immediate cause of the eighteenth-century Mughal collapse, and they need a sustained treatment.

In **1681**, Aurangzeb personally moved his court to the Deccan to direct the war against the Marathas in person. He left the imperial capital at Delhi essentially abandoned, with the imperial administration following the emperor south. The court would not return to Delhi during Aurangzeb's lifetime. Aurangzeb spent the next twenty-six years — from age sixty-three to age eighty-eight — campaigning in the Deccan in person, personally directing sieges, personally reading military dispatches, personally signing the orders that allocated jagirs and mansabs to officers in the field.

The first phase of the campaign (1681-89) annexed the two surviving Deccan Sultanates. The **Sultanate of Bijapur** fell in **September 1686** after a long siege of the capital city. The **Sultanate of Golconda** (famous for its diamond mines, including the original source of the Koh-i-Noor) fell in **September 1687** after another long siege. Both sultanates were annexed directly to Mughal rule, with their treasuries seized and their ruling families brought to Mughal court detention. On paper, this was a major Mughal victory — the entire subcontinent was now under nominal Mughal sovereignty, with only the southernmost tip and the Maratha hill country still outside imperial control.

In practice, the annexations were administrative disasters. The two sultanates' bureaucratic-military structures were incompatible with the Mughal mansabdari system. The local elites (Persian-Turkic in Golconda, mostly Bijapuri-Sunni in Bijapur) were absorbed into the Mughal nobility but with much-reduced status, producing chronic resentment. The local economy of the Deccan — which had been organized around the Bijapur and Golconda fiscal systems — collapsed under Mughal mismanagement. Agricultural revenue from the new Mughal Deccan provinces ran consistently below pre-conquest levels. The cost of maintaining Mughal garrisons across the new territory ran consistently above local revenue. The new Deccan provinces were a net fiscal drain on the empire.

The second phase (1690-1707) was the long Maratha counter-insurgency. With Bijapur and Golconda absorbed, the Marathas under Rajaram and Tarabai conducted an asymmetric war from their hill forts and through cavalry raids deep into Mughal territory. Mughal forces took Maratha forts (Sinhagad, Pratapgarh, Raigad, Panhala, Khelna — dozens of fort sieges across the Western Ghats) only to find that the Marathas had withdrawn to other forts. The Mughal armies controlled the towns and roads during the day; the Marathas controlled the same territory at night. The campaigns dragged on year after year, consuming armies, consuming revenue, consuming Aurangzeb's old age.

He died on March 3, 1707, at the camp at **Ahmednagar** in the Deccan, age eighty-eight, still on campaign, having achieved no decisive resolution. His will instructed his sons to bury him simply at the shrine of the Sufi saint **Zainuddin** at **Khuldabad**, near Aurangabad, with no mausoleum and no grave-marker. The instruction was honored. His grave is, by his own request, the most modest tomb of any Mughal emperor — a simple open-air enclosure with a plain rectangular stone marker. The contrast with the Taj Mahal is the single most striking thing about Mughal tomb-architecture.

---

What Aurangzeb left to his successors was an empire visibly at the limits of what its institutions could sustain.

The territorial extent in 1707 was the largest in Mughal history — roughly 4 million square kilometers, covering nearly the entire Indian subcontinent except the southernmost tip, the Himalayan periphery, and the Maratha hill country. The state revenue had grown to perhaps 380 million rupees per year (a contemporary estimate; modern scholars vary somewhat). The imperial nobility was the largest in Mughal history. The architectural and cultural infrastructure built up over the previous four reigns was substantially intact.

But underneath this surface, the structural problems were severe:

- The Deccan was not pacified. The Marathas continued the resistance after Aurangzeb's death, and the Maratha cavalry would shortly emerge as the dominant Indian military force.
- The Hindu-Muslim accommodation was broken. The jizya reimposition and the Maratha wars had radicalized opposition to Mughal authority across most of central and southern India.
- The fiscal system was overstretched. The imperial revenue was substantial but the imperial expenditure (military, court, administration) was substantially larger. The empire had been running long-term deficits financed by drawdowns of accumulated treasury reserves.
- The succession discipline was now wholesale family slaughter. Aurangzeb's three surviving sons — **Muazzam**, **Azam Shah**, **Kam Bakhsh** — would now fight a civil war among themselves the moment Aurangzeb died. The pattern would repeat in subsequent generations.
- The provincial administration had begun to ossify. Senior provincial governors were treating their jagirs as hereditary fiefs in practice, even though the mansabdari system formally prohibited it. The central control that Akbar had built (Chapter 3) was eroding.

The eighteenth-century Mughal decline was not yet inevitable in 1707, but the structural conditions for it were all in place. The next chapter is mostly about how those structural problems played out.

Cross-civ check: Aurangzeb's reign (1658-1707) was almost exactly contemporary with the personal rule of **Louis XIV** of France (1661-1715) — and the parallels are striking. Both monarchs reigned for forty-plus years. Both centralized state power and built up the largest standing armies in their respective civilizations. Both pursued long expensive wars at the empire's periphery (Louis's Dutch Wars and War of the Spanish Succession; Aurangzeb's Deccan Wars) that drained the treasury and exhausted the institutional cohesion of the state. Both reversed the more pluralistic religious policies of their predecessors (Louis's revocation of the Edict of Nantes in 1685 driving Huguenot Protestants out of France; Aurangzeb's jizya reimposition in 1679 alienating Hindu subjects). Both presided over visible peaks of cultural production at their courts (Versailles vs. the late-Mughal cultural complex). Both left successors who would preside over visible institutional decline. The convergence is genuinely useful for thinking about the structural dynamics of seventeenth-century absolutist monarchy.

The closer comparison, though, is **Charles V** of the Habsburg Empire a century earlier (Holy Roman Emperor 1519-56). Charles, like Aurangzeb, presided over the largest contiguous empire of his era (Spain plus the Holy Roman Empire plus the Spanish American colonies plus the Burgundian Low Countries), pursued aggressive wars in pursuit of confessional Catholic policy, exhausted the treasury through ambitious campaigns, and ultimately abdicated in despair. Aurangzeb did not abdicate but he died on campaign in the Deccan having visibly failed to achieve his strategic objectives. Both reigns illustrate the same pattern — ambitious centralizing monarchs whose territorial expansion outran their institutional capacity to govern, producing visible peaks followed by visible collapses.

Aurangzeb himself, in his last letters to his sons, expressed something close to despair. The most famous of these letters, written to his son **Azam Shah** shortly before his death, reads in part: *"I came alone and I go as a stranger. I do not know who I am, nor what I have been doing. The instant which has passed in power has left only sorrow behind it... I have not been the guardian and protector of the empire. My valuable time has been passed vainly... I have a dread for my salvation and with what torments I may be punished."* The letter is sometimes cited as evidence that Aurangzeb died regretting his own policies. The reading may be too convenient — pious self-deprecation was a standard convention of late-life Mughal correspondence — but the language captures something true about how the reign ended. The empire he had spent fifty years expanding was visibly slipping out of his control, and he knew it.

# Chapter 7 — The Long Decline

The fifty years after Aurangzeb's death produced one of the most spectacular state collapses of the early-modern world.

In **1707**, the Mughal Empire was the largest contiguous state in the world by population, with revenue larger than any single European state, and a territorial extent that covered nearly the entire Indian subcontinent. By **1757**, it had effectively ceased to exist as a sovereign state. Bengal had broken away. The Marathas controlled most of central and western India. The Sikhs were on the rise in the Punjab. Hyderabad was independent under the Asaf Jahi dynasty. Awadh was independent under the Nawab. Mysore was independent under the Hindu Wodeyar dynasty (and, after 1761, under the Muslim general Hyder Ali). The Persians had sacked Delhi in 1739 and walked off with the Peacock Throne. The Mughal emperor at Delhi was reduced to a powerless figurehead, his actual authority limited to the city walls of Shahjahanabad and the surrounding districts.

The collapse was not driven by a single decisive event. It was the cumulative result of about a dozen interlocking structural problems — the succession-war discipline that killed potential heirs at each accession, the fiscal exhaustion of the Deccan wars, the weakening of central control over provincial governors, the rise of regional Hindu and Sikh military powers, the increasing penetration of European trading companies along the coasts, the periodic eruptions of military catastrophe (Nader Shah in 1739, the Marathas at Panipat in 1761) — none of which the post-Aurangzeb emperors had the institutional capacity to address. The dynasty continued to exist nominally until 1858 (Chapter 9). But the actual empire had ended fifty years earlier.

---

The post-Aurangzeb succession war (1707-12) set the pattern.

Aurangzeb's three surviving sons — Muazzam (the eldest, governor of Kabul), Azam Shah (the second, who had been with Aurangzeb in the Deccan), and Kam Bakhsh (the youngest, governor of Bijapur) — each declared themselves emperor within weeks of their father's death. Azam Shah, the most senior in the Deccan, marched immediately on Delhi. Muazzam, having a smaller initial army, retreated north and gathered Punjab and Afghan forces. The two armies met at **Jajau** near Agra on **June 8, 1707**. Azam Shah was killed in the battle. Muazzam took the throne as **Bahadur Shah I** (bah-HAH-door SHAH the first — he was the first Bahadur Shah, distinguished from Bahadur Shah Zafar, the very last Mughal emperor a century and a half later, Chapter 9). He then defeated Kam Bakhsh in 1709 at Hyderabad; Kam Bakhsh died of wounds shortly after.

Bahadur Shah I attempted a more conciliatory policy than his father. He reopened negotiations with the Marathas (releasing Sambhaji's son Shahu from Mughal custody to act as a Mughal-friendly Maratha sovereign — this would not work as planned, but the attempt was real). He toned down the orthodox Sunni rhetoric. He began discussions with the Rajput rulers about restoring the marriage-alliance relationship that had broken down under Aurangzeb. The reign showed promise of institutional repair.

He died in 1712 after only five years on the throne, age sixty-nine. His own succession produced a four-way civil war among his sons that took two years to resolve. The eventual winner, **Jahandar Shah**, lasted ten months before being deposed and executed by his nephew **Farrukhsiyar** (with the active backing of two senior nobles, the **Sayyid Brothers** of Barha, who would shortly become Mughal-empire kingmakers). The pattern of repeated civil war between rival Mughal princes had now firmly established itself.

---

The **Sayyid Brothers** — **Hussain Ali Khan** (the elder, who handled military affairs) and **Abdullah Khan** (the younger, who handled civil administration) — were two senior officers from a Sayyid family of Barha (a small district near Saharanpur in modern Uttar Pradesh) whose careers had risen under Bahadur Shah I and who had emerged as the senior power-brokers of the post-Bahadur Shah period. They installed Farrukhsiyar in 1713 in exchange for the most senior court appointments. They then deposed and murdered him in 1719 when he attempted to govern independently of them. They installed three more puppet emperors in quick succession: **Rafi-ud-Darjat** (reigned three months in 1719 before dying of tuberculosis), **Rafi-ud-Daula** (reigned three months in 1719 before dying of opium overdose), and **Muhammad Shah** (a teenage prince installed in late 1719 because the brothers thought he would be easy to control).

The Sayyid Brothers' three-year reign-by-proxy (1719-22) is one of the more peculiar episodes in Mughal history — two non-royal officers running the empire through a sequence of imperial figureheads, openly treating the dynasty as their political instrument. Muhammad Shah turned out to be less pliable than they had expected. He conspired with rival nobles, particularly with the senior officer **Asaf Jah I** (the future Nizam of Hyderabad, see below), and the Sayyid Brothers were outmaneuvered. Hussain Ali Khan was assassinated in 1720; Abdullah Khan was defeated in battle at Hasanpur and died in prison. The puppet phase ended.

But the damage was done. The empire's political authority had visibly become a contested prize among rival factions of nobles, with the imperial dynasty as a counter rather than as a sovereign actor. Subsequent reigns would continue this pattern — formal Mughal emperors at Delhi, real political power exercised by senior nobles or by the emerging regional dynasties.

---

**Muhammad Shah** (ruled 1719-48), nicknamed **Rangila** ("the Colorful") for his patronage of music, painting, and refined court life, presided over the empire's most spectacular fragmentation.

He reigned for twenty-nine years — by far the longest reign of the post-Aurangzeb period, longer than several of his predecessors and successors combined — and did almost nothing to address the empire's structural problems. He patronized music (his court included some of the great Hindustani classical musicians of the period), patronized painting (the late-Mughal painting style of Muhammad Shah's reign is distinctive — looser, more decorative, with strong European compositional influence), patronized poetry (the great Urdu poet **Mir Taqi Mir** was active at his court). He held court ceremonies, received ambassadors, dispensed honors. He did not, in any meaningful sense, govern.

Under his reign, the great regional powers broke away from Mughal authority one by one.

The **Marathas** were the dominant case. After Aurangzeb's death, the Marathas under successive **Peshwas** (peshva, the Brahmin chief ministers of the Maratha state — by the early eighteenth century the actual governors of the Maratha confederacy, with the Bhonsle royal family reduced to ceremonial figures) expanded out of their western Deccan base into central and northern India. **Baji Rao I** (Peshwa 1720-40) led campaigns deep into the Mughal heartland, raiding Delhi itself in 1737 and forcing Muhammad Shah to accept Maratha territorial demands. By the 1740s, the Marathas were collecting **chauth** (one-fourth of land revenue) and **sardeshmukhi** (an additional one-tenth) as tribute from most of central India. The Mughal emperor was, in effect, a Maratha tributary across most of his nominal empire.

The **Nizamate of Hyderabad** was the second major case. **Asaf Jah I** (originally Mir Qamar-ud-Din Khan, also known as Nizam-ul-Mulk), the senior officer who had helped Muhammad Shah eliminate the Sayyid Brothers, was rewarded with the governorship of the Deccan in **1724**. Within two years, he had broken with the Mughal court and established the autonomous **Nizamate of Hyderabad** — nominally still acknowledging Mughal suzerainty (the Nizam continued to mint coins in the Mughal emperor's name and to have the Friday prayer recited in the emperor's name), but in practice fully independent in foreign policy, military command, and revenue collection. The **Asaf Jahi dynasty** Asaf Jah founded would rule Hyderabad until India absorbed it in 1948 — making it, in dynastic terms, one of the longest-lasting Indian successor states.

The **Nawabate of Bengal** was the third major case. **Murshid Quli Khan**, originally a Mughal revenue official of Persian origin, founded the autonomous Nawabate in **1717** while still nominally a Mughal officer. By the 1740s under **Alivardi Khan**, Bengal was an independent state in all but name — and the wealthiest province in Asia. Bengal's textile manufacturing (Dhaka muslins, Murshidabad silks), its rice agriculture, its expanding trade with the European companies (English at Calcutta, Dutch at Chinsurah, French at Chandernagore), made it the single most economically important Indian province, and its political detachment from Mughal authority meant that economic importance flowed away from the Mughal treasury rather than toward it.

The **Awadh Nawabs** were the fourth major case. **Saadat Khan**, originally a Mughal noble of Persian descent, was appointed governor of Awadh (the region around modern Lucknow in Uttar Pradesh) in 1722 and quickly converted the governorship into an autonomous principality. The Awadh Nawabate would last (in various forms, eventually as a British client state) until the British annexation in 1856.

The **Sikh Khalsa** was emerging as a serious military power in the Punjab — the Khalsa order had been founded by Guru Gobind Singh in 1699 (Chapter 6), and through the early eighteenth century the Sikhs developed a guerrilla-warfare tradition that would mature into the Sikh Confederacy of the late eighteenth century and the Sikh Empire under Ranjit Singh in the early nineteenth century.

The **Rajput rulers** had reverted to substantial autonomy after the Aurangzeb-era ruptures, particularly in the larger states (Mewar, Marwar, Amber, Bikaner). They paid tribute to whoever was militarily ascendant in the region — the Mughals when Mughal forces were nearby, the Marathas when Maratha forces were nearby — but ran their own internal affairs without much interference.

By the late 1730s, the Mughal Empire was, in territorial-political terms, mostly an aggregate of regional successor states paying nominal allegiance to a powerless emperor at Delhi.

---

Then **Nader Shah** arrived.

**Nader Shah Afshar** (NAH-der SHAH ahf-SHAR) was the brilliant Iranian warlord who had emerged from the chaos of the Safavid collapse (Safavid Persia Ch 8) to reunify Iran in the late 1720s, expel the Afghan Hotaki occupiers, and establish himself as the new Iranian sovereign in 1736. By 1738, having consolidated his Iranian rule, he turned east toward Afghanistan and India.

The pretext for invasion was a refugee problem. Several rebel Afghan factions, having been driven out of Iran by Nader's campaigns, had fled into Mughal-claimed territory in eastern Afghanistan. Nader sent emissaries to Muhammad Shah demanding that the Mughals expel the refugees or hand them over. The Mughal court, paralyzed and unfocused, gave evasive responses. Nader took this as casus belli (which he had been looking for anyway) and invaded.

His army of perhaps 80,000 troops crossed the Khyber Pass in late 1738. Kabul fell. The Mughal Punjab governor surrendered Lahore. By February 1739, Nader's army was in the upper Punjab, and the Mughal court at Delhi finally raised an army to confront him. Muhammad Shah personally took the field with a force of perhaps 300,000 men plus war elephants — on paper the largest army in the world at the time.

The two armies met at **Karnal**, about 75 miles north of Delhi, on **February 24, 1739**. The result was the most lopsided Mughal defeat in two centuries. Nader's smaller force, equipped with up-to-date Iranian-Ottoman gunpowder weapons and tactics, used speed, deception, and concentrated artillery fire to break the Mughal cavalry charges. The Mughal field army was annihilated within a single afternoon. The Mughal commander Khan Dauran was killed; the Nizam of Hyderabad (who had brought a contingent to the imperial army) escaped only by negotiating his own surrender separately. Muhammad Shah fled the field. The road to Delhi was open.

---

Nader Shah occupied Delhi for fifty-seven days, from March 20 to May 16, 1739.

The first three days passed in nominal peace. Muhammad Shah formally surrendered, was confirmed in his nominal sovereignty, and accompanied Nader on a tour of the city. Then a rumor spread that Nader had been killed in his quarters, the Delhi populace attacked the Iranian troops, and several thousand Iranians were killed in street fighting on March 22. Nader, alive and furious, ordered systematic reprisals. Iranian troops conducted a methodical sack of Delhi for the next several days. The chronicles record death-toll estimates of 20,000 to 30,000 Delhi residents killed in the reprisals (some modern scholars suggest higher numbers, perhaps 50,000), though the precise count is impossible to recover.

The looting was systematic. Nader's troops collected an estimated 700 million rupees in cash, gold, jewels, and goods — enough wealth that Nader famously suspended taxation in Iran for three years on his return. The most prestigious individual items taken were the **Peacock Throne** (Chapter 5) and the **Koh-i-Noor diamond**. The throne was disassembled in Persia after Nader's assassination in 1747, with the Koh-i-Noor passing through Afghan and eventually Sikh and British hands (Chapter 8). Nader also took the imperial library, the imperial treasury contents, and a substantial portion of the Mughal court's elephant herd.

Nader withdrew from Delhi in May 1739, formally restoring Muhammad Shah to the throne (a humiliation Muhammad Shah survived for another nine years) and crossing the Indus back into Persia in June. He had spent less than four months on Indian soil. He had effectively destroyed the Mughal Empire as a great power. The Mughal treasury was empty. The Mughal field army was destroyed. The Mughal prestige — which had been a substantial part of how the empire actually functioned, since regional governors paid attention to the Mughal court partly because of its symbolic weight — was visibly broken.

The political consequences cascaded immediately. Within a year, the regional successor states were treating the Mughal court with open contempt. The Nizam of Hyderabad stopped pretending to obey imperial orders. The Marathas pushed northward more aggressively. The Bengal Nawabate became fully independent in everything but coinage. The Sikh military power expanded. The Mughal emperor's actual authority shrank to the immediate environs of Delhi.

The Mughal Empire would continue to exist, in a nominal sense, for another 119 years (until 1858, Chapter 9). But the empire as an effective political entity ended at Karnal on February 24, 1739, and was given its formal funeral in the streets of Delhi that March.

---

The 1740s and 1750s saw the consolidation of the post-Mughal regional order.

In 1747, **Ahmad Shah Durrani** (also known as Ahmad Shah Abdali), an Afghan general who had served under Nader Shah, founded the **Durrani Empire** (the modern Afghan state's predecessor) and immediately began conducting raids into Mughal-claimed territory in the Punjab. Ahmad Shah Durrani would invade north India eight times between 1747 and 1767 — most consequentially in 1761, when his forces met the expanding Marathas at the **Third Battle of Panipat** (the third major battle on the same plain north of Delhi where Babur had defeated Ibrahim Lodi in 1526 and Akbar's forces had defeated Hemu in 1556, Chapters 1 and 3). The Maratha army of perhaps 100,000-150,000 troops was destroyed; Maratha casualties of 60,000 to 100,000 in the battle and pursuit temporarily checked their northern expansion. The battle is sometimes considered the moment when the Marathas lost the chance to establish themselves as the post-Mughal sovereigns of north India. The vacuum left by the Maratha defeat would be filled, gradually, by the British.

Muhammad Shah died in 1748, age forty-six, at Delhi. His son **Ahmad Shah Bahadur** (no relation to the Afghan invader of similar name — confusingly, both contemporary monarchs were named "Ahmad Shah") inherited a wreck of an empire. He was deposed and blinded by his vizier in 1754. His successor **Alamgir II** was assassinated in 1759 by his own vizier. The vizier installed **Shah Alam II** as the next emperor; Shah Alam II would survive on the throne until 1806 in spite of being captured by the Marathas, captured by the British, blinded by an Afghan raider in 1788 (Chapter 9), and reduced to ceremonial status while the British East India Company took over the actual governance of the territory in his name.

The most consequential of the eighteenth-century events, in the long retrospective view, was a small obscure clash that nobody at the time recognized as significant.

---

In **1756**, the new Nawab of Bengal, the twenty-three-year-old **Siraj-ud-Daulah** (sih-RAHJ uhd DOW-lah), captured the East India Company settlement at **Calcutta**. The motivation was a complex mix of factors: the Nawab's irritation at Company fortifications around Calcutta (which he considered a violation of his territorial sovereignty), the Company's harboring of fugitives from Bengali political feuds, the Nawab's general inexperience and erratic temperament, and the financial pressure from court rivals who wanted to seize Company merchant wealth.

The capture itself was a quick military operation. The Company garrison surrendered. The Nawab's forces occupied the settlement.

What happened next is the famous (and substantially mythologized) **Black Hole of Calcutta** episode. According to the British accounts that would shortly become central to the imperial mythology of the Company's Indian conquest, Siraj-ud-Daulah's troops crammed 146 captured Britons into a small cell at the Calcutta fort overnight, and 123 of them died of suffocation in the heat by morning. The chief contemporary source was **John Zephaniah Holwell**, a Company official who survived the cell and wrote a detailed account that was published in London in 1758 to enormous public reaction.

Modern scholarship has substantially demolished the most extreme version of Holwell's account. The actual numbers were much smaller — modern scholars estimate around 64 prisoners, with 18 to 43 deaths overnight depending on which secondary source you trust. The cell was real, the suffocation was real, the deaths were real, but the scale and the deliberate-atrocity framing of Holwell's account were exaggerated. The Black Hole story became central British justification for the conquest of Bengal, which is one reason the British accounts amplified it. The Indian counter-narrative, that Holwell exaggerated for political effect, is broadly correct on the scale question but does not erase the underlying fact that prisoners did die of suffocation in a Bengal jail.

The political consequence was, however, enormous. The British public reaction to the Black Hole story (whether accurate or exaggerated) created political support for a Company military expedition to recapture Calcutta. The expedition was led by **Robert Clive** (KLIVE), a thirty-two-year-old Company officer with prior Indian military experience. Clive recaptured Calcutta in January 1757. He then began maneuvering for a much larger objective — the replacement of Siraj-ud-Daulah with a more pliable Nawab who would grant the Company expanded commercial privileges. Clive's instrument was bribery: he negotiated secretly with **Mir Jafar**, Siraj-ud-Daulah's senior military commander, offering a fortune for Mir Jafar to defect during the inevitable confrontation between Company forces and the Nawab's army.

The confrontation came on **June 23, 1757**, on a mango grove called **Plassey** (PLAS-ee, a corruption of Palashi), about 95 miles north of Calcutta.

The next chapter is mostly about what came after Plassey.

Cross-civ check: the eighteenth-century Mughal collapse coincided with several major contemporaneous transformations elsewhere. The **Ottoman Empire** under Ahmed III and his successors was in mid-arc of its own long decline (Ottoman Empire Ch 7 and Ch 8) — the parallel structural problems (succession instability, fiscal exhaustion, regional governors becoming autonomous, European commercial penetration) are striking. **Safavid Persia** had collapsed entirely in 1722 (Safavid Persia Ch 8) and was now reorganizing under Nader Shah and his successors. **Qing China** under the **Yongzheng Emperor** (1722-35) and the **Qianlong Emperor** (1735-96) was at peak — the largest single Qing expansion was happening in exactly the years the Mughal Empire was collapsing. **Tokugawa Japan** continued its sakoku isolation (Edo Japan Ch 5), wealthy and stable but disconnected from the Eurasian political-economic transformation. The **British Industrial Revolution** had begun in textile-mechanization (the flying shuttle was patented in 1733; the spinning jenny in 1764; the steam engine substantially developed by 1769) — the same transformation that would shortly destroy the Indian textile industry that had been the foundation of Mughal economic prosperity. The eighteenth century was an era of profound global restructuring, and the Mughal collapse is best understood as one regional component of that larger restructuring rather than as a specifically South Asian story.

---

# Chapter 8 — The Company Takes Over

The conquest of India by the British East India Company is one of the strangest events in the history of empire. A chartered commercial corporation, founded in 1600 by a group of London merchants for the purpose of trading in Asian spices and textiles, gradually transformed itself over the course of the eighteenth century into the sovereign government of the most populous part of the early-modern world. By 1800, the East India Company governed about 20% of the human race. The conquest was not directed from London; the British government did not formally take over until 1858 (Chapter 9). It was conducted by Company employees, financed by Company revenues, justified by Company commercial interests, and produced what is almost certainly the largest sustained accumulation of imperial wealth in modern history.

The conquest happened in three phases. The first phase was the **conquest of Bengal** (1757-65), which produced the wealth and political base for everything that followed. The second phase was the **wars against the regional Indian powers** (1767-1818) — three Anglo-Mysore Wars against the Wodeyars and Tipu Sultan, three Anglo-Maratha Wars against the Maratha Confederacy. The third phase was the **wars against the Sikhs** (1845-49) that completed the Company's conquest of north India.

This chapter covers all three phases compactly. The Mughal emperor at Delhi remained throughout — nominally still the sovereign, in practice a pensioner of the Company, his actual authority limited to the Red Fort and a few square miles of surrounding city. The Company collected the revenue, made the laws, and fought the wars. The emperor signed the firmans the Company drafted for him. The arrangement was, in formal legal terms, an absurdity, but it served everyone's purposes well enough that nobody dismantled it for a century.

---

**The Battle of Plassey** on June 23, 1757, was less a battle than a stage-managed political assassination.

Robert Clive's force totaled about 3,000 men — 800 European soldiers from the Company's regular regiments, around 2,200 sepoys (Indian soldiers in Company service, mostly recruited from the Bhojpuri-speaking districts of Bihar and eastern Uttar Pradesh), about 8 cannons. Siraj-ud-Daulah's army totaled approximately 50,000 men — perhaps 18,000 cavalry, 30,000 infantry, 50 cannons, and a substantial elephant contingent. The numerical disparity was about 17 to 1 against the Company.

The numerical disparity didn't matter, because Clive had bribed Siraj-ud-Daulah's commander **Mir Jafar** to keep his troops out of the fighting. Mir Jafar commanded approximately two-thirds of the Bengal army. He had agreed, in a secret treaty signed with Clive in early June, to keep his forces in reserve and to refuse to engage the Company forces. In exchange, Clive would install him as the new Nawab of Bengal, with substantial cash payments to himself personally and to several other senior Bengal officers who were also in on the conspiracy.

The battle proceeded as Clive had engineered it. The fighting actually conducted was between Company forces and the loyal portion of Siraj-ud-Daulah's army (perhaps 12,000-15,000 men, commanded by the loyalist general **Mir Madan**, who was killed in the early fighting). A heavy mid-morning rain damaged the loyalist artillery (which was not protected) but not the Company artillery (which had been covered with tarpaulins). With Mir Madan dead and the loyalist artillery silent, Clive's Company forces moved forward, the loyal portion of the Bengal army began to retreat, Mir Jafar's contingents stood watching, and within a few hours the battle was over. Total casualties were minimal — perhaps 500 dead on the Bengal side, perhaps 70 dead on the Company side. Siraj-ud-Daulah fled the field, was captured a few days later, and was assassinated on Mir Jafar's orders.

Clive entered the Bengal capital **Murshidabad** in late June and installed Mir Jafar as the new Nawab. The financial settlement was extraordinary. Mir Jafar paid the Company £2.34 million (approximately £400 million in modern terms, conservatively estimated), with additional £234,000 personally to Clive himself. The new Nawab also granted the Company the **zamindari** (landlord rights) over an additional 24 districts around Calcutta, effectively converting much of southern Bengal into Company-administered territory.

Plassey is conventionally taken as the starting point of British India. The military victory was modest. The political coup was decisive. The Company had transformed itself from a commercial trading partner into the de facto sovereign of the wealthiest province in India.

---

**Mir Jafar**'s career as Nawab of Bengal was short. He proved (predictably) ineffective as a ruler — his court was riven by factional conflict, his revenue collection deteriorated, the Company found him uncooperative on additional commercial demands. The Company deposed him in 1760 and installed his son-in-law **Mir Qasim** as the new Nawab. Mir Qasim turned out to be more capable than the Company had expected, attempted to reorganize the Bengal military and resist Company demands, and was promptly deposed in turn (1763) and replaced with the now-old-and-pliable Mir Jafar again.

Mir Qasim's response was to ally with the Mughal emperor **Shah Alam II** and the Nawab of Awadh **Shuja-ud-Daulah** in a coordinated military effort to drive the Company out of Bengal. The combined armies of the three Indian rulers met the Company forces under **Hector Munro** at **Buxar** on October 22, 1764.

The **Battle of Buxar** was a more serious military engagement than Plassey. The Mughal-Awadh-Bengal coalition fielded perhaps 40,000 troops; Munro's force was about 7,000 (1,800 European, 5,200 sepoy). There was no bribery this time — the Indian commanders were committed and competent. But Company forces had now had seven years to consolidate their organizational and tactical advantages, and the Indian forces were uncoordinated. Munro's troops broke the coalition's center, the Indian armies retreated in confusion, and within a single day the Company had defeated the most serious Indian military challenge it would face for the next forty years.

The political consequences of Buxar were enormous. The Treaty of Allahabad in **1765** transferred the **Diwani** — the right to collect land revenue — over Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa from the Mughal emperor to the Company. Shah Alam II formally granted the Diwani in exchange for a Company pension of 26 lakh rupees per year (approximately £260,000). The Nawab of Awadh, Shuja-ud-Daulah, had to pay a war indemnity of 50 lakh rupees and accept a Company resident at his court. The Company now legally controlled the revenue of the wealthiest province in India, with the Mughal emperor's formal endorsement.

The actual administrative arrangement was peculiar. The Company collected the revenue but did not, initially, govern Bengal directly — it operated a kind of dual government, with the Nawab nominally administering civil and criminal justice while the Company ran the revenue collection and the military. The arrangement was dysfunctional (the Nawab had no resources, the Company had no political-cultural infrastructure), and by the 1770s it was being progressively unwound in favor of more direct Company administration. By 1772, under **Warren Hastings** as the new Governor-General, the Company had effectively become the direct government of Bengal.

---

The wealth that flowed from Bengal in the next four decades was, by modern estimates, the largest single accumulation of imperial wealth in early-modern history.

The Bengal land revenue collection alone produced approximately £2 million per year in the 1770s and grew steadily through the rest of the century. The Company's customs duties at Calcutta produced additional revenue. The Company's own commercial operations — buying Bengal cotton textiles, indigo dye, opium, and other commodities, selling them in Britain, China, and Southeast Asia — generated additional profits. The opium trade in particular became a major revenue source: Company-monopolized opium production in eastern India was sold at Company auctions in Calcutta, transported to Canton in China by private merchants, and exchanged for tea (which the Company sold in Britain at substantial profit). The opium-tea-textile triangle became the financial foundation of late-eighteenth-century British imperial wealth (the triangle's full arc — opium, cotton, empire — is the subject of Industrial Revolution Ch 19; the Manchester cotton machinery on the British end is Ch 3).

The Indian impact was correspondingly devastating. Bengal's economy contracted under Company rule. The textile manufacturing centers (especially around Dhaka, the muslin capital) declined as the Company's purchasing terms forced producers to sell at fixed low prices (the mechanized Manchester mills on the other end of this story are Industrial Revolution Ch 3). The agricultural sector deteriorated as the Company's revenue assessment was raised to maximize extraction. The **Bengal Famine of 1770** killed approximately 10 million people — about a third of the Bengal population — partly from drought but substantially exacerbated by Company revenue policies that maintained tax demands while grain prices spiraled. The famine was probably the worst in eighteenth-century world history, and it happened under Company rule directly.

Modern scholars have debated the extent to which "drain" of wealth from India to Britain contributed to British industrialization in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. The estimates vary substantially. The most cautious scholars suggest the drain was modest as a fraction of British GDP. The most aggressive scholars (most prominently the Indian historian **Utsa Patnaik**) suggest the drain was enormous and central to British industrialization. The honest answer is somewhere between — the drain was real, was substantial, and was probably significant in financing the British Industrial Revolution, but other factors (technological innovation, coal availability, institutional structures) were also important. What is not disputable is that the conquest of Bengal made the Company immensely rich and made Bengal substantially poorer.

---

The expansion to the south came through the **Anglo-Mysore Wars** (1767-99), four conflicts against the Hindu Wodeyar dynasty of Mysore (succeeded after 1761 by the Muslim general **Hyder Ali** and his son **Tipu Sultan**, TEE-poo SUL-tahn).

Hyder Ali, originally a soldier in Mysore service, had taken effective control of the Mysore state in the 1760s while keeping the Wodeyar maharaja on the throne as a figurehead. He was an able military commander who built up the Mysore army using up-to-date European military techniques (he hired French military advisors and equipped his troops with French-made muskets and artillery). He fought the First Anglo-Mysore War (1767-69) to a draw, fought the Second Anglo-Mysore War (1780-84) inconclusively. He died in 1782 during the Second War, leaving his son Tipu Sultan as his successor.

Tipu Sultan was a more remarkable figure than his father. He was a serious military innovator — most famously the pioneer of **military rocket warfare**, deploying iron-cased rockets in the Mysore army that delivered explosive warheads at ranges of up to two miles. The British copied the Mysore rockets directly: the **Congreve rocket**, developed by William Congreve in the early 1800s and used by the British army through the Napoleonic Wars and beyond, was substantially based on the Mysore rocket designs. The "rockets' red glare" in the American national anthem refers to Congreve rockets fired by the British navy at Fort McHenry in 1814 — derived directly, in technological lineage, from Tipu Sultan's Mysore artillery.

Tipu was also a serious diplomat. He sent embassies to Ottoman Constantinople (seeking support from the Ottoman caliph), to revolutionary France (signing a treaty of friendship with the French Republic in 1798), to various other anti-British powers, attempting to build a coalition against Company expansion. He patronized scholarship, built libraries, modernized his administration, and in many respects was the most administratively capable of the late-eighteenth-century Indian rulers.

He fought the Third Anglo-Mysore War (1790-92) and lost about half his territory to the Company. He fought the Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1798-99) and was killed defending his capital **Seringapatam** (sih-ring-ah-PAT-am, also called Srirangapatna, on an island in the Kaveri river south of Bangalore) in May 1799. The Company restored the Wodeyar dynasty to the Mysore throne as a Company-client kingdom and absorbed roughly two-thirds of the former Mysore territory directly into Company administration.

Tipu's defeat is sometimes cited as the moment when the Indian military challenge to the Company effectively ended. The judgment is too strong (the Marathas and Sikhs would still produce serious wars over the next half-century), but it captures something true: by 1800, no individual Indian ruler had the military or financial resources to seriously contest the Company's territorial expansion.

---

The **Anglo-Maratha Wars** (1775-82, 1803-05, 1817-18) destroyed the Maratha Confederacy and gave the Company control of central and western India.

The Marathas had emerged as the dominant Indian military power after the Maratha-Mughal wars under Aurangzeb (Chapter 6) and the post-Aurangzeb expansion under successive Peshwas (Chapter 7). By the late eighteenth century, the Maratha Confederacy was a loose alliance of five major chieftaincies: the **Peshwa** at Pune (the central authority, head of the Confederacy), the **Scindia** at Gwalior (the dominant force in north-central India), the **Holkar** at Indore (Malwa region), the **Bhonsle** at Nagpur (eastern Deccan), and the **Gaekwad** at Baroda (Gujarat). The combined Maratha military power was the largest single Indian political-military force at the end of the eighteenth century.

The first Anglo-Maratha War (1775-82) was a Company defeat — a Company expedition to install a Peshwa-claimant was crushingly defeated at the Battle of Wadgaon in 1779, and the Treaty of Salbai (1782) restored the prewar boundaries. The Company learned from this and prepared more carefully.

The second Anglo-Maratha War (1803-05) was the Company's decisive victory. The young Company general **Arthur Wellesley** (the future **Duke of Wellington**, who would defeat Napoleon at Waterloo twelve years later) defeated the combined Scindia-Bhonsle army at the **Battle of Assaye** on September 23, 1803. Wellington reportedly described Assaye later in his life as the toughest battle he ever fought — and the man had Waterloo to compare it to, which gives a sense of how seriously contested the Maratha military was. The Company also defeated the Marathas at the simultaneous Battle of Argaon and the siege of Gawilghur fort. The treaty of Surji-Anjangaon (1803) and Deogaon (1803) transferred substantial Maratha territories to the Company.

The third Anglo-Maratha War (1817-18) was the final destruction of Maratha independence. The Peshwa at Pune attempted to organize one last resistance; the Company crushed it; the Peshwa was deposed; the Maratha Confederacy was formally dissolved; the chieftaincies were reduced to Company-client princely states. By 1818, the Company had effective control of most of central and western India.

---

The **Anglo-Sikh Wars** (1845-46, 1848-49) completed the Company's conquest.

The **Sikh Empire** in the Punjab had been built up under **Maharaja Ranjit Singh** (ruled 1801-39), the most able Indian state-builder of the early nineteenth century, who had united the Sikh principalities of the Punjab, modernized the Sikh army using European military advisors (the Sikh Khalsa Army of the 1830s was probably the most up-to-date Indian army of its time), and built a state that controlled the entire upper Indus basin from Kashmir to Multan. After Ranjit Singh's death in 1839, the Sikh Empire fell into a succession crisis with multiple short-lived rulers and a powerful military council that increasingly dominated state affairs.

The Company, alarmed by the proximity of the powerful Sikh state to its Bengal territories and by the political instability of the post-Ranjit succession, provoked a confrontation in 1845. The First Anglo-Sikh War (December 1845 to March 1846) saw a series of hard-fought battles (Mudki, Ferozeshah, Aliwal, Sobraon) in which the Sikh army acquitted itself well but was eventually defeated by Company forces under Sir Hugh Gough. The Treaty of Lahore (1846) reduced the Sikh state to client status. The Second Anglo-Sikh War (1848-49) finished the job. The decisive Battle of Gujrat (1849) destroyed the remaining Sikh military power. The Punjab was annexed to British India in March 1849.

The annexation included one specific item that has become famous: the **surrender of the Koh-i-Noor diamond**, which had passed from Mughal possession after Nader Shah's 1739 sack (Chapter 7) through Afghan hands (the Durrani dynasty) into Sikh hands during Ranjit Singh's reign. The diamond was surrendered as part of the 1849 settlement, transported to England, recut by Garrard's of London at Queen Victoria's instruction in 1852 (the recut reduced its weight from 186 carats to 105 carats but improved its visual brilliance), and set into the British crown. It now sits in the Tower of London, the subject of recurring restitution claims by the Indian, Pakistani, Afghan, and Iranian governments. The diamond's path — Mughal court > Persian conqueror > Afghan king > Sikh maharaja > British queen — is in itself a compact map of the eighteenth- and nineteenth-century political collapse of South Asian sovereignty.

---

By 1849, the East India Company governed effectively the entire Indian subcontinent, either directly (in the major provinces — Bengal, Bombay, Madras, Punjab, Bihar, Orissa, parts of central India) or indirectly through client princely states (Hyderabad, Mysore, Awadh, Travancore, Kashmir, Rajput states, Sikh chieftaincies, several hundred smaller princely states). The Company had a private army of approximately 250,000 troops (mostly Indian sepoys, with a smaller European officer corps), a private revenue-collection apparatus, a private court system, and a private bureaucracy. It was, in operational terms, a sovereign state. The chartered commercial corporation founded by London merchants in 1600 had become the largest empire in Asia.

The Mughal emperor at Delhi remained throughout. **Shah Alam II** (1759-1806) had been the Mughal emperor at the time of the Diwani grant in 1765. His successor **Akbar Shah II** (1806-37) had been the Mughal emperor through the Anglo-Maratha Wars. **Bahadur Shah Zafar** (1837-57) was the Mughal emperor at the time of the Anglo-Sikh Wars. None of them had any actual political power. They received Company pensions, presided over ceremonial functions at the Red Fort, patronized the Urdu poetry and music that was the cultural specialty of late-Mughal Delhi, and signed whatever the Company wanted them to sign. They were, formally, sovereign monarchs of an empire that no longer existed.

The next chapter is mostly about the moment when the Company miscalculated, the sepoys mutinied, and the dynastic ceremony got dragged into the actual political conflict that ended both the dynasty and the Company.

Cross-civ check: the Company's Indian conquest happened during the same decades as several other major imperial transformations. The **American Revolution** (1775-83) coincided with the First Anglo-Maratha War and the post-Plassey consolidation of Company rule in Bengal — Britain lost its American empire while gaining its Indian empire, a substitution that was not lost on contemporary British observers. The **French Revolution** (1789-99) and the **Napoleonic Wars** (1803-15) coincided with the Third and Fourth Anglo-Mysore Wars and the Second Anglo-Maratha War — Tipu Sultan attempted to align with revolutionary France, and his death at Seringapatam in 1799 was directly relevant to British strategic concerns about French penetration of South Asia. The **Latin American independence wars** (1810-25) coincided with the late Anglo-Maratha campaigns. The **Opium Wars** in China (1839-42, 1856-60) coincided with the Anglo-Sikh Wars — and were closely connected, since the opium that the British forced China to import came from Company-controlled production in Bengal and Bihar. The Company's Indian conquest was one component of a global British imperial expansion that, between roughly 1763 and 1858, made Britain the dominant power of the nineteenth-century world. The Mughal collapse and the British rise are two halves of a single story.

---

# Chapter 9 — The 1857 Rebellion and the End

The Mughal Empire ended in **1858**, three hundred and thirty-two years after Babur's victory at Panipat.

The proximate cause was the **Indian Rebellion of 1857** (also called the **Sepoy Mutiny** in older British accounts, the **First War of Indian Independence** in some Indian nationalist accounts, and the **1857 Uprising** in most modern academic usage — the choice of name carries political baggage that is best acknowledged rather than papered over). The rebellion began as a mutiny of Indian soldiers in Company service at **Meerut** in May 1857, spread across north India over the following weeks, focused on Delhi where the eighty-two-year-old **Bahadur Shah Zafar** was reluctantly proclaimed emperor of India by the rebels, was suppressed by Company forces over the following fifteen months at enormous cost in life on both sides, and ended with the British government formally taking over the administration of India from the East India Company and exiling the last Mughal emperor to Burma. The Mughal dynasty died in this convulsion, after lingering for fifty years past any effective political relevance.

This chapter covers the rebellion compactly — the trigger, the spread, the ceremonial role of Bahadur Shah Zafar, the British suppression, the Delhi reprisals, the formal dissolution. Then it spends some time on the longer question of what the Mughals actually left to South Asia and to the world — which, even after 1858, was substantial.

---

The **trigger** was the Enfield rifle cartridges.

The Company had introduced the **Pattern 1853 Enfield rifle** to Indian service in 1857. The new weapon used pre-greased paper cartridges that the soldier had to bite open before loading. The grease was a mixture of animal fats — and a rumor spread among Indian sepoys (both Hindu and Muslim) that the grease was a mix of cow tallow and pig lard. For Hindu soldiers, biting beef-greased cartridges would be a major religious pollution (the cow being sacred). For Muslim soldiers, biting pork-greased cartridges would be a major religious pollution (pigs being haram). The combination of these religious offenses, applied simultaneously to two different religious communities by the same weapon, looked from the sepoy perspective like a deliberate Company plan to humiliate or even forcibly convert Indian soldiers.

The Company army administrators recognized the problem and quickly modified the cartridge greasing — switching to vegetable oil and beeswax, allowing soldiers to grease their own cartridges, withdrawing the original cartridges from issue. The damage was already done. The trust between the Company and its Indian soldiers had been substantially eroded for years by other grievances (low pay, poor treatment, restrictions on overseas service, the recent annexation of Awadh by Lord Dalhousie in 1856 which had stripped many sepoys of their home-state privileges). The cartridge issue was the last straw. The withdrawal of the cartridges did not undo the damage.

The first significant mutiny incident was at **Meerut** on **May 10, 1857**, when sepoys of the 3rd Bengal Light Cavalry mutinied after eighty-five of their colleagues had been court-martialed and sentenced to long prison terms for refusing to handle the Enfield cartridges. The Meerut mutineers killed several British officers and the British civilians they could find, then marched south through the night to **Delhi**, arriving on May 11.

---

At Delhi, the Meerut mutineers reached the Red Fort and presented themselves to the Mughal emperor.

**Bahadur Shah Zafar** (bah-HAH-door SHAH ZAH-far) was eighty-two years old. He had been the nominal Mughal emperor since 1837. His actual authority extended only to the Red Fort and the surrounding areas of Shahjahanabad. He was a serious Urdu poet (the pen name Zafar — "victory" — was his literary signature; his collected poetry, the **Diwan-i-Zafar**, is part of the canon of Urdu classical poetry) and a devoted Sufi disciple of the Chishti order. He had spent his reign managing the elaborate rituals of the Mughal court while drawing his Company pension and signing whatever the Company put in front of him. He was, by every contemporary account, a kind, cultured, indecisive man who had no personal interest in being part of an anti-British rebellion.

The Meerut mutineers presented him with a fait accompli. They had killed European officers; they had killed European civilians; they had marched on Delhi expecting the Mughal emperor to lead them. They proclaimed him emperor of India on the morning of May 11. He, reportedly, tried to refuse. They insisted. He acquiesced — partly under pressure, partly because his own Mughal court was sympathetic to the rebellion (his eldest surviving son Mirza Mughal was particularly enthusiastic), partly because the alternative was being killed or deposed by the mutineers themselves.

The proclamation made him, formally, the head of the rebellion. Whether he was a willing rebel leader or an unwilling figurehead is one of the questions that divides modern historical interpretation of the 1857 Uprising. The British court that would later try him for treason chose to treat him as the willing leader. Modern scholarship is more ambivalent — most historians now see Bahadur Shah as a substantially passive figure caught up in events he had no power to direct, but the dynastic-political symbolism of an actual Mughal emperor proclaimed by the rebels was undeniable, and he did sign rebel firmans and receive rebel commanders during the Delhi siege.

---

The rebellion **spread** across north India through May, June, and July 1857.

Major centers of mutiny included Meerut (May 10), Delhi (May 11), Lucknow (May 30), Cawnpore (Kanpur — June 5), Allahabad (June 6), Jhansi (June 6), Gwalior (June 14), and several dozen other smaller posts. The geographic distribution corresponded roughly to the recruiting grounds of the Bengal Army (Bihar, eastern Uttar Pradesh, Awadh, parts of Bengal). The Bombay Army and the Madras Army largely did not mutiny, partly because their recruiting was different and partly because their grievances were less acute. Most of the south, west, and far north of India did not rebel.

The character of the rebellion varied substantially by region. At Delhi, the rebellion was organized around the Mughal court and had a recognizable dynastic-political character. At Lucknow, the rebellion was organized around the deposed Awadh royal family (Begum Hazrat Mahal acting as regent for her son, the displaced heir) and had a regional-monarchical character. At Cawnpore, the rebellion was led by the **Nana Sahib** (an adopted son of the last Peshwa, with a personal grievance against the Company over his denied pension) and produced one of the most notorious atrocities of the rebellion when British civilians, including women and children, were massacred at the Bibighar after surrendering on a guarantee of safe passage. At Jhansi, the rebellion was led by **Rani Lakshmi Bai** (the widowed queen of the small principality of Jhansi, whose state had been annexed by the Company under the Doctrine of Lapse, which became a martial heroine in subsequent Indian nationalist mythology). The various regional rebellions had different leaders, different goals, and limited coordination.

The unifying feature was the breakdown of the Company-sepoy relationship. The British civilian and military death toll across north India was approximately 6,000 in the first weeks. The Indian death toll — both rebels killed in fighting and civilians killed in retaliatory British actions — would eventually run into the hundreds of thousands, possibly close to a million across the full eighteen-month period of the rebellion and its aftermath. The 1857 Uprising was the bloodiest single event in nineteenth-century Indian history.

---

The **British recapture of Delhi** in **September 1857** was the symbolic and military turning point.

A British force, the **Delhi Field Force**, was assembled at Karnal in late May and marched south to invest Delhi from the **Ridge** (a low ridge of high ground north of the city). The siege ran from June through September 1857, with the British force consistently outnumbered by the rebels inside the city but maintained by a constant flow of reinforcements from the Punjab (which had not rebelled, partly because Sikh and Punjabi Muslim soldiers in Company service had no particular reason to support the Mughal emperor and were happy to fight against the Bengal sepoys who had often been their professional rivals).

The decisive assault came on **September 14, 1857**, with British and Sikh-Punjabi forces breaking through the city walls at the Kashmiri Gate and fighting through the streets of Shahjahanabad over the following five days. The Red Fort was taken on September 19. Bahadur Shah Zafar fled the fort with his immediate family and took refuge at the **Tomb of Humayun** (his great-great-great-great-great-great-great-grandfather, the second Mughal emperor whose tomb had been built three hundred years earlier, Chapter 2). He was captured there on September 21 by a British officer, **William Hodson**.

What followed was systematic violence against the Delhi population. The British forces conducted house-to-house searches, executed Indian men suspected of having participated in the rebellion (the threshold of suspicion was sometimes very low), expelled the surviving Muslim population of Shahjahanabad from the city for several months, looted the Red Fort and the great houses of the Mughal nobility, and demolished portions of the city to widen streets for military access. The Delhi population in 1857 was approximately 160,000; by 1861 it had dropped to perhaps 50,000-60,000, partly from killings, partly from expulsions, partly from emigration.

The most notorious individual incident was Hodson's execution of Bahadur Shah's three sons (his heir Mirza Mughal, his second son Mirza Khizr Sultan, and his grandson Mirza Abu Bakr) on September 22, 1857, the day after their father's surrender. Hodson had captured the three princes near the Tomb of Humayun and was escorting them back to Delhi under guard. He stopped the convoy, ordered them to strip to their underclothes, ordered them to dismount, and shot them personally in the chest with his revolver. The justification Hodson gave later was that crowds were assembling to attempt a rescue and he could not safely continue with the prisoners. The actual reason was almost certainly personal — Hodson wanted to be the man who had killed the Mughal princes. The bodies were displayed publicly at Delhi for several days. The location of the killings, near a gateway in the Mughal city wall, was named **Khooni Darwaza** ("Bloody Gate") and is still called that.

The British reprisals at Delhi went on for months. The Mughal court was dispersed, the great Mughal-era Muslim landholding families were dispossessed, and the cultural-economic infrastructure of late-Mughal Delhi was substantially destroyed. The Urdu poet **Mirza Ghalib** (mir-ZAH GHAH-lib, 1797-1869), who had been the literary genius of late-Mughal Delhi and a favorite courtier of Bahadur Shah Zafar, lived through the siege and its aftermath in Delhi and recorded the catastrophe in letters and a Persian-language journal called **Dastambu**. Ghalib's letters from the post-siege period are some of the most affecting documents of Indian history — he describes the streets empty of people he had known, the houses of friends torn down for British military purposes, the sense of a world he had spent his life inside ending around him.

The British retook Lucknow in November 1857, suppressed the remaining major rebellions through 1858 (the Rani of Jhansi was killed in battle in June 1858; Nana Sahib disappeared and was never captured; smaller resistance fighters held out in Awadh and Central India until late 1858), and declared the rebellion over in late 1858. The total Indian casualties — combatants and civilians, killed and missing, including the famine and disease deaths driven by the war's disruption — are now estimated at hundreds of thousands at minimum, possibly close to a million.

---

The **dissolution of the Mughal dynasty** was made formal in 1858.

Bahadur Shah Zafar was tried by a British military commission in **January-March 1858**, sitting in the Diwan-i-Khas of the Red Fort (the same hall where the Peacock Throne had stood until Nader Shah took it in 1739). The charges were treason against the Crown of England, complicity in the murder of British officers and civilians, and conspiracy in the rebellion. The trial ran for forty days, with extensive testimony from witnesses to events at the Red Fort during the rebellion. Bahadur Shah was convicted on all charges. The sentence was exile to Rangoon (modern Yangon), where he was conveyed by ship in October 1858 along with his wife Zinat Mahal and his surviving family members. He died there on **November 7, 1862**, age eighty-seven, four years into exile. He was buried at an unmarked grave to prevent his tomb becoming a pilgrimage destination. His grave was located only in 1991 and is now a small commemorative shrine.

The **Government of India Act 1858** (passed by the British Parliament in August 1858) transferred power from the East India Company to the British Crown. The Company was dissolved as a sovereign authority. The British government appointed a **Viceroy of India** (the first being Lord Canning, the previous Governor-General) to administer India directly. The British monarch was henceforth styled "Empress of India" (a title formally adopted by Queen Victoria in 1876). The princely states that had been Company-client sovereigns now became British-Crown-client sovereigns, with substantially the same arrangements but a new sovereign at the top of the chain.

The Mughal dynasty was extinguished. The line of Babur, Humayun, Akbar, Jahangir, Shah Jahan, Aurangzeb, and the eighteenth-century successors had ended at Bahadur Shah Zafar's exile. The last surviving sons and grandsons of the dynasty died in obscurity in Rangoon, Calcutta, or Hyderabad over the next several decades. The descendants who exist today have no political claim and most live private lives in India and Pakistan.

---

What did the Mughals leave?

The simplest answer is the architecture. The major Mughal monuments — the Taj Mahal, Humayun's Tomb, the Red Fort at Delhi, Fatehpur Sikri, Akbar's Tomb at Sikandra, Jama Masjid in Delhi, Badshahi Mosque in Lahore, the Lahore Fort, the Shalimar Gardens at Lahore, the various provincial monuments — are still standing, mostly preserved, mostly on UNESCO World Heritage lists. Modern visitors to north India and Pakistan encounter Mughal architecture as the dominant pre-modern visual culture of the region. The architectural inheritance is substantively unbroken from 1530 to the present.

The cuisine is the second most visible inheritance. The biryanis, the kebabs, the naan and roti breads, the kheer and gulab jamun and jalebi sweets, the entire vocabulary of north Indian Persianate cooking (and much of Pakistani and Bangladeshi cuisine) — these are Mughal-era developments that have been continuously practiced from the early modern period to the present. The modern Indian-Pakistani food world is, gastronomically, a Mughal inheritance with regional and modern modifications.

The third inheritance is linguistic. **Urdu**, which had emerged as a Persianate register of north Indian Hindi-Khariboli during the late Sultanate period (Delhi Sultanate Ch 6) and matured as a literary language at the eighteenth- and nineteenth-century Mughal and Awadh courts (with the great Urdu poets Mir Taqi Mir, Mirza Ghalib, Allama Iqbal, and others), is now the national language of Pakistan and one of the major languages of north India. The Persian-derived vocabulary of Hindi and Urdu — for legal terms, administrative terms, courtly terms, romantic terms — is substantially Mughal in origin. Even Hindi, which has been deliberately Sanskritized in independent India to distinguish it from Urdu, retains a substantial Mughal-era Persian vocabulary in its everyday spoken form.

The fourth inheritance is political-institutional. The British Raj that succeeded the Mughals inherited the Mughal administrative-fiscal structure substantially intact, modifying it gradually rather than replacing it. The mansabdari-jagirdari system of the Mughal nobility evolved into the British Indian Civil Service and the princely-state system. The Mughal land-revenue assessment evolved into the British Indian land-revenue system. The Mughal-era provincial boundaries (suba, sarkar, pargana) substantially survived as British provincial-and-district boundaries, and substantially survive in the modern Indian and Pakistani administrative geography. The institutional template of the modern Indian and Pakistani states is, in significant respects, a Mughal template.

The fifth inheritance is the cultural-religious geography of the modern subcontinent. The Mughals expanded the Indian Muslim community substantially through the conversion of certain regional populations (eastern Bengal, western Punjab and Sindh, parts of the Deccan), and consolidated the Indo-Persian high-cultural synthesis that defined Indian Muslim identity. The eventual partition of British India in 1947, into majority-Hindu India and majority-Muslim Pakistan, mapped substantially onto the demographic-religious geography that the Sultanate and Mughal periods had created. The 1947 Partition is, in some respects, the long-delayed second-order consequence of the Mughal religious-political settlement and its eventual unraveling.

---

The **Mughal inheritance is contested** in modern South Asia, and the contestation is itself worth a brief discussion.

For Indian secular-nationalist historiography (the dominant academic tradition since independence in 1947, associated with Marxist-influenced scholars at Delhi University, Aligarh Muslim University, and Jawaharlal Nehru University), the Mughals are part of the inclusive composite culture of pre-modern India — a Persianate Islamic dynasty that integrated Hindu and Muslim elements into a single state and culture, that produced one of the world's great pre-modern civilizations, and whose legacy is part of the shared Indian cultural inheritance available to citizens of all religions.

For Indian Hindu-nationalist historiography (the tradition associated with V.D. Savarkar, K.B. Hedgewar, and the Sangh Parivar of the late twentieth and early twenty-first century), the Mughals are foreign occupiers who suppressed Hindu civilization, destroyed Hindu temples (most famously, in this reading, the temples at Mathura, Varanasi, and Ayodhya — the Babri Masjid at Ayodhya, allegedly built on the site of Lord Ram's birthplace, was destroyed by Hindu activists in 1992 in one of the most consequential single events of late-twentieth-century Indian politics), and represent an interruption of authentic Indian civilization that needs to be reversed in modern Indian politics. Akbar in this reading is a useful idiot whose tolerance helped Muslim political dominance; Aurangzeb is a religious fanatic whose policies are taken as paradigmatic of Muslim rule generally.

For Pakistani historiography (the tradition associated with Ishtiaq Hussain Qureshi and others), the Mughals are foundational ancestors of the Indian Muslim community and its eventual political expression in Pakistan. Aurangzeb is a particular Pakistani nationalist hero (a serious orthodox Muslim ruler who resisted the syncretist tendencies of his predecessors). Akbar is a more uncomfortable figure (his religious tolerance is sometimes read as departing from properly Islamic governance).

For Bangladeshi historiography, the Mughals are the dynasty under which eastern Bengal experienced its mass conversion to Islam (the demographic-religious shift that made Bangladesh majority-Muslim and that produced the eventual Bangladesh Liberation War of 1971 against Pakistan). The Mughal period is centrally important to Bangladeshi national identity, but with regional Bengali emphasis rather than the Delhi-centric framing that dominates Indian and Pakistani Mughal historiography.

For modern American and European scholarship on the Mughals, the dynasty is increasingly viewed as one of the major early-modern world states, comparable in importance to the Ottoman Empire, Ming-Qing China, Tokugawa Japan, and the European absolutist monarchies. The "early-modern global history" framework that has dominated academic world history since the 1990s places the Mughals as central rather than peripheral, with substantial scholarship on Mughal economic productivity, administrative innovation, cultural production, and global commercial connections.

The historiographical wars are ongoing. They will probably continue for as long as modern South Asian politics depends on competing readings of the pre-colonial past — which is to say, indefinitely. The honest scholarly response is the one this narrative has tried to take throughout: present the evidence, acknowledge the contested interpretive frameworks, and try to remain open to what the evidence supports without forcing it into any single political reading.

---

The **British "civilizing mission" question** also deserves a moment.

A central claim of nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century British colonial historiography (most famously in Macaulay's 1835 **Minute on Indian Education**, but the framework was much wider) was that British rule rescued India from Mughal-era despotism, religious obscurantism, and economic stagnation, bringing modern science, modern law, modern administration, and economic development to a backward society. The civilizing mission was the moral justification for the colonial project.

The historical record does not support this claim. Pre-British Mughal India was, by every available measure of pre-modern wealth, the richest of the early-modern world states. The Bengal that the Company conquered in 1757 had a per-capita income probably comparable to or higher than contemporary England. The textile manufacturing at Dhaka was the most sophisticated in the world. The Mughal administrative apparatus that the Company inherited was, in operational terms, more elaborate than anything the British had built in their own kingdom. The Mughal court culture was more refined than anything in early-modern Europe. There was nothing for British rule to "civilize."

What British rule did was extract. The Bengal Famine of 1770 killed approximately 10 million people in part because of Company revenue policies. The destruction of the Indian textile industry, accomplished partly through punitive tariffs in Britain that excluded Indian textiles from the British market while flooding the Indian market with Manchester mill-made cloth, deindustrialized the most economically advanced part of Asia within a generation. The British-imposed "Permanent Settlement" of 1793 created a parasitic landholding class in Bengal that contributed to long-term agricultural stagnation. The famines of the late nineteenth century (the Bihar famine of 1873-74, the Madras famine of 1876-78, the Indian famine of 1899-1900) killed millions of additional people under conditions of British administrative malpractice. The Indian per-capita income, which had been close to British levels in 1757, had fallen to perhaps a fifth of British levels by 1947 — a substantially impoverished India produced by two centuries of British rule.

The civilizing mission was a story the British told themselves. Modern scholarship — including modern British scholarship — has comprehensively dismantled it. The Mughal Empire, for all its institutional and political failings, was not a backward society awaiting British civilization. It was one of the great pre-modern civilizations, and its conquest by a chartered British corporation was a catastrophe for the Indian population that has been documented in modern economic history but is still imperfectly recognized in popular British and American historical memory.

---

The **Mughal story ends, in one sense, at Bahadur Shah Zafar's exile and death**. In another sense, it continues.

The architecture is still there. The Taj Mahal at Agra. The Red Fort at Delhi. Humayun's Tomb. Fatehpur Sikri. Akbar's Tomb at Sikandra. Itimad-ud-Daulah's Tomb. The Lahore Fort. The Shalimar Gardens. The Badshahi Mosque. The Jama Masjid at Delhi. Hundreds of smaller monuments scattered across north India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh — provincial Mughal-era mosques, tombs, gateways, gardens, residential buildings. The architectural inheritance is the most visible continuous Mughal presence in the modern subcontinent.

The cuisine is still there. The biryanis, the kebabs, the rotis, the sweets. The tea-and-spice culture of north India. The rich curries of the Mughal-influenced regional cuisines from Punjab through Hyderabad. Every restaurant called "Moti Mahal" or "Karim's" or "Dastarkhwan" in the Indian and Pakistani diaspora is, in some sense, a Mughal cultural inheritance.

The language is still there. Urdu is the national language of Pakistan and a major language of north India. Hindi, despite its post-1947 deliberate Sanskritization, retains substantial Mughal-era Persian vocabulary in everyday usage. The vocabulary of romance, of courtly elegance, of formal address, in north Indian Hindi-Urdu is largely Mughal-Persian in origin.

The religious geography is still there. The Indian Muslim community of perhaps 200 million people (in modern India alone — Pakistan and Bangladesh add another 400 million Muslims) is the second-largest national Muslim population in the world. The community's institutional, cultural, and intellectual traditions run through the Mughal period. The Sufi shrine networks of north India and Pakistan are mostly Mughal-era foundations or expansions. The Persian-Arabic vocabulary of Indian Islamic religious practice was substantially codified during the Mughal period.

The political inheritance is still there, in modified form. The administrative geography of modern India and Pakistan substantially derives from Mughal provincial boundaries. The land-revenue and tax-collection institutional template of the modern Indian and Pakistani states substantially derives from the Mughal mansabdari-jagirdari system. The basic institutional template of the modern South Asian state — centralized fiscal-military monarchy at the top, provincial governorships in the middle, district-level revenue collection at the bottom — is, in terms of its fundamental structure, a Mughal template that the British inherited and that the post-independence states continue to operate.

The Mughal Empire ended in 1858. The Mughal inheritance has not.

Cross-civ check: 1857-58 was a year of major imperial-political transformations globally. In the United States, the **Dred Scott decision** (March 1857) and the broader sectional crisis were running toward the **American Civil War** (1861-65). In China, the **Second Opium War** (1856-60) was being fought between Britain-France and the Qing Dynasty, with the British forces involved actively supporting the colonial expansion in India simultaneously. In Japan, the late-Tokugawa crisis was beginning the process that would lead to the **Meiji Restoration** of 1868 (Meiji Japan Ch 1) — Japan's response to European pressure was, deliberately, a study in not becoming India. In Italy, the **Risorgimento** (the Italian unification movement) was active under Cavour, with the eventual creation of unified Italy in 1861. In Russia, **Alexander II** had ascended the throne in 1855 and would issue the Emancipation Edict of 1861 freeing the Russian serfs. The mid-nineteenth century was an era of major political restructuring across the world, and the formal end of the Mughal dynasty was one component — the South Asian component — of the broader nineteenth-century transformation that produced the modern political world.

The closest comparison, in terms of dynastic-political end, is probably the end of the **Ottoman Empire** in 1922 (Ottoman Empire Ch 12). Both empires ended through external military pressure (British in the Mughal case; Allied World War I in the Ottoman case). Both ended with the formal deposition of a dynasty that had ruled for several centuries. Both had spent decades before the end as substantially decayed political entities surviving on the inertia of institutional habit and ceremonial ritual. Both left behind successor states that were defined in significant part by their relationship to the deposed dynasty (modern India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh defining themselves with and against the Mughal legacy; modern Turkey and the Middle Eastern Arab states defining themselves with and against the Ottoman legacy). Both legacies remain politically contested in their respective regions.

The Mughal dynasty died at Rangoon in 1862 with Bahadur Shah Zafar. The Mughal cultural inheritance is still a working part of the everyday life of perhaps 600 million people in the modern subcontinent. Both facts are true. Both deserve to be acknowledged. Babur, who arrived at Panipat in 1526 with a few thousand troops and a stack of borrowed Ottoman tactical manuals, would probably be amused by what eventually came of his refugee-prince's gamble.

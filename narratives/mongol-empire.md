# Chapter 1 — The Steppe Before Temüjin

In the middle of the twelfth century, on a landscape that no surviving map of the time bothered to describe in any detail, a child was born into a world that had no business producing the largest land empire in human history.

The world in question was the **Mongolian plateau** — the eastern half of the great Eurasian grassland belt, the same belt that had earlier produced the Scythians (Chapter 1 of the Scythians narrative) and the Xiongnu (Chapter 1 of the Xiongnu-Huns narrative). It was geographically enormous, ecologically harsh, and politically a mess. Multiple tribal confederations occupied overlapping pastures. Some had been Buddhists or Nestorian Christians for generations. Some still followed older shamanic traditions. They raided each other for horses, livestock, women, and revenge. They formed alliances and broke them within a year. They had never been unified, and most observers in the settled world (Chinese, Persian, Russian, European) assumed they never would be.

Within a single human lifetime, that assumption would be wrong by more than any prediction in recorded history has ever been wrong.

This is the story of the **Mongol Empire** — the political project that began in 1206 with a tribal assembly in northern Mongolia and ended in 1368 with a deposed emperor fleeing his Chinese capital, and during which the Mongols conquered more territory in two generations than Rome had managed in four centuries. It is the story of a civilization that destroyed cities so completely that some of them never reappeared on any map, and of the same civilization sponsoring the most ambitious astronomical observatory in the medieval world. It is one of the darkest stories in this app, and one of the most consequential. The Mongols did not create the Eurasian world we now describe as connected. But they were the moment when that connection, after thousands of years of being merely possible, became overwhelmingly real.

A note on "firsts" before we go further. The Mongols are credited with creating the largest contiguous land empire in history, the first true intercontinental postal system, the first state-issued paper currency widely circulated outside China, the first attempt at a Eurasia-spanning system of religious tolerance enforced by law, and (more darkly) the first medieval pandemic carried along an inter-civilizational trade network. Most of these claims are solid. All carry the standard caveat that "first" in history means "first surviving evidence of." The Mongols did not invent every technique they used. They were the first to combine them on a scale that matched the size of their empire.

---

To understand why the Mongols mattered so much, you have to understand why no one had done what they did before.

The **Eurasian steppe** is a belt of grassland stretching roughly 5,000 miles (8,000 km) from Hungary to Manchuria — wider than the continental United States. Its eastern half, the **Mongolian steppe**, is the harshest stretch. Winters routinely hit minus 40 degrees (the same in Fahrenheit and Celsius, the one point where the two scales meet). Summers are short and hot. The terrain is rolling grass broken by mountain ranges (the Altai in the west, the Khentii in the north and east, the Khangai in the center) and cut by rivers that freeze solid for months.

The people who lived on it practiced **pastoral nomadism**: they did not grow food, they grew herds, and they followed their herds in seasonal circuits across the grass. Their wealth was livestock — sheep, goats, cattle, camels, and above all horses. Their homes were the **ger** (a circular felt tent on a wooden lattice frame; "yurt" is a Turkic word that English borrowed for the same structure). Their political units were tribes and clans bound by kinship, marriage, and the personal authority of a chief.

This was an old way of life on the steppe, a thousand years older than the Mongols. The Xiongnu had practiced it. The Turks had practiced it. The Khitans had practiced it. So why hadn't anyone before the Mongols built an empire that lasted? The blunt answer is that the steppe naturally fragmented. Every successful chieftain produced multiple sons, each of whom inherited followers and pastures, and each of whom had reason to fight his brothers. The harsh ecology kept any one tribe from accumulating overwhelming surpluses. Foreign powers (China, Persia) were practiced at playing the tribes against each other through tribute, marriage alliances, and selective trade access — the Han dynasty (Han Dynasty Chapter 5) had perfected this strategy more than a thousand years earlier, and the technique still worked.

The result was a steppe that was politically incoherent for most of its history. There were brief moments of unification — Modu's Xiongnu confederation around 200 BCE, the Göktürk Khaganate in the sixth century — but they fragmented within a few generations. The default was tribal competition, not empire. To unify the steppe permanently you would need to do several things that no one had ever managed at once: defeat every rival confederation in turn, replace tribal loyalties with personal loyalty to a single ruler, codify a body of law that all tribes accepted, organize the warrior population into a single coordinated army, and survive long enough to see your sons inherit the system rather than tear it apart.

In the twelfth century, on the eve of the Mongol unification, the steppe contained roughly five major confederations whose names matter for what follows.

The **Tatars** (TAH-tars) occupied the eastern Mongolian plateau near the Khingan mountains. They were the largest and most prestigious confederation, allies of the Jin dynasty in northern China (the Jurchen-led empire that had displaced the older Liao dynasty), and frequent enemies of the Mongols proper. The name "Tatar" is the one that would later be projected onto all the steppe peoples by Europeans and Russians, even though the Mongols themselves destroyed most of the actual Tatars early in Genghis Khan's career. The slippage is one of history's stranger naming accidents — what Europe called "the Tatar Yoke" was overwhelmingly Mongol-led.

The **Kereit** (keh-RAIT) controlled the central plateau around the modern Mongolian capital. They were Nestorian Christians (a branch of Christianity that had spread east from Persia in late antiquity, named after the fifth-century theologian Nestorius and rejected as heretical by both Catholic and Orthodox churches but flourishing along the Silk Road for centuries). The Kereit ruler **Toghrul** (TOH-grul) held the Chinese honorific **Wang Khan** (WAHNG khan, "Prince Khan"), bestowed by the Jin court for his services against rivals.

The **Naiman** (NYE-mahn) occupied the western plateau toward the Altai. They too were largely Nestorian. The Naiman court used a Turkic dialect and the **Uyghur script** (a writing system the Naimans had borrowed from the more sophisticated Uyghur Khaganate, which had ruled the steppe two centuries earlier — the Mongols would borrow it in turn, and it remains the basis for the traditional Mongolian script today).

The **Merkit** (MEHR-kit) lived in the forested zone north of Lake Baikal. They were not exactly nomads (the forest steppe required a different way of life) but they were warlike enough to count.

And the **Mongols** proper, the smallest and least prestigious of the five, occupied a stretch of grass and forest along the Onon and Kerulen rivers in the north-central plateau. They were divided among multiple clans, of which the **Borjigin** (BOR-jih-gin, "blue-grey wolf") was a senior lineage but not, in 1162, a particularly powerful one. The Mongols had been tributaries of the Tatars and clients of the Kereit. They were, in modern terms, the underdogs.

The man who would change all of this was born into the Borjigin clan in roughly 1162 (the date is debated; some sources say 1155 or 1167, but 1162 is the most common scholarly figure). His name at birth was **Temüjin** (teh-MOO-jin), reportedly named after a Tatar chieftain his father had killed shortly before his birth — naming a child after a defeated enemy was a steppe custom intended to transfer the enemy's strength to the child. The boy who would become Genghis Khan was, before he was anything else, a child named for revenge.

---

A few things to understand about the steppe economy and military, because they are the operating system of everything that follows.

The Mongol horse was the linchpin of the entire system. The **steppe horse** (sometimes called the Mongol pony today) was small by European or Persian standards — roughly 13 to 14 hands, about the size of a large modern pony — but it was extraordinarily tough, able to survive Mongolian winters by pawing through snow to reach frozen grass, willing to drink from any water source, and capable of covering 50 to 60 miles (80–100 km) per day for weeks. A Mongol warrior on campaign brought three to five remounts and switched horses through the day to keep them fresh. No infantry army could match the pace, and no settled cavalry tradition (which depended on grain-fed horses too expensive to bring in large numbers) could match the logistics.

Every Mongol man was a warrior. Children learned to ride before they could walk in any meaningful sense, and they learned to shoot the **composite bow** (the same recurved bow tradition the Scythians had perfected fifteen hundred years earlier — layered wood, horn, and sinew, glued and cured for months, short enough to use on horseback but as powerful as much longer bows of single materials) before they were ten. Hunting was a year-round practice that doubled as military training. The annual winter hunt was conducted as a slow-shrinking circle of riders that drove game into a central kill zone — exactly the same maneuver, scaled down, that Mongol armies used to envelop enemies in the field.

What this meant in practice: the steppe could mobilize a far higher fraction of its population for war than any settled society. A Chinese empire of fifty million people might field an army of a hundred thousand professional soldiers, which is roughly two-tenths of one percent of the population. A Mongol confederation of one million people could field an army of a hundred thousand without disrupting daily life — ten percent of the population. The math was lopsided in the steppe's favor for any kind of warfare that did not depend on holding cities. The settled world's only consistent advantages were technology (siege engines, walls, manufactured weapons in volume) and numbers in static defense. When you combined a steppe army's mobility with siege technology — by integrating captured engineers, which is what the Mongols would do — the settled world's advantages disappeared.

No one had quite seen the implications of this combination yet. The Mongols would.

---

Cross-civ check: 1162, the year of Temüjin's birth, is roughly contemporary with the **Twelfth-century Renaissance** in western Europe, the **Komnenian dynasty's** revival of the Byzantine Empire, the **Ayyubid sultan Saladin** consolidating power in Egypt and Syria, the **Southern Song** dynasty in China managing a brilliant economic and technological golden age behind a defensive line on the Huai River, and the **Pala dynasty's** decline in eastern India. In the **Andes**, the **Chimú** were building their adobe capital at Chan Chan (Andean Kingdoms Chapter 2). In **Mesoamerica**, the **Toltec** had collapsed and the Maya cities of the Yucatan were entering their post-classic phase. None of these civilizations knew about each other in any meaningful way. Within sixty years, one Mongol empire would be in direct contact with most of them simultaneously.

# Chapter 2 — Becoming Genghis Khan

Temüjin was eight or nine years old when his father was poisoned.

This is where every account of Genghis Khan's life begins, including the **Secret History of the Mongols** (the anonymous Mongolian-language chronicle composed around 1240, the only narrative source in the Mongols' own language and the closest thing we have to an authorized internal version of the foundation story; the Secret History was eventually preserved in a Chinese phonetic transcription after the original Uyghur-script Mongolian copies were lost). Without the death of Temüjin's father, the Mongol Empire would not exist. With it, the boy who became Genghis Khan was forged.

The father in question was **Yesügei** (yeh-SOO-gay), a chieftain of the Borjigin clan and a battle-tested warrior who had killed enough Tatars to earn the lasting hatred of the entire confederation. Around 1171, Yesügei was traveling home after delivering young Temüjin to the family of his betrothed bride — steppe custom required a boy to live with his future in-laws for several years before marriage, partly to bond with the new family and partly as a kind of gentle hostage. On the journey home, Yesügei stopped at a Tatar encampment to share a meal. The Tatars recognized him. They poisoned his food. He made it back to his own camp before dying.

What happened next was not unusual on the steppe, but it is brutal to read. Yesügei's followers — the warriors who had served him out of personal loyalty — abandoned his widow Hoelun and her children. The clan stripped them of livestock, refused them a place at the seasonal migrations, and left them to starve on a stretch of steppe near the **Onon River**. Hoelun, by every account, kept her family alive by foraging for roots, fishing, and shaming her sons into hunting marmots and small game. They lived in a single ger with no men of fighting age, no herds worth mentioning, and no political standing whatever. Temüjin, the eldest, was at this point perhaps ten years old. The Mongol Empire's founder spent his formative years closer to homeless than to royalty.

The early teenage years contain one episode that the **Secret History** records and that historians take seriously as character-forming. Temüjin's older half-brother **Begter**, the son of Yesügei's second wife, had been bullying the family — hoarding food, claiming dominance. Temüjin and his younger full brother **Khasar** ambushed Begter while he sat watching the horses, shot him with arrows, and killed him. Hoelun reportedly raged at Temüjin for hours, comparing him to wild animals that eat their own kind. The episode is often cited as evidence that Temüjin understood power as something that had to be consolidated absolutely, even within a family, and that he would not tolerate rivals close enough to threaten him. The pattern would repeat at imperial scale.

---

Temüjin's first move toward something larger than survival came around 1184. He was twenty-two years old. He had reclaimed his father's bride **Börte** (BUR-teh) from her family's keeping and brought her to his small encampment. The marriage was a serious one — Börte would later bear his four legitimate sons, all of whom would rule major shares of the empire, and she would remain his most senior wife for the rest of his life.

A few months after the wedding, the **Merkit** raided the camp.

The raid was specifically a revenge attack. Years earlier, Temüjin's father Yesügei had himself kidnapped Hoelun from a Merkit man on her wedding day — that was how Temüjin's parents had met. The Merkit had not forgotten. When they learned that Yesügei's son had taken a wife of his own, they rode to take her in compensation. They succeeded. Börte was carried off and given to a Merkit warrior. Temüjin escaped on horseback and lost his bride in the same night.

Temüjin's response was the first act of leadership his career produced. He rode to the camp of his father's old sworn brother **Toghrul**, the Wang Khan of the Kereit, and called in the alliance Yesügei had once secured. He also called on his **anda** (AHN-dah) — sworn brother — **Jamukha** (jah-MOO-khah), a charismatic Mongol of equal age and pedigree who would be Temüjin's closest friend and bitterest rival across the next two decades. Toghrul provided several thousand Kereit warriors. Jamukha provided several thousand Mongol warriors. Temüjin himself contributed a much smaller force. Together they fell on the Merkit, recovered Börte, and demonstrated a principle that Temüjin would build the rest of his life around: alliances are the only thing that makes a small force into a large one, and a leader who can call in alliances is more dangerous than a leader who only commands his own followers.

Börte was rescued pregnant. The child she bore a few months later, **Jochi** (JOH-chee, "guest" or "visitor" in Mongolian, a name that historians believe references the uncertainty about his paternity), was raised as Temüjin's eldest son. Whether Jochi was actually Temüjin's biological son or the son of the Merkit captor was a question the family avoided in public and addressed never. It would become important after 1227, because Jochi was the senior heir by birth order, and the doubt about his paternity gave his brothers ammunition. The Mongol Empire's first succession crisis had its roots in a single missing season of 1184.

---

The next twenty years are, for the reader's purposes, a sequence of escalating tribal wars in which Temüjin slowly defeated every rival on the steppe. The detailed political ledger is not what the chapter is for — the **Secret History** spends hundreds of paragraphs on the names of allies and betrayers, and most readers do not need the granularity. The pattern is what matters.

Temüjin's method, refined across two decades of practice, had several distinct features that contemporary steppe politics did not.

**He absorbed defeated peoples instead of just looting them.** Conventional steppe warfare worked on a "raid-and-retire" logic: you defeated a rival tribe, took their herds, killed their warriors of fighting age, and went home. Temüjin instead distributed the defeated warriors among his own units, where they fought as full members under Mongol commanders. The losers became part of the next campaign's army. This is the same mechanism the Romans had used to absorb Italian peoples a thousand years earlier (Ancient Rome Chapter 2), and it produced the same result: an army that grew with every victory rather than shrinking from casualties.

**He promoted on merit rather than lineage.** Temüjin's senior commanders — **Subutai** (soo-buh-TAI), **Jebe** (JEH-beh), **Mukhali** (moo-KHAH-lee), **Boorchu** (BOH-or-choo) — were not Borjigin nobles. Jebe was a former enemy who had once shot Temüjin's horse out from under him in battle and who Temüjin spared on the explicit reasoning that a man who could shoot that well was worth more alive than dead. Subutai was the son of a blacksmith. Such people would never have led major formations under any other steppe ruler. Under Temüjin they led campaigns that conquered continents.

**He punished betrayal with consistent ruthlessness, but rewarded loyalty across former enemy lines.** When the warriors of the defeated **Tayichiud** clan brought their captured lord to Temüjin in hopes of reward, Temüjin had them executed for betraying their own master. When a single Tayichiud warrior refused to surrender his fleeing lord and confessed it openly, Temüjin made him a general. The message — made loudly and publicly, every time — was that loyalty was the supreme virtue, regardless of who you had originally been loyal to. After enough public examples, men who switched sides started fighting harder for Temüjin than they had for their original chiefs.

**He declared the Mongol identity to be larger than the Borjigin clan.** This is subtle and important. Under earlier steppe leaders, a confederation was a temporary alliance of tribes who kept their own identities. Under Temüjin, all the conquered peoples — Tatars, Kereit, Naiman, Merkit, the various Mongol clans themselves — were progressively reassigned to military units that mixed their origins. After a decade of this, "Mongol" stopped being the name of one tribe and became the name of the new political community. By 1206 there was no separate Tatar people in any meaningful sense; the surviving Tatars were Mongols.

By 1203 the Kereit were defeated and Toghrul, the old Wang Khan, was dead — the alliance from 1184 had ended in a falling-out and a war. By 1204 the Naiman were defeated at the Battle of **Chakirmaut** (chah-keer-MOWT), which incidentally delivered to Temüjin the Naiman scribes and the Uyghur script, giving the Mongols a writing system for the first time in their history. By 1205 Jamukha — Temüjin's old anda, who had become his most determined rival — was captured, refused mercy, and was executed in the noble fashion (without spilling his blood; for the steppe peoples, the soul lived in the blood, and bloodless execution by smothering or back-breaking was an honor).

In 1206, Temüjin called a **kurultai** — a great assembly of all the tribal leaders of the unified plateau — at the headwaters of the Onon River, where he had been born forty-four years earlier.

---

The **Kurultai of 1206** is the foundational moment of Mongol history.

A kurultai (KOO-rul-tai) was a steppe institution of long standing — an assembly of tribal leaders convened to elect a new ruler, ratify major decisions, or coordinate military campaigns. The Xiongnu had held them. The Turks had held them. Every steppe confederation worked through some version of the same process. What was different in 1206 was scale and permanence: every chief on the plateau was present, no rival confederation existed any longer, and the man being acclaimed was not a conventional steppe ruler but a new kind of leader proposing a new kind of state.

The assembly raised Temüjin on a felt carpet (a steppe enthronement ritual) and acclaimed him **Genghis Khan**. The name's exact meaning is debated. **Chinggis Qa'an** (CHING-gis kah-AHN) is the more accurate Mongolian transliteration; "Genghis" is a Latinized form that English uses by convention. Most scholars accept the translation as something close to "oceanic ruler" or "universal ruler" — *chinggis* possibly meaning "ocean" (the steppe word for the largest body of water imaginable), with the implied claim being authority over all the lands the ocean touched. It was a cosmically large title for a man whose father had been a minor chieftain.

The kurultai also ratified several institutions that would define the Mongol state for the rest of its existence.

The **Yassa** (YAH-sah, the Mongol legal code attributed to Genghis Khan) was promulgated as the empire's written law. The Yassa is one of those documents that is famous for existing without anyone alive today knowing exactly what it said — no complete copy survives, only fragments and references in Persian, Arabic, and Chinese sources. What the references agree on is that the Yassa codified rules on theft, murder, military discipline, hunting, water rights, religious practices, and the conduct of leaders. It made the dropping of food on the ground a punishable offense (a steppe-survival rule taken seriously). It mandated religious tolerance — clergy of all faiths were exempt from taxation and military service, and persecution of any recognized religion was a capital crime. It established that the death penalty applied equally to everyone, including members of the imperial family. The Yassa was not a constitution in the modern sense; it was a king's set of rulings. But it functioned as one, and for the first generations of the empire it was treated as binding on all Mongol rulers.

The **decimal military system** was formalized: every man in the empire was enrolled in an **arban** (a unit of ten), ten arbans formed a **zuun** (a hundred), ten zuuns formed a **mingghan** (a thousand), and ten mingghans formed a **tumen** (ten thousand, the largest standard formation). Every level had a commander appointed by Genghis Khan personally and answerable up the chain. The decimal system was not new — the Xiongnu had used it (Xiongnu-Huns Chapter 2) and most steppe empires before the Mongols had imitated it — but Genghis enforced it across deliberately mixed units. A tumen would contain men from a dozen former tribes, all serving under the same commander, all answering to the same chain. Tribal loyalty was being structurally replaced by army loyalty. For the rest of the empire's existence, the Mongol military was the only steppe army that could not be unmade by tribal defection.

The **Keshig** (KESH-ig) — the imperial guard — was expanded from a personal retinue into a formal institution of ten thousand men, drawn from the sons and brothers of the senior commanders. The keshig served as the khan's bodyguard, his administrative staff, his pool of future generals, and (not incidentally) his hostage system. A commander whose son served in the keshig had a strong motivation not to rebel.

The **Nine White Banners** — nine spear-shafts hung with white horsetails — became the empire's symbol. The number nine was sacred in Mongol shamanic tradition (the heavens were said to have nine layers), and the white horsetails referenced the steppe's most valuable possession. The banners would be carried at every kurultai for the next two centuries.

The empire that Genghis Khan ratified at the kurultai of 1206 contained perhaps one million people. It was militarily strong but economically modest — pastoral nomads producing meat, milk, hides, and felt, with limited access to manufactured goods and almost no tax base in the conventional sense. To survive, Genghis needed either trade or conquest. Trade required cooperation from the great settled empires bordering the steppe — the **Jin dynasty** in northern China, the **Western Xia** in the Tangut zone southwest of Mongolia, the **Khwarezmian Empire** in Central Asia and Persia. Conquest required almost the same thing: the army needed somewhere to go.

Genghis chose conquest, but he started by inviting trade and watching to see whether it would be honored.

It was not.

# Chapter 3 — The Eurasian Whirlwind

Genghis Khan was forty-four years old at his coronation, and he had twenty-one years left to live. In those twenty-one years he and his generals would conquer or destroy: the Western Xia kingdom, the Kara-Khitai, the Khwarezmian Empire, the northern half of Jin China, fragments of the Russian principalities, large portions of the Caucasus, and the eastern half of the Persian-cultural sphere. Geographically this is roughly the area from the Pacific Ocean to the Caspian Sea, north to south from the Siberian forests to the Indus River. The campaigns proceeded simultaneously on multiple fronts. They produced casualties on a scale not previously documented in any source the survivors of any of the involved civilizations left behind.

This chapter is what the **whirlwind** sounds like from inside a written tradition.

---

The first target was **Western Xia** (also called **Xi Xia** or, in the Mongol sources, **Tangut** — TANG-uht — after the Tangut people who founded the kingdom).

Western Xia was a semi-Sinicized kingdom of Tangut-speaking people in the upper Yellow River valley, roughly modern Ningxia and Gansu in northwestern China. It controlled the **Hexi Corridor**, the narrow strip of habitable land between the Gobi Desert and the Tibetan Plateau through which all overland trade between China and Central Asia had to pass — the same corridor that had triggered the original Han-Xiongnu wars more than a thousand years earlier (Han Dynasty Chapter 4). Western Xia was militarily competent, economically rich on Silk Road tolls, and strategically vulnerable: it sat between the Mongol steppe and the much larger Jin dynasty, and any time the Mongols moved south Xia was the first kingdom they encountered.

The first Mongol campaign against Western Xia in 1209 was, in Genghis's evident reasoning, a rehearsal. He needed to test his armies against a settled enemy with walled cities. Western Xia surrendered after the Mongols diverted the **Yellow River** to flood the capital city's defenses (the diversion did not work as planned and apparently flooded the Mongol camp instead, but the political effect was achieved). Xia agreed to pay tribute and accept Mongol overlordship without being formally annexed. Genghis turned his attention to the larger prize.

That prize was the **Jin dynasty**, the Jurchen-led empire that ruled northern China from the Yellow River to the Korean border. The Jin had displaced the older Liao dynasty about a century earlier. They were, by any measure, the larger and more sophisticated power: a population in the tens of millions, a tax base in the hundreds of thousands of pounds of silver per year, walled cities in the dozens, and an army that included native Chinese, Khitan auxiliaries, and Jurchen heavy cavalry.

Genghis invaded in 1211 with a force that was probably no larger than ninety thousand men. The campaign would last twenty-three years.

What the Mongols brought to the Jin war that no previous steppe army had brought was a willingness to integrate the technical specialists of conquered cities into their own system. The first Jin city to fall in 1211 was **Datong**. The Mongols took the surviving engineers, siege specialists, blacksmiths, and gunpowder technicians, organized them into specialized units, and used them against the next city. The next city's specialists were added to the next campaign. Within a few years the Mongol army was conducting siege warfare with **trebuchets** (counterweight catapults capable of throwing 200-pound stones), **fire-lance weapons** (bamboo or paper tubes filled with gunpowder and projectiles, the immediate ancestors of firearms), and **mines and counter-mines** under city walls — none of which the Mongols had possessed five years earlier. The army was a learning organization in a way that ancient armies almost never were.

The Jin capital **Zhongdu** (modern Beijing, then called **Zhongdu** — JONG-doo — the "central capital") fell in 1215 after years of intermittent siege. The fall produced one of the war's most consequential events at the human-individual scale: a Khitan official named **Yelü Chucai** (YEH-lyoo CHOO-tsai), a serious scholar of Confucian classics and a poet, was captured among the bureaucratic survivors and brought to Genghis Khan. Yelü Chucai was tall, bearded, fluent in multiple languages, and possessed of a kind of cool dignity that the steppe court immediately registered as authority. Genghis kept him on. Over the next two decades, Yelü would talk multiple Mongol khans out of policies that would have left north China a depopulated pasture, arguing that taxation produced more wealth than slaughter. He is plausibly responsible for several million lives. The Mongol Empire's habit of recruiting captured administrators rather than killing them is not a small detail of the period. It is the reason the empire grew rather than collapsing under the weight of its own destruction.

---

In 1218 the western front opened in a way the Mongols had not particularly intended.

Far to the west of the Mongol heartland, beyond the Tian Shan mountains, lay the **Khwarezmian Empire** (kwah-REZ-mee-an, sometimes spelled Khorezm or Khwarazm). Khwarezm was a powerful Turkic-Persian state that had emerged in the previous century, ruled in 1218 by the ambitious and politically inept **Shah Muhammad II**. Khwarezm's territory was vast — modern Iran, Afghanistan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, and parts of Kazakhstan. It contained some of the most sophisticated cities of the medieval world, including **Samarkand**, **Bukhara**, **Urgench**, **Merv**, **Nishapur**, and **Herat**. The empire was Persian-cultured (Khwarezmian elites spoke Persian, patronized Persian poetry, and ran their administration in Persian — the cultural-resurrection pattern that the Persian Empire narrative documents at length, Persian Empire Chapter 10). It was the richest and most populous state in the eastern Islamic world.

Genghis Khan, having spent the better part of a decade fighting the Jin, was not initially interested in Khwarezm. He sent a trade delegation — five hundred merchants, goods and gold, a request to open commerce between the two empires. The delegation reached the Khwarezmian frontier town of **Otrar** on the **Syr Darya** river. The local governor, **Inalchuq**, decided the merchants were spies. He had them all executed and confiscated their goods.

This was the kind of provocation that Genghis Khan responded to as if a religious obligation were involved. He sent envoys to Shah Muhammad demanding Inalchuq's punishment. Muhammad executed one of the envoys and sent the others home with their beards shaved off — a deep insult in the Islamic and steppe worlds alike.

In 1219, Genghis Khan invaded Khwarezm with an army that contemporary chroniclers estimated at a hundred and fifty thousand men. The number is probably exaggerated; modern estimates suggest a hundred thousand or fewer. Either way, Genghis's force was smaller than the army the Khwarezmian Shah commanded on paper, and it was operating a thousand miles from its supply base. Shah Muhammad chose to garrison every major city separately rather than concentrate his army for a field battle, on the theory that the Mongols could not sustain multiple sieges. The strategy was disastrous. The Mongols took the cities one at a time, and they had become, by 1219, the most devastatingly effective siege army in the world.

The campaigns of 1219 to 1221 are difficult to summarize without slipping into pure horror. **Bukhara** fell in 1220. The city's congregational mosque survived but was used as a stable. Genghis Khan reportedly mounted the pulpit and told the assembled survivors: "I am the flail of God. If you had not committed great sins, God would not have sent a punishment like me upon you." Whether he actually said it is uncertain — the line is preserved in the Persian historian **Juvayni's** account a generation later — but the city's craftsmen were deported to Mongolia, the garrison was massacred, and a portion of the population was used as a human shield in the assault on **Samarkand**.

Samarkand fell shortly after. The same pattern: craftsmen extracted, garrison massacred, defensive walls dismantled, population reduced. Samarkand had been one of the great cities of the Islamic world, a center of Persian poetry and Hanafi legal scholarship. It survived the Mongol conquest physically, but its character would not recover for nearly two centuries (Timur, claiming Mongol descent, would make Samarkand his capital in the 1370s, and by then the city had to be rebuilt almost from scratch — Chapter 9).

**Urgench**, the Khwarezmian capital in the lower Amu Darya delta, was the most ferocious siege of the war. The city held for months. When it finally fell in 1221, the Mongol commanders divided the population among the army for systematic killing — every soldier was given a quota — and then diverted the **Amu Darya** river through the city's foundations to wash the ruins flat. Contemporary sources claimed casualties of well over a million. Modern historians regard those figures as inflated propaganda (medieval armies could not actually count deaths at that scale), but accept that the Urgench massacre was one of the largest organized killings in pre-modern history. Nothing called Urgench would exist on the same site for centuries afterward.

**Nishapur** in eastern Iran — a city that had been the capital of the Persian poet **Omar Khayyam** a generation earlier — was destroyed in 1221 after a Mongol prince was killed by a stray arrow during the siege. The avenging force, commanded by Genghis's son-in-law **Tolui**, was ordered to leave nothing alive. The city was taken, the population killed, the buildings demolished, and the fields salted with skulls — chroniclers describe pyramids of severed heads built outside the gates: men's heads, women's heads, children's heads, separately. The horror was the point. The Mongols had developed an explicit doctrine: cities that resisted should be destroyed so completely that other cities would surrender at the first summons. The doctrine worked. Many Khwarezmian cities surrendered without resistance and survived. The ones that resisted did not.

**Shah Muhammad II** himself fled west across his crumbling empire and died of exhaustion on a small island in the Caspian Sea in 1220 or 1221, sources differ. His son **Jalal ad-Din Mingburnu** kept fighting. Genghis personally chased Jalal ad-Din across Afghanistan and finally cornered him at the **Indus River** in late 1221. Jalal ad-Din made his last stand with about thirty thousand men, lost the battle, and saved his life by spurring his horse off a cliff into the river and swimming to the Indian bank with his sword still in hand. Genghis Khan reportedly watched from the cliff and remarked to his sons that this was what a true man looked like. Jalal ad-Din lived another decade as a fugitive in northern India and Iraq before dying of less impressive causes. The Khwarezmian Empire, which had been the richest state in the eastern Islamic world three years earlier, was over.

---

While Genghis was destroying Khwarezm, Subutai and Jebe — two of his most capable generals — undertook a reconnaissance expedition that turned into one of the most extraordinary military campaigns in history. They were sent west to chase Shah Muhammad's remnants. They kept going. They rode around the southern shore of the Caspian Sea, defeated the Georgians in the Caucasus, crossed into the Pontic steppe, and at the **Battle of the Kalka River** in 1223 destroyed a combined army of Russian princes and Cuman steppe nomads who had marched against them. It was the first contact between Mongol forces and any state that European writers documented — Russian chronicles of 1223 describe the appearance of an unknown people from the east, who came, fought a battle, and disappeared. Subutai and Jebe rode back to Mongolia having scouted everything between the Caspian and the Dnieper. Twenty years later their report would be the basis of the great western campaign (Chapter 4). In the meantime, they had circumnavigated the Caspian Sea on horseback and conducted an entire war on the side. No campaign in Roman or Persian military history compares in distance covered.

---

By 1226, Genghis Khan was approximately sixty-four years old, and the campaigns had taken a physical toll. He returned to Mongolia and turned his attention to **Western Xia**, which had refused to send troops for the Khwarezmian campaign — a refusal that, under Mongol political logic, was a declaration of independence and therefore a declaration of war.

Genghis personally led the final Western Xia campaign in 1226–1227. The kingdom was destroyed; the dynasty's name was struck from the official Chinese historical record (Western Xia is the only major Chinese dynasty that does not receive a standard official history); the Tangut population was systematically reduced — though not, as is sometimes claimed, eliminated, since Tangut identity persisted into the Ming period.

In August 1227, Genghis Khan died.

The cause is not certain. Contemporary sources offer competing explanations: a fall from a horse during a hunt the year before, a wound that became infected, a natural illness, even (in one Persian account, almost certainly false) an injury inflicted by a captured Tangut princess in revenge. The **Secret History** says simply that he ascended to heaven during the final siege. He was sixty-five years old.

His generals kept his death secret until Western Xia surrendered. The body was taken north to a location that has never been publicly identified — Mongol custom required that a great khan's burial site be kept secret, and the soldiers escorting the body were said to have killed every person they encountered on the route to prevent the location from being known. In one version of the story, the funeral cortège killed two thousand people in five hundred miles of road. The grave has not been found. It is one of the most determined searches in modern archaeology, and it has produced a great many candidate sites and zero verified ones. The man who controlled the largest empire in history is buried somewhere in northern Mongolia, and no one alive knows where.

The empire he left covered roughly 13.5 million square miles (35 million km²) — about four times the size of the modern United States. He had been reigning for twenty-one years. His four legitimate sons by Börte had to figure out how to inherit it.

# Chapter 4 — The Sons of Genghis

The Mongol succession system was, on paper, simple. The empire was divided into **ulus** (OO-loos, "people" or "appanage") — territorial allocations to each of Genghis Khan's four legitimate sons by Börte. The eldest, **Jochi** (JOH-chee, the disputed-paternity son from Chapter 2), received the westernmost lands — the steppe and forest zones north of the Aral and Caspian seas, what would become the Golden Horde. The second son **Chagatai** (CHAH-gah-tai) received Central Asia from the Tian Shan mountains to the Aral, including the great cities of Samarkand and Bukhara. The third son **Ögedei** (UH-geh-day) received the Mongol heartland in the Altai region, plus the position of Great Khan over all the others. The youngest son **Tolui** (TOH-loo-ee), per Mongol custom that the youngest son inherits the family hearth, received the Mongol heartland in central Mongolia plus the bulk of the army.

In practice, the system was intricate and politically combustible. The four ulus were not equal in wealth or military power. The election of the Great Khan required a kurultai of all senior Genghisid princes, and that kurultai could disregard the founder's wishes. Jochi died (probably of natural causes) in 1227, six months before his father, leaving his own sons — including the future **Batu Khan** — to claim his ulus. Tolui died in 1232, possibly poisoned, possibly from drinking himself to death after a shamanic ritual in which he had volunteered to take on his older brother Ögedei's illness. The founding generation cleared out fast.

The empire that came out the other end was governed for the next thirty years by a sequence of Great Khans who managed simultaneously to expand the empire's territory and to create the political fractures that would eventually break it apart.

---

**Tolui's regency** (1227–1229) was a brief interlude per Mongol custom, since Tolui as the youngest son held the family hearth pending the formal kurultai. Tolui used the regency to complete operations against the Jin dynasty, and in particular to test the integration of Khitan and Chinese specialists into the Mongol command structure that Yelü Chucai (Chapter 3) had been advocating. The model worked. By 1229 the Mongol war machine was running on a hybrid steppe-and-Chinese institutional base.

The kurultai of 1229 elected **Ögedei Khan** (reigned 1229–1241) as Great Khan. Ögedei was, by Mongol-court standards, a moderate. He was personable, generous, alcoholic, and (when sober) shrewd. He continued his father's expansionist policy on every front while also building the institutions the empire would need to survive its own size.

The most important institution-building was the construction of **Karakorum** (kah-rah-KOR-um) as the empire's capital city. Genghis had used Karakorum as a base camp; Ögedei built actual walls, a palace, official residences, religious buildings, and a market district. This was a substantial step away from steppe tradition, which had no fixed capitals — every previous steppe empire had governed from rolling encampments or annual gathering points. Karakorum was a deliberate signal that the Mongol Empire intended to govern, not just raid. It contained Buddhist temples, mosques, a Nestorian church, and a substantial population of foreign craftsmen, engineers, and clerics imported from across the empire's territory. The city's most famous fixture was the **Silver Tree**, a fountain commissioned by Ögedei from a French silversmith named **Guillaume Boucher** (a Parisian who had been captured during the Hungary campaign and brought back to Mongolia, one of dozens of European specialists in Mongol service). The Silver Tree had four spouts that dispensed wine, mead, rice wine, and grape wine on demand, powered by a bellows operated by a mechanical angel at the crown. The fountain stood for the entire conceit of Karakorum: a steppe capital staffed by global specialists, dispensing the world's products to anyone who arrived at the court.

The European Franciscan friar **William of Rubruck** (the Flemish missionary who reached Karakorum in 1253 as the personal envoy of King Louis IX of France) saw the Silver Tree in operation and wrote one of the only Western descriptions of the medieval Mongol court. His account, the *Itinerarium*, is the source for almost everything Europeans of the period knew about how the Mongol capital actually functioned.

Ögedei also formalized two infrastructure projects that would matter enormously for the next century. The first was the **Yam** (also called the **örtöö**), a relay postal and travel system with stations roughly every 25 to 30 miles (40–50 km) along all major imperial roads. Each Yam station maintained a stable of fresh horses, food, and lodging, available to any traveler who carried a **paiza** (PYE-zah) — a metal pass in gold, silver, or wood depending on rank. The Yam allowed messages to cross the empire at unprecedented speed (a courier could cover 300 miles in a day under emergency conditions, switching horses every station) and gave merchants and diplomats a degree of secure travel that had not previously existed across most of Eurasia. The Yam was a working communication system on a scale that the Roman Empire had only approached at peak. It was the operating infrastructure of the Pax Mongolica that would emerge by mid-century (Chapter 7).

The second was **Sorghaghtani Beki** (sor-GHAGH-tah-nee BEH-kee), Tolui's widow, a Nestorian Christian Kerait noblewoman who emerged after her husband's death as one of the empire's most capable administrators. Ögedei, recognizing her abilities, made her responsible for the Tolui ulus. Sorghaghtani would over the next twenty years educate her four sons — **Möngke**, **Kublai**, **Hülegü**, and **Ariq Böke** — in administration, multiple languages, and political patience. All four would eventually rule major Mongol states. Three of them would be Great Khan (Möngke and Kublai) or founders of khanates that became their own dynasties (Hülegü). She is plausibly the most consequential woman in the Mongol Empire's political history, and her quiet positioning of her sons across two decades is the reason the Toluid line displaced the Ögedeid line in the empire's second generation.

---

The military expansion under Ögedei was even more dramatic than Genghis's.

In 1234, the **Jin dynasty** finally collapsed. The last Jin emperor committed suicide as the Mongol-Song combined army stormed his last refuge at **Caizhou**. Northern China was now Mongol territory. Whether the Mongols would govern it as a steppe-style tributary zone or as a Chinese-style empire was a question that would split the next generation.

In 1236, Ögedei dispatched the **Great Western Campaign** — the largest single military operation in Mongol history. The army was commanded jointly by **Batu Khan** (Jochi's son, claiming the western ulus his father had been promised) and **Subutai** (now in his late sixties, the most experienced general in the empire). Their combined force was roughly 150,000 men, drawn from across the empire's territory and reinforced by Turkic auxiliaries from the western steppe. Their orders were to subjugate everything between the Volga and the Atlantic.

They came close.

The campaign destroyed the Volga **Bulgars** in 1236, the **Cumans** (a Turkic steppe people of the Pontic-Caspian zone) over the next two years, and then turned on the Russian principalities. **Riazan** fell in 1237. **Vladimir** — the dominant principality of northeastern Rus' — fell in 1238 after a long siege; the city was burned, the cathedral set on fire with the population sheltering inside, and the surrounding region depopulated. **Kiev** (KEEV, also spelled Kyiv), the symbolic mother-city of Rus' culture, fell in December 1240. The Italian friar **John of Plano Carpini** (the papal envoy who traveled overland through these territories five years later, leaving the first detailed European report on the Mongols) described the post-Mongol Russian landscape as fields of bones with no living men to bury them. The phrasing is rhetorical, but the demographic effect was real.

The Mongol force then split. One column under Batu marched into **Hungary**. Another under Subutai's lieutenants marched into **Poland**.

The **Battle of Legnica** (LEG-neet-sah) in April 1241 destroyed a combined Polish-Silesian-Teutonic Knight army under Henry the Pious of Silesia. The Mongols left a hill of severed ears as the body count: nine sacks, by the Polish chronicler. The defeated knights' commander died on the field; his head was paraded on a pike to the next siege.

Two days later — and this is the timing that should be impossible without the Yam communication system — **Subutai** and Batu defeated the Hungarian army at the **Battle of Mohi** (MOH-hee), more than three hundred miles to the southeast. King **Béla IV of Hungary** escaped. His army was destroyed. The Mongol force pursued, sacking cities across Hungary through the summer and probing into Croatia and the Adriatic coast.

In the spring of 1242, the Mongol army stopped, turned around, and marched home.

The reason was that **Ögedei Khan had died** in December 1241, alone in Karakorum, of (probably) acute alcohol poisoning during a drinking party. The succession kurultai required the attendance of all senior Genghisid princes, including Batu and Subutai. They obeyed the protocol. Europe was saved by a steppe drinking culture and a constitutional procedural rule.

How far the Mongols would have gone if Ögedei had lived another five years is one of the great counterfactuals of medieval history. The Holy Roman Empire's defenses were weak. Italy was politically fragmented. France had no large standing army that could match the steppe formations in the field. The realistic answer is that the Mongols would have gone as far as their logistics allowed, and the limit on their logistics was probably the western forests of central Europe rather than the armies of any specific kingdom. They were not stopped by the West. They were called home.

---

The kurultai-and-regency interregnum after Ögedei lasted five years, dominated by his widow **Töregene Khatun**, who governed as regent and maneuvered to elect her own son **Güyük** (GOO-yook) as Great Khan. Güyük's election in 1246 was attended by the papal envoy **John of Plano Carpini**, who left the first detailed Western description of a Mongol coronation. Güyük's reign was short — he died in 1248, possibly of alcoholism, possibly poisoned, while marching west toward a confrontation with his cousin Batu Khan that would have produced the empire's first Mongol-on-Mongol civil war.

The next regent was Güyük's widow **Oghul Qaimish**, but the Toluid faction (the descendants of Tolui, organized by Sorghaghtani Beki) had been waiting. In 1251 the Toluids held a kurultai that elected **Möngke**, Sorghaghtani's eldest son, as Great Khan. The Ögedeid faction objected. The Toluids responded with a series of executions of Ögedeid princes that effectively ended the Ögedeid line as a competitor. Möngke's accession was the first Mongol succession decided by intra-family violence. It would not be the last.

**Möngke Khan** (reigned 1251–1259) was the most disciplined and ideologically rigorous of the Great Khans after Genghis. He restored central authority, conducted a comprehensive census of the empire, simplified taxation, and dispatched two enormous campaigns simultaneously. His brother **Kublai** he sent against the **Southern Song** dynasty in southern China — the half of China that had survived the destruction of the Jin and was now the wealthiest single state in the world. His brother **Hülegü** (HOO-leh-goo) he sent west, to subdue the Islamic territories that had survived the Khwarezmian destruction: the Persian principalities, the **Nizari Ismaili Assassins**, and the **Abbasid Caliphate** in Baghdad.

Hülegü left Mongolia in 1253 with an army of perhaps a hundred thousand men. He moved slowly. He had been instructed to subjugate every minor power along the way and to leave no source of potential resistance behind him. By 1256, he had reached the Persian highlands. The next chapter is what he did when he got there.

# Chapter 5 — The Sack of Baghdad and Ayn Jalut

The sack of Baghdad in February 1258 is one of the historical events that civilizations remember as a marker of before-and-after. The phrase "the Tigris ran black with ink and red with blood" is a refrain in later Arabic and Persian historical writing, and it was not pure metaphor. The Mongols destroyed the **Bayt al-Hikma** — the **House of Wisdom**, the great library of Baghdad — by throwing the manuscripts into the river. Contemporary witnesses described the river running black with the ink leaching out of the books, and red downstream where the corpses had been thrown. Both rivers ran the wrong color for weeks.

To understand why this mattered, you have to understand what Baghdad had been.

---

The **Abbasid Caliphate** had been founded in 750 CE — five centuries before the Mongols arrived — when the Abbasid family overthrew the previous Umayyad caliphate and moved the political center of the Islamic world from Damascus east to a new city on the Tigris River. That new city was Baghdad, founded in 762 CE by the second Abbasid caliph, **al-Mansur**, on a circular plan that gave it the nickname **Madinat al-Salam** (mah-DEE-naht al-sah-LAHM, "City of Peace"). For the next four centuries, Baghdad was the political capital of the Sunni Islamic world and one of the most important intellectual centers on earth.

By the time of the Mongol arrival, the political authority of the Abbasid caliph was largely ceremonial. The actual political power in the Sunni world had migrated decades earlier to regional sultans (Seljuks, Ayyubids, Khwarezmians) who held the caliph as a kind of constitutional figurehead — he ratified their rule by giving them robes of investiture and the official title of sultan, and they protected him from rivals. But the cultural and religious authority of the office was undiminished. The caliph was, in formal Islamic legal theory, the successor to the Prophet Muhammad and the religious head of the Sunni community. His existence anchored the legal and theological order of the entire Sunni world. The Sunni-Shia divide that runs through Islamic political history was, in a real sense, an argument about who the legitimate caliph was. To kill the caliph and abolish the caliphate was to do something unprecedented — it was the medieval equivalent of removing a fixed point from the map.

Baghdad in 1258 was also still one of the world's great cultural cities. The **Bayt al-Hikma** was at this point not the same institution it had been at its peak in the ninth century — the original House of Wisdom had been a translation academy that systematically translated Greek philosophical and scientific works into Arabic, producing the famous transmission lineage that later passed those texts back to Europe through Spain and Sicily — but it remained a major manuscript library. Baghdad's specialized libraries, mosque schools, and merchant houses contained accumulations of Persian, Arabic, Greek, Indian, and Syriac texts that did not exist anywhere else in such concentration. Some of those texts existed only in Baghdad. When the books burned, the texts were gone.

---

Hülegü arrived at Baghdad in January 1258. He had spent the previous two years working through the **Nizari Ismaili Assassins**, the Shia sectarian order whose stronghold at the mountain fortress of **Alamut** (ah-lah-MOOT, in the Alborz mountains north of modern Tehran) had defied every previous Persian and Seljuk ruler. The Assassins were a small, disciplined, theologically heterodox order whose method of political action involved sending trained adepts to murder specific high-value targets — viziers, sultans, generals — on suicide missions. The order had been founded by **Hassan-i Sabbah** in the 1090s and had successfully defied the Seljuk Empire for more than a century and a half. The English word "assassin" derives from the order's Arabic name, *Hashshashin*, the etymology of which is itself contested (it may relate to the use of hashish, but the connection is debated among modern Islamicists).

Alamut fell to Hülegü in 1256 — the first time the fortress had been taken in its history. The Mongols dismantled it. The Assassin grand library — said to contain manuscripts the order had hoarded for two centuries — was destroyed, with the partial exception of a small selection of scientific manuscripts that one captured scholar managed to save. That scholar was **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi** (nah-SEER al-DEEN al-TOO-see), a brilliant Persian polymath who had been a forced guest of the Assassins for many years — they had wanted him as a court philosopher, he had wanted to leave, the situation was unresolved. Hülegü, rather than killing al-Tusi with the rest of the captured Assassins, recognized his abilities and recruited him as the chief court scientist. Al-Tusi would become the most consequential single intellectual under Mongol patronage. He would design the **Maragheh Observatory** (Chapter 7). His mathematical work on planetary motion — including the **Tusi couple**, a geometric device using two circles to model linear motion as a combination of circular ones — would feed forward into the Copernican revolution three centuries later. His survival was an accident of Hülegü's specific judgment that an enemy library was less valuable alive than an enemy mathematician. The accident shaped European astronomy.

---

The siege of Baghdad lasted twelve days.

The Caliph **al-Musta'sim** (al-moos-TAH-sim, the thirty-seventh and final Abbasid caliph) had received Hülegü's demands months earlier: surrender, dismantle the city's defenses, deliver the city's wealth. He had refused. He had also failed to call for help from any of the surrounding powers — the Mamluks of Egypt, the Ayyubid remnants in Syria, the Seljuks of Anatolia — partly because he assumed his religious office would protect him, partly because the practical military force at his disposal was already insufficient. When Hülegü's siege engines arrived in late January 1258, Baghdad had perhaps fifty thousand troops behind its walls. The Mongol force was several times larger and better equipped.

The walls were breached on February 5. The garrison surrendered on the promise of safe passage and was massacred. The city was given over to the army on February 13. The killing lasted approximately a week.

The numerical claims for casualties run from two hundred thousand to over a million. The two hundred thousand is the lowest serious estimate; the higher figures are not credible at the population scale of medieval Baghdad, which had perhaps a quarter to a half million inhabitants at peak. The realistic estimate is something like a hundred thousand to two hundred thousand civilians killed in a city of perhaps four hundred thousand. By any standard this is one of the largest organized killings in pre-modern history.

The destruction of physical infrastructure was as systematic as the killing. Mosques were burned. Hospitals were destroyed. The canal system that had irrigated the agricultural hinterland for centuries was damaged in ways that would not be repaired in the medieval period — the agricultural productivity of southern Iraq did not recover its pre-Mongol levels for centuries. The libraries were thrown into the Tigris.

The Caliph al-Musta'sim was captured. Mongol custom forbade the spilling of royal blood, on the theological reasoning that the soul resided in the blood and the spilling of royal blood would offend the heavens. The custom was honored. The caliph was rolled up in a felt rug and stamped to death by horses, or kicked to death by Mongol soldiers, or crushed under a pile of his own treasury, depending on which contemporary chronicler you trust. He died bloodlessly. The Abbasid caliphate, the institution that had anchored Sunni political theology for half a millennium, was finished.

---

The strategic and cultural consequences of the sack of Baghdad were enormous, and they reshaped the Islamic world permanently.

The end of the Abbasid caliphate forced the Sunni world to reconsider where religious authority resided. Various claimants would emerge over the next centuries — the Mamluks of Egypt installed a relative of the slain caliph as a ceremonial Abbasid in Cairo, but he had no real authority; the Ottomans would later claim a transfer of the caliphate to their own dynasty, though the historicity of that transfer is debated; eventually Sunni Islam would settle into a system in which religious authority was diffused across the *ulema* (the scholarly class) rather than concentrated in any single office. The Mongol destruction of the institutional caliphate is part of why modern Sunni Islam looks the way it does.

The destruction of Baghdad also accelerated the political center-of-gravity shift that had been underway for some time. The eastern Islamic world's economic and intellectual capital moved east — into Persia, especially Tabriz and Maragheh under the Ilkhanate (Chapter 7), and eventually further into Central Asia under the Timurids — and west, into Mamluk Egypt and Ottoman Anatolia. Baghdad as a cultural center would not recover until the modern period. As a political center it would not recover at all.

The Persian-speaking cultural world — the cultural sphere that had been damaged but not destroyed by the Khwarezmian campaigns thirty years earlier — was now under direct Mongol rule. The pattern of cultural reconquest that the Persian Empire narrative documents (Persian Empire Chapter 10 — the Persians being conquered militarily and reconquering their conquerors culturally) would repeat: within two generations, Mongol rulers in Persia would be writing in Persian, patronizing Persian poets, building in Persian architectural idioms, and converting to Islam.

But that was the future. In 1258, what stood out was the scale of the destruction and the apparent unstoppability of the Mongol westward advance. Hülegü now turned his army toward Syria. The local Muslim rulers, including the Ayyubid sultan of Damascus, surrendered on his approach. He sent a delegation of envoys to **Cairo**, demanding the same of the Mamluks of Egypt.

He did not know that Möngke Khan was about to die.

---

**Möngke Khan died in August 1259**, near the Southern Song city of **Diaoyu** (DOW-yoo) in Sichuan, while personally commanding the southern Chinese campaign. The cause was probably dysentery, possibly a wound, possibly cholera. The death was unexpected, and Mongol succession protocol required that all senior princes return to Mongolia for the kurultai.

Hülegü, on receiving the news, took most of his army back east to deal with the political aftermath. He left a relatively small force — perhaps fifteen to twenty thousand men — in Syria under the Christian Mongol general **Kitbuqa** (kit-BOO-kah, a Naiman Nestorian who was one of Hülegü's most senior commanders).

The **Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt** had been founded only a few years earlier, in 1250, by a coup of the **Mamluks** themselves — slave-soldiers of mostly Turkic and Circassian origin, captured as boys from the steppe, raised as elite cavalry, and converted to Sunni Islam. The Mamluks had taken power from their Ayyubid masters during the Seventh Crusade and were now governing Egypt as a military oligarchy in which sultans were typically promoted from the senior ranks of the slave-soldier corps rather than inheriting from a dynasty. The sultan in 1260 was **Qutuz** (KOO-tooz), and his strongest general was **Baybars** (BAY-bahrz), who would later kill Qutuz and become sultan himself.

The Mamluks were the only major Islamic state in the Near East that the Mongols had not yet defeated. They were also the only Islamic state with a military culture comparable to the Mongols' own, because they were themselves recruited from the steppe and trained in the same archery-and-cavalry tradition. When Hülegü's envoys delivered his ultimatum to Cairo, Qutuz had them executed and their heads displayed on the city gates.

The Mamluk army marched north into Syria. Kitbuqa, with his reduced Mongol force, decided to fight rather than retreat. The two armies met on **September 3, 1260**, at a small valley in the Galilee called **Ayn Jalut** (eye-yn jah-LOOT, "Goliath's Spring" in Arabic, traditionally identified with the place where David fought Goliath in the Hebrew Bible).

The battle was fought in a way that was not, on paper, a fair contest of military culture. The Mamluks were better armored. The Mongols had artillery and steppe tactics. The Mamluks had been trained in the same steppe tactics. Baybars used the classic steppe maneuver of feigned retreat to draw the Mongols into a prepared killing zone in the valley. Kitbuqa, perhaps believing the retreat was real, pressed the pursuit. The Mamluk reserves emerged from concealment and closed the trap. Kitbuqa was killed. The Mongol force was destroyed.

Ayn Jalut was, in raw numerical terms, a small battle — perhaps fifteen thousand men on the Mongol side, perhaps thirty thousand on the Mamluk side, casualties in the thousands rather than the tens of thousands. Compared to Mohi or Legnica or any of the great campaigns of the previous decade, it was a minor engagement. But it was the first time a Mongol field army had been decisively defeated in open battle, and it set a precedent that the Mamluks would extend over the next forty years: Mongol westward advance in the Near East had limits. The Levant and Egypt would not become Mongol territory.

The standard explanation for the Mongol failure at Ayn Jalut emphasizes the reduced size of Hülegü's force after Möngke's death. There is something to this, but it is incomplete. The Mamluks were genuinely good. Baybars in particular was one of the finest field commanders of the medieval period, and he would defeat Mongol forces in additional engagements over the next two decades. Egypt's Nile-fed agricultural base meant that the Mamluks had logistical depth that Mongol armies operating thousands of miles from their grasslands lacked. The political fracturing that began with Möngke's death (Chapter 6) meant that no future Mongol force would have the unified command and full imperial resources Hülegü had brought west. Ayn Jalut was the high-water mark of westward Mongol expansion. It would never be matched again.

The image to keep is geographic. By 1260 the Mongol Empire stretched from the Yellow Sea on the Pacific coast of Korea to the Mediterranean coast of Syria. It had taken just over fifty years to build. And in that same year, the empire began breaking apart.

# Chapter 6 — Kublai and the Yuan

The phone call did not exist. Möngke Khan died in southern China in August 1259. **Hülegü** in Persia learned about it in September. **Kublai**, the senior Toluid prince in command of the Chinese front, learned about it in late September while besieging the Song city of **Wuzhou**. **Ariq Böke** (AH-rik BUH-keh), the youngest Toluid brother and the regent in Karakorum during Möngke's absence, learned about it within weeks.

A Mongol kurultai was the legitimate constitutional method for selecting the next Great Khan. Genghis Khan's Yassa (Chapter 2) required it. The kurultai had to be held in Mongolia. It had to include all senior Genghisid princes. And the new Great Khan had to be elected, not anointed.

Both Kublai and Ariq Böke called separate kurultais. Kublai's was held at his Chinese base at **Kaiping** in May 1260. Ariq Böke's was held at Karakorum a few weeks later. Each acclaimed his own candidate as Great Khan. Each refused to recognize the other's election. The empire's first openly contested succession had begun.

This was not a minor technical dispute. It was a constitutional crisis that exposed an unresolved question about what kind of empire the Mongol Empire was going to be.

**Ariq Böke's faction** represented the steppe traditionalists. The traditional reading was that the Mongol Empire was a steppe empire that had conquered settled territories, ruled them through indirect taxation and tribute, and remained anchored in the Mongolian heartland. The Great Khan should be elected at Karakorum by the senior princes of the steppe. The settled territories were sources of revenue, not centers of power. Cultural change should flow from the steppe to the conquered, not the other way around. Ariq Böke's supporters included most of the central Asian and Mongolian nobility, and his economic base was the Mongol heartland.

**Kublai's faction** represented the alternative. Kublai had spent his adult career governing the conquered Chinese territories. He had read Confucian classics, employed Chinese administrators, sponsored Chinese-style architecture, and concluded that the only sustainable way to govern China was as a Chinese-style emperor — using Chinese tax registers, Chinese administrative procedures, and a Chinese-style permanent capital. The Great Khan, in Kublai's view, should be where the empire's largest tax base was, which meant in or near China. Cultural change would necessarily flow both ways. Kublai's supporters included the Sinicized Mongol nobility and the Chinese bureaucratic class who had bet their careers on Mongol rule. His economic base was northern China.

The civil war is called the **Toluid Civil War** because both sides were Toluids — sons of Tolui, brothers of each other. It lasted from 1260 to 1264. Kublai had the larger economic base. Ariq Böke had the symbolic legitimacy of Karakorum. Kublai's strategy was to cut Karakorum's grain supply (the steppe capital depended on grain shipments from north China) and starve his brother into submission. The strategy worked. Ariq Böke surrendered in 1264 and died in custody two years later, possibly poisoned. Kublai was Great Khan.

But the price of victory was that the empire was no longer governable as a single political unit. Hülegü in Persia, who had endorsed Kublai but did not actually obey him, became the founder of the **Ilkhanate** as an effectively independent state. **Berke**, the new Khan of the **Golden Horde** in the western steppe, opposed Kublai entirely and went to war against Hülegü over the sack of Baghdad (Berke had converted to Islam years earlier and considered the destruction of the caliph an outrage). The **Chagatai Khanate** in Central Asia oscillated between loyalty to Kublai and independence depending on who was in charge.

After 1264, the Mongol Empire was a single political entity in name but four separate states in practice. Kublai's Yuan dynasty in China and Mongolia. The Ilkhanate in Persia and Iraq. The Golden Horde across the Pontic-Caspian steppe. The Chagatai Khanate in Central Asia. They cooperated when convenient, fought when not, traded extensively, and collected tribute from each other only theoretically. The Pax Mongolica that emerged in the next decades (Chapter 7) ran across the four khanates not because they were politically unified but because their elites shared kinship, language, and economic interests strong enough to keep the trade routes open through occasional warfare.

---

What Kublai did with northern China next is one of the most consequential cultural translations in medieval history.

Kublai had been planning to govern China as a Chinese-style emperor since the 1250s. After 1264, with the empire's political center safely under his control, he executed the plan.

In 1267 he began constructing a new capital city at the site of the Jin dynasty's old Zhongdu, which the Mongols had destroyed in 1215 (Chapter 3). The new city was named **Khanbaliq** in Mongol (KAHN-bah-lik, "City of the Khan") and **Dadu** in Chinese (DAH-doo, "Great Capital"). It is now Beijing. The city was laid out on a Chinese imperial grid with palaces in the center, four cardinal gates, and a deliberately ceremonial geometry borrowed from Confucian cosmology — but with Mongol-style enclosed hunting parks, Tibetan Buddhist temples, mosques for the Central Asian Muslim merchant community, and a Nestorian Christian church for the Kerait elements of the imperial household. It was a Chinese capital occupied by a steppe court that was governing China through Chinese institutions while remaining culturally distinct.

In 1269 Kublai commissioned a new writing system from his Tibetan Buddhist tutor, the **'Phags-pa lama** (PHAGS-pah, the Tibetan religious leader Drogön Chögyal Phagpa). The **'Phags-pa script** was an alphabetic system designed to render Mongolian, Tibetan, Chinese, and other languages of the empire in a single phonetic notation. It was supposed to be the empire's universal script. The technical achievement was real — 'Phags-pa was one of the earliest serious attempts at a phonetic alphabet for Chinese — but the political reception was tepid. Chinese officials continued to use Chinese characters. Mongolians continued to use the Uyghur-based Mongolian script. 'Phags-pa survived for a century as a ceremonial script for imperial seals and inscriptions, then fell out of use. Korean scholars in the 1440s would consult it when they designed the Hangul alphabet, which is its main long-term legacy.

In 1271, Kublai formally proclaimed a new Chinese-style dynasty, the **Yuan** (yoo-AHN — the name comes from the *Yi Jing*, the Book of Changes, where it can mean "origin" or "primal"). The proclamation was not just a name change. It signaled that Kublai was the legitimate emperor of all China by Chinese standards — that is, that he held the **Mandate of Heaven** (Ancient China Chapter 5), the Chinese theological doctrine that legitimate rule was conferred by the cosmic order on a single dynasty at a time. By taking the Mandate, Kublai was claiming continuity with the long sequence of Chinese dynasties stretching back through the Han (Han Dynasty narrative) to the Xia of myth. He was also claiming, implicitly, that the **Southern Song** dynasty — the southern Chinese state that had survived the Mongol destruction of the Jin and was now the Yuan's only remaining Chinese rival — no longer held legitimacy. The Mandate moved when the dynasty changed. The new dynasty was Yuan. Therefore the Song had to go.

---

The Song campaign, which had paused during the Toluid Civil War, resumed in 1267. The first major operation was the **Siege of Xiangyang** (shyang-YAHNG), the twin fortress-cities of **Xiangyang** and **Fancheng** on the Han River, which controlled access to the Yangtze River basin and therefore to the Song heartland. Xiangyang was militarily impossible to take by conventional Mongol siege. The walls were too thick, the riverine supply line too strong, the garrisons too well-trained. The siege lasted six years (1267–1273) without resolution.

What broke the deadlock was technology. In 1271, Kublai's court hired two Persian engineers — **Ismail** and **Ala al-Din**, both from the Ilkhanate, both specialists in counterweight trebuchets — to come to China and design siege artillery that could throw projectiles heavy enough to crack the Xiangyang walls. The engineers built what the Chinese sources called **Huihui pao** ("Muslim catapults") — counterweight trebuchets capable of hurling 200-pound stones over 200 yards. These were the same machines that European armies were beginning to deploy, the Mongols having transmitted the technology westward from the Persian world a generation earlier and then eastward into China. Xiangyang fell in 1273 within months of the new artillery's arrival. The whole episode is a small case study in how the Pax Mongolica worked: an engineering technique developed in the Islamic world was deployed in Persia under Mongol patronage, transmitted to China by Mongol-employed Persians, and used to break a Chinese fortress that Chinese siege technology could not crack.

After Xiangyang, the Song collapse was inevitable. The Yangtze cities fell one after another to Yuan forces under the general **Bayan of the Baarin** (a Mongol commander who had served at Hülegü's court and was returned to Kublai's service for the Song campaign). The Song court fled south, dragging the child emperor with it. **Hangzhou**, the Song capital, was occupied by Bayan in 1276 without a major siege; the empress dowager surrendered the imperial seal and the dynasty was formally extinguished. A small Song loyalist faction continued to fight at sea.

The final battle was at **Yamen** (yah-MEN), in the estuary of the Pearl River near modern Macau, in March 1279. The Song loyalist fleet was destroyed in a naval engagement that featured early **fire-lance weapons** — bamboo or paper tubes packed with gunpowder and projectiles, the immediate ancestors of firearms — used by Yuan forces against Song wooden ships. Over a hundred thousand Song loyalists, including the seven-year-old child emperor, died at Yamen. The senior Song minister **Lu Xiufu**, having lost the battle and unable to escape, picked up the child emperor and walked into the sea. The Song dynasty was ended.

The Yuan now ruled all of China — the first time in three centuries that China had been politically unified under a single dynasty. It was also the first time in Chinese history that all of China was ruled by a non-Chinese ethnic group. The political and cultural implications would echo through Chinese historical memory for the rest of imperial history.

---

The Yuan court at Khanbaliq became, in the late thirteenth century, one of the most cosmopolitan capitals in the medieval world.

**Marco Polo** (MAR-koh POH-loh, the Venetian merchant, 1254–1324, who traveled overland to China with his father and uncle and arrived at Kublai's court in 1275) is the most famous European visitor of the period, but he was far from the only one. Polo spent seventeen years in Yuan service, traveled extensively in southern China and Yunnan, and (per his own account, dictated to a fellow prisoner of war during a brief incarceration in Genoa in 1298) served as a salt-tax administrator and itinerant inspector for Kublai's court. His book — *The Description of the World*, also called *Il Milione* in the Italian tradition — is one of the most influential travel narratives ever written, and it inspired (among many others) Christopher Columbus, who carried a heavily annotated copy with him on his voyages two centuries later. Whether Marco Polo's account is fully reliable has been debated for centuries; he describes things that are clearly accurate (paper money, the use of coal as fuel, the postal Yam system) and things that are puzzling absences (he never mentions the Great Wall, tea, or footbinding). The current scholarly consensus is that the broad outlines of his travels are accurate but that some specific claims are exaggerated or borrowed from other sources.

Polo was preceded by the Franciscan **John of Plano Carpini** (the papal envoy at Güyük's coronation, Chapter 4), the Flemish missionary **William of Rubruck** (at Möngke's court in 1253–1254, Chapter 4), and followed by the Franciscan **John of Montecorvino**, who arrived at Kublai's court in the 1290s and became the first Catholic Archbishop of Beijing — a position he held until his death in 1328, by which time he had reportedly converted several thousand Chinese and Mongol Christians.

The court itself contained Tibetan Buddhist monks, Nestorian Christian clerks, Sunni Muslim engineers, Confucian scholars, Persian astronomers, and a permanent population of foreign craftsmen of every description. The empire's official religious policy continued the Yassa's principle of tolerance — Tibetan Buddhism was preferred at court (Kublai's chief consort **Chabi** was a devout Buddhist and a major patron of the Tibetan Buddhist establishment), but no religion was systematically persecuted, and the imperial treasury supported temples of all major faiths.

The Yuan's foreign-policy ambitions were as expansive as its domestic ones. Kublai sent fleets against **Japan** twice — in 1274 and 1281 — both of which were destroyed by typhoons that the Japanese called **kamikaze** (kah-mee-KAH-zay, "divine wind"). The 1281 invasion was the largest naval operation in pre-modern history: roughly 4,400 ships and 140,000 men, sailing from Korea and southern China. A typhoon in August 1281 destroyed most of the fleet at sea, drowning tens of thousands. The Japanese took the typhoon as evidence of divine protection, and the kamikaze concept entered Japanese national mythology where it would have, much later, a different and darker afterlife. Kublai also sent a fleet against the Javanese kingdom of **Singhasari** in 1293, which was deceived by a local prince named **Raden Wijaya** into helping him overthrow a rival, after which Wijaya turned on the Mongols and drove them off the island. It was the empire's most embarrassing overseas expedition, and Kublai never sent another.

Kublai Khan died in 1294. He was approximately seventy-nine years old. He had outlived three of his sons, including his designated heir **Zhenjin**. The Yuan dynasty would continue under his grandson **Temür Öljeitü** and a succession of weaker emperors for another seventy years, but the dynasty's height — politically, militarily, and culturally — was Kublai's reign.

By his death, Eurasia was permanently changed. China had been forcibly reconnected to the Persian and Islamic worlds, and through them to the Mediterranean and Europe. Paper currency was circulating from Khanbaliq to Tabriz. Manuscripts were moving along the Silk Road in volumes that had not been seen since the Tang dynasty. Persian astronomers were correcting Chinese calendars and Chinese silk-weavers were teaching Persian craftsmen new techniques. The Mongol moment had become the first sustained system of cross-civilizational exchange that any of these regions had ever experienced. Whether the system was good or bad depended on whether you were among the millions who had died for it or among the millions who lived along the trade routes that had survived it.

# Chapter 7 — The Four Khanates

By 1294, the Mongol Empire was four states.

This was not entirely a failure. The political fragmentation that followed the Toluid Civil War had hardened into a working system. The four khanates cooperated more often than they fought. They recognized each other's senior khans (with certain exceptions). They allowed merchants, missionaries, and diplomats to cross each other's territories under the protection of imperial **paiza** tablets. And they shared a kinship structure — every senior ruler in every khanate was a descendant of Genghis Khan or a Genghisid son-in-law — that gave the system a kind of constitutional family logic. The Mongol Empire after 1264 was not a centralized state. It was a **confederal dynastic system** held together by a dwindling but real shared identity.

This chapter is what the four khanates looked like at their relative peak in the late thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries.

---

The **Yuan dynasty** in China and Mongolia was the largest and richest of the four. Population perhaps eighty to a hundred million people — a number that includes the dramatic demographic losses of the conquest period, after which the Chinese population was substantially reduced from late-Song highs. The Yuan governed China through a hybrid bureaucracy that combined Mongol decimal-military structures at the top with Chinese-style ministries below. The political ranking of subjects under Yuan law was famously stratified by ethnic category: Mongols at the top, **Semu** ("various sorts" — non-Han non-Mongol Asians, especially Central Asian Muslims and Persians serving as administrators and merchants) second, **Hanren** (northern Chinese, who had been under foreign rule longer and were considered more reliable) third, and **Nanren** (southern Chinese, the recently conquered Song subjects) fourth. The system was not as rigid in practice as it sounds on paper — many Chinese officials served at high levels — but it produced significant resentment among the Han Chinese gentry, particularly the southern gentry whose paths to power through the traditional **civil-service examination** were closed for most of the Yuan period.

The closure of the examination system had cultural consequences. Educated Chinese who could not enter the bureaucracy turned to other forms of expression. The most consequential was the explosion of **zaju** (TZAH-joo) drama — the form of Chinese theater combining music, dialogue, dance, and acrobatics — which became the leading literary form of the Yuan period. Playwrights like **Guan Hanqing** wrote works such as **The Injustice to Dou E**, dramas of social commentary and emotional depth that are still performed today. The vernacular novel — including early versions of *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* and *Water Margin* — also developed in this period as another channel for Chinese literary energy locked out of the bureaucracy. Chinese culture under Yuan rule produced some of its most enduring popular forms in part because the political ladder for educated Chinese had been chopped off.

Yuan economic innovation was extensive. Kublai's **Chao** (CHOW) paper currency — backed by silver and silk reserves and made the sole legal tender of the empire — was the largest and most successful paper money operation of the medieval world. By the early fourteenth century, copper coins were rarely used in major commercial transactions in China; everyone used paper. The system worked beautifully for fifty years, then broke down when the late Yuan court began over-issuing currency to cover military and infrastructure expenses, leading to dramatic inflation. Marco Polo described the paper money operation with unconcealed astonishment: a piece of paper, declared by the emperor to be worth a certain weight of silver, accepted by everyone in the empire as if it actually were that weight of silver. In thirteenth-century Europe such a system was unimaginable.

The Yuan also extended the **Grand Canal** — the major north-south inland waterway of China — to run from Hangzhou directly to Khanbaliq, rerouting older Sui and Tang segments and adding new sections to bring grain barges from the Yangtze valley straight to the Mongol capital. The completed canal was approximately 1,100 miles (1,800 km) long and served as the economic artery connecting the agriculturally rich south to the politically central north. It would remain the spine of Chinese internal commerce until the construction of railways centuries later.

The Yuan court patronized one of the most accurate astronomical observatories in the medieval world. The astronomer **Guo Shoujing** (GWAW SHOH-jing) — working in collaboration with Persian astronomers from the Maragheh tradition (see Ilkhanate below) — produced the **Shoushi calendar** in 1281, calculating the length of the solar year to within 26 seconds of the modern figure. The Shoushi calendar was the most accurate calendar in the world at the time and remained in official use in China for over three centuries.

---

The **Ilkhanate** (IL-kah-nate, "subordinate khanate") in Persia and Iraq was the second-largest of the four states. Population perhaps fifteen to twenty million. Capital first at **Maragheh**, later at **Tabriz** in northwestern Iran, eventually at **Sultaniyya**.

The Ilkhanate is the khanate where the cultural-resurrection pattern that the Persian Empire narrative documents (Persian Empire Chapter 10 — the Persian habit of culturally absorbing every conqueror) operated most clearly. The Ilkhans were Mongols who within two generations were ruling as Persian-style monarchs. **Ghazan Khan** (reigned 1295–1304), the seventh Ilkhan and the most consequential, **converted to Islam** publicly in 1295, made it the Ilkhanate's official religion, took a Muslim name (Mahmud), and adopted Persian-style royal protocols. His chief minister **Rashid al-Din Hamadani** (rah-SHEED al-DEEN), a Jewish-born physician who converted to Islam and became one of the most extraordinary administrators of the medieval period, ran the Ilkhanate's tax reform, agricultural revival, and cultural patronage program for two decades.

Rashid al-Din's most enduring legacy was a literary one. He compiled the **Jami' al-tawarikh** (jah-MEE al-tah-WAH-reekh, "Compendium of Chronicles"), completed around 1307. The Compendium is, by serious scholarly consensus, the first true world history. It contains the histories of the Mongols (drawing on documents at the Ilkhan court that have not survived elsewhere), the Chinese (drawing on Chinese historians whom Rashid al-Din consulted directly), the Indians, the Jews, the Christian Europeans (Rashid al-Din corresponded with European clerics), the Muslims, and the pre-Islamic Iranians. It is illustrated, in surviving manuscripts, with miniatures that synthesize Persian, Chinese, and Byzantine artistic traditions in a new style. No historian before Rashid al-Din had attempted to write a single work covering all of the world's major civilizations using sources from each. The work is one of the great scholarly products of the Pax Mongolica, made possible by an empire that physically connected the source materials for the first time.

The other extraordinary cultural product of the Ilkhanate was the **Maragheh Observatory** (mah-RAH-geh), founded by Hülegü Khan in 1259 and led by **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi** — the same scholar Hülegü had rescued from Alamut (Chapter 5). Maragheh was, in its day, the most sophisticated astronomical observatory in the world. It contained a 14-foot mural quadrant, an armillary sphere, a parallactic ruler, and instruments for systematic stellar observation. Al-Tusi and his successors compiled the **Zij-i Ilkhani** (zeej-ee il-KAH-nee), a set of astronomical tables that corrected long-standing errors in Ptolemaic astronomy. Al-Tusi's mathematical work — including the **Tusi couple** — produced solutions to problems in planetary motion that fed forward, through transmission via Greek-speaking refugees in the Byzantine and Renaissance worlds, into the work of Copernicus three centuries later. Specific Copernican diagrams in *De Revolutionibus* (1543) match al-Tusi's diagrams in suspicious detail. The transmission lineage is debated but the visual coincidence is striking.

The Ilkhanate also produced **Sultaniyya** (sool-tah-NEE-yah), the planned capital city built by Ilkhan **Öljaitü** (uhl-JAY-too, Ghazan's brother and successor) in the early 1300s. Sultaniyya's centerpiece was Öljaitü's mausoleum, a massive double-shell dome — one of the most ambitious architectural feats of the medieval world, and a clear ancestor of the great domed mausoleums of later Persian and Indian Islamic architecture, including (eventually) the Taj Mahal. The dome stands today, partly restored. It is one of the most underrated buildings in world architectural history.

The Ilkhanate's political end came in 1335 with the death of Ilkhan **Abu Sa'id** (ah-BOO sah-EED) without an heir. The khanate fragmented into competing dynasties — the **Jalayirids**, **Chobanids**, **Muzaffarids** — none of which could reunify Persia. Persia would remain politically fragmented until **Timur** rebuilt a centralized state through conquest in the 1370s (Chapter 9).

---

The **Golden Horde** (so called from the Russian translation of the Mongol *Altan Ordu*, "Golden Tent" or "Golden Camp," the name of the senior khan's enormous ceremonial pavilion; the term came into Western usage centuries later, but it does correspond to the Mongol original) ruled the **Kipchak Steppe** — the grasslands of modern Russia, Ukraine, and Kazakhstan — and the Russian principalities. Capital at **Sarai** on the lower Volga. Population mostly Turkic-speaking steppe nomads with a substantial Slavic agrarian base in the Russian principalities.

The Golden Horde governed Russia through what later Russian historians called the **Tatar Yoke**: a system of tribute extraction in which the Russian princes were confirmed in office by the khan in Sarai, paid annual tribute (in silver, furs, and conscripted soldiers), and were periodically replaced when they displeased the khan or when a rival prince outbid them. The Russian Orthodox Church was exempt from taxation by Mongol decree (the Yassa's religious-tolerance principle, applied in practice) and used the period of foreign overlordship to consolidate its institutional position — the Mongol period is when Moscow began its rise to dominance in Russian church and state, partly because the Moscow princes were better than their rivals at managing the relationship with Sarai.

The Golden Horde's economic base was its position as the middle stretch of the trans-Eurasian trade corridor. From the late thirteenth century onward, Genoese and Venetian merchants operated trading colonies on the northern Black Sea coast — most importantly **Caffa** (modern Feodosia in Crimea), granted to Genoa around 1267. Caffa funneled Chinese silks, Persian spices, and Central Asian slaves into Mediterranean markets, and brought European silver and craft goods east. The Genoese-Mongol commercial relationship was one of the most important economic partnerships of the late thirteenth century, and it would matter dramatically for what was about to happen with the plague (Chapter 8).

The Golden Horde converted to Islam under **Berke Khan** (Genghis's grandson, the same Berke who had gone to war with Hülegü over Baghdad). Berke himself converted around 1252. The conversion was finalized as state policy under **Öz Beg Khan** (also spelled Uzbek; reigned 1313–1341), who made Islam the official religion of the Horde and forced the conversion of the Mongol nobility. Öz Beg's reign was the Horde's economic and political peak. The Moroccan traveler **Ibn Battuta** (the most-traveled person of the medieval world; he eventually covered 70,000 miles across Africa, the Middle East, India, China, and Spain in a thirty-year career) visited Öz Beg's court in 1332 and left the most detailed eyewitness account of Golden Horde society available anywhere.

---

The **Chagatai Khanate** in Central Asia was the most fragile of the four. It controlled the region from the Tian Shan mountains to the Aral Sea, including Transoxiana (the great trade region between the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers, containing Samarkand, Bukhara, and other formerly-Khwarezmian cities) and the **Tarim Basin** to the east. Its population was a mix of Turkic-speaking nomads in the steppe zones and Persian-speaking urban populations in the cities.

The Chagatai khans inherited the political problem of trying to govern both nomadic and urban subjects with the same administrative apparatus, and they spent most of their two centuries failing. The khanate fractured repeatedly, with the western half (Transoxiana — the urban and Islamic-mercantile zone) drifting toward Persian-Islamic culture and the eastern half (Moghulistan — the steppe zone) remaining nomadic and shamanic. **Kebek Khan** (reigned 1318–1326) attempted economic reforms, introducing a standard silver coin and building a fixed palace at Qarshi. **Tarmashirin Khan** (TAR-mah-shih-reen, reigned 1331–1334) converted to Islam and tried to enforce Islamic law on his nomadic subjects, which alienated the eastern tribes; he was assassinated, and the khanate split formally into the western Chagatai (Muslim) and Moghulistan (largely non-Muslim) by the 1340s.

The Chagatai Khanate would be the political base from which **Timur** would emerge a generation later (Chapter 9). Timur claimed Mongol descent through the Barlas tribe and ruled in the name of puppet Chagatai khans. His empire was the immediate political descendant of the Chagatai system, and through the Timurids the Chagatai legacy fed into the eventual Mughal Empire of India.

---

What held this four-khanate system together — economically, even when the khanates were politically at odds — was the **Pax Mongolica**.

The Pax Mongolica (the "Mongol Peace") was the period from roughly the 1260s to the 1340s during which long-distance trade, diplomatic exchange, and intellectual transmission across Eurasia operated at a level not seen before and not seen again until the early modern period. Its operating mechanisms were several.

**The Yam relay system** (Chapter 4) provided physical infrastructure for travel — stations, fresh horses, food, lodging, all available to anyone holding an imperial **paiza** tablet. The Yam stations operated across all four khanates with reasonable interoperability; a merchant with the right tablet could travel from China to Persia using Yam infrastructure that was continuously maintained by local Mongol authorities.

**Religious tolerance** (the Yassa's principle, never abandoned even after various khanates converted to Islam or Buddhism) meant that merchants and missionaries of any faith could operate across the empire with reasonable safety. Christian friars, Muslim qadis, Buddhist monks, and Jewish administrators all moved along the Mongol routes.

**Commercial security** was enforced by Mongol garrisons against bandits, and merchants' losses were sometimes compensated from Mongol treasuries. The reduction in robbery and arbitrary local tolls made overland trade dramatically cheaper than it had been in the centuries before the conquest.

**Standardized currency** — paper money in China, silver coinage in the Ilkhanate, the Genoese silver-based commercial system in the Golden Horde — meant that prices could be compared across regions and contracts could be enforced.

**Information networks** connected merchants and rulers across the empire. A merchant in Tabriz could (and many did) receive reliable news about prices in Beijing within months. A diplomatic missive from the Ilkhan to the Yuan could reach its destination in ninety days. By the standards of any earlier period, this was effectively real-time communication.

The Pax Mongolica is the reason Marco Polo could exist as a phenomenon. It is also the reason that within a decade of the system's collapse, the Black Death would be in Italy.

Cross-civ check: at the height of the Pax Mongolica, around 1300, the Ilkhanate was in trade and diplomatic contact with the **Mamluk Sultanate** in Egypt (despite intermittent warfare), the **Byzantine Empire** in Constantinople, the **Republic of Venice** (and through Venice, the Holy Roman Empire and France), the **Delhi Sultanate** in India, and the various smaller Christian and Muslim states of the Caucasus and Anatolia. The Yuan was in trade contact with **Goryeo Korea**, the **Kamakura shogunate** in Japan (despite the failed invasions, low-grade trade resumed), various Southeast Asian kingdoms, and through Indian Ocean shipping with the Islamic world. The Golden Horde was in active commercial relationship with the Italian city-states, the Hanseatic cities of the Baltic, the Russian principalities, and the Lithuanian Grand Duchy. There has never been another period in pre-modern history when so many distinct civilizations were so simultaneously in active contact along regular trade routes.

# Chapter 8 — The Silk Road, the Plague, the Decline

The Pax Mongolica produced unprecedented connection. The connection killed perhaps a third of Eurasia's population.

The Black Death of the 1340s and 1350s is one of the foundational catastrophes of the medieval period, and it spread in the way it spread because of the very Mongol-built trade infrastructure that had brought silk, spices, paper, gunpowder, astronomical knowledge, and Marco Polo's stories to places that had never seen them. This chapter is what the empire looked like as it began to come apart, and how the same connections that had been its strongest accomplishment turned, in a single decade, into its greatest cost.

---

The first thing to track is the religious transformation of the Mongol elite, because it shaped every other dynamic of the late period.

In the conquering generation, the Mongols had been pluralists. Genghis Khan's Yassa (Chapter 2) mandated tolerance of all major religions, exempted clergy from taxation, and treated religious persecution as a capital offense. The court at Karakorum had hosted Buddhist temples, Nestorian churches, mosques, and shamanic shrines simultaneously. Möngke Khan presided over a public theological debate at Karakorum in 1258 between Buddhists, Daoists, Christians, and Muslims, with Möngke (a Tengrist shamanist himself) functioning as moderator (the Buddhists were declared the winners; the more important point is that the debate was held, with imperial sponsorship, with all parties protected, in the Mongol heartland).

By 1330, three of the four khanates had converted to one of the religions of the conquered.

**The Ilkhanate** went Muslim in 1295 with **Ghazan Khan's** conversion. The conversion was strategic — Ghazan needed legitimacy with his Persian-speaking Muslim subjects, who outnumbered the Mongol elite by a factor of at least fifty — but it was also genuine. Ghazan's reforms (Chapter 7) were carried out as a Muslim ruler, with Persian Islamic legal advisors, in conscious continuity with the long Persian tradition of Islamic monarchy. By Ghazan's death in 1304, the Ilkhanate was fully a Persian-Islamic state in everything but the ruling dynasty's blood, and even that was diluting through marriage with Persian aristocratic women.

**The Golden Horde** went Muslim under **Öz Beg Khan** in 1313 (Chapter 7), in a more politically consolidating conversion. Öz Beg's predecessor Berke had already been a Muslim, but Berke's conversion had been personal and his descendants had drifted back. Öz Beg made it state policy and forced the noble class.

**The Chagatai Khanate** divided in 1334 with the assassination of **Tarmashirin Khan** (Chapter 7), who had tried to convert the whole khanate to Islam and had been killed by traditionalists. The result was a permanent split: the western Chagatai zone (Transoxiana, the Persian-cultural cities) became Muslim; the eastern Chagatai zone (Moghulistan, the steppe zone) remained largely Buddhist, shamanic, or syncretic for several more centuries.

**The Yuan** in China was the exception. The Yuan court patronized Tibetan Buddhism heavily — Kublai's wife Chabi was a Tibetan Buddhist patron, and Tibetan lamas held high status at Khanbaliq for the rest of the dynasty — but it never made Tibetan Buddhism the imperial state religion in a way that excluded other faiths. Confucianism remained the operative ideology of the Chinese bureaucracy. Daoism, Buddhism, and Islam coexisted under formal protection. The Yuan was the most religiously plural of the late Mongol states, and that pluralism would matter when the dynasty came under pressure.

The mechanisms of conversion at the elite level are worth pausing on, because the question "how does a steppe pagan ruling class become Muslim within two generations" is not obvious. The answer is that the rulers married into the populations they had conquered. Mongol khans took wives from local aristocracies, especially Persian and Turkic-Muslim aristocracies in the western khanates. The wives raised the next generation with their own languages, religions, and habits. Mongol noble children grew up bilingual, with Muslim mothers and Turco-Mongol fathers, and the religious balance shifted toward the mothers' side over a few generations. This is also how the khans of the Golden Horde turned into Turkic-speaking Muslim sultans by 1400, and how the Chagatai aristocracy became the Persian-cultured elite that produced the Timurids and the Mughals (Chapter 9). The Mongol elite in the western and central khanates ceased to be culturally Mongol within four or five generations after the conquest. They had been absorbed.

---

The other thing to track in the early fourteenth century is the slow erosion of the Pax Mongolica itself.

The Ilkhanate fragmented after **Abu Sa'id's** death in 1335 (Chapter 7). Persian trade routes that had been continuously secured by Ilkhan authority were now subject to the feuding of regional warlords. Caravans still moved, but with reduced frequency and higher insurance costs.

The Chagatai split after Tarmashirin (1334) reduced the security of the central Asian segment of the Silk Road. Bandit activity in Transoxiana increased. Some merchants began rerouting to maritime routes through the Indian Ocean rather than overland routes through Central Asia.

The Yuan was beginning a long decline that historians have sometimes blamed on inflation (the over-issuance of paper money to cover military expenses), sometimes on natural disasters (the Yellow River shifted course catastrophically in 1344, flooding millions of acres and killing a still-debated number of people), sometimes on political infighting at the imperial court, sometimes on growing Han Chinese resentment at Mongol ethnic stratification. All of these were factors. The dynasty was visibly weakening by the 1340s.

The Golden Horde was at its peak under Öz Beg (died 1341) but went into civil-war fragmentation under his successors. The period after 1359 — what Russian historians call the **Velikaya Zamyatnya** (Great Troubles) — saw twenty different Golden Horde khans in twenty years, most assassinated by the next claimant.

The Pax Mongolica had been a working system for roughly seventy years. It had never been frictionless — the khanates had fought each other repeatedly, banditry had never been eliminated, plagues and famines had always disrupted local commerce — but the overall framework had held. By 1340, the framework was fraying everywhere simultaneously.

And then the plague arrived.

---

The **Black Death** is the modern English name for the second pandemic of bubonic and pneumonic plague — the bacterial infection caused by **Yersinia pestis**, transmitted primarily by fleas riding on rodents (especially the black rat, *Rattus rattus*), with secondary transmission between humans through respiratory droplets in the pneumonic form. The first historically documented plague pandemic, the **Plague of Justinian**, had hit the Byzantine and Sassanid empires in the sixth century (Persian Empire Chapter 9) and recurred for two centuries before fading.

The second pandemic — what we now call the Black Death — had its origins in Central Asia, almost certainly in the **Tian Shan-Issyk Kul region** of modern Kyrgyzstan, where genetic analysis of plague victims' remains in the 2020s confirmed the presence of *Yersinia pestis* in graves dated to the 1330s. The plague had probably been endemic in Central Asian rodent populations for centuries, but in the 1330s and 1340s it broke out into human populations on a scale that overwhelmed local containment.

From its Central Asian source, the plague spread along Mongol trade routes in three main directions. East along the routes to China — there are credible Chinese chronicle reports of devastating epidemics in Yuan territory in the 1330s and 1340s, and the population of China apparently dropped substantially during this period, though attributing the drop entirely to plague is complicated by the parallel famines and rebellions. West along the routes to Persia and the Caucasus — the Ilkhanate's successor states reported epidemics in the 1340s that are most consistently identified with plague. North along the routes to the Volga and the Pontic steppe — the Golden Horde's territories were severely affected.

The plague reached the **Genoese trading colony of Caffa** in 1346. This is the famous moment. The Golden Horde Khan **Janibeg** was besieging Caffa over a commercial dispute. According to the Italian chronicler **Gabriele de' Mussis** (writing from Piacenza in the 1350s), Janibeg's army was hit by the plague during the siege, and rather than waste the dead, he had the corpses catapulted over the walls into the city. The story is sometimes cited as the earliest documented instance of biological warfare. Whether it actually happened in this specific way is contested — de' Mussis was not at Caffa, and his account is the only source — but something epidemiologically equivalent did occur. Plague reached Caffa during the siege. The Genoese garrison eventually evacuated by sea. The Genoese galleys carrying the survivors stopped in Constantinople, in Sicily, in Genoa itself. The plague went with them.

By the autumn of 1347, plague was in Sicily. By 1348, it was in southern France, Spain, and Italy. By 1349, it was in northern France, England, and the Low Countries. By 1350, it was in Scandinavia and the Baltic. The mortality rate in affected European populations is debated; the lowest serious estimates are around twenty-five percent, the highest are around fifty percent. The most-cited modern estimate is around one third of Europe's population dead within the first three years. Repeating outbreaks over the next century kept the population suppressed.

The Mongol responsibility for this is a delicate question. Plague was not invented by the Mongols, and the original pathogen reservoirs in Central Asian rodents had existed long before the empire. But the connectivity that the Pax Mongolica had built was the necessary infrastructure for spreading the pathogen across continents at the speed it traveled. Without the Yam network, without the Genoese-Mongol commercial alliance, without the integrated trade infrastructure between China, Persia, and the Mediterranean, the plague might have spread eventually but would have moved more slowly. The Mongol-built system that had carried Marco Polo to China in fifteen years carried *Yersinia pestis* from the Tian Shan to England in fifteen months. Connection had a cost.

---

The Yuan dynasty's collapse came from a combination of factors that the plague accelerated but did not single-handedly cause.

The most consequential of those factors was the **Yellow River**.

The Yellow River — China's Sorrow, the great loess-laden waterway whose floods had shaped Chinese civilization for thousands of years (Ancient China Chapter 1) — shifted its course catastrophically in 1344. Massive flooding destroyed agricultural land across northern China and forced the displacement of millions. The Yuan court ordered the construction of an enormous dike-and-redirection project that conscripted 150,000 laborers from the Yellow River valley. The project worked, in the engineering sense — the river was successfully redirected — but the conscription, combined with the famines and epidemics of the 1340s, produced exactly the kind of mass discontent that medieval Chinese rebellions had always emerged from.

The rebellion that emerged was the **Red Turban Rebellion**, which erupted in 1351. It was led at first by **Han Shantong**, a charismatic preacher who claimed descent from the Song imperial family and promised the return of the Maitreya Buddha (the future Buddha of Mahayana eschatology, whose arrival would mark a new age). The movement combined **White Lotus** millenarian Buddhism (a heterodox Buddhist tradition with messianic elements that had circulated in Chinese folk religion for centuries) with anti-Mongol nationalism. The rebels wore red turbans as a sign of recognition and rallied around the slogan that the Yuan dynasty had lost the Mandate of Heaven.

Han Shantong was caught and executed early in the uprising, but the rebellion spread. From its many regional leaders emerged one figure who would matter most: **Zhu Yuanzhang** (JOO yoo-AHN-jahng), a peasant orphan from Anhui who had been raised in a Buddhist monastery and had risen through one of the Red Turban factions. Zhu was politically ruthless, militarily competent, and good at consolidating allies. By 1356 he had taken Nanjing. By 1367 he had defeated his rival Red Turban factions and consolidated the rebellion's forces under his own command. In January 1368 he proclaimed a new dynasty — the **Ming** ("Brilliant") — with himself as the **Hongwu Emperor**.

In September 1368, Ming armies marched on Khanbaliq. The last Yuan emperor, **Toghon Temür** (TOH-gohn teh-MOOR), did not stay to defend the city. He took his court, his treasury, and as many of the imperial guard as could be assembled, and fled north to the old Yuan summer capital at **Shangdu** (the city Marco Polo had called Xanadu). When Ming forces approached Shangdu the next year, he fled further north into the Mongolian steppe. He died in 1370.

The Yuan dynasty had ruled China for ninety-seven years. The flight of Toghon Temür did not extinguish the dynasty entirely — his descendants maintained what historians call the **Northern Yuan**, ruling a reduced Mongol state on the steppe for another two centuries — but it ended Mongol rule over China proper. The Mandate had moved.

The Ming's first project, under the Hongwu Emperor, was to expel as many remaining Mongol officials and ethnic Mongols from China as could be expelled, restore the civil-service examination system, and reassert Han Chinese ethnic identity as the basis of imperial legitimacy. Hongwu also restored the Great Wall — extending and rebuilding the older fortifications into the magnificent stone structure that tourists see today. The wall that defines China's northern frontier in the modern imagination is not Qin Shi Huang's wall (which was rammed earth, mostly eroded by the fourteenth century, Qin Dynasty Chapter 5). It is the Ming wall, built specifically against the descendants of Genghis Khan.

---

# Chapter 9 — The Long Echo

The Mongol Empire, as a single political entity, ended in 1368 with the flight of Toghon Temür. The four khanates' direct successor states limped on for various periods: the Yuan continued as the Northern Yuan on the Mongolian steppe until the seventeenth century; the Golden Horde fragmented into successor khanates (Crimean, Kazan, Astrakhan, Sibir) that survived in some cases into the eighteenth century; the Chagatai zone produced new dynasties (Timurid, Moghul) that claimed Genghisid descent; the Ilkhanate's successor dynasties were absorbed into the Timurid empire. By the early sixteenth century, no state in the world was a direct continuation of the Mongol political order in any meaningful institutional sense.

But the empire's effects did not end with its political structures. They continued for centuries, in some cases up to the present, and they are the reason the Mongol Empire is included as a civilization in this app rather than as a footnote in someone else's. This chapter is what the Mongols left behind.

---

The first and biggest legacy was demographic. The Mongol conquests killed somewhere between thirty and sixty million people, the lower figures from cautious modern historians and the higher figures from contemporary chroniclers who tended to inflate. Even the cautious numbers are extraordinary in pre-modern terms — the conquests killed perhaps seven to ten percent of the world's total population. Northern China lost a substantial share of its pre-Mongol population. Persia and Central Asia took even harder hits. The agricultural infrastructure of southern Iraq — the canal system that had supported civilization on the lower Tigris and Euphrates for thousands of years (Mesopotamia narrative) — was destroyed in the 1250s and never fully repaired in the medieval period.

The demographic recovery is a longer and stranger story. By the sixteenth century, populations in most affected regions had recovered. But the political and economic balance had shifted. China under the Ming was wealthier per capita than China under the late Song had been, partly because the Mongol period had cleared away accumulated population pressure, in the way that the Black Death is sometimes argued to have done in Europe. Persia after the Timurids was more politically centralized than Persia before the Mongols had been. The medieval social structures of the Russian principalities, the Anatolian Seljuks, the Khwarezmian sultanates — none of these survived the Mongol period intact, and what replaced them was usually more centralized, more bureaucratic, and more militarily organized than what came before.

The genetic legacy is harder to quantify but real. A famous 2003 study identified a Y-chromosome lineage that appears in roughly 8% of men across a region from northeastern China to Uzbekistan, which the authors hypothesized originated with Genghis Khan and his close male relatives during the empire-building period. The hypothesis has been criticized — the Y-chromosome could trace to many possible founders in the relevant period — but the underlying point is solid: the Mongol elite had access to an enormous reproductive base across a vast geographic area, and the genetic effects of empire are still measurable in modern populations. Around sixteen million men today are estimated to share this Y-chromosome lineage. That is a remarkable number. It is also a reminder of what the conquests actually entailed at the personal scale.

---

The political legacies were the deepest and most extended.

In **Russia**, the Mongol period (1237 to roughly 1480, depending on which event you take as the end) reshaped the political geography permanently. The Russian state that emerged from the long shadow of the Golden Horde was different from the Kievan Rus' that had preceded it: more autocratic, more centralized, more focused on Moscow as the dynastic center, and more militarized along its eastern frontier. Russian state-building borrowed Mongol administrative practices, including the census-based taxation system and the use of post-relay messengers. The Russian word for treasury (*kazna*), customs (*tamozhnya*), and money (*dengi*) are all of Turkic-Mongol origin. The Tsar's title — beginning with **Ivan III** in the late fifteenth century — was claimed in conscious imitation of the Caesars of Byzantium, but the Russian state's actual administrative practice owed at least as much to Sarai as to Constantinople. When Russian armies eventually destroyed the last Mongol successor states in the seventeenth century, they did so with state machinery substantially borrowed from those same Mongol successor states.

Russian historical memory of the Mongol period — the "Tatar Yoke" — is uneven. For several centuries it was a foundational national grievance: the Mongols had broken Rus' and forced Russia into a long defensive crouch from which it had to fight free. Modern Russian historiography has nuanced this. The Yoke really did slow Russian economic development and did really centralize power in undemocratic ways. But the Yoke also made Russia what it is, and the modern Russian state's geographic shape — the extension into Siberia, the Volga, the steppes — runs along corridors first integrated by the Golden Horde.

In **China**, the Yuan period left a more institutional inheritance than is sometimes acknowledged. The Ming, despite its anti-Mongol founding rhetoric, kept much of the Yuan administrative structure: the provincial system, the postal Yam (renamed but functionally identical), the Grand Canal extension, the integration of southern and northern tax bases. The Ming inherited a unified China that had been politically divided for three centuries before the Yuan, and the unification was a Mongol accomplishment that the Ming did not undo. The next great Chinese dynasty — the **Qing**, founded by the Manchus in 1644 — was itself a foreign-conquest dynasty that explicitly modeled its rule on Yuan precedents, including dual Mongol-Chinese administrative structures and the patronage of Tibetan Buddhism for political legitimacy with the Mongols. The Qing emperors wrote in Manchu, Mongolian, Tibetan, Chinese, and Uyghur on their imperial seals — the multi-script practice that Kublai had attempted with 'Phags-pa, finally implemented six centuries later by a different conquest dynasty drawing on the Mongol model.

In the **Islamic world**, the Mongol legacy was paradoxical. The Mongols had destroyed the Abbasid caliphate, killed the caliph, sacked Baghdad, and ended the political coherence of the Sunni heartland. They had also produced, within two generations, the most brilliant period of Persian culture in Islamic history. The **Timurid dynasty** of the late fourteenth and fifteenth centuries — founded by Timur, who claimed Mongol descent through the Barlas tribe and ruled in the name of Chagatai puppet khans — built capitals at Samarkand and Herat that became the centers of a Persian-Islamic Renaissance. **Ulugh Beg's observatory** at Samarkand in the 1420s extended the Maragheh tradition. The miniature painting of the **Herat school** under the patronage of Timurid princes produced the visual idiom that would become the standard for Persian, Ottoman, and Mughal manuscript art. The architectural style of the **Bibi-Khanym Mosque** and the **Gur-i Amir** mausoleum at Samarkand was the direct ancestor of the Mughal style of seventeenth-century India.

The most direct continuation of the Mongol political tradition into the modern period was the **Mughal Empire** of India, founded in 1526 by **Babur** — a great-great-great-great-grandson of Timur on his father's side and a fourteenth-generation descendant of Genghis Khan on his mother's side. The dynastic name "Mughal" is, etymologically, just the Persian-Indian pronunciation of "Mongol." The Mughals ruled most of the Indian subcontinent from 1526 to roughly 1707 (formal succession lasted to 1857, but real power evaporated in the early eighteenth century). Their administrative system was Persian-Mongol hybrid; their military was Mongol-style cavalry adapted to Indian conditions; their court culture was Persian-Mongol with strong Hindu absorption. The Taj Mahal, built by **Shah Jahan** in the 1630s and 1640s, is the great architectural product of the Mughal-Mongol tradition: a Persian-Timurid double-shell dome (the lineage runs through Sultaniyya, Chapter 7) on a scale that no earlier Mongol-tradition building had attempted, with **char-bagh** Persian gardens on the Mongol model around it. The Taj is a Mongol building, four centuries removed from Genghis Khan and through six dynastic transformations, but recognizably continuous.

---

The cultural legacy was equally diffuse and equally real.

**Gunpowder** — the Chinese invention that the Mongols transmitted west during the campaigns of the 1240s and 1250s — would, within two centuries of arriving in Europe, transform European warfare and create the military foundations of the early modern state. The transmission would not have happened, at the speed it happened, without the Mongol conquest of the Khwarezmian region and the Mongol military's habit of integrating Chinese and Persian engineers across continents. European cannons and arquebuses of the fifteenth century are descendants, several technological generations removed, of Yuan fire-lances at the Battle of Yamen.

**Paper** had been moving along the Silk Road since the Tang dynasty, but the Mongol period accelerated paper transmission westward. The first European paper mills were built in the late thirteenth century, in Italy and Spain, and the technology had been transmitted through Islamic intermediaries who had themselves received it through Mongol-era contacts. Within two centuries, paper would be the medium for the European printing revolution.

**Astronomical knowledge** — the corrected Ptolemaic models from al-Tusi at Maragheh — would feed forward through Byzantine Greek-language transmission into the Renaissance, where they would surface in Copernicus's *De Revolutionibus* (1543) and contribute to the scientific revolution. The Mongol Empire connected the Persian and Chinese astronomical traditions for the first time at sustained scale; both traditions were transformed by the contact.

**Cuisine** moved with the empire in directions that are still visible today. The Persian-style dumpling tradition — from which descended the modern manti, dim sum, and pierogi — was refined and standardized under Yuan rule. Cuban-style sweet noodles in Persian cuisine derive from Mongol-era contact with Chinese noodle traditions. The flatbread cultures of Central Asia and the meat-and-rice traditions of the modern Middle East both bear the marks of Mongol logistical transmission. Less prestigiously: the **buuz** and **manti** that exist today as Mongolian and Turkic dumplings derive from steamed dumpling traditions the Mongols absorbed from Chinese cooking and carried west.

---

What was destroyed forever and what was built that lasted are different questions, and the honest answer is uncomfortable.

What was destroyed forever: the Abbasid caliphate, in its institutional sense, was not rebuilt. The agricultural infrastructure of southern Iraq did not recover its pre-Mongol productivity in the medieval period. The Khwarezmian Empire is gone — the cities of Urgench, Bukhara, Samarkand survived in various forms, but the integrated polity is long destroyed. The library of the House of Wisdom is lost, and we will never know exactly what was in it. Some specific cultural traditions — the lost compositions of Khwarezmian poets, the lost theological commentaries of the Bukhara madrasas, the lost manuscripts of Alamut and Baghdad — are not recoverable.

What was built that lasted: the political integration of China. The Persian-Islamic synthesis that produced the medieval and early modern Iranian world. The Russian state, in a form derived substantially from the Golden Horde administrative tradition. The Mughal Empire of India, with its enormous and lasting cultural products. The trade routes between Europe and East Asia, which would be reactivated in the early modern period and would never be fully broken again. The intellectual transmission of astronomy, mathematics, gunpowder, paper, and printing in the directions and at the speeds that produced both the European Renaissance and the late medieval Islamic and Chinese golden ages.

---

This is the awkward summary. The Mongol Empire was one of the most destructive things that ever happened to Eurasia. It killed more people in absolute terms than any pre-modern political event for which we have records. It permanently ended civilizations and institutions that had taken centuries to build. It did so with explicit doctrinal intent — terror was a strategy, not an accident — and it did not particularly care about the lost knowledge or the lost lives.

It was also one of the most consequential constructive things that ever happened to Eurasia. It physically connected civilizations that had been functionally separate for thousands of years. It made the routine transmission of ideas, technologies, and goods across the continent possible at a speed and scale that nothing before had matched. It reset the relationship between the steppe and the agricultural civilizations along its borders in ways that ended one of history's most persistent military stalemates. It produced political and cultural products — the Yuan unification of China, the Ilkhanate Renaissance, the Timurid efflorescence, the Mughal Empire, the modern Russian state — that shaped the world in which the early modern global system would emerge.

Both things are true. Neither cancels the other. The right way to read the Mongol Empire is to keep both in mind at the same time and refuse to settle on a single moral verdict, because no honest single verdict is available.

A small final image. In 1206, the year of the foundational kurultai, Genghis Khan was a forty-four-year-old chieftain who had unified a collection of tribal confederations that had never been unified. The total population under his command was roughly a million people, about the population of a single mid-sized medieval city. The largest empire he could plausibly have imagined — the Khwarezmian, the Jin, the Song — would not have been thought of as conquerable from the Mongolian plateau. Within his lifetime he conquered two of those three. Within fifty years of his death, his grandsons had conquered the third, plus most of the territories the first two had controlled, plus most of the steppe west of Mongolia, plus the Russian principalities, plus Persia, plus the Caucasus, plus parts of Europe.

There is no other comparable expansion in recorded history. The Roman Empire took five hundred years to build. The Persian Empire under Cyrus, the closest analogue in scale, took thirty years and was about a third as large. The British Empire of the modern period took two and a half centuries. The Mongols built the largest contiguous land empire in history in fifty years, governed it as a unified state for twenty more, and continued to govern its successor pieces for another two centuries.

Then it was over, and the survivors went back to writing the history of what had happened to them. They wrote in Persian, and Chinese, and Arabic, and Russian, and Latin, and Italian, and Mongolian (in the Uyghur script that the Mongols had captured at Chakirmaut in 1204). The Mongols had not written much of it themselves — the Secret History was nearly the only authorized narrative — but they had created the conditions in which all the other civilizations could finally write about each other.

The empire was the bridge. Most of what crossed the bridge was terrible. Some of it was magnificent. All of it would shape the world that came next.

Cross-civ final check: the end of the Yuan in 1368 is roughly contemporary with the **Hundred Years' War** in western Europe (1337–1453), the **Trecento** in Italy that produced Petrarch and Boccaccio, the early **Mali Empire** under Mansa Musa's successors (Mansa Musa's famous pilgrimage was 1324, near the height of the Pax Mongolica's last years), the **Aztec consolidation** in central Mexico (Tenochtitlan founded 1325), and the late **Khmer Empire** in Cambodia. The Mongol period had transformed the relationships among most of the Eurasian civilizations that the reader has encountered earlier in this app — the Persian Empire (whose Sasanian afterlife the Ilkhans completed, Persian Empire Chapter 10), the Han Dynasty's Chinese imperial tradition (which the Yuan inherited and the Ming reclaimed, Han Dynasty narrative), the steppe traditions of the Scythians and Xiongnu (which the Mongols brought to their largest scale), the Indus Valley successor cultures of the Indian subcontinent (which the Mongol-descended Mughals would eventually rule). The Mongol Empire is the only civilization in this app that touches almost every other one. Its echo, four centuries after Toghon Temür's flight from Khanbaliq, was still shaping the world.

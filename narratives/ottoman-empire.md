# The Ottoman Empire — Six Centuries on the Bridge

**Goal:** Narrative for the Anatolian Succession chain, picking up where Byzantine Empire ends. Covers 1299 (Osman I's traditional founding) through 1922 (the abolition of the sultanate) — 623 years, the second-longest single political continuity in this app, behind only Byzantium itself. The through-line: the Ottoman Empire was the institution that inherited the Eastern Mediterranean from Byzantium and ran it for six centuries by being almost everything its predecessor had been — multi-ethnic, multi-confessional, ceremonially ferocious, administratively patient — translated into Turkish, Persian, and Arabic, with the cross replaced by the crescent and Hagia Sophia kept standing through every transition. They built a slave-soldier corps that ruled the army for three centuries, conquered the Balkans and most of the Arab world, took Constantinople with the largest cannons ever cast, codified law for three continents, and produced the architectural climax of Hagia Sophia's tradition. Then they spent the last three hundred years losing it all in slow motion. The empire ended in 1922 in a railway carriage. Modern Turkey, modern Greece, modern Israel, modern Iraq, modern Bosnia, and a long list of other modern states are still working out what the Ottomans left them.

**Status:** First draft, 12 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — The Frontier Beylik

The Ottoman Empire begins, by tradition, with a man, a dream, and a small flat-roofed town in northwestern Anatolia.

The man was **Osman I** (oss-MAHN, c. 1258–1326), the chieftain of a small Turkic warrior band on the western frontier of the collapsing Sultanate of Rum. The dream came to him while sleeping at the house of his future father-in-law, the Sufi shaykh **Edebali** (eh-deh-bah-LEE): a vision of a tree growing from his chest until its branches covered the world. The town was **Söğüt** (suh-OOT, "the willow"), a frontier settlement in the foothills of the Bithynian highlands, about ninety miles southeast of where Constantinople still flickered as the wounded capital of the dying Roman Empire. The dream is a thirteenth-generation Ottoman court legend recorded centuries after the event by writers who already knew how the story ended, and it is almost certainly a piece of dynastic propaganda. The town is real. The man is real, in the sense that he existed and was the ancestor of every Ottoman sultan who followed, but almost nothing about Osman's life can be reconstructed from contemporary sources. Even the date of his death is uncertain.

What is not uncertain is that during Osman's adult lifetime, the political order of Anatolia broke into pieces, and that he was one of the dozen or so warlords who happened to be in the right place when it did.

---

The right place was the Bithynian frontier — the wedge of northwestern Anatolia where the Sea of Marmara, the Black Sea, and the inner plateau meet. The frontier had been a contested zone for centuries. The Byzantine Empire had ruled it from Constantinople for nine hundred years (Byzantine Empire Ch 1–10). The **Seljuk Sultanate of Rum** (a Turkic dynasty that took its name because it ruled territory whose people were *Romaioi* — Romans — Byzantine Empire Ch 7) had taken most of the Anatolian interior after the Battle of Manzikert in 1071, leaving the empire with only a strip of coastal cities. By the late thirteenth century both sides were exhausted, and a third party had broken both of them: the **Mongols**.

The Mongol Ilkhanate (the Persia-based khanate of the four Mongol successor states, Mongol Empire Ch 7) had crushed the Seljuks of Rum at the **Battle of Köse Dağ** (KUH-seh DAH, "Bald Mountain") in 1243, reducing the sultanate to a tribute-paying vassal that decayed steadily for the next half-century. By Osman's youth the Seljuk sultans were Mongol puppets; by his old age the sultanate had ceased to function in any meaningful sense. What replaced it across Anatolia was a patchwork of perhaps two dozen **beyliks** (BAY-liks, "principalities," from Turkish *bey*, "lord") — small Turkic warrior states under chieftains who had been Seljuk frontier commanders or who had taken advantage of the chaos to set up shop on their own. Each beylik controlled a few towns and a stretch of grazing land. Each was theoretically Muslim and Turkic. None controlled enough territory to call itself an empire. Most have been forgotten by everyone except specialists.

Osman's beylik was one of the smallest and most peripheral. It sat at the western edge of the beylik zone, with the Byzantine fortified towns of Bithynia immediately to the west and the larger **Germiyanid** beylik to the south. Osman's father **Ertuğrul** (er-TOO-rool) had reportedly been granted the Söğüt district by a late Seljuk sultan in exchange for military service against the Byzantines and against rival Turkic chieftains. Ertuğrul died around 1280. Osman, then in his early twenties, inherited what amounted to a few hundred horsemen, a town, and a license to raid westward.

He did so for about forty years.

---

The license to raid westward had a name and an ideology. The name was **gaza** (gah-ZAH, Arabic for "raid" or "expedition"), and in the frontier zone of late thirteenth-century Anatolia it had hardened into a specifically religious concept: an organized armed expedition against a Christian or pagan enemy on behalf of Islam, conducted by frontier warriors known as **ghazis** (GAH-zees). The ghazi tradition was old in the Islamic world — it had been a feature of every Muslim-Christian frontier from al-Andalus to the Caucasus — but on the Anatolian frontier it had a particular sharpness. The Byzantine villages just over the hills were reachable, lightly defended, and rich. The Bithynian fortified cities (Bursa, Nicaea, Nicomedia) were tougher targets but enormously richer if they could be taken. A ghazi leader who could promise plunder, glory, and (theologically) salvation could attract recruits from across Anatolia.

This is the crucial fact about the early Ottoman state. It was, in its first generations, not really a state at all. It was a magnet. Osman and his successors offered something the other Anatolian beyliks did not: an open frontier, an active raiding zone, a chance for any Turkic horseman with a horse and a bow to ride west and become wealthy. Recruits came from across Anatolia. Some were Sunni Muslim warriors looking for gaza. Some were nominally Muslim Turkmen tribesmen who were really still pagan or shamanist and were following the fighting because it paid. Some were defectors from neighboring beyliks. A surprising number, in the first century, were Christian warriors — Greek and Slavic and Albanian fighters who had fallen out with their own lords and joined the Ottoman side as paid auxiliaries. The early Ottomans cared about loyalty, courage, and competence. They did not yet care much about religious purity. The state that emerged from this magnet would look very different a hundred years later, but at the founding moment it was a multi-ethnic, multi-confessional armed company organized around a frontier raiding license.

Osman's Sufi connections mattered for this. The shaykh Edebali, whose daughter Osman married (her name is given variously as Bala Hatun or Mal Hatun in later sources — even his wife's name is contested), was a leader of the **Babai** (bah-BYE-ee) Sufi network, a heterodox dervish movement that had spread across Anatolia in the wake of the Mongol disruption. The Babais were not orthodox urban Sunnis. They were itinerant mystics who moved easily across confessional boundaries, spoke Turkish rather than Arabic or Persian, and had a long history of frontier organizing. Their support gave the early Ottomans religious legitimacy without forcing them into the doctrinal narrowness of the established Islamic schools. Sheikh Edebali was, by all accounts, the political theologian of the Ottoman founding, and his role is one of the better-documented features of an otherwise obscure period.

---

What Osman actually did is harder to recover than what he meant. The contemporary sources are almost entirely Byzantine, and they are not interested in him personally — he appears as one of several Turkic warlords harassing the Bithynian frontier. The Ottoman chronicles that name him as the founder of the dynasty were composed in the fifteenth century, more than a hundred years after his death, and they project the dynastic clarity of the later empire backward onto a chaotic frontier period that did not, at the time, look like the founding of anything in particular.

What can be reconstructed is approximately this. Around 1299 — the traditional foundational date, though it is conventional rather than precisely documented — Osman declared his independence from any nominal Seljuk overlordship, minted a coin in his own name (or had one minted; the surviving examples are problematic), and began calling himself an autonomous bey. By the early 1300s his band had grown large enough to fight pitched battles. In 1302 he won a notable victory over a Byzantine force at the **Battle of Bapheus** (BAH-fee-us, near modern İzmit), the first time his beylik had defeated a regular Byzantine army in the open field. The Byzantine defeat at Bapheus shocked Constantinople and drove the empire to hire the Catalan Company (Byzantine Empire Ch 10) — a desperate move that, as the Byzantine chapter records, made things worse. The Catalans pushed Osman back briefly but could not be controlled and turned on the Byzantines themselves, leaving Osman free to resume his expansion.

By the 1310s the Ottoman beylik controlled most of the Bithynian countryside. The walled cities held out longer. **Bursa** (BUR-sah, the major Byzantine city of Bithynia, called Prusa in Greek), surrounded by Ottoman forces from roughly 1313 onward, finally surrendered in 1326. Osman was already dying by the time the city fell — he reportedly heard the news on his deathbed — and the credit went to his son.

---

The son was **Orhan** (or-HAHN, c. 1281–1362), and he is the figure in the early Ottoman story whose actions can actually be tracked.

Orhan inherited the beylik on Osman's death in 1326 and ran it for thirty-six years. Under his rule, the Ottoman state crossed the threshold from raiding band to organized polity. He took Bursa and made it the dynasty's first proper capital. He minted Ottoman silver coins (**akçes** — AHK-ches — small flat silver pieces that became the standard imperial denomination for the next three centuries) systematically rather than sporadically. He commissioned the first identifiably Ottoman architecture — modest mosques, schools, and bath complexes in Bursa and the surrounding towns, in a style that drew on Seljuk precedents but was beginning to develop its own character. He took the title **Sultan** for himself in some inscriptions, though the usage was inconsistent and the practical meaning uncertain.

Orhan also took the city of **Nicaea** in 1331 — a moment of major symbolic importance. Nicaea (modern İznik) had been the temporary capital of the Byzantine government-in-exile during the Latin occupation of Constantinople from 1204 to 1261 (Byzantine Empire Ch 9). It was the city of the First and Seventh Ecumenical Councils. It was the second city of the Greek-speaking Orthodox world. Its fall to the Ottomans was a blow that Constantinople could not absorb. Orhan made it a center of Ottoman religious learning, founded a madrasa (a Muslim religious school) within the captured walls, and integrated Nicaea's Greek population into the Ottoman administrative system on terms remarkably generous by the standards of the age. Most converted to Islam over the following generations; some did not, and continued to live as Orthodox Christians under Ottoman rule. The pattern that would later be formalized as the **millet system** (Chapter 6) was already in operation in rough form. Orhan also took **Nicomedia** (modern İzmit) in 1337, completing the Ottoman conquest of Bithynia and giving the beylik a coastline on the Sea of Marmara.

The geography mattered. Once the Ottomans held the Marmara coast, they were within sight of Europe. The strait at the Sea of Marmara's western end — the **Dardanelles** (also called the Hellespont, the narrow passage between the Aegean and the Marmara) — separated Anatolia from the European province of Thrace by less than a mile of water at its narrowest point. The European shore was Byzantine territory in name and almost completely undefended in fact. The Ottoman beylik now had the option, if it could find a pretext, of crossing into Europe.

The pretext arrived in 1346.

---

The pretext was a Byzantine civil war. **John Kantakouzenos**, the senior aristocrat who had been regent for the boy emperor John V Palaiologos and had fallen out with the regency council, was fighting a six-year war for control of Constantinople (Byzantine Empire Ch 10). He needed military allies. The Ottomans had cavalry. Kantakouzenos married his daughter **Theodora** to Orhan in 1346 — a Christian Byzantine princess given as a bride to a Muslim Turkic sultan, an arrangement scandalous to traditional Byzantine sensibilities but accepted as the price of military aid. Orhan sent troops across the strait to fight on Kantakouzenos's behalf. They fought, won, and went home.

In 1352 Kantakouzenos called them back for a renewed phase of the civil war. They came again. This time they fought in Thrace and the southern Balkans, and they began to notice that the territory they were riding through was lightly defended, agriculturally rich, and full of Christian peasants who had no particular reason to fight for an emperor in Constantinople they had never seen. In March 1354 a major earthquake destroyed the walls of the Byzantine fortress at **Gallipoli** on the European side of the Dardanelles. Orhan's eldest surviving son **Süleyman Pasha** (soo-LAY-mahn pah-SHAH, not to be confused with the later Süleyman the Magnificent — the Ottomans reused names mercilessly across centuries, and a reader needs to track each generation separately) happened to be conveniently nearby with a force of perhaps ten thousand men. He occupied the ruined fortress, refused to leave when Byzantine envoys arrived demanding the territory back, and began rebuilding the walls under Ottoman control.

This was the turning point. The Ottomans now had a permanent foothold in Europe, and they had it through a combination of opportunism, family alliance, and timely seismic activity. Süleyman Pasha died in a hunting accident in 1357, but the foothold held. Orhan, now seventy-six years old and frail, designated his second son **Murad** as heir and watched Ottoman raids spread across Thrace.

When Orhan died in 1362, the Ottoman beylik had grown from a few hundred horsemen at Söğüt to a state controlling Bithynia, parts of western Anatolia, and an expanding bridgehead in southeastern Europe. It had a capital, a coinage, a developing administrative system, and a religious infrastructure of mosques and madrasas. It was still smaller and less powerful than the largest of the rival Anatolian beyliks (the **Karamanids** of central Anatolia, who claimed direct succession from the Seljuks of Rum, were considered the senior power by most contemporary observers). But it had something the other beyliks did not: an open frontier into Christian Europe and a religious-ideological framework — gaza — for exploiting it.

The Karamanids were doing nothing of the sort. They were squabbling with their Turkic neighbors over towns in central Anatolia. The Ottomans were riding across Thrace toward the Bulgarian border.

---

A note on what makes the early Ottoman story unusual is worth pausing on, because it bears on everything that follows. Most successful conquering states in world history have started from a unified ethnic or religious base and expanded by absorbing other groups. The Ottomans did the opposite. They started as a magnet for everyone willing to ride west, with no particular ethnic or even religious unity, and they only gradually acquired the consolidated Sunni-Muslim-Turkic identity that the later empire would project.

This is documented in the surviving chronicles, sparse as they are. The early Ottoman armies contained Greek Christians, Slavic Christians, Albanian Christians, and Turkic-speakers of every shade of Muslim observance. The early Ottoman administration borrowed heavily from Byzantine fiscal practice — the tax rolls of the conquered Bithynian cities continued to function under Ottoman management, and the Byzantine officials who had been running them were often kept in place. The early Ottoman religious establishment was Sufi-influenced, syncretic, and tolerant of practices that would later be condemned as heretical. The early Ottoman court contained Christian wives, Christian advisors, and Christian soldiers.

What changed this was not Osman or Orhan. What changed it was institutional consolidation under their successors, particularly Murad I — the next chapter's protagonist — and the gradual evolution of the Ottoman state from a frontier raiding band into a centralized military empire with its own theology, its own bureaucracy, and its own slave-soldier corps. By 1450 the Ottoman state would look entirely different from the one Orhan had handed off in 1362. But the founding magnet is part of why the empire grew so fast: it was easier to join than to fight, and the price of joining was low.

Cross-civ check: Osman's lifetime (c. 1258–1326) was the height of the **Pax Mongolica** (Mongol Empire Ch 7), when the four Mongol khanates were maintaining a continental trade system from China to Persia. Marco Polo was traveling to Kublai Khan's court while Osman was raiding Bithynia. The **Yuan dynasty** in China was at its peak. The **Mamluk Sultanate** in Egypt — the slave-soldier state that had repelled the Mongols at Ayn Jalut in 1260 — was the dominant Sunni power of the Mediterranean world; the early Ottomans were a backwater compared to it. The **Ilkhanate** in Persia was about to convert to Islam under Ghazan Khan in 1295. In Europe, **Dante** was writing the *Divine Comedy* during Osman's last years. In England, the **Magna Carta** had been signed forty years earlier. In China, the **Song dynasty** had recently fallen to Kublai's Mongols. The mid-thirteenth and early-fourteenth centuries were one of the more crowded moments in pre-modern history, and the tiny Ottoman beylik on the Anatolian frontier was, at the time, one of the least important things happening in it.

# Chapter 2 — Conquering the Balkans

By 1362, the Ottomans had a foothold in Europe. By 1396, they had most of the Balkans, the Janissary corps, and a reputation that would terrify Christian Europe for the next three centuries. The transformation took thirty-four years, two sultans, and a series of battles that did to the medieval Balkan kingdoms what the Mongols had done to the Sultanate of Rum: broke them faster than they could organize a coherent response.

The agent of most of this was **Murad I** (mu-RAHD, ruled 1362–1389), Orhan's second son, who took the Ottoman throne at around thirty-six and spent twenty-seven years methodically extending it. Murad was the first Ottoman ruler whose actions we can track in detail — Byzantine, Bulgarian, Serbian, Italian, and early Ottoman sources all converge on him, and the picture they produce is consistent. He was a careful, patient strategist with a particular gift for institutional design. The Ottoman state's transformation from raiding beylik to organized empire happened on his watch, and most of the institutional features that would define the empire for the next four centuries were either invented or formalized by him.

---

Murad's first major act was to take **Adrianople** in 1369.

Adrianople (modern Edirne in European Turkey, sometimes spelled Edirne in modern usage and Adrianople in older Western sources) was a Byzantine city of considerable importance. It sat on the Maritsa River in eastern Thrace, about 130 miles northwest of Constantinople, at a strategic crossroads where the main land routes from the Balkans to Constantinople converged. The Roman emperor Hadrian had refounded it in the second century and given it his name. The Visigoths had defeated the Roman emperor Valens there in 378 (Ancient Rome Ch 9), one of the most catastrophic Roman defeats of the late imperial period. The Bulgar Tsar Krum had won a major victory there in 813. The Byzantine Empire had recovered it in the early twelfth century and held it more or less continuously since.

In 1369 Murad's forces took the city, probably without a major siege — the Byzantine garrison was too small to hold against a serious Ottoman investment, and the surrounding Thracian countryside was already under Ottoman control. Murad made Edirne his new capital. This was a significant decision. Bursa remained the dynastic-religious center, where Osman and Orhan were buried and where the great early Ottoman mosques stood. But the working capital — the city where the sultan held court, where the army assembled, where the bureaucracy operated — was now in Europe, not Anatolia. The Ottoman state had crossed psychologically as well as militarily. It was a European empire with Anatolian roots, and it would remain so until 1453.

The choice of Edirne also had a strategic logic. From Edirne, Ottoman armies could march in any direction across the Balkans. Northwest into Bulgaria. North into Wallachia (the principality that became part of modern Romania). West into Macedonia and Serbia. South into Greece. Each campaigning season, the army assembled at Edirne and rode out. Each campaigning season returned with more territory.

---

The Bulgarian campaigns came first. The **Second Bulgarian Empire** — the medieval Bulgarian state that had reformed in 1185 after a long period of Byzantine occupation — had been weakening for a century. It had lost its Black Sea ports to the Genoese. It had been pummeled by Hungarian invasions from the north. By the 1360s it was effectively three competing kingdoms, each ruled by a different Shishman family member, none capable of mounting a coordinated defense. Murad picked them off one at a time. The Tsar of Tarnovo accepted Ottoman vassalage in 1373. The smaller principalities of Vidin and Dobrudzha followed within a few years. By 1380 the Bulgarian state, in any meaningful sense, had ceased to exist. Its full incorporation into the empire would happen under Murad's successor, but the work was already done.

The Serbian campaigns followed. The **Serbian Empire** of Stefan Dušan (Byzantine Empire Ch 10) had been the dominant Balkan power in the 1340s, but Dušan had died young in 1355, and his empire had fragmented within a few years into competing Serbian principalities. The largest, ruled by Prince **Lazar Hrebeljanović** (la-ZAR hreh-bell-YAH-no-vich, c. 1329–1389), controlled what is now central Serbia. The others were already paying tribute to Murad by the early 1380s. Lazar tried to organize a Serbian-led coalition against the advancing Ottomans, drawing in Bosnian, Albanian, and minor Bulgarian forces.

The decisive battle came on June 15, 1389, on the **Field of Blackbirds** (*Kosovo Polje* in Serbian, KAW-soh-voh PAW-lyeh, in what is now southern Kosovo).

The **Battle of Kosovo** is one of those events that has been so heavily mythologized that recovering the actual military engagement is almost impossible. The contemporary sources — Serbian, Bulgarian, Bosnian, Italian, and Ottoman — agree on a few facts and disagree on most others. What can be reconstructed: the Ottoman and Serbian-led armies were comparable in size, perhaps thirty thousand each; the battle was fought through the day of June 15; both Murad I and Prince Lazar were killed; the Ottoman side eventually held the field, but the casualties on both sides were so severe that it is contested whether the battle should be classed as a tactical Ottoman victory or a draw.

The Serbian tradition holds that Murad was killed by a Serbian noble named **Miloš Obilić** (MEE-losh oh-BEE-lich), who feigned defection, was admitted to the Ottoman camp on the eve of battle, and stabbed Murad to death in his tent before being cut down by guards. The Ottoman tradition holds that Murad was killed during or after the battle by a wounded Serbian found among the bodies. Several versions of each story circulate. The political fact, regardless of the specific mechanism, is that the Ottoman sultan was killed at the close of an engagement that was more costly than expected, and that the Ottoman command structure had to be improvised on the field.

The improvisation worked. Murad's elder son **Bayezid** (BYE-eh-zeed) was present at the battle. Within hours of his father's death, Bayezid had his younger brother **Yakub** strangled — eliminating the rival claimant to the throne and consolidating his own succession before the surviving Ottoman commanders could organize an alternative. The Ottoman army was held together. The Serbian survivors, leaderless after Lazar's death, fragmented. Lazar's widow Princess Milica accepted Ottoman vassalage on her dead husband's behalf and married her daughter to the new Ottoman sultan as part of the settlement. Serbia became a tribute-paying Ottoman vassal, contributing troops to subsequent Ottoman campaigns. The Battle of Kosovo did not destroy Serbia in 1389. But it broke any prospect of Serbian-led resistance to Ottoman expansion, and it began a cultural memory that would, six centuries later, still be operating in Balkan politics.

The Serbian Orthodox tradition canonized Lazar as a martyr. The Battle of Kosovo became the founding myth of Serbian national consciousness — every nineteenth-century Serbian nationalist text, every twentieth-century Serbian political speech invoking the suffering of the nation, every twenty-first-century rally about Kosovo, traces back to that single afternoon in 1389. The historical reality was murkier. The mythological reality is one of the most consequential facts in modern Balkan politics.

---

The new sultan was **Bayezid I** (1389–1402), and he was a different kind of Ottoman from his father.

Murad I had been patient. Bayezid was not. He was nicknamed **Yıldırım** (YUHL-duh-ruhm, "the Thunderbolt"), reportedly for the speed at which he moved his armies — Ottoman forces under Bayezid would appear in front of a fortified city days before the defending commander knew he was on his way. He was personally brave, occasionally reckless, and possessed of a strategic ambition his father had carefully avoided. Murad had built an empire in Anatolia and the Balkans by careful pressure on weaker neighbors. Bayezid wanted to take Constantinople, conquer the rest of the Balkans, dominate Anatolia outright, and project Ottoman power as far as Hungary and the Black Sea.

In the eight years between 1389 and 1396, he came close to doing it. He blockaded Constantinople from 1394 onward, building a fortress on the Asian side of the Bosporus (the **Anadoluhisarı**, "Anatolian Castle") to control the strait. He campaigned aggressively in Anatolia, conquering several rival beyliks (the Karamanids, Aydinids, and Saruhanids) and absorbing their territories — moves that doubled the empire's Anatolian holdings but offended the Turkic religious establishment, which considered war between Muslim states unseemly. He pushed the Balkan frontier north to the Danube, putting pressure on Hungary. And in 1396 he provoked, and crushed, the largest Christian crusader army that had been raised against the Ottomans up to that point.

---

The crusade was organized by King **Sigismund of Hungary** (SIG-iss-mund, who would later become Holy Roman Emperor), with substantial French, German, English, Bohemian, and Burgundian contingents. The strategic plan was to march down the Danube to Constantinople, relieving the Byzantine capital and breaking Ottoman power in the Balkans simultaneously. The expedition was widely advertised across Europe as a holy war, attracting volunteer knights from as far as Scotland. By summer 1396 the crusader army assembled — the Christian sources put it at over fifty thousand men, which is probably an exaggeration; the modern estimates run from twenty to thirty thousand, still the largest western European force to operate in the Balkans for a generation.

The crusaders besieged the Ottoman frontier fortress of **Nicopolis** (nih-KOH-poh-liss, on the Danube in modern Bulgaria) in September 1396. Bayezid, who had been blockading Constantinople, raced north with about fifteen thousand Ottoman regulars and a substantial Serbian vassal contingent under Stefan Lazarević (Prince Lazar's son). The two armies met on September 25. The crusader cavalry charge, led by the French and Burgundian knights, hit the first Ottoman line and overran it. The knights then continued forward into terrain Bayezid had specifically chosen for the engagement — a slope studded with sharpened stakes that disabled cavalry — and were met by the Ottoman reserve, including the Serbian heavy cavalry. The Christian knights, heavily armored and now without horses, were systematically slaughtered.

The **Battle of Nicopolis** (September 25, 1396) was the worst Christian defeat against the Ottomans since Manzikert. Sigismund escaped down the Danube. The French and Burgundian commanders were captured. Bayezid had several thousand prisoners executed in cold blood the next day, in a deliberately staged spectacle witnessed by the surviving captive commanders — the message being that western European nobles who took up arms against the Ottoman state would not be ransomed under any conventional code of medieval chivalric warfare. The high-ranking French prisoners (including **John the Fearless** of Burgundy) were eventually ransomed for enormous sums, but the scale of the executions among lower-ranking knights ended any prospect of further large-scale European intervention for a generation. After Nicopolis, no major western crusade was organized against the Ottomans for nearly fifty years.

Bayezid returned to Anatolia, resumed the blockade of Constantinople, and began making preparations for what was supposed to be the conclusive Ottoman siege.

---

He never got to launch it. While he was preparing to take Constantinople, a different conqueror was preparing to take him.

Before that story (Chapter 3), one more institutional development from Murad's and Bayezid's reigns deserves explicit attention, because it would shape the empire for the next four centuries.

The **Janissary Corps** (yan-iss-AIR-eez, from Turkish *yeni çeri*, "new troops") was created during Murad I's reign, probably around 1363. The Ottomans had already been recruiting non-Turkic soldiers — primarily Greek and Slavic prisoners of war — and converting them to Islam to serve as imperial troops. What Murad did was systematize the practice and turn it into a permanent corps with its own organization, training, pay structure, and esprit de corps. The earliest Janissaries were Christian boys captured in raids or surrendered as tribute by conquered Christian populations, converted to Islam, and trained for military service. The corps was small at first, a few thousand men. It would grow to be the elite infantry of the empire, the institution that took Constantinople in 1453, the force that brought down the Mamluk Sultanate in 1517, the unit that made the Ottoman army a different kind of fighting force from any of its rivals.

The recruitment system that fed the Janissary corps had its own name: the **devshirme** (DEV-shir-meh, "collection"), and it was formalized under Murad I as a regular tribute imposed on the empire's Balkan Christian subjects. Boys aged eight to eighteen were collected from Christian villages, taken to Edirne (and later Istanbul), converted to Islam, given Turkish names, and sorted into educational tracks based on aptitude. The most able became palace administrators or eventually grand viziers. The rest became soldiers. The full mechanics of the system are detailed in Chapter 6, where they fit the larger institutional picture better. What needs noting here is that the institution that would dominate the Ottoman army through the empire's classical age was already in place by the time Bayezid was preparing to take Constantinople, and it was being staffed by the children of the empire's Balkan Christian subjects.

By 1400 the empire had absorbed most of the Balkans, controlled most of Anatolia, was blockading Constantinople, had a slave-soldier corps with no European parallel, and looked unstoppable.

It was about to be stopped.

Cross-civ check: at the moment of Nicopolis (1396), the **Ming dynasty** was in its first generation under the Hongwu Emperor (Mongol Empire Ch 8); the **Mali Empire** in West Africa was past its peak under Mansa Musa's distant successors; the **Aztec Triple Alliance** in central Mexico was just consolidating, with **Tenochtitlan** founded only seventy years earlier; **Timur** was finishing his second great campaign through Persia and India; **Korea** was about to enter the **Joseon dynasty** in 1392; and the **Hundred Years' War** between England and France was in a relatively quiet phase between Crecy and Agincourt. The Ottoman Empire of Bayezid I was, by 1400, one of the largest and most aggressive of the world's expanding powers. Within two years it would briefly cease to exist.

---

# Chapter 3 — The Tamerlane Catastrophe

The conqueror who stopped Bayezid was **Timur** (TEE-moor, c. 1336–1405), known in Europe as **Tamerlane** (a corruption of *Timur-i Lang*, "Timur the Lame," a nickname referring to a permanent battlefield injury). He was, by any reasonable measure, the most consequential warlord of the late fourteenth century, and his sudden interest in Anatolia in 1402 was the worst thing that could have happened to the Ottoman state.

Timur's career is told in detail in the Mongol narrative (Mongol Empire Ch 9), so the summary here is brief. He was a Turkic-speaking warrior from the Barlas tribe of Transoxiana (the region between the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers in modern Uzbekistan), who claimed Mongol descent through a Genghisid maternal line and ruled in the name of puppet Chagatai khans. From his capital at **Samarkand**, he had spent the previous thirty years rebuilding a Mongol-style empire across the Iranian world — conquering Persia, the Caucasus, much of Mesopotamia, and parts of northern India. By 1400 he ruled the largest single state in Eurasia outside the Ming dynasty. He was sixty-four years old and not slowing down.

What brought him to Anatolia was a combination of refugees and arrogance. Several Anatolian beyliks that Bayezid had absorbed had sent their displaced rulers to Timur's court, asking for restoration. The most consequential was **Sultan Ahmad Jalayir** of Baghdad, whose Jalayirid dynasty Timur had crushed and who had fled to Bayezid for refuge — exactly the reverse of the displaced Anatolian rulers' pattern. Timur and Bayezid exchanged a series of increasingly hostile letters in 1399 and 1400. Each accused the other of harboring his enemies. Bayezid's letters were particularly aggressive, addressing Timur with deliberately insulting language. The exchange has the quality of two confident conquerors stress-testing each other — but Timur was the more experienced commander, and Bayezid had not understood what he was provoking.

In 1400 Timur sacked **Sivas** in central Anatolia, in a brutal demonstration of what he was capable of. Bayezid's young son Ertugrul was reportedly among the casualties. In 1401 Timur sacked Damascus and Baghdad, eliminating his other strategic concerns. In summer 1402 he marched west into Anatolia at the head of an army that contemporary chronicles put at over a hundred thousand men — likely an exaggeration, but the force was certainly larger than anything Bayezid could put in the field, and it included the heavy battlefield component that had made Timur's army the most feared on the continent: war elephants, brought from his Indian campaigns.

Bayezid lifted the blockade of Constantinople — the city would have fallen within a year had he kept it up — and rode east to meet Timur on his own ground. The Ottoman army was a smaller, more regionally specialized force: perhaps 70,000 men, including the Janissary infantry, the Anatolian and Rumelian feudal cavalry, and a contingent of his Serbian vassal Stefan Lazarević. The Ottoman army's strength was its discipline and the quality of its individual units; its weakness was that several of its components were ethnically and politically unreliable in the specific situation now unfolding.

---

The two armies met on July 28, 1402, on the **Çubuk Plain** outside **Ankara** (modern Turkey's capital, then a regional Anatolian city) in central Anatolia. Bayezid had marched his army hard for several days through a hot, dry late-July landscape, and his troops arrived already exhausted. Timur had reached the field first, taken up a defensive position near a freshwater stream, and forced Bayezid to fight without water in midsummer heat. The first significant Ottoman tactical disaster was thirst.

The second was defection. The Anatolian feudal cavalry contingents that Bayezid had absorbed by force when he conquered the rival beyliks contained large numbers of soldiers who recognized the deposed beys riding in Timur's army. Some of these troops switched sides during the battle, going over to Timur's wing. The Ottoman left flank collapsed. The Janissary infantry held their ground in the center — they had nowhere to defect to, and they fought with the discipline that would become their hallmark for the next two centuries — but they were now isolated. The Serbian vassal contingent on the right under Stefan Lazarević also fought hard. The center and the right could not save the day.

By late afternoon the Ottoman army was disintegrating. Bayezid himself, with a small bodyguard and his sons, fought through the wreckage trying to escape. He was overtaken, knocked from his horse, and captured alive. The **Battle of Ankara** (July 28, 1402) was the most catastrophic Ottoman defeat in the empire's six-century history. The Ottoman army was destroyed. Most of Bayezid's sons escaped, but they were now scattered across Anatolia and the Balkans without a coordinating central authority. The Ottoman state, as a unified polity, ceased to exist for the next eleven years.

What Timur did with Bayezid is one of the most-discussed episodes in late medieval history, partly because the contemporary sources contradict each other and partly because later European writers added so many embellishments that recovering the original treatment is hard. The Ottoman tradition, written under Bayezid's restored descendants, holds that Timur kept Bayezid in a golden cage paraded behind his army as a humiliation, and that Bayezid eventually died of grief and despair within months of his capture. The Timurid sources are gentler — they describe Bayezid as treated honorably and dying of natural causes the following March, perhaps from a stroke. The European tradition, working from secondhand reports, picked the most colorful version: Bayezid in a cage, used as Timur's footstool, his wife Olivera (a Serbian princess, Stefan Lazarević's sister) forced to serve naked at Timur's banquets. Most modern historians consider all three details — the cage, the footstool, and the Olivera humiliation — to belong to the same category of European literary embellishment, surfacing in late-medieval and early-modern Christian sources writing about a triumphant Muslim warlord and a fallen Christian-married Muslim sultan, rather than in the contemporary Timurid or Ottoman record. What is well established: Bayezid died in Timur's custody on March 8, 1403, less than eight months after his capture; he was approximately fifty-four years old; he had been the unstoppable conqueror of the Christian Balkans, and he had ended his life as a captive of a Turkic warlord he had never expected to face.

Timur did not stay in Anatolia. He had no interest in administering the territory or replacing the Ottoman dynasty. He installed his preferred candidates among Bayezid's sons as nominal vassals, restored several of the beyliks that Bayezid had absorbed (the Karamanids, in particular, recovered their independence and would be a thorn in Ottoman flesh for the next eighty years), and then withdrew his army eastward in late 1402. He died in February 1405 while preparing an invasion of Ming China, leaving a Timurid succession war that consumed his own empire for the next several decades and ended his territorial unity.

What Timur left behind in Anatolia was a power vacuum that the Ottomans had to climb back out of, with no help from anyone, while every neighboring power — Christian and Muslim — tried to take advantage of their weakness.

---

The eleven years between 1402 and 1413 are known in Ottoman historiography as the **Interregnum** (Turkish *Fetret Devri*, the "Era of Discord").

Bayezid's surviving sons fought a four-cornered civil war for the throne. The four main contenders were:

**Süleyman Çelebi** (CHEH-leh-bee, an honorific roughly equivalent to "lord" or "noble"), Bayezid's eldest surviving son, based in Edirne and controlling the Balkan provinces. Süleyman had escaped from the Battle of Ankara and made his way to Europe, where the Janissary corps and most of the Balkan vassals supported him. He was a competent but heavy-drinking ruler, and his court became known for its corruption.

**Isa Çelebi**, based in Bursa and controlling the Bithynian heartland. He fought a brief war against his brother **Mehmed**, lost, and was eventually eliminated.

**Mehmed Çelebi**, based in Amasya in north-central Anatolia, controlling the Black Sea-facing provinces and the Anatolian feudal cavalry. He was the youngest of the major contenders, the most patient, and the eventual winner.

**Musa Çelebi**, who switched sides repeatedly, allied with Wallachian and Byzantine forces, and at one point briefly held Edirne. He had a sharper military aptitude than his brothers but was politically less adept.

The fighting was intricate, multi-front, and protracted. Süleyman ruled the Balkans from 1402 to 1411, when he was overthrown by Musa with Wallachian backing. Musa held Edirne briefly in 1411–1413, alienating the Janissaries with high-handed treatment. In summer 1413 Mehmed crossed from Anatolia into Europe, allied with the Janissary corps and the senior Ottoman commanders Süleyman had alienated, defeated Musa at the **Battle of Çamurlu** in central Bulgaria on July 5, 1413, and reunited the empire under his own rule. Musa was strangled with a bowstring after the battle.

Mehmed I (ruled 1413–1421) then spent eight years cleaning up the wreckage: restoring central authority over the Anatolian feudal lords who had reasserted independence during the Interregnum, putting down a series of rebellions among Sufi networks (the most famous being the **Sheikh Bedreddin** uprising of 1416, a syncretic religious-social movement that combined heterodox Sufism with peasant grievances against the Ottoman fiscal system; Bedreddin was hanged at Serres in 1416), restoring tribute relationships with the Balkan vassal states, and rebuilding the army and treasury.

The Ottoman state Mehmed I left to his son in 1421 was smaller than Bayezid's empire had been in 1400 but functional. It had survived the worst near-death experience any Islamic state had endured since the Mongol conquest of Baghdad in 1258. It still controlled most of the Balkans, most of western Anatolia, and the strategic Bosporus-Marmara region. Constantinople was once again a Byzantine vassal under Ottoman pressure. The recovery had been a near-run thing. It would not have happened without Mehmed I's patient consolidation, and the Ottomans of the next generation would speak of him as the second founder of the dynasty.

Cross-civ check: while the Ottoman Interregnum was tearing the empire apart, the **Ming dynasty** in China was completing its consolidation and dispatching the great fleets of **Admiral Zheng He** to the Indian Ocean (1405 onward — the first Zheng He voyage left the Yangtze the same year Timur died). The **Council of Constance** (1414–1418) was meeting in central Europe to resolve the **Western Schism** of competing popes. The **Hussite Wars** were beginning in Bohemia. **Joan of Arc** would be born in 1412, the year Musa took Edirne. The **Inca Empire** was beginning its expansion under Pachacuti's father in the central Andes. And the **Aztec Triple Alliance** was about to launch its major imperial expansion under the first Moctezuma. The early fifteenth century was a period of consolidation across Eurasia after the disruption of the late fourteenth, and the Ottoman recovery from Ankara was one of the more dramatic stories of the moment.

---

Mehmed I died in May 1421, possibly from a stroke, possibly from a chronic illness contracted during the Bedreddin campaign. He was succeeded by his seventeen-year-old son **Murad II** (ruled 1421–1444 and 1446–1451 — he abdicated once and returned), who would carry the empire from recovered backwater to the brink of its greatest conquest.

Murad II was, by Ottoman dynastic standards, an unusual figure. He was thoughtful, given to introspection, twice tried to retire to a Sufi lodge in Manisa to spend his life in religious contemplation, and twice was pulled back to the throne by court factions and military emergencies. He was also a very competent battlefield commander when he had to be, and his reign saw the first serious attempts to take Constantinople since the Interregnum — the failed siege of 1422, in which the Theodosian Walls held against Ottoman cannon for the first but not the last time.

The 1422 siege was significant for what it foreshadowed. Murad II's army included the first real **Ottoman gunpowder artillery** — bombards capable of throwing stone balls weighing several hundred pounds at the city walls. The technology was still primitive. The cannons fired slowly, were prone to bursting, and could not be aimed precisely. The Theodosian Walls absorbed the bombardment and held. But the principle had been demonstrated: gunpowder weapons could, in theory, breach masonry walls that no previous siege technology had been able to crack. The Ottomans took the lesson seriously. Over the next thirty years, they would invest steadily in artillery development, hire the best European gun-founders they could find, and build the largest cannon park in the world. By 1453, Mehmed II would have the artillery park his grandfather had begun developing.

Murad II also fought the last major medieval crusade. In 1444, encouraged by a Hungarian-Polish king named **Władysław III** (vlah-DEE-swahf, also called Vladislaus or Wladyslaw in older sources), a coalition of Hungarian, Polish, Wallachian, and Bulgarian forces marched south, allegedly in violation of a peace treaty Murad had just signed. Murad — who had abdicated earlier that year in favor of his twelve-year-old son and gone back to his Sufi retreat at Manisa — was begged by his viziers to return. He came back. The two armies met at **Varna** on the Black Sea coast of Bulgaria on November 10, 1444. The Christian forces were initially successful; the young king Władysław led a personal cavalry charge that nearly reached Murad's tent. Then the charge was caught by the Janissaries, Władysław was killed, his head was put on a spear and paraded around the battlefield, and the crusader army disintegrated. The **Battle of Varna** ended any further crusader hopes in the Balkans, eliminated the most credible Christian field army the Ottomans would face for the next century, and rendered Constantinople's diplomatic isolation complete.

A second Christian coalition under the Hungarian general **John Hunyadi** (HUN-yah-dee) tried again at the **Second Battle of Kosovo** in October 1448. Murad II won that one too, in a three-day engagement that destroyed Hunyadi's reputation and forced Hungary to abandon active resistance to Ottoman expansion in the Balkans. Murad died in February 1451, at the age of forty-six, after a cerebral hemorrhage during a banquet in Edirne. He was succeeded — for the second time — by his now nineteen-year-old son.

The son was **Mehmed II**. The next chapter is his.

---

# Chapter 4 — The Conquest of Constantinople

Mehmed II is one of the most-studied conquerors in medieval history, and the basic outline of his reign needs little embellishment. He took the throne in 1451 at nineteen years old. He took Constantinople in 1453 at twenty-one. He spent the next twenty-eight years remaking the Ottoman state into a world empire, taking the title **Kayser-i Rûm** (KAY-ser-ee ROOM, "Caesar of Rome") and treating the city he had conquered as the natural seat of a renewed Roman imperial tradition, with himself as the Roman emperor's legitimate successor. By the time he died in 1481, the Ottoman Empire was the most powerful state in the Mediterranean basin, and the medieval world that had centered on Constantinople for eleven hundred years had been replaced by something different.

What made him capable of all this is, at least in part, accident of timing — his predecessors had built the army, the institutions, and the artillery park he inherited — but it is also genuine personal capacity. Mehmed was multilingual (Turkish, Arabic, Persian, Greek, and probably some Hebrew and Italian), heavily educated by the standards of any fifteenth-century court, and possessed of an unusual combination of strategic patience and sudden tactical aggression. He was personally capable of designing fortification systems, conducting siege engineering calculations, and discussing Greek philosophy with captured Byzantine scholars. He was also ruthless to a degree that shocked even his Ottoman contemporaries. The fratricide law that the Ottomans formalized under his reign — empowering a sultan upon accession to legally execute his brothers and nephews to prevent succession wars — was, on Mehmed's part, a calmly considered policy decision, not an emotional reflex. He was the first Ottoman ruler whose ambition was global rather than regional, and he was the first Ottoman ruler with the institutional and technological tools to pursue that ambition effectively.

---

The siege of Constantinople, told in detail in the Byzantine narrative (Byzantine Empire Ch 11), can be summarized here from the Ottoman side.

Mehmed's preparations took two years. His first move was to build a fortress on the European side of the Bosporus, directly across from the existing Ottoman Anadoluhisarı (which Bayezid I had built in the 1390s). The new fortress was called **Rumelihisarı** (ROO-meh-lee-hee-sah-RUH, "the European Castle"), and it was constructed in the spring and summer of 1452 in just over four months — an architectural feat made possible by mobilizing thousands of laborers and cutting stone from the surrounding hillsides on a massive scale. With both castles operating, the Ottomans could close the strait between the Black Sea and the Sea of Marmara at will, controlling all maritime traffic. Any merchant ship trying to reach Constantinople from the Black Sea grain ports — the city's principal source of food — now had to ask Ottoman permission. The Byzantine emperor **Constantine XI Palaiologos** could see the new castle from his palace windows. He understood what it meant. He sent envoys to Mehmed pleading for a withdrawal. Mehmed's reply, recorded in the Greek sources, was that the Bosporus belonged to him and that further Byzantine interference would be treated as an act of war.

His second move was the artillery park. Mehmed hired a Hungarian engineer named **Orban** (or-BAHN), a master gun-founder who had originally offered his services to Constantine XI and been turned down because the Byzantine treasury could not pay his fee. Mehmed could pay. Orban moved to Edirne and supervised the casting of the largest cannons the world had yet seen. The biggest, called the **Basilica** (a Greek loan-word, which the Ottomans pronounced *Şahi*), is reported in the post-conquest Greek chronicles to have had a barrel about twenty-seven feet long and to have thrown a stone ball of around twelve hundred pounds — figures that may be inflated, since the surviving sources are post-facto accounts written by chroniclers describing a weapon they wanted readers to find legendary, but the orders of magnitude are at least plausible against later sixteenth-century Ottoman bombards that survive intact. The casting and transport were themselves engineering challenges: the Basilica required a team of sixty oxen to move, and a road crew of two hundred men and the engineering corps to grade the path between Edirne and Constantinople in advance. Several smaller bombards were also cast. Together, the artillery park represented the largest concentrated investment in gunpowder weaponry up to that point in human history.

His third move was the army. Mehmed assembled approximately eighty thousand to a hundred thousand men — Janissary infantry as the elite shock force, Anatolian and Rumelian feudal cavalry as the conventional army, irregular volunteers (*bashı-bozuks*, BAH-shuh boh-ZOOKS — literally "spoiled heads," a term for unruly or undisciplined troops, used here for the cannon-fodder tier sent in to wear down the defenders) as the expendable assault force, and a fleet of perhaps a hundred to a hundred and fifty ships gathered in the Sea of Marmara. The defending Byzantine garrison numbered perhaps seven thousand, supplemented by Italian volunteers — most importantly seven hundred Genoese under **Giovanni Giustiniani Longo**, who had crossed from Genoa with his troops at his own expense.

The siege began on April 6, 1453.

---

The fifty-three days that followed are among the most-narrated sieges in history. The major events fit the broad outline already sketched in the Byzantine chapter: the cannons hammered the Theodosian Walls, opening breaches that defenders desperately repaired overnight; the Ottoman fleet failed to force the entrance to the Golden Horn, blocked by the great chain stretched across the harbor mouth; on the night of April 22, Ottoman engineers built a greased wooden ramp over the hill behind Galata and dragged seventy ships overland into the upper Golden Horn, neutralizing the chain and creating a new front; the defenders had to spread their already-thin manpower across multiple threatened sections of wall; the cannon damage at the Saint Romanus Gate eventually became unrepairable; the Italian volunteers and the Greek defenders fought together with surprising solidarity given the deep theological tensions of the previous decades.

The Ottoman sources add a few details that are useful for understanding what was happening on the besiegers' side. The Janissary corps was Mehmed's last and best assault force, and he held them in reserve through most of the siege, throwing in the *bashı-bozuks* and the regular Anatolian troops first to wear down the defenders. By late May, after seven weeks of failed assaults, morale in the Ottoman camp was uneven. Several senior viziers — most prominently the longtime grand vizier **Halil Pasha**, who was the senior military advisor inherited from Murad II's reign — counseled Mehmed to lift the siege and negotiate terms. The siege was costing more than expected; the European powers were rumored to be assembling a relief fleet (in fact, only a small Venetian squadron sailed and arrived too late); the Ottoman regular soldiers were exhausted. Mehmed listened to the arguments, considered them, and rejected them. He gave the order for a final concentrated assault, with the Janissary corps committed in person, for the night of May 28–29.

The **Fall of Constantinople** (May 29, 1453) is the event whose name everyone in Christendom remembered for the next four centuries. Mehmed's troops broke through at the Saint Romanus Gate after Giustiniani was wounded and his Genoese began withdrawing. Constantine XI was last seen rushing into the press of Ottoman troops near the breach, having reportedly thrown off his imperial purple. His body was never definitely identified.

The plunder was authorized by Ottoman law and custom for three days. Mehmed cut it short to one. By the evening of May 29 he had ridden into the city, taken control of his own troops, and proceeded to the **Hagia Sophia** — full of refugees who had fled there praying through the previous night — to claim his greatest physical prize. He ordered the cathedral consecrated as a mosque. The first Islamic prayers were performed there on Friday, June 1, 1453, three days after the assault. The Christian inscriptions and figurative mosaics were eventually plastered over rather than destroyed; the building was reused, not demolished. Mehmed II is reported to have walked through the empty cathedral after the ceremony and quoted a Persian couplet about the impermanence of imperial glory: "The spider weaves the curtain in the palace of the Caesars / The owl calls the watches in the towers of Afrasiab." It is one of the more melancholic moments of triumph in conqueror's history.

The Ottoman state was now in possession of Constantinople. Mehmed renamed it nothing, formally — the city remained "Konstantiniyye" in Ottoman official usage for the next four hundred years, with the name "Istanbul" (a corruption of the Greek *eis ten polin*, "into the city," which Greek speakers had been using as a colloquial shorthand for centuries) as a parallel popular name that gradually displaced the formal one. In 1930 the Turkish Republic standardized "Istanbul" as the official name. From 1453 onward the city was the working capital of the Ottoman Empire, the largest and richest city in the Mediterranean, and the symbolic center of an Islamic imperial project that consciously inherited Byzantine and Roman precedents.

---

What Mehmed did with the captured city in the months and years after the conquest is at least as interesting as the conquest itself.

His first task was repopulating it. Constantinople in May 1453 was a depopulated wreck — the city's population had been declining for centuries, and the siege casualties and post-conquest enslavements had reduced it further. Mehmed needed bodies. He pursued an aggressive policy of forced and incentivized resettlement: Greek Orthodox families from across the empire were ordered to relocate to Constantinople; Armenian families from eastern Anatolia were brought in; Sephardic Jews who would soon be expelled from Spain (Chapter 5) were welcomed; Anatolian Turks were resettled in entire neighborhoods. By 1480 the city's population had recovered to perhaps a hundred thousand. By 1530 it would be the largest city in Europe. By 1600 it was probably the largest city in the world, with roughly seven hundred thousand inhabitants — larger than Beijing, larger than Cairo, larger than Paris and London combined.

His second task was building. The city of Constantinople had not had a major construction program for two centuries. Mehmed launched one. **Topkapı Palace** (TOHP-kah-puh, "Cannon Gate Palace," named for the gate near which it stood), begun in 1459, became the working residence of the Ottoman sultans for nearly four hundred years. It was not a single grand structure on the Western European model. It was a complex of pavilions, courtyards, and gardens, organized around three successive courtyards of increasing privacy, with the inner courtyards reserved for the sultan, his family, and his most senior administrators. The architectural model was as much Persian and Central Asian as Byzantine — pleasure gardens, water features, kiosk-style buildings — and it gave the Ottoman court a distinctive character that contrasted sharply with the heavy stone palaces of European monarchs. Topkapı was a working capital, a school, a treasury, and a religious institution all at once. The Sultan's private quarters, the harem, the divan (the council chamber where the grand vizier conducted state business), the imperial kitchens, and the palace school were all on the same grounds.

The **Fatih Mosque Complex** (FAH-tih, "the Conqueror's Mosque"), begun in 1463 on the site of the former Church of the Holy Apostles (which had been the burial place of Byzantine emperors since Constantine the Great — Mehmed's choice of this specific site was symbolically deliberate), was the first major imperial mosque complex in the new capital. Like later Ottoman imperial complexes, it was a *kulliye* (kool-LEE-yeh) — a unified institutional cluster combining a mosque, a hospital, a soup kitchen, multiple madrasas, and a tomb-mausoleum, all built and endowed as a single charitable foundation. The kulliye model was an Ottoman institutional invention, and it would shape the urban geography of every major Ottoman city for the next four centuries. The original Fatih Mosque was destroyed in an earthquake in 1766 and rebuilt in a different style; only fragments of the Mehmedian original survive.

The **Grand Bazaar of Istanbul** (KAH-pah-luh CHAR-shuh, "Covered Market") began as a small structure in 1461, expanded under Mehmed's successors, and grew into one of the world's largest covered markets — a network of sixty-one streets and over four thousand shops, sheltered under multiple domes, organized by guild and trade. By 1500 the Bazaar was the central commercial node of the Ottoman Mediterranean, with rooms for foreign merchants, banks of money-changers, and a strict regulatory regime overseeing prices, weights, and quality. It is still operating, six centuries later, on roughly the same footprint.

His third task was institutional consolidation. Mehmed issued the **Kanunname** (kah-noon-NAH-meh) of 1477 — an imperial law code organizing Ottoman administrative and criminal practice into a single corpus. The Kanunname's most discussed provision was the **fratricide law**: "And to whichever of my sons the sultanate shall pass, it is fitting that for the order of the world he shall kill his brothers. Most of the *ulema* (religious scholars) have approved this. Let them act accordingly." The provision was not a literary flourish. It was the formal legal basis for what would become standard Ottoman succession practice: upon ascending the throne, a new sultan was authorized to execute his brothers (and any potential rival nephews) to prevent the kind of civil war that had wrecked the empire after Bayezid I's death. The cost of this in human terms was real — sultans through the sixteenth century routinely had several dozen relatives strangled with bowstrings on their accession — but the political effect was that the Ottoman state did not have a major succession war for two centuries.

The Kanunname also organized the **timar system**, the Ottoman variant of the Byzantine theme system (Byzantine Empire Ch 3). Like Byzantine *stratiotika ktemata*, *timars* were land grants given to soldiers — primarily to the **sipahis** (SEE-pah-hee, the Anatolian and Balkan feudal cavalry) — in exchange for hereditary military service. A sipahi held his timar from the sultan, drew its revenue, and was obliged to report for military duty with a specified number of additional armed retainers when called up. Unlike European feudal grants, timars were not full property; they were revocable and reassignable, and the sultan retained ultimate ownership. The timar system, combined with the Janissary corps (Chapter 6), gave the Ottomans an army of roughly fifty thousand cavalry plus ten to twenty thousand professional infantry, drawn from the empire's own land base, paid through institutional rather than monetary means, and far more flexible than the comparable European feudal armies. It would be the institutional foundation of Ottoman military power for the next two centuries.

---

Mehmed's later campaigns extended Ottoman control across the eastern Mediterranean and the Balkans, and the highlights are worth tracking briefly.

In 1461 he eliminated the **Empire of Trebizond** (Byzantine Empire Ch 11), the last surviving Byzantine successor state — a small Pontic kingdom on the Black Sea coast under the Komnenos dynasty that had outlasted Constantinople by eight years. Trebizond surrendered after a brief siege; the last emperor David Komnenos was executed two years later along with most of his sons.

In the same year the Ottomans completed the absorption of the **Despotate of the Morea**, the Peloponnesian fragment ruled by Constantine XI's brothers. Thomas Palaiologos escaped with his family to Italy. His daughter **Zoe Palaiologina** (later Sophia) eventually married **Grand Duke Ivan III of Moscow** in 1472, a marriage that transferred the Byzantine imperial inheritance to Russia and laid the groundwork for the Russian "Third Rome" doctrine (Byzantine Empire Ch 12).

Mehmed's wars with **Venice** (1463–1479) ended with Ottoman gains across the Aegean: Negroponte (modern Euboea, the long island off the eastern coast of Greece) fell in 1470, after a violent siege that shocked Christian Europe; Venice ceded several Aegean islands and Albanian coastal cities; the eastern Mediterranean balance shifted decisively against the Italian maritime republics. Albanian resistance under **Skanderbeg** (skan-der-BEG, born George Kastrioti — c. 1405–1468, an Albanian noble who had been raised at the Ottoman court as a hostage and Janissary trainee, then defected when Murad II sent him to Albania to suppress a revolt; he turned the suppression into a twenty-five-year-long Albanian insurgency that became one of the legendary resistance movements of late medieval Europe) was a serious irritant during the 1450s and 1460s, but it was personal to Skanderbeg himself and dissolved after his death in 1468.

In 1480 Mehmed launched two major operations that, had they both succeeded, would have remade the Mediterranean again. The first was the **Siege of Rhodes** — an attempt to take the heavily fortified Hospitaller stronghold in the Aegean that controlled shipping lanes between the eastern Mediterranean and the Levant. It failed; the Knights Hospitaller, dug in behind impressive walls, repelled the Ottoman fleet and army. The second was the **invasion of Otranto** in southeastern Italy — an Ottoman expeditionary force under the Albanian-born admiral **Gedik Ahmed Pasha** that took the Italian town of Otranto in August 1480, slaughtered most of its male population, and held a beachhead on the Italian mainland for thirteen months. Pope Sixtus IV briefly considered fleeing Rome. The invasion of Italy was abandoned in late 1481 only because Mehmed II had died on May 3 of that year (probably from natural causes, though contemporary rumor suggested poison administered by his own son Bayezid II), and his successors had to consolidate the throne before continuing the campaign. Otranto was recovered by Christian forces. The Italian invasion was never resumed.

Mehmed's reign had transformed the Ottoman Empire. He had inherited a state recovering from the Interregnum, with one major Christian capital still independent. He left a state that ruled most of the Balkans, all of Anatolia and the Aegean, and a working empire centered on the city that had been the symbolic seat of Roman Christendom for eleven centuries. His self-styled title — Kayser-i Rûm — was not delusional. By the standards of the institutional and territorial inheritance, he was the legitimate Roman emperor of the eastern Mediterranean, ruling from the same throne in the same city, with continuity in many of the same administrative practices, and a court that included Greek Orthodox patriarchs, Armenian merchants, Italian humanist painters (he commissioned **Gentile Bellini** to paint his portrait in 1480 — the first realistic European portrait of an Ottoman sultan), and Persian astronomers. He had ended one civilization and become the senior partner of its successor.

His tomb in the Fatih Mosque complex was inscribed with a verse comparing him to the prophet Sulayman — Solomon, in the Ottoman court tradition, was the ideal of the just and wise universal monarch. The next great Ottoman ruler would be named after that prophet, and he would push the comparison further than anyone had imagined.

Cross-civ check: 1453, the year Constantinople fell, was also approximately the year **Johannes Gutenberg** finished printing his first Bible in Mainz — the technologies that would shape the next century of European thought were emerging at the same moment as the Ottoman political consolidation. The **Hundred Years' War** between England and France ended the same year (October 1453, at the Battle of Castillon). In China, the **Ming dynasty** under the Jingtai emperor was recovering from the Tumu Crisis of 1449. In the Andes, the **Inca Empire** was beginning its great expansion under Pachacuti. In Mesoamerica, **Moctezuma I** was the Aztec emperor, presiding over a major expansion of the Triple Alliance. Mehmed II's conquest of Constantinople is often described as the event that ended the Middle Ages in European historiography. From a global perspective, it was one of several great political events of the 1450s, all of them reshaping their respective regions, all of them happening in parallel.

# Chapter 5 — Selim, Süleyman, the Lawgiver

If Mehmed II's reign was the moment the Ottoman Empire became a world power, the seventy-five years between his death in 1481 and the death of his great-grandson Süleyman in 1566 were the moment it was at its operational peak. The empire doubled in size. It absorbed the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt, the Hijaz (with the holy cities of Mecca and Medina), most of Iraq, Syria, the North African coast as far as Algeria, Hungary, and most of the Balkans. It built the architecture by which Ottoman civilization would be remembered — the imperial mosques of Istanbul, the great kulliye complexes, the unified visual vocabulary of dome, half-dome, minaret, and tilework. It codified the legal system that would harmonize Islamic religious law with secular imperial decree across three continents. It produced the cultural idiom — Ottoman Turkish poetry on Persian models, miniature painting at the imperial workshop, Iznik tile and ceramic, court music — that would become the standard of refined taste from Sarajevo to Baghdad to Cairo.

This chapter covers the three rulers who presided over this expansion: **Bayezid II** (1481–1512), **Selim I** "the Grim" (1512–1520), and **Süleyman I** "the Magnificent" (1520–1566). Three sultans, three temperaments, one trajectory.

---

**Bayezid II** is the least studied of the three, partly because he ruled between two more dramatic father-and-son figures and partly because his reign was deliberately calmer than the campaigns surrounding it. He was Mehmed II's elder son, took the throne after a brief succession struggle with his younger brother **Cem** (JEM) — a struggle which ended with Cem fleeing to Christian Europe, where he became a high-value diplomatic hostage shuttling between the Knights Hospitaller, the French king, and successive popes for the next thirteen years until his death in 1495 in suspicious circumstances. Bayezid II spent the early years of his reign consolidating institutional gains rather than launching new conquests, and his diplomatic skill in keeping Cem's potential as a rival neutralized while not provoking a Christian crusade in his name was one of the more delicate political achievements of the late fifteenth century.

His reign produced one of the most consequential humanitarian decisions in Ottoman history. In 1492, the Catholic monarchs **Ferdinand and Isabella** of Spain issued the **Alhambra Decree**, ordering the expulsion of all Jews from Spain who refused conversion to Christianity. Around 200,000 Sephardic Jews were forced to flee — to Portugal, to Morocco, to the Italian city-states, and across the Mediterranean to anywhere that would accept them. Bayezid II opened the Ottoman Empire to them. He reportedly sent ships to Iberian ports to facilitate the migration and ordered Ottoman authorities to receive Jewish refugees with dignity. The **Sephardic Jewish** community established in cities across the Ottoman world — Salonika (modern Thessaloniki, which by 1500 had become the largest Sephardic Jewish city in the world, with the Jewish population outnumbering Greeks and Turks combined for the next four centuries), Istanbul, Edirne, Sarajevo, Smyrna (modern Izmir) — would shape Ottoman commerce, medicine, finance, and printing for the remainder of the empire's existence.

The political logic of Bayezid's decision was genuine pragmatism. The Sephardic Jews brought commercial networks across the western Mediterranean, technical skills (especially in medicine, mathematics, and metalworking), and an established expertise in printing — the first Hebrew printing presses in the Ottoman Empire were established by Sephardic refugees in the 1490s, decades before Arabic-script printing was permitted (Chapter 9). But the decision was also shaped by Bayezid's reading of the situation. Later Ottoman tradition attributes to him a remark that Ferdinand was considered a wise king but had impoverished his own country and enriched Bayezid's — a perfectly crafted aphorism that probably owes more to subsequent polishing than to a contemporary record, but that captures the policy logic accurately. The Spanish expulsion was, by any rational political calculus, an act of national self-mutilation. The Ottoman gain was Spain's loss, and Bayezid was clear-eyed about both halves of that equation.

The Sephardic resettlement was part of a larger pattern. The Ottoman state in the late fifteenth century was the most religiously diverse polity in the Mediterranean basin — Sunni Muslim majority, with substantial Greek Orthodox, Armenian Christian, Jewish, Catholic, Druze, Alawite, and Yezidi populations, plus various smaller heterodox Sufi communities. The institutional framework that managed this diversity (the millet system, Chapter 6) was already operational in rough form, and Bayezid's reign was when it began to be formally codified. The empire was not a tolerant state in any modern sense — non-Muslims paid additional taxes (the **jizya** head tax and the **harac** land tax), faced legal disabilities in Islamic courts, and could not bear arms — but it was a structured pluralist state, and within its limits, the Ottoman framework offered protections that no Christian European monarchy of the period could match.

Bayezid II also began the Ottoman state's serious engagement with **maritime science**. The Ottoman admiral **Piri Reis** (PEE-ree RAY-ess, c. 1465–1553), nephew of an earlier Ottoman corsair, served in the eastern Mediterranean fleet during Bayezid's reign. He is the figure whose work would, in 1513, produce the most discussed cartographic document of the early sixteenth century: the **Piri Reis World Map**. The map (or rather the surviving fragment of it, found in the Topkapı archives in 1929) shows the western coastline of Africa, the eastern coastlines of the Americas, and a portion of Antarctica — with annotations in Piri Reis's own hand acknowledging that he drew on more than twenty source maps including Christopher Columbus's lost original chart of the Caribbean. The Antarctica appearance is overstated in popular accounts; what the map actually shows in that region is harder to identify and may represent a misunderstood or distorted depiction of the South American coastline. What is genuinely remarkable about the Piri Reis map is the speed at which the new geographic knowledge from Iberian voyages had reached an Ottoman naval officer's drawing table — the Americas had been encountered in 1492, and within twenty years Piri Reis was incorporating them into a synthesis with classical Ptolemaic geography. The Ottoman state was a participant in the Age of Exploration, even if its naval commitments were primarily Mediterranean.

Bayezid II abdicated in 1512, under pressure from his son Selim and the Janissary corps. He died one month later, possibly poisoned by Selim, possibly of natural causes — the historical sources are uncertain.

---

**Selim I** (ruled 1512–1520), called **Yavuz** (yah-VOOZ, "the Stern" or "the Grim") in Ottoman tradition, was the shortest-reigning of the three sultans in this chapter and arguably the most consequential. He took the throne by displacing his father and executing his brothers and nephews. He spent the next eight years on campaign almost continuously. By the time he died of an infected boil in 1520, he had doubled the empire's size, claimed the Caliphate, and absorbed the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt — the dominant Sunni Muslim state of the eastern Mediterranean for the previous three centuries.

His first major campaign was eastward, against the **Safavid Empire** of Persia.

The Safavids were a new force in the early sixteenth century. The dynasty had been founded in 1501 by **Shah Ismail I** (iss-mah-EEL, c. 1487–1524), a charismatic young Turkmen warrior-poet from the **Safaviyya** Sufi order in Azerbaijan, who had united the Turkic tribes of northwestern Iran under a militant **Twelver Shi'a** ideology and conquered most of Persia within a decade. The Safavid state was, in its first generation, an explicitly Shi'a state — Shah Ismail had imposed Twelver Shi'ism as the official religion of his empire, persecuted Sunni populations, and aggressively recruited from heterodox Turkmen groups in eastern Anatolia. Many of the recruits came from the **Qizilbash** (kuh-ZUHL-bahsh, "Red-Heads," named for the red headgear that marked them as Safavid loyalists) — Turkmen tribal warriors whose Sufi-influenced religious practice was a direct competitor to the Sunni-centered Ottoman religious establishment.

This was an existential challenge. The Ottomans were a Sunni state; the great religious authority they claimed was Sunni; their eastern Anatolian provinces were full of Turkmen populations whose loyalty was now being competed for by the Safavid Shah. In 1511 a major Qizilbash uprising in eastern Anatolia (the **Şahkulu rebellion**) had nearly destabilized the entire province before being suppressed. Selim, on taking the throne in 1512, identified the Safavid threat as the most serious facing the empire. He ordered a massive purge of suspected Qizilbash sympathizers within the eastern Anatolian Turkmen population — modern estimates of the killed run as high as forty thousand — and then prepared for war with Ismail.

The two armies met on August 23, 1514, at **Chaldiran** (CHAHL-dur-AHN, in northwestern Iran near modern Maku). The Ottoman force was about sixty thousand, with the Janissary infantry, the timar-based cavalry, and a substantial artillery park. Ismail's force was about forty thousand, almost entirely Qizilbash cavalry, with no artillery of his own. The battle was decided by the Ottoman cannons. The Janissaries took the Safavid frontline cavalry under sustained fire from chained-together field guns, opened gaps in the line, and let the Anatolian sipahis exploit the breaks. Ismail himself was wounded and barely escaped. The Safavid army was shattered. The Ottomans took **Tabriz**, the Safavid capital, briefly — they could not hold it through the winter — and demonstrated that gunpowder weaponry would, from now on, be the difference between Ottoman and traditional Islamic military forces.

The **Battle of Chaldiran** is one of those events whose immediate consequences and long-term consequences are different. The immediate consequence was Ottoman security on the eastern frontier — the Safavid expansion into Anatolia was halted permanently, and Shah Ismail spent the rest of his life avoiding pitched battles with the Ottomans. The long-term consequence was the institutionalization of the **Sunni-Shi'a sectarian divide** as the operative religious geopolitics of the Islamic world, with the Ottomans as the Sunni superpower and the Safavids as the Shi'a counter-state. This divide is still operative in the politics of the modern Middle East. Iraqi-Iranian relations, Saudi-Iranian relations, the Lebanese sectarian system, the Bahraini civil tensions — all of them trace, in some part, to the Ottoman-Safavid rivalry that became unbridgeable in 1514.

---

Selim's second major campaign was southward, against the **Mamluk Sultanate**.

The Mamluks (mam-LOOKS, "owned ones," from the Arabic word for "slave") were a slave-soldier dynasty that had ruled Egypt and Syria since 1250 — a roughly contemporaneous slave-soldier state to the developing Janissary system, but with deeper roots and a longer institutional tradition. The Mamluks had defeated the Mongols at **Ayn Jalut** in 1260 (Mongol Empire Ch 5), expelled the last Crusaders from the Holy Land in 1291, and ruled the eastern Mediterranean as the dominant Sunni Muslim power for the previous two and a half centuries. Their empire included Egypt, the Levant, the Hijaz with the holy cities of Mecca and Medina, and the trade routes connecting the Indian Ocean to the Mediterranean.

By 1516 the Mamluk state was institutionally exhausted. The slave-soldier recruitment system — which had relied on Circassian and Turkic boys imported from the Black Sea region — was being throttled by Ottoman control of the Black Sea trade. The Mamluk military had not adopted gunpowder firearms or artillery to any significant extent, holding instead to the heavily armored mounted archery model that had defined Mamluk warfare for centuries. The Mamluk economy had been undermined by the Portuguese Indian Ocean voyages that had begun in 1498 — Vasco da Gama's arrival in Calicut had opened a maritime route around the Cape of Good Hope that bypassed the Egyptian-Mediterranean spice trade, costing the Mamluks a major share of their revenue.

Selim moved fast. The Ottomans defeated the main Mamluk army at **Marj Dabiq** (MARJ dah-BEEK) in northern Syria on August 24, 1516 — a battle decided, like Chaldiran, by Ottoman gunpowder superiority over a force that fought as if firearms had not been invented. The Mamluk Sultan **Qansuh al-Ghawri** was killed in the battle (probably from a stroke during the engagement, though the sources disagree). The Ottomans took Damascus, then Aleppo, then the rest of Syria. They marched south through Palestine into Egypt. The successor Mamluk Sultan **Tuman Bay II** tried to organize a final resistance at **Ridaniya**, just outside Cairo, on January 22, 1517. He lost. Cairo fell. Tuman Bay was captured several weeks later and hanged from the Bab Zuwayla gate of his own capital.

In the conquest of Egypt, Selim acquired three things of fundamental importance.

The first was **the Hijaz**, with the holy cities of Mecca and Medina. The Mamluks had been the guardians of the **Two Holy Cities** since 1250. The Hijaz was now Ottoman. The annual pilgrimage caravan (the **hajj**) and its security infrastructure passed under Ottoman authority. The Sharif of Mecca acknowledged Ottoman sovereignty. The sultan's title became "**Servant of the Two Sanctuaries**" — a designation that gave the Ottomans a unique religious legitimacy across the Sunni Muslim world.

The second was **the Caliphate**. The Caliphate had been a contested institution since the Mongol sack of Baghdad and execution of the last Abbasid Caliph in 1258 (Mongol Empire Ch 5). A surviving Abbasid line had been kept as ceremonial puppet caliphs at the Mamluk court in Cairo since then, with no real authority but with the symbolic weight of three centuries of formal continuity. Selim, on taking Cairo, reportedly received the formal transfer of the Caliphate from the last shadow Abbasid, **Al-Mutawakkil III**, who was brought to Istanbul and pensioned. The historicity of this transfer is debated — there is no contemporary documentary evidence of a formal ceremony, and the doctrine that the Caliphate had been transferred in 1517 was probably elaborated retrospectively in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. What is clear is that from 1517 onward, the Ottoman sultans claimed the title **Caliph of the Muslims** in addition to their other titles, and the claim was widely accepted across the Sunni Islamic world. The institutional fusion of sultanate and caliphate would become a central feature of Ottoman political theology for the next four hundred years, and its eventual abolition in 1924 (Chapter 12) would close one of the longest-running theological-political institutions in human history.

The third was Egypt's tax base. The Egyptian treasury, even in its weakened sixteenth-century state, was one of the largest single revenue sources in the Mediterranean basin. The Egyptian grain economy continued, after a brief disruption, to feed Istanbul's growing population — the same role Egypt had played for Constantinople's growing population a millennium earlier (Byzantine Empire Ch 1). The Egyptian textiles, the Indian Ocean trade routes, the customs duties on the Suez and Alexandria trade — all of these now flowed into Ottoman coffers. The empire's annual revenue roughly doubled overnight. The Janissary corps could be expanded. The fleet could be rebuilt. The artillery park could be modernized. Selim's territorial conquests had paid for the next half-century of Ottoman expansion.

He died in September 1520 of an infected boil on his back, while preparing what would have been his next campaign. He was forty-nine.

---

His son **Süleyman I** (ruled 1520–1566), called the **Magnificent** in the West and **Kanuni** (kah-noo-NEE, "the Lawgiver") in the Ottoman tradition, took the throne at twenty-six and ruled for forty-six years — the longest single reign in Ottoman history. He inherited the empire at the moment of maximum institutional and territorial momentum, and he ran it through what every subsequent Ottoman generation would treat as the empire's golden age.

Süleyman's reign was so comprehensively productive that summarizing it is difficult. His military campaigns alone took up most of every year for most of his reign — he led thirteen major campaigns in person, more than any other Ottoman sultan. The institutional, legal, and architectural achievements of his reign would shape the empire's character for the next three centuries. His personal correspondence with European monarchs (especially with **King Francis I of France**, with whom he forged the **Franco-Ottoman alliance** of 1536, the first formal diplomatic agreement between a Christian and Muslim European power, and one that would shape European politics for the next two centuries) gave him a reputation in western Europe as the most formidable monarch of his age. The court of Süleyman was the place where Ottoman classical culture reached its concentrated peak.

Take the campaigns first.

In 1521, the year after his accession, Süleyman captured **Belgrade** — the Hungarian fortress at the confluence of the Danube and Sava rivers that had been the strategic key to defending central Europe. Mehmed II had failed to take Belgrade in 1456. Süleyman took it in his first major campaign. The road to Hungary was now open.

In 1522, he took **Rhodes** — the Hospitaller stronghold his great-grandfather Mehmed II had failed to capture in 1480. The siege lasted six months and cost the Ottomans heavily, but the Knights Hospitaller eventually surrendered on negotiated terms and were allowed to evacuate to Sicily and eventually Malta. With Rhodes Ottoman, the eastern Mediterranean shipping lanes were secure.

In 1526, at the **Battle of Mohács** (MOH-hahch) in southern Hungary on August 29, Süleyman destroyed the **Kingdom of Hungary**. The Hungarian king **Louis II** was killed (drowned in a swamp during the rout). Most of the Hungarian aristocracy died with him. Hungary fragmented into three pieces over the next several years: the central plain became an Ottoman province administered from Buda (the western half of modern Budapest, taken in 1541); the eastern portion became the **Principality of Transylvania**, an Ottoman vassal under a Hungarian noble dynasty; the western strip remained under Habsburg rule. Hungary as an independent kingdom ceased to exist for the next 150 years.

In 1529, Süleyman besieged **Vienna** — the first Ottoman attempt on the Habsburg capital. The siege was a stretch — the Ottoman supply lines were over a thousand miles long, the campaign season was running out, the autumn rains were beginning to make Hungarian terrain impassable for siege artillery — and after three weeks of unsuccessful assaults, Süleyman lifted the siege on October 14 and withdrew. The First Siege of Vienna marked the practical limit of Ottoman expansion into central Europe. Habsburg forces would never push the Ottomans back as far as the Hungarian plain in Süleyman's lifetime, but the Ottomans would never get further west than the suburbs of Vienna either. The European-Ottoman frontier had stabilized along a line that would not move significantly for the next 150 years.

In 1534, Süleyman took **Baghdad** from the Safavids — a campaign that brought all of Iraq under Ottoman control. The Tigris-Euphrates valleys, which had been the heart of the Islamic world under the Abbasid Caliphate, were now Ottoman. The Twelver Shi'a holy cities of Najaf and Karbala fell into Ottoman hands. The acquisition was symbolically enormous — the Ottoman sultan now ruled the city the Caliph had ruled — and it gave Süleyman additional grounds for the **Servant of the Two Sanctuaries** title his father had claimed.

Throughout the rest of his reign, Süleyman alternated campaigns against the Habsburgs in Hungary, the Safavids in Persia and Iraq, the Portuguese in the Indian Ocean (Ottoman fleets repeatedly tried to expel the Portuguese from the Red Sea and the Persian Gulf, with mixed success), and various Christian states in the Mediterranean. The naval war was largely managed by **Hayreddin Barbarossa** (HYE-reh-din bar-bah-ROH-sah, c. 1478–1546, an Aegean-born corsair-turned-Ottoman-admiral whose Mediterranean fleet dominated the central and western Mediterranean for two decades), whose victory at the **Battle of Preveza** in 1538 destroyed a combined Christian fleet under the Genoese admiral **Andrea Doria** and gave the Ottomans naval supremacy in the Mediterranean for the next thirty years.

---

Now take the institutional and cultural side.

Süleyman's title **Kanuni** ("the Lawgiver") came from his comprehensive codification of Ottoman secular law. The Ottoman legal system rested on two pillars. The first was **sharia** — Islamic religious law, derived from the Quran, the hadith (the recorded sayings and actions of the Prophet Muhammad), and the centuries of juridical commentary developed by Sunni legal scholars. The second was **kanun** — secular imperial law, issued by the sultan to handle areas where sharia was silent or where administrative practicality required additional rules: taxation, land tenure, military organization, criminal procedure for non-religious offenses, market regulation. The two systems coexisted in tension. Sharia was the universal religious-moral framework. Kanun was the practical operating code of the empire's administration.

Süleyman's chief jurist, the **Sheikh ul-Islam** (the highest religious authority of the empire — the office had been formalized under Mehmed II) **Ebussuud Efendi** (EH-boos-SOOD), spent decades of careful work harmonizing the two systems. Ebussuud's interpretive method was to find sharia-compatible justifications for kanun provisions where possible, and to acknowledge the sultan's authority to legislate in areas not explicitly addressed by sharia. The resulting **Kanun-i Osmani** (the systematic codification of Ottoman secular law completed during Süleyman's reign) became the operational legal framework for the empire across three continents. Ottoman judges in Cairo, Damascus, Sarajevo, Buda, Sofia, and Istanbul applied a single legal code, with regional adaptations, to populations of every religion. By the standards of any sixteenth-century legal system, this was remarkable.

The architecture is the other defining feature of the period, and its great figure was **Mimar Sinan** (mee-MAR see-NAHN, c. 1490–1588) — the Ottoman chief architect for fifty years and one of the most consequential builders in human history.

Sinan was, like many of the Ottoman state's senior officials, a devshirme recruit. He was born in the Ottoman Anatolian province of Cappadocia to an Orthodox Christian (probably Armenian or Greek) family, taken in the devshirme around 1512, converted to Islam, trained in the imperial palace, and assigned to the Janissary corps as a military engineer. He served on Süleyman's campaigns for over twenty years, building bridges, roads, fortifications, and siege engines across the empire. He was appointed chief imperial architect in 1539, at about age forty-nine, and held the post for the next forty-nine years. The buildings he designed and supervised during that period — about three hundred and fifty across the empire, of which over a hundred survive — established the visual vocabulary of Ottoman classical architecture.

Sinan's central problem was the same one Justinian's architects Anthemius and Isidore had faced ten centuries earlier (Byzantine Empire Ch 2): how do you cover a large central interior space with a single dome, while keeping the worshipful interior lit, structurally sound, and aesthetically coherent? Anthemius and Isidore had invented the **pendentive** — the triangular concave surface that transitions from a square base to a circular dome — as their solution at Hagia Sophia. The pendentive remained the structural basis of Byzantine and Ottoman dome architecture for the next thousand years. Sinan's contribution was to refine, rationalize, and extend the system across an entire family of imperial mosques.

His great early work was the **Şehzade Mosque** (sheh-ZAH-deh, "the Prince's Mosque"), completed in 1548, built for Süleyman's deceased son Mehmed. The Şehzade is symmetrical in plan, with a central dome supported by four half-domes — the first time this exact configuration had been attempted, and a step beyond the Hagia Sophia model.

His acknowledged masterpiece in Istanbul was the **Süleymaniye Mosque** (soo-lay-mah-NEE-yeh), completed in 1557 — a massive imperial complex containing the mosque, four madrasas, a hospital, a soup kitchen, a primary school, a hammam (public bath), and various other charitable institutions. The Süleymaniye's central dome is 173 feet high and 86 feet across, supported by two half-domes on the longitudinal axis. It is a more rationally proportioned, structurally cleaner descendant of Hagia Sophia. Sinan said of it that it was the work of his apprenticeship.

His self-proclaimed masterpiece was the **Selimiye Mosque** in Edirne, completed in 1574, when he was about eighty-four years old. The Selimiye's central dome is 108 feet across — surpassing Hagia Sophia by about six feet — and supported by eight piers arranged in an octagon, an entirely new structural system that allowed the dome to appear as if it were floating without visible support. Sinan considered it the work of his maturity. He may have been right. The Selimiye is now a UNESCO World Heritage site and is generally considered, by serious students of architectural history, to be one of the supreme achievements of the medieval and early modern world.

The ornament was as important as the structure. The interior of the Süleymaniye and Selimiye mosques used **Iznik tiles** — the polychrome ceramic tiles produced in the imperial workshops at Iznik (the old Byzantine Nicaea) from the early sixteenth century onward, with their characteristic blue, turquoise, red, and green floral and arabesque motifs against a white background. Iznik tilework set the visual standard for Ottoman religious and palatial decoration for the next two centuries. The light filtered through stained-glass windows. The calligraphic medallions inscribed with Quranic verses provided the major focal points. The sound of the mu'addhin's call to prayer floated from the minarets — Sinan typically used four minarets for an imperial mosque, with multiple balconies (*şerefe*) corresponding to each minaret's height ranking.

Sinan was eighty-eight when he died in 1588. He had designed mosques, palaces, mausoleums, baths, bridges, aqueducts, hospitals, and schools across an empire stretching from the Danube to the Tigris. He had trained generations of architects who would continue the tradition for the next century. His tomb is in a small mausoleum he designed for himself, near the Süleymaniye complex, with an inscription giving his name in plain Arabic script and his title — "Mimar Sinan" — without further elaboration. He had not needed to elaborate. Anyone walking past it knew what he had built.

---

Süleyman's wife **Hürrem Sultan** (HUR-rem) — born **Roxelana** in Ruthenia (modern western Ukraine), captured as a teenager in a Tatar slave raid, brought to the Ottoman harem, and rising through the harem hierarchy to become Süleyman's principal favorite — would change the structure of Ottoman dynastic politics for the next century, and her story properly belongs to the next chapter where the post-Süleyman world is shaped by her precedent. What needs noting here is that Süleyman, in 1533 or 1534, freed Hürrem from slave status and married her in a public legal ceremony — the first time an Ottoman sultan had legally married a woman since the early fifteenth century. The previous practice had been concubine relationships, with the mothers of potential heirs deriving their status from their sons rather than from formal marriage. Süleyman's marriage to Hürrem was unprecedented and shocked court traditionalists. It also concentrated political power in the imperial harem in a way that had not existed before, and the long-term consequences would become one of the defining features of post-Süleyman Ottoman politics.

Süleyman died on the night of September 6, 1566, during the siege of the Hungarian fortress of **Szigetvár** (SIG-et-var). He was seventy-two years old. The grand vizier **Sokollu Mehmed Pasha** kept his death secret from the army for six weeks, finishing the siege successfully and getting the body back to Istanbul before the news broke. The transition to the next sultan went smoothly. The empire's institutional momentum was so strong that Süleyman's death was, organizationally, almost a non-event.

The empire he left was the largest, richest, and most institutionally sophisticated political entity in the world outside the Ming Dynasty. It controlled the Balkans, all of Anatolia, the Levant, Egypt, the Hijaz, Mesopotamia, the North African coast as far west as Algiers, and substantial parts of the Caucasus. It had a population of perhaps thirty million. It had the most professional army in Europe, the largest navy in the Mediterranean, and a working multi-religious legal and administrative system that no other contemporary state could match. It had just produced, under Sinan, an architectural achievement that would not be surpassed for centuries.

The next two hundred years would be a story of slow erosion of all of this. The institutions would prove brittle. The frontier would stop advancing. The court would become increasingly self-absorbed. The empire would, eventually, begin to lose territory it had taken centuries to acquire. But none of that was visible in 1566, when the Ottomans were burying their longest-reigning sultan and the Janissaries were chanting their loyalty oath to his son.

Cross-civ check: the year of Süleyman's death (1566) was approximately when **Akbar the Great** of the **Mughal Empire** — the Persian-Mongol-descended dynasty in India — was beginning his major reign, when **Elizabeth I** of England had been on the throne for eight years, when the **Counter-Reformation** in Catholic Europe was reaching its peak, when **Ivan the Terrible** of **Russia** had been declaring himself Tsar for nineteen years, when the **Spanish Empire** under Philip II was at its territorial peak in the Americas, and when the **Ming dynasty** under the Jiajing Emperor was in its long mid-period decline. The Ottoman Empire of the mid-sixteenth century was contemporary with most of the early modern world's great political consolidations. It was as large as any of them and structurally as sophisticated as the most sophisticated.

# Chapter 6 — The Janissaries, the Devshirme, the Millet

This chapter is a pause. The political narrative of Chapters 4 and 5 has been driving forward through campaigns and conquests, and the next chapter will resume that drive through the slow disintegration of the post-Süleyman court. But before it does, three institutions are worth examining in detail, because they were the operating mechanisms by which the Ottoman state ran. None of them are unique inventions — each had earlier precedents in Islamic, Byzantine, and steppe practice — but the Ottoman versions were more developed, more systematized, and more durable than their counterparts elsewhere. They are the slave-soldier corps of the **Janissaries**, the recruitment system that fed it (the **devshirme**), and the religious-pluralism framework that managed the empire's non-Muslim populations (the **millet system**).

These three together explain how a state ruling thirty million people of dozens of ethnicities and several major religions could function as a coherent administrative entity for four centuries. The political stories are easier to remember. The institutional stories are why the political stories were possible.

---

Start with the Janissary corps.

The **Janissaries** (yan-iss-AIR-eez, from Turkish *yeni çeri*, "new troops") were the standing professional infantry of the Ottoman Empire from approximately 1363 (under Murad I) to 1826 (when Mahmud II destroyed them, Chapter 9). For most of those four hundred and sixty-three years, they were the most consistently effective infantry force in the world. They were the first Ottoman troops trained from childhood to use firearms in coordinated formations. They were the units that took Constantinople, defeated the Mamluks at Marj Dabiq and Ridaniya, won at Mohács and Chaldiran, marched on Vienna twice, and stood as the personal guard of every sultan from Murad I to Mahmud II.

What made them distinctive was the recruitment pattern. The Janissary was not a free Muslim subject who had volunteered for military service. He was a **kapıkulu** (kah-puh-koo-LOO, "slave of the Porte" — the Ottoman name for the slave class of the imperial household). He had been taken from a Christian family in childhood through the devshirme, converted to Islam, given a Turkish name, trained and educated in the imperial system, and made the personal property of the sultan. His legal status was technically slavery. His operational reality was an elite military profession with steady pay, organized barracks life, hereditary recognition, and access to the most senior positions in the Ottoman state.

The institutional logic was Byzantine, Mamluk, and Abbasid. Both the Byzantine **tagmata** (the elite professional units of the imperial guard) and the Mamluk slave-soldier system had demonstrated that personally loyal slave-soldiers, owing their entire training and status to the ruler, made better elite troops than aristocratic levies whose loyalty was complicated by family interests. Slaves had no families in the original sense — their families had been left behind — and could not start dynasties to compete with the ruling one. They were, in this sense, structurally analogous to the Byzantine eunuchs (Byzantine Empire Ch 6) and the Mamluk corps in Egypt and Syria. The Ottomans took the slave-soldier idea, refined it, and turned it into the central pillar of their military system.

The corps was organized in regiments called **ortas** (OR-tahs), each of about a hundred men, with elaborate insignia and traditions. The Janissary commander (the **Ağa** of the Janissaries) was one of the senior officials of the imperial court, with direct access to the sultan and a major voice in succession politics. The corps had its own legal jurisdiction, its own internal discipline, its own pay structures, and its own ceremonial roles. Janissaries fought on foot in close formations, primarily armed with **arquebuses** (early matchlock firearms) from the late fifteenth century onward. They were trained in coordinated volley fire — entire ranks discharging muskets simultaneously and reloading in sequence — at a level of coordination that European armies would not match for another century. They were paid in cash from the central treasury, supplemented by ceremonial gifts from the sultan on his accession, on military victories, and on major holidays.

The cohesion of the corps came from the **bektashi** Sufi order, which functioned as the spiritual home of the Janissaries from the late fifteenth century onward. Every Janissary was nominally a Bektashi member. The order's syncretic theology — drawing on Sufism, Twelver Shi'ism, and folk traditions — gave the slave-soldier corps a specific religious identity that distinguished them from both the orthodox Sunni religious establishment and the Christian populations from which they had been recruited. Bektashi rituals, songs, and food (the **pilav**, the rice-and-meat dish that became symbolic of Janissary identity) were part of corps culture for centuries.

By the mid-sixteenth century the Janissary corps numbered about twelve thousand men. By the early seventeenth century it had grown to about forty thousand. The expansion was driven by Ottoman fiscal needs: as the timar-based feudal cavalry became less militarily relevant against gunpowder-armed European armies, the central government compensated by expanding the cash-paid Janissary corps. The expansion would, eventually, be the corps's undoing — by the eighteenth century, the Janissaries had become a hereditary urban guild more interested in protecting their pay and privileges than in military training. But in the period of Ottoman peak power (roughly 1450 to 1600), the Janissary corps was the most disciplined, best-trained, and best-equipped infantry force in the Mediterranean basin.

---

Now the recruitment system.

The **devshirme** (DEV-shir-meh, "collection" or "gathering" in Ottoman Turkish) was the institutionalized recruitment of Christian boys from the empire's Balkan provinces for conversion to Islam and service in the Ottoman state. It was a tribute, in the technical sense — a tax paid in human beings rather than in money or goods — and it was levied on the empire's Balkan Orthodox Christian populations approximately every four to seven years from roughly 1380 to 1638. By the time the system was effectively abandoned in the seventeenth century, it had brought somewhere between two hundred thousand and two hundred fifty thousand boys into the Ottoman state apparatus.

The mechanism was carefully regulated. A senior Ottoman official (typically a Janissary commander) would arrive in a Balkan province with an imperial warrant authorizing the levy. He would assemble the local Orthodox priests, who had access to baptismal records, and require them to identify boys aged eight to eighteen who met the criteria. The criteria were specific: only sons of Christian families, with a strong preference for second sons (the eldest was usually exempt to keep the family's farm running), unmarried, physically sound, intelligent, of good character. Recruiters were specifically forbidden from taking only sons, married youths, married converts to Islam, gypsies, Jews, Armenians, or boys from urban families with administrative connections — the levy targeted the Orthodox Christian peasantry of the rural Balkans almost exclusively.

The selected boys were taken to assembly points (typically Edirne or Istanbul), examined by court officials, and divided into two streams.

The **first stream**, comprising the most physically capable but less academically promising, was assigned to **Turkish village families** in Anatolia for several years. Here the boys learned Turkish, basic Islam, agricultural and military skills. They were not slaves in this period — they lived as members of their host families — but they were under Ottoman state oversight and could not return home. After several years they were assembled at the imperial barracks in Istanbul and inducted into the Janissary corps as **acemi oğlanlar** (ah-jeh-MEE oh-lahn-LAR, "novice boys"), where they completed military training and were eventually assigned to active Janissary regiments.

The **second stream**, comprising the most intelligent and physically attractive, was sent directly to the **Enderun** — the **Inner Palace School** at Topkapı, established by Mehmed II around 1455 and refined by his successors. The Enderun was the elite educational institution of the empire. Its curriculum included Ottoman Turkish, Arabic, Persian, mathematics, calligraphy, music, archery, swordsmanship, riding, falconry, Islamic theology, history, and the elaborate court etiquette required for service in the imperial household. The school operated in conditions of strict discipline and genuine intellectual rigor. Pupils lived in the palace under the close supervision of senior pages and palace eunuchs. They progressed through a hierarchy of seven academic-administrative chambers, each with its own specialization. Graduation, after fourteen to twenty years of training, led to assignments in the imperial bureaucracy, the diplomatic corps, the provincial governorships, the military command structure, or the imperial household itself.

The most successful Enderun graduates rose to the highest offices of the empire. Of the approximately 215 grand viziers in Ottoman history, more than half were of devshirme origin. **Sokollu Mehmed Pasha** (so-kol-LOO meh-MED pah-SHAH, c. 1505–1579), who effectively ran the Ottoman state from the late 1560s until his assassination in 1579 and who was perhaps the greatest grand vizier of the entire imperial period, had been taken as a devshirme boy from a Bosnian Serbian Orthodox family in approximately 1518, at about age thirteen. **Pargalı Ibrahim Pasha** (par-gah-LUH ee-brah-HEEM, c. 1495–1536), Süleyman the Magnificent's favorite and grand vizier for thirteen years, had been taken from a Greek family from the western Greek coast. **Mimar Sinan**, the architect of the Süleymaniye and Selimiye mosques, was a devshirme graduate (Chapter 5). The military commanders, the grand admirals, the provincial governors — the highest administrative class of the empire — were largely a slave class, recruited as children from the conquered Balkan Christian populations and trained in the imperial palace.

The ethical complexity of this system was real and is difficult to summarize without sliding into either condemnation or apologetics. From a modern human-rights standpoint, the devshirme was a forced child levy, a violation of religious freedom, a separation of children from their families, and a system of slavery — even if the form of slavery was unusually privileged by historical standards. From a sixteenth-century perspective, the system offered the recruited boys access to opportunities that would have been unimaginable in their home villages: education, social mobility, careers at the highest levels of one of the world's great empires. There are well-documented cases of Balkan Christian families bribing Ottoman officials to *include* their sons in the devshirme rather than to exclude them. There are also well-documented cases of families hiding their sons, or marrying them off underage, to avoid the levy. Both responses existed simultaneously, and the relative balance varied by region, period, and family circumstance.

The boys themselves, once recruited, generally identified as Ottomans rather than as their birth peoples. The senior devshirme graduates were, by the mid-sixteenth century, the institutional core of the empire — a class with their own *esprit de corps*, their own marriage patterns (devshirme graduates often married daughters of the imperial family or of senior Ottoman aristocrats), and their own political interests. Many maintained connections with their home villages — sending money, building mosques and schools, occasionally returning to visit — but their primary loyalty was to the imperial system that had made them. The devshirme was, in this sense, a remarkably effective integration mechanism: it took the empire's most marginal subject population (rural Balkan Christian peasants) and transformed them, individually, into the empire's most powerful administrative class.

The system declined in the seventeenth century for reasons that had little to do with its inherent design. The Janissary corps gradually became hereditary — the legal prohibition against Janissaries marrying was relaxed, then ignored, and Janissary sons increasingly inherited their fathers' positions without going through devshirme training. The free Muslim population, growing in proportion to the Christian population, increasingly demanded access to Janissary careers and successfully pushed for inclusion. The administrative ranks were increasingly filled by free Muslim graduates of the empire's growing madrasa system rather than by devshirme alumni. By 1638 the regular devshirme levies had ended. By the late seventeenth century, the institution was effectively gone. What remained was a Janissary corps that had lost its slave-soldier basis, was no longer centrally trained, and was increasingly a hereditary urban guild — an institution that had outlived the recruitment system that had originally given it its quality.

---

Now the system that managed the empire's non-Muslim majority — and majority is the right word for most of the empire's territory and most of its history. The Ottoman Empire was, in 1500, about half non-Muslim, with substantial Greek Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Jewish, Coptic, Syriac Christian, and Druze populations. In 1700 it was still about forty percent non-Muslim. The system that managed this religious diversity was the **millet** (mil-LET, "nation" or "religious community" in Ottoman Turkish, from the Arabic *milla*).

The millet system was not a sudden invention. Its precedents went back to Mehmed II's appointment of **Patriarch Gennadios II** as the head of the Greek Orthodox community in 1454 (Byzantine Empire Ch 12), and beyond that to early Islamic precedents for organizing **dhimmi** (DIM-mee, "protected") non-Muslim populations under their own religious authorities. What Ottoman practice did was systematize and extend the model. By the sixteenth century, the major non-Muslim communities of the empire were organized into formally recognized millets, each headed by its religious leader, each enjoying internal autonomy in matters of personal status (marriage, divorce, inheritance, education), each responsible for collecting its own taxes for transmission to the imperial treasury, and each granted recognized legal protections.

The four classical millets were:

The **Rum millet** (the Greek Orthodox community, named *Rum* from the Arabic-Ottoman term for "Roman" — a designation that the Ottomans inherited from the Byzantine self-identification, Byzantine Empire Ch 1). The Patriarch of Constantinople was the head of the Rum millet, with jurisdiction over the Greek Orthodox populations of the empire — a community that included not only ethnic Greeks but Bulgarian, Serbian, Romanian, and Albanian Orthodox Christians (the national Orthodox patriarchates of Bulgaria, Serbia, and the Romanian principalities all came under the Constantinople patriarch's jurisdiction at various points in the Ottoman period). The Rum millet was the largest single non-Muslim community in the empire, with perhaps eight to ten million members at peak.

The **Armenian millet**. The Armenian Apostolic Church, with its own patriarch in Istanbul (separate from the Rum patriarch), governed the Armenian Christian population. The Armenian community was concentrated in eastern Anatolia, the Caucasus, and the major commercial cities (Istanbul, Izmir, Aleppo). It had its own ecclesiastical structure, its own legal traditions, and its own commercial networks — Armenian merchants dominated the empire's silk trade, the Persian-Indian trade routes, and substantial portions of the international banking system.

The **Jewish millet**. After the Sephardic resettlement under Bayezid II (Chapter 5), the Ottoman Jewish population was the largest in the world. It was organized under a chief rabbi (the **Hahambaşı**) in Istanbul, with regional chief rabbis in the major Jewish cities. The Jewish community combined Sephardic, Romaniote (the older Greek-speaking Jewish population that predated the Sephardic arrival), and Ashkenazi populations. Jewish merchants, doctors, financiers, and translators were enormously important in Ottoman commerce and diplomacy — the imperial physician was almost always Jewish from the late fifteenth through the seventeenth centuries.

A fourth, smaller community was the **Catholic millet**, organized later (formally in the early nineteenth century) for the Latin-rite Catholic populations — primarily Italian merchants in Istanbul and Izmir, and the Maronite Catholic communities of Lebanon. The Maronite-Catholic relationship with Rome and France gave this community a particular geopolitical weight that increased through the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.

Each millet operated according to its own internal religious law for matters within the community. A Greek Orthodox merchant disputing an inheritance with another Greek Orthodox merchant would have his case heard in the Greek Orthodox ecclesiastical court, applying canon law and Byzantine civil law (the tradition descending from Justinian's Corpus Juris Civilis was preserved in the Greek Orthodox legal system, Byzantine Empire Ch 12). An Armenian merchant marrying his daughter to another Armenian dealt with the Armenian church. A Jewish family sending a son to school dealt with the Jewish community organization. These were not merely private matters — they were legally recognized public functions of the religious authorities, with enforcement support from the Ottoman state.

Cross-community matters — disputes between members of different millets, criminal cases, taxation, property issues touching multiple communities — went to the Ottoman state courts, applying sharia and kanun. A Greek Orthodox merchant suing a Muslim merchant went to a sharia court. A Jewish-Armenian commercial partnership disputing a debt went to the Ottoman judge.

The millet system had important limitations that should not be glossed over. Non-Muslims paid additional taxes (the **jizya** head tax, the **harac** land tax). They could not, in principle, ride horses or bear arms. They were required to wear distinctive clothing or markings (the requirements varied by period and region; in some periods they were enforced strictly, in others ignored). Their houses could not be taller than Muslim houses on the same street. They could not testify against a Muslim in an Islamic court. New non-Muslim places of worship could not be built without sultanic permission, though existing ones were generally protected. Conversion from Islam to another religion was a capital offense (the reverse direction, conversion to Islam, was actively encouraged and rewarded). Non-Muslims were excluded from the Janissary corps after the early period, from the senior religious offices, and from the highest courtly positions.

But within these limits, the system offered protections that no contemporary Christian state matched. There were no Inquisitions in the Ottoman Empire. There were no organized expulsions of religious minorities (the Sephardic Jews fled *to* Ottoman lands, not from them). There were no forced mass conversions on the Spanish-Inquisition model. There were periodic local persecutions and occasional anti-Christian or anti-Jewish riots, but the systematic state-sponsored persecution that characterized parts of late medieval and early modern Christian Europe was not a feature of Ottoman governance. A Greek Orthodox Christian, an Armenian, or a Jew living in fifteenth- or sixteenth-century Ottoman Istanbul lived under more durable legal protections than the same person would have lived under in Catholic Madrid, Anglican London, or Lutheran Wittenberg.

The millet framework also explains how the empire could function across such enormous religious diversity for so long. By organizing non-Muslims as legally recognized corporate communities with internal autonomy, the Ottoman state outsourced the management of religious diversity to the religious communities themselves. The state did not have to adjudicate Greek Orthodox marriage disputes or Armenian inheritance cases or Jewish divorce procedures — those were handled by the relevant patriarch, archbishop, or rabbi, with the state providing only enforcement when needed. The system was administratively efficient, ideologically coherent (within the Islamic theological tradition of dhimmi protection), and remarkably stable. It survived for four centuries with substantial structural continuity.

---

Three of the most distinctive Ottoman institutions, then, share a common feature: they were ways of integrating populations the empire ruled but did not wish to assimilate.

The Janissary corps integrated young Christian boys into the empire's most elite military and administrative class, while keeping them as a structurally separate slave-soldier caste with their own discipline and esprit de corps.

The devshirme system extracted talented children from the Balkan Christian peasantry, converted them to Islam, trained them at imperial expense, and channeled them into careers at the highest levels of the state — while explicitly *not* requiring or expecting their birth communities to convert as a whole.

The millet system organized the empire's non-Muslim majority into recognized religious communities with internal autonomy, allowing them to maintain their religious, legal, and cultural identities under Ottoman sovereignty without forcing assimilation to Islamic practice.

Each system was, in its own way, a recognition that the empire's unity did not require the empire's homogeneity. The Ottoman approach was the opposite of the homogeneity-driven nation-state model that would emerge in nineteenth-century Europe. It was an imperial framework — pluralistic, hierarchical, multi-ethnic, multi-religious — that operated by managing differences rather than by erasing them.

This is also, ultimately, why the empire's nineteenth-century encounter with European nationalism would be so destabilizing (Chapters 9 and 10). The institutional framework that had managed religious and ethnic diversity for four centuries was specifically incompatible with the nation-state model that European powers were exporting to the Ottoman world. The Greek Orthodox communities of the empire, organized as a religious millet, would gradually transform under the influence of Greek nationalism into a national community demanding political independence. The same would happen, in different forms, for Bulgarians, Serbs, Romanians, Armenians, and eventually Arabs and Turks themselves. The institutional brilliance of the millet system in 1500 was its capacity to manage diversity without political fragmentation. The institutional brittleness of the same system in 1850 was its incapacity to absorb the new ideology of national self-determination.

The framework that had been the empire's strength for four hundred years became the empire's weakness in its last century.

# Chapter 7 — The Sultanate of Women and the Cage

Süleyman the Magnificent died on September 6, 1566, at age seventy-two, in his command tent during the siege of Szigetvár in southern Hungary. The grand vizier Sokollu Mehmed Pasha kept his death secret from the army for six weeks, finishing the siege successfully and delivering the body to Istanbul for burial in Süleyman's own mosque complex.

The empire passed smoothly to Süleyman's son **Selim II** (ruled 1566–1574). It would never again be ruled by a sultan who personally led major battlefield campaigns. Of the eighteen Ottoman sultans between Selim II and the empire's collapse, perhaps two — Murad III's son Ahmed I (briefly) and Mahmud II in the early nineteenth century — exercised the kind of personal political mastery that Süleyman had embodied. Most of the rest were figureheads. Some were children. Some were mentally unstable. Some were conscientious but unprepared. None had come of age on military campaign with a senior commander as a tutor, the way Süleyman had come of age riding with his father Selim I and his grandfather Bayezid II.

What replaced sultanic personal rule, in the period from 1566 to roughly 1700, was a different model of governance: a court-based bureaucratic system in which the grand vizier ran the imperial administration on a daily basis, the harem ran the politics of dynastic succession, and the senior religious authority (the Sheikh ul-Islam) ran the religious-legal apparatus. The sultan in this system was a ceremonial-religious figure whose personal capacity mattered less than the court coalition supporting him. The system worked surprisingly well for surprisingly long. It also produced two distinctive features that Western observers found scandalous and that Ottoman traditionalists found troubling: the **Sultanate of Women** and the **Cage system**. Both deserve careful attention, because they reshaped how Ottoman power operated for over a century, and because the conventional Western narratives about them are usually more dramatic than accurate.

---

The **Sultanate of Women** (*Kadınlar Saltanatı* in Ottoman Turkish) was the period from approximately 1533 to 1683 during which the women of the imperial harem — particularly the **valide sultans** (vah-LEE-deh sool-TAHNS, "mother sultans" — the mothers of reigning sultans) and senior favorites — exercised major political power, often dominating decisions on appointments, foreign policy, taxation, and succession.

The phenomenon began with Hürrem Sultan, Süleyman the Magnificent's wife (Chapter 5). Hürrem was the first formally married wife of an Ottoman sultan since the early fifteenth century, and her elevation to that status broke the previous pattern in which the mother of a potential heir held political power only in proportion to her son's prospects. Hürrem held political power because Süleyman loved her, listened to her, and brought her into the inner circle of his policy-making. Her surviving correspondence with Süleyman, with her son-in-law (the grand vizier **Rüstem Pasha**, who was married to Hürrem and Süleyman's daughter Mihrimah), and with various foreign monarchs (she corresponded with the Polish queen, with the Persian shahbanu) shows her engaged in detailed political calculation. She was not running the empire from behind the scenes, but she was a senior member of the policy-making apparatus, and her political weight was unprecedented.

After Hürrem's death in 1558, her institutional precedent persisted. The senior women of the harem — the valide sultan, the favored consorts of the reigning sultan, and the sisters and daughters of the imperial family — continued to operate as a coordinated political bloc. Their power varied with the personal capacity of each sultan and the specific dynamics of each generation, but the basic pattern was set.

The two figures who most clearly embody the Sultanate of Women are **Nurbanu Sultan** and **Kösem Sultan**, the latter of whom is among the most consequential individual figures in seventeenth-century Ottoman history.

**Nurbanu Sultan** (NOOR-bah-noo, c. 1525–1583) was a Venetian-Greek noblewoman, born **Cecilia Venier-Baffo** on the Aegean island of Paros, captured by Ottoman corsairs as a teenager, brought to the imperial harem in Istanbul, and rising through the harem hierarchy to become the favored consort of Selim II. When Selim II died in 1574, her son **Murad III** took the throne and Nurbanu became valide sultan. She held that position until her death in 1583, during which she effectively shared decision-making authority with her son and the grand vizier. Her surviving political correspondence — including a remarkable exchange with Catherine de' Medici of France — shows her negotiating Ottoman foreign policy directly. She is also notable for her sustained patronage of mosque construction; the **Atik Valide Mosque** in Istanbul, built by Mimar Sinan in 1583, was her commission.

**Kösem Sultan** (KUH-sem, c. 1589–1651) is the most consequential of the harem-based political figures. Born **Anastasia** to a Greek Orthodox priest's family on the island of Tinos in the Aegean, captured around age fifteen and brought to the harem, she became the favored consort of **Ahmed I** (ruled 1603–1617). She had multiple sons by Ahmed I. After Ahmed's death she became the valide sultan when her son **Murad IV** took the throne in 1623, retained influence under her son **Ibrahim** (ruled 1640–1648), and emerged as the senior valide sultan during the minority of her grandson **Mehmed IV** in 1648. For most of the period from 1623 to her assassination in 1651, Kösem was the most politically powerful figure in the empire. She managed grand vizier appointments, controlled foreign policy negotiations, mediated between rival factions of the Janissary corps and the religious establishment, and personally directed major imperial decisions during the chaotic mid-seventeenth century.

Kösem's reign within the harem is also notable for its internal contestation. When her grandson Mehmed IV came to the throne in 1648 at age six, his mother **Turhan Sultan** (a Ukrainian-born harem favorite, of similar background to Kösem herself) became the formal valide sultan. Kösem, as the senior valide of the previous generation, retained substantial independent authority. The two women became rival power centers within the harem, and the conflict was eventually resolved by Kösem's assassination in September 1651 — strangled in her bedchamber, on the orders or at minimum the approval of Turhan Sultan, by harem eunuchs and senior court officials. Kösem was approximately sixty-two years old. She had been a major political figure for over forty years, the longest individual political career of any woman in Ottoman history.

The Sultanate of Women is sometimes treated, in older Ottoman and European historiography, as a corrupting feminization of imperial politics that was a major cause of Ottoman decline. This narrative is not credible on closer inspection. The harem-based political system worked well or poorly depending on who was running it, in exactly the same way the sultan-led political system had worked well or poorly depending on who was sultan. The genuinely effective valide sultans (Nurbanu, Kösem in her better moments, Turhan) ran the empire competently through periods when the formal sultan was a child or otherwise incapacitated. The genuinely incompetent ones (and there were several) damaged the empire in proportion to their power.

What the Sultanate of Women does represent is a genuine institutional shift: from rule by warrior-sultans on military campaign, to rule by court-based coalitions in which the harem was a major node. The shift was not a corruption of an earlier ideal. It was an adaptation to the realities of governing a fully formed multicontinental empire from a single capital, where the sultan's capacity to lead campaigns in person had become physically and politically impractical. The court-based system was, in its way, a more institutional and less personal form of governance — one that relied on collective decision-making among the senior figures of the imperial household rather than on individual sultanic genius.

---

The other distinctive institutional development of this period was the **Cage system** (Turkish *Kafes*, kah-FESS, "cage").

The system needs to be understood against the backdrop of the **fratricide law** that Mehmed II had codified in 1477 (Chapter 4). For the previous century and a half, the standard procedure on the accession of a new Ottoman sultan had been the immediate execution of his brothers and nephews — typically by strangulation with a silken bowstring — to prevent succession wars. The procedure was institutionally efficient. It also became increasingly difficult to justify as the empire's fertility patterns produced more royal sons. **Mehmed III**, on his accession in 1595, executed nineteen of his brothers in a single ceremony. The strangulation of nineteen children at a single moment — the youngest were toddlers — generated enormous popular outrage. Even the religious establishment, which had endorsed the fratricide law for over a century, expressed concern.

The institutional response, formalized under **Ahmed I** in 1603, was the **Cage system**. Instead of executing all of the new sultan's brothers and nephews, they would be confined to a specific apartment complex within Topkapı Palace — the **Kafes** — where they would live under permanent house arrest, with limited contact with the outside world. They would receive personal servants (carefully selected to be loyal to the reigning sultan), food, books, music, and certain harem privileges. They would be denied military training, administrative responsibility, contact with potential supporters, and any role in state affairs. They would remain in the Kafes for their entire adult lives, unless circumstances required them to be brought out — most commonly when the reigning sultan died without an adult heir, in which case the most senior surviving brother or uncle from the Kafes would be brought out, dressed in imperial robes, and elevated to the throne.

The Cage system was less brutal than mass fratricide. It was also institutionally disastrous, in ways that took several generations to become apparent.

A sultan who had spent his entire adult life — sometimes thirty or forty years — in a small palace apartment with eunuch servants and decorative concubines was not prepared to govern an empire when the throne suddenly fell to him. **Ibrahim I** (ruled 1640–1648), brought out of the Kafes at age twenty-five after his elder brother Murad IV had unexpectedly died, was so traumatized by the transition that contemporary sources describe him as initially refusing to leave his room — he was convinced the messengers announcing his accession were assassins sent by his brother to lure him to his death. When he did emerge, his subsequent eight-year reign was characterized by mental instability, erratic decision-making, and a court life dominated by a series of harem favorites whose political influence was even more direct than that of the formal valide sultans of previous generations. He was eventually deposed by a coalition of the Janissaries, the religious establishment, and his own mother Kösem, and executed in August 1648.

**Mustafa I** (ruled 1617–1618 and 1622–1623) had been confined to the Kafes for fourteen years before his first accession at age twenty-six. His mental state was such that he could barely speak coherently in his first audiences. He was deposed within ninety-six days, sent back to the Kafes, brought out a second time four years later when **Osman II** was assassinated, found again to be incapable of governing, and deposed a second time after sixteen months. He died in the Kafes a few years later. The historical sources are sympathetic to him — he was clearly a victim of the institutional system rather than an architect of his own incapacity — but he was not a functional sultan, and his two brief reigns were periods of administrative paralysis.

The aggregate effect of the Cage system, across the seventeenth century, was a dynasty of sultans whose preparation for office was systematically inadequate. Some of them were intelligent men whose intelligence had been wasted by decades of confinement. Some had genuine mental illness exacerbated by the conditions of their captivity. None had any practical experience of administration, military command, foreign relations, or even routine social interaction with people of varied status. The court-based system of governance — the grand vizier, the valide sultan, the senior bureaucrats — adapted to fill the vacuum, and the empire continued to function. But the institutional brittleness was real, and the consequences would compound over time.

---

The political narrative of the post-Süleyman period needs to be summarized briefly, because it sets the stage for the empire's first major territorial losses.

**Selim II** (1566–1574), called **the Sot** for his serious drinking problem, ruled through his grand vizier Sokollu Mehmed Pasha. The major event of his reign was the **Battle of Lepanto** on October 7, 1571 — the largest naval battle in the Mediterranean in centuries, in which a coalition Christian fleet under the Spanish prince **Don John of Austria** destroyed the Ottoman Mediterranean fleet off the western coast of Greece. The Christian victory was real — perhaps two hundred Ottoman galleys sunk or captured, twenty-five thousand Ottoman sailors and soldiers killed — but its strategic consequences were limited. The Ottoman state rebuilt its fleet within a year. Sokollu reportedly remarked to a Venetian ambassador that "in cutting off our beard, you have only made it grow back stronger; in cutting off your arm at Cyprus, we have done you permanent harm." Cyprus, taken by the Ottomans in 1570 just before Lepanto, remained Ottoman for the next three centuries. Lepanto's psychological impact on Christian Europe was enormous; its operational impact on Ottoman power was modest.

**Murad III** (1574–1595) ruled through Sokollu until the grand vizier's assassination in 1579, then through a series of less competent successors. The **Long Turkish War** with the Habsburgs (1593–1606) was the first major Ottoman conflict in which the empire's military advantage over the Habsburg armies had clearly disappeared — the war ended in stalemate with the **Treaty of Zsitvatorok**, the first treaty in which the Ottomans formally recognized the Habsburg Holy Roman Emperor as an equal sovereign rather than a tributary.

**Mehmed III** (1595–1603) executed his nineteen brothers on his accession (described above). His death in 1603 led to the formal institution of the Cage system under his son Ahmed I.

**Ahmed I** (1603–1617) was sympathetic, religiously sincere, and relatively competent. His commission of the **Sultan Ahmed Mosque** (the **Blue Mosque**) — built by Sinan's pupil **Sedefkar Mehmed Aga** between 1609 and 1616, with six minarets and an interior covered in over twenty thousand blue Iznik tiles — produced one of the architectural climaxes of the late classical Ottoman period. The Blue Mosque was deliberately built directly opposite Hagia Sophia, on the site of the former Byzantine Great Palace, with the intent of creating a paired architectural complex unmatched in any contemporary capital.

**Murad IV** (1623–1640) was the last Ottoman sultan to lead a major field campaign in person. He took **Baghdad** back from the Safavids in 1638 — the city had been lost to Shah Abbas I in 1623 — and stabilized the eastern frontier. He was also a notably violent ruler, given to executing courtiers and citizens in person on minor pretexts, and his reign produced widespread popular fear of the imperial court. He died at age twenty-seven, possibly from cirrhosis (he had inherited the family drinking problem) or possibly from the effects of long-term mercury treatment for syphilis.

After Murad IV came the disastrous Ibrahim I (described above), and after Ibrahim came the long minority of **Mehmed IV** (1648–1687), during which the empire was rescued from administrative collapse by the institutional intervention of a single grand-vizieral family.

---

The **Köprülü** (KUH-pruh-loo) family was a late-flowering example of Ottoman administrative recovery. The patriarch, **Mehmed Pasha Köprülü** (c. 1575–1661, of Albanian-Christian devshirme origin), was appointed grand vizier in September 1656 at age eighty-one, on conditions that he himself dictated to the court: full authority over all imperial appointments, the right to confiscate property without specific imperial sanction, immunity from harem political interference, and complete control of military operations. The Köprülü appointment was, in effect, an institutional recognition that the empire was being run by the central court coalition rather than by the sultan personally, and that decisive grand-vizieral authority was needed to halt the administrative drift.

Mehmed Köprülü ran the empire for five years until his death in 1661. He executed several thousand officials he considered corrupt or disloyal. He ended the Cretan War of attrition with a Venetian peace settlement that recovered most of Crete. He restored the empire's finances. He was succeeded by his son **Fazıl Ahmed Pasha Köprülü** (1635–1676), who continued the same authoritarian-reform pattern for fifteen years and is sometimes considered the most effective grand vizier of the late seventeenth century. Together, the two Köprülüs gave the empire what amounted to a generation of competent central administration, during which the territorial losses of the previous decades were halted and some recoveries were made.

By the early 1680s, the empire under the Köprülü system was institutionally stable, militarily formidable, and apparently positioned for renewed expansion. The grand vizier in 1683 was **Kara Mustafa Pasha** (KAH-rah moos-tah-FAH, son-in-law of Mehmed Köprülü, married to a Köprülü daughter, considered a Köprülü-faction figure even though he was not a blood relative of the dynasty). He decided that the moment had come to do what no Ottoman commander since Süleyman the Magnificent had attempted: to take Vienna.

The siege he launched in July 1683 would be the turning point of the empire's three-and-a-half-century military trajectory. The next chapter is its story.

Cross-civ check: the seventeenth century was the period of the **Thirty Years' War** in central Europe (1618–1648), the **English Civil War** (1642–1651), the **Manchu conquest** of Ming China and the founding of the **Qing dynasty** (1644 onward), the **Mughal Empire**'s peak under Aurangzeb (after 1658), and the consolidation of the **Tokugawa Shogunate** in Japan. The Ottoman seventeenth century was, by contrast, a period of court-based bureaucratic governance under sultans of varying capacity, with the Köprülü family providing the institutional rescue at the end. By the standards of seventeenth-century governance worldwide, this was not exceptionally bad. By the standards of Süleyman's reign a century earlier, it was a long disappointment.

---

# Chapter 8 — The Long Defeat

The story of Ottoman territorial decline is conventionally dated from 1683 and the failure to take Vienna. The decline was real, but the causal story is more complex than a single battle. What changed in the late seventeenth century was not the empire's military capacity in isolation — Ottoman armies remained large, well-equipped, and capable of defeating most non-Habsburg European forces well into the eighteenth century. What changed was the relative balance with the Christian European powers, which were experiencing a sustained military, fiscal, and technological transformation that the Ottoman state could not match. The empire did not collapse. It stopped advancing. Eventually, after a series of major wars, it began to retreat.

This chapter covers the period from 1683 to 1730, which is the period during which the empire's strategic posture shifted from offensive to defensive, during which it lost its first major territories to Christian powers in over three centuries, and during which the first hesitant attempts at institutional reform began. It is not a period of dramatic catastrophe. It is a period of slow, sustained pressure that the Ottoman institutional system could not relieve.

---

The **Second Siege of Vienna** began on July 14, 1683.

Kara Mustafa Pasha had marched a massive Ottoman army out of Belgrade in May, gathering vassal contingents along the way (Crimean Tatars, Wallachian and Moldavian forces, Hungarian rebel units under **Imre Thököly**, who had risen against Habsburg rule and made common cause with the Ottomans). The total force, by early July, was approximately one hundred and twenty thousand men with three hundred cannon — the largest Ottoman field army assembled since Süleyman's day. The Habsburg Emperor **Leopold I** evacuated Vienna with his court on July 7, leaving the city in the hands of a garrison commander named **Ernst Rüdiger von Starhemberg** with about eleven thousand soldiers and the Viennese militia.

The siege lasted just over two months. The Ottoman engineering corps dug parallel siege trenches in textbook fashion, brought their cannons within range of the city walls, and began systematic bombardment. By mid-September the Ottoman mines had brought down significant sections of the walls, and the city was on the verge of falling. The defenders were exhausted, food was running short, and the next coordinated Ottoman assault was likely to succeed.

What broke the siege was a relief army.

Pope **Innocent XI** had spent the previous year mobilizing a coalition of Christian powers — the **Holy League** — to defend Vienna and roll back Ottoman power in central Europe. The coalition included Habsburg Austria, Poland-Lithuania, several German principalities, Venice, and (later) Russia. The largest field component was the Polish army under King **John III Sobieski** (yon SOH-byes-kee), one of the most effective commanders in late seventeenth-century Europe. Sobieski's force linked up with German contingents under **Charles V of Lorraine** at the Danube north of Vienna in early September and marched south to relieve the city.

On September 12, 1683, the combined Christian relief army — about eighty-five thousand men — attacked the Ottoman besieging force from the rear and right flank, on the slopes of the **Kahlenberg** (a hill northwest of Vienna). The attack caught Kara Mustafa unprepared. He had assumed that the Ottoman covering screen would delay any relief force long enough for him to take the city first; he had not accounted for the speed of the Christian coalition mobilization or for the tactical flexibility of Sobieski's hussar cavalry. The Ottoman besieging army was routed within several hours. Sobieski's famous letter to the Pope — "*Venimus, vidimus, Deus vicit*," "We came, we saw, God conquered" — became one of the iconic statements of the Christian-Ottoman conflict.

The casualties were substantial — perhaps fifteen thousand Ottomans killed, including most of the senior staff officers and a significant portion of the artillery park. The withdrawal was disorganized. The siege equipment, the camp treasury, and Kara Mustafa's personal tent (with its luxurious furnishings, including the green ceremonial banner given to him by the sultan) were all captured. The relief armies followed up with a counter-offensive that pushed the Ottoman frontier deep south of the Danube within weeks.

Kara Mustafa retreated to Belgrade, where on December 25, 1683, he was strangled with a silken bowstring on the orders of Sultan Mehmed IV — the standard Ottoman fate for grand viziers who had presided over major military disasters. His head was sent to Istanbul as proof of execution. The Köprülü era of competent grand-vizieral governance had ended in catastrophe.

The Second Siege of Vienna, in retrospect, was the high-water mark of Ottoman expansion. The empire would never again threaten central Europe with serious force. The military momentum that had carried the Ottoman frontier across the Balkans and into Hungary for three hundred years had finally exhausted itself.

---

What followed was the **Great Turkish War** of 1683–1699, in which the Holy League pursued the Ottoman retreat across Hungary, the Peloponnese, the Crimea, and Wallachia. The Habsburg armies, freed from the Vienna siege and reinforced with imperial troops, took **Buda** in 1686 — ending one hundred and forty-five years of Ottoman rule over the Hungarian capital. **Belgrade** fell in 1688 (the Ottomans recovered it in 1690 in a brief counteroffensive, then lost it again later). The Venetians recovered most of the **Peloponnese** in 1685–1687. Russian forces under Peter the Great's predecessors began pushing south against the Crimean Tatars and the Black Sea Ottoman provinces.

The war was the longest single conflict in Ottoman history (sixteen years) and one of the most disruptive. The empire lost not just territory but operational confidence. The Janissary corps mutinied repeatedly during the war, in some cases refusing to leave Istanbul to fight. Provincial governors increasingly operated as semi-independent warlords. The treasury was emptied. The currency, the silver akce that had been the empire's standard for three centuries, was debased to roughly one-third of its former silver content. The administrative coherence of the empire, which had been the legacy of the Köprülü reforms, fragmented under the pressure.

The war ended at the **Treaty of Karlowitz** (now Sremski Karlovci in Serbia) in January 1699. The treaty was negotiated under English and Dutch mediation — itself a remarkable indicator of the empire's diminished diplomatic position. Under its terms:

The Ottomans ceded **Hungary and Transylvania** to the Habsburgs (apart from a small slice retained around Belgrade and the Banat).

They ceded **Podolia** (in modern western Ukraine) to Poland-Lithuania.

They ceded most of the **Peloponnese**, the Aegean islands of Aegina and Lefkada, and the Dalmatian coast hinterland to Venice.

They ceded **Azov** (on the Black Sea coast at the mouth of the Don) to Russia.

The territorial losses were the first substantial Ottoman losses to Christian powers since the empire's founding three centuries earlier. The European frontier had moved south for the first time in Ottoman history.

What the Treaty of Karlowitz also represented, for the first time, was an Ottoman acknowledgment that they were now operating in the same diplomatic system as their European counterparts — bound by treaty obligations, subject to international mediation, fighting wars that ended in negotiated settlements rather than in the surrender of a defeated enemy. The Westphalian state system that had emerged in central Europe after the Thirty Years' War was being extended to the Ottoman frontier. The empire had to learn to operate within it.

---

The empire's first response to Karlowitz was a generation of attempted reform under a new grand vizier — and the political ambivalence of that response is the second main feature of this chapter.

**Sultan Ahmed III** (ruled 1703–1730) presided over the period that Ottoman historians call the **Lale Devri** — the **Tulip Era** — running roughly from 1718 to 1730. The era took its name from the fashion among the Ottoman elite for elaborate tulip cultivation, with new tulip varieties bred competitively at imperial gardens, individual prized bulbs sold for prices that would have astonished any seventeenth-century Dutch tulip speculator, and entire palace complexes designed around tulip gardens for nighttime viewing illuminated by candles set on the backs of trained tortoises (a detail that the European visitors of the period reported with disbelief but which the Ottoman sources confirm). The court of Ahmed III at the **Sa'dabad Palace**, built on the upper Golden Horn in 1722–1723 in a deliberately European-influenced style, was the most cosmopolitan and most leisure-oriented Ottoman court since Süleyman.

The grand vizier driving the Tulip Era's cultural programs was **Damat Ibrahim Pasha** (DAH-mahd ee-brah-HEEM, "Ibrahim the Son-in-Law," married to a daughter of Ahmed III), who had concluded the **Treaty of Passarowitz** with Austria and Venice in 1718 (which had cost the empire additional Balkan and Aegean territories but had stabilized the frontier) and who was using the post-treaty peace to attempt a series of cautious modernizing reforms. The reforms were ambivalent — partly serious institutional engagement with European models, partly aristocratic fashion-following, partly genuine cultural openness to Western European arts and sciences.

The most consequential of the Tulip Era reforms was the introduction of **printing** to the Ottoman Empire.

The Ottoman delay in adopting printing technology is one of the more discussed features of the empire's institutional history, and it is worth a brief explanation. Printing presses had been operating in Ottoman territory since the late fifteenth century, but only for the empire's non-Muslim communities. Sephardic Jewish printers had been printing Hebrew books in Salonica and Istanbul since the 1490s. Greek Orthodox printers had been printing Greek liturgical books from the early sixteenth century. Armenian printers had operated since 1567. What was prohibited was the printing of texts in Arabic script — books in Arabic, Persian, and Ottoman Turkish, the three languages of Islamic religious scholarship and Ottoman administrative practice. The prohibition was not absolute legal ban but a combination of factors: the religious establishment's preference for traditional manuscript culture (which had developed sophisticated calligraphic standards over centuries and which was associated with the manual labor of religious scribes), a guild-economic interest of the manuscript copyists' guild in Istanbul (estimated at perhaps eighty thousand professional copyists in the seventeenth century), and a general administrative caution about the reproduction-control implications of printed texts in a multi-religious empire.

The first Ottoman press in Arabic-script Turkish was established by **Ibrahim Müteferrika** (mu-teh-fer-ree-KAH, c. 1674–1745), a Hungarian-born Ottoman convert to Islam who had taken Ottoman service and worked as a translator and diplomatic functionary. With imperial endorsement, Müteferrika opened his press in Istanbul in 1727 and began producing Ottoman Turkish books — primarily history, geography, and political science. His first major publication was a Turkish translation of a classical Arabic dictionary; his most famous was the *Cihannüma* (the **Universal Geography**), a comprehensive geographical reference incorporating recent European cartographic knowledge. Müteferrika's press operated successfully for fifteen years, producing seventeen titles in twenty-three volumes. After his death in 1745 the press was inactive for several decades before being restarted under different management. Ottoman printing remained a niche activity until the early nineteenth century, when commercial pressures from European imports and state-sponsored newspapers finally drove the institutional adoption of large-scale printing.

The Tulip Era also produced the empire's first systematic engagement with European scientific and military literature. Ottoman diplomats sent to Paris, Vienna, and Madrid produced detailed reports on European institutions, technology, and military organization — the **Yirmisekiz Çelebi Mehmed Efendi** mission to Paris in 1720–1721, which produced the **Sefaretname** (a diplomatic memoir of the Paris embassy that became one of the most-read Ottoman travel books of the eighteenth century), is the most famous example. Translations of European technical works on mathematics, fortification, and gunnery began to appear in Ottoman scholarly libraries.

But the reforms were also resented. The Tulip Era's conspicuous luxury — the elaborate gardens, the European-style architecture, the imported French goods, the social practices borrowed from foreign embassies — alienated the Janissary corps, the religious establishment, and the urban poor. The economic crisis of the late 1720s (a combination of currency debasement, inflation, and a major plague outbreak in 1726) sharpened the political tension. The Janissaries, who had been losing their military relevance for over a century but retained their nuisance value as an urban political force, were particularly hostile to a reform program that they correctly perceived as eventually intended to replace them.

---

In September 1730, a Janissary irregular named **Patrona Halil** — an Albanian-born former bath attendant turned Janissary auxiliary — organized a popular uprising in Istanbul. The trigger was a combination of war taxes (Ahmed III had ordered a campaign against Persia, requiring new levies), economic distress, and accumulated resentment of the Tulip Era's conspicuous court culture. The uprising spread rapidly through the city. Within forty-eight hours, the Sa'dabad Palace had been sacked, the Tulip gardens burned, and thirty senior officials of the Damat Ibrahim Pasha government — including Ibrahim himself — executed by the mob. Sultan Ahmed III was deposed and confined to the Kafes (where he died eight years later). His nephew **Mahmud I** was placed on the throne.

The **Patrona Halil revolt** ended the Tulip Era abruptly and demonstrated the political limits of top-down modernization in the Ottoman context. The revolt was suppressed within several months — Patrona Halil himself was arrested and executed in November 1730 — but the damage had been done. The reforms were rolled back. The reformers were dead. The empire's first sustained engagement with European institutional models had failed. For the next half-century, no Ottoman administration would attempt comparably ambitious reforms.

The empire entered the eighteenth century with intact institutions but diminished capacity. It would lose more wars to Russia and Austria over the next several decades. It would cede further territories. It would sink, gradually, into the strategic position that nineteenth-century European observers would call **the Sick Man of Europe**.

The institutional rescue, when it eventually came, would be more radical than anything Damat Ibrahim Pasha had attempted, and it would arrive only after the Ottoman state had run out of other options.

---

# Chapter 9 — Reform and Backlash

The hundred years between the Patrona Halil revolt of 1730 and the formal launch of the Tanzimat reforms in 1839 are, in Ottoman historiography, the period of the empire's deepest institutional crisis. The territorial losses continued. The economic gap with western Europe widened. The administrative coherence of the central state weakened, replaced increasingly by the autonomous power of provincial governors (the **ayan**) and the religious establishment. Three sultans in this period — Selim III, Mahmud II, and Abdülmecid I — attempted serious institutional reform. The first was killed for it. The second succeeded by destroying his own elite military corps. The third launched the most sustained reform program in Ottoman history.

This chapter is their story. It is also the story of the institutional violence required to break the empire out of its eighteenth-century paralysis — and of the limits of that violence, since the reforms ultimately failed to halt the empire's geopolitical decline.

---

The eighteenth century brought a series of military defeats that reshaped Ottoman strategic geography.

The **Russo-Turkish War of 1768–1774** ended with the **Treaty of Küçük Kaynarca** (kuh-CHOOK ky-nar-JAH), one of the most consequential treaties in Ottoman history. Russia, under Empress Catherine the Great, had defeated the Ottomans repeatedly in Romania, the Crimea, and the Aegean (where the Russian fleet had sailed all the way from the Baltic to defeat the Ottoman navy at the **Battle of Çeşme** in 1770). The treaty's terms included Ottoman cession of significant Black Sea territories, Russian recognition as protector of Ottoman Orthodox Christians (a provision that would be invoked repeatedly over the next century to justify Russian intervention in Ottoman internal affairs), and the practical Russian annexation of the Crimea (formalized in 1783).

The loss of the Crimea was strategically devastating. The **Khanate of Crimea**, an Ottoman vassal since the late fifteenth century, had been the Tatar northern bulwark of the empire — a horse-warrior society that had repeatedly raided Russian and Polish-Lithuanian territory and whose armies had supplemented Ottoman field forces in Balkan campaigns. With Crimea gone, the Ottoman Black Sea was an open sea, and the empire's northern frontier was permanently vulnerable.

The Russo-Turkish War of 1787–1792, the Greek revolutionary disturbances in the Aegean of the late 1820s, the loss of Algiers to France in 1830, and successive smaller wars and territorial adjustments through the early nineteenth century all extended the same pattern. The empire was losing wars to states it had once dominated. The institutional explanation was clear to senior Ottoman observers: the empire's military and administrative systems, designed in the sixteenth century, could not match the more centralized, technologically advanced, and bureaucratically rationalized states that European modernization had produced.

The first sultan to attempt a serious institutional response was **Selim III** (ruled 1789–1807).

---

Selim III had been deeply read in European political and military literature during his time in the Kafes (he was confined there from age four to age twenty-eight, during the long reign of his uncle Abdülhamid I). He came to the throne convinced that the empire required fundamental institutional reform along European lines. His reform program, called the **Nizam-ı Cedid** (nih-ZAHM-uh jeh-DEED, "the New Order"), was the most ambitious modernization project the empire had attempted.

The centerpiece was a new infantry corps — also called the Nizam-ı Cedid — recruited from free Anatolian Muslim peasants, trained on European models with European drillmasters (mostly French officers brought in for the purpose), equipped with modern uniforms and modern firearms, and paid from a separate dedicated treasury. The corps was deliberately structured to be parallel to and eventually a replacement for the Janissaries, whose military uselessness was now obvious to everyone but who still constituted the empire's largest single military expenditure.

Selim III also reformed the timar-based provincial cavalry system, attempted to standardize tax collection across the empire, opened technical schools for naval engineering and military medicine, established new printing operations, and sent diplomatic missions to Paris, London, Vienna, and Berlin with instructions to study and report on European administrative practice. The first permanent Ottoman embassies in European capitals dated from his reign — a major institutional innovation, since Ottoman diplomatic practice had previously relied on temporary missions rather than resident representation.

The Janissaries hated all of it. The Nizam-ı Cedid was a direct existential threat to their institutional position. The reform-funding diversions from the central treasury reduced their pay. The new bureaucratic procedures eroded their political influence. The European-trained officer corps was a competing source of military prestige. By the early 1800s, the Janissary corps in Istanbul and across the empire was openly hostile to the entire reform program.

In 1807, a Janissary uprising in Istanbul, supported by the religious establishment and several senior Ottoman officials who had personal grievances against Selim's reform circle, deposed Selim III and placed his cousin Mustafa IV on the throne. Selim was confined to the Kafes. The Nizam-ı Cedid was dissolved. The reform program was cancelled. Several months later, in a counter-coup organized by reform-aligned officials, Mustafa IV ordered Selim's execution to prevent his restoration. Selim III was murdered in the Kafes in July 1808.

The counter-coup that killed Selim also deposed Mustafa IV, executed him a few months later, and placed his half-brother **Mahmud II** on the throne. Mahmud was twenty-three years old. He had spent his childhood in the Kafes. He had been an admirer of his cousin Selim's reform program. And he had just witnessed, in concentrated form, the institutional violence that any serious reform attempt would require.

---

**Mahmud II** (ruled 1808–1839) is one of the most consequential Ottoman rulers of the late empire, and his reign is best understood as a long, patient preparation for a single decisive act.

The act was the destruction of the Janissary corps.

The institutional analysis Mahmud II conducted in the early years of his reign was clear-eyed. The Janissaries were the central obstacle to any serious modernization. Their political power was largely a nuisance value rather than military value — they were no longer effective field troops by the early nineteenth century — but their capacity to organize urban riots, to depose sultans, and to mobilize the religious establishment against reform programs had been demonstrated repeatedly. Selim III had tried to bypass the Janissaries by building parallel reformed units. The strategy had failed. Mahmud II concluded that the only solution was complete elimination.

The preparation took eighteen years.

Mahmud II spent the period from 1808 to 1826 carefully building his political base. He consolidated personal control over the central administration. He secured the support of the senior religious establishment by pursuing an explicitly Sunni-orthodox religious policy and by patronizing major religious scholars. He cultivated alliances with the **ayan** — the provincial notables whose autonomous power had grown during the eighteenth century — by formally recognizing their local authority in exchange for military and fiscal cooperation. He rebuilt the central artillery corps under reformed leadership. Critically, he worked carefully and quietly with reform-minded Janissary officers to identify and replace the corps's most committed traditionalists, while leaving the bulk of the corps unaware of what was being prepared.

In June 1826 Mahmud announced a new reform: a parallel infantry unit called the **Eşkinciyan** (esh-kin-jee-YAHN), structured similarly to Selim III's Nizam-ı Cedid but with stronger institutional backing. The Janissaries — predictably — interpreted the announcement as a threat and began organizing a revolt.

On June 14 and 15, the Janissary corps in Istanbul rose in armed rebellion, exactly as Mahmud had calculated they would. They occupied their barracks, issued demands for the cancellation of the new corps, and prepared to march on the imperial palace in the standard pattern of Janissary revolts going back centuries. What they did not know was that Mahmud had spent the previous several years preparing for this exact moment.

The Sheikh ul-Islam issued a religious **fatwa** declaring the Janissary revolt to be religiously illegitimate. Mahmud's reformed artillery corps surrounded the Janissary barracks and bombarded them with cannon fire — including the use of grapeshot at point-blank range when individual Janissaries tried to escape. The barracks were systematically destroyed. The Janissaries inside were killed or burned alive. Across Istanbul over the following days, identifiable Janissaries were rounded up and executed; their bodies were left in public places. Janissary officers in provincial garrisons were arrested and executed in coordinated operations across the empire over the following weeks.

The death toll has never been precisely established. Modern estimates range from six thousand to twenty thousand Janissaries killed in Istanbul alone during the four-day **Auspicious Incident** (*Vaka-yı Hayriye*, vah-kah-yuh hi-REE-yeh — the Ottoman official designation for the event, deliberately framed as a positive moment of imperial restoration). Several thousand more were killed in provincial operations. The entire institutional structure of the corps was systematically dismantled. The Janissary regimental insignia and traditions were officially banned. The Bektashi Sufi order, which had been the Janissaries' spiritual home, was suppressed and its lodges closed. The four-hundred-and-sixty-three-year-old institution that had taken Constantinople, defeated the Mamluks, and stood as the personal guard of every sultan since Murad I was eliminated within a single summer.

The Auspicious Incident was the most violent single act of state-on-state institutional destruction in Ottoman history. It was also the prerequisite for everything that followed. With the Janissaries gone, the path to comprehensive modernization was open. Mahmud II spent the remaining thirteen years of his reign building the institutional foundation for the Tanzimat reforms his son would launch.

---

The remaining Mahmud II reforms are easier to summarize. He created a new conscription-based standing army modeled on European lines. He reformed the central administration into something resembling modern ministries. He standardized the imperial currency. He instituted the first imperial census in 1831. He commissioned new official histories. He patronized translation projects to bring European technical and scientific literature into Ottoman Turkish. He built new schools, including the precursors of what would become the **Imperial Medical School** and the **Imperial Engineering School**. He sent dozens of young Ottomans to Paris, Vienna, and Berlin for European-style higher education, building the cohort of European-trained officials who would run the Tanzimat administration.

He also faced two major crises that would have ended a less skilled ruler.

The first was **Greek independence** (1821–1832). The Greek War of Independence began as an uprising of the Phanariot Greek elite of Constantinople and rural Greek populations in the Peloponnese, mobilized by **philhellene** sentiment in western Europe and supported by Russia. The war was brutal on both sides. The Ottomans called in Egyptian forces under **Ibrahim Pasha** (the son of Egypt's quasi-autonomous governor Mehmed Ali Pasha) for the suppression. By 1827 the Egyptian-Ottoman forces had largely defeated the Greek rebels. Then a combined British-French-Russian fleet intervened, destroying the Ottoman-Egyptian fleet at the **Battle of Navarino** in October 1827 in one of the more decisive naval engagements of the nineteenth century. The result was the establishment of an independent Greek state in 1832 — the first major successful nationalist secession from Ottoman rule, and the template that other Balkan independence movements would follow over the next century.

The second was the **Egyptian Crisis** of 1831–1833 and 1839–1841. **Mehmed Ali Pasha** (also written as Muhammad Ali, MEH-med ah-LEE, 1769–1849, an Albanian-born Ottoman commander who had been appointed governor of Egypt in 1805 and had built it into the most modernized province in the empire), encouraged by the Greek War complications, demanded compensation for his contributions to the Greek campaign and was refused. He launched an invasion of Ottoman Syria in 1831 and defeated successive Ottoman field armies. By 1832 his son Ibrahim Pasha had taken Damascus and Aleppo and was advancing on Anatolia. The Ottoman state was, briefly, on the verge of military collapse. The empire was rescued only by Russian intervention — Tsar Nicholas I sent a fleet and an army to defend Istanbul, in exchange for which the Ottomans signed the **Treaty of Hünkar İskelesi** (hoon-KAR is-keh-leh-SEE) in 1833, granting Russia exclusive naval rights in the Bosporus straits and effectively making the Ottoman Empire a Russian protectorate.

The British, alarmed by Russian dominance over the Ottoman state, intervened diplomatically in subsequent years to roll back the Hünkar İskelesi terms and to push Mehmed Ali back into Egypt — which was accomplished after a second crisis in 1839–1841 with the Convention of London. The settlement granted Mehmed Ali hereditary governorship of Egypt (effectively making it a separate state under nominal Ottoman suzerainty, an arrangement that would last until British occupation in 1882) but stripped his other territorial gains. The Ottoman state survived, but only because the great powers of Europe had decided that its survival was strategically preferable to its collapse. The empire had become an object of European great-power management rather than an independent political actor in its own right.

---

Mahmud II died in July 1839, in the middle of the second Egyptian crisis. He was fifty-three years old. His reform program had eliminated the Janissaries, modernized the army, restructured the administration, and laid the foundations for further institutional development. He had also failed to halt the empire's geopolitical decline, and his legacy was a state that was institutionally stronger but strategically more dependent on European patronage than at any point in its previous history.

He was succeeded by his son **Abdülmecid I** (ruled 1839–1861), who at sixteen years old took the throne with the empire still in the middle of the Egyptian crisis. The young sultan was guided by a circle of senior reformers who had grown up in Mahmud II's bureaucratic system — most importantly **Mustafa Reşid Pasha** (mu-STAH-fah reh-SHEED, 1800–1858), an experienced Ottoman diplomat who had served as ambassador to Paris and London and who was the architect of the Tanzimat program.

On November 3, 1839, three months after Abdülmecid's accession, Mustafa Reşid Pasha read the **Gülhane Hatt-ı Şerifi** (GUL-hah-neh hat-tuh sheh-REE-fee, the **Imperial Edict of the Rose Garden**) in front of the assembled imperial court, foreign ambassadors, and senior religious authorities at the Gülhane gardens of Topkapı Palace. The edict announced a comprehensive program of legal and administrative reform: equal protection of life, property, and honor for all Ottoman subjects regardless of religion; an end to arbitrary execution and confiscation by imperial decree; tax reform to replace tax-farming with regular state collection; military conscription on a regularized basis; and the formal end of the *millet*-based legal disabilities for non-Muslim subjects in matters of state law (though sharia continued to apply in religious matters).

The Gülhane edict launched the **Tanzimat** (TAHN-zee-maht, "Reorganizations") period — the most sustained institutional reform program in Ottoman history, running from 1839 to roughly 1876. The story of what the Tanzimat reforms accomplished, what they failed to accomplish, and how they interacted with the rising tide of Balkan nationalism is the subject of the next chapter.

Cross-civ check: the early nineteenth century was the period of the **Napoleonic Wars** and their settlements (1799–1815), the **independence movements** in Spanish America (1810–1825), the **Greek War of Independence** itself, the **Industrial Revolution** in Britain entering its second phase, the early consolidation of the **Qajar Dynasty** in Iran, and the late **Mughal Empire** falling under increasing British control. Mahmud II's reforms in the Ottoman Empire were contemporary with similar institutional modernization programs in **Egypt** under Mehmed Ali Pasha, in **Persia** under the early Qajars, in **Japan** under the late Tokugawa shogunate, and in **China** under the late Qing. Each of these states was attempting to adapt to the increasing institutional and military pressure from western European powers. Each was succeeding, in its own way, partially. The Ottoman case was neither uniquely successful nor uniquely failed — it was one variant of a global early-modern state-modernization pattern, with its own specific successes and limitations.

# Chapter 10 — The Sick Man of Europe

The phrase "Sick Man of Europe" was attributed (in a private 1853 conversation with the British ambassador) to Tsar Nicholas I of Russia, describing the Ottoman Empire as a dying patient whose territories should be partitioned among the European great powers before its final collapse. The phrase was not original to the Tsar — variants had been circulating in European diplomatic correspondence for decades — but it captured what had become, by the mid-nineteenth century, the standard European framing of the Ottoman state. The empire was, in this framing, a polity in terminal decline, holding together only because the European great powers could not agree on how to divide it.

The framing was both true and false. It was false in the sense that the Ottoman state of 1850 was institutionally stronger than the Ottoman state of 1750 — the Tanzimat reforms had produced a more centralized, more bureaucratic, more militarily capable empire than the eighteenth-century version. It was true in the sense that the empire's relative position vis-à-vis the European great powers had continued to deteriorate, and that the strategic survival of the Ottoman state was now genuinely dependent on European patronage rather than on Ottoman power.

This chapter covers the period from 1839 to 1908 — the seventy years of the Tanzimat reform era, the autocratic counter-reform under Sultan Abdülhamid II, and the Young Turk revolution that ended the autocracy. It is a period of substantial institutional change, recurring military disasters, accelerating territorial loss in the Balkans, and the gradual emergence of a new political ideology — Turkish nationalism — that would eventually replace Ottomanism as the empire's organizing principle.

---

The **Tanzimat reforms** of 1839–1876 produced more institutional change than any previous reform period in Ottoman history.

The most consequential changes came in three domains. The **legal** transformation imported French criminal and commercial codes, established secular state courts (the *nizamiye* courts) running in parallel with the sharia courts, and abolished both the death penalty for apostasy and (after 1857) the slave trade — fundamentally rebalancing the relationship between religious and secular law that Süleyman's Kanunname had originally negotiated three centuries earlier. The **administrative** transformation centralized provincial governance on French departmental lines, ended the autonomous *ayan* system that had run the empire's interior since the eighteenth century, and built a state council on European parliamentary models. The **civic** transformation, formalized in the **Hatt-ı Hümayun** edict of 1856 issued at the end of the Crimean War under explicit British and French pressure, granted non-Muslim subjects full legal equality in state law and opened state administrative careers to them. Alongside these came the educational and military pillars — state primary and secondary schools (the *rüşdiye* and *idadiye* networks), the French-language **Galatasaray Imperial Lyceum** (founded 1868) whose graduates would dominate the late Ottoman administration, the **Darülfünun** (the precursor of Istanbul University) in 1863, conscription-based military forces trained on European lines, and a navy and artillery park modernized through partnerships with European arms manufacturers.

The reforms were genuine, comprehensive, and sustained. By the 1870s the Ottoman Empire had a unified administrative structure, a partially codified secular legal system, a growing public school network, a regular army, and a state bureaucracy staffed by European-educated officials operating in something approximating modern administrative routines. The empire was not, by 1875, the same kind of state it had been in 1825.

What the reforms did not accomplish — and this is the more important point for the chapter's argument — was halting the territorial losses in the Balkans, addressing the rising European-supported nationalist movements among the empire's Christian populations, or modernizing the Ottoman economy fast enough to keep pace with European industrial growth. The institutional reforms produced a more capable state. The geopolitical situation continued to deteriorate. The two trends ran in parallel, and the second one, by 1878, would matter more than the first.

---

The major Ottoman foreign-policy event of the mid-nineteenth century was the **Crimean War** (1853–1856).

The war began as a Russian-Ottoman dispute over the protection of Christian holy sites in Palestine — Russia claimed the right to protect Orthodox Christians under the terms of the 1774 Küçük Kaynarca treaty; France claimed similar rights for Catholics under separate older agreements; the Ottomans tried to balance the two and ended up alienating the Russians sufficiently to provide Tsar Nicholas I with a pretext for war. The Russians invaded the Ottoman Romanian principalities (Wallachia and Moldavia) in mid-1853. The Ottomans declared war in October 1853.

What made the war historically distinctive was the European intervention. Britain and France, alarmed by Russian expansion against the Ottoman state, declared war on Russia in March 1854 and committed major military forces to the campaign. The Sardinian Kingdom joined the alliance in 1855. The combined Anglo-French-Ottoman-Sardinian forces fought the Russians primarily in Crimea (the war's geographic name), where the **Siege of Sevastopol** (October 1854 to September 1855) became one of the most costly and famous military operations of the nineteenth century — the engagement during which **Florence Nightingale** transformed military nursing, **William Howard Russell** of the Times invented modern war correspondence, and the **Charge of the Light Brigade** (October 1854) became one of the most romanticized military disasters in English-language poetry.

The war ended with the **Treaty of Paris** in March 1856. Russia ceded territory and was prohibited from maintaining a Black Sea fleet. The Ottoman Empire's territorial integrity was guaranteed by the great powers. Most consequentially for Ottoman internal affairs, the empire was formally admitted to the Concert of Europe — the diplomatic system of the major European powers — and the Hatt-ı Hümayun reform edict of 1856 was issued in part as a response to British and French pressure for further reforms in exchange for European protection.

The Crimean War was an Ottoman victory in the conventional sense — the Ottomans had survived a Russian attempt to dismember them, retained their territories, and entered the European diplomatic system as a recognized member. It was also a demonstration of the empire's complete strategic dependence on European patronage. Without British and French intervention, the war would have been lost. The empire's survival was now structurally dependent on the great powers' willingness to defend it.

---

The Tanzimat era reached its climax under **Sultan Abdülaziz** (ruled 1861–1876) and his successor **Murad V** (ruled briefly in 1876 before being deposed for mental incapacity), during a period of accelerating institutional change and accelerating Balkan crisis.

Abdülaziz's reign is bookended by two major projects that capture the period's contradictions. The **Dolmabahçe Palace** (DOL-mah-bah-cheh, "Filled-In Garden" — the palace was built on landfill at the upper Bosporus), commissioned by Abdülmecid I in 1843 and completed in 1856, became the new imperial residence. It replaced Topkapı as the working palace of the sultans. Architecturally it was European Baroque-Rococo on a vast scale — fourteen tons of gold leaf in the ceilings, the largest crystal chandelier in the world (a gift from Queen Victoria, weighing four and a half tons), French furnishings, German silver, English textiles. It was deliberately European in style, and it represented the Tanzimat-era Ottoman elite's aspiration to be recognized as part of European civilization. The palace was also catastrophically expensive, contributing significantly to the imperial debt that would eventually become unmanageable.

The other project was the **Bulgarian Massacres** of 1876.

In the spring of 1876, a poorly organized Bulgarian nationalist uprising broke out in the central Bulgarian highlands, encouraged by Russian agents and exiled Bulgarian intellectuals. The Ottoman regular army was occupied with similar insurgencies elsewhere in the Balkans, so the suppression was conducted primarily by **bashi-bazouk** irregulars and Circassian refugees who had been settled in the area after fleeing Russian conquest of the Caucasus in the 1860s. The suppression was extraordinarily brutal — entire villages massacred, churches burned with congregations inside, the towns of Batak and Perushtitsa subjected to atrocities that contemporary investigators (including the American journalist **Januarius MacGahan**, whose dispatches for the London Daily News produced detailed eyewitness accounts) documented in horrifying specificity. Estimates of the dead range from twelve thousand to thirty thousand, mostly Bulgarian Orthodox civilians. The events became an international scandal. **William Gladstone**, the British Liberal leader, published a pamphlet called *Bulgarian Horrors and the Question of the East* that became one of the most-read political pamphlets in nineteenth-century Britain and that fundamentally shifted British public opinion against Ottoman support.

The combination of fiscal collapse (the empire formally defaulted on its foreign debt in October 1875), the Bulgarian crisis, and rising Russian pressure produced a coup in May 1876. Abdülaziz was deposed by a coalition of reformist officials and senior military commanders. He was found dead a week later — officially a suicide; almost certainly murdered. His nephew Murad V was placed on the throne, suffered a mental breakdown within weeks, and was deposed in turn after a brief reign. The throne passed to Murad's younger brother **Abdülhamid II**.

---

**Abdülhamid II** (ruled 1876–1909) is the most controversial Ottoman ruler of the modern period. The standard nineteenth- and twentieth-century European framing painted him as the **Red Sultan** — a paranoid autocrat who reversed the Tanzimat reforms, suppressed the Ottoman press, ran a vast secret police network, and presided over the **Hamidian Massacres** of Armenian Christians in eastern Anatolia in 1894–1896. The standard framing was, in important respects, accurate. The more recent revisionist scholarship has argued that Abdülhamid II was also one of the most institutionally productive Ottoman rulers of the nineteenth century — that his reign saw substantial expansion of railway, telegraph, education, and industrial infrastructure, and that his autocratic methods were a rational response to genuine threats to the empire's coherence.

Both readings can be true simultaneously. Abdülhamid II was, in his political instincts, a rigorous autocrat who distrusted the constitutional and parliamentary institutions the Tanzimat reformers had attempted to establish. He was also genuinely committed to the institutional development of the empire and presided over substantial modernization that the constitutionalist reformers had not been able to achieve.

His first major action was paradoxical. In December 1876, he promulgated the **first Ottoman Constitution** — a document drafted by the senior Tanzimat reformer **Midhat Pasha**, providing for a parliament with both elected and appointed chambers, a bill of rights, and significant limitations on the sultan's authority. The Constitution was the most ambitious Ottoman institutional reform of the nineteenth century. The first parliament met in March 1877. Abdülhamid II then suspended the Constitution and dissolved the parliament in February 1878, citing the **Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878** as a national emergency requiring autocratic governance.

The Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878 was the second major Russian invasion of the empire's European territories in a generation. The Russians took most of Bulgaria, the Ottoman Caucasus, and substantial portions of eastern Anatolia. The **Treaty of San Stefano** (March 1878) imposed brutal terms on the Ottoman state: full Bulgarian independence (with Bulgaria including most of Macedonia and substantial Aegean coastline), Romanian independence, Serbian and Montenegrin independence with substantial territorial expansion, autonomy for Bosnia-Herzegovina, and Russian annexation of Ottoman Caucasian territories. The treaty's terms were so favorable to Russia that the other European great powers refused to accept them, and at the **Congress of Berlin** (June 1878) the European powers under **Otto von Bismarck**'s mediation revised the settlement: Bulgaria was reduced in size (Macedonia returned to Ottoman control); Bosnia-Herzegovina was assigned to Austrian administration (under nominal Ottoman sovereignty); Cyprus was assigned to British administration as compensation for British support of Ottoman positions. The Ottomans lost approximately one-third of their European territory and roughly twenty percent of their total population in the war and the resulting settlement.

The Berlin settlement was a watershed. The Ottoman state had now lost effective sovereignty over Bulgaria, Romania, Serbia, Bosnia, and Cyprus within a single decade. The Balkan provinces that had been the empire's European heartland for five centuries were largely gone. What remained — Albania, Macedonia, Thrace, the Aegean coast — would be lost in the Balkan Wars of 1912–1913. The Ottoman European Empire that had been built by Murad I, Bayezid I, and Mehmed II between the 1360s and the 1450s was, by 1878, a few decades from complete dissolution.

---

Abdülhamid II's autocratic methods after 1878 were comprehensive. He ran the imperial administration personally from his palace at **Yıldız** (YUL-duz, "Star"), a complex of pavilions and gardens on the European hillside above the Bosporus that he had built specifically as his political headquarters and that he very rarely left. He maintained an extensive secret police and intelligence network. He suppressed the Ottoman press, particularly any commentary on constitutional or political topics, with strict censorship. He cultivated relationships with the senior religious authorities of the empire, presenting himself as the Caliph of Sunni Muslims worldwide and developing a **pan-Islamic** ideology as the new ideological basis for Ottoman political coherence. He maintained close personal control over senior appointments, security operations, and major foreign-policy decisions.

He also presided over substantial institutional development. The **Hijaz Railway**, built from Damascus south to Medina (eventually extended to Mecca but completed only as far as Medina before the empire's collapse), was completed in 1908 and became the most ambitious Ottoman engineering project of the period — a religiously legitimated project funded by donations from Muslims around the world, intended to facilitate the hajj and to extend Ottoman state authority into the Hijaz. The **telegraph** network was built out across the empire. The **Imperial Naval School**, **Military Medical School**, and **Mülkiye** (the imperial civil-service academy) produced a generation of European-educated technocrats. The empire's first modern universities, technical schools, and professional academies dated from his reign. The state developed a relatively functional postal service, modernized cartography, basic public health institutions, and the early beginnings of an industrial sector.

The dark side of his reign was real and is best documented by the **Hamidian Massacres** of 1894–1896.

The eastern Anatolian provinces had a substantial Armenian Christian population — perhaps two million people in the 1890s — concentrated in the highlands of the empire's eastern frontier zone. Armenian nationalist movements, influenced by similar Balkan independence movements and supported by Armenian communities in the Russian Caucasus, had emerged in the 1880s. Ottoman authorities, particularly Abdülhamid II personally, viewed Armenian nationalism as an existential threat — Armenian autonomy or independence in eastern Anatolia would, they correctly calculated, encourage further European intervention and accelerate the empire's territorial dissolution.

The response was systematic violence. From 1894 to 1896, organized massacres of Armenian populations were conducted across eastern Anatolia by **Hamidiye** irregular cavalry (Kurdish tribal levies organized by the imperial state for frontier security) and by Ottoman regular troops. Cities and villages were attacked, churches burned, populations killed in coordinated operations that bore the institutional fingerprint of state organization. The death toll is contested but credible estimates range from one hundred thousand to three hundred thousand killed across the three-year campaign. The European press reported the events extensively. Western European public opinion was outraged. Western European governments were unable or unwilling to intervene effectively.

The Hamidian Massacres established the pattern that would be extended catastrophically during World War I. The Ottoman state had identified its Armenian Christian population as a politically unreliable population whose loyalty to the empire could not be assumed in a crisis. The state's response combined administrative pressure, demographic engineering, and organized mass violence. The full institutionalization of this approach — and its escalation into a systematic genocide — would happen under Abdülhamid II's eventual successors. The pattern began under his rule.

---

The end of Abdülhamid II's autocracy came from within his own institutional system.

The **Young Turks** (a loose translation of *Jön Türkler*, in turn a Turkish rendering of the French *Jeune-Turcs*) were a movement of European-educated Ottoman intellectuals, military officers, and administrators who had emerged during Abdülhamid II's reign. They were the products of the institutional developments of the period — graduates of Galatasaray Lisesi, the Imperial Military College, the Mülkiye, and the European universities where Ottoman students had been studying since the Tanzimat era. They were committed to constitutional government, secular state institutions, modernization on European lines, and the preservation of the empire as a coherent multi-ethnic polity.

The most consequential Young Turk organization was the **Committee of Union and Progress** (CUP, *İttihat ve Terakki Cemiyeti*), founded in 1889 by medical students at the Istanbul Imperial Military Medical School and gradually expanded into a broader political movement. The CUP combined a relatively traditional intellectual constitutionalist faction (centered in Paris and Geneva, where many leaders lived in exile) with a more activist military faction concentrated in the Ottoman Balkan army. By 1907 the CUP had organized cells across the Ottoman officer corps, particularly in the Balkan garrisons stationed in **Salonika** (which was an unusually cosmopolitan multi-ethnic city — a major center of Sephardic Jewish, Greek, Bulgarian, and Turkish populations, with a substantial German and French commercial presence — and which became the operational headquarters of the CUP's military faction).

In July 1908, several CUP-affiliated army units in the Balkan provinces launched a coordinated mutiny, demanding the restoration of the 1876 Constitution. The mutinies spread rapidly across the Ottoman army. Abdülhamid II, faced with the prospect of a general military uprising, restored the Constitution on July 24, 1908, and recalled parliament. The **Young Turk Revolution** had succeeded in its initial constitutional demand within several weeks.

What followed in 1908–1909 was a complex political struggle. Abdülhamid II attempted a counter-coup in April 1909, organizing a religious-conservative uprising in Istanbul. The CUP-led **Action Army** marched on Istanbul from Salonika under General **Mahmud Şevket Pasha**, suppressed the uprising, and deposed Abdülhamid II on April 27, 1909. He was confined to a villa in Salonika (and later, after 1912, returned to Istanbul under house arrest, where he died in 1918). His brother **Mehmed V Reşad** was placed on the throne as a constitutional monarch. The CUP took effective political control of the empire.

The Young Turk era (1908–1918) would be the empire's last decade, and the next chapter is its story.

Cross-civ check: the late nineteenth century was the period of the **Meiji Restoration** in Japan (1868 onward), the **Self-Strengthening Movement** in late Qing China, the rise of European **imperialism** in Africa and Asia, the consolidation of **nationalism** as the dominant European political ideology, the **American Civil War** and Reconstruction, and the late stages of the **Industrial Revolution**. The Ottoman experience was one variant of a broader pattern of non-Western state response to European industrial-imperial ascendancy. Some of these responses — Meiji Japan, in particular — produced remarkably successful modernizations that allowed the state to retain full sovereignty. Some — Qajar Persia, the late Qing — produced partial modernizations that left the state weakened but surviving. The Ottoman case fell in the second category. The state survived to 1918, but it survived as a smaller, weaker, more dependent version of itself, with the institutional foundations for a different kind of post-imperial successor state being slowly assembled by the European-educated reformers who would eventually become the founders of the Turkish Republic.

# Chapter 11 — The Great War and Aftermath

The decade between the Young Turk Revolution of 1908 and the Mudros armistice of 1918 is the most compressed and catastrophic period in Ottoman history. In ten years the empire fought four major wars (the Italian War of 1911–1912, the First Balkan War of 1912–1913, the Second Balkan War of 1913, and the First World War of 1914–1918), lost almost all its remaining European territory and most of its Arab provinces, presided over a systematic genocide of its Armenian population, and effectively ceased to exist as a functioning state. The Ottoman state that walked into the November 1918 armistice was a shell. The recovery and transformation that followed in 1919–1923 produced not a restored Ottoman Empire but a fundamentally different state — the modern Republic of Turkey — which is the subject of the final chapter.

This chapter is the wartime story.

---

The Young Turk government's first decade (1908–1914) was politically chaotic. The CUP had won constitutional restoration but had not yet consolidated political power. Three major political crises shook the new constitutional regime in its first years.

The **Italian-Ottoman War** of 1911–1912 saw Italy invade and conquer the Ottoman provinces of Tripolitania and Cyrenaica (which became the Italian colony of Libya). The Ottoman military response was inadequate — the empire could not project power across the Mediterranean against a serious European naval power — and the war ended with formal Ottoman recognition of Italian sovereignty over Libya in October 1912.

The **First Balkan War** of October 1912 to May 1913 was the catastrophe that ended Ottoman rule in Europe. A coalition of Bulgaria, Serbia, Greece, and Montenegro, encouraged by the Italian victory and seeing the Ottoman state as terminally weak, declared war on the empire. The combined Balkan armies overran Macedonia, Albania, Thrace, and most of the Aegean coast within weeks. The Ottoman state lost almost all its remaining European territory in seven months. Salonika — the second-largest city of the empire and the operational center of the CUP itself — fell to the Greeks on November 8, 1912. **Adrianople** (Edirne) — the city Murad I had taken in 1369 and which had been the empire's working capital before Constantinople — fell to the Bulgarians in March 1913. The empire's European frontier had collapsed.

The war ended with the **Treaty of London** (May 1913), which forced the Ottomans to cede everything west of a line approximating modern Turkey's European border. They retained eastern Thrace (the Edirne province) and the Bosporus straits, and almost nothing else.

The **Second Balkan War** of June-July 1913 was a redistribution of the Balkan spoils — Bulgaria attacked its former Greek and Serbian allies over the division of Macedonia and was rapidly defeated by a coalition that now included the Ottoman state. The Ottomans recovered Edirne and a slice of eastern Thrace from the Bulgarians. This was the empire's last successful military operation. The Edirne recovery was emotionally significant — the empire had recovered the city of Murad I — but the strategic situation remained desperate.

By August 1913 the Ottoman state had lost approximately 83% of its European territory and 69% of its European population in two wars. The Balkan empire that had been the dynasty's territorial heartland for six hundred years was effectively gone. The CUP's response was political consolidation: a January 1913 coup by a faction of CUP officers known as the **Three Pashas** — **Enver Pasha**, **Talaat Pasha**, and **Cemal Pasha** — established what would effectively be a triumvirate government for the remainder of the empire's existence. The constitutional regime continued to operate formally, but real power was held by Enver as War Minister, Talaat as Interior Minister, and Cemal as Naval Minister.

---

The decision to enter the **First World War** on the side of Germany and Austria-Hungary was made by the Three Pashas in late October 1914, against the preferences of significant portions of the Ottoman political class. The strategic calculation was clear-eyed: the empire was diplomatically isolated; Britain and France had repeatedly demonstrated willingness to support Russian and Greek nationalist threats against Ottoman territory; Germany was the only major power offering substantial military assistance and equipment; an Ottoman alliance with the Central Powers might, if Germany won, allow recovery of lost territories in the Caucasus and the Balkans. The bet was catastrophic. It cost the empire its existence.

The Ottoman war effort, October 1914 through October 1918, fought on multiple fronts:

**The Caucasus front** against Russia, where Enver Pasha personally launched an ambitious winter offensive in December 1914 against the Russian army in eastern Anatolia. The **Battle of Sarıkamış** (December 1914 to January 1915) was an unmitigated disaster — the Ottoman Third Army, perhaps 90,000 strong, was caught in the eastern Anatolian highlands in winter conditions for which it was not equipped, was decisively defeated by the Russians, and lost perhaps three-quarters of its strength to combat, frostbite, and starvation. The eastern Anatolian frontier collapsed. Russian forces would advance into eastern Anatolia for the next three years until the Russian Revolution of 1917 ended the Russian war effort.

**The Mesopotamian front** against the British, who landed at Basra in November 1914 and pushed up the Tigris-Euphrates valley over the next four years. The Ottomans achieved a remarkable victory at the **Siege of Kut al-Amara** (December 1915 to April 1916), capturing a British force of about thirteen thousand under General **Charles Townshend** — the largest British military surrender since the American Revolution. The victory was followed by British reinforcement and renewed advance. **Baghdad** fell to the British in March 1917. By war's end the British held all of Mesopotamia.

**The Sinai-Palestine front** against the British forces based in Egypt. The Ottomans launched a Suez Canal offensive in early 1915 that failed at the canal itself. The British counter-offensive moved north through Sinai in 1916–1917 under General **Edmund Allenby**, supported by **Arab Revolt** forces under **Sharif Hussein of Mecca** and his sons (advised by **T.E. Lawrence**, the British liaison officer whose memoirs would become the most famous English-language source on the Arab Revolt). Jerusalem fell to Allenby in December 1917. Damascus fell in October 1918.

**The Gallipoli front** against the British, French, Australian, and New Zealand forces who attempted in 1915 to force the Dardanelles, take Constantinople, and knock the Ottomans out of the war. The **Gallipoli Campaign** (April 1915 to January 1916) was the Ottoman war effort's one major strategic success. Allied landings at multiple points on the Gallipoli peninsula were contained by Ottoman defensive operations under the German general **Otto Liman von Sanders** and his Ottoman subordinate commander **Mustafa Kemal Bey** (later Atatürk), who had become a divisional commander at age thirty-four and who established his military reputation in the campaign. After eight months of trench warfare and approximately a quarter of a million casualties on each side, the Allied forces evacuated. Gallipoli became, simultaneously, the foundational battle of Australian and New Zealand national identity (the **Anzac** legend), the campaign that destroyed Winston Churchill's reputation as First Lord of the Admiralty (he had championed the operation and resigned after its failure), and the campaign that established Mustafa Kemal as a national figure in the late Ottoman state.

---

In the middle of this multi-front war, in the spring of 1915, the Ottoman state began the systematic destruction of its Armenian Christian population.

The **Armenian Genocide** of 1915–1916 needs to be addressed directly and accurately, because it is one of the defining events of late Ottoman history and one of the most consequential atrocities of the early twentieth century.

The Armenian Christian population of eastern Anatolia, perhaps two million strong before 1914, was concentrated in the highlands of the Ottoman eastern frontier — provinces that were also the immediate war zone of the Russian-Ottoman Caucasus campaign. The CUP government, particularly Talaat Pasha as Interior Minister, identified the Armenian population as a politically unreliable population whose loyalty in wartime could not be assumed and whose proximity to the Russian frontier made them a strategic liability. The institutional precedent of the Hamidian Massacres of 1894–1896 (Chapter 10) was already established. The wartime decision was to extend that approach to the systematic destruction of the entire community.

The genocidal program had three components. First, the **deportation orders** of April 1915 onward, which directed the systematic removal of Armenian populations from their home villages and cities. The deportees — predominantly women, children, and the elderly, since adult Armenian men had already been conscripted into the Ottoman army and many had been executed in 1914–1915 — were marched south through Anatolia toward concentration sites in the Syrian desert. The marches were conducted under conditions deliberately designed to produce mass death: minimal food and water, no shelter, organized banditry by escort forces and local irregulars, deliberate route choices through hostile terrain, periodic massacres of marching columns. Second, the **massacres** in cities and villages, conducted by Ottoman regular troops, **Special Organization** (Teşkilât-ı Mahsusa) paramilitary units, and Kurdish tribal forces under official Ottoman direction. Third, the **concentration sites** in the Syrian and Mesopotamian desert (most notoriously **Deir ez-Zor**), where surviving deportees were held under conditions producing mass death from starvation, disease, exposure, and continued violence.

The death toll is contested in detail but well established in magnitude. The standard scholarly estimate is between one and one and a half million Armenian deaths during the genocide period, out of a pre-war population of approximately two million. Substantial Armenian populations also lived in the **Russian Caucasus** and (after the war) in the **Soviet Armenia** that became the modern Republic of Armenia, but the Ottoman Armenian community of eastern Anatolia was destroyed as a coherent population. Many of the survivors fled to Russia, France, the United States, and the Levant, founding the modern **Armenian diaspora** that continues to be one of the most consequential exile communities in modern world history.

The genocide was conducted with systematic state organization. Detailed Ottoman administrative records of the deportations and massacres survive in the Ottoman archives, supplemented by the contemporary reports of foreign diplomats (the American ambassador **Henry Morgenthau Sr.**, whose memoir became one of the foundational documentary sources), foreign missionaries, foreign journalists, and surviving Armenian witnesses. The institutional fingerprint of state organization is unambiguous in the documentary record. The argument that the events were spontaneous communal violence, occasional excesses of regional officials, or unintended consequences of legitimate wartime population transfers — arguments that have been advanced by various Turkish state and academic sources from the 1920s to the present — is not supported by the primary documentary evidence.

The **Republic of Turkey**, since its founding in 1923, has officially denied that the events constituted genocide. The denial is a continuing diplomatic and historical issue. Most major academic institutions, most other state governments, and the international scholarly consensus on twentieth-century atrocities have recognized the events as a genocide for several decades. The Turkish state's official position remains that the events were a wartime population displacement with associated casualties on multiple sides, that the death toll has been exaggerated, and that the use of the term "genocide" is historically inaccurate. The continuing dispute has shaped Turkish-Armenian diplomatic relations, Turkish-American relations, Turkish-EU relations, and the Republic's domestic political culture for the past century.

Honest reckoning with the events is the responsibility of any account of late Ottoman history. The Ottoman state, in 1915–1916, organized and executed the systematic destruction of one of its own constituent populations as a deliberate state policy in wartime. The death toll was over a million people. The institutional and political continuity between the late Ottoman state that conducted the genocide and the early Republican state that succeeded it is direct — the same individuals, the same political organization (the CUP became the political ancestor of the Republican People's Party), the same institutional culture. The Armenian Genocide is one of the founding events of the modern Middle East. It continues to shape the politics of the region a century later.

---

The Ottoman war effort collapsed in late 1918. The Bulgarian capitulation in September 1918 opened the empire's European frontier to Allied advance. The Allenby offensive in Palestine and Syria swept Ottoman forces northward through October. The Russian Empire's collapse in 1917 had created a brief opportunity for Ottoman reconquest of lost Caucasus territories, but those gains were lost when German military support evaporated.

On October 30, 1918, the Ottoman Empire signed the **Armistice of Mudros** (named for the harbor on the Aegean island of Lemnos where the negotiations were conducted) with the Allied powers. The terms were severe: Ottoman demobilization, Allied occupation of strategic territory, Allied control of the straits, and effective Allied supervision of the Ottoman administration. The empire was, formally, a defeated state under Allied occupation.

Allied forces moved into Istanbul in November 1918. The British and French naval presence in the Bosporus was the first foreign military occupation of Constantinople since the Latin Empire of 1204–1261 (Byzantine Empire Ch 9). The Ottoman government continued to function in name, but real authority was held by the Allied High Commissioners. The Three Pashas — Enver, Talaat, and Cemal — had fled the country on a German submarine the day before Mudros was signed. Enver was killed in 1922 fighting Soviet forces in Central Asia. Talaat was assassinated in Berlin in March 1921 by an Armenian Genocide survivor named **Soghomon Tehlirian** (in a politically targeted killing whose subsequent trial — Tehlirian was acquitted on grounds of temporary insanity, with the genocide explicitly entered as evidence — became a major international cause célèbre and contributed to the postwar international legal vocabulary of "crimes against humanity"). Cemal was assassinated in Tbilisi in July 1922.

The peace settlement was the **Treaty of Sèvres** (signed August 10, 1920, after extensive negotiations among the Allied powers and the Ottoman government). The treaty was the most punitive settlement imposed on any Central Power after the war. Its terms included:

The cession of all Arab provinces to **British and French mandates** (Britain to administer Iraq, Palestine, and Transjordan; France to administer Syria and Lebanon).

The cession of **eastern Anatolia** to a planned independent Armenia and an autonomous Kurdistan.

The cession of **western Anatolia** (the Smyrna region, now called Izmir) to Greece.

The cession of **eastern Thrace** to Greece.

The internationalization of the **Bosporus straits** under Allied supervision.

Allied military and economic control over the remaining Ottoman state.

The treaty terms reduced the Ottoman state to a small rump territory in central and northern Anatolia. The territorial losses were complete: the Arab provinces gone, the Greek-administered western Anatolia and eastern Thrace gone, the planned Armenian and Kurdish autonomous states carving out the eastern half of Anatolia. What remained was less than a third of the empire's pre-war territory.

The Sèvres treaty was signed by the Ottoman government in Istanbul. It was rejected by a different Ottoman political authority that had emerged in central Anatolia — the **Turkish nationalist movement** based at **Ankara**, led by **Mustafa Kemal Pasha**, the hero of Gallipoli.

---

The **Greco-Turkish War of 1919–1922** was the war that destroyed the Sèvres settlement.

Greek forces had landed at Smyrna in May 1919 with British and French logistical support, intending to establish the Greek territorial administration of western Anatolia provided for under the planned peace settlement. The Greek military presence in Anatolia provoked an immediate Turkish nationalist response. Turkish military officers — primarily veterans of the Ottoman war effort, organized around Mustafa Kemal — assembled a resistance movement based in central Anatolia. The movement was simultaneously anti-Greek (the immediate enemy), anti-Allied (rejecting the Sèvres settlement), and anti-Sultanate (rejecting the Istanbul government's signing of the treaty). It was a Turkish nationalist movement, with explicit ethnic Turkish self-conception, articulated as a national-liberation movement against foreign occupation and Greek invasion.

The war was fought over three years across central and western Anatolia. The Greek army initially advanced east from Smyrna, taking Bursa, Eskişehir, and Afyon, and pushing close to Ankara in the summer of 1921. The Turkish nationalist defense at the **Battle of Sakarya** (August-September 1921) halted the Greek advance after a three-week engagement. The Greek forces, overextended and undersupplied, began a withdrawal in 1922. The Turkish counteroffensive of August-September 1922 broke the Greek army at the **Battle of Dumlupınar** and pursued the retreating Greek forces back to Smyrna. The city fell to Turkish forces on September 9, 1922. A massive fire destroyed most of the Greek and Armenian neighborhoods of the city in the days following, killing tens of thousands of civilians. The Greek population of western Anatolia, perhaps a million people whose ancestors had lived in the region continuously since the eighth century BCE, fled to Greece in the largest single refugee crisis of the early twentieth century.

The Turkish nationalist victory in the Greco-Turkish War effectively destroyed the Sèvres settlement. The Allied powers were unwilling to commit further military forces to enforce a treaty that had not been accepted by the de facto government of Turkey. Negotiations for a replacement settlement opened at **Lausanne** in November 1922.

The **Treaty of Lausanne** (signed July 24, 1923) replaced Sèvres with substantially better terms for the Turkish state. The Ottoman Arab provinces remained under British and French mandate (a non-negotiable Allied position). The planned Armenian and Kurdish autonomous states were abandoned (with massive consequences for the Armenian and Kurdish populations of eastern Anatolia, who had been counting on international support that did not materialize). Western Anatolia and eastern Thrace remained Turkish. The Bosporus straits were internationalized but under conditions that allowed Turkish administrative authority. A massive **population exchange** between Greece and Turkey was formalized: approximately 1.2 million Greeks deported from Anatolia to Greece, approximately 400,000 Turks deported from Greece to Turkey. The exchange was conducted on the basis of religion rather than language or self-identification — Greek Orthodox populations to Greece, Muslim populations to Turkey, with very limited exceptions. It was the largest single state-organized population transfer of the twentieth century before the partition of British India in 1947.

The Lausanne treaty established the borders and the demographic foundations of the modern Republic of Turkey.

---

Before the Lausanne treaty, while negotiations were still ongoing, the Turkish nationalist movement had taken a step that eliminated the institutional basis of the Ottoman Empire: the abolition of the sultanate.

On November 1, 1922, the **Grand National Assembly** of Turkey (the parliament of the nationalist movement, based in Ankara) passed a law abolishing the office of sultan and declaring the Ottoman dynasty to have no further governmental role. The last sultan, **Mehmed VI Vahdettin** (ruled 1918–1922), fled Istanbul on a British warship two weeks later, on November 17, 1922. He was the thirty-sixth and final sultan of a dynasty that had begun with Osman I six hundred and twenty-three years earlier.

The Ottoman Empire, as a continuous political institution, had ended. The Caliphate continued briefly under Vahdettin's cousin **Abdülmecid II**, who was named Caliph but not Sultan in November 1922 — a deliberate institutional split between the religious and political offices that had been merged since Selim I's claim of 1517. The arrangement was unstable. The Republican government would eliminate it within eighteen months. That story is the next chapter.

Cross-civ check: 1922 — the year the Ottoman sultanate was abolished — was also the year **Mussolini's** March on Rome installed Italian Fascism, the year the **Soviet Union** was formally established, the year **British rule in India** entered its terminal phase under Gandhi's first non-cooperation movement, the year of the **Irish Free State**'s independence, and the year the **Versailles settlement** was beginning to visibly fail across central Europe. The post-WWI dissolution of the multi-ethnic empires (Ottoman, Habsburg, German, Russian) created the political geography of the modern Middle East, central Europe, and northern Eurasia in a single concentrated period of state formation. The Ottoman case was one of four major imperial dissolutions in the period. Its consequences continue to shape the politics of the eastern Mediterranean a century later.

# Chapter 12 — The Republic and What Endured

The Ottoman Empire ended on November 1, 1922. The Republic of Turkey was proclaimed on October 29, 1923. In the eleven months between those two events, and in the year and a half that followed, the institutional, religious, and political framework that had defined the Ottoman state was systematically dismantled by the new Republican government and replaced with a different framework on a different model. The transformation was the most rapid and most thoroughgoing institutional change in any of the major successor states emerging from the post-WWI imperial dissolutions, and it was conducted by a single political movement under the leadership of a single figure.

The figure was **Mustafa Kemal**, who would adopt the surname **Atatürk** ("Father of the Turks") in 1934 when surnames were made compulsory. The political movement was the Republican People's Party. The transformation produced modern Turkey. Its consequences continue to shape Turkish politics, identity, and foreign relations a century later.

This chapter is about that transformation, about the legacy the Ottoman Empire left across the territories it had once ruled, and about what specific elements of the Ottoman institutional and cultural inheritance continue to operate in the modern world.

---

**Mustafa Kemal** (1881–1938) was a graduate of the Ottoman Imperial Military College, a junior officer in the Three Pashas era, the divisional commander who had stopped the Anzac advance at Gallipoli in 1915, and the senior officer who had organized the Turkish nationalist resistance against the Greek invasion in 1919–1922. He combined exceptional military and political ability with an explicit commitment to the wholesale reconstruction of the Turkish state along secular European lines. He was not, by temperament or training, an Ottoman traditionalist. He had no patience for the institutions of the Caliphate, the Ottoman dynastic system, or the cultural traditions of the late empire. His project was to replace those institutions, deliberately and rapidly, with new institutions modeled on European national-state precedents.

The transformation proceeded in several stages.

In 1922 the **sultanate was abolished**, separating the Caliphate from the temporal monarchy.

In 1923 the **Republic was proclaimed**. Ankara, the central Anatolian city that had been the capital of the nationalist movement, was made the new capital of the Republic — replacing Istanbul, the imperial capital of nearly five centuries.

In 1924 the **Caliphate was abolished**. Abdülmecid II, the last Caliph, was deported to France with the rest of the Ottoman dynastic family. The eight-hundred-year-old institutional continuity of the Sunni Caliphate (which had passed from the Abbasids to the Mamluks to the Ottomans) was definitively broken. The Caliphate has not been reconstituted since.

In 1925 the **religious-legal framework** of the empire was systematically replaced. Islamic religious courts were abolished. Religious orders (the Sufi tariqats — the Mevlevi, Bektashi, Naqshbandi, and other orders that had been embedded in Ottoman religious life for centuries) were closed. The traditional religious dress code was abolished — the **fez** (the cylindrical red felt hat that had been the symbol of Ottoman official identity since Mahmud II's clothing reforms in the 1820s) was banned, and European-style clothing was made mandatory for state officials. The Islamic lunar calendar was replaced with the Gregorian solar calendar.

In 1926 the **Civil Code** of the Swiss Confederation was adopted as the basis of Turkish family and personal law, replacing the Ottoman sharia-based personal status law. The transformation included the abolition of polygamy, the introduction of equal civil rights for women (including the right to vote and to hold office, granted in 1934 — earlier than in many European states), and the institution of secular marriage as the only legally recognized form of marriage.

In 1928 the **Latin alphabet** was adopted to replace the Ottoman Arabic-script alphabet that had been the standard written form of Turkish for six centuries. The transition was rapid — roughly six months from the adoption decision to the legal mandate — and was accompanied by a state-sponsored mass literacy campaign. The rupture with the previous textual tradition was deliberate and severe. Within a generation, most Turks could not read documents written in the Ottoman script. The Ottoman literary inheritance — divan poetry, court chronicles, religious texts, administrative documents, six centuries of accumulated writing — became inaccessible to the general population without specialized scholarly training.

In the 1930s the **Turkish Language Society** undertook a systematic project to purge Turkish vocabulary of Persian and Arabic loan-words, which had constituted perhaps half of formal Ottoman Turkish vocabulary. The replacements were either revived archaic Turkic words, neologisms coined from Turkic roots, or in some cases European loan-words. The result was that modern Turkish — particularly its written form — became substantially different from late Ottoman Turkish, with vocabulary differences that are roughly equivalent to the differences between modern English and Anglo-Saxon Old English. A modern Turkish speaker reading a Tanzimat-era newspaper article requires substantial scholarly assistance.

The aggregate effect of these reforms was an unusually rapid and complete cultural rupture. The Republican government had deliberately and systematically severed the new Turkey from its Ottoman past in matters of religion, law, dress, language, alphabet, and political structure. The transformation was, in important respects, more thoroughgoing than the parallel modernizations in Meiji Japan, post-Tsarist Russia, or post-imperial China — each of which retained more substantial continuity with their pre-modernization cultural inheritance. Turkey, under Atatürk, conducted what was effectively a state-organized cultural revolution against its own immediate past.

The political logic of the transformation was clear. The Ottoman framework had been permanently associated, in the minds of the Republican leadership, with imperial collapse, military defeat, and external dependency. The new Turkey was to be a different kind of state — secular, ethnically Turkish, European-modeled, and politically defensible against European pressures because it had explicitly adopted European institutional forms. The strategic calculation was that Turkey could only retain full sovereignty if it stopped looking, institutionally, like the kind of state European powers expected to dismember.

---

What survived from the Ottoman period, despite the Republican rupture?

A great deal more than the Republican founding rhetoric acknowledged.

**Geographic and demographic.** The territory of modern Turkey is, in its broad outlines, the Ottoman Anatolian core — the region that had been continuously under Ottoman rule from the early fourteenth century onward. The cities are the Ottoman cities. The road and water infrastructure has Ottoman foundations beneath the Republican-era and modern overlays. The agricultural geography of grain belts, olive coasts, and fruit-growing regions follows patterns that were established under Ottoman fiscal and irrigation systems. The Turkish population is the demographic descendant of the Ottoman Anatolian-Muslim population — modified by the population exchange with Greece in 1923 and by various subsequent migrations, but recognizably continuous with the Ottoman population.

**Religious.** The Sunni Hanafi Islamic tradition that the Ottoman state had patronized for six centuries remained the religious tradition of the great majority of the modern Turkish population. The mosques continued to operate. The five-times-daily call to prayer continued from Ottoman-era minarets. Ramadan continued to be observed. The hajj continued to be made. The everyday religious life of Turkish Muslims did not undergo a Republican-era transformation comparable to the institutional transformation of religious institutions. The Republic is constitutionally secular, but the population is religiously Muslim, and the relationship between official secularism and popular religiosity has been one of the defining tensions of Turkish political life for the past century. The Sufi orders, formally banned in 1925, never disappeared — they continued to operate informally throughout the Republican period and have re-emerged into more public visibility since the 1980s.

**Architectural.** The visual landscape of Turkish cities is unmistakably Ottoman. Istanbul's skyline — defined by Hagia Sophia, the Süleymaniye, the Sultan Ahmed Mosque, and the network of imperial mosques and palaces built between the fifteenth and seventeenth centuries — is the same skyline that Sinan helped create. The provincial Ottoman mosques, the kulliye complexes, the Topkapı palace, the Iznik tilework, the courtyard houses with their wooden lattice screens (the *kafes* and *cumba* of traditional Ottoman urban architecture) all continue to define the visual character of Turkish cities and many cities across the former Ottoman lands. The Ottoman aesthetic continues, even where the Republican-era state distanced itself from Ottoman political institutions.

**Linguistic.** Despite the language reforms, modern Turkish remains the descendant of Ottoman Turkish, in roughly the same way that modern English is the descendant of Middle English — substantially modified, but recognizably continuous. Ottoman vocabulary is recoverable through scholarly training. The Persian and Arabic loan-words that were officially purged in the 1930s have, in many cases, returned to colloquial usage. The literary tradition is, with effort, accessible to modern readers.

**Culinary.** The Ottoman cuisine — kebabs, pilavs, börek, baklava, Turkish coffee, mezes, the elaborate savory and sweet pastry traditions of the imperial palace kitchens — remained essentially unchanged through the Republican transformation. Modern Turkish cuisine is, more or less directly, the cuisine of the Ottoman court and the Ottoman commercial cities. Many of the cooking traditions carried over to the successor states across the former Ottoman lands — the cuisine of Greece, Bulgaria, Lebanon, Syria, and Egypt is shaped by the Ottoman culinary inheritance to a degree that local nationalist sentiment has often tried to obscure but which is unmistakable to any cross-cultural diner.

**Musical.** Ottoman classical music (*Türk sanat müziği*), Sufi music traditions, the Mevlevi semazen ritual, Janissary band music (the precursor of European military marching band traditions), and various folk traditions all continued in modified form into the Republican period and remain part of contemporary Turkish musical culture.

---

The Ottoman legacy beyond modern Turkey is broader and harder to summarize, because it stretches across roughly a third of the modern Mediterranean and Middle Eastern world.

In the **Balkan successor states** — Greece, Bulgaria, North Macedonia, Albania, Bosnia-Herzegovina, Kosovo, Serbia, Montenegro, parts of Romania, parts of Croatia and Hungary — the Ottoman period left enduring marks. The mosques and Ottoman urban architecture in cities like Sarajevo, Skopje, Sofia, and Pristina; the lingering ethnic and religious diversity (Bosnian Muslims, Albanian Muslims, the Pomak Muslim populations of Bulgaria and Greece, the Sephardic Jewish populations of the Balkan cities) that maps onto Ottoman-era millet patterns; the cuisine, music, and folk traditions; the linguistic substratum — Turkish loan-words remain common in Balkan languages, and the languages themselves were shaped by centuries of multilingual Ottoman administration. The negative legacy is also real: the long-running ethnic and religious conflicts of the Balkans, which exploded in the wars of the 1990s, drew on grievances and identity formations that had been shaped during the Ottoman period and the wars of Ottoman dissolution.

In the **Arab successor states** — Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Jordan, Israel/Palestine, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, the Hijaz, Yemen, Libya, Tunisia, Algeria — the Ottoman legacy was structurally important even where it has been politically downplayed by Arab nationalist narratives. The administrative geography of the modern Arab states was substantially shaped by the late Ottoman *vilayet* (provincial) structure, with many post-WWI mandate borders following Ottoman provincial lines. The religious institutions — the system of religious endowments (*waqfs*), the structure of Islamic legal scholarship, the administration of holy sites — were continuous with their Ottoman precedents. The architecture of Damascus, Aleppo, Cairo, Baghdad, and the smaller Levantine and Anatolian cities preserved Ottoman building traditions extensively. The Hijaz Railway, built under Abdülhamid II, continued to operate in modified form for decades after the empire's collapse.

The **borders** of much of the modern Middle East are post-Ottoman borders. The borders between Turkey and Syria, Iraq and Syria, Iraq and Saudi Arabia, Saudi Arabia and Jordan, Jordan and Israel/Palestine — these were drawn after 1918 by the Allied mandate authorities, sometimes following Ottoman administrative lines and sometimes overriding them. The various twentieth- and twenty-first-century conflicts of the Middle East — the Arab-Israeli conflict, the Lebanese civil wars, the Iraq wars, the Syrian civil war, the Kurdish conflicts in Turkey and Iraq — have all involved disputes over borders, populations, and institutional arrangements that trace, in some part, to the Ottoman dissolution.

The **religious geography** of the Sunni-Shi'a divide in the Middle East was shaped by the Ottoman-Safavid rivalry that began at Chaldiran in 1514 and continued for the next two centuries (Chapter 5). The modern political tensions between Sunni Saudi Arabia, Sunni Egypt, and the Sunni Gulf states on one side, and Shi'a Iran, the Shi'a-led government of Iraq, the Shi'a Hezbollah of Lebanon, and the Alawite-led government of Syria on the other side, are recognizable extensions of the Ottoman-Safavid conflict in modern form.

The **Caliphate question**, dormant for nearly a century after Atatürk's abolition in 1924, has periodically resurfaced. The early-twentieth-century pan-Islamic movements that sought to revive the caliphate failed. The mid-twentieth-century Arab nationalist movements were largely uninterested in restoring it. Various twenty-first-century radical movements (most notoriously the so-called Islamic State of Iraq and Syria, ISIS, which proclaimed a caliphate in 2014) have invoked the Ottoman caliphate as historical precedent. The substantive theological and political questions raised by the abolition — about the relationship between political authority and religious authority in Sunni Islam, about the institutional form of pan-Islamic religious leadership — remain unresolved.

---

What was the Ottoman Empire, in the end?

It was a multicontinental polity that lasted six hundred and twenty-three years, ruled at peak about thirty million people of dozens of ethnicities and several major religions, and operated as one of the central political organizations of the Mediterranean basin from the late fourteenth century to the early twentieth.

It was the institutional successor to Byzantium — taking over Constantinople, the surviving Roman administrative apparatus of the eastern Mediterranean, and the Greek and Slavic populations of the Balkans, and running them under a different religious framework and a different ruling dynasty for four centuries. The Roman Empire that had refused to die for eleven hundred years (Byzantine Empire Ch 1) finally received an institutional successor that, in some respects, continued its role.

It was, for two centuries, the most powerful state in its region — the empire that took Constantinople, defeated the Mamluks, conquered the Hungarian plain, dominated the Mediterranean, codified law for three continents, and produced an architectural climax in the work of Mimar Sinan that ranks among the supreme achievements of any pre-modern civilization.

It was, for three centuries, the empire that managed religious and ethnic diversity through the millet system on a scale that no contemporary Christian European state attempted, providing protected status to religious minorities under conditions that — for all their limitations — were more durable than anything available in early modern Catholic or Protestant Europe.

It was, for one century at the end, the Sick Man of Europe — the polity whose dissolution shaped the political geography of the modern Middle East, whose final wars produced one of the twentieth century's first systematic genocides, and whose collapse produced the modern Republic of Turkey along with sixteen other successor states across three continents.

Its institutional inheritance is everywhere across the former Ottoman lands. The mosques of Sarajevo and Cairo, the bazaars of Istanbul and Damascus, the legal codifications that fused sharia and kanun, the architectural vocabulary that descends from Hagia Sophia through Sinan to the modern provincial mosque, the administrative geography of the Middle East, the Sunni-Shi'a sectarian map, the Greek-Turkish-Bulgarian-Armenian diaspora communities scattered across the world, the Sephardic Jewish heritage that traces through Salonica back to medieval Spain — all of these are Ottoman.

The Ottoman Empire fell in 1922. Its inheritance is operating, contested, and unfinished. Modern Turkey, modern Greece, modern Bulgaria, modern Israel, modern Lebanon, modern Iraq, modern Saudi Arabia, modern Egypt, and modern Bosnia are still working out — some peacefully, some with difficulty, some catastrophically — what the empire left them. The empire that had ruled at peak from the Algerian coast to the Caucasus and from the Hungarian plain to the Yemeni highlands left, when it fell, an institutional and cultural inheritance that no nation-state successor has been able to absorb completely, and that continues to shape the politics of three continents a century after the last sultan boarded a British warship and sailed away from his city.

The empire is gone. Its world is still being inherited.

Cross-civ final check: the Ottoman Empire's six centuries (1299–1922) overlap, in turn, with the Yuan and Ming and Qing dynasties of China, with the entire arc of the Mughal Empire of India, with the Safavid and Qajar dynasties of Persia, with the early modern consolidation of European nation-states, with the rise and fall of the Russian Empire, with the entire colonial period of European expansion into the Americas, Africa, and Asia, and with the emergence of the modern global system of nation-states. Among the great early modern empires, only the Russian Empire — which lasted from approximately 1547 to 1917, with various formal start and end dates depending on definition — had comparable longevity, and the Russian Empire is itself one of the polities most directly shaped by the Byzantine inheritance that the Ottomans had simultaneously inherited and replaced. The two great heirs of Byzantium — the Russian Empire claiming the Third Rome and the Ottoman Empire claiming Kayser-i Rûm — operated in parallel for nearly four hundred years before the Russian Empire ended in revolution in 1917 and the Ottoman Empire ended in republican proclamation in 1922 and 1923. They were, between them, the two longest-running political continuities in early modern history. They both ended within five years of each other. The age of the great multicontinental empires ended with them.

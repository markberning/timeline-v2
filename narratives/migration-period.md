# Chapter 1 — The Domino Falls

In the year 376 CE, a Roman army watching the lower Danube saw something that had no precedent in living memory: an entire nation, hundreds of thousands of people, standing on the river's far bank and begging to be let in.

These were the **Goths** (pronounced with a hard "g," as in "goat"), a Germanic people the Romans had known and fought and traded with for over a century, and they were not raiding. They were fleeing. They had their wagons, their herds, their old people and their children, and they were asking the Roman Empire, the thing they had spent generations harassing, for asylum. Something had come up behind them out of the east, something that had broken the most powerful Gothic kingdom on the steppe with frightening speed (the sources do not tell us in how many campaigns, only that it was fast) and was still coming, and the Goths had decided that throwing themselves on the mercy of Rome was less terrifying than staying to meet it.

That something was the **Huns**. This book is about what happened next: the roughly two centuries in which the western half of the Roman Empire was overrun, dismantled, and replaced, not by one invasion but by a chain reaction of peoples in motion that historians, borrowing a nineteenth-century German word, call the **Völkerwanderung** (pronounced "FUR-kuh-VAN-deh-roong," literally "the wandering of the peoples"). It is the story of how the ancient Mediterranean world came apart and how medieval Europe was assembled out of the pieces.

It is not, mostly, the story of the Goths themselves. They get their own book ([[the-goths]]), because their journey from the Danube to a kingdom in Spain is long enough to deserve one. This book is about the wider shattering they were the first crack in: the Huns who started it, the Vandals who took Rome's breadbasket and then Rome itself, the Franks who would become France, the collapse of the Western emperors, and the Eastern Empire's catastrophic attempt to put it all back together. The Goths walk through these pages constantly, but in the background. The foreground is the system, and the system breaking.

---

A word, once, about how we know any of this, because it shapes everything that follows and it is more honest to say it now than to keep apologizing for it later.

Almost every written source for these two centuries was produced by Romans, Greeks, or Christian churchmen writing within the surviving (Eastern) empire. The peoples doing the migrating, Huns, Vandals, most of the early Franks, wrote little or nothing that survives, and what little they did was usually written down generations later by their descendants once those descendants had Latin clergy to do it for them. So when this book says a battle happened "in 451," or that a king "decided" something, the reader should hear an implied caveat that does not need repeating every sentence: the dates, numbers, and especially the motives are reconstructed from a handful of sources who were often far away, often hostile, and often writing decades after the fact. Where a claim is genuinely contested, this book will say so. Where it is reasonably solid, it will say that too. The general rule is that the *shape* of events is well attested and the *vivid details* are the part to hold loosely, because vivid details are exactly what ancient writers liked to improve.

With that on the table, the place to start is with the people who knocked over the first domino, because nobody in the Roman world had any idea who they were.

---

The Huns were not Germani. This is the single most important thing to understand about them, and it is easy to get wrong, because the Romans lumped almost everyone beyond the frontier under the catch-all sneer "barbarian," and a modern reader can absorb that flattening without noticing.

The Germanic peoples, Goths, Vandals, Franks, Saxons and the rest, spoke languages from the Germanic branch of **Indo-European**, the same vast language family that produced Latin, Greek, Persian, and Sanskrit. They were the cold-country, forest-and-farm peoples Rome had been managing along the Rhine and Danube for four centuries ([[germanic-tribes]] tells that long story). The Huns were something else entirely. They were **steppe nomads**: horse-herding, tent-dwelling, cattle-driving people from the Eurasian steppe, the immense belt of open grassland that runs for thousands of miles from the plains of Hungary east across Ukraine and Central Asia to Mongolia. Their language is essentially unknown. We have a few personal names and almost nothing else, and scholars have argued for over a century about whether it was Turkic, related to the later Mongolic languages, something Iranian, or something with no surviving relatives at all. The honest answer is that we cannot classify it, but it was certainly not Germanic and almost certainly not Indo-European. To put the distance in terms a reader can feel: a Goth meeting a Roman shared a deep common linguistic ancestor with him, the way English and Russian share one, distantly. A Goth meeting a Hun shared nothing, the way English and Chinese share nothing. These were not cousins of the Germani. They were strangers to everyone.

Where did they come from? The honest answer is that we do not securely know. A long tradition connects them to the **Xiongnu** (pronounced "shyong-NOO"), a steppe confederation that had menaced Han-dynasty China centuries earlier, but the link is debated and the evidence is thin enough that careful historians treat it as a hypothesis rather than a fact. What is solid is the *behavior*. The Huns appeared on the western steppe, north of the Black Sea, in the 370s, and they were a kind of military machine the Roman and Germanic worlds had never had to fight. Their power was the horse and the bow. Every man rode, from childhood. Their weapon was the **composite bow**, a short, brutally powerful bow built up in layers of wood, horn, and sinew so that a weapon small enough to shoot from a galloping horse hit as hard as a much larger one, and they could shoot it accurately at the gallop, wheel away, and shoot again before an enemy could close. Against this a Roman infantry unit, trained to lock shields and grind down an enemy that came to meet it, had nothing to grind down: the Huns simply rode in a wide circle out of reach, shot the formation full of arrows until it broke or charged, and if it charged they fell back faster than it could follow and shot it some more. They did not hold ground; they had no cities to defend and nothing to besiege. They could not be brought to a decisive battle they did not want, and they could not be exhausted by burning farms they did not own. Roman armies were built to fight other armies that stood still and fought. The Huns refused to be that, and for a generation no one had an answer.

The other thing the Huns did, and it is the engine of this entire book, is that they absorbed. When the Huns broke a people, they did not necessarily destroy or replace them. Often they incorporated them, took their warriors, their tribute, and their submission, and rolled them into a growing confederation under Hunnic overlordship. So the thing pressing on the Danube frontier was not a fixed nation of a known size. It was a snowball, and everyone it touched either ran or was rolled into it.

---

Here is the mechanism, stated plainly, because the whole rest of the book is variations on it.

The Huns hit the easternmost Gothic confederation, the **Greuthungi** (pronounced "groy-THUNG-ee," later remembered as the ancestors of the Ostrogoths), on the open steppe north of the Black Sea around 375, and broke them. The shock rolled west into the next Gothic group, the **Tervingi** (pronounced "TER-ving-ee," later the core of the Visigoths), who did not wait to be broken and instead ran for the Danube and Rome. That is one domino knocking the next. The Goths' arrival inside the empire in 376, the catastrophic battle three years later at **Adrianople** in 378 where the Goths destroyed an entire Eastern field army and killed **Valens** (the emperor of the Eastern half of the empire, on the throne since 364; for an emperor to be killed in battle by barbarians was almost unheard of, a humiliation Rome had not suffered on this scale in centuries), and the uneasy settlement of the Goths inside the Balkans in 382, all of that is the Gothic story, and it is told in full elsewhere ([[the-goths]]). What matters here is the principle it established and the precedent it set.

The principle: a people pushed hard enough from behind does not negotiate over whether to move. It moves, and the only question is who is in the way.

The precedent: faced with a whole armed nation it could not destroy, Rome did the thing it would now do over and over until it had given the empire away. It cut a deal. It admitted the Goths as **foederati** (pronounced "feh-deh-RAH-tee," from the Latin *foedus*, "treaty"), allied peoples settled on Roman soil who kept their own leaders and their own internal law and served in the Roman army in exchange for land, supplies, and gold. This was not invented in 382; Rome had been settling defeated barbarians under treaty for two centuries, planting groups of beaten Germani as farmer-soldiers on emptied frontier land along the Rhine and Danube where they could work the soil and be called up to fight. But it had always done that on Rome's terms, with broken and divided peoples. It had never before done it with a whole intact nation that arrived under its own kings, on its own terms, having just annihilated a Roman army. The difference between a settled prisoner population and an armed allied nation living inside your border with its own command structure is the difference, eventually, between a province and a kingdom.

This is, in fact, an old trap (Carthage had armed and settled foreign mercenaries it could not pay, and they turned on it in the savage Mercenary War and nearly destroyed the city from inside, [[carthage]]; Gaulish factions had hired Germanic Suebi who then refused to leave, [[celtic-cultures]], [[germanic-tribes]]), seven hundred years old by the time Rome learned it the hard way. Rome learned it anyway, because at every step the immediate alternative looked worse than the deal in front of it. That sentence is the closest thing this book has to a thesis, and it will be true on almost every page.

The Huns had knocked over the first domino. For the next thirty years the line of falling tiles ran mostly through the Balkans and the Gothic story, far enough from the Western capital that the men running Italy could persuade themselves it was a containable eastern problem. It was not. On the freezing last night of the year 406, while the Roman world was looking the other way, the dominoes reached the Rhine, and the half of the empire that had told itself it was safe began to come apart in earnest.

# Chapter 2 — The Rhine Gives Way

The Western Roman Empire of the year 400 was held together, more than by any emperor, by one man, and the most revealing thing about him is that he was half-barbarian.

His name was **Stilicho** (pronounced "STILL-ih-koh"), and his father was a Vandal, one of the Germanic peoples that this chapter is about to turn loose on Gaul. His mother was Roman. He had risen through the army to become the supreme military commander of the West and the effective regent for the boy-emperor **Honorius** (pronounced "hoh-NOR-ee-us"), a weak and suspicious man who reigned, in the technical sense of occupying the throne, from 393 to 423, and who governed almost nothing. Stilicho was the West's general, diplomat, and strongman, and the fact that the Roman Empire's survival rested on a man of Vandal blood is not an irony the book is inventing for effect. It is the central fact of the age. By 400 the line between "Roman commander" and "barbarian warlord" had become so blurred that the question "which side is this person on" frequently had no clean answer, including for the people asking it about themselves.

Stilicho spent the early 400s fighting fires he could not put out, only move. In 405 a large mixed Gothic-led war host under a leader the sources call **Radagaisus** (pronounced "rah-dah-GUY-sus") crossed into Italy itself and drove deep toward Florence before Stilicho cornered and destroyed it and had Radagaisus executed. It was a Roman victory, and it was also a warning, because to assemble enough troops to crush Radagaisus, Stilicho had to strip soldiers off other frontiers, above all the Rhine. The Rhine was the western empire's oldest, most heavily fortified border, the line Julius Caesar had fixed as Rome's edge when he conquered Gaul in the 50s BCE and four and a half centuries of Roman policy had maintained since ([[germanic-tribes]]). In the winter of 406 it was thinner than it had been in living memory, and on the far side of it, pushed west by the same Hunnic pressure churning the whole barbarian world, several entire peoples were looking for somewhere to go.

---

On the last night of 406, traditionally given as 31 December, they crossed. The "New Year's Eve" date comes from a single line in Jerome, the great Christian scholar of the day, naming the night the barbarians broke into Gaul; a minority of modern historians, rereading the chronology, have argued for late 405 instead. The year is firm; the famous midnight-on-the-river detail is best held as a memorable approximation, not a logged fact.

The **Crossing of the Rhine** put three peoples into Gaul more or less at once, and they are worth introducing properly, because two of them are not what the reader might assume.

The **Vandals** (the name is Germanic; the modern English word "vandalism" was coined from them in the eighteenth century, long after the fact, and tells you more about later propaganda than about the Vandals) were a Germanic people, Indo-European-speaking, from the forest-and-farm world east of the Rhine, the same broad cultural sphere as the Goths and the Franks. They came in two related groups, the Hasding and the Siling Vandals. They were, in language and origin, exactly the kind of people Rome had been managing on the Rhine for centuries.

The **Suebi** (pronounced "SWAY-bee") were also Germanic, a name attached to a sprawling and shifting set of peoples from deep in central Europe, the same broad Suebic world Caesar had fought when he drew that Rhine line ([[germanic-tribes]]).

The **Alans** (pronounced "AL-anz") were not Germanic at all, and this is the detail that matters. The Alans were an **Iranian-speaking** people, originally from the steppe and the Caucasus region between the Black and Caspian Seas. Their language belonged to the Iranian branch of Indo-European, the same branch as Persian, which makes them linguistic cousins of the Persians and only very distant relations of the Germanic peoples they were now marching alongside, roughly as distant as English is from Hindi. They were horse people, steppe people, not forest farmers, and they had been swept up and scattered by the same Hunnic shock that started this whole book. Some Alans had been absorbed into the Hunnic confederation; these were Alans who had run the other way and ended up bound to a Germanic migration heading into Gaul. That a steppe Iranian people, a Germanic forest people, and another Germanic people crossed the Rhine together as allies is a perfect snapshot of what the Völkerwanderung actually was: not one nation invading, but the debris of a continent in motion, clumping into whatever traveling alliance could survive.

These three peoples poured across the frozen river and spent roughly three years devastating Gaul, the rich, long-settled, thoroughly Roman heartland that is now France. They did not arrive with a plan to found kingdoms. They arrived as a moving disaster looking for land and food, and the Roman state, which should have met them at the river, was not there, because its army was in Italy and its politics had just shattered in the worst possible place.

---

The shattering happened in Britain, and it happened because of the Rhine, not separately from it. With the field army drawn into Italy to crush Radagaisus and the frontier left bare, the soldiers furthest from the capital concluded that the central government had abandoned them, and reached for the late Roman army's standard remedy for an absent emperor: they made one of their own.

When the Rhine collapsed, the Roman troops stationed in the diocese of Britain did what Roman armies in a panic had done for three centuries: they proclaimed their own emperor, on the theory that a local strongman was better than a distant boy who could not protect them. After two short-lived candidates they settled on a common soldier with a promising name, **Constantine III** (no relation to **Constantine the Great**, the emperor who a century earlier had reunited the empire by force and made Christianity its favored religion, and whose name had carried the shine of victory ever since; this Constantine simply took it because it sounded lucky, which tells you something about how the late Roman army chose emperors). In 407 Constantine III crossed from Britain into Gaul with the British garrison to make himself master of the West, fighting the barbarians and Honorius's loyalists at the same time.

This is the moment Roman Britain effectively ended, and the mechanism is worth being precise about, because the popular version ("the legions marched out and never came back") is roughly true but skips the why. Constantine III took the mobile field troops out of Britain to fight his civil war in Gaul. They were never replaced, because there was no functioning central authority left to replace them and every soldier was needed elsewhere. A few years later, around 410, with Gaul in chaos and the city of Rome itself under Gothic attack ([[the-goths]]), the British civitates, the self-governing Roman towns of the island, sent to the emperor Honorius asking for help against raiders. The reply, as the Eastern historian Zosimus reports it, was essentially that the cities should see to their own defense. Zosimus wrote his history of the empire's decline around the year 500, roughly a century after these events, and is our only real source for the moment, which means the famous "Rescript of Honorius" rests on a single, late, and somewhat garbled passage that historians still argue over. Whatever the precise wording, the substance is not in doubt: after roughly four centuries, Roman government in Britain stopped, not with a dramatic withdrawal but with a request for help that was declined and then never followed up. What happened on the island after that, the long fade of Roman life and the arrival of Angles, Saxons, and Jutes across the North Sea, is its own story ([[anglo-saxon-england]]). For this book, Britain is the first whole province to simply fall off the map, and it fell off not because barbarians conquered it but because Rome's own civil wars drained it and then forgot it.

And while the Rhine was breaking and Britain was sliding away, Rome did the most self-destructive thing available to it. In 408, with the frontier wide open and barbarians loose in Gaul, a court intrigue convinced the paranoid Honorius that Stilicho, his half-Vandal generalissimo, the one man who had repeatedly saved the West, was secretly plotting with the barbarians. Honorius had him arrested and executed. This is the chapter's hinge and its bitterest joke: at the exact moment the empire most needed the soldier of Vandal blood who kept beating barbarians, the empire killed him for having barbarian blood. Within two years the leaderless West suffered the unthinkable in Italy itself, the sack of the city of Rome ([[the-goths]]). Constantine III's revolt collapsed not long after; he was captured and executed in 411.

The damage was now structural and permanent, and its scale is hard to take in even at this distance. In the space of about five years a fortified river border four and a half centuries old had ceased to exist, an entire province had quietly fallen out of the empire, the Western field army had been burned up on a civil war and a Gothic crisis, and the only man who understood how to hold the system together had been killed by the government he served. Nobody alive had a map for what came next. The frontier was not coming back, and the peoples already inside it now had to become something, because there was no longer a Roman state in the West capable of telling them what to be.

# Chapter 3 — Kingdoms Inside the Empire

Here is the strange thing about the fifth century, the thing that makes it different from a simple story of invasion and conquest: most of the barbarian kingdoms that replaced the Western Roman Empire were not founded by conquest. They were founded by treaty. Rome gave the land away, on paper, with its own seal on it, and then discovered it could not take it back.

The instrument was the **federate settlement**, the *foederati* system from Chapter 1, and by the 410s and 420s it had become the central mechanism of Western politics. The mechanism deserves to be laid out concretely, because "Rome settled them as federates" is a result, and the reader needs the process. When a Roman government could neither defeat a barbarian people nor expel it, it offered a deal: the people would be assigned a defined territory inside the empire; its king would keep command of his own warriors; in return that king and his army were legally Roman allies, obliged to fight Rome's other enemies (very often other barbarians) when ordered. The Roman population in the assigned territory kept its own Roman law, its own bishops, and in principle its own property; the barbarians were quartered on it under arrangements historians still argue about. The argument is whether the newcomers took shares of the land itself or shares of the tax revenue the land produced, and the surviving evidence points different ways in different places, so the honest answer is that it varied. On parchment it looked like a Roman government deploying allied troops. On the ground it meant an armed foreign people with its own king now lived permanently inside the empire, drew Roman revenue, and could not be removed by the government that had "settled" it. Every such settlement was a Roman administrative document that was also, in slow motion, an independent kingdom.

The first peoples to turn the deal into durable kingdoms were not the most famous ones. They were the **Burgundians**.

---

The **Burgundians** (pronounced "BUR-gun-dee-anz") were a Germanic people, the same broad Indo-European, east-of-the-Rhine forest-farm world as the Vandals and Franks, who had crossed in the general collapse and were settled by Roman treaty on the middle Rhine around the city of Worms (a Roman town on the river in what is now western Germany) by about 411. Their first kingdom there left a long shadow: centuries later it became the seed of the great medieval German legend of the Nibelungs, the dragon-and-gold epic that the nineteenth-century composer Richard Wagner turned into his four-opera *Ring* cycle. The historical Burgundians and the legendary ones are joined by a real thread of memory and a great deal of later invention; the saga remembered that a Burgundian kingdom on the Rhine was annihilated, which is the one true bone inside the myth, and that annihilation is the actual story here. The first Burgundian kingdom did not last long. In the 430s the Roman commander Aetius (the dominant Western general of the next two decades, his full story in Chapter 6) destroyed it, with Hunnic mercenaries doing a large part of the fighting. This is the first place the Huns appear inside the story rather than behind it, and it is worth noting that Rome did not only fear them; it also hired them. Aetius had lived among the Huns as a young man and could call on Hunnic horsemen for pay, so the same people churning the eastern frontier were, for a generation, also Rome's most effective hired muscle against everyone else, until they stopped being for hire (Chapter 5). The Burgundian survivors were resettled by treaty far to the south, around Lake Geneva and the Rhône valley in what is now eastern France and western Switzerland, well away from the exposed Rhine. There they built a second, longer-lasting Burgundian kingdom whose name still sits on the French region and the wine. The Burgundians matter here less for themselves than as the clearest early demonstration of the pattern this whole book turns on: a people is settled by treaty, smashed by Rome using other barbarians, resettled by treaty, and ends up a kingdom Rome can no longer control.

The biggest treaty kingdom of all was Gothic. After the years in the Balkans, the sack of Rome in 410, and a long migration through southern Gaul and into Spain and back ([[the-goths]] tells this in full), the Visigoths were settled in 418 by formal Roman treaty in **Aquitaine** (pronounced "AH-kih-tane"), the rich southwest of Gaul, with their capital at Toulouse. This is the one to fix in mind, because it is the template: the first time the Western government assigned a major, prosperous, intact province to an armed barbarian people under its own king as a permanent home, on parchment, as Roman policy. The Visigothic kingdom of Toulouse is the prototype of the post-Roman successor state, and what it built in Gaul and later in Spain is the Gothic book's story, not this one. What this book takes from it is the precedent: after 418, "give a province to a barbarian king and call him an ally" was no longer an emergency improvisation. It was the constitution of the Western Empire.

Meanwhile the Rhine-crossers were still loose in Spain. After three years wrecking Gaul, the Vandals, Suebi, and Alans had been pushed south, across the Pyrenees into **Hispania** (the Roman name for the Iberian Peninsula, modern Spain and Portugal) around 409, partly under pressure from the Visigoths, who were doing exactly the federate job Rome had hired them for: fighting Rome's other barbarians on Rome's behalf. In Spain the four groups divided the provinces among themselves. The Siling Vandals and the Alans took the heaviest losses to Visigothic and Roman attacks; the surviving Alans, having lost their king and most of their fighting strength, gave up independent existence and folded themselves into the Hasding Vandals, which is why the Vandal kings afterward styled themselves "King of the Vandals and Alans." That is how a distinct Iranian-speaking steppe people disappears from history: not massacred to the last man but absorbed, its name surviving only as a title on someone else's king.

The longest-lived of all the Rhine-crossers' kingdoms was the quietest. The **Suebi** settled the far northwest corner of Hispania, **Gallaecia** (pronounced "gal-EYE-kee-ah," roughly modern Galicia and northern Portugal), around 411 and built a kingdom there that, against every expectation, lasted until the Visigoths finally absorbed it in 585, outliving the Western Roman Empire itself by more than a century. The Suebic kingdom of Gallaecia is one of the great forgotten states of European history, and its survival is the chapter's real lesson. The peoples whose names everyone remembers, the ones who sacked Rome and terrorized emperors, mostly did not last. The ones who lasted were the ones who stopped migrating: found a defensible corner nobody powerful wanted badly enough to fight for, settled it, and quietly became the government, until the difference between "barbarian war-band" and "the local state" had simply worn away. Survival in this age went not to the most spectacular but to the most boring. The Vandals were about to prove the rule by being the spectacular exception.

# Chapter 4 — The Vandals

The Vandals are the people who proved that a migrating barbarian nation did not have to settle for a corner of someone else's empire. It could take the richest piece, build a fleet, and become a Mediterranean power that the empire had to come and destroy in person.

The man who did it was **Geiseric** (pronounced "GUY-suh-rik," also spelled Gaiseric or Genseric), king of the Hasding Vandals and the Alans from 428 to 477, and he is one of the genuinely formidable political minds of the age, a fact the "vandalism" caricature has buried. He was, by the hostile Roman sources' own grudging account, lame from a fall, cold, calculating, ruthless, and a far better strategist than almost any Roman he faced. In 429 he made the decision that defined the western Mediterranean for the next century. He took the entire Vandal-Alan people, by the most-cited figure around eighty thousand of them, and ferried them across the Strait of Gibraltar out of Spain and into **Roman North Africa**. The eighty-thousand number comes from **Victor of Vita**, a North African Catholic bishop who wrote a bitter history of the Vandal kingdom and its persecution of Catholics in the 480s, two generations after the crossing. (A figure of this kind, handed down by a hostile churchman writing decades later, is the sort of detail this book treats as plausible-but-uncheckable rather than precise; modern historians use it for rough scale, not headcount.)

This was not a raid. It was a national relocation across a sea, and the target was chosen with precision. Roman North Africa, the coastal band of modern Tunisia, eastern Algeria, and western Libya, was not a frontier backwater. It was the breadbasket of the Western Empire, the single most valuable economic asset Rome had left. The mechanism that made it so was the ***annona*** (pronounced "uh-NOH-nah"): the state-run food supply, a centuries-old system under which African grain was levied as tax-in-kind, loaded onto a government-organized merchant fleet, shipped across the Mediterranean, and distributed to feed the population of the city of Rome at public expense. It was not a market; it was a logistical artery, the thing that physically kept the old capital alive, and the African tax revenue on top of it paid the Western army. Geiseric did not march on Carthage to plunder it. He marched on it to *own* the artery, and he understood, in a way few barbarian leaders did, that holding the switch on Rome's food supply was worth more than sacking Rome itself.

---

The conquest took ten years and it was patient. The Vandals moved east along the African coast, and in 430 they besieged the great port city of **Hippo Regius** (pronounced "HIP-poh REE-jee-us," on the coast of modern Algeria). The siege has a famous casualty. The bishop of Hippo was **Augustine** (pronounced "aw-GUS-tin" or "AW-gus-teen"), the most influential Christian thinker of the entire ancient world, whose *Confessions* and *City of God* shaped Western Christianity for the next thousand years and beyond, and who had in fact written *City of God* partly in response to the Gothic sack of Rome in 410, arguing that the eternal city of God was not the same as the perishable city of Rome. Augustine died inside Hippo during the Vandal siege in 430, an old man watching the Roman African world he had spent his life in being dismantled around him. The image of the West's greatest Christian intellect dying as the Vandals battered the walls is the kind of detail that is almost too neat, and it happens to be true.

In 439 Geiseric took the prize itself. **Carthage** had been, for six centuries, one of the great cities of the Roman world. Long before that it had been something else entirely: the capital of a Phoenician-descended maritime empire that fought Rome in the Punic Wars and was annihilated by it in 146 BCE, then refounded as a Roman colony and grown into the empire's second city in the West ([[carthage]] tells the older story). There is a grim symmetry, which contemporaries noticed, in a sea-power barbarian kingdom rising again at Carthage to throttle Rome's grain, six hundred years after Rome had destroyed the original Carthaginian sea power for the same threat. Geiseric took the city in 439, by most accounts through a surprise move while Roman attention was elsewhere, and he made it his capital. The Western Empire had now lost its breadbasket not to a raid but to a permanent rival kingdom.

The economic consequence was immediate and it is one of the most underrated turning points in this whole story. The *annona*, the African grain artery that had fed the city of Rome for centuries, simply stopped at the African end, because the African end now belonged to Geiseric. Rome the city did not starve overnight, but the Western Empire's largest single source of revenue and the machine that fed its capital were both gone, and both were now in the hands of a king who could switch them on and off as a weapon. An empire that cannot feed its capital or pay its army from its own taxes is no longer an empire in any functional sense. After 439 the Western Roman state was, fiscally, a hollow shell with a court inside it.

---

Then Geiseric did the thing no one expected a Germanic land people to do. He built a navy.

This was genuinely strange. The Germanic peoples of this era were not sailors; their power was warriors on land. But Geiseric grasped that whoever held Carthage and the African coast sat astride the central Mediterranean, and he built a Vandal fleet that within a year or two was raiding Sicily, Sardinia, the Balearic Islands, and the Italian coast at will. For about a generation the Vandals were the supreme naval power of the western Mediterranean, and the Roman and East Roman attempts to dislodge them by sea repeatedly failed, sometimes catastrophically. A barbarian kingdom had not just taken Rome's breadbasket; it had taken Rome's sea. This is one of the genuine "firsts" of the period: no Germanic people before had ever been a Mediterranean naval power (the Gothic sea-raids of the 250s, Greek-crewed and Gothic-led, had been Black Sea and Aegean affairs, [[the-goths]]), and none would be again until the Norse centuries later took to longships in a wholly different sea ([[vendel-scandinavia]] traces how that northern seafaring world was built). For the Vandals it was invented from necessity in a single generation, and it worked.

The fleet made possible the most famous Vandal act of all. In 455, after the murder of the Western emperor Valentinian III had thrown Italian politics into chaos and a complicated marriage-and-succession dispute (Geiseric had a dynastic claim through his son's marriage to a Roman princess) gave him a pretext, Geiseric sailed a Vandal army straight to the mouth of the Tiber and took the city of Rome. The **Sack of Rome in 455** lasted, by the standard accounts, about two weeks. It is worth being careful here, because this is exactly the kind of vivid event later writers reshaped. The contemporary evidence suggests the Vandals were systematic and thorough looters rather than indiscriminate burners; the bishop of Rome is said to have negotiated terms that spared the city wholesale slaughter and arson (the same bishop, Pope Leo, walks into the center of the next chapter, so he is left for it). The reputation for mindless destruction, the "vandalism" that gave the word to modern languages, is largely a later French coinage from the 1790s and a great deal of subsequent bad-press, not a clean reading of what happened in 455. What is not in doubt is the scale of the haul (the Vandals stripped imperial and religious treasure on a massive scale and shipped it back to Carthage along with high-value captives) and the symbolism. Forty-five years after the Goths had sacked Rome ([[the-goths]]), it had happened again, this time by a fleet from Africa, and this time the Western Empire was too weak even to retaliate. The Vandals went home to Carthage with the loot, and they kept everything: the city, the breadbasket, the fleet, the leverage. Geiseric had taken a beaten land people that crossed the Rhine on foot in 406 and, in one lifetime, turned it into the dominant power of the western Mediterranean and the only barbarian kingdom that could starve Rome at will. He died in his bed in 477, undefeated, having outlasted nearly every Roman emperor he had ever dealt with. It was the single most successful migration of the entire period, and nothing the Western Empire still had could touch it.

---

# Chapter 5 — Attila

While the Vandals were taking Africa, the man who had started the whole chain reaction back in Chapter 1, or rather his people, came to a single terrifying point. For about twenty years in the middle of the fifth century, the Huns stopped being a pressure transmitted through other peoples and became a direct force in their own right, under one ruler, and his name has outlived almost everyone else's in this book: **Attila** (pronounced "AT-ih-lah" or "uh-TILL-uh").

For the first two generations after 375 the Huns had been mostly a cause, not an actor. They sat on the Hungarian plain and the steppe behind it, taking tribute, hiring out as mercenaries to Romans (the West's general Aetius, who had lived among them as a hostage, used Hunnic troops repeatedly; his full story is Chapter 6), and rolling subject peoples, above all a large body of Goths, the **Gepids** (pronounced "GEP-idz," an East Germanic people related to the Goths, settled in the middle Danube region), and others, into their confederation. The Huns' power by the 430s rested as much on the Germanic and other subject warriors they commanded as on Huns themselves; this was a steppe empire, and steppe empires were always confederations of the conquered held together by a successful leader and the flow of plunder and tribute. Remove the successful leader and the flow, and they came apart with startling speed. Hold onto that. It is how this chapter ends.

Attila and his brother **Bleda** (pronounced "BLEH-dah") became joint kings of the Huns around 434. In 445 Attila murdered Bleda and ruled alone, removing the last internal check on a man whose entire strategy was the systematic extraction of gold by terror. Between roughly 441 and 450 he turned that strategy on the Eastern Roman Empire, devastating the Balkans (the mountainous region between the Danube and Greece, the land bridge guarding the approaches to Constantinople) in a series of campaigns and forcing the wealthy Eastern capital to buy him off. The size of the payments is one of the few hard numbers Priscus gives us: a treaty in 443 set the annual tribute at 2,100 pounds of gold and the back-payment owed at 6,000 pounds, and a harder treaty after the 447 campaign roughly tripled the annual figure. To feel the scale, the higher rate was on the order of what it cost to maintain a substantial field army, paid every year, simply to make one man not attack. This was the Hunnic system at its purest: not conquest, not settlement, but a protection racket run at the scale of an empire. The Eastern Empire paid because, with the Vandals loose in the Mediterranean and the West collapsing, fighting the Huns to a finish looked even more expensive than the tribute.

---

We have, for once in this book, a real eyewitness, and he deserves a proper introduction because almost everything vivid we know about Attila as a person comes from him.

His name was **Priscus** (pronounced "PRISS-kus"), a Greek-speaking Eastern Roman, a trained rhetorician and minor official, who in 449 accompanied a diplomatic embassy from Constantinople to Attila's court somewhere on the Hungarian plain. Priscus wrote up what he saw in a history that mostly does not survive, but the long passage describing the embassy was quoted at length by a later compiler and so came down to us, and it is the single most valuable primary source for the migration period: a literate, observant, slightly ironic Roman diplomat actually inside Attila's camp, recording what the place looked like. **Priscus's account** is why we can say anything concrete at all about the Hunnic court rather than just relaying Roman terror-propaganda.

What he describes is not a tent of savages. It is a large timber-built complex of halls and stockades, a royal compound, with Attila's wooden hall the grandest structure, attended by servants, with the court of subject Germanic kings and Roman renegades around him. (Priscus actually meets one of the renegades: a Greek who had been captured, prospered among the Huns, and now argued to Priscus's face that life under Attila was freer and fairer than life under Roman tax collectors. Educated Romans crossed over because the empire's own subjects were not always better off than its enemies, and Attila's court paid and protected men who could read, write, and negotiate for him.) Priscus records a banquet in detail: gold and silver plate and rich food set before the guests and the lesser lords, and Attila himself eating plain meat off a plain wooden platter and drinking from a plain wooden cup while everyone around him used precious metal. What Priscus reports is the contrast; he does not tell us Attila's reasons. But the contrast is worth pausing on, because the obvious reading, and it is a reading, an inference from the scene rather than something Priscus states, is that the austerity was deliberate. Surrounded by the looted wealth of two empires, Attila appears to have performed plainness, signaling that he, unlike the soft Romans and his own gilded subjects, did not need it, that his power came from the warrior and not the gold. If that reading is right, a man who can make wealth grovel by refusing it is a more dangerous politician than one who merely hoards it. What is on firmer ground is the tone of the source itself: Priscus, a Roman, clearly half-admired the man he was sent to flatter, and that complicated portrait, neither monster nor noble savage, is exactly why the source is trusted where the screaming chroniclers are not.

One more thing from the era's writers, flagged rather than repeated as fact: Roman sources describe some Huns as practicing the artificial elongation of infants' skulls by binding, and archaeology has indeed found deliberately deformed skulls across the Hunnic-period steppe and into Europe. The practice is real and attested. The lurid Roman descriptions of Hunnic appearance and "monstrousness," on the other hand, are ethnographic horror-writing of exactly the kind this book keeps warning about, and should be read as Roman fear, not Hunnic reality.

---

In 451 Attila turned west, and the chain reaction briefly ran in reverse: instead of pressure transmitted through the Germani into Rome, the Huns came directly into the heart of the West. **Attila invaded Gaul** with an enormous army, the Huns themselves plus a great mass of subject Germanic peoples, and drove through the cities of the Rhine and into central Gaul, sacking as he went.

What stopped him is the most revealing battle of the age, because of *who fought it on each side*. The Roman commander was **Aetius** (pronounced "ah-EE-shus" or "ah-EE-tee-us"), the last truly capable Western generalissimo, of whom much more in the next chapter. Aetius did not have a Roman army that could face the Huns alone; there was barely a Roman army left. So he did the thing the whole book has been describing: he assembled a coalition of the very barbarian kingdoms Rome had been settling for fifty years. The decisive contingent was Visigothic, the Aquitaine federates from Chapter 3, under their old king **Theodoric I** (not to be confused with the later Theodoric the Great, the Ostrogoth, who appears in Chapters 6 and 7; same name, two different famous Gothic kings two generations apart). Burgundians, Franks, and others joined too.

The **Battle of the Catalaunian Plains** (pronounced "kat-uh-LAW-nee-an," somewhere in the Champagne region of northeastern France; the exact site is unknown and debated) was fought in 451 and was one of the largest battles of late antiquity. It was, in its essence, a coalition of Germanic kingdoms led by a Roman general against a Hunnic confederation that was itself mostly Germanic subject troops. The old Roman-versus-barbarian frame had completely dissolved; this was barbarian Europe fighting itself with a Roman holding one of the leads. And the lead was not firmly in hand. Aetius's decisive contingent, the Visigoths, had every reason to fight the Huns and no particular reason to love Rome; the coalition held together for one battle out of shared fear of Attila, not out of loyalty to Aetius, and it would not outlive the day. Holding such an alliance steady long enough to fight at all was itself the harder half of Aetius's achievement. The battle was savage and indecisive in the technical sense, but Attila was checked, halted, and forced to retreat, the only major reverse of his career. The Visigothic king Theodoric I was killed in the fighting, trampled or struck down in the chaos, and his death became a celebrated heroic legend among his own people, a measure of how hard the day had been.

The Catalaunian Plains did not destroy Attila. The very next year, 452, he came back, this time straight into Italy, sacking the great northern city of Aquileia so completely that its refugees, by a much-loved tradition, helped found the lagoon settlements that became Venice (this is a charming origin story repeated for a thousand years; treat it as legend with a kernel, not as documented fact). Northern Italy lay open. And then Attila turned around and went home, and why he did is one of the famous puzzles of the period.

The legend, and it became one of the foundational legends of the medieval papacy, is that Pope **Leo I** rode out personally to meet Attila on the river Mincio in 452 and, by sheer moral authority, persuaded the scourge of God to spare Rome and withdraw. Leo (pronounced "LEE-oh," bishop of Rome 440–461, later called Leo the Great) is the man the previous chapter held back. He was one of the architects of **papal supremacy**, the claim that the bishop of Rome, as heir of the apostle Peter, held authority over the whole Christian Church and not merely his own city, an idea still in its early and contested days in the fifth century and one this man did much to push forward. A pope who faced down the terror of two empires and turned him back by words alone was exactly the kind of story that claim needed, which is precisely why it should be handled with care. **Pope Leo's meeting with Attila** is a real event; an embassy including Leo did go to Attila, and Attila did withdraw shortly after. The meeting happened. Whether the meeting *caused* the withdrawal is the contested part, and the papal legend leaves out the other reasons a coldly practical commander had to leave anyway: by the modern reconstruction his army was being wrecked by disease and famine in a ravaged Italy, his supplies were thin, and an Eastern Roman force was raiding the Hunnic homeland behind him. (That reconstruction is itself an assembly of clues from later sources, not a single eyewitness account, and historians debate its weighting.) The legend became powerful precisely because the Church needed it to be true: it is one of the clearest cases in this book of an event being remembered for what it was made to mean rather than what it demonstrably did.

---

Then the entire thing evaporated, almost overnight, exactly as a steppe empire built on one man was always going to.

In 453 **Attila died**, suddenly, the night after a feast celebrating yet another marriage, of, by the standard account, a haemorrhage, choking on his own blood in his sleep. The account is usually traced back to Priscus, but Priscus's own narrative of the death does not survive; it reaches us through Jordanes, a sixth-century writer who summarized a now-lost Gothic history a full century after the event, and through still later compilers. So the famous version is a tradition transmitted at two or three removes, not Priscus speaking to us directly, which is reason enough to hold the lurid bedchamber detail loosely. There was no plausible Hunnic institution to replace him, because the Hunnic empire *was* him plus the flow of plunder and the fear. His sons fought each other over the inheritance, as steppe heirs did, and the subject peoples the Huns had rolled up over three generations did the arithmetic and rebelled.

In 454, at the **Battle of Nedao** (pronounced "NEH-dow," somewhere in the province of Pannonia, in modern Hungary; the river and site are not securely identified), a coalition of the Huns' own former Germanic subjects, led by the Gepids under their king Ardaric, with Ostrogoths and others, destroyed the Hunnic army and the sons of Attila with it. The Hunnic empire did not decline. It vanished, inside a single year of Attila's death, and the Huns as a power simply cease to appear in the sources. This is the clearest demonstration in the entire book of the deepest mechanism of the age: a barbarian power built purely on a successful war-leader and the loyalty he could buy with victory and plunder was, by its nature, only as durable as the man at the top. Attila's empire reached from the Rhine to the steppe and lasted barely a generation. The Roman Empire it had terrorized was a creaking, hollow, badly-governed wreck, and it would still be standing, in the East, a thousand years later, because it was built on institutions and not on a man. The thing the Huns set in motion in 375 outlived the Huns by centuries. That is the irony Chapter 6 has to live inside, because the Western Empire was about to die anyway, and not from the Huns at all.

# Chapter 6 — The Fall of the West

The Western Roman Empire did not fall in a battle. There is no Hunnic horde at the gates, no apocalyptic siege, no last emperor dying sword in hand. It fell the way a company goes bankrupt: slowly, then in a single anticlimactic afternoon when someone finally said out loud that the thing everyone could already see was true. The date is famous, 476, and the most important thing this chapter has to do is explain both why that date matters and why a great many serious historians think it matters far less than the textbooks pretend.

The man holding the West together in its last functional decades was **Aetius** (the general from Chapters 3 and 5, now in full). Later writers, and a string of historians after them, called him "the last of the Romans," and the label is not unfair. Aetius ran the Western Empire as military strongman and power behind the throne from the 430s to the 450s. His method was not conquest; the West had nothing left to conquer with. His method was *management of barbarians by other barbarians*: balancing the Visigoths against the Vandals, hiring Huns against the Burgundians, the Burgundians and Visigoths against the Huns, keeping every barbarian power in the West checked by another so that none could swallow Italy and what was left of the central state. And what was left was thin: by the 440s the Western government effectively controlled Italy, a slice of southern Gaul, and the dwindling tax base those regions still yielded, having already lost Britain, most of Spain, and the African breadbasket. Aetius was not defending an empire so much as a fortified remnant of one. The Catalaunian Plains in 451 was his masterpiece, a coalition he stitched together precisely because Rome itself could no longer field a decisive army. It worked exactly as long as Aetius personally ran it, which is the problem with strongman systems and the reason this book keeps returning to it.

In 454 the emperor **Valentinian III**, jealous and badly advised, murdered Aetius with his own hand at court. There is a famous line attached to the act, in which an unnamed bystander tells the emperor he has cut off his right hand with his left. It is a good line and it makes the point exactly, but it has the shape of a later moral epigram polished by chroniclers writing after the fact rather than a verbatim record of something said in the room, and it is best read as the judgment history passed on the killing, not a transcript of it. Within a year, in 455, Valentinian was himself assassinated by men loyal to the murdered Aetius, and the resulting power vacuum is what gave Geiseric his pretext to sail on Rome (Chapter 4). After 455 the Western Empire enters its terminal phase, and its shape is the single most telling fact about the fall.

---

From 455 to 476, the Western throne went through roughly nine emperors in twenty-one years. Almost none of them ruled in any meaningful sense. They were figureheads, raised up and discarded by whoever commanded the actual soldiers, and the man who commanded the actual soldiers, for most of that period, was not Roman and could not legally become emperor himself.

His name was **Ricimer** (pronounced "RIH-kih-mer"), and he is the perfect emblem of the end. Ricimer was of Suebic and Visigothic royal descent, a Germanic barbarian by blood and an **Arian** Christian by faith. (Arianism was the rival, condemned form of Christianity most of the Germanic peoples followed, holding Christ to be lesser than God the Father rather than co-equal with him; the Roman population of the West held the opposite, and the gulf between the two ran through everything. The next chapter is built on it; for now it is enough to know an Arian was, to Roman eyes, the wrong kind of Christian.) As supreme commander of the Western army Ricimer was the real ruler of Italy from 456 to 472. But he could not take the purple himself, because a barbarian and an Arian was unthinkable as Roman emperor, so he became a kingmaker instead, installing pliable emperors and removing them when they showed independence. This is the late Western Empire in one biography: real power held by a Germanic general who exercised it through a series of Roman puppets because the *symbol* of Roman legitimacy still mattered even though the *substance* of Roman power was entirely in barbarian hands.

One of those emperors deserves more than a name, because he was the last serious attempt to reverse the decline, and his failure is the chapter in miniature. **Majorian** (pronounced "muh-JOR-ee-an"), emperor 457–461, was, by the testimony of even hostile sources, an able and energetic ruler. He passed laws shielding provincials from the tax collectors and money-lenders who were stripping them, forbade the demolition of Rome's ancient buildings for their stone, reformed the revenue system, and rebuilt a fleet from nothing for the campaign that mattered: the recovery of Vandal Africa, the breadbasket whose loss had hollowed the Western state in the first place (Chapter 4). He marched an army through Gaul and into Spain to embark it, and then, on the Spanish coast in 460, his invasion fleet was burned at anchor before it could sail, betrayed, the sources strongly imply, by men inside his own side. The one realistic plan to restore the West died with those ships. Majorian made peace with Geiseric on humiliating terms, marched his army home, and was met on the road by Ricimer, who could not tolerate a competent and independent emperor. Ricimer arrested him, deposed him, and had him executed five days later, in 461. He was, by the universal verdict of those who write about the period, the best emperor the West produced in its final century, and he reigned four years and ended in a ditch by the road. The eighteenth-century English historian Edward Gibbon, whose *History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* is still the most famous book ever written on this subject and whose framing still shapes how the period is taught, singled Majorian out as the last Western emperor who might have saved the West. After him there was no serious attempt; there were only puppets, and a man named Ricimer choosing them.

There was one genuine pocket of continued Roman life, and it is worth a sentence because it punctures the tidy story. In northern Gaul, cut off from Italy by the barbarian kingdoms, a Roman commander named Aegidius and then his son **Syagrius** (pronounced "see-AG-ree-us") held an independent rump Roman territory, the **Domain of Soissons** (pronounced "SWAH-sonn"). "Roman administration" there meant something concrete: Syagrius still kept a standing armed force, still collected taxes from the towns under him, still ran Roman courts in Latin under Roman law, and still styled his authority in Roman terms, simply without an emperor over him to report to. It was a working scrap of the Roman state running on inertia, and it kept running for years after there was officially any Western Empire to belong to. It survived until the Franks destroyed it in 486, meaning a piece of functioning "Roman Gaul" outlived the Western Roman Empire itself by a decade. The fall, in other words, was not a clean line drawn under a date. It was a long fade, with working fragments of Rome surviving here and there well past the moment the textbooks declare the empire gone.

---

The famous date. In 476 the army of Italy, by now overwhelmingly composed of Germanic federate soldiers, demanded land settlement in Italy itself, the same federate deal the rest of the West had already become. When the regime refused, the soldiers' commander, a Germanic officer named **Odoacer** (pronounced "OH-doh-AY-ker," of mixed and disputed barbarian descent, raised inside the Roman system like so many others), led them in a coup, removed the last Western emperor sitting in Italy, and made himself king. (The mainstream sources call Odoacer a Scirian; the **Scirians** were a small East Germanic people from the middle Danube, long since scattered and absorbed into the moving warbands of the age. The "part Hunnic" element is one minority reading of one source and should be taken as a maybe, not a fact.)

That last emperor is one of history's great ironic footnotes. He was a boy, a usurper's son, installed by his father barely a year earlier, and his name combined the name of Rome's legendary founder with the name of its first emperor in a diminutive: **Romulus Augustulus** (pronounced "ROM-yoo-lus aw-GUS-tyoo-lus"), "little Augustus." The Western Roman Empire, by the textbook story, ended when a Germanic soldier-king deposed a powerless teenager whose name was a mockery of the empire's own origins. Odoacer did not even kill him; he pensioned the boy off to a villa in Campania and, in a move that tells you exactly how little 476 meant to the people living through it, sent the imperial regalia *back to Constantinople*. The regalia were the physical insignia of a Western emperor, the diadem and the imperial robes, the objects that made an emperor an emperor; returning them was a deliberate statement that the West would have no more emperors of its own. The accompanying message said exactly that: the West no longer needed a separate emperor, and he, Odoacer, would govern Italy as the Eastern emperor's man, with the title of king.

Now the hedge, because this is the single most over-told moment in European history and the responsible thing is to take it apart.

The case that 476 matters: it is the last time anyone sat on a throne in Italy claiming to be Western Roman emperor. After it, Italy is ruled by barbarian kings who do not pretend otherwise. Symbolically, the line stops there.

The case that 476 barely matters, which is the majority scholarly view now and has been for decades: by 476 the Western "emperor" had been a powerless puppet for twenty years, and Italy had been run by Germanic generals the whole time; Odoacer simply stopped bothering with the puppet. There was still, technically, a legitimate Western emperor alive, **Julius Nepos** (pronounced "NEE-pos"). Nepos had been installed as Western emperor in 474 with Eastern backing, then chased out of Italy in 475 by the coup that put little Romulus on the throne, and had retreated to Dalmatia (the eastern Adriatic coast, in modern Croatia), where he went on calling himself emperor and, more to the point, where Constantinople went on recognizing him as the legitimate Western emperor while treating Romulus as a usurper's puppet. So the man Odoacer deposed in 476 was, in the East's eyes, not the real emperor at all. Nepos was not actually dead until his murder in 480, which makes the legal extinction of the Western line arguably 480, or arguably never, since the Eastern emperor always claimed to be emperor of the *whole* Roman world and simply regarded the West as temporarily under barbarian management. The Roman Empire did not "fall" in 476 in the East at all; it continued, governed from Constantinople, for another thousand years ([[byzantine-empire]]), and contemporaries in 476 did not experience the year as the end of anything. Most of them did not notice. The "fall of Rome in 476" is a periodization invented by much later historians who needed a tidy fence between "ancient" and "medieval," and the fence is drawn at 476 mostly because it is a clean and dramatic image, not because the structure visibly collapsed that year ([[ancient-rome]] tells the long arc of the empire itself).

The truthful summary is this: there was no single fall. There was a long structural dissolution, fiscal, military, and territorial, running from at least the Rhine crossing of 406, in which the Western Roman state lost the ability to tax, feed, and defend itself, and was gradually replaced from the inside by the barbarian kingdoms it had itself created through the federate system. 476 is the conventional headstone. The death was a process, and it was already most of the way done.

But "the death was undramatic" is not the same as "nothing died." Something genuinely ended in that century, even if not in that year: a single political authority that had run, in some form, from Britain to the Euphrates for five hundred years, that had moved grain across a sea to feed a million people, that had kept a professional standing army and a tax system and a common law over a quarter of humanity. By 476 a Roman child in Gaul or Spain or Africa would never again be ruled by a Roman emperor, and most of the machinery that word had once meant was simply gone. The anticlimax is the point, not a reason to think nothing was lost. An entire way of organizing the world had quietly stopped working, and the people who would now have to organize it instead were the barbarian kings the empire had spent a century inviting in. The one whose kingdom would matter most for the next thousand years was being assembled, just then, in northern Gaul, by a man Odoacer had probably never heard of.

# Chapter 7 — The Successor Kingdoms

By the 480s the political map of the former Western Empire was a patchwork of barbarian kingdoms: Vandals in Africa, Visigoths in Spain and southern Gaul, Burgundians in the Rhône valley, Suebi in Galicia, Ostrogoths about to take Italy, and, in the north of Gaul, a still-small people just consolidating into the kingdom that would outlast them all and give its name to a country. The deep question this chapter answers is not "who conquered what." It is: why did most of these kingdoms melt away within a couple of centuries, while one of them became the spine of medieval Europe? The answer is almost entirely about religion, and it turns on one baptism.

The kingdom that won was the **Franks**, and the man who made them was **Clovis** (pronounced "KLOH-vis"; the name is the same name that later French softens into "Louis," which is worth knowing because eighteen kings of France would carry it). The Franks were a Germanic people, Indo-European-speaking, the same broad cultural world as the Goths and Vandals, who had been settled along the lower Rhine for generations as Roman federates, fighting in and alongside the Roman army for a century before they ever ruled anything. Clovis became king of one Frankish group around 481 and spent thirty years doing what no one else in this book manages: he did not just carve out a kingdom, he welded a durable nation and a dynasty (the Merovingians) that would rule for two and a half centuries and then hand off to the line of **Charlemagne**, the Frankish king who around the year 800 would be crowned the first emperor in the West since 476 and whose realm is the seed of both France and Germany ([[early-medieval-europe]]).

His military record is the conventional part. In 486 he destroyed the **Domain of Soissons**, the last Roman holdout in Gaul (Chapter 6), absorbing its territory and its Roman population and administrators. He beat the **Alemanni** (pronounced "al-uh-MAN-ee," a confederation of Germanic peoples pressing on the upper Rhine, a different group from the Rhine-crossers of Chapter 2) around 496, then the Burgundians, and then, decisively, the Visigoths. But the military story is not why the Franks won, and saying "he conquered a lot" would be giving you the result without the mechanism. The mechanism is the baptism.

---

Here is the religious problem, and it is the hinge of the entire post-Roman world, so it is worth slowing down for.

Almost every Germanic ruling people that took over a piece of the Western Empire, the Visigoths, the Vandals, the Burgundians, the Ostrogoths, was Christian, but they were the *wrong kind* of Christian from the point of view of the Roman population they ruled. They were **Arians**. Arianism, named for a fourth-century priest called Arius, was a theological position holding that Christ the Son was created by, and subordinate to, God the Father, rather than being co-eternal and of one substance with him. The mainstream imperial Church, the one the surviving Roman population of the West belonged to, held the opposite (Christ co-eternal and consubstantial with the Father) and had formally condemned Arianism as heresy at the Council of Nicaea in 325 ([[ancient-rome]]). The Goths and the peoples who learned their Christianity from the Gothic missionary tradition had been converted to the Arian version in the fourth century (the mechanism of that conversion, and the Gothic bishop Ulfilas who created a Gothic alphabet to translate the Bible, belong to the Gothic book, [[the-goths]]).

The political consequence was poison, and it is a mechanism, not a footnote. In a Vandal, Visigothic, Burgundian, or Ostrogothic kingdom, the warrior ruling class was Arian and the conquered Roman population, including its single most powerful surviving institution, the Catholic bishops, was Nicene Catholic. The two groups worshipped separately, often would not intermarry across the divide, and regarded each other as heretics. The Roman bishops, who in the wreck of imperial administration had become the de facto civic leaders of their cities, organizing food, charity, defense, and the last threads of Roman literacy, were structurally unreconcilable with their own Arian kings. Some Arian kings, the Vandal Huneric most brutally, responded with outright persecution of the Catholic clergy, which only deepened the gulf. An Arian kingdom sat permanently on top of a population whose natural leaders it could never co-opt. That is a kingdom with a fault line running under its foundations.

Clovis did the one thing that fixed this, and it was probably the single most consequential decision of the entire migration period. He converted directly to **Catholic** Christianity, not Arianism, somewhere around 496–508 (the year is debated).

Almost everything we know about Clovis comes from one writer, and he needs introducing because he shapes the whole story. **Gregory of Tours** was a Gallo-Roman bishop writing in the 590s, two generations after Clovis, whose *History of the Franks* is the main narrative source for the Frankish kingdom's founding. He was also, by his own design, gloriously partisan: a Catholic bishop telling the story of how a Catholic king and the Catholic Church saved Gaul, in which Clovis is God's chosen instrument and his Arian and pagan enemies get the villain's lighting. Gregory's account of the conversion frames it as a desperate battlefield vow against the Alemanni, the Christian god granting victory in exchange for belief. The scene runs closely parallel to the famous conversion of Constantine the Great two centuries earlier, and scholars genuinely divide over whether Gregory built that parallel on purpose, to cast Clovis as a new Constantine, or whether the resemblance is the natural shape any "general converts and then wins" story falls into. Either way, the dramatic battlefield version is best read as edifying narrative rather than stenography. What is not in doubt is the conversion itself and which side of the divide it landed on. The **Baptism of Clovis** meant that the only major barbarian king in the West was now the same religion as the Roman population and, crucially, the same religion as the Catholic bishops. Overnight Clovis was not a heretic occupier the Gallo-Roman elite had to endure. He was their co-religionist, their defender, the orthodox king, and the bishops became his administrators, his propagandists, and his partners. The single institution that every other barbarian kingdom was structurally at war with became, in Francia, the Frankish crown's strongest ally. This is the foundation stone of medieval Europe: the alliance of the Frankish monarchy and the Catholic Church, and it is the direct seedbed of the world of [[early-medieval-europe]] and ultimately of Charlemagne.

The payoff came fast. In 507, at the **Battle of Vouillé** (pronounced "voo-YAY," near Poitiers in western France), Clovis destroyed the Visigothic army and killed their king Alaric II. The Visigoths, the prototype federate kingdom from Chapter 3, were driven out of Gaul almost entirely and confined to Spain, where they would rule for nearly two more centuries with their capital at Toledo and where, late in the sixth century, they too finally abandoned Arianism for Catholicism (under King Reccared in 589) precisely because the religious fault line had become untenable, vindicating Clovis's logic decades after his death. The deep story of the Visigoths in Spain is the Gothic book's ([[the-goths]]); what matters here is the principle their defeat proved. The Catholic king beat the Arian kings, and not by accident.

---

The successor kingdoms did one more thing that mattered as much as religion in the long run, and it shows they understood they were not destroying Rome so much as taking custody of it: they wrote down law.

The barbarian peoples had governed themselves for centuries by unwritten custom carried in the memory of elders ([[germanic-tribes]] explains why a society without a state had no need to write its law down). The successor kings, ruling mixed populations of barbarians and Romans, suddenly did need it written, in Latin, and the codes they produced are the most underrated achievement of the period. Clovis had Frankish customary law codified as the **Lex Salica** (pronounced "lex SAL-ih-ka," "Salian law"), around 507–511, a text that governed the Franks and influenced European law long after anyone remembered why. One clause barred daughters from inheriting certain family land. A thousand years later, French and English lawyers digging for precedent seized on that single sixth-century farm-inheritance rule and used it to argue that no woman could inherit the French crown, a doctrine that helped trigger the Hundred Years' War. A barbarian field-law about who got the homestead was still deciding who got to be king of France in the fourteenth century, which is the absurdity. The Burgundian king Gundobad issued separate codes for his Burgundian and his Roman subjects. The Visigothic king Alaric II, just before Vouillé, issued the **Breviary of Alaric** in 506, a simplified digest of *Roman* law for his Roman subjects, an Arian Gothic king carefully preserving Roman jurisprudence for the Romans he ruled. This dual-law pattern, one body of law for the conquerors, the surviving Roman law for the conquered, is the characteristic legal signature of the whole successor world, and it tells you these kings saw themselves not as the abolishers of Rome but as its heirs and managers, keeping the Roman population governed by Roman rules while their own warriors lived by their own.

The man who most explicitly claimed the role of Rome's heir was not a Frank. He was the Ostrogothic king **Theodoric the Great** (the second famous Theodoric; not the Visigoth killed at the Catalaunian Plains in Chapter 5). Theodoric, sent into Italy by the Eastern emperor in 489 to deal with Odoacer, defeated him and then had him killed in 493. The well-known version, that Theodoric struck him down personally at a banquet sworn to peace, rests on a single late and partisan source and is the kind of detail later writers liked to sharpen; it may be a literary heightening rather than an exact account, but the treachery itself, a negotiated peace followed by murder, is not seriously doubted. Theodoric then ruled Italy for over thirty years as a king who governed Romans through Roman administrators, kept the Senate, the consulship, the grain dole, and the law courts running, employed Roman intellectuals, and presented his Gothic kingship as the legitimate continuation of Roman government in Italy. Among those intellectuals was the philosopher Boethius, whom Theodoric later imprisoned and executed on suspicion of treason, and who used his time in the cell to write the *Consolation of Philosophy*, a dialogue in which Philosophy, personified, argues to a condemned man that fortune is fickle and worldly loss cannot touch what truly matters. It became one of the most copied and translated books of the entire Middle Ages, read for a thousand years partly because almost no one was better placed than a brilliant man awaiting execution to make the argument convincing. For a generation Theodoric was the effective overlord of the entire barbarian West, tied to the Visigoths, Vandals, Burgundians, and Franks by marriage alliance, the standard tool of the age: kings married their daughters and sisters into rival royal houses so that an attack on one ally was an attack on a son-in-law's or brother-in-law's kin, a web of obligation meant to make war between the partners politically costly. It worked while Theodoric lived and unravelled the moment he did not. The full Ostrogothic story belongs to the Gothic book ([[the-goths]]). What matters here is that around the year 500, the most stable, prosperous, and culturally Roman-looking state in the former Western Empire was an Arian Gothic kingdom run from Ravenna by a man who could not read easily but understood, completely, that the prestige of Rome was worth keeping even after its power was gone. The successor kingdoms were not the anti-Rome. They were Rome's executors, fighting over the estate.

And then the East decided the estate was not for sale.

# Chapter 8 — The Eastern Reconquest

There is a version of this story where the Western Empire falls, the barbarian kingdoms settle in, the dust slowly clears, and medieval Europe grows organically out of the wreckage. That version is wrong by about fifty years and one emperor, because the Eastern Roman Empire, which had never fallen and never stopped regarding itself as *the* Roman Empire with the West merely temporarily mislaid, decided in the sixth century to take the West back. The attempt half-succeeded, and the cost of the half-success did more damage to Italy than the entire previous century of barbarian migration. This is the cruelest irony in the book, and it has to be handled carefully, because the people who tried hardest to save Roman civilization in the West are the ones who finished destroying it.

The emperor was **Justinian** (pronounced "juss-TIN-ee-an"), who ruled the Eastern Roman Empire from 527 to 565, and the full account of his reign, the great domed church of Hagia Sophia, the comprehensive law code, his powerful wife Theodora, the Nika riots of 532 (a stadium crowd's fury that exploded into a week of burning that nearly toppled him), the long wars with Persia, belongs to the Eastern Empire's own book ([[byzantine-empire]]). What this book needs is the part that reached into the West. Justinian came to the throne with a coherent and, by the realistic standards of his own time, near-impossible ambition: the *renovatio imperii*, the restoration of the Roman Empire to its old western boundaries. It looked deranged to sober contemporaries for concrete reasons, not poetic ones: the West was a thousand miles of other people's kingdoms away, the East was already locked in an expensive war with Persia on its other frontier, and the treasury could not obviously fund a second great war at the same time. Reconquering the West meant betting the solvent half of the Roman world on a campaign most of his own advisers thought unaffordable. He had the wealth of the intact East, a brilliant general in **Belisarius** (pronounced "buh-LIH-sah-rius"), and an opportunity, because the Vandal and Ostrogothic kingdoms had both fallen into succession crises that gave him a pretext.

---

He went for the Vandals first, and it was stunningly fast. In 533 Belisarius landed in North Africa with a relatively small but highly professional army. The **Vandal kingdom**, which had taken Geiseric a decade of patient campaigning to build (Chapter 4) and had been the supreme power of the western Mediterranean for a century, collapsed in a matter of months. Belisarius beat the Vandal king Gelimer in two battles, entered Carthage almost unopposed, and by 534 it was over. The Vandals' century of dominance had been built on the fleet; on land, against a disciplined professional army that had simply landed behind the fleet and ignored it, they had no equivalent edge, were caught with their best forces away in Sardinia, and twice broke when an attack went wrong at the decisive moment. A sea power undone the instant the war moved ashore is the whole Vandal story in one campaign. Gelimer surrendered, was brought to Constantinople, made to walk in a Roman triumph through the streets (a ceremony the city had not staged in centuries), and then, having submitted, was not killed but pensioned off with estates in the East, the same soft landing Odoacer had given Romulus Augustulus. The Vandal warriors were broken up and drafted into the Eastern army, and the Vandals, as a distinct people, simply ceased to exist within a generation, like the Alans before them and the Huns before them. A century of Vandal Africa ended in a single short campaign, which tells you how hollow even the most spectacular barbarian kingdom could be once a real, funded, professional army came for it.

Then Justinian turned to Italy and the Ostrogoths, and here the wheels came off, slowly, over almost twenty years. The **Gothic War** in Italy, beginning in 535, was supposed to be a repeat of the African campaign: a quick professional reconquest of a barbarian kingdom in crisis. It was not. The Ostrogoths, after early collapses, found capable leaders (above all the king Totila, whose campaigns are the Gothic book's story, [[the-goths]]), and the war became a grinding, back-and-forth catastrophe lasting until 554, fought up and down the entire Italian peninsula. The city of Rome is the war in miniature. Belisarius took it in 536 and then held it through a year-long Gothic siege so brutal that the defenders cut the aqueducts to deny the besiegers, ending the supply that had watered the city for eight centuries. A few years later the Gothic king Totila took it back, and at one point, by the contemporary account, deliberately drove out or removed nearly the entire remaining population so that for a stretch the ancient capital of the world stood literally empty. Then it changed hands again, and again. Rome was besieged or sacked five times in twenty years by armies that both claimed to be saving it. The war was finally ground out not by Belisarius but by **Narses** (pronounced "NAR-seez"), an elderly court eunuch turned general. (Castrated men were a normal feature of the late Roman and especially the Eastern court; because a eunuch could found no dynasty and was barred from the throne, emperors trusted eunuchs with great power precisely because they could not use it to become emperor, and a few, Narses above all, turned out to be formidable field commanders.) Narses broke the last Gothic armies around 552 and brought Italy formally back under imperial rule, sealed by Justinian's **Pragmatic Sanction** of 554, a sweeping imperial decree (a *pragmatic sanction* was a law issued to settle the permanent constitutional arrangements of a territory) that re-established Roman law, restored confiscated property, and reorganized the administration of the peninsula.

---

And here is the point of this chapter, the thing that makes the Eastern reconquest a tragedy rather than a triumph.

The Gothic War destroyed Italy. Not metaphorically, not in the sense of "ended an era," but materially, demographically, physically. Theodoric's Ostrogothic Italy, the one from the end of Chapter 7, had been the best-preserved fragment of the Roman world: cities still functioning, the Senate still meeting, aqueducts still running, the grain dole still operating, the countryside still farmed. Twenty years of total war between two armies that both wanted to *keep* Italy, and therefore fought over every city in it again and again, did what a century of barbarian migration had not. Our fullest account comes from **Procopius** (pronounced "proh-KOH-pee-us"), a Greek-speaking lawyer from Caesarea who served on Belisarius's staff as a legal and administrative aide and wrote the major contemporary history of Justinian's wars. He is an unusually good source for the parts he witnessed in person, the African campaign and the early years in Italy, where he was on the spot; he is a step further removed for the long later phases of the Gothic War, which he reconstructed from reports rather than from his own eyes, and his other writings show he had his own sharp opinions about Justinian. Used with that calibration, Procopius is the closest thing the period has to a front-line correspondent. He describes Italian cities depopulated, the countryside reverting to swamp and waste, populations starved to death inside besieged walls, Rome itself reduced for a time to a near-empty ruin with the Senate effectively extinguished as a functioning body. The reconquest "restored" Roman Italy by wrecking the last living piece of it. The barbarians had taken over a working Roman world and largely kept it running because they wanted to live in it. The empire that came to liberate it fought it to rubble, because liberating it meant taking every fortified city in it by force, twice.

There is a structural lesson here that ties the whole book together, and it deserves to be said on its own. The barbarian kingdoms, for all the "vandalism" the word implies, generally had every incentive to *preserve* what they had taken; they were moving in, not burning down. The single most destructive event in the entire two-century story of the Western collapse was not a barbarian invasion at all. It was the Roman Empire's own war to reverse the collapse. The people who cared least about keeping Roman Italy intact, it turned out, were the Romans who came to save it, because saving it on their terms meant storming every city the Goths held, and the Goths held all of them.

And the empire could not even keep what it had paid so dearly to retake. While Justinian's armies were bleeding Italy white, two things were happening that he could not control and barely understood, and they are the subject of the last chapter.

---

# Chapter 9 — The End of Antiquity in the West

Most books about the fall of Rome end with a battle or a deposition. This one ends with the weather and a disease, because the thing that finally closed the ancient world in the West was not a barbarian king. It was, in the space of about a decade, the sky going dark and a third of the people dying, on top of everything in the previous eight chapters. The migration period does not end with a conquest. It ends with a planet-scale catastrophe landing on a world that had already been hollowed out and was in no condition to absorb it.

In the year **536**, the sun dimmed. This is not a metaphor and it is not legend; it is one of the better-attested events in the whole book, which is why it can be stated more plainly than most. Multiple contemporary writers across the Mediterranean, Procopius in the East among them, independently recorded that for somewhere between a year and eighteen months the sun gave a feeble, bluish light, like a perpetual eclipse, that it cast no proper heat, that the moon too was dim, and that crops failed and frosts came in summer. For a long time historians treated these reports as exaggeration. They are not. The **dust veil of 536** is now confirmed by hard physical evidence completely independent of the written sources. A tree lays down a wide growth ring in a warm year and a thin one in a cold year, so a long-lived tree is a year-by-year temperature diary; trees across the Northern Hemisphere all recorded a catastrophic, multi-year collapse in growth beginning in 536. Ice cores tell the matching story: each annual layer of polar ice traps whatever was in the air that year, and the layers around 536 hold massive spikes of volcanic sulfate, the chemical signature of one or more colossal eruptions throwing enough material into the stratosphere to dim the sun across the planet. The current scientific reading is that 536 begins one of the coldest decades in the last two thousand years, a genuine volcanic winter, and that the famine it caused fell across the entire post-Roman world at exactly the moment that world was least able to feed itself. The same darkened sky fell hardest of all on the thin-margin farming country of Scandinavia, where it nearly depopulated the Germanic homeland ([[vendel-scandinavia]]). This is the rare case in the book where the physical evidence does not just support the written sources but sharpens them, instead of the usual situation where the texts are all we have.

Into that cold, hungry, half-empty world came the worst pandemic the West would see until the Black Death eight centuries later.

---

In 541 a plague broke out in Egypt, reached Constantinople in 542, and then spread along the Roman world's still-functioning sea-lanes and trade routes into North Africa, Italy, Gaul, Spain, and on to the British Isles. The **Plague of Justinian** was the first historically documented pandemic of *Yersinia pestis*, the same bacterium that caused the Black Death. The identification of the pathogen is now firm, not a guess: it rests not only on Procopius's clinical description of the swellings (the buboes) but on the bacterium's own DNA recovered from the teeth of people buried in sixth-century plague graves. What the genetics settles is *what* the disease was; how *bad* it was is a separate and genuinely contested question. Older scholarship put the toll in the tens of millions and treated it as a continent-emptying catastrophe on the scale of the Black Death; a strand of recent work argues the most apocalyptic numbers are an artifact of dramatic sources and limited evidence, and that the real mortality, while severe, may have been closer to the low millions and very unevenly spread. Historians actively disagree, sometimes sharply, and the reader should hold the specific death tolls loosely. What is not seriously disputed is that it was a real bubonic pandemic, that it hit a population already weakened by the climate disaster of 536 and (in Italy) by twenty years of Justinian's war, that it recurred in waves for the next two centuries, and that it broke the back of Justinian's reconquest. The Eastern Empire strong enough to retake Africa and Italy in the 530s could no longer raise the men or the money to defend those gains in the 550s, and over the following years it began losing ground in Italy almost as soon as it had taken it, the next chapter of which is the Lombards. The reconquest's failure was sealed not on a battlefield but by a bacterium.

So when the last great migration of this book arrived, there was almost nothing left to defend Italy with.

---

In 568, just fourteen years after Narses had finished ruining and "restoring" Italy, a new Germanic people walked into it almost unopposed. The **Lombards** (pronounced "LOM-bardz," from Latin *Langobardi*, traditionally derived from "long-beards") were a Germanic people, the same Indo-European, originally-northern world as the Goths and the rest, who by tradition had migrated south from somewhere around the lower Elbe or the Baltic over centuries and had spent the early sixth century settled in Pannonia (the old Roman province on the middle Danube, roughly modern western Hungary, a short march northeast of the head of the Adriatic and the open road into Italy) as Eastern Roman allies, absorbing other peoples, the Gepids especially, on the way, in exactly the snowball fashion this whole book has described. Their king was **Alboin** (pronounced "AL-boyn"), a figure who comes down to us wrapped in a particularly grim saga: he had killed the king of the rival Gepids and, by a story Lombard tradition preserved and later Lombard historians repeated, had a drinking cup made from the dead king's skull and forced the dead king's daughter, whom he had taken as his wife, to drink from it, which by the same tradition is why she eventually had him murdered. Treat the skull-cup as the Lombards' own dark legend about themselves rather than a securely documented fact; what is documented is the migration.

Alboin led the entire Lombard people, plus the assorted other groups they had rolled up, out of Pannonia and into Italy in 568. They met little serious resistance, because Italy had been destroyed twice over (by the Gothic War, then by plague and famine) and the exhausted Eastern garrison could not hold the open country. The Lombards took the Po valley, made Pavia their capital after a long siege, and over the following decades carved up the peninsula. But they could not take everything; the Eastern Empire clung to a string of fortified zones (Ravenna, Rome, the south, the coasts), governed eventually through an official called the Exarch. The result was the political shape Italy would keep for two centuries and in some sense for over a thousand years: not a unified country but a permanent patchwork of Lombard duchies and Byzantine enclaves, with the bishop of Rome (the pope) increasingly governing the city and its hinterland as a power in his own right because no one else functionally could. "Governing" was literal: the popes of this era organized the grain supply and charity for a starving city, paid and negotiated with Lombard war-bands to keep them off Rome, repaired walls, ransomed captives, and ran the vast estates the Church had accumulated as, in effect, the city's treasury and civil service. The spiritual office had quietly become the local state because the local state had stopped existing. The Lombard invasion of 568 is the last of the great migrations of the Völkerwanderung, and it is fitting that it ends not with a new empire but with fragmentation made permanent.

---

So what was the migration period, in the end?

Trace the chain back. A steppe people Rome had never heard of, the Huns, non-Germanic, non-Indo-European, language unknown, came off the grass in 375 and broke the Goths. The Goths fell into the empire. The pressure ran down the line of peoples like dominoes, and the Rhine gave way in 406, and the western frontier never re-formed. The peoples who poured through, Vandals, Suebi, Alans, Burgundians, Goths, Franks, were not, mostly, conquerors with a plan; they were a continent in motion looking for land and food, and the Roman state, at every step, chose the deal in front of it over the worse alternative, until it had given away its provinces, its breadbasket, its army, and finally its own existence in the West. The Huns peaked under Attila and vanished within a year of his death, because a power built on one man is only as durable as the man. The Western Empire dissolved so gradually that 476 is best understood as a convenient headstone on a death that took seventy years. The Eastern Empire's attempt to reverse it all destroyed Italy more thoroughly than the migrations had. And then the sky darkened and the plague came, and what was left of the ancient economic and demographic world in the West simply could not be put back.

What survived the catastrophe is the point, and it is why this book hands forward rather than just stopping. It was not the empire. It was the kingdoms the empire had created through the federate system and could not control: above all the Catholic Franks under the Merovingian heirs of Clovis, whose alliance with the Roman Church became the structural foundation of medieval Europe, the world that grows directly out of this wreckage and is the subject of [[early-medieval-europe]]. And it was the Church itself, whose bishops had quietly become the only continuous administrative institution standing when the imperial government dissolved, carrying Latin literacy, civic organization, and a thread of Roman continuity across the gap. Out on the western edge there was the other strand, the one that left the Roman world entirely: the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes crossing the North Sea into the island Rome had let go in 410. Where the Franks moved into a still-working Roman Gaul and ruled it through its Latin Church, taxes, and surviving elite, the newcomers in Britain arrived in a province whose Roman institutions had already collapsed and built on top of the rubble rather than inheriting it, even the Latin language and the imperial Church largely lost there for a century or more. That is what "genuinely post-Roman" means, and it is its own story ([[anglo-saxon-england]]). And running underneath all of it, the Gothic thread, the people who were the first crack and who built kingdoms in Italy and Spain on the way to vanishing, which deserves and gets its own telling ([[the-goths]]).

The ancient world in the West did not fall in a day or in a battle. It was knocked off balance by people fleeing other people, kept upright for a while by deals that gave away a little more each time, hollowed from the inside until the shell could not bear its own weight, and then finished off, almost as an afterthought, by a volcano and a bacterium. What rose in its place was not Rome restored. It was Europe, beginning.

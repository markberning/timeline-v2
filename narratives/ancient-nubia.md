# Ancient Nubia — Between the Deserts, Along the Nile

**Goal:** Narrative for the first TL in the Nubian Tradition chain. Covers roughly 3800–1070 BCE — from the A-Group culture and the Egyptian invention of the name "Land of the Bow" through the rise, peak, and fall of the Kerma kingdom and the long New Kingdom occupation that followed. Like Indus Valley and early China, Nubia is a civilization known mostly through archaeology, complicated here by the fact that nearly every surviving *written* source about it was produced by Egyptians — often by Egyptians at war with it. The narrative has to read Egyptian propaganda against the archaeological evidence and push back where the two disagree.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — Land of the Bow

The land south of Egypt has always been called something by somebody else.

The Egyptians called it **Ta-Seti** (pronounced "tah-SET-ee"), meaning "Land of the Bow," in honor of the archers who lived there. The Greeks later called it **Aethiopia**, "land of burnt faces," a name that eventually drifted southeast and stuck to a different country. The Romans called its southern half **Nubia**, probably from an Egyptian word for gold. Medieval Arab geographers called its kingdoms **Bilad al-Sudan**, "land of the blacks," which became the name of the modern country where most of this book takes place.

None of those are the name the people who lived there called themselves. We do not know the name the people who lived there called themselves, because for most of their history they did not write it down. They were writers' neighbors, not writers, and the writers who did record them wrote in a language that belonged to somebody else. This is the central problem of the story you are about to read, and it is worth saying up front: almost every surviving written word about ancient Nubia was produced by Egyptians, who were not always telling the truth and never telling the whole story. We are going to lean hard on archaeology — on pottery, tombs, temples, and the footprints of mud-brick cities — to push back when the Egyptian sources are clearly lying.

But the land was there, and people lived on it, and some of what they built is still standing four thousand years later. Let's start with the land.

---

If you follow the Nile south from Cairo, the river runs clean and wide for about 500 miles (800 km) through a narrow green ribbon of farmland flanked by desert. This is Egypt. The ribbon gets greener as you go south, the desert closes in on both sides, and eventually the river hits a town now called **Aswan** (pronounced "ass-WAHN"). At Aswan, the Nile runs into a rock.

Specifically, it runs into a granite ridge cutting across its path. The granite is too hard to erode cleanly, so the river does not flow over it in a neat waterfall. It flows *through* it in a mile or more of rapids, islands, boulders, and whitewater channels. Ancient travelers called these stretches **cataracts** (pronounced "KAT-uh-rakts"), and if you grew up picturing cataracts as waterfalls, adjust: a Nile cataract is a long, dangerous stretch of rapids caused by hard bedrock pushing up through the riverbed, not a single drop. A fully loaded Egyptian river boat could not navigate one without being dragged overland.

There are six cataracts along the Nile between Aswan and the river's main junction at modern Khartoum (the capital of Sudan). That junction is where the Nile actually begins, in the geographical sense: it is the point where two upstream rivers — the **White Nile**, flowing in from the great lakes of central Africa to the south, and the **Blue Nile**, flowing in from the Ethiopian highlands to the southeast — meet and become the single Nile that carries water north all the way to the Mediterranean. The two tributaries get their color names from the kind of silt they carry: the White Nile runs clear and pale, the Blue Nile runs dark and sediment-heavy, especially during the summer rains in Ethiopia that drive the annual Nile flood. The Blue Nile contributes the vast majority of the flood waters and the fertile silt that made Egyptian agriculture possible; the White Nile contributes steadier year-round flow. For the entire stretch of river this book covers, the "Nile" is the combined river north of Khartoum — and everything happens between Khartoum and the Mediterranean coast.

The cataracts are numbered from north to south, so the **First Cataract** is the one at Aswan, and the **Sixth Cataract** is the farthest south, just north of Khartoum. Between the First and Second Cataracts the river passes through a stretch now almost entirely drowned under Lake Nasser, the enormous reservoir created by the Aswan High Dam in the 1960s. Between the Second and the Sixth, the river runs through the heartland of ancient Nubia, through landscapes that are hotter, drier, and more demanding than Egypt proper.

The cataracts are the main reason Egypt and Nubia ended up as two separate countries instead of one continuous Nile Valley civilization. They were natural choke points: you could defend a border at a cataract, tax the traffic passing it, or just give up trying to push further upstream. For four thousand years, the First Cataract at Aswan was Egypt's southern frontier, and everything beyond it — everything deeper into the continent, everything between Aswan and the White and Blue Niles meeting at Khartoum — was Nubia.

Nubia itself had two halves, and the names for them are backward from how a modern map-reader wants to think. Rivers are described upstream or downstream, not north or south, so **Lower Nubia** is the *northern* half (closer to Egypt, downstream) and **Upper Nubia** is the *southern* half (farther from Egypt, upstream). Lower Nubia is the stretch from the First Cataract to the Second — narrower, drier, rockier, easier for Egypt to reach and harder to farm. Upper Nubia is everything from the Second Cataract south — wider floodplains, deeper soils, a real river valley broad enough to support big cities. When you hear about the gold mines, Lower Nubia is usually where they were. When you hear about the great Nubian kingdoms, Upper Nubia is usually where they were based.

One more name to get straight. Egyptians had two geographic terms for the parts of Nubia they cared about. **Wawat** (WAH-waht) was Lower Nubia, between the First and Second Cataracts — the part they could usually reach with an expedition force. **Yam** (yahm) was a more distant land far to the south, probably the region around the Third Cataract that would later become the heart of the Kerma kingdom. Wawat was where you went for gold. Yam was where you went for the really exotic stuff: ebony, ivory, incense, leopard skins, live animals, and occasionally a dancer.

A third name, **Kush** (rhymes with "push"), doesn't appear in Egyptian sources until the Middle Kingdom and then gradually swallows everything else. By the New Kingdom, "Kush" has become the catchall Egyptian term for Nubia and its rulers, and it will eventually become the name the Nubians themselves use when they finally conquer Egypt a thousand years later (Kingdom of Kush Ch 2-3).

So: one river, six cataracts, two Nubias, and a sequence of names (Ta-Seti, Wawat, Yam, Kush) that should tell you a neighbor is paying close attention.

---

Now for the people.

In 3800 BCE, when our story opens, the Nile valley south of the First Cataract was already home to farming and herding communities — people growing emmer wheat and barley in the narrow strip of floodplain, running cattle, fishing the river, and hunting in the desert fringes. They were part of a broad cultural world that also included **predynastic Egypt** downstream. ("Predynastic" is the term Egyptologists use for the era before the unification of Egypt under a single pharaoh — that is, before about 3100 BCE, the point at which the traditional king lists start counting dynasties from the First Dynasty onward. Predynastic Egypt is the eight or ten centuries of pre-pharaonic village and town cultures along the lower Nile, already farming and making pottery and trading, but not yet unified under a single king or writing in full hieroglyphs.) At this distance of time the archaeology shows us, on the Nubian and Egyptian sides of the border together, a single mixing bowl rather than two cleanly separate peoples. Nobody in 3800 BCE would have said "I am Nubian" or "I am Egyptian." Those categories didn't exist yet. What existed was a series of villages up and down the river, each with its own cemetery and its own pottery style and its own grudges. Over in southern Iraq, at almost exactly the same moment, the Sumerian town of Uruk was starting its explosive growth into what would become the world's first real city (Mesopotamia Ch 3) — the upper Nile and the lower Tigris were two zones of the Bronze Age that were crossing the complexity threshold on parallel tracks.

Egyptian records from a few centuries later call the land south of Aswan **Ta-Seti**, the Land of the Bow, in honor of its archers. When Egypt began to organize itself into administrative districts called **nomes** (a Greek word meaning "province"), Ta-Seti was the first one — the southernmost nome of Upper Egypt, the province right at the border. The name is a clue. Before Egypt was even unified as a single kingdom, before the pharaohs, before the pyramids, the reputation of Nubian archers was already famous enough that the province at the edge of the Nubian world was named for their weapon. When the Egyptians met Nubians on a battlefield, the thing they remembered afterward was the bows.

Nubian archers were long-range specialists. They used a **composite bow** — a bow built up in layers from multiple materials (typically a wooden core with animal horn glued to the belly and sinew glued to the back) rather than being carved out of a single stick of wood. A composite bow stores more energy for the same length, shoots farther, and is more compact than a simple wooden self-bow, which is why it was the high-end weapon of choice across the ancient world wherever archers mattered. Nubian bowyers were good at them, and Nubian archers had a reputation across thousands of years, in Egyptian, Greek, and Roman sources, for hitting what they aimed at. The Egyptians hired them as mercenaries from the very beginning and never stopped. Nubian archers served in Egyptian armies throughout the Old Kingdom, the Middle Kingdom, and the New Kingdom; Nubian mercenaries turn up in texts from Syria to Mesopotamia. If there was a single cultural export that Nubia sent north more reliably than gold, it was archers.

The standard Egyptian propaganda image of a pharaoh in triumph shows him standing in a chariot, drawing a bow, shooting foreign enemies. The irony is that a lot of the time, the guys actually shooting for him in the line behind him were Nubian.

---

The people who lived in Lower Nubia between roughly 3500 and 2800 BCE were given a bland letter-label by the first archaeologists who found them: the **A-Group Culture**. The naming convention is worth understanding, because we are going to meet a B-Group (briefly), a C-Group, and a Pan-Grave culture before this chapter is over, and the letters are going to look confusing.

An "archaeological culture" is not an ethnicity or a kingdom or a named people. It is a cluster of distinctive material traits — pottery styles, burial customs, tool kits, house plans — that archaeologists find together in the ground and group under a single label. The label is a filing system, not a self-identification. The A-Group people did not call themselves the A-Group. They called themselves something we don't know, in a language we can't read, because they didn't write. When the British-led expedition that excavated much of Lower Nubia in the early twentieth century started finding distinct groups of cemeteries and village sites, they labeled the successive layers with letters, in alphabetical order, because they had no better information to go on. The labels stuck.

What we know about the A-Group we know from their graves and their trash.

The graves are simple by pyramid standards but unusually rich for their era. A-Group burials in Lower Nubia routinely contain imported Egyptian pottery, copper tools, **stone palettes** (flat carved stones used for grinding mineral pigments into cosmetics — eye paint, body paint, ritual coloring — which in predynastic Egypt gradually evolved into elaborately decorated ceremonial objects that were traded as status markers and placed in elite burials as grave goods), beads, and in some cases items that look like Egyptian royal insignia: decorated incense burners, stone maces with carved heads, and cylinder-shaped stone seals used to stamp a personal mark into wet clay. This is how we know they were trading intensively with predynastic Egypt: the Egyptian stuff shows up in their graves. The trade flowed both ways. A-Group pottery is distinctive enough that archaeologists can identify a single sherd from across a gallery: the surfaces are burnished to a high sheen, the forms are mostly bowls and beakers, and the best pieces are thin-walled, small, and painted or rippled with a distinctive zigzag texture. Egyptian palette makers wanted the beautifully veined stone from Nubia; Nubian elites wanted Egyptian copper and the stonework that came with the copper.

The pottery itself is one of the technical wonders of the ancient world. A-Group potters made hand-built bowls and beakers with walls thin enough that light passes through them when you hold them up to the sun — the so-called "eggshell ware." No wheel. No kiln in the modern sense. Just sharp hands, long experience, and a wood fire. Egyptian pottery of the same era, made on a slow wheel by specialists, is thicker, coarser, and less impressive as craft. When later historians say that Nubia was a backward cultural province in the shadow of a more advanced Egypt, they are wrong on technical grounds as well as moral ones. In some things, the Nubians were the better artisans.

The A-Group lived in small villages along the river, grew grain, herded cattle, hunted, and fished. There is no sign of cities in the Mesopotamian sense, no sign of writing, no sign of kings with sweeping administrative reach. What there is, on the other hand, is increasingly unmistakable evidence of social hierarchy. Some A-Group graves are much richer than others. Some are so much richer that the people excavating them started to wonder whether they were looking at ordinary village elites or at the tombs of actual kings.

That wondering led, in 1979, to one of the noisiest arguments in the history of African archaeology.

---

The argument happened at a place called **Qustul** (KOOS-tool), on the east bank of the Nile in what was then Lower Nubia and is now underwater beneath Lake Nasser. In the 1960s, before the Aswan High Dam flooded the region, a salvage expedition from the University of Chicago raced to excavate the sites that the rising water was about to drown forever. At Qustul, they found a cemetery containing about thirty-three large tombs, some of them oversized by any A-Group standard, dating to around 3200 BCE. The cemetery was given the designation **Cemetery L**, in the unromantic alphabetical order that archaeologists use when they run out of memorable names.

Cemetery L was a shock. The tombs were big. They contained hundreds of ceramic vessels, carved stone objects, beads, weapons, and — most interestingly — several incense burners with carved decoration. On one of those incense burners, someone had carved a scene that appeared to show a figure seated in a boat, wearing what looked like an early version of the **White Crown** of Upper Egypt (the tall bowling-pin-shaped royal hat that would become one of the two crowns of unified pharaonic Egypt). In front of him, also on the boat, was what appeared to be a royal palace facade called a **serekh** — a rectangular symbol that later became the standard Egyptian way of enclosing a king's name.

In 1979, a University of Chicago archaeologist named **Bruce Williams** published a paper arguing that the Qustul Cemetery L burials were not just rich Nubian graves. They were the tombs of kings. Specifically, he argued, they were the tombs of *pharaonic* kings — a line of A-Group Nubian rulers whose royal iconography, including the White Crown and the serekh, predated the first confirmed Egyptian pharaohs by a generation or two. The contemporary Thinite kings at Abydos — Iry-Hor, Ka, Scorpion II — were developing their own royal trappings at precisely the same moment (Early Dynastic Egypt Ch 4). If Williams was right, then Egyptian kingship itself had been borrowed from Nubia. The institution that would define Egypt for three thousand years might have come from Ta-Seti, not from the Nile Delta.

This was, to put it mildly, controversial. It has never stopped being controversial. Some scholars took Williams's argument seriously and pointed out that the material at Qustul does, genuinely, look royal by the standards of that era. Others argued that the iconography on the incense burner could be read in other ways — as a depiction of an Egyptian king visiting or being honored by a Nubian elite, or as shared regional imagery that nobody had invented yet. The strongest objection is that the Qustul tombs, even at their richest, were smaller than the earliest confirmed Egyptian royal tombs at Abydos (a site in southern Egypt where the first pharaohs were buried). A lot smaller. And the White Crown itself appears in Egyptian art before the Qustul burners, in contexts that nobody disputes.

So where does the argument stand now? Roughly like this. It is a minority view today that the Qustul Cemetery L rulers were pharaohs in the full sense, but almost nobody denies that they were some kind of kings, or that A-Group elites at Qustul and other sites were running something much more complex and hierarchical than the "village chief" model that older histories used to assume. What is certain is that somewhere around 3300 to 3100 BCE, Nubian and Egyptian elites along the Nile were developing royal trappings at the same time — crowns, boats, thrones, seals — and the idea that Egypt invented all of it and Nubia was just a late borrower is no longer defensible. The two civilizations were sharing a symbolic vocabulary. Who borrowed what from whom, and when, and in what direction, is still being argued. (This is, incidentally, the same shape of argument that keeps haunting Mesopotamian/Egyptian disputes over who invented writing first — Mesopotamia Ch 3 — and it is a feature of the Bronze Age generally: when multiple civilizations develop in close contact, the question of who is "first" at any given innovation often turns out to be the wrong question.)

And then the argument gets moot, because the Nubian kings disappear.

---

Around 2800 BCE, give or take, the A-Group simply vanishes from the archaeological record of Lower Nubia.

This is not a slow decline with a few holdouts and a fade to black. It is abrupt. Cemeteries stop. Village sites are abandoned. The distinctive eggshell pottery vanishes. For the next four centuries or so, Lower Nubia is almost empty. Archaeologists have tried hard to find the "B-Group" that should logically have followed the A-Group, but the B-Group turned out to be a cataloging mistake — graves that were just A-Group graves in different clothing — and the whole letter has been officially retired. There is no B-Group. There is just a gap.

What happened? The least controversial answer is that the early kings of unified Egypt — the founders of what historians call the First and Second Dynasties, the first real pharaohs — invaded. Egyptian inscriptions from the early dynastic period brag about destroying Ta-Seti. One tablet from around 3000 BCE shows an early Egyptian king (possibly **Narmer**, the traditional founder of the First Dynasty, or one of his immediate successors) standing in triumph over a bound Nubian captive (Early Dynastic Egypt Ch 5–6). A later carving near Aswan, from the reign of a king named **Djer**, depicts a battle scene and a line of bound prisoners labeled as Ta-Seti enemies. Other early dynastic records count thousands of cattle and prisoners taken from Nubian raids. The details are propaganda, but the pattern is consistent across multiple reigns: from roughly 3100 through 2800 BCE, early Egyptian kings conducted repeated military campaigns into Lower Nubia.

Were those campaigns enough to depopulate the whole region? Possibly. Lower Nubia in 3000 BCE was not densely populated to begin with — a string of villages along a narrow river, with total population probably in the low tens of thousands. A sustained campaign of raids, enslavements, and cattle-seizures could plausibly have pushed the survivors south into Upper Nubia, east into the desert, or into the Egyptian population itself as captives. Some archaeologists think the depopulation was never as total as it looks and that we are missing whatever the survivors were doing because it left less archaeological signal. Either way, the visible A-Group civilization, with its eggshell pottery and its rich burials and its contested proto-kings, ends around 2800 BCE.

This is the point in the story where, if you only read Egyptian sources, you would conclude that Nubia had been extinguished. It had not. It had just moved upstream, out of Egyptian reach, and south of the Second Cataract it was about to build something bigger than anything the early dynastic pharaohs could have imagined.

---

Before we cross the Second Cataract, one more thing — a caveat that applies to this whole book, not just this chapter.

When a book about ancient history says "first" — "first city," "first kingdom," "first writing" — what it means is always "first surviving evidence of." Archaeology is a tiny sample of an enormous vanished world. A fire, a flood, a later city built on top of an earlier one, a shift in a river's course, a thousand years of farming plowing the remains into dust: any of these can erase an entire civilization's first drafts. The Nile itself is actively hostile to preservation — its annual floods scoured villages off the landscape for ten thousand years, and its modern dams have drowned more ancient sites than any earthquake in history. What survives for us to dig up is the most visible, the most durable, and the most geographically lucky tenth of a percent of what was there.

So when this book says Nubia had "Africa's first urban society outside Egypt" at Kerma, what it really means is: the first urban society outside Egypt *whose remains we've found, that were old enough to be in the running, and that we've been able to agree are genuinely urban*. That's still a striking claim, and we're going to get to the evidence in Chapter 3. But keep the caveat in mind every time a "first" turns up. Archaeological firsts are real, but they are always a little bit about luck.

Now, south to the Second Cataract, and past it, and into the long silence before Kerma.

# Chapter 2 — After the Silence

For most of the fourth millennium BCE, Lower Nubia had been one of the most densely inhabited and commercially active stretches of the Nile south of the delta. A-Group villages had dotted the banks, long-distance trade with Egypt had flowed back and forth at industrial scale, and the elites buried at Qustul had accumulated enough wealth and symbolic machinery to trigger the "were they kings?" argument we just watched unfold.

Then, from about 2800 BCE to 2400 BCE, Lower Nubia looks like nobody was home.

This is strange on its own terms, and it is stranger when you look at what the rest of the ancient world was doing at the same moment. In southern Iraq, the Sumerian city of Uruk (OO-rook) had been the biggest urban center on earth for centuries, and other Sumerian city-states were now fighting each other in the wars that produced the Royal Tombs of Ur (Mesopotamia Ch 4). East of the Indus River, walled towns like Kot Diji were being built — the first rumblings of the Harappan urban explosion that would produce Mohenjo-daro a few centuries later (Indus Valley Ch 3). In northern China, the first Longshan walled towns were going up along the Yellow River (Ancient China Ch 6). The early third millennium BCE was one of the most actively urbanizing centuries in human history so far — and Lower Nubia, which had been keeping up as recently as the Qustul generation, dropped out of the race.

The four-century gap is one of the stranger features of Nubian archaeology. It is called, in the scholarly literature, the **"dark age"** of Lower Nubia, and it is genuinely dark: few graves, no notable settlements, almost no pottery assemblage rich enough to give a culture a letter-name. Whatever was going on along the Nile between the First and Second Cataracts during the Old Kingdom of Egypt, it was not leaving the kind of archaeological signal that the preceding A-Group or the following C-Group left.

This is also the place to pin down a set of Egyptian period names we are going to use constantly from here on. Egyptologists divide pharaonic history into three great stable periods separated by two breakdowns, and the reader can keep the basic shape in mind with a four-sentence summary. The **Old Kingdom** (roughly 2686 to 2181 BCE) is the era of the great pyramids — the Fourth Dynasty of Sneferu, Khufu, and Khafre, plus the four dynasties before and after, when Egypt was first unified, centrally governed from the city of **Memphis** (in the north, near modern Cairo), and building the most famous monuments in its history. The **Middle Kingdom** (roughly 2055 to 1650 BCE) comes after a century-and-a-half collapse called the First Intermediate Period, and it is the era when Egypt was run from **Thebes** (THEEBZ — a city in the south near modern Luxor, not to be confused with the unrelated Greek city of the same name) and when the Nubian fortress program we will meet in Chapter 4 was built. The **New Kingdom** (roughly 1550 to 1070 BCE) comes after another collapse called the Second Intermediate Period, and it is Egypt's imperial golden age — Hatshepsut, Thutmose III, Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, Ramesses II, the conquest of Nubia, the wars in Syria, Abu Simbel. After the New Kingdom comes the Third Intermediate Period and then the long decline of pharaonic independence under foreign rule. Almost everything in this book happens somewhere in that five-phase sequence, and any time you see an Egyptian dynasty number (Fourth, Twelfth, Eighteenth, Twenty-fifth, etc.) you can place it by remembering which kingdom it belongs to: the Fourth Dynasty is Old Kingdom (pyramids); the Twelfth is Middle Kingdom (forts); the Eighteenth and Nineteenth are New Kingdom (empire).

Back to the question of who was missing from Lower Nubia.

This does not mean nobody was alive there. It probably means the population was smaller, scattered, and mobile — herders moving their cattle between the river and the desert, living in temporary camps, burying their dead without the rich grave goods that preserve well, and making pottery in styles that archaeologists have a hard time telling apart from earlier or later traditions. People who leave few traces are easy to miss. What we can be confident of is that Lower Nubia stopped being an organized, urbanized landscape for four centuries.

What happened to the missing people? The answer is almost certainly that they got pushed off the stretch of river they used to live on, in two directions. The Egyptians pushed them off from the north. The remaining Nubian population drifted south, out of Egyptian reach, into the broader floodplains where the Kerma kingdom was about to incubate. Both movements were going on simultaneously, and together they produce the archaeological signature of a region that looks empty because both forces acting on it were pulling the people elsewhere.

Start with the push.

---

Egyptian military raids into Lower Nubia did not stop with the early dynastic period. They continued, more or less as official policy, throughout the Old Kingdom. We know this not from narrative history (because Egyptian sources don't do narrative history the way we think of it) but from the royal boasts carved into temples, standing inscription slabs called stelae (STEE-lee — we'll meet them properly in Chapter 4), and tomb walls: short, dry entries that record how many prisoners, how many cattle, how many gold rings, how many villages burned.

The most spectacular of these entries comes from the reign of **Sneferu** (SNEH-froo), the first pharaoh of the **Fourth Dynasty** (an Old Kingdom dynasty — the pyramid-builders). Sneferu ruled around 2600 BCE and is best known as the father of Khufu (builder of the Great Pyramid at Giza) and as the experimental pyramid-builder in his own right. His first pyramid at Meidum partially collapsed; his second at Dahshur had to change its angle halfway up to stop the walls from crushing inward, producing the shape now called the **"Bent Pyramid"** because the lower half rises at a steep angle and the upper half at a shallower one. Less famously, Sneferu was also an enthusiastic raider of Nubia.

A fragmentary document called the **Palermo Stone** records his Nubian expeditions. (The Palermo Stone is a surviving chunk of an Egyptian king list — a kind of royal annal, carved in stone, that recorded year-by-year events under each pharaoh. It takes its name from the museum in Palermo, Sicily, where the largest surviving fragment lives.) According to the Palermo Stone's entry for Sneferu, one of his campaigns into Nubia brought back "7,000 captives and 200,000 cattle." The numbers are almost certainly propaganda — suspiciously round, and inflated by the boasting conventions of royal inscriptions. But even a tenth of the headline figure is a catastrophic shock to the small population living in narrow strips of farmland along a river. A raid of that scale doesn't just reduce the number of people. It reduces the *capacity* of the region to rebuild. Prisoners become Egyptian labor. Cattle become Egyptian wealth. The social structures that allowed a village to hold onto its land, repair its houses, and marry off its daughters get shredded. A few rounds of this, spaced out over a generation, can empty a stretch of riverbank.

This was Egypt's Nubian policy for most of the Old Kingdom: raid, extract, repeat. The gold mines of the eastern desert, the quarries of the granite ridges, and the trading routes that ran up the river were all accessible to Egypt without bothering to govern the land in between. What Egypt wanted from Lower Nubia, it took directly, and what it took most often was people and cattle. The Old Kingdom was not, incidentally, doing this to anyone else on the same scale. Egyptian campaigns in Libya or the Sinai during this era were smaller, less frequent, and less obviously aimed at stripping a population. Mass forced-population transfer as a systematic policy would not become routine in the ancient world until the Neo-Assyrian empire industrialized it more than 1,800 years later (Mesopotamia Ch 11). Sneferu was, in some rough proto-form, doing it first. Nubia bore the brunt.

So that is half of the explanation for the silence of Lower Nubia: it was being systematically stripped by its northern neighbor.

The other half is more interesting. The people who used to live in Lower Nubia had somewhere to go, and the place they went to is where this story starts getting good.

---

The place is Upper Nubia, past the Second Cataract, in the broad floodplain around the **Third Cataract**. The river widens here. The desert pulls back a little. There is enough farmland to support serious populations and enough water to sustain real cities. If you were a Nubian family with cattle and a sense of which way the political winds were blowing in the twenty-sixth century BCE, you went south, past the cataracts, past the reach of Egyptian raiding parties, and into the long stretch of river where Egypt's expeditions had not yet bothered to go.

Egyptian writers were aware that something was down there. They called it **Yam** — the more distant of their two Nubian geographic terms, the one beyond Wawat, the one that was the source of the really exotic trade goods. Exactly where Yam was on a modern map has been argued about for a century, but the best current guess is that it corresponds to the region around the Third Cataract, in what is now northern Sudan, near the modern town of Kerma. If that's right, then when Old Kingdom Egyptians wrote about "the ruler of Yam" or "the people of Yam," they were writing about the early ancestors of the kingdom that was about to turn the southern Nile into a rival Nile, with its own cities, its own temples, and its own kings.

We will spend most of the rest of this book with those ancestors. But at the beginning of the twenty-fifth century BCE, Yam was still more rumor than destination from the Egyptian point of view — a place the Egyptians knew existed because rare and valuable goods came from there, but not a place they governed, controlled, or even visited often. An expedition into Yam was an adventure, not an administrative errand. Egypt was not yet capable of the sustained river logistics needed to push a serious force past the Second Cataract, and the Second Cataract itself was dangerous enough to bottle up any army that tried.

So Upper Nubia incubated. Cattle herding, pottery, trade, and whatever political consolidation was happening among the villages along the river were proceeding without Egyptian interference for roughly four centuries. When the archaeology finally catches up with them, in the next chapter, they are going to turn out to have built a city.

---

Meanwhile, in Lower Nubia, the gap was finally being filled by a new group. They turn up in the archaeological record around 2400 BCE, and the bland alphabetical naming convention we met last chapter gives them a predictable label: the **C-Group**.

The C-Group was not the A-Group coming back. It was something visibly new, even though the new thing shared a lot of habits with the old thing — enough that some scholars still argue about how much continuity and how much discontinuity is really there. What makes the C-Group distinctive in the dirt is a set of traits that turn up together at site after site, which is how any archaeological culture gets identified:

Round stone-lined graves, instead of the simple pits the A-Group had dug. Low stone rings built on top of the graves. Incised pottery bowls with geometric patterns — triangles, zigzags, hatching — that the potters filled in with white pigment so the patterns jumped out. Evidence of a cattle-centered lifestyle, with cattle skulls sometimes buried alongside human owners. Small settlements along the river, often fortified with stone walls or palisades, that look more defensive than their A-Group predecessors.

The white-filled incised pottery is the signature. C-Group pots are immediately recognizable; you can hand an undecorated sherd to an Africanist archaeologist and they can tell you "C-Group" the way a wine taster identifies a vintage. The style is completely different from Egyptian pottery of the same era, which is wheel-thrown and plain, and it is completely different from the A-Group eggshell tradition, which was thinner and shinier but less decorated. The C-Group was doing its own thing.

The cattle orientation is the other giveaway. C-Group graves routinely contain cattle skulls or horn cores, often arranged in elaborate patterns, sometimes with more skulls than any individual household could have owned. Cattle were not just food and wealth for the C-Group; they were status objects, religious symbols, and grave goods in their own right. The pattern fits a broader African cattle-keeping tradition that you can trace from the ancient Sahara through the modern Maasai, in which an individual's identity and spiritual life are tangled up with the individual's herd. The C-Group are, in a real sense, the earliest archaeologically clear example of a cattle-centered African pastoral tradition that would persist and spread across the continent for thousands of years after them.

What matters most about the C-Group, for the long arc of this story, is that they stayed. They dug in. They maintained their distinctive pottery tradition, their distinctive burial tradition, and their distinctive cattle-centered identity for almost a thousand years, from 2400 BCE to 1550 BCE, through multiple waves of Egyptian contact, pressure, and outright occupation. The C-Group is the reason we can say with confidence that Nubian cultural identity under Egyptian domination was not erased. It was stubborn. It survived, and it was visibly and deliberately different from the Egyptian alternative even when Egypt was literally in charge.

Whether the C-Group were the descendants of the A-Group, returning after four centuries in the desert or the south, is genuinely uncertain. Some of the continuities — the fondness for the same stretch of river, some pottery decoration elements, the broad economic pattern — suggest they could have been. Other differences — the new grave form, the new pottery tradition, the heavier cattle emphasis — could also be the result of fresh migration from the desert west of the Nile, from the depopulated Sahara as it was drying out during the third millennium BCE. The current best-attested explanation is probably a mix: survivors of the old A-Group tradition merging with incoming pastoralist groups from the western desert, absorbed into a new synthesis along the river.

And while the C-Group was re-settling the north, a city was rising in the south.

---

# Chapter 3 — Harkhuf's Donkeys

Around 2500 BCE, in the broad floodplain south of the Third Cataract, somebody started building a wall around a cluster of houses. This was not the first wall any Nubian community had ever built, and we will never know the name of whoever organized the labor that built it. But the wall was big, and it enclosed something that was starting to look much more like a town than a village, and the town was starting to look as if the people running it had ideas. The town's modern name, taken from the small Sudanese village that sits next to the site today, is **Kerma** (KAIR-ma).

Kerma is one of the most important sites in African archaeology, and its story is going to take up the next three chapters of this book, because Kerma is not just a place. It is a kingdom, a culture, and a civilizational experiment that ran parallel to pharaonic Egypt for a thousand years. At its peak, the city of Kerma would hold more than ten thousand residents — small by the standards of Uruk or Memphis but by a long way the largest settlement in Africa outside the Egyptian delta. It would build mud-brick temples bigger than anything outside Egypt. It would bury its kings with corridors of sacrificed retainers numbering into the hundreds. It would trade with half the ancient world. And it would, for a long and anxious century, make Egyptian pharaohs seriously worried about what was upstream.

But at 2500 BCE, all of that is still ahead. At 2500 BCE, Kerma is just a walled town with ambitions.

What exactly it was at the very beginning is hard to reconstruct, because the earliest layers at Kerma sit beneath three thousand years of later construction. It is not one of those easy sites where the ancient layers have nothing on top of them except more ancient layers (what archaeologists call a **tell** — a mound-like site, common in the Middle East, where a town was abandoned and rebuilt on the same spot over and over until the whole location became a small hill of accumulated rubble). Kerma was lived on continuously, so the oldest parts are buried under the youngest. The best picture we have is that early Kerma was a regional center for a cluster of surrounding villages, growing grain and herding cattle, trading upriver and downriver, and gradually assembling the kind of surplus wealth that allows a town to build something that isn't a house.

The something-that-isn't-a-house is what makes Kerma famous. It is a building the Nubians called a **deffufa** (dih-FOO-fah), a word we've borrowed directly from the local Sudanese Arabic term. A deffufa is a massive free-standing mud-brick structure, solid or nearly solid at the base, with chambers and passages carved into the upper levels. The best-known example is the **Western Deffufa** at Kerma, which sits at the heart of the ancient city and still rises more than 50 feet (about 18 meters) above the plain — a rectangular mud-brick mountain more than 170 feet (50 meters) long. It is one of the oldest and largest mud-brick structures standing anywhere in Africa, and it is still there today, baking in the Sudanese sun, as it has been for more than four thousand years.

What was the deffufa for? Almost certainly a temple of some kind, though the details are not fully recoverable. There are staircases that lead up into the structure, chambers at the top that may have held ritual equipment, and offering spaces around the base. The overall shape is unlike anything in contemporary Egyptian architecture — it is not a pyramid, not a mastaba (an Egyptian **mastaba** is the rectangular flat-roofed tomb of Old Kingdom nobles, shaped roughly like a long low brick oven, built over a burial shaft), and not a processional temple in the later Egyptian sense. It is its own form. The scale is what stops you: to build a solid mud-brick structure fifty feet high and fifty meters long, on the Nile in the twenty-fourth century BCE, you need a sustained labor force over years, you need somebody telling them what to build, and you need enough political stability that the builders don't abandon the project halfway through. The Western Deffufa is, in other words, a monument that proves the existence of a state.

And if you are trying to build a theory of where African states come from, the Western Deffufa becomes important evidence. At the moment it rose, around 2400 BCE, Kerma was doing something that almost nobody else on the continent was doing outside the Nile delta of Egypt. To place that moment in the wider Bronze Age world: Mesopotamia was already deeply urban. The Sumerian city of **Uruk** (in what is now southern Iraq, the first large-scale city in human history) had peaked centuries earlier, and Hierakonpolis in Upper Egypt had already grown to 5,000–10,000 people by ~3600 BCE (Early Dynastic Egypt Ch 3), and the Sumerian city-states of the Early Dynastic period were fighting each other over water rights in the wars whose royal cemeteries at **Ur** we will meet in the next chapter — by 2400 BCE Mesopotamia had two thousand years of urban history behind it (Mesopotamia Chs 3–4), and a king named Sargon of Akkad was only a couple of generations away from stitching it all into the world's first empire (Mesopotamia Ch 5). Over in the Indus River valley, in what is now Pakistan, the planned city of **Mohenjo-daro** and its gridded contemporaries had already crossed the urban threshold around 2600 BCE and were in full flower when Kerma's first walls went up (Indus Valley Ch 4). In northern China, a cluster of Longshan walled towns — including the stone-walled hill complex called **Shimao** — were coming into their own as fortified proto-state centers (Ancient China Ch 6). Egypt was in the middle of the Old Kingdom, finishing the great pyramids at Giza. The Americas were centuries away from anything you could call urban.

But sub-Saharan Africa? Nothing. The whole rest of the continent, as far as the current archaeology shows, was still a world of villages and pastoralist camps and seasonal settlements. Kerma is genuinely the oldest big thing in the archaeological record of Africa south of Egypt — and it is also the *last* of the great Bronze Age cradles to cross the urban threshold, arriving about 1,400 years after Uruk, 700 years after the first predynastic Egyptian towns, and roughly 200 years after Mohenjo-daro. That puts Kerma in the same rough generation as Longshan China — and, remarkably, as Caral on the coast of Peru (~2900 BCE), the first urban center in the Americas (Early Andean Ch 3). Two first-on-their-continent urban civilizations, developing at roughly the same moment, both systematically under-covered in world history. Archaeology south of the Sahara is genuinely thin, and future discoveries could shift the picture, but at the current state of evidence Kerma is the fifth cradle and the African one.

First in Africa, and early. A thousand years before Great Zimbabwe. Two and a half thousand years before the kingdoms of Mali and Ghana. Three thousand years before the African urban traditions that modern Western readers are more likely to have heard of. The Kerma kingdom is so early that most general-audience books about African history skip it entirely, because they start around 500 BCE and Kerma ended a thousand years before that. We are going to give it the attention it deserves.

---

The Egyptians noticed.

Old Kingdom Egypt had been trading with Nubia for a long time already, but the Nubia it traded with in the Early Dynastic and early Old Kingdom had been a Nubia of villages and herders. What started showing up in Egyptian records in the middle Old Kingdom was different. There are increasingly frequent Egyptian references to the "ruler of Yam," which is new — a *ruler*, singular, suggesting some kind of centralized political authority south of the cataracts that the Egyptian scribes recognized as rulership in their own sense of the word. There are larger and more elaborate Egyptian expeditions heading south to trade with this ruler, bringing back not just bulk goods like gold and ebony but also exotic specialty items: live leopards, hunting dogs, incense in sealed jars, and, in at least one famous case, a pygmy dancer.

The Egyptian texts that describe these expeditions are weirdly specific about logistics. They don't read like triumph inscriptions; they read like business trip reports. And one of them, carved onto the front of a tomb at Aswan, has survived in almost complete form, which means we actually have a first-person account of an Egyptian expedition into Upper Nubia during the Old Kingdom. The account is one of the most important single documents in early Nubian history, and the expedition leader's name was **Harkhuf**.

---

Harkhuf (HAR-koof) was the governor of **Elephantine** (ell-ef-an-TEE-nay). Elephantine is a small island in the Nile at Aswan, sitting right at the First Cataract. It served throughout the Old Kingdom as Egypt's southern border post and its main jumping-off point for anything heading further upstream. The island was the provincial capital of the first nome of Upper Egypt — Ta-Seti, the Land of the Bow, Nubia's Egyptian-side name (Chapter 1). Governing Elephantine meant governing Egypt's Nubia desk: managing the trade, taxing the river traffic, organizing the expeditions, and leading them yourself when the stakes were high enough.

Harkhuf served two pharaohs, **Merenre** and then **Pepi II**, across the second half of the twenty-fourth century BCE, right at the tail end of the Old Kingdom. During his career he led four separate trading expeditions from Elephantine south into Yam. Each one was a donkey caravan — a long line of pack animals, with human drivers and armed escort, heading overland along the desert routes that bypassed the worst of the cataracts. Boats were useless past the Second Cataract in most seasons. Donkeys, not boats, were the real infrastructure of Old Kingdom trade with Upper Nubia. Harkhuf's fourth and largest expedition reportedly brought back 300 donkey-loads of trade goods. Imagine a single line of three hundred pack animals, each one carrying incense, ebony, ivory, leopard skins, throw-sticks, and assorted luxuries, stretching for a mile or more along a desert road north of Kerma, and you have an accurate picture of what Harkhuf's trade relationship with Yam actually looked like on the ground.

We know all this because Harkhuf commissioned a long autobiographical inscription on the front of his tomb at Qubbet el-Hawa, a hillside cemetery overlooking the Nile at Aswan. The inscription tells, in his own voice, the story of his four expeditions, what he brought back, and how the trip went. This was not standard practice — most Old Kingdom officials did not carve detailed business travelogues on their tomb walls. Harkhuf's inscription is a first-person narrative, and because it is a first-person narrative about Nubian trade from the Nubian side of the cataracts, it is one of the richest sources in all of Egyptian literature for what the south was actually like.

Two things about the inscription are worth sitting with. The first is that Harkhuf does not talk about Yam the way he would talk about a conquered province. He talks about it the way a merchant talks about a business partner — describing the ruler, describing the quality of the goods, describing the precarious diplomacy required to get in and out safely. At one point, Harkhuf's caravan encountered a military situation where two Nubian groups were at war with each other, and the "ruler of Yam" provided Harkhuf with an armed escort through the trouble zone. This is not a pharaoh's narrative of domination. This is a Nubian king sending soldiers to protect an Egyptian trade caravan as a favor, and getting paid for it. The Egyptian trade relationship with Upper Nubia in the late Old Kingdom was not an occupation. It was a negotiation, between parties who could stop each other from doing what they wanted to do.

The second thing worth sitting with is the pygmy.

---

Toward the end of Harkhuf's inscription, he describes how he had acquired, on his fourth expedition, a small dancing man whom he intended to bring back to Egypt. The Egyptian word used in the text is **deneg** (DEN-eg), usually translated as "pygmy" or "dwarf." The person in question was almost certainly one of the Pygmy peoples of central Africa (the short-statured forest-dwelling groups, today called Mbuti, Twa, or Baka, living in the Congo basin rainforests far to the south of Nubia), not a dwarf in the modern medical sense. Harkhuf had acquired this dancer from a more distant southern land, through whatever trade chain reached from Kerma to the equatorial forests, and the idea was that the dancer would perform the "dance of the god" at the royal court. The dance of the god was a specific Egyptian religious performance involving a small dancer and a ritual impersonation of the sun god — one of the liturgies performed around the pharaoh himself on high festival days — and it worked best when the dancer was physically small enough to fit the ritual role. Live human rarities like this were part of the broader Bronze Age gift-diplomacy economy, the same economy that shipped carnelian beads from Harappa to Sumerian Ur and lapis lazuli from Afghanistan to the pharaoh's jewelers.

The court Harkhuf was sending the dancer to was at **Memphis**, the Old Kingdom capital. (Memphis sat at the point where the Nile delta opens up, just south of modern Cairo — the natural choke point controlling traffic between the delta and Upper Egypt. It was Egypt's capital from the First Dynasty almost continuously through the Old Kingdom. Throughout this book, when you see "Memphis" with no other qualifier, think "Old Kingdom capital, northern Egypt, near modern Cairo.")

When the caravan was still traveling back through Nubia, Harkhuf sent a courier ahead to inform the pharaoh of what was coming.

Pepi II, the pharaoh in question, was eight years old.

A note about Pepi II's age is worth pausing on, because the man is an oddity of Egyptian history. The traditional Egyptian king lists give Pepi II something close to a ninety-year reign, which would make him the longest-reigning monarch in recorded human history if taken literally. Most modern Egyptologists think the ninety-year figure is an exaggeration in the surviving sources but that Pepi II did genuinely rule for many decades — a long reign that saw the slow unraveling of the Old Kingdom's central authority under a pharaoh who was steadily aging out of active governance. When Harkhuf's courier reached him with news of the dancer, though, all of that was decades in the future. He was a little boy on a big throne.

The child pharaoh wrote back. The letter survives, copied in full onto Harkhuf's tomb wall, because Harkhuf thought it was the greatest thing that had ever happened to him. Pepi II's reply ordered Harkhuf to guard the dwarf night and day, to ensure he did not fall off the boat on the way down the river, to protect him against all possible accidents, and to deliver him alive and well to the royal court, at which point Harkhuf would be rewarded more richly than any previous expedition leader. The tone is unmistakable. It is the tone of an eight-year-old who has just found out he is about to receive the coolest birthday present of his life, writing in the diplomatic register because the eight-year-old happens to be pharaoh.

Harkhuf was so proud of the letter that he copied its entire text onto his tomb — not summarized, not paraphrased, but word for word, including the royal name and titles and the breathless instructions about the dwarf. Four and a half thousand years later, the letter is still there, carved in hieroglyphs on the Qubbet el-Hawa cliffside, and it is one of the oldest surviving pieces of personal correspondence from any ancient civilization.

For the purposes of this book, Harkhuf's inscription is valuable for three reasons. It confirms that Upper Nubia in the twenty-fourth century BCE had a centralized political authority that the Egyptians recognized as a "ruler." It confirms that the relationship across the cataracts was one of trade and negotiation, not domination. And it confirms, through the specific goods on the donkey caravan — the incense, the ivory, the leopard skins, the dancer — that Kerma and its neighbors were linked into a trade network that reached south and west into parts of Africa the Egyptians themselves had never seen. Kerma was already, in the late Old Kingdom, functioning as a middleman between sub-Saharan Africa and the Mediterranean world. The deneg dancer had not been born in Kerma. He had traveled to Kerma from somewhere further south, where somebody else had been trading with somebody else, down chains of connection we cannot fully reconstruct.

---

And then, in the middle of the twenty-second century BCE, the Old Kingdom collapsed.

The collapse is a long, messy historiographical problem that belongs properly to an Egyptian history, not a Nubian one, so the short version will do. Pepi II's enormous reign (whatever its real length) ended with a weakened central government, a string of inadequate successors, breakdowns in provincial authority, a series of low Nile floods that produced bad harvests, and eventually a full fragmentation of Egypt into competing regional powers. Historians call the period that followed the **First Intermediate Period** — a polite way of saying "Egypt fell apart for about 150 years." The end of the Old Kingdom came somewhere around 2180 BCE. Egypt would not be reunified until 2055 BCE, when a dynasty based at **Thebes** in southern Egypt (the city near modern Luxor we met in the kingdom-framework note in Chapter 2) succeeded in pulling the country back together and starting the Middle Kingdom.

During that century and a half, Egypt's grip on Nubia loosened. The raiding stopped, because Egypt was busy raiding itself. Egyptian expeditions south became infrequent or ceased. The "ruler of Yam" probably experienced this as a gift: a prolonged vacation from the most demanding trading partner in the ancient world, and an opportunity to consolidate power along the Nile without interference.

Archaeologically, Kerma during the First Intermediate Period shows continuing growth. The city expanded. More houses were built. The Western Deffufa continued in use and was gradually enlarged. New structures appeared in the surrounding cemetery area. Whatever Kerma's political development looked like, it did not slow down when Egypt did. If anything, Egypt's absence gave Kerma room to grow into the kind of kingdom that would, a few centuries later, start making Egyptian pharaohs genuinely anxious.

When Middle Kingdom Egypt finally pulled itself back together and looked south again, the thing it saw upstream was not the loose network of villages and traders that the Old Kingdom had known. It was a real kingdom, with a real city, real kings, and real armies. And the Middle Kingdom's response to that kingdom, starting in the twentieth century BCE, was going to be one of the most thorough fortification programs in the history of the ancient world.

---

# Chapter 4 — Forts Along the Cataracts

The Middle Kingdom pharaohs did not like what they saw when they looked south.

The city of Kerma had grown during Egypt's long interregnum — more houses, more walls, more deffufas rising above the skyline, and a visibly consolidated royal authority running the place from the middle of it all. The C-Group villagers of Lower Nubia had continued their stubborn cattle-and-pottery lives under only minimal Egyptian supervision. The gold mines of the eastern desert had been worked only sporadically in the century and a half of Egyptian disorder, which meant gold production was low and the routes to the mines were no longer secure. From the perspective of a newly reunified Egyptian state starting over in the twenty-first century BCE, the Nile south of Aswan was a problem that had gotten worse while Egypt wasn't looking.

The Middle Kingdom's answer was to fortify the border.

Not fortify in the loose, symbolic sense that most ancient frontiers got. Fortify in the sense of building, over several generations, a chain of roughly a dozen genuine military fortresses along a 250-mile (400 km) stretch of the Nile between the First Cataract and the Second, each one designed to house a garrison, project force up and down the river, and lock down traffic past the cataracts. (Egyptologists count the forts differently depending on whether subsidiary installations and forward watchposts are lumped in with the main strongholds — the main chain is usually given as eleven to thirteen forts, with more small outworks beyond that.) This was one of the most intensive frontier fortification programs anywhere in the ancient Bronze Age world. The Middle Kingdom pharaohs were really, genuinely, deeply worried about Kerma.

The closest contemporary parallel anywhere on earth is in fact Chinese. At almost exactly the same moment, in northern China along the Yellow River, the Longshan-era walled towns were being built using a rammed-earth technique called **hangtu** (HONG-too — a construction method where workers pounded layer after layer of damp earth between wooden forms until the wall became hard enough to resist battering, the same basic technology that would eventually build the earliest Great Wall), and the walled site of Shimao was throwing up stone-walled terraces in its own defensive posture against whatever was threatening it (Ancient China Ch 6). The early second millennium BCE was a period in which multiple Bronze Age states, independently, were discovering how much masonry a serious frontier cost. Egypt's twelve-fort chain and China's hangtu walls were two expressions of the same problem.

The reader should take a moment to appreciate how unusual this is. Ancient superpowers did not usually build forts against neighbors they considered unimportant. Egypt did not build forts against the Libyans in the western desert on anything close to this scale, and the Libyans were a recurring military nuisance. The Middle Kingdom forts on the Nubian border are, by themselves, sufficient evidence that Middle Kingdom Egypt considered Kerma a peer competitor — not a nuisance to be raided, not a source of gold to be extracted, but a rival state powerful enough to require permanent defensive architecture. No other ancient Egyptian frontier ever received this kind of investment.

The prime mover of the program was **Senusret III** (SEN-oos-ret), a pharaoh of the Twelfth Dynasty who reigned around 1860 BCE and who is remembered in Egyptian sources as one of the most aggressive, effective, and personally terrifying kings the Middle Kingdom ever produced. Senusret III is the king who finally finished what his father and grandfather had started, extending the Nubian fortress chain all the way to the Second Cataract and making Egypt's grip on Lower Nubia something Kerma had to take seriously. He is also the king whose statues, preserved in museums from Cairo to New York, show him with a deeply lined, grim, almost exhausted face — one of the earliest ancient portraits that looks like a specific individual human being rather than an idealized royal mask. Whether the lines on his face were an accurate likeness or a propaganda statement ("your king bears burdens so that you don't have to"), the overall effect is of a man with work to do, and the work involved Nubia.

---

The forts were serious pieces of engineering.

A typical Middle Kingdom Nubian fort was rectangular, built on a high defensible site along the river, surrounded by a dry moat (sometimes two concentric moats), with walls of mud brick laid on stone foundations, punctuated by regularly spaced towers projecting outward. The walls were thick enough to withstand battering rams and tall enough that an assault with siege ladders would be dangerous for the attackers. The gates were protected by angled entryways that forced attackers to turn a corner with their shield side exposed to defenders. There were internal barracks, granaries, bakeries, wells, administrative buildings, a small temple, and housing for the garrison's officers. Some forts included dedicated areas for the garrison's families, meaning the soldiers were not just rotating through but actually living there with wives and children.

The two largest forts in the chain were **Buhen** (BOO-hen) and **Mirgissa** (mir-GIS-sah). Both sat at the Second Cataract, the chokepoint where the river is most dangerous for boats and the land route is most predictable for caravans. Buhen is the more famous, partly because it was more thoroughly excavated before it was drowned under Lake Nasser in the 1960s and partly because its reconstructed plans are some of the most impressive ancient military architecture drawings in any textbook. Buhen had two concentric defensive walls, a ditch, a bastioned gatehouse, loopholes for archers, and an estimated capacity for a garrison of several hundred soldiers with supporting civilian staff. It was a castle by any reasonable definition of the word — a thousand years before the concept of a castle is supposed to exist anywhere else.

Mirgissa was arguably even more ambitious. In addition to its main fortress, its subsidiary outworks, and its garrison facilities, Mirgissa had something that no other Middle Kingdom fort had: a **slipway**. A slipway is a long, prepared track of wood and mud plaster, lubricated with river mud, along which boats could be physically dragged overland. The Mirgissa slipway bypassed the worst of the Second Cataract rapids by hauling river boats out of the water, sliding them along a lubricated track for more than a quarter-mile (roughly 400 meters) of parallel ground, and refloating them upstream of the whitewater.

This was engineering in service of a specific strategic goal. The cataracts, as we have been saying since Chapter 1, are the reason Egypt and Nubia ended up as two separate countries — because boats could not cross them and a river civilization without river logistics couldn't project sustained force. The Mirgissa slipway was Egypt's answer to that problem. With a slipway, Egyptian river forces could get past the Second Cataract and operate upstream. Without one, they couldn't. The fact that the Middle Kingdom invested in building and maintaining a multi-hundred-meter wooden track for hauling boats overland tells you, more clearly than almost any other single piece of evidence, how badly they wanted to be able to reach Kerma.

---

And then there is the Semna stele.

**Semna** (SEM-nah) was one of the southernmost fortresses in the chain, sitting right at the Second Cataract itself, facing south into the territory that had historically belonged to Kerma and its neighbors. At Semna, Senusret III erected a stone **stele** — a carved standing stone, the standard Egyptian medium for public inscriptions — that contained what is essentially the text of a border-closure decree. The standard English translation of the Semna boundary stele (Miriam Lichtheim's reading in *Ancient Egyptian Literature* Vol. I) runs, in rough paraphrase: the king has made this southern boundary in his year 8, and no Nubian is allowed to pass it, either by land or by river, with a ship or with herds of cattle, *except* for a Nubian who has come to do authorized trade at a named border post, or on a diplomatic errand, or for some other approved matter. Any Nubian ship attempting to head north past this point is forbidden forever.

In plain terms: no Nubian crosses the line without Egyptian permission, and the river traffic south of it belongs to Egypt.

A second Semna stele, erected a few years later by the same king, adds a personal flourish that has haunted Egyptological writing ever since. The stele addresses Senusret III's future successors and warns them: any son of his who maintains this boundary is his true son, and any son who abandons it and fails to fight for it is not his son and was not born to him. The pharaoh, apparently anxious about what future kings might do under pressure, tried to bind his successors with a curse — using the royal filiation itself as the test of legitimacy.

This is one of the oldest surviving explicit border-closure decrees from any ancient civilization. It is not just a military order — it is a political doctrine, written in stone, that says "Egypt ends here, Nubia begins here, nobody crosses without our permission, and we care enough about this line to threaten any future king who gives it up with posthumous disinheritance." That kind of formal border thinking is extraordinarily early. It predates the Roman frontier concept by nearly two thousand years. The Middle Kingdom pharaohs, confronting Kerma, had arrived at something that looks a lot like a modern nation-state's border — and then spent two centuries defending it.

---

What were they defending against?

Not a raiding party. Not a population of nomads. Raiding parties would not have justified thirteen permanent masonry forts and a slipway for getting boats past the cataract. What the Middle Kingdom was defending against was a rival state — an organized, centralized, capable, and growing kingdom based upstream at Kerma, which had spent the First Intermediate Period and the early Middle Kingdom quietly getting more dangerous. The Semna stele is not the grandiose propaganda of a secure empire. It is the deliberately stern posture of a kingdom that has decided the status quo is barely tolerable and needs to be locked down hard before it deteriorates further.

The forts worked, for a while. From around 1900 BCE to about 1700 BCE, the Second Cataract held. Egyptian administration extended into Lower Nubia as far south as the Semna line. Gold mining resumed at industrial scale. The C-Group people of Lower Nubia lived under an Egyptian administrative overlay that extracted taxes, labor, and soldiers, while leaving the basics of their village life largely intact. Kerma was contained, and the Middle Kingdom's southern frontier was as stable as any ancient frontier ever got.

And Nubians worked for Egypt in large numbers throughout this period. Not as reluctant subjects but, often, as paid professionals. This is one of the features of ancient Nubia that surprises modern readers most, because it runs so strongly against the monolithic "Egypt conquered Nubia" story. Nubian mercenaries show up throughout Middle Kingdom Egyptian military records — especially archers, but also scouts, skirmishers, and desert-knowledgeable guides. Some of them were drawn from a group the Egyptians called the **Medjay** (MED-jai), a desert-dwelling Nubian people whose name the Egyptians eventually turned into a common noun meaning "police" or "desert patrol." By the New Kingdom, a Medjay was not an ethnic term at all — it was a job description. A Medjay was a scout, a lawman, a desert guard, and often a member of an internal security force within Egypt itself, regardless of where he had actually been born. When you read about "Medjay" in later Egyptian sources, what you are reading is the linguistic fossil of a specific Nubian people so reliably good at their job that their name became the Egyptian word for the job.

Another group, the **Pan-Grave Culture** (so named by archaeologists for their shallow circular grave pits, which look like cooking pans when excavated), were Nubian pastoralists from the Eastern Desert who also served in the Egyptian military during the late Middle Kingdom and Second Intermediate Period. The Pan-Grave burials contain distinctive pottery and ornaments, and enough of them have been found inside Egyptian territory to show that Pan-Grave warriors and their families were settling in Egypt itself, forming communities of Nubian-origin soldiers in the Egyptian countryside. This was not a persecuted minority hiding from the authorities. This was a paid warrior class that the Egyptian state was actively recruiting from, integrating into Egyptian life, and relying on for security.

Egypt's relationship with Nubia during the Middle Kingdom was, in other words, more complicated than any single word captures. Egypt fortified the border. Egypt issued a decree forbidding Nubian river traffic. Egypt also hired Nubian soldiers, welcomed Nubian mercenary communities, and built its internal security around Nubian-named forces. The border was not a wall against an enemy so much as a managed interface between two peer populations, with Egypt trying to control which Nubians got in and on what terms.

---

Through all of this, Kerma itself kept growing.

The Middle Kingdom's forts could contain Kerma at the Second Cataract, but they could not reach it. An Egyptian army trying to cross the Second Cataract with intent to march on Kerma faced several hundred miles of unfriendly country, a difficult river, and an enemy whose capital was defended by Kerma's own population of several thousand adult men, most of them archers. A quick strike was impossible. A sustained campaign required logistical support that the Middle Kingdom could only generate at enormous cost. So Kerma was held in a kind of stalemate during the twentieth, nineteenth, and eighteenth centuries BCE — contained at the Second Cataract but unreachable beyond it, trading with Egypt through authorized channels but running its own affairs in its own territory without interference.

This was Kerma's golden age. Free from the threat of direct Egyptian intervention, and protected by its distance from the Egyptian supply base, the kingdom used the two centuries of containment to build out the most impressive urban and political structure in Africa outside Egypt. By around 1750 BCE, Kerma had a population of more than 10,000, a complex of temples and royal buildings on a scale unmatched anywhere else on the continent outside the Nile Delta, a distinctive high-fired pottery tradition that was being exported as far north as Thebes, and a royal cemetery whose largest tombs contained the retinues of dead kings buried alongside them. This is the period archaeologists call **Classic Kerma**, and it is the subject of our next chapter.

But before we leave the forts, one more detail. The Middle Kingdom border held until about 1700 BCE, when the whole political situation in Egypt started coming unglued for reasons that had nothing to do with Nubia. A group of foreign rulers known as the **Hyksos** (HICK-sos) were taking over northern Egypt from a power base in the Nile Delta. The Hyksos were migrant communities from the Levant (the coastal strip east of the Mediterranean, roughly modern Lebanon, Israel, and western Syria), speakers of a Semitic language related to later Hebrew and Aramaic rather than to Egyptian, who had been drifting into the delta for generations before they took it over. We will meet them properly in Chapter 6. For now the only thing the reader needs to know is that they existed, they were in the north, and their presence meant the Theban pharaohs of southern Egypt were about to be squeezed between two enemies: Hyksos to the north, Kerma to the south.

At that point, Kerma stopped being a contained rival and became, briefly and dangerously, an active military threat. The forts were still standing, but the state behind them was distracted, and Kerma was about to take advantage.

# Chapter 5 — Classic Kerma

To understand what Kerma was by 1700 BCE, you have to walk its cemetery.

The city itself, with its towering Western Deffufa and its crowded residential quarters and its administrative buildings, is easy enough to picture from the archaeology. The excavation teams that have worked at Kerma since the early 1900s have mapped the footprint of what is clearly a real city — streets, houses, workshops, storage areas, temples. At its peak, Classic Kerma held somewhere over 10,000 people inside its walls. That is not Uruk or Memphis or Mohenjo-Daro. It is not in the same size class as the biggest Bronze Age cities. But it is, by a long way, the biggest city in Africa outside Egypt in its era, and it is a real urban environment — not a large village, not a ceremonial center with a few attached houses, but a working city that had to feed itself, police itself, organize its labor, and project political authority over a large hinterland.

But the city is not what takes your breath away. The cemetery is.

A few kilometers east of the city proper, on the edge of the floodplain, the kings of Kerma were buried in enormous circular **tumuli** (TOO-myoo-lye — the Latin-derived plural of tumulus, meaning a burial mound). A tumulus is a mound of earth piled over a grave, and in most cultures that use them — from Neolithic Europe to Iron Age Scythia to the early Koreans — they run from a few meters to a few tens of meters in diameter. The Kerma royal tumuli are bigger. The largest of them are nearly the size of a football field across. Their surfaces were originally covered in black and white pebbles arranged in patterns, so that from a distance the cemetery would have looked like a landscape of giant decorated discs. The entire royal necropolis of Kerma stretches for more than a mile along the eastern edge of the site. Thousands of graves, ranging from simple individual burials to the monumental royal tumuli, cluster along the cemetery avenue in an arrangement that was clearly planned and controlled.

Inside the big tumuli, the archaeology gets unsettling.

---

When the Harvard archaeologist **George Reisner** excavated the royal tumuli of Kerma in the early 1920s, he was looking for what you would expect from a royal burial ground: the main tomb chamber containing the king, with a selection of grave goods around him, probably including some evidence of sacrificed animals. What he found was the king, the grave goods, and several hundred additional human bodies.

The bodies were arranged in long corridors radiating out from or alongside the central burial chamber. They were not randomly distributed; they were laid out in what looked like deliberate rows. They included men, women, and children, of varying ages and body types, in clothing that ranged from simple to elaborate. They were not beaten or mutilated. Several of them appeared to have been placed alive — their positions suggesting that the bodies had composed themselves into sleeping postures at the moment of death, rather than having been dropped in after being killed elsewhere.

The interpretation that Reisner reached, and that has held up in its broad strokes despite a century of argument about the specifics, is that the dead in the corridors were **retainers** — members of the king's household and court — who were killed (or who took their own lives) at the king's funeral in order to accompany him into the afterlife. In the largest Kerma royal tumuli, the retainer count runs into the hundreds. Reisner's own reports on the biggest tumuli gave figures of three to four hundred additional bodies per tomb, and while later re-analyses have quibbled with exact numbers, the broad magnitude is undisputed: we are talking about hundreds of people sacrificed at a single king's burial.

This is a genuinely shocking scale, and it is worth walking through the comparative record carefully, because the numbers put Kerma in an unusual position among Bronze Age royal burial traditions. The major Bronze Age cradles that left substantial royal cemeteries all handled the question of retainer sacrifice differently, and the comparison is illuminating.

**Egypt** predates them all: King Djer's First Dynasty tomb at Abydos (~3000 BCE) contained 338 subsidiary burials — the earliest large-scale retainer sacrifice in any of the Bronze Age cradles (Early Dynastic Egypt Ch 6). The practice lasted only a few generations before the Egyptians abandoned it, but at its peak the numbers dwarf even Kerma.

**Mesopotamia** had the famous Royal Tombs of Ur, excavated by Leonard Woolley in the 1920s, where the largest royal grave (Queen Puabi's, around 2600 BCE) contained 74 sacrificed attendants lined up in the death pit (Mesopotamia Ch 4). That is the upper end for Mesopotamian royal sacrifice — an order of magnitude fewer than Kerma's corridors.

**Northern China** has the late Longshan site of Shimao (roughly 2000 BCE), where the builders walled up decapitated young women's heads in foundation deposits, and the early Shang royal tombs at Yinxu where retainer sacrifice in the range of dozens per tomb was standard (Ancient China Ch 6). Again, real and grim, but not at Kerma's numerical scale.

**The Indus Valley** has nothing comparable at all. The Harappan cities produced almost no royal burials of any kind — no kings in gold masks, no deep shaft tombs, no retainer pits, no conspicuous accumulation of wealth in graves whatsoever (Indus Valley Ch 8). If the Harappans had royal retinues, they did not kill them at their masters' funerals.

**The Andes** join the Indus as a non-participant — Caral (~2600 BCE) shows no retainer sacrifice, no weapons, no hierarchical burial at all (Early Andean Ch 3). Interestingly, much later Andean cultures (the Moche, the Inca) would practice elaborate sacrifice, making the Andean trajectory the inverse of most Old World patterns: starting peaceful and developing sacrifice later.

The Indus and the Andes are the outliers, and they are outliers in a direction no one would have predicted: toward egalitarian burial custom in some of the world's first urban civilizations.

**Kerma** is the high end. Hundreds of retainers in the largest tumuli, an order of magnitude larger than Ur's death pits, maintained across multiple royal generations as a routine part of how a Kerma king was buried. No other Bronze Age burial tradition currently known produced corridors of that density around a single central chamber.

What does this tell us about Kerma? Two things, mostly.

First, the Kerma state had the kind of absolute royal authority that could mobilize this level of ritual sacrifice. Hundreds of people do not volunteer to die together on any ordinary day. The power structure at Kerma was willing to spend a catastrophic amount of human capital on a single ceremony, and it had the political machinery to carry the ceremony out. This is not a tribal chiefdom. This is a state with teeth.

Second, and this is harder to reconstruct, the Kerma religious system placed extraordinary weight on the afterlife of the king. The sacrificed retainers were not generic victims; they were the king's people, going with him into whatever continuation of existence the Kerma cosmology envisioned. The practice presupposes a worldview in which the continuity of the royal household mattered across the death barrier, and in which belonging to that household conferred not just obligations in life but obligations after death. Whatever the specific beliefs were — and without texts, we can't recover them in detail — they were powerful enough to sustain mass retainer burial as a repeated royal practice for generations.

We do not have to romanticize this. Retainer sacrifice is, from any modern moral perspective, an atrocity. But we also do not have to pretend it is uniquely Nubian barbarism, because it isn't. The technical contribution of the Kerma evidence to world archaeology is that it shows this kind of mass royal burial running parallel to, and at a much larger scale than, the Sumerian or early Chinese examples. Kerma was not a backward outlier. It was the extreme case on a spectrum of Bronze Age royal practices that ran from Egypt's First Dynasty through Ur through Shimao to Kerma, all converging on the basic idea that kings should be accompanied into death by the people who served them in life. The Harappans and the early Andeans decided not to play that game at all — and the fact that they are the outliers is one of the most interesting data points in Bronze Age cultural history.

---

There was a second monumental building at Kerma, beyond the famous Western Deffufa in the city proper. The **Eastern Deffufa** was a separate mud-brick complex sitting out in the royal cemetery, a few kilometers from the city, and functioning as the mortuary temple associated with the royal burials. The Eastern Deffufa is not as tall as its western cousin, but it is still a substantial construction — walled enclosures, chambers, an altar space, and a clear ritual orientation toward the cemetery itself. At its peak, it would have been the site of the funerary processions that accompanied a royal burial out from the city to the cemetery, through the approach corridors, and into the final sealing of a royal tumulus.

Taken together — the city, the Western Deffufa, the cemetery, the Eastern Deffufa, the retainer burials, the imported luxury goods, the population over ten thousand, the defensive walls — Classic Kerma is an urban and political package that is unambiguously a state, and unambiguously a state with its own character. It is not a miniature Egypt. It borrows from Egypt (in architectural techniques, in some pottery forms, in trade patterns) but it does not imitate Egypt. The royal cemetery form is Kerman, not Egyptian. The deffufa as an architectural type is Kerman, not Egyptian. The emphasis on cattle as grave goods is Kerman, not Egyptian. The retainer sacrifice tradition is Kerman, and at a scale not seen in Egypt at all. Classic Kerma is a genuine African urban civilization with its own structural logic, running parallel to pharaonic Egypt but on its own terms.

---

If the cemetery is what Kerma is remembered for, the tulip beaker is what Kerma was known for at the time — by Egyptian merchants, by Egyptian royal courts, by anybody in the Bronze Age Mediterranean who cared about having beautiful things on a table.

A **Kerma fine ware** tulip beaker is a hand-built ceramic cup, tulip-shaped, with walls so thin and so evenly potted that they look machine-made to a modern eye. The walls are almost eggshell thin, a continuation of the A-Group craft tradition from Chapter 1 carried to a much higher level of technical refinement. The color scheme is dramatic: a polished black rim band on top of a brilliant red lower body, with a thin gray zone separating the two. Achieving that effect required controlling the firing atmosphere precisely, letting oxygen in during part of the firing and cutting it off during the rest, so that the same clay body produced two different colors on different parts of the same vessel. Egyptian wheel-thrown pottery of the same era is thicker, coarser, and monochrome. The Kerma fine ware is more technically impressive than anything Egypt was producing in pottery terms, and the Egyptians knew it. Tulip beakers have been excavated from elite Egyptian contexts as far north as Thebes, which means Kerma was exporting its best craft products into the Egyptian luxury market as trade goods alongside the more predictable gold and ivory. For at least one product category, the direction of luxury trade ran *north* from Nubia to Egypt, not the other way.

Tulip ware is the best single window into the Kerma economy because it is the product that Kerma's competitors acknowledged by paying for it. Kerma was also producing bronze daggers and razors (borrowing and locally modifying Egyptian metalworking techniques), linen and leather and dyed textiles, and working volumes of ivory and gold that flowed through the city's workshops on their way to richer markets upstream and downstream. But the beakers are the part that survived intact, and the beakers are the part that tells you what Kerma thought of itself: a place whose artisans made things other people wanted to buy.

Classic Kerma had, in other words, a manufacturing economy. It wasn't just a gold-and-ivory pipeline. It was a city whose artisans produced finished goods that Egyptian elites considered worth buying, and whose kings used trade surplus to fund the construction of deffufas, the operation of the royal cemetery, and the upkeep of a military and political apparatus large enough to make the Middle Kingdom pharaohs nervous.

---

One question that bothers every serious reader of Kerma history: why no writing?

Kerma had all the ingredients that other ancient civilizations used to develop writing. It had long-distance trade that needed accounting. It had a centralized state that needed record-keeping. It had elite classes who had contact with Egyptian scribes and Egyptian documents. It had religious institutions that could have underwritten a priestly scribal tradition. It had the wealth to train and support specialists. And yet, as far as the archaeological record currently shows, Kerma produced no writing system of its own. The earliest surviving Nubian script — a set of characters called **Meroitic** (meh-ROW-it-ik), developed by the later Kingdom of Kush — does not appear until more than a thousand years after Classic Kerma ended.

Why not? There are several possible answers and no consensus.

The closest analogue in the Bronze Age is the Indus Valley Civilization (Indus Valley Ch 6). The Harappans of Mohenjo-daro and Harappa ran a huge, sophisticated, urban society for six or seven hundred years and left behind a corpus of short inscriptions on small stone seals — the Indus script — which no one has ever successfully deciphered and which almost nobody thinks is a true writing system in the full sense of encoding a spoken language phonetically. The Indus script and Kerma's silence are the two great "missing voices" of the Bronze Age, and they are different kinds of missing. The Harappans had *something* that looks like writing and we can't read it; the Nubians appear to have had nothing that looks like writing at all. But both cases force the same kind of question on the archaeologist: how does a state that complex govern itself without the thing the other great ancient states treated as a fundamental tool of governance?

Three candidate answers for Kerma. First: Kerma may have had a writing system on perishable materials — papyrus, leather, wood, wax tablets — that simply didn't survive the way Egyptian papyrus survived in dry desert tombs. If Kerma's administrative records were written in a local script on papyrus and kept in palaces rather than tombs, they could be gone without a trace.

Second: Kerma may have used Egyptian scribes and the Egyptian language for the small amount of writing it needed, rather than developing its own script. There is some evidence for this — a few Egyptian documents found at Kerma, a few administrative seals with Egyptian markings — suggesting that Kerma elites outsourced their formal writing needs to imported or Egyptian-trained scribes. This would be a case of a culture reaching the point where writing would be useful and solving the problem through borrowing instead of invention.

Third, and this is the one that makes some Nubianists uncomfortable: Kerma may really have run a complex urban state for a thousand years without writing. Writing is not universally necessary to statehood. The Incas of Peru — a fifteenth-century-CE Andean empire that ruled some twelve million people from its capital at Cuzco using a road network as long as the Roman empire's — ran that whole system using knotted cords called **quipu** (KEE-poo), in which information was encoded in the pattern, length, color, and knot position of dangling strings rather than in any phonetic script. The quipu tradition is not a late Inca invention — the earliest known quipu fragments come from Caral (~2600 BCE), making this the world's longest-running non-textual information technology at roughly 4,000 years (Early Andean Ch 3). Whether the quipu counts as "writing" in the strict sense is still debated; what is not in doubt is that the Incas did not need alphabetic writing to run their empire. Kerma might have been an earlier version of the same phenomenon — a state that compensated for the absence of writing with oral traditions, memory specialists, physical tokens, and administrative systems we have not yet recognized in the archaeological record.

The honest answer is that we don't know which of these is right, and it is probably some mix of all three. The absence of Kerma writing is a frustrating gap in what we can reconstruct about the kingdom, because writing is the one thing that lets ancient people speak to us in their own voice. Without it, Kerma is silent. Every word in this chapter about what Kerma was like has been reconstructed from material remains and Egyptian outside observation. What Kerma kings thought of themselves, what their theology looked like in detail, what name they used for their own country — all of this is gone, probably permanently, because the people who could have told us did not leave writing we can read.

---

There is one more feature of Classic Kerma worth mentioning before we move into its final crisis. Kerma had diplomatic and military relations that stretched far beyond the Egyptian frontier.

Scattered finds from the Classic Kerma period show Kerma-style objects as far north as the eastern Mediterranean coast, including in Hyksos-controlled northern Egypt, and Kerma pottery in contexts that hint at regional trade networks reaching into what is now Ethiopia and the Red Sea coast. Kerma was not an isolated upriver kingdom. It was plugged into the wider Bronze Age world — aware of the Hyksos in Lower Egypt, connected to the incense trade from the Horn of Africa, sending craft products to trading partners who were themselves linked to the broader ancient network that ran from Mesopotamia through the Levant and into Egypt.

And by around 1650 BCE, Kerma's diplomatic reach into the Hyksos-controlled north was about to produce a genuine two-front crisis for the Theban pharaohs who were trying to re-unify Egypt and rebuild the Middle Kingdom's collapse. The Theban pharaohs did not like being squeezed between Hyksos to the north and Kerma to the south. The Theban pharaohs were going to do something about it. Their response would end the Kerma kingdom.

# Chapter 6 — The Squeeze and the Fall

In the seventeenth century BCE, the political map of the Nile valley started to break.

The Middle Kingdom that had built the forts had unraveled. Another interregnum, which historians call the **Second Intermediate Period**, was underway. The northern delta of Egypt had fallen under the control of the Hyksos — those "rulers of foreign lands" we briefly met at the end of Chapter 4. A fuller introduction is overdue, since the Hyksos are about to play a central role for one chapter.

The Hyksos were speakers of a Semitic language, not Egyptian. That matters, and it matters in a way that's worth unpacking. Egyptian belongs to a language family called Afro-Asiatic, but its closest relatives inside that family are North African languages — Berber, Cushitic tongues from the Horn of Africa — not the languages the Hyksos spoke. The Hyksos spoke something ancestral to, or closely related to, the Semitic languages we know best: Akkadian (the ancient Mesopotamian tongue of Hammurabi and the Assyrian kings — Mesopotamia Ch 5 onward), Hebrew, Aramaic, Arabic. The gulf between Egyptian and Hyksos is about the same as the gulf between a Western European language and a Middle Eastern one. Modern English speakers trying to learn Arabic get a rough sense of the same linguistic distance. The Hyksos were genuinely foreign in the strong sense of the word — originally migrants from the Levant (the coastal strip east of the Mediterranean, modern Lebanon, Israel, and western Syria) who had come into the Nile delta over several generations as traders, settlers, and mercenaries before any of them were rulers.

By around 1650 BCE, they weren't just settlers anymore. They had established a dynasty that controlled Lower Egypt and called itself Pharaoh. They had been in Egypt long enough to be culturally Egyptianized in the public-facing parts of their rule — they adopted pharaonic titles, royal iconography, Egyptian religious forms — but their linguistic and ethnic origins remained visibly Levantine, and the Theban Egyptians to the south considered them an illegitimate foreign occupation of the sacred throne of Upper and Lower Egypt.

The Hyksos controlled the north. The Theban pharaohs of the **Seventeenth Dynasty** controlled the south, specifically the stretch of Upper Egypt from around Thebes (modern Luxor) down to the First Cataract at Aswan. Between these two Egyptian powers ran a long, unhappy border through the middle of the country — skirmishes and raids and intermittent diplomatic exchanges but no decisive conflict for several generations. It was a stalemate.

And south of Thebes, past the First Cataract, the old Middle Kingdom fort chain was still standing but no longer properly garrisoned. Kerma, sensing the weakness, had moved.

---

The best evidence for what happened during the Kerma–Hyksos coalition comes not from any Kerma source (because we have no Kerma sources, period) and not from the Hyksos side either (almost nothing of the Hyksos royal archives has survived from their delta capital at **Avaris**, which sat in what is now the northeastern Nile delta). It comes from the winner. Specifically, it comes from a royal Theban monument called the **Second Kamose Stele**, erected at Karnak (the great temple complex just north of Thebes) to commemorate the wars of a pharaoh named **Kamose** (KAH-moh-say), the last king of the Theban Seventeenth Dynasty, older brother of the man who would eventually finish the job of throwing the Hyksos out of Egypt.

Kamose's stele tells the story of his campaign against the Hyksos from his own point of view, and the point of view is furious. The most-quoted passage describes a specific incident: a Hyksos courier, carrying a letter from the Hyksos king in Avaris south toward Kerma, was intercepted by Kamose's troops somewhere in the desert west of the Nile. Kamose read the letter and had its text carved onto his victory stele — not paraphrased, not summarized, but quoted, because he wanted posterity to know exactly what his two enemies had been saying to each other behind his back.

The letter is addressed from the Hyksos king (a ruler named **Apophis**, AP-oh-fiss, ruling from Avaris) to the "Ruler of Kush." Apophis writes, roughly: come north, don't hold back, the Theban king is hemmed in here with me and has nowhere to go, let's split the land of Egypt between us. It is an unambiguous two-front alliance, preserved on the monument of the Theban pharaoh who intercepted it and considered it justification for the wars that followed.

This is also, incidentally, the moment when the name **Kush** becomes firmly attached to the Nubian kingdom in Egyptian sources. "Ruler of Kush" is how Apophis addresses his Kerma counterpart in the intercepted letter. The word had been drifting into use for a century or two, gradually displacing the older geographic terms Wawat and Yam. From the Second Intermediate Period onward, when Egyptians wrote about the kingdom upstream, they wrote about Kush. We will use "Kerma" in this book because the kingdom's capital was the city of Kerma and the archaeological culture is called Kerma, but you should know that the Egyptians of the seventeenth and sixteenth centuries BCE, and everyone afterward, called it Kush.

Kamose's counterattack was decisive in the short term but not in the long term. He pushed the Hyksos back. He raided northward. He also raided southward against the Kushite holdings in Lower Nubia. He did not, however, actually destroy either enemy before his death. That job fell to his younger brother and successor, **Ahmose I** (AH-moh-say). The two brothers' names are frustratingly close — Kamose starts with a K, Ahmose with an A, but both are pronounced with the same AH-moh-say sound at the end — and if you keep them straight, Kamose is the older brother who started the war and Ahmose is the younger brother who finished it. Ahmose ruled from around 1550 BCE and is considered the founder of the **Eighteenth Dynasty** and the **New Kingdom**, the era of Egypt's imperial zenith.

Ahmose drove the Hyksos out of Egypt. He pushed them back across the Sinai, captured their capital at Avaris, and ended their dynasty. This is the famous story of Egyptian reunification, and it is told in every Egyptian history book. What those books often leave out is what Ahmose did next. Having cleaned out the north, Ahmose turned around and marched south. He reoccupied the old Middle Kingdom forts, reclaimed Lower Nubia up to the Second Cataract, and began preparing a sustained southern campaign that would be finished by his successors. The Theban dynasty had learned its lesson from the two-front squeeze. The kingdom of Kush was not going to get another chance to coordinate with anybody.

---

The final destruction of Kerma came under a king named **Thutmose I** (THOOT-moh-say), the third pharaoh of the Eighteenth Dynasty, who took the throne around 1504 BCE and who had an idea about what "southern boundary" should actually mean.

Thutmose I was not the first New Kingdom pharaoh to campaign against Kush. He was not even the second. But he was the first one who decided that containment at the Second Cataract was no longer good enough, and that the Kushite kingdom itself — the city, the royal cemetery, the deffufas, the whole Classic Kerma civilization — needed to be eliminated as a strategic threat to Egypt.

The campaign Thutmose I launched was an enormous logistical effort. Egyptian river forces, supported by land columns, pushed past the Second Cataract, past the Third Cataract, past Kerma itself, and all the way to the region of the **Fourth Cataract** — more than 600 miles (1,000 km) upstream from the Egyptian border. The logistical problem that had bottled up earlier pharaohs at the Second Cataract (the whitewater that stopped their boats) had been solved a century earlier by the Middle Kingdom slipway at Mirgissa, and New Kingdom Egypt, with its more aggressive military apparatus, had rebuilt and expanded that infrastructure. By the time Thutmose I's force moved south, getting Egyptian boats past the cataracts was a solved engineering problem rather than an impossible one. Along the way, Egyptian soldiers captured, sacked, and destroyed the city of Kerma. The Western Deffufa, which had stood for more than a thousand years, was damaged but not toppled (it still stands today). The royal cemetery was shut down. The administrative structures of the kingdom were broken. The ruler of Kush — we do not know his name, because Kerma did not write and the Egyptian sources do not record it — was killed, captured, or fled south into the uncontrolled interior.

What Thutmose I did next is the detail that sticks with you. On his way back downstream, he commemorated the victory with a stele at a place called **Tombos**, near the Third Cataract, well inside Nubia. The stele describes, in the formal register of Egyptian royal inscriptions, the fate of one of the Nubian captives taken on the campaign: his body was hung "head-downward at the prow of the falcon-ship of his majesty." The falcon-ship was the name of Thutmose I's royal boat. Sailing downriver with a corpse hanging upside down from the bow was a public announcement, to every riverside village the boat passed on its way back north, that this particular southern campaign had ended with a body on a boat. It was a message to Nubia: come back in the next generation, try your luck again, and there will be another body on another boat.

The Kingdom of Kerma, after roughly a thousand years of continuous existence, was over.

---

It is worth pausing here to grieve, briefly, the lost voices of Kerma.

We will never hear a Kerma poem, or a Kerma prayer, or a Kerma child's lullaby. We will never know the name of the Kerma god who was worshipped in the Western Deffufa, or the name of the king who ordered the Eastern Deffufa built, or the names of the hundreds of retainers who went into the royal tumuli. We will never read a Kerma account of the Hyksos alliance, or a Kerma description of what the city looked like at sunset, or a Kerma explanation of why some people chose to die with their king. Every single thing we know about Kerma comes from its physical remains and from the outside perspective of its Egyptian enemies, who had political reasons to describe it as an enemy to be defeated rather than as a civilization to be understood.

This is the inheritance of writing's absence. Egyptian civilization, with its papyrus and its hieroglyphs, has a thousand-year head start on Kerma in every cultural category that requires written evidence, because Egyptian voices survived and Kerma voices did not. If Kerma had developed a script of its own and written on durable enough material — or if Egyptian archaeologists had been less efficient about removing later layers to reach earlier ones — we might know as much about Classic Kerma as we know about Old Kingdom Egypt. We don't. The Kerma civilization, which by any reasonable measure was one of the most impressive Bronze Age African polities outside Egypt itself, survives in our books as a set of buildings, a style of pottery, a cemetery full of bones, and a sketch of its political history as written by its enemies.

The good news is that the defeat of the Kerma kingdom did not mean the end of Nubian civilization. What the Egyptian sources present as a total conquest was actually the beginning of a new phase — a phase in which Nubian elites, Nubian religious traditions, Nubian soldiers, and Nubian cultural practices would be absorbed into the Egyptian New Kingdom as provincial subjects, but would also quietly carry forward the continuity of Nubian identity under Egyptian overlay. Five centuries after Thutmose I destroyed Kerma, the descendants of those absorbed Nubians would become the **Kushite pharaohs**, the kings of the Egyptian Twenty-fifth Dynasty, and they would — incredibly — conquer Egypt itself.

That story is the next TL in this chain (Kingdom of Kush Ch 1). For this one, we still have five centuries of Egyptian Nubia to work through, and that is the subject of the last two chapters.

---

# Chapter 7 — Province of Gold

After Thutmose I, Nubia was no longer a separate country. It was a province of Egypt.

The administrative system the New Kingdom pharaohs invented for governing it was a thing we haven't really talked about yet, and it was an innovation — at least for Egypt, and at least on this scale. (The broader claim that it was a *world-first* provincial administration would be an overclaim: in Mesopotamia, Sargon of Akkad had begun appointing his own loyal governors to rule conquered city-states roughly 1,800 years earlier — Mesopotamia Ch 5 — so the basic idea of running conquered territory through a pharaoh-appointed governor had precedent further east. What was new about the Viceroy of Kush was the scale, the permanence, and the specific bureaucratic machinery Egypt developed around it.) Up until the New Kingdom, Egypt had never had to run a colonial administration of a conquered territory on this scale. The Old Kingdom had extracted from Nubia through raids; the Middle Kingdom had contained Nubia through forts. Neither of those approaches involved the actual civil governance of a conquered province. Once Kerma was destroyed and the whole Nile valley from the First to the Fourth Cataract was nominally Egyptian territory, somebody had to collect the taxes, run the temples, administer the courts, manage the gold mines, handle the garrisons, deal with the local populations, and report the whole thing back to the pharaoh in Thebes. The New Kingdom invented a governor for this job and gave him one of the most revealing titles in Egyptian administrative history: **Viceroy of Kush**.

The full Egyptian title was usually something like *sa-nesu en Kush*, meaning "King's Son of Kush," which did not necessarily mean the viceroy was an actual son of the pharaoh — it was an honorific suggesting that he held royal-level authority over his province, as if he were a member of the royal family. In practice, the Viceroy of Kush was usually a senior Egyptian official handpicked by the pharaoh, often with military and administrative experience, and sometimes an aristocrat from a trusted Egyptian noble family. He was the highest civil authority in Nubia. He had responsibility for everything from tax collection to temple maintenance, and he reported directly to the pharaoh in Thebes. Under him sat a layered bureaucracy of deputies, garrison commanders, temple priests, mine overseers, and district administrators, both Egyptian and Nubian, forming a colonial administrative structure as elaborate as anything the later Roman empire or the Achaemenid Persian empire (the first Persian empire, founded by Cyrus the Great around 550 BCE, which governed everything from the Aegean to the Indus with a similar layered provincial system) would invent.

The Viceroy's main job was gold.

---

Nubia's most important export to Egypt, throughout the New Kingdom, was gold. Not the only export — ebony, ivory, cattle, incense, and slaves all moved north along with it — but gold was the indispensable one. The gold that filled the treasuries of Thebes, the gold that plated the chapels and the obelisks, the gold that paid tribute to foreign kings and bribed allied states, the gold that gave Egypt diplomatic leverage across the Bronze Age Near East — all of that gold, or almost all of it, came out of Nubia. When the New Kingdom pharaoh in Thebes looked at his treasury and saw the wealth that let him intimidate the Hittites, buy off the Mitannians, and build whatever monument he wanted, he was looking at Nubian gold filtered through the Viceroy's office.

Nubia's gold was mostly in two regions. The first was the **Wadi Allaqi** (WAH-dee ah-LAK-ee), a desert valley east of the Nile in Lower Nubia that drained a set of gold-bearing rock formations in the Red Sea Hills. The second was the region around the Fourth Cataract in Upper Nubia, where additional gold-bearing rock had been worked since the Old Kingdom on a smaller scale. Both regions contained quartz veins that carried native gold — not gold dust in riverbeds, but gold embedded in solid rock that had to be extracted by hand. The mining technique was brutal. Gangs of laborers, many of them prisoners of war, convicted criminals, or debt-bondaged Nubians, broke up the quartz with stone and later metal hammers, carried the fragments to a central crushing area, pulverized them into powder, washed the powder to separate the heavier gold particles from the lighter rock fragments, and collected the gold. The whole process was carried out in desert conditions with minimal water, under a broiling sun, with inadequate food and high mortality.

The best surviving eyewitness account of ancient Nubian gold mining comes not from the New Kingdom itself but from **Diodorus Siculus** (dye-oh-DOR-us SIK-yoo-lus — a Greek historian who wrote a world history around 30 BCE, more than a thousand years after the peak of New Kingdom mining but while the same general technology and labor methods were apparently still in use). Diodorus visited or interviewed people who had visited the mines, and his description of what he saw is the most cited passage in the entire ancient literature on the subject. He describes chained workers being driven into cramped underground shafts without protection, children carrying the ore, old men crushing it, women sieving it, everybody too exhausted to speak and too malnourished to stand up straight. The translation varies but the judgment does not: "Death is more welcome to them than life, such hardship do they endure." Diodorus is writing twelve centuries after Thutmose I, so he isn't a direct witness to New Kingdom conditions, but the continuity of ancient mining methods and the Nubian archaeological record both suggest that the New Kingdom version of the job was not much kinder. The gold that made Egypt a superpower came out of the ground at the price of Nubian bodies.

What the New Kingdom got out of this was extraordinary. Egyptian gold production during the **Eighteenth** and **Nineteenth Dynasties** — both of which are New Kingdom dynasties in the shared framework we set up in Chapter 2, the Eighteenth running from Ahmose I through Tutankhamun and the Nineteenth containing Ramesses II — reached a scale the ancient Mediterranean would not see again for centuries. New Kingdom Nubia was producing gold at a rate that supported one of the richest royal treasuries of the Bronze Age Mediterranean, bankrolled major monument construction projects, and gave Egyptian diplomats the resources to buy the loyalty of foreign rulers across the Levant and the Aegean.

The best surviving evidence of how important Nubian gold was to the wider Late Bronze Age diplomatic system comes from a stash of clay tablets discovered in the late nineteenth century at the Egyptian site of Tell el-Amarna, the short-lived capital built by the pharaoh **Akhenaten** (AH-ken-AH-ten) around 1350 BCE. (Akhenaten is famous in Egyptian history for a different reason: he attempted a religious revolution, replacing the traditional pantheon with an exclusive worship of the sun disk itself, and when he died his successors rolled back the revolution and tried to erase his name. His son Tutankhamun is the "boy pharaoh" whose intact tomb Howard Carter found in 1922. We'll touch Akhenaten's religious experiment only in passing; what matters here is that he briefly moved the Egyptian capital to Tell el-Amarna and that the diplomatic correspondence from his reign, preserved when the site was abandoned, is our best window into Late Bronze Age international relations.)

The tablets are the **Amarna Letters** — more than three hundred diplomatic documents, written in Akkadian cuneiform (the Babylonian language of international diplomacy in the Late Bronze Age, the era's closest equivalent to modern English), addressed to the pharaoh by his fellow Great Kings and by the minor rulers of Syrian and Canaanite city-states who were nominally under Egyptian protection. The Amarna Letters are covered in detail from the Mesopotamian side in Mesopotamia Chapter 8, which describes how the pharaoh was writing to and receiving letters from four fellow Great Kings:

- **The Kassite kings of Babylon**, who had taken over Mesopotamia after the old Hammurabi-era Babylonian dynasty collapsed, ruling from Babylon in what is now Iraq.
- **The Mitanni kings**, a Hurrian-speaking dynasty (Hurrian is a language family unrelated to either Egyptian or Akkadian) ruling a large territory in what is now northern Syria and southeastern Turkey.
- **The Hittites**, an Indo-European-speaking empire (their language is in the same broad family as later Greek, Latin, and English, and completely unrelated to either Egyptian or Akkadian) centered in central Anatolia, modern Turkey — the same empire Ramesses II would later fight at Kadesh.
- **The Middle Assyrian kings**, who were the forerunners of the terrifying Neo-Assyrian empire that would dominate the Near East three centuries later (Mesopotamia Ch 10–11), speaking another Semitic language related to Akkadian.

All four groups, plus dozens of smaller Syrian and Canaanite rulers, wrote to Egypt maintaining a delicate brotherhood-of-kings diplomacy around marriage alliances and gold.

The single most common substantive request in the Amarna Letters is for gold. Kassite and Mitanni kings write to the pharaoh with formulaic complaints — "my brother, your messengers have been received with honor, now please send gold, lots of it, for my temples and my palace, because in your country gold is as common as dust" — and the gold they are asking for is specifically *Nubian* gold. The Kassite king Burnaburiash II in one letter explicitly asks for "much gold" because a previous shipment had been short, and scholars who have studied the letters are clear that the gold Egypt was in a position to send (or not send) was coming out of Wadi Allaqi and the upper Nile mines. The Amarna Letters are the other end of Nubia's gold pipeline. When Apophis and Kerma had been squeezing Thebes a century and a half earlier, Egypt was gold-poor and isolated. When the Amarna kings were writing their "your country is full of gold" letters, Egypt had taken back the mines, and the letters are the proof that everybody else in the Bronze Age knew exactly where that gold came from.

A separate document worth mentioning briefly is the **Turin Papyrus Map**, dating from around 1150 BCE, at the very end of the New Kingdom. It covers an area of Egyptian desert east of Thebes where Egypt was also mining gold on a smaller scale, and it is one of the oldest surviving geological maps in human history — labeled gold mining areas, paths to reach them, quarries, settlements, and topography sketched in two colors. The Turin map is not itself a Nubian map, but it is the best surviving example of how the New Kingdom Egyptian state visualized its mining operations. A map of this kind presupposes organized, repeatable, technical surveying of a mining region. You do not draw a map of gold mines unless your state is committed to working the mines as a permanent industrial operation. Nubia's gold mines were run like an industry.

---

The Viceroy of Kush did not live in Kerma. Nobody lived in Kerma anymore, because Thutmose I's campaign had essentially ended the city as a political center. The main administrative capital of the New Kingdom's Nubian province was a different site — a place the Egyptians called **Miam** (MEE-am) and the modern archaeologists call **Aniba** (ah-NEE-bah), located in Lower Nubia a bit north of the Second Cataract, on the west bank of the Nile. Aniba/Miam had been occupied for centuries already as a C-Group community, but under New Kingdom administration it grew into a substantial town with Egyptian-style architecture, a garrison, a governor's residence, and a series of Egyptian-style tombs for the local nobility — tombs that are some of our best evidence for the cultural phenomenon we will talk about next chapter, the blending of Nubian and Egyptian elite identity under New Kingdom rule.

The Viceroy's administrative reach extended from Aniba south through a chain of subordinate centers and garrisons. Important nodes in the chain included the old Middle Kingdom forts at Buhen and Mirgissa (now refurbished as New Kingdom administrative centers rather than defensive installations), a new foundation at **Sesebi**, the major religious complex at **Soleb** (which we will get to in a moment), and the spiritual heart of Egyptian-era Nubia at **Jebel Barkal**. A typical message from the pharaoh to the Viceroy would be carried south by courier from Thebes to Aniba, then passed through this network of subordinate officials to wherever it needed to go. A typical tribute shipment would run in the other direction: gold from the mines, cattle from the C-Group villagers, ivory and ebony from deeper trade networks, all assembled at Aniba and shipped north to the royal court.

This was a functioning colonial administration. It was running for four hundred years. It is the direct model, whether or not anyone at the time noticed the resemblance, for the way later empires — Persians, Romans, Arabs, Ottomans, British — would eventually govern their own foreign provinces.

---

Of all the sites in New Kingdom Nubia, the most religiously important was **Jebel Barkal** (JEH-bel BAR-kal — meaning "Pure Mountain" in Arabic, a name that was assigned later; the ancient name was different).

Jebel Barkal is a flat-topped sandstone butte rising out of the plain near the modern Sudanese town of Karima, about 200 miles (320 km) upstream from Kerma, well south of the Fourth Cataract. Viewed from certain angles, the mountain has a distinctive feature: a tall pillar of rock that sticks up near its corner, resembling an abstracted human figure or a standing cobra depending on your inclination. To Egyptian eyes looking at this mountain, the standing rock pillar looked like a **uraeus** — the rearing cobra figure on an Egyptian pharaoh's crown, symbol of royal divinity. Egyptians who reached Jebel Barkal during New Kingdom campaigns recognized it immediately as sacred, and they interpreted the pillar as a sign that the god **Amun** was physically present in the mountain. (Amun, AH-mun, was the king of the Egyptian pantheon during the New Kingdom. His main temple was at **Karnak**, the enormous temple complex on the eastern bank of the Nile just north of Thebes, which grew across the New Kingdom into one of the largest religious structures ever built in the ancient world, with walled precincts, obelisks, hypostyle halls the size of cathedrals, and processional avenues of sphinxes. When Egyptian sources call a god "the Amun of Karnak," they mean the specific manifestation worshipped in that temple complex.)

Thutmose III, grandson of Thutmose I and the most accomplished military pharaoh of the early New Kingdom, built the first major Egyptian temple at Jebel Barkal around 1450 BCE, consecrated to Amun and explicitly linking the sacred mountain of Nubia to the god of Thebes. The implicit theology was that Amun had two dwellings — the main one at Karnak, the other here at Jebel Barkal — and that the pharaoh who could claim both was the legitimate king of the entire Nile valley, Egypt and Nubia together. Jebel Barkal became the southern spiritual anchor of Egyptian royal ideology. Every New Kingdom pharaoh who cared about Nubia at all paid religious respect to the mountain, built or maintained temples at its base, and had his priests conduct ceremonies linking him to the Amun of Napata (the later name for the Jebel Barkal region).

The reason Jebel Barkal matters for the long arc of Nubian history is that Egyptian religious investment in the site took hold of the local Nubian population in a way that outlasted Egyptian political control. When Egypt eventually withdrew from Nubia in the eleventh century BCE, the local elites continued to worship Amun at Jebel Barkal. They maintained the temples. They kept the priestly traditions. They raised their children in an Amun-centered religious system that had originally come from Egypt but had now become authentically Nubian. Five centuries later, the Kushite kings of the Twenty-fifth Dynasty — the Nubian conquerors who briefly ruled Egypt itself — would claim Jebel Barkal as their ancestral holy place and use their devotion to Amun as legitimation for their pharaonic ambitions. The long-term consequence of Thutmose III's temple at Jebel Barkal was that Egypt effectively gave Nubia a god, and the Nubians held on to the god even after they had shaken off the givers.

This is one of the stranger ironies of ancient religious history. Amun was a Theban god, a local deity who had risen to the top of the Egyptian pantheon during the Middle Kingdom and become the imperial god of the New Kingdom. He was as Egyptian as a god could be. And yet, by the ninth or eighth century BCE, the most zealous and politically ambitious worshippers of Amun on the planet were Nubian kings at Jebel Barkal, preparing the invasion that would briefly make them pharaohs of Egypt on the strength of their theological credentials. The god had emigrated, and the god had made a new home.

---

Jebel Barkal was the spiritual anchor, but it was not the only monument the New Kingdom left in Nubia. Across four hundred years of Egyptian administration, pharaohs competed to build temples up and down the Nubian Nile — Thutmose III and Amenhotep II's modest but well-preserved **Temple of Amada**, whose walls record the grimmest royal inscription in the province (Amenhotep II's boast about executing seven captured Syrian princes and displaying their bodies as warnings to anyone considering rebellion); Amenhotep III's elegant **Temple of Soleb**, with its processional avenue of ram-headed sphinxes and its finely carved reliefs integrating Nubia into the ritual geography of the New Kingdom; forts-turned-administrative-centers at Buhen, Mirgissa, Aniba; the governor's residences and priestly quarters that served the viceregal bureaucracy.

But the single most famous, and the most aggressive, Egyptian monument ever built in Nubia came a century after Soleb, under a king who understood propaganda as well as any Bronze Age ruler who ever lived.

---

**Ramesses II** (RAH-meh-seez, sometimes spelled Ramses II or Rameses II) ruled Egypt from around 1279 to 1213 BCE — a reign of roughly sixty-six years, long enough that many of his subjects never lived under any other king. He fought the famous battle of **Kadesh** (KAY-desh — a frontier city in what is now western Syria, contested between Egypt and the Hittite empire of central Anatolia, the scene of one of the earliest battles in human history for which we have detailed written accounts from both sides) against the Hittites, fathered so many children that the traditional count runs into the dozens, built monuments in quantities that would be absurd in any other pharaoh's career, and is generally considered the most self-celebrating Egyptian king in recorded history. Ramesses built temples everywhere. But the two he built at a place called **Abu Simbel** (ah-boo SIM-bel), in Lower Nubia about 180 miles (290 km) south of Aswan, are in a class by themselves.

Abu Simbel consists of two rock-cut temples carved directly into the face of a sandstone cliff on the west bank of the Nile. The larger temple is dedicated to Ramesses himself, with a nominal co-dedication to the gods Amun (king of the Egyptian pantheon, whom we met at Jebel Barkal), **Ra** (the sun god, often depicted as a falcon-headed figure with a sun disk above his head, associated with the daily cycle of rising and setting, the other great imperial Egyptian deity), and **Ptah** (the creator god of Memphis, patron of craftsmen, associated with the subterranean and the hidden). Listing Ramesses alongside Amun, Ra, and Ptah was a theological statement: the king is their equal, of their rank, deserving of the same worship. The smaller temple, slightly to the north, is dedicated to Ramesses's chief wife **Nefertari** (nef-er-TAR-ee) and to the goddess **Hathor** (a motherly, protective deity often associated with music, dance, joy, and female fertility, sometimes depicted with cow horns and a sun disk between them). Both temples are carved entirely into the cliff. They are not free-standing buildings. You walk into them through doorways cut in the rock face, and the entire interior — columns, chambers, sanctuaries, statues — is hollowed out of solid sandstone.

The entrance to the main temple is what sticks in the visitor's mind forever. Four enormous seated statues of Ramesses II, each about 66 feet (20 meters) tall, are carved out of the cliff face itself, staring east across the Nile toward the rising sun and the upstream lands of Nubia. The statues are not additions stuck onto the cliff; they are the cliff, shaped into the form of the pharaoh. A six-story office building carved out of rock is a useful mental anchor. When you stand at their feet and look upward, the effect is overwhelming, which is exactly the effect Ramesses intended. Abu Simbel was built as a permanent visual declaration to anyone sailing up or down the river: the pharaoh is here, he is enormous, he is watching you, and Nubia is his.

Inside the temple, the walls are covered with reliefs commemorating Ramesses's military victories, with particular emphasis on the Battle of Kadesh — a battle in which Ramesses's advance guard got surrounded by a Hittite ambush and the pharaoh had to lead a personal charge to break out, a story Ramesses considered so heroic that he had it carved on at least five different monuments across Egypt. The innermost sanctuary contains seated statues of Amun, Ra, the deified Ramesses, and Ptah. The temple was oriented with extraordinary astronomical care so that twice a year, on dates corresponding roughly to the anniversaries of Ramesses's birthday and his coronation, the rising sun shone directly through the entrance and illuminated the faces of three of the four sanctuary statues — all except Ptah, the god of the hidden depths, who stayed in shadow. This is not a coincidence. It is the product of a deliberate engineering effort to align the temple's axis with the sun's position on specific dates, and the Egyptian priests who built Abu Simbel were calculating the solar geometry with enough precision to get it right.

Abu Simbel's solar alignment has a modern sequel worth a paragraph on its own. In the 1960s, when the Aswan High Dam was flooding the Nile valley upstream and threatening to drown the temple under Lake Nasser, an international salvage operation disassembled Abu Simbel stone by stone, hauled it 200 feet (60 meters) higher up the cliff, and reconstructed it on a new site safely above the new waterline. It was one of the most ambitious archaeological engineering projects ever attempted. The engineers who moved the temple made a serious effort to preserve the original solar alignment, and in the new position they nearly succeeded: the sun still illuminates the sanctuary statues on dates very close to the originals, but close inspection suggests the alignment is slightly off from the ancient builders' calculations. The story has been told and retold as an anecdote about ancient precision, with varying degrees of accuracy — some versions overstate the offset, some understate it — but the underlying point is real: moving a temple with its astronomy intact is hard, and getting it exactly right is harder.

Abu Simbel is the most extravagant single statement of Egyptian dominance ever made in Nubia. It was built to say, unambiguously, that Nubia was Egyptian territory, that the pharaoh's presence in Nubia was eternal, and that Nubian elites should look up at those four colossal stone faces and know whose province they were living in. The fact that less than two centuries after Abu Simbel was carved, the pharaoh's presence in Nubia was no longer eternal — that the New Kingdom was crumbling, the Viceroy of Kush had lost contact with the court, and Nubia was slipping out of Egyptian control — is one of the more bitter ironies of ancient monument-building. Abu Simbel outlasted the thing it was meant to represent by 3,000 years.

# Chapter 8 — The Cultural Bridge

Of all the features of New Kingdom Nubia, the strangest and most important is the thing that happened to the elites.

Here is the situation as of, say, 1400 BCE. Egypt has been governing Nubia as a colonial province for a century. The Viceroy of Kush runs the administration out of Aniba. The gold mines are operating. The temples are being built. Nubian laborers and soldiers are being recruited into Egyptian service in large numbers. The C-Group pottery tradition is still alive in the Lower Nubian villages, and the cultural continuity of ordinary Nubian life has not been erased — the Egyptian presence is an administrative overlay, not a cultural replacement at the village level.

But at the elite level, something weird is happening. The children of the Nubian nobility — the sons of local chiefs, the heirs of village headmen, the members of the minor ruling families that had survived the destruction of Kerma and the reorganization of the province — were being systematically taken to the Egyptian royal court in Thebes, educated there, and returned home as adult administrators.

This is documented in Egyptian sources as an official policy. It is sometimes called the **kap** system, from the Egyptian word for the royal nursery where royal children and elite hostages were raised together. Egyptian princes were educated alongside the sons of foreign rulers — Syrian princes, Libyan princes, and Nubian princes — under the direct supervision of the Egyptian court. The foreign children learned the Egyptian language, the Egyptian script, Egyptian religious practice, Egyptian administrative procedure, Egyptian military technique, and Egyptian aesthetic sensibilities. They also, importantly, formed personal relationships with the Egyptian royal heirs they grew up with. When the foreign child reached adulthood, he was sent home to rule his native province as an Egyptian-trained, Egyptian-loyal local ruler. When the Egyptian child became pharaoh, he had a set of foreign-born provincial administrators already in place, all of whom had been his boyhood classmates.

This is often described as the oldest systematic colonial elite-education program in world history, and it is close to the truth, but the fuller picture should acknowledge a quieter Mesopotamian precedent. About a thousand years earlier, Sargon of Akkad (the first Mesopotamian emperor, Mesopotamia Ch 5) had installed his own daughter **Enheduanna** as high priestess at the temple of the moon god at Ur — not as a religious offering but as a political control mechanism, placing a trusted family member inside the ritual machinery of a conquered Sumerian city. The Egyptian *kap* system was more systematic, operated on more subject elites at once, and ran for more generations than Sargon's one-daughter experiment, but the underlying idea — using close education and personal relationships to bind subject elites to the imperial center — was not invented in New Kingdom Egypt. What New Kingdom Egypt did was operationalize it on a provincial scale. Later ancient empires would eventually rediscover the approach: the Persians ran something similar for the sons of their satraps (Persian Empire Ch 2), the Romans educated provincial aristocrats in their own household schools, the British built their colonial administration in India on exactly this model.

And in Nubia, the result was a generation of Nubian nobles who were fluent in Egyptian, worshipped Amun, wrote in hieroglyphic, dressed in Egyptian linen, decorated their tombs with Egyptian funerary iconography, took Egyptian personal names alongside their Nubian ones, and — at the same time — retained enough of their Nubian identity to be recognized as Nubians by their own people. They were bridge figures, fluent in both cultures, governing the province on behalf of the pharaoh while maintaining relationships with the local population that no purely Egyptian administrator could have matched.

---

The best-documented individual example of this Egyptianized Nubian elite is a man named **Hekanefer** (HEH-kah-ne-fer — the name is actually Egyptian, and we do not know what his Nubian birth name was, if he had one; we know him only by the name he used in his Egyptian-language tomb inscriptions).

Hekanefer was the **Prince of Miam** (the Egyptian name for Aniba in Lower Nubia) during the reign of **Tutankhamun** (TOOT-ankh-AH-mun, the famous boy pharaoh who ruled around 1340 BCE and whose tomb, discovered intact by Howard Carter in 1922, is one of the most famous archaeological finds of all time). As Prince of Miam, Hekanefer was the local Nubian ruler of the region around Aniba, operating under the authority of the Viceroy of Kush and, ultimately, the pharaoh. He was a Nubian by birth, educated in Egypt, returned home to rule, and thoroughly Egyptianized in his public presentation.

We know about Hekanefer because his tomb at Toshka (in Lower Nubia, a few miles north of Abu Simbel) has survived, and because he also appears in a famous painted scene in the Theban tomb of a man named Huy, who was the Viceroy of Kush under Tutankhamun. In the Huy tomb scene, which is one of the most vivid surviving depictions of Egyptian–Nubian ceremonial interaction, a procession of Nubian princes is shown presenting tribute to the pharaoh. The princes are dressed in long robes, leopard skins draped over one shoulder, with distinctive Nubian hair styles and ornaments. They carry gold rings, ivory tusks, ebony logs, exotic animals (including giraffes, which is a particularly specific and probably accurate detail — giraffes were and still are a Nubian rather than Egyptian animal), and jars of valuable trade goods. One of the princes in the procession is labeled "Hekanefer, Prince of Miam."

Here is the interesting part. In Huy's tomb, Hekanefer is depicted in full Nubian dress — the leopard skin, the Nubian hairstyle, the tribute-bearer's posture. He is visibly Nubian. He looks Nubian. He is identified as Nubian. This is how the Egyptian viewer of Huy's tomb painting was meant to see him.

In Hekanefer's *own* tomb, a few miles away in Lower Nubia, Hekanefer is depicted entirely differently. He wears Egyptian aristocratic clothes. His name is written in standard Egyptian hieroglyphs. The religious iconography is Egyptian — the gods he worships are Egyptian gods, the prayers on his walls are standard Egyptian funerary prayers, and the afterlife journey shown on his walls is the standard Egyptian one: the dead person travels through a series of underworld obstacles to reach a final courtroom presided over by **Osiris** (the green-skinned god of the dead and of the afterlife, traditionally the brother-husband of Isis), where the dead person's heart is weighed on a set of scales against a feather representing truth and cosmic order. If the heart balances the feather, the dead person is admitted to the **Field of Iaru** (EE-ah-roo, sometimes spelled Aaru), a paradise version of the Nile Delta imagined as an eternal reed field where the blessed dead farm and feast forever. If the heart is heavier than the feather — meaning the person's deeds on earth have been too wicked — a waiting monster called Ammit devours them and they cease to exist. This package of images (weighing of the heart, Osiris's courtroom, Field of Iaru) is the core Egyptian afterlife theology of the New Kingdom, and Hekanefer's tomb contains the full version of it. There is almost nothing visibly "Nubian" about Hekanefer's own tomb. He presents himself, to posterity and to the Egyptian gods, as a fully Egyptian nobleman.

This double presentation tells us everything we need to know about the cultural situation of Nubian elites during the New Kingdom. Hekanefer was simultaneously a Nubian prince (in the context of Egyptian court ritual, where his Nubianness was part of the symbolic theater) and a fully Egyptian nobleman (in the context of his own religious preparations for the afterlife, where being Egyptian was the only acceptable identity for someone who expected to reach the fields of Iaru in the west). He was not just "Egyptianized." He was *performatively bicultural*, moving between identities according to context, code-switching in a way that would be familiar to a modern bicultural immigrant elite. He lived in two worlds at once, and the archaeological record preserves both of them.

Hekanefer is one specific case, but the pattern is visible at many Nubian elite burial sites during the New Kingdom. Nubian tomb owners at Aniba, Toshka, Debeira, Mirgissa, and other sites present themselves as Egyptian in their own tombs while retaining Nubian identity markers in ceremonial contexts. The pattern ran deep enough that some Egyptologists now talk about a "third space," neither fully Nubian nor fully Egyptian, in which Nubian elites operated during the New Kingdom centuries, using Egyptian religious and administrative vocabulary to manage their own social worlds.

---

The political importance of this bicultural elite would not become clear until the New Kingdom ended.

And the New Kingdom ended, along with a lot of other things, in one of the most consequential bad centuries in Bronze Age history. Between roughly 1200 and 1150 BCE, the entire eastern Mediterranean went through a crisis called the **Late Bronze Age Collapse**. Scholars have been arguing about its causes for a century and the best current picture involves several compounding stresses at once: prolonged drought across much of the region, crop failures and famine, migrating populations that have come to be called "Sea Peoples" in the Egyptian sources, breakdown of the international diplomatic network that had been holding the great kingdoms together (the Amarna Letters system from Chapter 7), internal rebellions, the collapse of centrally managed palace economies, and a cascading loss of the specialized knowledge that kept Bronze Age states running. The Hittite empire of Anatolia disappeared. The Mycenaean palaces of Greece were burned. Ugarit, the great Syrian trading city, was destroyed. Egypt itself was invaded by the Sea Peoples in two major battles under Ramesses III (the great-grandson of Ramesses II) and survived but was badly weakened. Mesopotamia went through its own version of the same collapse — the Kassite dynasty of Babylon fell, the Aramean tribes overran Babylonia, and the Middle Assyrian state contracted hard (Mesopotamia Ch 9).

Egypt, weakened rather than destroyed, entered what historians now call the **Third Intermediate Period** — the third of the three periodic breakdowns in pharaonic central authority that we met back in Chapter 2, and the worst of them. Central government faltered. Regional power bases in the delta and at Thebes pulled in different directions. The priesthoods of Amun at Karnak and of other major temples grew politically powerful at the expense of the royal court. Royal legitimacy became a matter of who could hold a particular city rather than who sat on the throne at Memphis or Thebes.

During this long slow collapse, Egypt's grip on Nubia loosened and then released. The Viceroy of Kush, whose office had been the linchpin of Egyptian administration in the province, became increasingly disconnected from the royal court. Tribute shipments shrank. Communication with Thebes became irregular. Egyptian garrisons in Nubia went unreinforced, unpaid, and eventually unmanned. The gold mines stopped producing on the old industrial scale. The temples at Jebel Barkal and Soleb and Amada kept functioning, but their priests began to operate more independently, answering to local patronage networks rather than to a distant Theban court.

Around 1070 BCE, Egypt formally withdrew from Nubia, or rather stopped pretending to govern a province it no longer had the resources to administer. The last Viceroy of Kush was a man named **Panehsy** (PAH-neh-see). His name is itself a small piece of evidence about the era: in Egyptian, *Panehsy* simply means "the Nubian," which may suggest that by this point in the New Kingdom's decline, even the office of Viceroy had passed into the hands of a person of Nubian origin. That would be a final irony of the colonial administration — an Egyptianized Nubian governing Egypt's Nubian province as the Egyptian state itself was falling apart. Panehsy ended his career as a warlord in southern Egypt during the civil disorders of the late New Kingdom, rather than as a functioning provincial administrator.

When Egypt withdrew, it left Nubia to the Nubians.

Specifically, it left Nubia to the Egyptianized Nubian elites — the descendants of Hekanefer and his peers, people who had been trained to think in Egyptian categories, who worshipped Egyptian gods, who knew how to write Egyptian hieroglyphs, who had been running Egyptian-style administrations in the Nubian provinces for a century or more, and who now found themselves in charge of a landscape with no distant pharaoh to report to. These people did not forget they were Nubians. They also did not abandon the Egyptian cultural apparatus they had been raised with. They kept the temples running, they kept worshipping Amun, they kept using the Egyptian language for administrative and religious purposes, and they started — quietly, over generations, without anyone in contemporary Egypt noticing — to build a new Nubian kingdom on the foundations of the old Egyptian province.

The new kingdom centered itself at **Napata** (NAH-pah-tah — the region around Jebel Barkal), because Jebel Barkal was the spiritual anchor of the Amun cult and because it was far enough upstream to be safe from any future Egyptian interference. The kingdom was called, in its own later documents, the **Kingdom of Kush** (Kingdom of Kush Ch 1). It is the direct political and cultural successor to the Egyptian province of Nubia, built by the bicultural Nubian elite who had inherited Egyptian institutions and made them their own. And about three centuries after Egypt's withdrawal, the Kushite kings of Napata would do something that nobody in 1070 BCE would have predicted. They would march north, cross the First Cataract, enter Egypt, and conquer it. For about a hundred years during the eighth and seventh centuries BCE, the pharaohs of Egypt would be Nubian — members of the Twenty-fifth Dynasty (Kingdom of Kush Ch 2-4), worshippers of Amun at Jebel Barkal, descendants of the Egyptianized elites who had learned to run a province under Tutankhamun's Viceroy and had then, after the Egyptian state disappeared out from under them, kept running it on their own terms.

But that story belongs to the next book in this chain (Kingdom of Kush Ch 2-4).

Let's close this one by looking back at the whole arc.

We started in Chapter 1 with the A-Group culture, a pre-urban Nubian tradition that was trading with predynastic Egypt when the first pharaohs were still just the chiefs of small delta kingdoms. We watched the A-Group build burials rich enough to produce the Qustul kingship controversy, and then we watched the whole culture disappear under early dynastic Egyptian pressure in Chapter 2's four-century silence. We watched the C-Group re-settle Lower Nubia and quietly maintain a cattle-centered tradition that would survive a thousand years of Egyptian contact without being erased. We followed Harkhuf's donkey caravans south to the ruler of Yam in Chapter 3, and watched the city of Kerma rise in the floodplain of Upper Nubia, becoming the first real urban center in Africa outside Egypt itself, and the first city in the history of the continent's interior that can legitimately be called a capital. We watched Middle Kingdom Egypt, terrified of what Kerma was becoming, build thirteen fortresses along the Second Cataract and declare a formal border that was meant to last forever. We watched Classic Kerma reach its peak in Chapter 5, burying its kings with corridors of hundreds of retainers, producing the finest pottery in the Bronze Age world, and operating a diplomatic reach that extended into Hyksos-controlled Egypt and down into the incense trade of the Red Sea. We watched, in Chapter 6, the Kerma–Hyksos alliance squeeze Theban Egypt between two hostile powers, and we watched the Theban pharaohs — first Kamose, then Ahmose, finally Thutmose I — destroy Kerma in retaliation. We watched, in Chapter 7, New Kingdom Egypt absorb Nubia as a province under the Viceroy of Kush, exploit its gold mines on an industrial scale, and build some of the most impressive temples in the ancient world at Jebel Barkal and Soleb and Abu Simbel. And we watched, in this final chapter, an Egyptianized Nubian elite emerge out of the province's administrative nursery — bicultural, loyal, fluent in Egyptian categories but retaining Nubian identity — and inherit the whole apparatus when Egyptian state power withdrew during the Bronze Age collapse.

The story that runs through all eight chapters is not a story of one civilization defeating another. It is a story of two civilizations in prolonged contact, exchanging people and goods and ideas and soldiers and gods across a border that neither of them could fully close, for almost three thousand years. Nubia influenced Egypt at least as much as Egypt influenced Nubia — the Egyptian pantheon absorbed Nubian deities, Egyptian armies depended on Nubian mercenaries, Egyptian economic power was built on Nubian gold, and by the end of the New Kingdom, Egypt itself was about to be governed by Egyptianized Nubians who had learned the job too well. The conventional picture of a dominant Egypt ruling a subordinate Nubia is half the story. The other half is that Nubia, quietly and persistently, was always in the room, always holding a bow, always sending its children to the schoolroom and its gold to the treasury, and always waiting for its moment.

The moment would come. When it did, the Nubian pharaohs would take the throne of the country that had once tried to close its border against them, and they would claim to have been the legitimate heirs of Amun all along. And in one particular sense, they were. Five centuries after Egypt withdrew, the most devout worshippers of the Egyptian god Amun on the planet were not in Thebes or at Karnak. They were at Jebel Barkal, in the mountains of Upper Nubia, praying toward the sacred pillar that Thutmose III had first recognized as a uraeus fifteen hundred years before. The priesthood of Amun, the ritual calendars of the temples, the theological language of divine kingship — all of it had migrated south during the Third Intermediate Period, taken root in Napata, and been carried forward by the Kushite rulers as their own inheritance. When they marched north to take the pharaoh's throne, they were not foreigners claiming to be Egyptians. They were Amun's own people coming home.

That is the end of Ancient Nubia. The next book in this chain picks up with the Kingdom of Kush (Kingdom of Kush Ch 1-8) and the long, strange, magnificent story of what happened next.

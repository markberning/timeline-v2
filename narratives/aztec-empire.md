# Chapter 1 — Aztlan and the Eagle on the Cactus

The most powerful empire the Western Hemisphere had ever seen began as a band of refugees that nobody wanted.

In 1519 CE, when Spanish conquistadors first crested the pass between the volcanoes Popocatépetl and Iztaccíhuatl and looked down into the **Basin of Mexico** (the high-altitude bowl, ringed by mountains, that holds modern Mexico City), they thought they were dreaming. Below them, on a chain of islands in a vast salt-and-freshwater lake, stood a city of stone causeways, whitewashed pyramids, and floating gardens, with a population somewhere between 150,000 and 250,000. It was larger than any city in Spain, larger than Rome, larger than Paris, larger than London. The chronicler **Bernal Díaz del Castillo** (a foot-soldier in Cortés's army who later wrote the most readable eyewitness account of the conquest, finishing his book in his eighties in Guatemala) wrote that some of the soldiers asked whether what they were seeing was real, or a vision out of the romance novels they had read as boys.

It was real. It was called **Tenochtitlan** (teh-nohch-teet-LAHN, "the place of the prickly pear cactus"). And it was, by any measure, one of the great cities the world has ever produced.

Two hundred years earlier, the people who built it had been so poor and so despised that other tribes drove them off their land for fun. They were not natives of central Mexico. They were latecomers, immigrants, a people on the run, stuck doing the worst jobs for the worst pay, the kind of group that other settled peoples treated as a problem rather than a partner. How they got from there to running the largest empire in the history of the pre-Columbian Americas is one of the most remarkable rags-to-riches stories on record. It also took less time than the Roman rise from city to empire by a margin of several centuries.

Their name, the one they used for themselves, was **Mexica** (meh-SHEE-kah). The label "Aztec" comes from a different word, **Aztlan** (AHST-lahn), the legendary homeland they claimed to have left, and it was applied to them mainly by 19th-century historians. The Mexica themselves rarely used it. We use it because the empire they built drew in many peoples beyond the Mexica proper, and "Aztec Empire" has become the standard name in English. Where this narrative needs to talk specifically about the people of Tenochtitlan, it will say "Mexica." Where it needs to talk about the imperial system that included their allies and the dozens of tributary peoples, it will say "Aztec." That distinction matters more than it sounds.

A note on what we know and don't know. The Aztec Empire is unusual in this series because it ends in 1521, on the cusp of the modern era, and because two parallel literate traditions (Aztec painted books and Spanish narrative writing) overlap for the final century. We have far more material than we have for any earlier American civilization. We also have a particular kind of distortion: the Mexica themselves rewrote their own past more than once, the Spanish wrote about them with a mix of admiration, contempt, and propaganda, and the most influential single source, the **Florentine Codex** (a twelve-volume encyclopedia of Aztec culture compiled by the Franciscan friar **Bernardino de Sahagún**, sah-ah-GOON, between roughly 1540 and 1577 from interviews with Aztec elders), was assembled fifty years after the conquest by a Spaniard whose Aztec informants had every reason to color what they remembered. "First" claims and confident dates in this narrative carry the same caveat that applies to every TL in this series: we are looking at the past through whatever lens survived, and the lens has fingerprints all over it.

---

The Mexica's origin story begins with a place called **Aztlan**, "the place of whiteness," somewhere to the north of the Basin of Mexico. Aztlan was an island in a lake, with seven caves at its center where the seven Nahua-speaking peoples (the Mexica and their linguistic cousins) emerged. Or possibly Aztlan never existed and the whole thing is a charter myth invented later to give the Mexica a homeland to be proud of. Modern archaeologists are split. Some place it in the modern Mexican state of Nayarit. Others place it in the American Southwest. Most accept that the Mexica did migrate from the north, but the literal Aztlan with the seven caves is probably mythology dressed up as geography.

What is reasonably solid is the language family. The Mexica spoke **Nahuatl** (NAH-watl), a language belonging to the **Uto-Aztecan** family, which also includes Hopi, Comanche, Shoshone, and the languages of the Tarahumara of northern Mexico. Uto-Aztecan is a different family from the Mayan languages of the southeast (Maya Civilization Ch 1) and from the Oto-Manguean languages of the Zapotec and Mixtec to the south (Zapotec Civilization Ch 1). The distance between Nahuatl and Yucatec Maya is comparable to the distance between English and Persian: same continent of language families in the abstract, but mutually unintelligible and culturally distinct. The Mexica arriving in central Mexico were not coming home to people who already spoke their language. They were strangers.

Around 1100 CE, give or take a century, the Mexica left Aztlan, prompted (or so they later said) by their patron god **Huitzilopochtli** (wee-tsee-loh-POACH-tlee, "Hummingbird of the South" or "Hummingbird on the Left," a war god, in this period a relatively narrow tribal patron rather than a major cosmic figure — Chapter 3 covers the later promotion that turned him into the sun itself). Huitzilopochtli was carried in a portable shrine on the back of one of his priests, and he gave directions: keep going south, keep going south, and one day I will give you a sign. The migration took over two centuries. They stopped at place after place. They were thrown out of place after place. They walked, they fought, they ate what they could.

The setting they were walking into was the wreckage of a much older civilization. The Basin of Mexico had been the heart of a great regional system since at least 100 BCE, when the city of **Teotihuacan** (teh-oh-tee-WAH-kahn) had risen to become the largest city in the Western Hemisphere, with a population of 125,000 to 200,000 (Teotihuacan Ch 1). Teotihuacan had collapsed in flames around 550 CE, and in the centuries afterward, a confusing succession of smaller polities had risen and fallen. The most prestigious of these was the city of **Tula**, the capital of the **Toltecs** (TOLE-teks), a Nahuatl-speaking people who had dominated central Mexico from roughly 900 to 1150 CE. By the time the Mexica arrived, Tula too had been destroyed and abandoned. But the memory of Toltec rule was the gold standard of legitimacy in the Basin of Mexico. Anyone claiming to rule needed Toltec descent. The Mexica, who had no such descent, would have to invent it later.

When the Mexica wandered into the Basin of Mexico, sometime in the late 13th century, the basin floor was already crowded. Dozens of city-states (each called an **altepetl**, ahl-TEH-petl, "water-mountain," the basic Mesoamerican political unit, a city plus its territory) competed for land, water, and labor. The major players included Culhuacan, Azcapotzalco, Texcoco, Xochimilco, Coyoacan, Tlacopan, and many others, none of them friendly to newcomers. The Mexica showed up uninvited, and the existing residents made it clear they were unwelcome.

---

Around 1248, the Mexica settled briefly at **Chapultepec** (chah-POOL-teh-pek, "grasshopper hill"), a wooded hill on the western shore of Lake Texcoco that had reliable freshwater springs. Chapultepec was a good location, which is exactly why the existing inhabitants of the basin objected to having Mexica squatters there. A coalition of nearby city-states attacked, drove the Mexica out, and killed many of their leaders. The survivors fled south.

Their refuge was the city-state of **Culhuacan** (kool-WAH-kahn), an old altepetl on the southern shore of the lake whose ruling family claimed direct descent from the Toltecs. The Culhua took the Mexica in, but the hospitality was barbed. They settled the refugees on **Tizapan** (tee-ZAH-pahn), a stretch of stony, snake-infested ground that nobody wanted, on the assumption the snakes would solve the Mexica problem. The Mexica, however, ate the snakes. Within a generation they were thriving, intermarrying with their hosts, and serving as Culhua mercenaries in the constant low-grade warfare that filled the basin's politics.

This period, the **Mexica vassalage at Culhuacan**, mattered for two reasons. First, intermarriage with Culhua nobles gave the Mexica a thin but real claim to Toltec blood, which they would lean on heavily for centuries afterward. Second, it ended in disaster. The Mexica, asked by the Culhua king **Cocoxtli** to provide a daughter for what the Mexica understood as a marriage alliance, instead sacrificed and flayed her in honor of a goddess. The detail varies in the sources (one tradition says the priests believed she was meant to become a deity; another says they were trying to flatter Cocoxtli with the highest religious honor they could imagine), but the outcome was the same. Cocoxtli's father attended the ceremony, saw a Mexica priest dancing in his daughter's skin, and called for blood. The Mexica fled.

They had nowhere to go. To the west and north, the Tepanec confederation (the dominant power in the basin at the time) was hostile. To the east, Texcoco was an ally of the Tepanec. To the south, Culhuacan now wanted them dead. The only direction left was into the lake itself.

In 1325 (the date is conventional, drawn from later Mexica chronicles, and might be off by a few years either direction), Mexica priests sighted the sign Huitzilopochtli had promised them more than two centuries earlier. On a small, rocky island in the marshy western lobe of Lake Texcoco, a giant **eagle** perched on a **prickly pear cactus**, devouring a snake. (The image survives today on the Mexican flag; the cactus and eagle are the national emblem of Mexico, and the lineage from Mexica founding myth to modern coat of arms is direct.) This, the priests announced, was the place. The wandering was over.

The site was, by every objective measure, terrible. It was a mosquito-ridden marsh in the middle of a salty lake. There was no farmland. There was no fresh water (Lake Texcoco was brackish in its central portions, drinkable only at the freshwater inflows around the edges). There was no stone for building. There was no timber. The only assets were defensive: the surrounding water made the island hard to attack, and the existing powers in the basin had not bothered to claim it because nobody else thought it was worth claiming.

That last point was decisive. The island fell within the formal territory of Azcapotzalco, the Tepanec capital. The Mexica negotiated permission to settle there in exchange for tribute and military service. They became, formally, vassals of the Tepanec king **Tezozomoc** (teh-zoh-ZOH-mok), an aging warlord who would dominate the Basin of Mexico for the next half-century. The Mexica agreed because they had no other option. They named their new home Tenochtitlan, "the place of the prickly pear cactus." A short time later, a faction split off and founded a sister city, **Tlatelolco** (tlah-teh-LOL-ko), on a neighboring island just to the north. Tlatelolco would later become the empire's greatest marketplace; in 1325, it was just another mud island.

The Mexica started building. They drove wooden piles into the lake bed, piled mud and reeds on top to extend the available land, and laid out the first version of a plan they would refine for the next two centuries. They built a small temple on the highest point of the island, a shrine to Huitzilopochtli that would, six rebuildings later, become the **Templo Mayor** (TEM-plo MAH-yor, "Greater Temple," the twin-pyramid that anchored the city). The first version was a modest wooden hut. By 1521, the seventh version would be 200 feet (60 meters) on a side at its base and 150 feet (45 meters) high, the tallest structure in the city.

---

Cross-civ check: 1325, the conventional founding date of Tenochtitlan, sits in the middle of a remarkable century. In England, the Hundred Years' War with France was about to begin (1337). In China, the Mongol Yuan dynasty was on its last legs (Ming would replace it in 1368). In West Africa, the Mali Empire was at its height under Mansa Musa, who had just made his famous gold-distributing pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324. In the Andes, the Inca were still a small mountain kingdom around Cuzco, just beginning their own expansion (Andean Kingdoms Ch 8). The Maya in the Yucatán were in their **Postclassic** period, with Mayapan as the dominant city after the collapse of the great Classic-era centers (Maya Civilization Ch 7). The Aztecs and the Inca, the two American empires the Spanish would conquer in the 1520s and 1530s, were both founding their capitals within the same generation. They never knew the other existed.

The Mexica in 1325 were a small client kingdom on a salty island, paying tribute to a Tepanec king they hated, building reed huts on mud they had piled up themselves, and worshipping a hummingbird-shaped war god who had told them they would one day rule the world. Almost no one in the Basin of Mexico would have bet on them. Within a hundred years, almost no one in the Basin of Mexico would dare bet against them.

# Chapter 2 — From Vassal to Hegemon

The Mexica spent their first century in Tenochtitlan as someone else's hired help.

For a hundred and three years, from 1325 to 1428, they paid tribute to the Tepanec capital of **Azcapotzalco** (ahs-kah-poh-TSAHL-ko, "place of the anthills," on the western shore of Lake Texcoco) and fought as Tepanec mercenaries in the wars that the aging tyrant **Tezozomoc** waged against his neighbors. They were good at it. The Mexica had no farmland of their own and no traditional craft industry (the chinampa system that would later feed the city was not yet built out at scale, and Tlatelolco's market was still developing), so military service was the main thing they had to sell. They sold it well, and they got paid in territory: small grants of land in conquered towns where Mexica warriors could collect their own tribute, separate from what the Tepanec king collected. By the early 15th century, individual Mexica nobles had become wealthy on these grants, and Tenochtitlan itself had grown from a few thousand people to perhaps 30,000.

But mercenaries who get too rich start to look like rivals. And the Mexica, for all their loyal service, were never going to be Tepanec. They had their own god, their own dynasty, their own ambitions. The longer they served, the more dangerous they became. Tezozomoc, no fool, kept them dependent through a system of close marriage alliances that worked, until his death, and then catastrophically failed.

Before that crisis broke, the Mexica needed kings, and they got them. In 1376, the city's elders selected **Acamapichtli** (ah-kah-mah-PEACH-tlee, "handful of reeds"), a Mexica noble whose mother was a Mexica princess and whose father was Culhua. The half-Culhua descent was not optional. The Mexica needed an external claim to legitimacy, and Culhua blood meant Toltec blood, which meant a credible claim to descend from the prestigious civilization (Chapter 1) that all Nahuatl-speaking peoples used as the gold standard of royal pedigree. Acamapichtli became the first **tlatoani** (tlah-toh-AH-nee, "speaker," literally "he who speaks well," the title of the Mexica king). The plural is *tlatoque*, and within a generation, the title would be applied to the rulers of every altepetl in the basin. For now, Acamapichtli was the first of his line.

Acamapichtli's reign (1376–1395) was unspectacular by design. He consolidated the Mexica royal household, fathered children by women of multiple Mexica calpulli (Chapter 6) to bind the city's clan factions to the throne, served Tezozomoc loyally in Tepanec wars, and oversaw the second major construction phase of the Templo Mayor. When he died, the elders elected his son **Huitzilihuitl** (wee-tsee-LEE-witl, "Hummingbird Feather," not to be confused with the god Huitzilopochtli — the names share the hummingbird element but the deity is "Hummingbird of the South" while the king is "Hummingbird Feather"; Mesoamerican naming conventions used a small set of bird and animal motifs in many recombinations). Huitzilihuitl reigned from 1396 to 1417 and made the most important political move of the early dynasty: he married a daughter of Tezozomoc, formally embedding the Mexica royal line into the Tepanec ruling family. This was the same political logic that had guided Acamapichtli's descent from the Culhua, applied to the current dominant power. Huitzilihuitl's son by this marriage, **Chimalpopoca** (chee-mahl-poh-POH-kah, "Smoking Shield"), would inherit the throne and, by blood, would be Tezozomoc's grandson.

That blood was supposed to protect the Mexica. Instead, it killed Chimalpopoca.

---

In 1426, Tezozomoc finally died, after ruling Azcapotzalco for over fifty years. Tepanec power had been built around him personally, and his death triggered the kind of succession crisis that authoritarian states with no clear inheritance rules tend to produce. The throne should have gone to his designated heir, but a younger son named **Maxtla** (MAHKS-tlah) seized it instead, killing his brother in the process. Maxtla then turned on every relative he distrusted, and the half-Mexica grandson of Tezozomoc, sitting on the throne of Tenochtitlan, was high on the list.

In 1427, Chimalpopoca was assassinated. The sources disagree on the details (some say Maxtla's agents had him killed in his palace; others say he was captured, imprisoned, and committed suicide in detention). What is clear is that the assassination left Tenochtitlan without a king, surrounded by enemies, and one strong push away from being wiped out altogether.

The Mexica elders met in emergency session and chose **Itzcoatl** (eets-KOH-ahtl, "Obsidian Serpent"), the son of Acamapichtli by a slave woman, an older man with a reputation as a war leader and a stubborn pragmatist. Itzcoatl was not a member of the legitimate royal line by the standards of his time (the slave-mother lineage was a serious mark against him). The elders chose him anyway because he was the only candidate willing to fight. The legitimate heirs argued for surrender to Maxtla; Itzcoatl argued for war. The Mexica went to war.

They were not going to win it alone. Tenochtitlan's army, even by generous estimates, numbered at most a few thousand fighting men. Azcapotzalco could put ten or fifteen times that number in the field. What Itzcoatl had was a network of grievances. Maxtla had alienated his own family, attacked his neighbors indiscriminately, and made enemies of the surviving heirs of the Tepanec confederation. Most importantly, he had attacked **Texcoco** (tesh-KO-ko, the major altepetl on the eastern shore of Lake Texcoco) and driven its young king-in-exile into hiding. That king's name was **Nezahualcoyotl** (neh-zah-wahl-KOH-yotl, "Fasting Coyote"), and he was about to become the most famous individual in Aztec history, depending on which Aztec city you asked.

---

Nezahualcoyotl was a remarkable person, the kind who shows up once in a generation in any culture, and the only individual in this entire narrative who could plausibly be called a philosopher-king. He was born in 1402, the son of Texcoco's ruler **Ixtlilxochitl I**, who had been killed by Tezozomoc's forces when Nezahualcoyotl was a teenager. Hidden in the mountains by loyal nobles, Nezahualcoyotl spent his early adulthood as a fugitive, hunted by Maxtla's assassins, moving from refuge to refuge while plotting his return. By 1428, he had built a coalition.

Itzcoatl, recognizing an obvious ally, made the deal. In 1428, Tenochtitlan, Texcoco (under Nezahualcoyotl), and the smaller Tepanec city of **Tlacopan** (tlah-KOH-pahn, on the lake's western shore) formed the **Triple Alliance**, the pact that would become the political core of the Aztec Empire for the next ninety years. The terms were carefully calibrated. Tenochtitlan and Texcoco would each receive two-fifths of all conquered tribute, and Tlacopan (the junior partner, included to give the alliance a foothold on the western shore and to break up what remained of Tepanec power) would receive one-fifth. The 2:2:1 ratio was the central political arithmetic of the empire, and it held, with some erosion in Tlacopan's share, until the Spanish arrived.

Why did the alliance work? The mechanism was simple but effective. Each capital ran its own internal affairs, kept its own king, and maintained its own military traditions. They jointly agreed on which towns to conquer, jointly campaigned, and divided the spoils by formula. There was no central capital and no common citizenship. A Texcocan was not a subject of Tenochtitlan, and vice versa; both were members of an alliance that had agreed to exploit everyone else. Crucially, the three capitals were close enough geographically (all within sight of each other across the lake) to coordinate effectively, but distant enough to maintain separate identities. The arrangement avoided the trap that has destroyed many federations: nobody had to accept being absorbed into anybody else's system, and the predictable payoff of joint conquest gave everyone a continuing reason to cooperate.

The alliance's first target was Maxtla. The combined armies besieged Azcapotzalco for 114 days in 1428, sacked the city, and captured the tyrant himself. **Maxtla was sacrificed at the founding ceremony of the Triple Alliance**, his heart cut from his chest with an obsidian blade and burned as an offering to Huitzilopochtli, in front of the assembled armies of the three allied cities. The Tepanec confederation collapsed. Azcapotzalco was demoted to a tributary. The market that had been Mesoamerica's premier slave market for a century was relocated. And the Mexica, in the space of a single year, transformed themselves from a vassal city-state into one of three co-ruling capitals of an emerging empire.

---

Itzcoatl spent the next twelve years (1428–1440) doing the unglamorous work of consolidation. With Texcocan allies on his eastern flank and Tlacopan blocking the western approach, he conquered the freshwater-lake cities to the south of Tenochtitlan one after another: **Xochimilco** (sho-chee-MEEL-ko, "field of flowers," famous for its chinampas, Chapter 4), **Cuitlahuac**, **Mizquic**, and **Coyoacan**. These conquests gave the Mexica direct control over the most fertile chinampa zones in the basin, which solved the food-supply problem that had haunted Tenochtitlan since its founding. The southern lakes were freshwater, perfect for chinampa agriculture, and the conquered towns now owed tribute payable in maize, beans, and squash. For the first time in their history, the Mexica did not need to import every grain of corn they ate.

Itzcoatl also did something stranger and more telling. Around 1430, he ordered the **burning of the codices** (a *codex* in Mesoamerican usage was a long folded screen of bark paper or deer hide, painted with pictographs and phonetic glyphs, used for histories, ritual calendars, tribute lists, and divination — the closest thing the Aztecs had to a book): the destruction of older painted books that recorded Mexica history. The justification, recorded later by Aztec elders, was that the existing histories were inaccurate, that they portrayed the Mexica as the impoverished migrants and mercenaries they had been, and that "it is not fitting that our people know these pictures. Our people, our subjects, will be lost and our land destroyed, for these lies are many." The codices were burned. New histories were commissioned to replace them. The new histories backdated Mexica greatness, claimed Toltec ancestry more directly, recast Huitzilopochtli as a major cosmic deity rather than a minor tribal spirit, and generally constructed the propaganda that would sustain Mexica imperial identity for the next ninety years.

This is one of the more important political acts in the empire's history, and it deserves to be flagged for what it was. Itzcoatl's regime did not just write its own version of history. It deliberately destroyed the older versions to prevent comparison. The Mexica we know about, the empire of cosmic destiny and Toltec ancestry and divinely sanctioned conquest, is in important ways a creation of the post-1430 propaganda program. Where the older histories said "we were poor wanderers nobody wanted," the new histories said "we were always destined to rule." Where the older histories said "Huitzilopochtli was a tribal patron god," the new histories said "Huitzilopochtli is the sun, and the sun requires our service." The man behind much of this rewriting was Itzcoatl's nephew and chief advisor, who held an office Itzcoatl had created specifically for him: the **cihuacoatl** (see-wah-KOH-ahtl, "Snake Woman," a title not a gender; he was male, the office name referred to a goddess associated with childbirth and the earth, framing the cihuacoatl as the feminine domestic complement to the warrior-king). His name was **Tlacaelel** (tlah-kah-EH-lel), and he would hold the office for nearly sixty years, outlasting four tlatoani. He is the closest thing to a Cardinal Richelieu the Mexica produced, and probably the single individual who did more than any other to define what the Aztec Empire ultimately became.

When Itzcoatl died in 1440, he left a Mexica state that was unrecognizable from what he had inherited thirteen years earlier. From a vassal of the Tepanec, paying tribute and providing mercenaries, the Mexica had become co-rulers of the most powerful alliance in the Basin of Mexico. From a city of perhaps 30,000 people on a salt-marsh island, Tenochtitlan had grown into an emerging metropolis fed by chinampa tribute from a dozen subject towns. From a tribal religion, Huitzilopochtli had been promoted to a cosmic role that justified continuous warfare. The empire was not yet built. But the engine that would build it was running.

# Chapter 3 — Tlacaelel and the Engine of Conquest

The man who made the Aztec Empire what it was never sat on the throne.

**Tlacaelel** (tlah-kah-EH-lel, "Sad-Hearted One" or "Manly Heart," depending on the etymology you trust) was the half-brother and chief advisor of Motecuzoma I, the nephew of Itzcoatl, the cousin of Acamapichtli's other descendants, and through marriage and family politics he was related, somehow or other, to nearly every important Mexica noble of his generation. He held the title of **cihuacoatl**, the vice-royalty Itzcoatl had invented for him in 1428, and he held it from then until his death around 1487 — fifty-nine years, across four reigns. He directed the empire's bureaucracy, managed the judicial system (Chapter 6), commanded the army when the tlatoani was elsewhere, and (the role that history remembers most) reformed the state religion so thoroughly that the Aztec Empire of 1500 was, in important ways, a religious revolution that he had personally engineered.

He was offered the throne three times, after Itzcoatl's death in 1440 and again in 1469 and 1481. Three times he refused. The Aztec sources, all written long after his death, treat this as a sign of supernatural humility. The more cynical reading is that he had figured out that being kingmaker was a more durable position than being king. Kings die in battle, get assassinated by rivals, get poisoned by their advisors; advisors who outlast four kings tend to keep their heads. Tlacaelel kept his.

His religious reforms, which he began under Itzcoatl and pushed through fully under **Motecuzoma I** (moh-tay-koo-ZOH-mah, "He Who Frowns Like a Lord," sometimes spelled Moctezuma or Montezuma — the original Nahuatl was *Motēuczōma*; "Motecuzoma" is the closest English approximation, and we'll use it throughout for both Motecuzoma I and Motecuzoma II), reframed the basic relationship between the Mexica and the cosmos. Before Tlacaelel, Huitzilopochtli was a relatively narrow tribal deity: the patron god of the Mexica specifically, a war god, a guide who had brought them out of Aztlan and led them to the eagle-on-cactus prophecy. After Tlacaelel, Huitzilopochtli was the sun. He was the deity whose daily rising required cosmic maintenance, and the maintenance required human blood. Specifically, it required the blood of brave warriors, which meant the Mexica needed to fight wars constantly, capture brave warriors constantly, and sacrifice them constantly, or the sun would not rise.

This was, in cosmological terms, an enormous promotion. A regional war god was now the literal motor of the universe. The promotion was paired with a creation story, the Five Suns mythology (Chapter 5 covers it in detail), in which the gods themselves had sacrificed their lives to set the current sun in motion. Aztec religion was not asking humans to invent a new obligation; it was asking them to honor an existing one, to repay a cosmic debt the gods had voluntarily contracted at Teotihuacan. The debt-and-payment framing was politically effective because it made sacrifice feel like an inheritance rather than an imposition. The Mexica were not killing strangers because they enjoyed it; they were keeping the universe running because someone had to.

The mechanism was beautifully constructed, in a horrible way. By making the central state god the sun itself, Tlacaelel made every act of imperial expansion a religious obligation. Refusing to wage war would be refusing to feed the sun, which would mean cosmic collapse. The sacrificial demand could only be met by capturing live warriors in battle (regular slave-trading and famine-driven self-sale of slaves provided plenty of bodies, but Huitzilopochtli specifically required warriors taken in honorable combat, so the regime had to keep fighting wars). Imperial expansion stopped being optional. It became a sacrament.

The reform package included more than the sun-god promotion. Tlacaelel oversaw the second great wave of codex-rewriting (after Itzcoatl's initial burning, Chapter 2), establishing the Mexica as the chosen people of the sun and backdating the sacrificial cosmology so that it appeared to have been Mexica practice from the beginning rather than a 15th-century innovation. He standardized the monthly festival cycle (Chapter 5), institutionalizing the eighteen public sacrificial events that turned the calendar into a year-round ritual machine. He formalized the **Flower Wars** (more on these below) as a permanent mechanism for replenishing the captive supply when conquest campaigns slowed. And he shaped the priesthood itself: the calmecac school system (Chapter 6) educated the noble boys who staffed the temples, and Tlacaelel's reforms tightened the link between high-priestly office and the state bureaucracy he ran.

Some scholars argue this is too clean a theory and that the real reforms were messier and slower than the Aztec sources make them sound. There is fair reason for the skepticism: most of what we know about Tlacaelel comes from histories written by his great-grandson, **Hernando de Alvarado Tezozomoc** (writing in the late 1500s, several generations after the events), who had every reason to make his ancestor look like an architect of empire. Other scholars argue the reforms are well-attested in multiple independent sources and that the sun-god framing of Huitzilopochtli is genuinely a 15th-century innovation. The current scholarly reading lands somewhere in the middle: Tlacaelel did substantially reshape Mexica religion, the sun-god promotion of Huitzilopochtli is real, but the reforms were more gradual than the legendary single-handed transformation the post-conquest sources describe. Either way, by 1500 the cosmology of imperial sacrifice was the Mexica state ideology, and Tlacaelel's name was attached to it.

---

The **Flower Wars** deserve a section to themselves, because they were where Tlacaelel's machine became most visible. Beginning around 1455 (during the worst drought of the century, the **One Rabbit famine**, when sacrificial demand was running ahead of the conquest pipeline), the Aztec state institutionalized a recurring, ritualized form of warfare against the city-states of Tlaxcala, Cholula, and Huejotzingo on the eastern side of Popocatépetl. The war was scheduled. The combatants knew each other. The objective was not territorial conquest but the capture of high-quality warriors for sacrifice, with both sides agreeing to constraints (set times, set locations, no mass civilian casualties) that limited but did not eliminate the violence. **xochiyaoyotl** (sho-chee-yah-OH-yotl, "flower-warfare") was the formal Nahuatl name; the "flower" referred to the captured warrior, who would "bloom" by giving his life on the altar.

What were they *for*? The conventional explanation is religious: ritual battles to feed the sun. Modern scholarship adds practical functions: they kept the army in fighting trim, they pressured the Tlaxcalans without the cost of a full conquest that the rugged terrain would have made expensive, and they served as the empire's primary military training program for young warriors. All three readings are probably true at once. What matters for understanding Tlacaelel's machine is the structural function: the Flower Wars guaranteed a steady supply of sacrificial victims even when conquest campaigns went quiet. The system never had to stop. The sun would always be fed. And in pulling Tlaxcala into a permanent state of low-grade war rather than absorbing it, the empire created the eighty-year reservoir of grievance that would, in 1519, hand Cortés his decisive ally (Chapter 8). Tlacaelel built the machine to last. He did not foresee what would happen when the machine met something it had never been designed to absorb.

---

The kings he served were, by and large, capable men. They had to be.

**Motecuzoma I** (r. 1440–1469) is sometimes called Motecuzoma the Elder to distinguish him from his more famous great-grandson. He was Itzcoatl's nephew, Tlacaelel's brother, and the first tlatoani who got to rule a Mexica state that was already powerful rather than building it from scratch. His campaigns pushed the empire well beyond the Basin of Mexico for the first time: east into the **Huastec** (WAHS-tek) lowlands of the northern Gulf Coast (which lay roughly 200 miles east of the Basin of Mexico, across the eastern mountain ranges, in the lowland zone where rubber and vanilla grew), south into Oaxaca where his armies conquered **Huaxyacac** (wahsh-YAH-kahk, the predecessor of modern Oaxaca City) and the surrounding Mixtec and Zapotec territories (Zapotec Civilization Ch 8). The conquests brought rubber, cotton, vanilla, gold, jade, and **cochineal** (the carmine-red dye produced from a cactus-eating insect) into Tenochtitlan's tribute lists. They illustrated the machine Tlacaelel had built — every campaign yielded both economic tribute and live captives for the temples — but they did not invent it. The machine ran the campaigns; the campaigns did not make the machine.

Motecuzoma I also presided over the calamities that defined his reign in Aztec memory. In 1449, a catastrophic flood inundated Tenochtitlan, and Nezahualcoyotl (Chapter 2), now the firmly established king of Texcoco, designed and supervised the construction of a 10-mile-long dike across the lake — the **Albarradón de Nezahualcoyotl**, the largest hydraulic engineering project in the pre-Columbian Americas, faced with stone, sluice-gated, and stout enough to separate the brackish water of the eastern lake from the freshwater chinampa zone (Chapter 4). Without it, Tenochtitlan would not have survived another century. In 1454, the **One Rabbit famine** (the worst drought of the 15th century) collapsed harvests across central Mexico for four consecutive years; the chronicles describe people selling themselves into slavery, parents giving children to coastal Totonacs in exchange for maize, and the population genuinely believing the world was about to end. The famine reshaped Aztec society — slavery expanded permanently (Chapter 6), tribute from outside the basin became indispensable, and the **New Fire Ceremony** of 1455 was remembered as the most terrified calendrical transition in Mexica history (Chapter 5).

By 1469, when Motecuzoma I died, the Aztec Empire stretched from the Gulf Coast to parts of the Pacific, with tribute flowing from over 200 conquered towns. The empire's basic shape was set. The dike protected the capital. The Flower Wars supplied the sacrificial pipeline. Tlacaelel was still in his office, planning the next round, and would outlive his brother by nearly twenty years.

# Chapter 4 — The Floating City

We need to talk about the city itself.

By the 1490s, Tenochtitlan was the largest city in the Western Hemisphere, larger than any city in Spain or France, and one of the four or five largest cities on earth. Estimates of its population vary because we have no census: archaeological reconstruction of the city's residential footprint, combined with figures recorded by Spanish observers in 1519, points to between 150,000 and 250,000 residents at the moment of contact. Even the low end of that range makes it bigger than London (about 50,000 in 1500), Paris (around 200,000 but on the high end of contemporary estimates, and probably overstated), and Seville (about 70,000). The high end makes it comparable to Constantinople or Beijing. Add the sister-city **Tlatelolco**, which by 1473 had been administratively absorbed under Tenochtitlan's rule but remained a distinct urban center, and the metropolitan complex on the lake's central islands held perhaps 200,000 to 300,000 people.

This was not normal for the Americas. Outside Tenochtitlan, the largest Aztec city was Texcoco, with maybe 60,000 residents. The next tier of cities (Cholula, Tlaxcala, Huexotzingo) held 20,000 to 40,000. After that, you dropped quickly to towns of a few thousand. Tenochtitlan was an outlier within its own empire by a factor of three or four, supported by a tribute system that pulled food and labor from a region of perhaps five to six million people across central Mexico.

The city was built on islands, and the islands were mostly artificial. The natural high ground in 1325 amounted to a few rocky outcrops totaling perhaps half a square mile. By 1500, the urbanized area was over five square miles. The difference was made by chinampas, dredged earth, and pile-driven fill. The Mexica had spent two centuries growing their city outward into the lake, by hand, one wicker-and-mud platform at a time.

---

**Chinampas** (chee-NAHM-pahs, sometimes called "floating gardens" in popular accounts, though they don't actually float) were the agricultural foundation of the city. The technology predates the Mexica by centuries (it was practiced at Xochimilco well before the Mexica arrived in the basin), but the Mexica scaled it up dramatically after 1430, when they conquered the southern lake towns and gained direct control of the chinampa zones.

Here's how a chinampa works. You stake out a rectangular plot in the shallow freshwater portion of the lake. You weave a fence of wicker around the perimeter. You shovel mud, lake-bottom muck, and decomposed vegetation inside the fence until you've built up an island three or four feet above the water level. You plant willows along the edges; their roots grow downward and lock the chinampa in place. You plant your crops on top: maize, beans, squash, chili peppers, amaranth, tomatoes, flowers. The water table sits within an arm's reach of the surface, which means the crops never need to be irrigated; the roots simply tap into the lake. The canals between chinampas (typically about 10 feet wide) double as transport corridors and as fertilizer reservoirs: when canals silt up, you dredge them and pile the rich black mud on top of the chinampas. Each chinampa might be 30 feet by 200 feet, and a family could work several.

The yields were astonishing by pre-modern standards. A well-managed chinampa could produce up to seven harvests per year (compared to one for a typical European wheatfield), feeding perhaps fifteen people per acre under intensive cultivation. The chinampa zone south of Tenochtitlan covered roughly 35 square miles at its peak, which is to say, roughly twice the size of the developed island of Manhattan. The Aztecs were running the most productive agricultural system in the Americas, and possibly in the world, in the middle of a salty highland lake at 7,400 feet elevation, on a foundation of mud and willow roots. The system fed Tenochtitlan in years when the basin's hill-slope farming failed, and it continued to feed Mexico City through the colonial period; small chinampas are still worked at Xochimilco today, the lone surviving fragment of the system. The Maya had developed a structurally similar raised-field system in the lowland swamps of the Petén centuries earlier (Maya Civilization Ch 1), and the Andean cultures around Lake Titicaca had built their own version on the altiplano (Early Andean Civilizations Ch 1) — three independent inventions of the same basic idea, in three radically different environments.

---

The city itself was laid out with a precision that the Spanish chroniclers, accustomed to medieval European street plans, found uncanny.

Tenochtitlan was divided into four quarters (campan), oriented to the cardinal directions and meeting at the **Sacred Precinct** (huey teocalli, way teh-OH-kahl-ee, "great temple" or "great holy place," the walled complex at the center of the city that contained the Templo Mayor and dozens of other temples). The four quarters were further subdivided into **calpulli** (kahl-POOL-lee, neighborhood-clan groups, Chapter 6), each with its own local temple, school, and council. Streets and canals ran in a regular grid, both designed to be used: the streets for foot traffic, the canals for canoe traffic. The Spanish chroniclers compared it to Venice, which is the right comparison in spirit, but Tenochtitlan's canal network was more extensive than Venice's by an order of magnitude. Bernal Díaz wrote that the canals were so busy you could not throw a stone without hitting a boat.

Three great **causeways** connected the island to the mainland: one running west to **Tlacopan**, one running south to **Iztapalapa** (with a branch to Coyoacan), and one running north to **Tepeyac**. Each was about 15 to 25 feet wide, raised eight feet or so above the lake surface, paved with cut stone, and punctuated at intervals by removable wooden bridges over channels left for water flow and (more pointedly) for defensive use. The bridges could be lifted out, isolating Tenochtitlan from the mainland, which was the single most important defensive feature of the city. The Spanish would learn this the hard way during La Noche Triste (Chapter 8).

The causeways were not the only links. Two **aqueducts** brought freshwater into the city, since Lake Texcoco itself was too brackish to drink. The first, the **Chapultepec Aqueduct**, was designed by Nezahualcoyotl himself and completed around 1466. It was an extraordinary piece of engineering: a double-pipe system running about 2.5 miles from the freshwater springs of Chapultepec hill to the city, made of stuccoed stone troughs supported on a low causeway. The two pipes ran in parallel so that one could be cleaned while the other carried water; this wasn't a luxury but a necessity, since the system was prone to silt and algae buildup. Drinking-water vendors in Tenochtitlan filled their canoes at the aqueduct's terminus and paddled the water through the canal network to households that wanted it. The Chapultepec Aqueduct fed the city for fifty years, until it was destroyed by Cortés during the siege of 1521, and the Spanish later rebuilt it on the same alignment because no one could think of a better design.

A second aqueduct, the **Acuecuexatl** (ah-kway-kway-SHAHTL, from the Coyoacan springs), was built by **Ahuitzotl** (whom we'll meet properly in Chapter 7 — for now: the warrior-king who pushed the empire to its maximum extent in the 1490s) in 1499. It was an engineering ambition that exceeded the engineering capacity. The flow was too strong, the channels too unprotected, and in 1500 the system overflowed into Lake Texcoco, raised the water level several feet, and flooded large sections of Tenochtitlan. Ahuitzotl, by some accounts, died of injuries sustained when a low doorway collapsed on him as he fled the rising water (the connection between the flood and his death two years later is debated, but he certainly never fully recovered, and the construction failure haunted his last years). The flood is a useful reminder that Aztec engineering, brilliant as it was, was not infallible. The same hydraulic system that fed the city could also drown it.

---

The center of everything was the **Sacred Precinct**, a walled enclosure roughly 1,300 feet on a side at the heart of the city. By 1500, the precinct contained 78 separate buildings, most of which we can identify only from descriptions in the Spanish chronicles and from the foundations excavated in modern Mexico City. These included the Templo Mayor (Phase VII by 1500, the seventh successive enlargement, built over the ruins of the previous six), temples to Quetzalcoatl, Tezcatlipoca, Coatlicue, and dozens of lesser deities, the great **tzompantli** (tsom-PAHN-tlee, "skull rack" — a wooden frame on which the heads of sacrificial victims were displayed in rows), a ceremonial ball court, and the **calmecac** school for noble boys (Chapter 6, the academy where the sons of the nobility were trained). Archaeological excavation of the tzompantli in the 2010s recovered hundreds of skulls from the foundation of the rack and confirmed the historical accounts. The whole complex was enclosed within the **coatepantli** ("serpent wall," a low stone wall topped with sculpted serpent heads) and entered through four gates corresponding to the four quarters of the city.

The Templo Mayor itself was the seventh and largest version of the original 1325 shrine. By 1487, when Ahuitzotl rededicated it, the pyramid stood about 150 feet (45 meters) tall, with two parallel staircases, each over 100 steps, leading to twin shrines at the summit. The southern shrine, painted red and white, housed Huitzilopochtli; the northern, painted blue and white, housed Tlaloc. The painted polychrome was the city's signature visual: Aztec architecture was not the bare stone we see in ruins today but the same kind of brilliantly painted plaster surface that had defined Teotihuacan a thousand years earlier (Teotihuacan Ch 2). When Spanish observers tried to describe Tenochtitlan, they kept reaching for the word "white" to capture the lime-plastered surfaces, but they noted the "great red and blue temples" rising above the white. From the lake, the city would have been a ridge of color — white walls, red and blue pyramid summits, painted murals on every important building, all reflecting in the lake water.

The dedication of the Phase VII Templo Mayor in 1487 by Ahuitzotl is one of the more contested episodes in Aztec history. The Mexica chronicles claim that 80,400 victims were sacrificed over four days at the rededication, with the lines of captives stretching out of the city for miles and the priests working in shifts. The number is almost certainly exaggerated; modern estimates cluster around 4,000 to 14,000, and even the lower figure assumes industrial-scale ritualized killing that would have required a specific organizational apparatus to manage. What is not in doubt is that the rededication was unprecedented in scale, that captives from many recently conquered Gulf Coast and Oaxacan towns were sacrificed (a deliberate political gesture: the rededication asserted Mexica dominance over the new tributaries), and that the event was remembered for generations as the moment Aztec sacrificial practice reached its full public scope. Ahuitzotl was the kind of ruler who liked his statements grand.

At the foot of the Huitzilopochtli stairway, set flush with the plaza floor, lay the **Coyolxauhqui Stone**, a circular relief eleven feet across that depicts the moon goddess **Coyolxauhqui** (koy-ol-SHAH-wkee, "Bells on Her Cheeks," sister of Huitzilopochtli) at the moment of her dismemberment: limbs splayed, head severed, body curled into a circle. The myth behind the image: their mother, Coatlicue, conceived Huitzilopochtli by a feather that fell from the sky; Coyolxauhqui, scandalized, conspired with her four hundred star-brothers to kill their mother before the new child was born; Huitzilopochtli leapt from the womb fully armed, killed Coyolxauhqui, and dismembered her on a hill where her limbs scattered. The architecture and the myth lined up exactly. Sacrificial victims, after their hearts were cut out at the temple summit, were tumbled down the steps to land on her image — every sacrifice reenacting the cosmic battle, every victim playing the role of Coyolxauhqui falling, every body that landed on the stone a renewed assertion of the warrior-sun-god's victory over the moon. The stone was rediscovered in 1978 by electrical workers digging near the cathedral in central Mexico City, an event that triggered the **Templo Mayor Project**, the long-running modern excavation directed by **Eduardo Matos Moctezuma** (eh-DWAR-doh MAH-tos mok-tay-ZOO-mah, by descent related to the Aztec royal line, and the central archaeologist of his ancestors' temple) that has progressively uncovered the temple foundations beneath the colonial city. More new evidence about the late Aztec Empire has come from this single excavation than from any other in Mesoamerica.

---

The **Tlatelolco Market**, on the sister island just north of Tenochtitlan, was the empire's commercial heart and one of the largest marketplaces in the world. Bernal Díaz, who saw it firsthand in 1519, wrote that some of his fellow soldiers, who had traveled in Constantinople and Rome, said they had never seen a market so well-organized or so full of people. The numbers given by various Spanish observers (40,000, 60,000, 80,000 daily visitors) are guesswork, but the lower estimate is plausible and represents a foot traffic that few European cities of 1519 could match. The market sold maize, beans, chili peppers, tomatoes, salt, cacao, vanilla, rabbits, dogs, turkeys, fish, frogs, salamanders (axolotls were eaten in Tenochtitlan in great numbers, until the Spanish found them disgusting and they fell out of fashion), feathers, jade, gold, silver, cotton, henequen fiber, sandals, cloaks, slaves, medicinal herbs, and dozens of other categories. Each commodity had its own section. Each section had its own court of magistrates that adjudicated disputes on the spot. Cheating, defective merchandise, and price gouging were grounds for criminal prosecution.

The market ran on **cacao beans** as small-denomination currency. A turkey cost 100 beans. A tamale cost 1 bean. A canoe cost about 1 cotton **quachtli** (KWACH-tlee, a standardized cotton cloth that served as the larger-denomination currency, equivalent to about 65 to 300 cacao beans depending on quality). The system worked because cacao was both a luxury beverage and a durable, divisible, portable commodity, and because the empire's tribute system funneled cacao production from the Soconusco coast (Chapter 7) into central Mexico in industrial quantities. Cacao-as-currency was not a Mexica invention; the Maya had been using cacao beans as a standardized medium of exchange across the lowland markets for centuries (Maya Civilization Ch 2), and the Aztecs had inherited the convention along with the trade routes. The Spanish, seeing the market for the first time, were dumbfounded: an economy this large running without coin or letter of credit, ordered by traditional law and visual inspection.

The market was also where the **pochteca** (poach-TEH-kah, the long-distance merchant guild) operated their warehouses and conducted their business. The pochteca were one of the most curious institutions of the empire: a hereditary class of professional merchants, with their own neighborhoods, their own gods, their own dress, their own legal courts, and their own complicated relationship with the imperial state. They will get a chapter of their own (Chapter 6).

---

What did everyday life feel like in this city? Loud, crowded, and largely outdoors. Houses were one-story adobe-and-stone constructions for commoners and two-story plastered compounds for nobles. Most domestic life happened in interior courtyards, since the climate at 7,400 feet is mild year-round (Tenochtitlan's average annual temperature is about 60°F; nights can be cold but freezes are rare in the city center). Cooking was done over clay hearths fueled by wood and charcoal, both increasingly expensive as deforestation around the basin progressed (Chapter 8). The diet centered on maize prepared as tortillas and tamales, with beans, squash, chili, amaranth, sage seeds, and prickly pear as supplements; meat was occasional and came mainly from turkey, dog, fish, and the lake's astonishingly varied small game (frogs, salamanders, lake-fly larvae, even dragonfly nymphs). The Mexica drank water, **atolli** (ah-TOH-lee, a thick maize gruel), **pulque** (POOL-kay, the fermented sap of the maguey cactus, mildly alcoholic, the everyday alcoholic drink of central Mexico), and, for the wealthy, **xocolatl** (sho-KOH-lahtl, the bitter cacao beverage that gave the modern English word "chocolate"). Public drunkenness on pulque was severely punished, in theory; the elderly were exempt and could drink as they pleased.

The smell of the city was distinctive. Spanish observers, who knew European cities were filthy, were impressed at how clean Tenochtitlan was. The streets were swept daily by a corps of municipal cleaners. Public latrines were available. Human waste was collected in canoes for use as fertilizer in the chinampas, an integrated nutrient-recycling system that European cities would not match for another four centuries. The air would have smelled of burning copal incense from the temples, cooking maize, lake water, and, on festival days, blood. Compared to contemporary Seville, Tenochtitlan would have been startlingly clean and startlingly bloody at the same time.

# Chapter 5 — The Sun That Demanded Blood

Aztec religion was the most theatrical in the ancient world.

Other civilizations sacrificed humans (the Mesopotamians and the Shang both practiced it at certain periods, the Carthaginians did, the Maya did, the Andean cultures did, a number of European prehistoric cultures did), but no other civilization staged human sacrifice on the scale, with the regularity, or with the cosmological elaboration that the Aztecs did. The Templo Mayor was the most actively used sacrificial site in human history. The state religion required the regular spilling of human blood as the literal mechanism that kept the universe running. And the religion was performed publicly, on the highest pyramid in the city, in front of crowds, by costumed priests, with formal liturgies and choreographed processions, every single month of the year.

This is uncomfortable material, and it deserves to be presented with as much honesty about the evidence as the rest of the empire's history. The basic facts (regular sacrifice, the Templo Mayor as the central site, the connection between warfare and sacrificial supply, public ritual performance) are not seriously disputed; they are documented in Aztec sources, Spanish sources, and the archaeological record (skull racks, sacrificial knives, butchered remains in temple contexts have all been recovered). What is contested is *scale*: how many sacrifices per year, how many at major dedications, how the demand interacted with imperial expansion. The Aztec sources, written by descendants of the priestly elite, tend to inflate the numbers as a sign of religious devotion. The Spanish sources, written by people who needed to justify the conquest, tend to inflate the numbers as a sign of barbaric darkness. Modern estimates range from a few thousand sacrifices per year across the entire empire on the low end to twenty thousand or more at peak. We will probably never have a definitive count. What we can say is that the practice was systematic, pervasive, and central to the imperial state, and that calling it "occasional" or "ceremonial" misses the point.

We should also resist the modern temptation to read Aztec religion as either uniquely barbaric or as a kind of misunderstood purity. It was a real religion, with internal logic, performed by people who genuinely believed they were doing necessary cosmic work. It was also a system that benefited a specific political class (the priestly and warrior elites who organized and consumed the rituals) at the expense of vast numbers of conquered peoples and slaves whose bodies fed it. Both things are true. The Aztec sources are explicit that human sacrifice was a religious necessity, that cosmic destruction would follow if it stopped, and that the gods needed blood to function. The same sources are explicit that the war machine that fed the sacrificial system also expanded the empire, enriched the elite, and intimidated potential rebels. Religion and politics were not separable; they were the same engine.

---

The cosmology, in summary form: the Aztecs believed that the world was now in its **fifth age** (or "fifth sun"), and that four previous worlds had been destroyed in succession (by jaguars, wind, fire, and water). The current age was created at Teotihuacan, where the gods had gathered after the destruction of the fourth world to bring up a new sun. The gods sat around a great fire, and the question was who would throw himself in to be reborn as the new sun. A wealthy, beautiful god named **Tecuciztecatl** volunteered first but lost his nerve on the threshold of the flames. A poor, scabrous god named **Nanahuatzin**, by contrast, threw himself in without hesitation, and was reborn as the sun. Tecuciztecatl, shamed, then jumped in too and was reborn as the moon, but the gods threw a rabbit at his face to dim his brightness (which is why the Aztecs saw a rabbit on the moon, the way other cultures see a face). When the new sun rose, it would not move; it sat there glaring at the world, demanding that the other gods sacrifice themselves so it would have the energy to begin its daily journey. They did. All of them. And ever since, the sun's daily movement across the sky has required the same kind of sacrifice that originally set it in motion: blood, freely given, by the creatures of the world.

This was, on Tlacaelel's interpretation (Chapter 3), the foundational reason for human sacrifice. The Aztecs were repaying the cosmic debt incurred when the gods sacrificed themselves at Teotihuacan. The current world, the fifth sun, would last only as long as the cycle of sacrifice continued. When it stopped, the sun would stop, the world would end, and another age would begin. And the Mexica, as the chosen people of Huitzilopochtli (the sun himself, in his warrior aspect), bore the primary responsibility for keeping the cycle going.

The fact that the Aztecs literally walked among the ruins of Teotihuacan when they made these arguments is one of the strangest things about their religion. They knew the ruins existed. They had named them ("the place where the gods were born"). They collected artifacts from the site, mostly green obsidian objects, and reused them in their own rituals. They incorporated Teotihuacan into their creation mythology as the literal birthplace of the current world. But they did not know who had built it (Teotihuacan Ch 8 covers the absence of a name for the original builders). To the Aztecs, the great pyramids 30 miles to the northeast were divine architecture, evidence of the gods' presence at the moment of cosmogenesis. They made pilgrimages to the site, they performed rituals there, they wrote it into their sacred geography. Teotihuacan, dead for nearly a thousand years by the time the Mexica encountered it, had a second life as an Aztec religious site.

---

The pantheon was crowded. The Florentine Codex catalogs hundreds of named deities, ranging from major cosmic figures down to local patron gods of individual neighborhoods, and the relationships among them were tangled enough that even the priests disagreed on the details. A handful of the major figures, though, dominated public worship and the calendar of festivals.

**Huitzilopochtli**, the war god turned sun god (Chapter 1, 3), the patron of the Mexica themselves, was the demanding center of state religion: he wanted warrior captives, and he wanted them constantly. Across the twin shrines at the summit of the Templo Mayor, his shrine faced that of **Tlaloc**, the ancient rain god, identifiable across more than a millennium of Mesoamerican iconography by his goggle eyes and fanged mouth. Tlaloc had been worshipped, under the same iconography, at Teotihuacan a thousand years earlier (Teotihuacan Ch 7) and traces back further still to Olmec storm-deity imagery (Olmec Civilization Ch 4) — a continuous lineage of more than two thousand years. The pairing of war-and-sun with rain-and-agriculture at the apex of the city's religious architecture was not arbitrary. It reproduced, in stone and paint, the empire's two ultimate dependencies: military success and reliable rainfall. Tlaloc demanded children, particularly during the dry season, on the belief that their crying would bring more rain. This is one of the more disturbing facts of Aztec religion, attested in multiple sources and archaeologically confirmed by the buried remains of sacrificed children in deposits beneath Tlaloc's shrine.

The other great cosmic powers ran along complementary axes. **Tezcatlipoca** (tes-kah-tlee-POH-kah, "Smoking Mirror") was the sky-and-night counterpart to Huitzilopochtli's solar warrior: a trickster god of sorcery, royalty, and discord, often depicted with an obsidian mirror in place of one foot. **Quetzalcoatl** (ket-sahl-KOH-ahtl, "Feathered Serpent") was the deity of wind, learning, priesthood, and creation, and the oldest continuous figure in the Mesoamerican pantheon — feathered serpents appear in Olmec iconography over a thousand years earlier (Olmec Civilization Ch 4), the Maya knew him as Kukulkan (Maya Civilization Ch 7), and he had a major temple at Teotihuacan (Teotihuacan Ch 3). His trajectory is itself a small drama: his cult was politically *suppressed* at Teotihuacan around 250 CE (Teotihuacan Ch 3 and Ch 7), then revived by the Toltecs at Tula, then inherited by the Aztecs, who turned the legend of his eastward departure into a cosmological end-times prophecy. A god the people of Teotihuacan once tore down had, a thousand years later, become the deity whose return the Aztecs feared above all others. The Aztecs gave him a major role in their creation mythology — he descended into the underworld to retrieve the bones of humanity and give them new life — and they kept the older Toltec story about his departure eastward across the sea, with a vow to return in a year called One Reed in the cyclical calendar. The Year One Reed of the early 16th century corresponded to 1519. We will come back to that.

The mother and earth and underworld powers were as important as the sky deities, and as fearsome to look at. **Coatlicue** (koh-aht-LEE-kway, "She of the Serpent Skirt") was the earth and mother goddess, mother of Huitzilopochtli, Coyolxauhqui, and the four hundred star-brothers (Chapter 3, Chapter 4); her surviving eight-foot basalt sculpture, commissioned around 1490 and now in the National Museum of Anthropology in Mexico City, wears a skirt of writhing serpents and a necklace of human hands and hearts. The statue is one of the most powerful objects in pre-Columbian art, and one of the most disturbing: when it was first excavated in 1790, the ecclesiastical authorities of Mexico City ordered it reburied as a pagan object too dangerous to leave on display. It was excavated and reburied multiple times before finally entering the museum in the 19th century. **Xipe Totec** (SHEE-pay TOH-tek, "Our Lord the Flayed One") presided over springtime renewal — the year shedding its old husk like a snake shedding skin — and his annual festival, **Tlacaxipehualiztli** (tlah-kah-shee-peh-WAH-leets-tlee, the second of the eighteen monthly festivals), was the most disturbing event in the regular ritual cycle. Captives were sacrificed, then their priests wore the flayed skins for the next twenty days as the skin progressively rotted away, symbolizing the new green corn stalk emerging from the dry husk of the old. This is documented in detail in the Florentine Codex and confirmed by archaeological remains. The priests believed they were enacting a vital agricultural metaphor on which the next year's corn crop depended; modern observers should not pretend the ritual was less than it was, and also should not pretend it was simply gratuitous. **Coyolxauhqui** (Chapter 4) was the dismembered moon, **Huehueteotl** (way-way-TEH-otl, "Old Old God") was the ancient fire deity also worshipped at Teotihuacan, and dozens more — neighborhood patrons, deities of pulque and birth and rain and travel — filled out a pantheon in which there was always one more god to feed.

---

The Aztec calendar was a parallel cosmological apparatus, a clockwork that organized when each ritual was due. The system used two interlocking calendars that turned together like gears.

The **tonalpohualli** (toh-nahl-poh-WAH-lee, "count of days") was the 260-day ritual calendar, made by combining 20 named days with 13 numbers. The 260-day cycle dates back to at least the Olmec period (Olmec Civilization Ch 7) and was shared across Mesoamerica; the Maya used the same cycle (Maya Civilization Ch 2). Each day had its own divinatory meaning, and skilled priests (known by the title **tonalpouhque**, "counter of days") used the calendar for naming children, scheduling weddings, predicting outcomes, and diagnosing illness.

The **xiuhpohualli** (shee-oo-poh-WAH-lee, "count of years") was the 365-day solar calendar, made of 18 months of 20 days plus 5 final "useless" days (**nemontemi**) at the end of the year, considered unlucky. Each of the 18 months had its own festival, with its own deity, its own ritual sequence, and (almost without exception) its own form of human sacrifice. The Toxcatl festival in May, dedicated to Tezcatlipoca, featured the sacrifice of a young man who had been chosen a year in advance to live as the living embodiment of the god, dressed in his regalia, attended by servants, accompanied by four wives in his final twenty days, before climbing the temple steps and having his heart cut out at the summit. The next day, a new young man was selected to begin the year's incarnation. The festival of **Ochpaniztli** in autumn, dedicated to **Toci** (the grandmother of the gods), involved the sacrifice and flaying of a woman whose skin was then worn by a priest performing a ritual sweeping of the temple. The cycle of monthly sacrifices was continuous, with no calendrical gap.

The two calendars (tonalpohualli of 260 days, xiuhpohualli of 365) interlocked into a larger cycle of **52 years**, called **xiuhmolpilli** (shee-oo-mol-PEEL-ee, "binding of the years"). After 52 years, both calendars returned to their shared starting position. The transition between cycles was the moment of greatest cosmic vulnerability in the entire Aztec calendrical system. The Aztecs feared that at the end of one bundle of years, the world might fail to begin a new one, and the fifth sun would simply expire.

To prevent this, the priests performed the **New Fire Ceremony** (xiuhmolpilli, "binding of the years," the same word used for the 52-year cycle itself). The most elaborately documented New Fire Ceremony was that of **1507**, recorded in the **Codex Borbonicus**. The ritual unfolded as follows. In the days before the ceremony, all fires across the empire were extinguished. Hearths were swept clean. Pottery was broken. Pregnant women were locked in granaries with masks of maguey leaves on their faces, on the belief that if the world did end, pregnant women might turn into demons that would devour the survivors. Children were kept awake all night by their parents, who pinched them every time they nodded off, on the belief that any child who slept through the New Fire would turn into a mouse.

The priests, dressed in the regalia of all the major deities, climbed to the summit of **Huixachtlan hill** (wee-shahk-TLAHN, the "Hill of the Star," about 7 miles southeast of Tenochtitlan) at sunset. They watched the constellation Pleiades. When the Pleiades reached zenith, signaling that the world had not ended, the head priest laid a sacrificial victim on the altar, opened his chest, and drilled new fire on his sternum using a wooden fire-drill on a wooden hearth, the ancient method. When the spark caught (and it always caught, the priests being expert in this), it was set into a torch, then carried by relay runners to every temple and household in the empire. Within hours, the entire central Mexican plateau was relit from a single flame, drilled on a single human chest, on a single hilltop. The world had survived another 52 years.

The Mexica chronicles record three New Fire Ceremonies during the imperial period: 1403 (under Huitzilihuitl), 1455 (under Motecuzoma I, during the One Rabbit famine, especially fraught), and 1507 (under Motecuzoma II, the most elaborately documented). A fourth would have occurred in 1559 if the empire had survived; instead, the Catholic missionaries in colonial Mexico did everything they could to suppress the ceremony, with mixed success.

The ceremony is one of the most striking examples of public ritual in the ancient world, and it should be set alongside the Roman Saecular Games or the Egyptian Sed Festival as a comparable performance of cosmic renewal. The Romans renewed their state every 110 years; the Egyptian pharaoh renewed his rule every 30; the Mexica renewed the world itself every 52. The scale of belief was, by any standard, enormous.

# Chapter 6 — Ladders and Cages

Aztec society had a structure that was rigid by some measures and surprisingly mobile by others.

At the top sat the **tlatoani** of Tenochtitlan, who by 1500 was the most powerful individual on the continent. The tlatoani was not strictly hereditary: each successor was elected by a council of senior nobles from a pool of eligible candidates, all of whom belonged to the royal lineage descended from Acamapichtli. Election usually fell to a brother, son, or nephew of the previous ruler, but the council could and did skip candidates considered unsuitable. The system was a mix of hereditary monarchy and oligarchic election; in practice, it produced a string of capable warrior-kings, with two notable failures (**Tizoc**, the brief reigning fifth tlatoani in the 1480s whom we'll meet in Chapter 7, was considered too weak and was probably poisoned; **Cuauhtémoc**, the last tlatoani who would face Cortés's siege in 1521, ruled in collapsing circumstances and could not have saved the empire even if he had been a genius).

Below the tlatoani came the **pipiltin** (pee-PEEL-teen, the noble class, plural of *pilli*). The pipiltin were defined by descent: anyone who could claim direct descent from a tlatoani or from one of the empire's founding lineages. They held the senior offices in government, the priesthood, and the military, were exempt from manual labor, owned land in their own right (most other land was held communally by calpulli), wore distinctive clothing (cotton fabrics and dyed mantles forbidden to commoners), and received the best education at the **calmecac** schools. They were also, in theory, held to higher standards: nobles caught in adultery, drunkenness, or theft faced harsher penalties than commoners, on the principle that those who wielded the empire's authority should be its strictest exemplars. In practice, justice was probably more flexible than the ideal.

Below the pipiltin came the **macehualtin** (mah-seh-WAHL-teen, the commoner class). The macehualtin were the vast majority of the population: farmers, artisans, soldiers, fishermen, market sellers, porters, builders. They held land through their **calpulli** (kahl-POOL-lee, the neighborhood-clan group, originally a kin-based unit but by 1500 more of a residential and occupational organization). The calpulli was the basic unit of Aztec civic life. It allocated chinampa plots to its members, organized labor levies for state projects, ran its own school (the **telpochcalli**), maintained its own local temple, and selected representatives to the city's senior councils. Each calpulli had a council of elders that adjudicated minor disputes and set local rules. The calpulli system distributed power widely enough that ordinary Mexica had a real stake in the empire's success, and they could draw on their calpulli for support in times of crisis.

The macehualtin were not stuck where they were born. The Aztec system included a working ladder of social mobility centered on warfare. A commoner who captured one enemy warrior in battle received a small honor; capturing two or three increased his rank; capturing four entitled him to the title of **eagle warrior** or **jaguar warrior** (the elite military orders, distinguished by their costumes — eagle-feather suits with bird-headdress helmets, or jaguar-pelt suits with snarling-jaguar helmets). At the highest level, a commoner who distinguished himself sufficiently in warfare could be elevated to the rank of **cuauhpilli** (KWAH-oo-PEEL-lee, "eagle noble"), a kind of meritocratic nobility that was distinct from the hereditary pipiltin but enjoyed many of the same privileges. The system was a working channel for social mobility, and it was the empire's primary mechanism for incorporating talented commoners into the warrior elite. The Roman Empire, comparable in scale, maintained a similar system of military advancement (although nothing as formalized as the Aztec eagle and jaguar orders), and the structural logic was identical: a continuously expanding empire produces opportunity for ambitious commoners, which keeps the lower orders invested in the system rather than rebelling against it.

Below the macehualtin came the **mayeque** (mah-YEH-keh), a smaller class of landless commoners who worked land owned by nobles in exchange for a share of the produce. The mayeque are sometimes described as serfs, but the analogy is imperfect; they were free in person but tied to land obligations.

At the bottom were the **tlacotin** (tlah-KOH-teen, slaves). Aztec slavery was different from racial chattel slavery in the Americas after 1500. The tlacotin were not a hereditary class; their children were born free. Slavery resulted from specific causes: criminal punishment, debt, or self-sale during famine. Slaves could own property, marry, save money, and purchase their own freedom. They were not racially differentiated from their owners. The institution was real and exploitative, but it operated more like Roman or Mediterranean slavery than like the colonial-Atlantic slavery that would replace it after 1521. A subcategory, **tecpan tlacotin**, were slaves attached to noble households and palace administration. Captives taken in war, who would become sacrificial victims, were not classified as tlacotin; they were a separate category, considered already given to the gods, distinct from the regular slave population.

---

Children were schooled in one of two institutional systems, depending on their birth.

The **calmecac** (kahl-meh-KAHK, "house of lineage" or "house of the noble line") were schools attached to the major temples in each city. They educated the sons of the pipiltin and a select group of commoner boys destined for the priesthood. The curriculum was demanding: religious ritual, calendar mathematics, astronomy, the painting of codices (the Aztec script was a mix of pictographs and phonetic glyphs, complex enough that mastering it took years), Nahuatl rhetoric, history, law, and military discipline. Calmecac students lived at the school, slept little, fasted often, performed self-mortification (cutting their tongues, ears, or genitals with maguey thorns to draw blood as offerings), and were beaten for infractions. Graduates filled the empire's administrative, religious, and high-military offices. The calmecac was the institutional core that reproduced the elite generation after generation.

The **telpochcalli** (tel-poach-KAHL-lee, "house of the youth") were neighborhood schools attached to each calpulli. They educated commoner boys in martial combat, civic duty, communal labor, and the basics of religious participation. Graduates of the telpochcalli formed the bulk of the Aztec army and the trained labor pool that built the empire's roads, canals, and pyramids. The schooling was less academically demanding than the calmecac but no less rigorous physically; telpochcalli students worked on public construction projects, performed sweat-bath rituals, and were trained from childhood in handling the macahuitl (the wooden sword edged with obsidian blades, the standard Aztec weapon — *obsidian* is volcanic glass, sharper than steel when freshly knapped, though brittle; central Mexico had abundant high-quality obsidian sources, and the empire's whole edged-weapon tradition rested on them).

Girls received less formal schooling but had their own institutional paths. The most studious might become **cihuatlamacazqui**, female priestesses attached to certain temples, who could be married or celibate depending on the institution. Most girls learned weaving, cooking, household management, and religious ritual from their mothers and from local female elders. Aztec gender roles were strict by modern standards but allowed more autonomy than was typical in early modern Europe. Women owned property in their own names, could initiate divorce, ran market stalls, and could become priestesses, healers, or midwives. The most educated noble women, like **Macuilxochitzin** (the daughter of the cihuacoatl Tlacaelel), composed Nahuatl poetry and were celebrated for their literary work; her surviving poems are among the most beautiful texts in the Aztec corpus.

Marriage was monogamous in legal form for commoners but polygamous for nobles, who could maintain multiple wives in separate households. Sexual conduct was strictly regulated: adultery was punishable by death, applied to both men and women, and enforced. Public drunkenness was, in theory, also a capital offense for the young, though the elderly were exempt. The legal system was harsher than Roman law in its written code but probably patchier in its enforcement, particularly in remote tributaries.

---

The judicial system was relatively sophisticated. Cases proceeded through a hierarchy of courts: local calpulli councils handled minor disputes, district courts handled serious crimes, and the **tlatocan** (tlah-TOH-kahn, the supreme council of the tlatoani) served as the empire's high court. Judges were full-time professional specialists, drawn from the calmecac graduates, paid by the state, and held to strict standards: bribery of a judge was a capital offense, and judges who took bribes were executed publicly as a deterrent. The 80-article legal code formalized by **Nezahualcoyotl** of Texcoco around 1445 was widely admired and partially adopted across the empire; it specified penalties for murder, adultery, theft, official corruption, battlefield cowardice, and other offenses, with detailed gradations based on the social status of victim and perpetrator. Texcoco, under the long reigns of Nezahualcoyotl (1431–1472) and his son **Nezahualpilli** (1472–1516), became the most legally and intellectually sophisticated polity in central Mexico, and it served as the cultural counterweight to Tenochtitlan's military and religious dominance.

Nezahualcoyotl deserves a sketch of his own. He was a poet, an architect, a hydraulic engineer (the Albarradón that saved Tenochtitlan from flooding was his design), a religious skeptic (his philosophical poetry rejects the dominant sacrificial cosmology in favor of a more abstract, possibly monotheistic conception of "the giver of life"), and a working king who held one of the empire's three capitals for forty-one years. His court at Texcoco was a center of poetry, scholarship, and royal patronage that drew artists, philosophers, and scientists from across central Mexico. About thirty of his Nahuatl poems survive in collected manuscripts compiled after the conquest. They are not what one expects from a 15th-century warrior king. Sample lines, in an approximate translation that follows the spirit of Miguel León-Portilla's standard scholarly renderings rather than any single one of them word-for-word: "Truly do we live on Earth? / Not forever on Earth; only a little while here. / Although it be jade, it will be broken. / Although it be gold, it is crushed. / Although it be a quetzal feather, it is torn asunder. / Not forever on Earth; only a little while here." The voice is thoroughly Mexican and unmistakably medieval-Mesoamerican; it is also a voice that the Renaissance Italian humanists, working in the same century on the other side of the Atlantic, would have recognized as a fellow practitioner of philosophical lyric. Petrarch (1304–1374) and Nezahualcoyotl (1402–1472) are roughly contemporaries, and their concerns overlap.

---

The **pochteca** were one of the most distinctive institutions of the empire and require explanation, because nothing comparable existed in the contemporary Old World.

The pochteca were the long-distance merchant guild, a hereditary class of professional traders who organized armed expeditions to distant markets and brought back luxury goods. They lived in their own neighborhoods (the Pochtlan and Acxotlan calpulli of Tenochtitlan), worshipped their own patron god (**Yacatecuhtli**, "Lord of the Vanguard"), wore distinctive plain dress, kept their own legal courts (Chapter 4), and operated under a strict guild discipline. They were technically commoners, but the wealthiest pochteca families rivaled the nobility in their material wealth, and by the late empire, the line between rich pochteca and poor pipiltin had genuinely blurred. The state simultaneously valued them (for the goods they brought) and resented them (for their independence and wealth), and the relationship was managed through a combination of formal recognition and informal tension.

A pochteca expedition was a serious undertaking. A typical caravan included perhaps fifty to two hundred merchants, with porters carrying loads on tumplines (forehead-straps; Mesoamerica had no pack animals), interpreters, and armed guards. The merchants traveled in disguise when crossing hostile territory, learning the local dialects and customs to pass as foreigners. They carried specific Tenochtitlan products outbound (cotton cloth, obsidian blades, slave-grade copper, rabbit-fur capes, dyestuffs) and returned with luxuries that Tenochtitlan could not produce: cacao beans (the bulk currency, but also the high-grade dark-roasted beans for the elite chocolate trade), quetzal feathers (the iridescent green tail feathers of the resplendent quetzal, the supreme prestige material in Mesoamerica), jade and obsidian ornaments, jaguar pelts, gold dust, amber, rubber, and tropical bird feathers of every color. They also gathered intelligence: pochteca expeditions were the empire's primary source of information about the political condition of distant kingdoms, and pochteca who returned reporting that a particular tributary was preparing rebellion or a particular independent kingdom was vulnerable to conquest were rewarded with tlatoani audiences and noble titles.

The pochteca network reached from the Aztec heartland in central Mexico to the **Soconusco** (or **Xoconochco**, sho-koh-NOACH-ko) cacao coast on the Pacific, on the border of modern Guatemala (Chapter 7), to the Maya region of Yucatán in the southeast (where pochteca traders may have witnessed the late Postclassic Maya cities firsthand, Maya Civilization Ch 8), and reportedly as far north as the American Southwest. Goods of unmistakably Aztec character (specific copper bell types, cotton fabrics, mosaic mirrors) appear in late prehistoric sites in modern New Mexico, suggesting that pochteca trade reached at least as far as the Pueblo cultures, although direct pochteca presence that far north has not been confirmed. On the eastern side of the continent, a contemporaneous prestige-exchange network centered on Cahokia moved copper, marine shell, and ceremonial goods comparable distances through non-market gift exchange (Mississippian Culture Ch 6).

---

The shape of the system, viewed from a height, comes back to the chapter's title. Aztec society was a series of ladders set inside a series of cages. A macehualli could climb to cuauhpilli on captured warriors. A talented commoner boy could land in the calmecac and rise into the priesthood or administration. A merchant could outearn a noble. A clever woman could become a priestess, a healer, a market judge, a poet. The ladders were real. They were also bounded. Hereditary nobility kept the highest offices for itself. The pochteca, for all their wealth, never sat at the top of the empire's political order. The mayeque worked land they would never own. The tlacotin remained tlacotin until their debts were paid. And the engine that drove the whole structure — Tlacaelel's machine of warrior capture and cosmic obligation — required a constant supply of bodies that the system itself produced and consumed. Mobility for some was made possible by constraint for many. The arrangement worked, for almost a century, because the empire kept growing and the rewards kept arriving. When the growth stopped, in the early 1500s under Motecuzoma II (Chapter 7), the ladders started to feel less like opportunities and more like the bars of a cage that everyone, suddenly, was inside together.

# Chapter 7 — Ahuitzotl and the High Tide

Three rulers brought the empire to its zenith.

**Axayacatl** (ah-shah-YAH-kahtl, "Water Mask," r. 1469–1481) succeeded his grandfather Motecuzoma I at age 19. He was vigorous, ambitious, and a competent if not brilliant general. His reign opened with the **conquest of Tlatelolco** in 1473. Tlatelolco, the sister city on the neighboring island, had remained politically separate from Tenochtitlan for nearly 150 years, with its own ruling line and its own market. By the 1470s, the relationship had soured: Tlatelolco's tlatoani, Moquihuix, had attempted to build an anti-Tenochtitlan coalition. Axayacatl crushed it. Moquihuix died in the assault on the Templo Mayor of Tlatelolco. The market, the most important commercial institution in central Mexico, was annexed under Tenochtitlan's administration. Tlatelolco never recovered its political independence, but its market continued to flourish under the unified administration, and its calpulli structure remained intact.

Axayacatl also pushed the empire to one of its catastrophic limits. In 1479, he led a major campaign against the **Tarascan Empire** of Michoacán, the most powerful kingdom in Mesoamerica that the Aztecs never conquered. The Tarascans (also called the **Purépecha**, poo-RAY-pay-cha, after their own name for themselves) ruled from their capital at **Tzintzuntzan** (tseen-TSOON-tsahn, "place of the hummingbirds," coincidentally similar to a Nahuatl-style name despite being in a completely unrelated language) on the western shore of Lake Pátzcuaro. The Tarascans had a number of advantages: defensible mountain terrain, skilled metalworkers (the Tarascans were among the few Mesoamerican civilizations to master copper and bronze metallurgy, putting them centuries ahead of the Aztecs in metal weaponry), and a centralized state that mobilized armies efficiently. At **Tlaximaloyan** in 1479, the Aztec army of perhaps 32,000 men met a Tarascan army of perhaps 50,000. The Aztecs were routed. Estimates put their losses at over 20,000 men, the worst military defeat in Mexica history. The frontier between the two empires froze along a line of fortified Aztec border towns, and no later tlatoani would attempt the conquest. The Tarascans remained independent until they made their own arrangement with the Spanish in 1522, after the fall of Tenochtitlan.

In 1479, Axayacatl also commissioned the **Sun Stone** (sometimes called the Aztec Calendar Stone), a massive basalt disc nearly 12 feet in diameter and weighing 24 tons, carved with the central deity **Tonatiuh** (the sun) surrounded by symbols of the four previous cosmic ages, the twenty named days of the tonalpohualli, and decorative borders showing the cosmic ordering of the world. The stone was intended for a ceremonial platform in the Sacred Precinct, where sacrificial victims could be slain on top of it. It was buried after the conquest, rediscovered in 1790 in the Zócalo (the central plaza of colonial Mexico City), and is now the most famous single object of pre-Columbian art, displayed in the National Museum of Anthropology.

Axayacatl died in 1481, possibly of injuries from the Tlaximaloyan defeat. His successor was his older brother **Tizoc** (TEE-sok, "He Has Drilled Holes"), a weak king whose two campaigns ended in embarrassing failures and whose ill-attended military expeditions returned without enough captives for proper religious obligations. Tizoc was almost certainly assassinated by poison in 1486, after just five years on the throne, in what may have been the only successful coup against a sitting tlatoani in Mexica history. (The poisoning is documented in multiple sources but the details are uncertain. It was probably orchestrated by Tlacaelel, the cihuacoatl, who at this point was in his eighties and had outlasted four kings; the political logic suggests his hand. The Mexica state did not formally acknowledge the assassination, but the speed of Tizoc's replacement and the absence of any official mourning campaign points strongly to the conclusion.) Tizoc did manage to commission one major monument: the **Tizoc Stone**, a massive sacrificial slab carved with 15 conquest scenes showing himself dragging captives by the hair. The propaganda was wildly ahead of his actual accomplishments, but the stone is a beautiful piece of late-Aztec sculpture and it survives.

---

**Ahuitzotl** (ah-WEET-sotl, "Water Beast," r. 1486–1502) was the youngest of the three brothers (Axayacatl and Tizoc both being his elders), and he was the empire's greatest general. He took the throne, by all accounts, with the explicit goal of erasing his brother Tizoc's failures, and he proceeded to do so on a scale that nearly doubled the empire.

Ahuitzotl rededicated the **Phase VII Templo Mayor** in 1487, the four-day ceremony with thousands of sacrificial victims (Chapter 4). He pushed the empire's frontiers in every direction. To the southeast, he conquered the **Gulf Coast Huastec** territories that had rebelled against Motecuzoma I. To the south, he extended Aztec rule deep into Oaxaca, completing the conquest his great-grandfather had begun (Zapotec Civilization Ch 8 covers the receiving end). To the southwest, in 1494, he pushed Aztec armies all the way to the **Tehuantepec** isthmus and onto the **Pacific** coast, conquering the wealthy Soconusco region. Soconusco (Chapter 4, Chapter 6) was the source of the highest-grade cacao in Mesoamerica, and bringing it under direct Aztec administration meant that the Tlatelolco market suddenly had a guaranteed supply of luxury cacao at tribute prices. The cacao trade alone justified the campaign.

Ahuitzotl's reach was, by any measure, the maximum extent of the empire. By 1500, the Aztec Empire covered roughly 80,000 square miles of central and southern Mexico, organized into about 38 tributary provinces, drawing tribute from over 300 conquered towns. The annual tribute (catalogued in detail in the post-conquest **Codex Mendoza**, a pictorial manuscript commissioned around 1541 by the first Spanish viceroy of Mexico — a *viceroy* being the king's personal stand-in for governing a colony — to document the Aztec tribute system the Spanish were inheriting) included over 7,000 tons of maize, beans, and chia seeds; 200,000 mantles of cotton cloth; 32,000 reams of paper; 16,000 rubber balls; 20 lip-plug ornaments of jade; thousands of warrior costumes; tens of thousands of cacao beans (the small-denomination currency); and an extraordinary range of specialized products from each region. The system was the most extensive tribute network in the pre-Columbian Americas, and it ran on a rough division: subject towns owed quarterly or annual deliveries, plus emergency levies during major construction projects or imperial events. The contemporary Inca Empire, building its own continental-scale state at the same time on the other side of the Americas (Andean Kingdoms Ch 8), ran the opposite system: rather than extracting goods, the Inca state extracted *labor* through the **mit'a** rotation, in which subject communities owed periodic stretches of agricultural and construction work to the state. Two empires, neither aware the other existed, had landed on radically different solutions to the same problem of extracting value from a tributary periphery.

The empire was not a unitary state. It was a layered network of subject polities, with wide variation in their relationship to the center. Some tributaries were directly administered by Aztec governors (the **calpixque**, kahl-PEESH-keh, tribute-collectors stationed in major regional centers, who managed the assessment and collection of goods). Others were left under their own kings, who paid tribute and provided military service when called but otherwise governed their own affairs. A few key strategic locations were directly settled by Mexica colonists. The diversity of arrangements is one reason the empire collapsed so quickly: when the center was attacked, many tributaries had no particular reason to defend it, and several actively joined Cortés in 1519 (Chapter 8).

Ahuitzotl's last years were shadowed by the Acuecuexatl flood of 1500 (Chapter 4). He died in 1502, possibly of injuries sustained during the flood. The succession went to his nephew (Axayacatl's son), a quiet, devout, and bookish man named **Motecuzoma II Xocoyotzin** (sho-koh-YOH-tseen, "the Younger" — the suffix distinguishes him from Motecuzoma I). The elders chose him because he was Ahuitzotl's nearest qualified male relative who was not a military disappointment. They got something other than what they expected.

---

**Motecuzoma II** (r. 1502–1520) is one of the more debated figures in 16th-century history. The traditional Spanish-tinted version, which dominated the narrative for centuries, portrays him as superstitious, paralyzed, weak, and ultimately complicit in the empire's destruction. The revisionist version, increasingly favored by modern Mesoamerican scholarship, portrays him as a competent administrator who reorganized the empire, centralized its bureaucracy, expanded its scribal apparatus, and did exactly what most rulers in his situation would have done in the face of an unprecedented and (to him) inexplicable foreign invasion. Both versions probably contain truth, and the more interesting reading is the one that holds them together.

The Motecuzoma II of the first decade of his reign was the most administratively ambitious tlatoani the empire had ever produced. He centralized the bureaucracy, replacing many of Ahuitzotl's provincial governors with men loyal to himself. He expanded the **codex tradition** — the painted books recording tribute, history, ritual, and law — to the point where his scribes effectively built the documentary base on which the post-conquest **Codex Mendoza** would later be drawn. Around 1507, his scribes produced the **Codex Borbonicus**, the most beautiful surviving Aztec calendar codex (Chapter 5), recording the ritual calendar and the New Fire Ceremony of that year in painted detail. He restructured the imperial elite: noble titles that had been awarded loosely under previous kings were standardized, and access to the highest court positions was tightened to keep them within a small group of high-ranking pipiltin. The cumulative move was toward a more centralized, more documented, more legible state — the kind of bureaucratic consolidation that ambitious early-modern monarchs were attempting at the same moment in Europe and Asia. Imperial expansion continued in parallel, though at a slower pace: campaigns into Oaxaca to consolidate Ahuitzotl's gains, the inconclusive Flower War rhythm against Tlaxcala continuing, expeditions onto the southern Pacific coast. The empire reached its maximum extent under his rule, but the rate of new conquests had clearly slowed. The system was hitting the natural limits of pre-modern logistics — the further from Tenochtitlan, the harder a campaign was to supply — and the rewards of expansion were thinning out for the cuauhpilli warrior nobility, the very class that the tightening of court access had also alienated.

The administrative confidence broke, by the testimony of every surviving source, in the second decade of his reign. Aztec sources record **eight omens** in the years 1509–1519: a comet that appeared every night for a year; a temple that caught fire by itself; a lightning strike on another temple in a clear sky; a comet during the day; the lake boiling without cause; a weeping woman heard at night through the streets, calling "my children, my children, where shall I take you?"; a strange bird with a mirror in its head, captured by fishermen and presented to Motecuzoma, in which he saw warriors riding on the backs of unknown beasts; and twin men with two heads who appeared and then disappeared. The omens are recorded primarily in the Florentine Codex, fifty years after the events they describe, and there is no way to separate genuine pre-conquest portents from post-conquest narrative reconstruction. What is consistent across the sources is the shape of Motecuzoma's reaction: the centralizing, documenting, codex-commissioning king of the early reign turned inward. He withdrew. He consulted astrologers and priests with increasing desperation. He spent more time in private chambers and less in the council halls. The same impulse to control and standardize that had served him as an administrator now expressed itself as an obsessive search for the right ritual, the right reading, the right portent that would tell him what was coming.

What was coming, the calendar was helpful enough to underline. The Year One Reed in the **52-year cycle** corresponded to 1519 in our calendar, and according to the legend told and retold for generations, the god **Quetzalcoatl** (Chapter 5) had departed eastward across the sea long ago, vowing to return in a year called One Reed. The legend was older than the Aztecs, present in Toltec tradition, and was probably not understood as a literal political prophecy — more as a cosmological cycle that might or might not turn out to have political significance. Modern scholars reasonably argue that the post-conquest sources amplified the Quetzalcoatl-return narrative to provide a face-saving explanation for why the Aztecs failed to crush Cortés on first contact. Motecuzoma probably did not literally believe Cortés was Quetzalcoatl. But he knew the legend, the year was Year One Reed, and the appearance of strange men with strange beasts on the eastern coast in that specific year would have been deeply unsettling to anyone who could read a calendar. The administrative reformer and the haunted reader of omens were the same man, in sequence, in the same chair.

The post-conquest Spanish accounts of Motecuzoma in 1519 — frozen, indecisive, alternately conciliatory and remote — have been understandably read as the portrait of a weak king. Read alongside the picture of his early reign, they look more like the portrait of a strong administrator whose tools had stopped working: a man who had spent a decade tightening every lever in the imperial machine, only to find that the lever he most urgently needed was not on the board. He responded to Cortés the way he had responded to everything else, by reaching for documentation and consultation and ritual control. Those reflexes, which had built the most centralized bureaucracy in Aztec history, were exactly the wrong reflexes for the moment in front of him. Inca Atahualpa, half a generation later, would receive Pizarro under similar pressure of cosmic uncertainty (Andean Kingdoms Ch 8), with similar paralysis, with the same eventual outcome.

In April 1519, lookouts on the Gulf Coast brought news to Tenochtitlan that men with white skin and beards had landed at Veracruz, riding animals taller than dogs, carrying weapons that spat fire and thunder, accompanied by a Nahua-speaking woman who served as their interpreter. They were marching west. Whatever this was — Quetzalcoatl returning, a foreign embassy, an invasion — it was the largest test the empire had ever faced, and it had arrived in the year the calendar had warned about.

---

# Chapter 8 — The End of the Fifth Sun

In April 1519, **Hernán Cortés** landed near modern Veracruz with about 530 men, sixteen horses, ten cannon, and a small number of large dogs. He was 34 years old, a minor Spanish noble of unimpressive birth, a failed law student, and a regional administrator on Cuba whose patron, the Cuban governor Diego Velázquez, had given him command of an exploratory expedition and then changed his mind and tried to recall him. Cortés sailed anyway, in defiance of orders, and he understood that his political position depended entirely on succeeding spectacularly enough to retroactively justify his insubordination. The 500 men with him were not picked troops; they were a mix of soldiers, sailors, and adventurers, equipped with steel armor and edged weapons, but few of them had any combat experience above small skirmishes. Against the 200,000-soldier Aztec military, on paper they should have lasted weeks at best.

They lasted long enough to win because Cortés understood something Motecuzoma II did not. The Aztec Empire was not a unified state. It was a tributary system extracting goods and warriors from hundreds of subject peoples, many of whom hated it, several of whom were willing to fight on the side of anyone who would help them break free. Cortés's first political move was to land near the **Totonac** city of **Cempoala** (sem-poh-AH-lah). The Totonacs were a non-Nahua people of the central Gulf Coast, ethnically and linguistically distinct from the Mexica, who had been conquered by Motecuzoma I some seventy years earlier (Chapter 3) and had owed Aztec tribute since. Cempoala itself was a recent and uneasy tributary, close enough to the coast to receive the brunt of Aztec tax pressure. The Totonacs hated Aztec tribute collectors. They saw an opportunity. Cortés had Aztec collectors who happened to be in Cempoala arrested in front of the Totonacs (then secretly released them to maintain the option of negotiating with Tenochtitlan), and then declared the Totonacs free of Aztec tribute under his protection. The political message was clear: the Spaniard was offering to break the Aztec system. Within weeks, the Totonacs had pledged thousands of warriors. Cortés had his first allies.

He then **scuttled his own ships** in the harbor at Veracruz, eliminating the option of retreat for his 500 Spaniards, founded the Spanish settlement of **Villa Rica de la Vera Cruz** (the legal jurisdiction that gave him independent authority from Cuba), and marched inland.

---

The march from the coast to Tenochtitlan covers about 200 miles (320 km) over difficult terrain, including the high passes between the volcanoes Popocatépetl and Iztaccíhuatl. Cortés took six months. The reasons were diplomatic and political, not logistical. He needed to convert the 500 Spaniards plus a few thousand Totonac auxiliaries into a force that could realistically take Tenochtitlan, and the only way to do that was to find more allies along the route.

The crucial alliance was with **Tlaxcala**.

The Tlaxcalans, eighty years deep into the **Flower War** with Tenochtitlan (Chapter 3), had cut a particular kind of grim deal with the Aztec Empire. They had remained nominally independent but at the cost of being raided continuously for sacrificial victims, tens of thousands of their men taken to the Templo Mayor over generations. They were surrounded on three sides by Aztec tributaries, salt and cotton and other essentials had been embargoed by the Aztecs for decades, and their accumulated grievances were as deep as any in Mesoamerica. When Cortés appeared on their borders in September 1519, however, they treated him as another invader. The Tlaxcalans first attacked Cortés's column with 30,000 to 50,000 warriors, in some of the most brutal fighting of the entire campaign; the Spaniards held formation by murderous use of their cannon and crossbows, but several Spanish soldiers were killed and more captured for sacrifice. After two weeks of inconclusive battle, Tlaxcalan leaders met to debate. The hawks wanted to destroy the strangers. The doves, led by the elderly senator **Xicotencatl the Elder**, argued that the Spanish were a tool that could be used against Tenochtitlan, and that the Tlaxcalans should ally with them.

The doves won. Tlaxcala became Cortés's most important ally in the conquest, eventually contributing tens of thousands of warriors to the final siege of Tenochtitlan. The Tlaxcalan-Spanish alliance was the single political fact that made the conquest possible. Without Tlaxcala, Cortés's 500 Spaniards would have been cut off and destroyed. With Tlaxcala, the conquest became a civil war within Mesoamerica, with the Spanish providing technological and tactical edge but the bulk of the fighting force being Mesoamerican.

Two other figures from the early phase of the conquest deserve to be named.

**Malintzin** (mah-LEEN-tseen), known to the Spanish as **Doña Marina** and to later Mexican history as **La Malinche**, was a Nahua woman who had been sold into slavery as a child by her own mother (a noblewoman of Coatzacoalcos who, after remarrying, wanted to clear the way for her new husband's children to inherit). She ended up among the Maya of Tabasco, where she learned Yucatec Maya in addition to her native Nahuatl. The Maya gave her, along with nineteen other women, to Cortés as a peace offering after a battle. Cortés, who already had a Spanish-Maya interpreter (a Spanish friar named Jerónimo de Aguilar, who had been shipwrecked in the Yucatán years earlier and had learned Maya in captivity), realized within days what Malintzin's bilingualism meant: he could communicate with any Nahuatl-speaking polity through a two-step translation chain (Spanish to Maya through Aguilar, Maya to Nahuatl through Malintzin). Within months, Malintzin had learned enough Spanish to translate directly. She became Cortés's translator, advisor, lover, and eventually the mother of his son. Her role in the conquest is enormous: she negotiated alliances, decoded political situations, and probably saved Cortés's life multiple times by detecting plots before they unfolded. Modern Mexican opinion of Malintzin has shifted multiple times: from villain (the woman who betrayed her people) to victim (a slave forced into impossible choices) to political agent (a survivor who navigated unprecedented circumstances and shaped them as best she could). All three readings have a basis in the evidence. The Mexican word *malinchismo*, meaning "preference for foreigners over one's own people," derives from her name and is still in use as a political insult.

**Cuitláhuac** (kweet-LAH-wahk, "Excrement Owner," a respectful name in Nahuatl, where bodily references carried no shame), Motecuzoma's brother and the king of Iztapalapa (one of the lake-shore cities), was the principal Aztec hawk in the high command who consistently advised aggressive resistance to the Spanish, was overruled by Motecuzoma II repeatedly, and eventually replaced his brother as tlatoani after Motecuzoma's death (see below). His brief reign would last 80 days.

---

Cortés reached **Cholula** (cho-LOO-lah, the great pilgrimage city to the southeast of Tenochtitlan, with a pyramid larger by volume than the Pyramid of the Sun at Teotihuacan, Teotihuacan Ch 2) in October 1519. Cholula was nominally independent but strongly aligned with Tenochtitlan. Cortés camped in the city and spent several days there. Then, on October 18, 1519, he ordered his troops to massacre several thousand Cholulan nobles and warriors in the temple plaza. The pretext was that Malintzin had warned him of a Cholulan plot to ambush the Spanish at Motecuzoma's order; the existence of the plot is debated by modern historians (some sources strongly suggest it was real, others suggest Cortés invented it as cover for a calculated act of terror). What is clear is that the **Cholula massacre** sent a brutal political signal across central Mexico: the Spanish would respond to even suspected resistance with extreme violence. Tributary cities along Cortés's route that had been hesitating now hurried to declare for him.

On November 8, 1519, **Cortés met Motecuzoma II** on the southern causeway leading into Tenochtitlan. The meeting is one of the most photographed-in-the-imagination encounters in world history. Motecuzoma rode in a litter borne by nobles, dressed in the regalia of a tlatoani (cotton mantles trimmed with gold, gold sandals, a turquoise diadem on his forehead, and rich plumes of quetzal feathers). Cortés was on horseback in steel armor. They exchanged formal greetings through Malintzin. Motecuzoma escorted the Spanish into the city and installed them in the **palace of Axayacatl**, an old royal compound near the Templo Mayor. The decision to admit Cortés to the city, rather than fight him on the causeways, has been called the most consequential single mistake in Mexican history. Motecuzoma's reasoning is unrecoverable; the explanations range from Quetzalcoatl-return paralysis to a calculated decision to neutralize the Spanish threat by bringing it inside the walls where his army was strongest. Whatever his thinking, the decision was catastrophic.

Within two weeks, Cortés had **taken Motecuzoma II hostage** in his own palace. The mechanism was simple intimidation: Cortés visited Motecuzoma with armed soldiers, accused him of complicity in an attack on Spanish coastal positions, and demanded that the tlatoani come live as a guest in the Spanish quarters as proof of good faith. Motecuzoma went. The senior Aztec nobility was divided on what to do; the tlatoani's instructions, transmitted through Cortés's hands, were to maintain calm. For seven months, Cortés effectively ruled the Aztec Empire through Motecuzoma, with tribute being collected normally and the city government continuing under Spanish supervision.

---

Then everything fell apart.

In May 1520, Cortés had to leave the city. A second Spanish expedition, sent from Cuba by Velázquez specifically to arrest Cortés for his earlier insubordination, had landed at Veracruz under the command of **Pánfilo de Narváez**. Cortés took half his force and marched east, defeated Narváez's larger but disorganized army at Cempoala (and absorbed most of Narváez's men into his own command), and headed back to Tenochtitlan.

In his absence, his lieutenant **Pedro de Alvarado** (the red-haired Spaniard the Aztecs nicknamed "Tonatiuh," the Sun) managed the Spanish position in the city. On May 22, 1520, during the festival of **Toxcatl** (the great festival of Tezcatlipoca, Chapter 5, when 600 to 1,000 of the empire's senior nobles and warriors gathered unarmed in the Sacred Precinct for the choreographed dances), Alvarado ordered his troops to seal the gates of the precinct and slaughter every Aztec inside. The **Massacre at the Templo Mayor** killed several hundred to several thousand Aztec nobles in a single afternoon, depending on which source you trust. Alvarado's stated justification was that the Aztecs had been planning an attack on the Spanish; modern historians are skeptical, and the more likely explanation is that Alvarado, with too few men and too many enemies, attempted a preemptive decapitation of the Aztec leadership.

Whatever his reasoning, the result was that the Aztec population finally turned on the Spanish openly. When Cortés returned with his reinforced army, he marched into a city in open revolt. The Aztec command structure had been damaged by the massacre but not destroyed; the surviving nobles selected Motecuzoma's brother **Cuitláhuac** as their war leader, and the entire population of the city had risen to fight. Cortés tried to use Motecuzoma to calm the crowds; according to Spanish sources, the captured tlatoani went to a balcony of the Spanish quarters and tried to address his people, who responded by hurling stones and arrows at him. Motecuzoma was struck and died of his wounds three days later, on June 29, 1520. (Aztec sources give a different account: that Motecuzoma was murdered by the Spanish when his usefulness ended. The truth is contested; the Spanish version is uncorroborated, but the projectile-strike account is the more economical reconstruction of events. Either way, the empire's tlatoani was dead at Spanish hands, in custody, in the middle of his own city.)

With Motecuzoma's death, Cortés's leverage over the city evaporated. The Spanish position became militarily impossible. They were 500-plus men in a city of 200,000 that had finally and decisively turned hostile.

---

On the night of **June 30 – July 1, 1520**, Cortés ordered a desperate retreat from Tenochtitlan, west along the Tlacopan causeway. The Aztecs had pulled out the wooden bridges; the Spanish had built portable wooden bridges of their own to span the gaps. The Spanish withdrew through driving rain in the dark, weighed down by the gold and treasures they had stolen during their seven-month occupation (Cortés had ordered that any soldier could carry as much gold as he could manage; many soldiers stuffed their armor and clothing with gold ingots, an act of greed that would prove fatal when they had to swim or fight). The Aztecs attacked from canoes on both sides of the causeway. Spanish soldiers, weighed down by gold, fell into the water and drowned. Spanish prisoners taken alive were sacrificed at the Templo Mayor over the following days.

The night is remembered in Spanish history as **La Noche Triste** ("The Sorrowful Night," from a tradition that Cortés sat under a tree on the western shore at dawn and wept for his dead). The Spanish lost about 600 of the roughly 1,300 Spanish soldiers (now reinforced by Narváez's men), thousands of their indigenous allies, and almost all of their cannon, gunpowder, and gold. Several days later, the Aztecs hauled drums up to the Templo Mayor and beat them while priests sacrificed the captured Spanish soldiers, in full view of the surviving Spanish on the lake shore. Bernal Díaz, who was there, never forgot the sound of the drums; he wrote about them decades later as the most terrible noise he had ever heard.

It was, in conventional military terms, a Spanish disaster. Half the European force had been destroyed in a single night. The Aztecs had retaken their city. The empire still held its tributary network. The Tlaxcalan alliance was untested by serious defeat. Any reasonable observer in July 1520 would have concluded that Cortés's gamble had failed, that the Spanish should withdraw to Veracruz and accept the loss, and that the Aztec Empire would survive. Bernardino de Sahagún would write later that this was the moment when the empire could have been saved.

It wasn't.

---

The Spanish retreat to Tlaxcala took two weeks of hard fighting. They were down to about 400 Europeans and a few thousand Tlaxcalan auxiliaries. Aztec forces under Cuitláhuac followed them, attacking as they could. At the **Battle of Otumba** in July 1520, Cortés's depleted force met an Aztec army roughly 40,000 strong on an open plain. Cortés's cavalry, the few remaining horses he had managed to bring out of Tenochtitlan, broke the Aztec center by killing the Aztec commander and capturing his battle standard (the loss of the standard, in Mesoamerican military convention, signaled defeat regardless of the actual military situation). The Aztec army withdrew. The Spanish reached Tlaxcala alive.

And then the empire's other enemy arrived.

**Smallpox**, a disease the Americas had never seen before, was reportedly introduced to the Mexican mainland in April 1520 by an African slave who came over with the Narváez expedition; the chronicles name him **Francisco de Eguía**, though the patient-zero attribution to a single individual is contested by modern historians and was probably a tidied-up account constructed after the fact. What is not in doubt is that smallpox arrived with the Narváez landing. The virus, against which indigenous populations had no immunity, spread westward through central Mexico through summer and autumn 1520, reaching Tenochtitlan during the post-Noche-Triste recovery period. The Aztec sources describe it as **huey zahuatl** ("great rash"), a disease that had no name in Nahuatl because nothing like it had ever existed in the Americas. The mortality was catastrophic. Estimates of population loss in central Mexico from this single 1520 epidemic range from 25 percent to 50 percent. The disease did not discriminate by class or military function: peasants died, nobles died, priests died, generals died, the tlatoani himself died. **Cuitláhuac**, who had ruled for only 80 days, died of smallpox in late November 1520, ending one of the most brilliantly led military campaigns in indigenous American history before he could finish the job he had started.

The empire's command structure was wrecked. The military leadership, the administrative class, the priesthood, the experienced officers, all of them had been disproportionately exposed during the epidemic and disproportionately killed. The Aztec sources describe villages where there were not enough living people to bury the dead, and corpses were pulled from houses and dumped into mass graves or left where they lay. Tenochtitlan, a city of 200,000 that depended on a complex labor and food-distribution apparatus, faced food shortages because too many of the people who ran the system were dying.

Cuitláhuac was succeeded as tlatoani by his cousin **Cuauhtémoc** (kwah-oo-TEH-mok, "Descending Eagle"), the last tlatoani, a young man (probably about 25) who had survived the epidemic and who was determined to fight for the city. By his accession, the world was already largely lost.

---

In Tlaxcala, Cortés rebuilt his army. He drew on Tlaxcalan auxiliaries (perhaps 50,000 to 100,000), additional reinforcements from Cuba, fresh gunpowder and cannon, and (most consequentially) thirteen **brigantines** (small sailing-and-oared warships) that he had ordered built in Tlaxcala from the timbers of his scuttled coastal ships, transported overland in pieces, and reassembled on the lakeshore. The brigantines were the first European warships ever launched on a New World inland lake, and they would be decisive. They allowed the Spanish to control Lake Texcoco, cut off canoe traffic in and out of the city, and bombard the causeways from positions the Aztecs could not reach.

The **Siege of Tenochtitlan** began on May 26, 1521, with about 700 Spanish soldiers and somewhere between 75,000 and 200,000 indigenous allies (Tlaxcalans, Texcocans, Totonacs, and a growing number of formerly subject Aztec tributaries who saw which way the war was going). The Spanish cut the Chapultepec aqueduct, eliminating Tenochtitlan's main freshwater supply. They blockaded the causeways. They began grinding through the city quarter by quarter, fighting through canals and over rooftops, demolishing buildings as they advanced (so that the Aztec roof-jumping defensive tactics would have no platforms). The Aztecs fought brilliantly, as they always had. They sacrificed captured Spanish soldiers atop the Templo Mayor in full view of the besieging army, hauling captives up the steps and removing their hearts in plain sight, then rolling the bodies down the steps onto the Coyolxauhqui Stone. Spanish soldiers in the besieging army watched their comrades die this way and described it as the most terrible thing they had ever seen.

But the city was starving. Smallpox was killing more defenders than Spanish weapons were. The supply of fresh water was gone. The chinampas were cut off from access. After 75 days of siege, on August 13, 1521, **Cuauhtémoc** attempted to escape the city by canoe with a small group of nobles and family. He was intercepted by a Spanish brigantine and captured. The same day, the city formally surrendered.

The Aztec sources describe what was left as "a city of corpses." The most famous Aztec lament for the fall of the city, recorded in the **Cantares Mexicanos** (the early colonial-era anthology of Nahuatl poetry), reads in part: "Broken spears lie in the roads / We have torn our hair in our grief / The houses are roofless now, and their walls / are red with blood."

Cuauhtémoc was tortured by Cortés (the Spanish poured boiling oil on his feet) to make him reveal the location of Tenochtitlan's gold treasury. According to the Aztec tradition, Cuauhtémoc bore the torture without breaking; according to the Spanish tradition, he revealed nothing because there was less treasure than the Spanish had hoped for. He was kept alive as a puppet for several years and finally executed in 1525 during Cortés's expedition to Honduras, on charges of plotting rebellion. Modern Mexico considers him a national hero, and his statue stands at the head of one of Mexico City's main avenues.

The empire was over. Tenochtitlan was a ruin. The Spanish founded **Mexico City** on top of it, deliberately demolishing the temples and using the stones to build the cathedral and the colonial palaces. The Templo Mayor was buried beneath the colonial central plaza (the Zócalo), where it lay for 450 years until the 1978 rediscovery of the Coyolxauhqui Stone (Chapter 3). The chinampas were drained over the next century, the lake itself progressively reduced and finally eliminated by 19th-century drainage projects, and the basin floor that had once held one of the largest cities in the world became one of the largest cities in the world again, in an entirely different form.

---

What survived?

The Nahuatl language survived. Over a million people in modern Mexico still speak it as a first language; it is one of Mexico's official indigenous languages. Many Spanish words come from Nahuatl, and from Nahuatl into English: avocado (ahuacatl), chocolate (xocolatl), tomato (tomatl), chili (chilli), coyote (coyotl), peyote (peyotl), tequila (from a place name), Mexico itself (Mexihco). The Mexican flag still carries the eagle on the cactus devouring the snake, the Mexica's founding sign.

The chinampas survived in fragmentary form at Xochimilco, where farmers still maintain a network of canals and floating gardens that look essentially as they did in 1500, and which is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The codex tradition partially survived. Most pre-conquest pictorial books were destroyed in the conquest or in early colonial book-burnings (the Spanish friars considered them devil's works), but a handful (the Codex Borbonicus, the Codex Borgia, the Codex Mendoza, the Codex Florentine, perhaps two dozen others) survived in libraries in Europe and Mexico. Combined with the post-conquest manuscripts compiled by Sahagún, by Aztec elders working in the colonial period, and by Spanish chroniclers, they preserve a fraction of pre-conquest Aztec knowledge — far less than what was lost, but more than survives for any other indigenous American civilization except the Maya.

The medical tradition survived in fragments. The **Badianus Manuscript**, written in 1552 by an Aztec physician trained at the colonial Colegio de Santa Cruz de Tlatelolco and translated into Latin by another graduate, preserves illustrated descriptions of pre-conquest Aztec herbal medicine. It is the oldest medical text from the Americas. Aztec medicine was, in many areas, more advanced than European medicine of the same period — botanical pharmacology in particular, where the Aztec **ticitl** healers maintained working knowledge of over a thousand medicinal plants.

The poetry survived. The Cantares Mexicanos and the Romances de los Señores de la Nueva España preserve Nahuatl poetry from before and immediately after the conquest, including Nezahualcoyotl's poems and the great laments for the fall of Tenochtitlan.

The genetic legacy survived: most modern Mexicans descend from the Mexica and their neighbors, and the Spanish-Indigenous mestizo population that emerged in the colonial period now numbers over 100 million people across Mexico and the United States.

What did not survive was the political fact of an indigenous Mesoamerican imperial state, the religious cycle of public sacrifice, the calendrical apparatus that organized cosmic time, and the trade networks that had connected central Mexico to the Pacific cacao coast and the American Southwest. These were destroyed deliberately by the Spanish authorities in the decades after the conquest, and, less directly, by the demographic catastrophe that followed European contact: the population of central Mexico fell from perhaps 12 million in 1519 to roughly 1 million by 1605, a loss greater than the Black Death in any European country. The empire was destroyed by smallpox, by Tlaxcala, by Cortés, and by Motecuzoma's hesitations, but the civilization beneath the empire was destroyed by the diseases that came with Cortés's ships.

---

A final cross-civ check. The fall of Tenochtitlan in 1521 sits in the same century as the Protestant Reformation (Luther's 95 Theses, 1517), the rise of the Ottoman Empire under Suleiman the Magnificent (succeeded 1520), the Mughal conquest of India (Babur's victory at Panipat, 1526), the consolidation of the Ming Dynasty's mid-period in China, the late Inca expansion (Andean Kingdoms Ch 8 — the Inca Empire was at its peak when Pizarro arrived in 1532, eleven years after Tenochtitlan's fall, and would be destroyed by the same combination of factors), and the European Renaissance entering its mature phase. A related but distinct version played out in the eastern woodlands of North America, where Hernando de Soto's entrada of 1539–43 carried epidemic disease into the still-functioning Mississippian chiefdoms (Mississippian Culture Ch 9). The early 16th century was a global hinge: the closing of the Old World's isolated regional histories, the beginning of an integrated global system. The Aztec Empire, which had risen from a vassal city-state to one of the largest and most sophisticated states in the world in less than two centuries, was caught at the moment of its greatest extent by the first wave of European-American contact, and it did not survive the encounter. Neither did most of the populations that the empire had ruled. The conquest was not the Spaniards' achievement alone; it was a catastrophic intersection of microbial biology, internal Mesoamerican politics, technological mismatch, and individual human decisions, and any of those factors changed in another direction would have changed the outcome. But once the encounter had begun, no realistic alternative outcome would have left the empire intact in its 1519 form. The Fifth Sun set in 1521, and the world that came after was a different world.

The Mexica had told themselves, generation after generation, that the cosmos required regular blood to keep functioning. They were not, by the standard of all the previous cosmic ages they remembered, wrong. The cosmos had ended four times before. It would, they knew, end again. They had hoped, and labored, and sacrificed, to push that ending as far away as they could.

It came in the year One Reed. They had had advance warning of that for centuries. They were prepared for it as well as they could be. It still ended them.

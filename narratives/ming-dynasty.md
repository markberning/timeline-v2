# Ming Dynasty — The Han Restoration

**Goal:** Narrative for the Ming dynasty (1368–1644 CE), the 276-year Han Chinese restoration after a century of Mongol Yuan rule. The Ming was founded by Zhu Yuanzhang — the only Chinese founding emperor of pure peasant birth in the entire imperial period — who took the reign name Hongwu and built one of the most centralized monarchies in pre-modern world history. His son the Yongle Emperor moved the capital to Beijing, built the Forbidden City, and dispatched Admiral Zheng He on seven gigantic naval expeditions that reached the East African coast ninety years before Vasco da Gama. The middle Ming was an age of eunuch power and the catastrophic Tumu Crisis. The late Ming produced the Single Whip silver tax reform that tied China to global silver flows, the Imjin War defending Korea against Hideyoshi, the four great vernacular novels, the philosophical school of Wang Yangming, and Matteo Ricci at the Beijing court. The Chongzhen Emperor hanged himself on Coal Hill behind the Forbidden City in April 1644 as Li Zicheng's rebel army poured into Beijing; six weeks later the Ming general Wu Sangui let the Manchus through Shanhaiguan Pass, and the Qing dynasty was founded. Ming legacies — Beijing as the imperial capital, the Forbidden City, the Great Wall in the form tourists visit today, the four great novels, blue-and-white porcelain — are the cultural template against which every later Chinese regime measured itself.

A note on names and pronunciation. Ming emperors are usually referred to by reign name (Hongwu, Yongle, Wanli) rather than personal name; this is the standard practice in English-language Ming historiography. Chinese names use modern pinyin transliteration. Pronunciations are given on first use for the unfamiliar ones.

A note on "first" claims. The Ming inherited a thousand-year-old Chinese imperial tradition with detailed bureaucratic record-keeping. As with every TL in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of." Ming sources are unusually rich because the dynasty maintained continuous archives in Beijing for 276 years — but those archives were heavily edited by Qing court historians compiling the official Ming Shi (1739), and the late-Ming chaos destroyed much that the Qing did not bother to preserve.

# Chapter 1 — Hongwu's Founding

The Ming dynasty was founded by a man who began life as the youngest son of a destitute tenant farmer in Anhui province, was orphaned at sixteen by famine, spent four years as a begging Buddhist monk, and joined a peasant rebellion in his twenties because the local Yuan officials were threatening to burn down the monastery that had sheltered him.

That man was **Zhu Yuanzhang** (joo yu-ahn-JAHNG), and his arc from peasant orphan to founder of one of the longest-lived Chinese dynasties is one of the most extreme social mobility trajectories in pre-modern world history. Yuan-Dynasty Ch 8 covers his rise during the Red Turban rebellions and the campaigns of the 1350s and 1360s in detail; this chapter picks up where that one ends, on the morning after the Mongol Yuan court fled Khanbaliq in September 1368 and Zhu Yuanzhang's general Xu Da rode into the abandoned capital of China without opposition.

What Zhu Yuanzhang built over the following thirty years — until his death in 1398 — is one of the most idiosyncratic and ferocious autocracies in the entire imperial period. The institutional shape of the Ming, set in the founder's first generation, would constrain every successor down to the Chongzhen Emperor's suicide in 1644. Some of those institutions worked brilliantly. Some were catastrophically inflexible. All of them carried the unmistakable mark of the founder's experience as a starving child watching his parents die of famine in a Yuan-administered village.

This is the chapter about the founding generation.

---

A pause first, because the founder needs introducing properly.

Zhu Yuanzhang took the reign name **Hongwu** (HONG-woo, "Vastly Martial") when he proclaimed the dynasty at Nanjing in January 1368. By convention he is referred to as the **Hongwu Emperor** rather than by his personal name in subsequent contexts; this is standard for Ming emperors, who are almost always referenced by reign name. He was forty years old at the proclamation, having spent the previous sixteen years as a Red Turban officer, regional warlord, and finally the dominant rebel power in central and southern China after defeating his rivals at the Battle of Lake Poyang in 1363 and the siege of Suzhou in 1367 (Yuan Dynasty Ch 8).

Hongwu was, by all accounts, a physically unprepossessing man. The famous court portrait shows a long, asymmetrical face with a protruding chin and pockmarks; he was reportedly self-conscious about his appearance and executed several court painters whose portraits flattered him too obviously or not enough. He was also illiterate when he joined the Red Turbans — he learned to read as an adult, became reasonably literate by the standards of his class, but never reached the elite literary culture of the Confucian scholar-officials he would spend his reign both relying on and slaughtering. His personal correspondence survives in considerable quantity, and the prose is direct, blunt, and frequently violent in tone — the voice of a man who had clawed his way from begging-monk to emperor and who never forgot which side of that transition he had started on.

The reign that begins in 1368 must be understood as the work of someone who had personally watched the Yuan administrative system fail. He had seen his parents die of famine because the Yellow River floods of the 1340s had collapsed the agricultural base while the Yuan tax-collectors continued demanding their grain (Yuan Dynasty Ch 7). He had watched local Yuan officials condemn villagers on tortured testimony. He had spent four years walking through famine-stricken countryside as a begging Buddhist novice, sleeping in temple porches and roadside ditches. The Ming administrative system he built was designed by someone who had stood on the receiving end of administrative failure for the formative years of his life.

What that designer wanted, above all, was a state that would never again let the things he had survived as a child happen to anyone. What he built was a state that prevented those things by making the entire imperial system answer directly to the emperor's personal supervision — and that, when the emperor was someone other than Hongwu, did not work nearly as well.

---

The 1368 expulsion of the Mongols deserves a paragraph of its own.

When Xu Da's Ming army approached Khanbaliq in the late summer of 1368, the Yuan emperor **Toghon Temur** (in Chinese reign-name, Shundi) made no serious attempt to organize a defense. The Mongol nobility had been evacuating their families and treasures north into the steppe for weeks. The keshig (the Mongol imperial bodyguard) was understrength and demoralized. The Han Chinese garrison troops had no particular reason to fight for a foreign dynasty whose collapse had been visibly underway for a decade. On the night of September 14, 1368 — the date varies slightly across sources — Toghon Temur abandoned the imperial palace with his court and what treasure could be loaded onto wagons, and rode north through the Juyong Pass into Mongolia. The next morning, Xu Da's army entered the city without opposition.

For the first time since the Mongol conquest of the Southern Song in 1279 (Tang-Song China Ch 9 and Yuan Dynasty Ch 1), all of China was under Han Chinese rule. The dynasty that had ruled it — the Yuan — was, in formal terms, finished. In practice, the Yuan court that fled to Mongolia continued to exist as the **Northern Yuan** for roughly the next two centuries, periodically threatening the Ming frontier and occasionally invading; but it never recovered control of the Chinese provinces. The 1368 expulsion is the date that subsequent Chinese tradition treats as the end of the Mongol period, and it is the founding event of the Ming.

Hongwu's proclamation explicitly framed the new dynasty as the restoration of Han rule against the foreign occupiers. The four-class ethnic system that had placed Han Chinese at the bottom of Yuan legal hierarchy (Yuan Dynasty Ch 2) was abolished. The Confucian civil-service examinations that the Yuan had suspended were restored. The Mongol legal code was repudiated. Tibetan Buddhism was demoted from court religion back to an officially tolerated minor faith. The imperial seals, the court robes, the ritual calendar, the official histories — every visible apparatus of Chinese imperial governance was deliberately re-Sinified.

But the Ming was not simply the restoration of the Tang or Song. It was something new: a more centralized, more autocratic, more rural-focused state than any of its predecessors. The differences were the founder's contribution.

---

The first major institutional change came with the **Hu Weiyong purge** of 1380.

**Hu Weiyong** (HOO way-YONG) had been Hongwu's Chief Minister (Zhongshu Sheng) since 1377, the senior official of the regular bureaucracy and the link between the emperor and the rest of the government. In 1380, Hongwu accused him of plotting rebellion in collusion with Japanese, Mongol, and Vietnamese contacts. Hu Weiyong was executed along with his entire extended family. Over the following decade, the investigation expanded to encompass an estimated 30,000 alleged accomplices — officials, clerks, scholars, military officers, even commoners with peripheral connections — most of whom were also executed.

The Hu Weiyong purge was not in itself unprecedented in Chinese history. Founder-emperors had purged dangerous subordinates before. What was unprecedented was Hongwu's response to the purge: he abolished the office of Chief Minister entirely, and the Central Secretariat (Zhongshu Sheng) along with it. The six Ministries (Personnel, Revenue, Rites, War, Justice, Public Works) that had previously reported to the Chief Minister now reported directly to the emperor himself. The intermediate layer of senior administrative coordination, which every Chinese dynasty since the Han had operated, was simply removed.

The consequences were enormous. The emperor took on an impossible administrative workload — Hongwu personally reviewed something like 1,600 memorials a day in the years immediately after the abolition, by his own grumbling testimony. The system worked while it was Hongwu doing the work, because Hongwu was an obsessive workaholic with thirty years of experience in administrative management; it began to fail almost immediately under his successors, who were not. The eventual workaround — the rise of the **Grand Secretariat** (Neige), an informal body of secretaries who unofficially took on the Chief Minister's coordinating function — emerged in the early fifteenth century but never had the formal authority that the abolished Central Secretariat had possessed. The Ming emperor was either a workaholic micromanager or a roadblock; there was no institutional middle ground.

The decision to abolish the Chief Minister's office was the single most consequential structural choice of the Ming founding. It concentrated power in the throne to a degree unmatched in any earlier Chinese dynasty. It also concentrated risk: when the throne was occupied by an incompetent, indolent, or insane emperor, the entire administrative system suffered for decades at a stretch with no institutional remedy. The middle and late Ming would test this design under almost every imaginable stress, and the results would be uneven at best.

The Hu Weiyong purge was followed by a second round in 1393, the **Lan Yu purge**, in which Hongwu's most successful military commander **Lan Yu** (lahn YOO) was executed along with an estimated 15,000 alleged accomplices. The Lan Yu purge eliminated almost all the surviving senior generals of the Ming founding, leaving the army in the hands of the imperial princes (Hongwu's adult sons, scattered across the northern frontier as regional commanders). The strategy was to remove any potential military threat before Hongwu's death; the consequence — as the next chapter will show — was that the only surviving military power blocs after Hongwu's death were the imperial princes themselves, and one of them would shortly use his army to overthrow the legitimate succession.

---

The administrative architecture Hongwu built around the abolished Chief Minister deserves a closer look.

The **lijia** (LEE-jah) system, formalized in 1381, was the centerpiece of Ming rural administration. Every 110 households were grouped into a li, with the ten wealthiest serving rotating annual leadership terms responsible for tax collection, conscription quotas, security, and the maintenance of household registers. Each household's occupation was registered as hereditary — military, civilian, artisan, salt-producer — and movement out of one's registered occupation required formal permission. The system was designed to administer rural China through volunteer-rotating local elites without the cost of an extensive paid bureaucracy.

The lijia was a remarkable institutional achievement and an extraordinarily restrictive one. It tied Chinese peasants to their villages, codified their labor obligations, and put the burden of tax assessment on local notables who knew their neighbors' affairs intimately. It worked reasonably well in Hongwu's lifetime, when the founder personally policed the system through inspectors and brutal punishments for malfeasance. It decayed steadily over generations as wealthy households evaded service, registers fell out of date, and the rotating headships were increasingly imposed on impoverished households who could not refuse them.

The **Hongwu Code** (Da Ming Lu), promulgated in its final form in 1397, was the most comprehensive Chinese legal code since the Tang Code of 653. It governed the Ming legal system for the entire dynasty and provided the template for the Qing Code that succeeded it. It was also exceptionally harsh by the standards of Chinese legal tradition. Hongwu's personal Grand Pronouncements (Da Gao), supplementing the Code, legalized flaying alive for corruption above 60 ounces of silver — and explicitly stated that the punishment was to be administered to the corrupt official in his own former office, with the resulting skin stuffed with straw and displayed as a warning to the official's successor. Modern historians debate how often this particular punishment was actually carried out (the surviving evidence is thin), but the fact that it was inscribed in formal law tells the reader something about the founder's attitude toward bureaucratic accountability.

The **weisuo** (WAY-swoh, "garrison") military system reorganized the Ming army into hereditary military households assigned to permanent garrisons across the country. Each garrison was supplied with farmland on which the soldiers worked when not training, theoretically making the army self-supporting. The institutional logic was to prevent the warlordism that had destroyed the Tang's fubing system after the An Lushan Rebellion (Tang-Song China Ch 4) and to spare the Ming treasury the cost of paying a professional standing army. Like the lijia, the weisuo worked initially and decayed steadily. By the Tumu Crisis of 1449 (Chapter 4), the Ming would discover that its hereditary military households could not actually field a competent army.

The **Nine Frontier Garrisons** (Jiu Bian) organized the northern military command into nine major military districts stretching from Liaoning in the east to Gansu in the west, each based at a fortified town with a substantial standing army. The Nine Frontier system was the institutional basis on which later Ming emperors would conduct the massive Great Wall reconstruction project (Chapter 4). It was also one of the few Hongwu-era institutions that genuinely functioned through the entire dynasty — northern frontier defense was the one area where the Ming consistently invested actual resources at scale, and the institutional infrastructure scaled accordingly.

---

The most consequential of Hongwu's institutional decisions, in retrospect, was one he explicitly tried to prevent.

Hongwu officially distrusted **eunuchs** with a passion. He had inscribed an iron tablet at the entrance to the imperial palace forbidding eunuchs from holding political office; he forbade them from learning to read; he limited the number employed at court to roughly 100. The model he was trying to avoid was the late Tang and the late Han, both of which had been progressively destabilized by powerful palace eunuch factions (Han Dynasty Ch 8 and Tang-Song China Ch 4 both cover the role of court eunuchs in dynastic decline).

Despite the prohibitions, Hongwu also created the Twenty-Four Departments of the imperial household staff — the inner palace administrative offices, all of which were inevitably staffed by eunuchs because their function was to operate inside the imperial residence where adult men with intact reproductive capacity could not be allowed. The Twenty-Four Departments were the institutional seed of what would, within a generation of Hongwu's death, become a parallel administration that rivaled and often exceeded the regular Confucian bureaucracy in real power. The **Directorate of Ceremonial** (Si Li Jian, sih LEE jyahn) eventually controlled the flow of memorials between the outer government and the emperor — meaning that the eunuchs decided which documents the emperor saw and which were quietly filed without action. The **Eastern Depot** (Dong Chang, dong CHAHNG) ran an internal secret police force answerable only to the emperor through eunuch intermediaries. By the Tumu Crisis (Chapter 4), eunuchs would be commanding armies in the field; by the Wei Zhongxian period (Chapter 8), a single eunuch would be running the empire while a literate emperor played at carpentry.

The pattern is one of Hongwu's most striking institutional failures: he saw the eunuch problem clearly enough to inscribe a warning in iron, then created the administrative structures that made the eunuch problem inevitable. The institutional logic was unavoidable — once the Chief Minister had been abolished and the emperor had become the sole coordinator of a vast administrative apparatus, the eunuchs who controlled access to the emperor's person became the indispensable filter through which the rest of the system operated. Hongwu himself could do without them because he insisted on personally reading every memorial; his successors could not. The iron tablet was reportedly removed (or simply ignored) by the Yongle Emperor within a few years of Hongwu's death.

---

A pause, on the founder's character.

Hongwu's reign is remembered in Chinese tradition as one of the most cruel and one of the most administratively effective in the imperial period. Both characterizations are accurate. He purged tens of thousands of officials. He micromanaged the empire with obsessive personal attention. He executed his own former Red Turban comrades, his own daughters' husbands, his own grandchildren on suspicion of plotting. He invented bureaucratic punishments — the imperial caning (tingzhang) administered in open court, the public flaying for corruption — that subsequent Ming emperors continued to use. His paranoia was constant and lethal, accelerating dramatically after the death of his beloved wife Empress Ma in 1382, who had reportedly served for years as the moderating voice against his worst impulses.

He was also one of the most agriculturally successful early Ming-era rulers. Population recovered from the Yuan-era catastrophes; agricultural production expanded; the rural infrastructure was rebuilt. The Yellow River was contained, for a generation, by serious investment in dikes and conservancy. The state granaries were kept full. Famine relief was managed competently. Peasants in Hongwu-era China were poorer and more restricted than peasants in Tang or Song China had been, but they were also less likely to die in a state-aggravated famine. By the standards of the immediate Yuan-Ming transition — when perhaps a third of the population had died in the rebellions and famines of the 1340s and 1350s (Yuan Dynasty Ch 7) — the Hongwu reign was a decisive recovery.

The contradiction is the founder's signature. Hongwu cared, at a deep visceral level, about the survival of the rural population from which he had emerged. He also distrusted, at an equally deep level, the Confucian scholar-officials whose participation he needed to actually administer that population. The result was an autocracy in which the emperor was personally engaged with rural well-being while simultaneously executing the bureaucrats who tried to implement his policies. The system worked because Hongwu was capable of holding both ends of the contradiction in his own person. The system failed his successors because most of them were not.

When Hongwu died in 1398 at age sixty-nine, the throne passed not to one of his surviving adult sons but to his eldest grandson — the **Jianwen Emperor** (jyahn-WEN), aged twenty-one, son of the crown prince who had predeceased Hongwu in 1392. Jianwen was a serious young man with a Confucian education, distrusted his uncles (the imperial princes commanding the northern frontier armies), and immediately began trying to demote them. The most powerful of those uncles — **Zhu Di** (joo DEE), the Prince of Yan, based at Beiping (modern Beijing) and commanding the front-line army against the Mongols — refused to accept his demotion. The civil war that followed is the subject of the next chapter.

The dynasty's first thirty years had been the founder's. The next twenty would belong to a usurper who reshaped what the founder had built.

Cross-civ check: Hongwu's reign (1368–1398) overlaps with the **late medieval crisis** in Europe — the Hundred Years' War's middle phase, the Avignon and Roman Papacies of the Western Schism beginning 1378, the Peasants' Revolt in England (1381), the plague-driven population collapse that had killed perhaps a third of Europeans in the late 1340s. The **Ottoman Empire** (Ottoman Empire Ch 2) was consolidating its control of the Balkans under Murad I; the Ottoman victory at the Battle of Kosovo came in 1389. In Persia, **Timur** (the Turco-Mongol warlord, Mongol Empire Ch 7) was building his catastrophic empire — Hongwu and Timur were near-exact contemporaries, and Timur was actively planning an invasion of Ming China when he died in 1405. In **Mali** the empire was still riding the wealth that Mansa Musa had accumulated half a century earlier. In the **Andes**, the **Inca** were just beginning their imperial expansion under Pachacuti. The Ming founding was the eastern end of a wave of state-rebuilding that swept across post-plague Eurasia in the late fourteenth century — old orders had collapsed almost everywhere, and new dynasties were everywhere reorganizing the wreckage.

# Chapter 2 — Yongle and the Forbidden City

Hongwu died on June 24, 1398, in the imperial palace at Nanjing. His grandson the Jianwen Emperor succeeded him at age twenty-one. The dynasty was thirty years old, militarily dominant, financially solvent, and apparently stable.

Within four years, the throne had been seized by a usurper, the imperial palace at Nanjing had burned, the legitimate emperor had vanished — possibly killed in the fire, possibly escaped in the disguise of a Buddhist monk, in either case officially unaccounted for ever after — and one of the most consequential reigns in Chinese imperial history had begun.

The usurper was **Zhu Di** (joo DEE), Hongwu's fourth son, the Prince of Yan, the regional military commander on the Mongol frontier. He took the reign name **Yongle** (YONG-leh, "Perpetual Happiness"). He ruled for twenty-two years, until 1424. He moved the capital from Nanjing to Beijing, built the Forbidden City, commissioned the largest reference work in pre-modern human history, dispatched seven gigantic naval expeditions across the Indian Ocean to East Africa (Chapter 3), led five personal campaigns into the Mongolian steppe, and reasserted Chinese imperial power on a scale not seen since the High Tang seven centuries earlier.

He was also the most contested figure in Ming historiography, because the way he came to power was an act of aggression against his own family, and because the methods he used to consolidate power involved the systematic execution of officials loyal to his nephew. The Yongle Emperor's reign is the early-modern Chinese case study of the question that ancient and medieval historians from Tacitus to Ibn Khaldun debated endlessly: can a regime founded on naked usurpation produce great and lasting public works? In Yongle's case the answer is yes, with conditions.

This is the chapter about the usurper.

---

The civil war that brought Yongle to power was called the **Jingnan Campaign** (jing-NAHN, "Pacification of the Difficulties"). It lasted four years, from 1399 to 1402.

The trigger was Jianwen's attempt to reduce the power of the imperial princes. Hongwu had distributed his adult sons across the northern frontier as regional military commanders — partly to give them productive roles, partly to hold the Mongol frontier without depending on Han Chinese generals (the Lan Yu purge of 1393 had eliminated the most successful generals of Hongwu's reign, leaving the princes as the surviving military power blocs). By the time Hongwu died, his fourth son the Prince of Yan held the most strategically important command, the front-line army facing the Mongols at Beiping. His other surviving brothers held lesser commands at Datong, Xi'an, Kaifeng, and Chengdu.

Jianwen, advised by Confucian scholars who saw the imperial princes as a structural threat to civilian rule, demoted three of his uncles in the first year of his reign and demanded that the others surrender troops and territory. Zhu Di refused. He gathered his army, declared that he was rescuing the emperor from corrupt advisors (the standard formula for Chinese rebellion under the legitimate-emperor framework), and marched south. The Jingnan Campaign was the four-year war between his veteran northern army and the Jianwen government's larger but less experienced central forces.

The military details are not the point. What matters is that Yongle won, partly because his northern army was hardened by years of Mongol warfare while Jianwen's troops were not, partly because key central commanders defected, partly because Yongle's own generalship was substantially better than anything the Jianwen regime could field. In July 1402, his army crossed the Yangtze and entered Nanjing. The imperial palace caught fire in the chaos; the Jianwen Emperor's body was never identified; rumors persisted for centuries that he had escaped disguised as a Buddhist monk. Yongle proclaimed himself emperor on July 17, 1402, and reigned as the Yongle Emperor until his death in 1424.

The post-victory consolidation was as brutal as anything Hongwu had managed. Yongle ordered the systematic execution of officials who had served Jianwen — most notoriously the scholar **Fang Xiaoru** (fahng shyow-ROO), a leading Confucian moralist who refused to draft the proclamation legitimizing the new reign. Yongle reportedly threatened to kill Fang's nine clans (the standard Chinese euphemism for "all your relatives"); Fang reportedly answered that Yongle could kill ten clans and he would still refuse. Yongle took him at his word: Fang Xiaoru, his nine clans, and a tenth clan made up of his students were all executed, an estimated 873 people in total. The episode entered Chinese moral memory as the foundational example of Confucian loyalty in the face of usurpation.

But once the consolidation was complete, the new emperor began to build.

---

The first project was the **relocation of the capital** from Nanjing to Beijing.

Beijing — known in Yuan times as **Khanbaliq** (kahn-BAH-lik) and in Hongwu's day as **Beiping** (bay-PING, "Northern Pacified") — had been Yongle's personal power base since the 1380s. It was strategically positioned to monitor the Mongol frontier (the northern border ran roughly along the line of the present Great Wall, only about 50 miles north of the city). It was the city where Yongle's veteran army had been raised, where his own household was based, and where his political legitimacy was strongest. Nanjing, by contrast, was Jianwen's capital and the seat of the Confucian official class that Yongle distrusted and was systematically purging.

The decision to move the capital was made formally in 1403, but the execution took two decades. Beijing had to be rebuilt almost from scratch — Hongwu's army had partially demolished the old Yuan capital in 1368, and substantial reconstruction was required to make it suitable as an imperial seat. The Forbidden City had to be built. The Grand Canal had to be re-cut to supply Beijing with grain from the Yangtze valley. Hundreds of thousands of households had to be relocated north to populate the new capital. The full transition was completed in 1421, when Yongle formally moved the imperial court north and proclaimed Beijing the official Ming capital.

The strategic logic was sound. Beijing put the emperor on the front line of the Mongol frontier — exactly where Yongle, who would lead five personal campaigns into the steppe, wanted to be. It anchored Ming political identity in the northern half of the empire, balancing the southern bias of the Hongwu-era founding. It signaled that the dynasty was committed to active northern defense rather than retreating to the agricultural heartland.

The strategic costs were equally real. Beijing was 700 miles from the Yangtze rice-producing region that paid most of the Ming's taxes. The capital had to be supplied by canal grain transport across the entire span of the empire. The logistical strain was enormous and would shape Ming policy for the next 240 years. Every Ming financial crisis would translate directly into questions about whether the Beijing grain shipments could be maintained. Every Ming military setback in the north would translate into questions about whether the capital itself was defensible. The decision to put the capital at Beijing was a permanent commitment to engaged northern defense — and when the dynasty could no longer afford that defense, in the 1640s, Beijing fell first.

But the decision also produced the city that became, and remains, the political center of China. Beijing has been the imperial capital, with brief interruptions, for 600 of the 660 years since Yongle's relocation. The Republic of China moved the capital briefly to Nanjing after 1928; the People's Republic moved it back to Beijing in 1949 and has kept it there. The city the Yongle Emperor built is the city the People's Republic still governs from.

---

The architectural centerpiece of the new capital was the **Forbidden City** (Zijincheng, dzih-jin-CHENG, "Purple Forbidden City"), the imperial palace at the political heart of Beijing.

The Forbidden City was constructed between 1406 and 1420. It contains 980 buildings on a 180-acre site, surrounded by a 26-foot-high wall and a 170-foot-wide moat. The plan is organized along a strict north-south central axis — the emperor's symbolic axis between earth (south) and the polestar (north) — with three great halls of state at the center: the **Hall of Supreme Harmony** (the largest wooden building in China, used for the most important imperial ceremonies), the **Hall of Central Harmony** (a smaller transitional space), and the **Hall of Preserving Harmony** (used for banquets and palace examinations). The inner palace, behind these three outer halls, contained the emperor's residential quarters and the residences of the empress, consorts, and royal children.

The construction itself was one of the largest building projects of pre-modern world history. The numbers are debated but the order of magnitude is reliable: roughly a million laborers worked on the site over fourteen years, including specialized teams of stonemasons and carpenters from across the empire. The largest single stone in the complex, a marble carving in front of the Hall of Preserving Harmony, weighs about 200 tons and was hauled from a quarry 43 miles away in the dead of winter, dragged over roads iced down with water poured from wells dug along the route — one of the more striking pieces of pre-modern logistical engineering. The wood for the supporting columns of the Hall of Supreme Harmony was sourced from the rainforests of southwestern China, with logs floated down the Yangtze and then up the Grand Canal to Beijing — single tree trunks of nanmu (a slow-growing hardwood) over 60 feet long, the largest of which took years to harvest and transport.

The architectural symbolism was deliberate and exhaustive. The number of decorative figures on each roof corner indicated the building's rank in the imperial hierarchy (the Hall of Supreme Harmony has the maximum 10 figures; lesser buildings have 7, 5, 3). The colors were imperial — yellow tiles for the imperial roofs (yellow being the color of the emperor and of the central element earth in the Five Elements scheme), red walls for protection. The entire complex was oriented to face south (the emperor faces south toward his subjects, who face north toward him). The Forbidden City was, simultaneously, a residence, an administrative office complex, a ceremonial theater, and a vast cosmological diagram in built form.

It is essentially intact today. The Republic of China expelled the last Qing emperor in 1924 and turned the Forbidden City into a museum — the **Palace Museum** — that opened in 1925. The People's Republic continued the museum function. The complex survived the Cultural Revolution because Premier Zhou Enlai posted PLA troops to protect it from Red Guard destruction. It is now a UNESCO World Heritage site and the most-visited museum in the world by attendance (15 million visitors a year). It remains, after more than 600 years, the largest preserved palace complex on Earth.

The Forbidden City is what the Yongle Emperor is most remembered for, and the achievement justifies the memory. It was built by a usurper using forced labor on a colossal scale; it was also, by any sensible historical standard, one of the great architectural projects of pre-modern human civilization.

The **Temple of Heaven** (Tian Tan), completed in 1420 at the southern edge of Beijing, was the second-most-important building of the Yongle building program. The complex contained altars and ceremonial halls used for the annual imperial sacrifices to Heaven (performed at the winter solstice) and prayers for the harvest (performed in early spring). The most distinctive structure, the **Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests**, is a circular three-tiered wooden building constructed without nails, set on a three-tiered marble platform — one of the masterpieces of Chinese religious architecture. It is also a UNESCO World Heritage site.

---

The cultural project of Yongle's reign matched the architectural project in ambition.

The **Yongle Encyclopedia** (Yongle Dadian) was a massive compilation of Chinese knowledge commissioned in 1403 and completed in 1408 by over 2,000 scholars working at Nanjing. Its purpose was to incorporate every existing Chinese text — every classic, history, philosophical treatise, technical manual, religious work, encyclopedia, dictionary, gazetteer, and folk text — into a single organized reference work. The completed Yongle Encyclopedia ran to 11,095 volumes, containing roughly 370 million Chinese characters, organized by rhyme of the entry-word.

The numbers deserve a moment to sink in. **The Yongle Encyclopedia was the largest reference work in human history before Wikipedia.** No other pre-modern compilation came remotely close to its scale. The European medieval encyclopedias of the same period — Vincent of Beauvais's *Speculum Maius* and similar works — ran to a few volumes covering a few thousand pages. The major contemporary Islamic compilations — the great Ottoman astronomical and historical works of the same century — were similarly modest in comparison. The Yongle Encyclopedia was several orders of magnitude larger than anything else attempted anywhere in the world before the eighteenth century.

(A useful inline cross-civ comparison: the *Bihar al-Anwar* of the Safavid-era cleric Majlisi (Safavid Persia Ch 5), the most comprehensive Shia hadith collection ever assembled, ran to 110 volumes — a substantial work, completed in the 1680s, two and a half centuries after Yongle's encyclopedia. The European *Encyclopédie* of Diderot and d'Alembert, the great Enlightenment-era reference work, ran to 28 volumes when completed in 1772. The English *Encyclopædia Britannica*'s third edition of 1797 ran to 18 volumes. The Yongle Encyclopedia at 11,095 volumes would not have a comparably-sized rival anywhere in the world for nearly six centuries.)

The fate of the Yongle Encyclopedia is one of the great cultural losses of pre-modern history. The original was kept in the imperial library at Nanjing and later moved to Beijing. A single complete duplicate was made in the 1560s under the Jiajing Emperor — fortunately, because the original was almost entirely destroyed in late-Ming and Ming-Qing fires and chaos. The duplicate also suffered enormous losses across the following centuries. Most of the surviving volumes today were dispersed during the Anglo-French sack of the Beijing summer palaces in 1860 and the Boxer Rebellion looting of 1900; surviving volumes are now scattered across libraries in Beijing, Taipei, Tokyo, Cambridge, London, and several American university libraries. Only about 400 of the original 11,095 volumes survive in any form. The single most ambitious compilation of human knowledge before the digital era is, in terms of surviving content, mostly gone.

The encyclopedia was Yongle's personal project, partly motivated by a desire to assemble in one place every text bearing on Chinese statecraft, but also partly motivated by something less elevated: a desire to demonstrate that the usurper-emperor was the legitimate cultural patron of Han civilization in all its accumulated depth. The legitimacy argument worked. The Yongle Encyclopedia became one of the durable symbols of Ming cultural authority, and subsequent Confucian scholars who might otherwise have refused to recognize Yongle's legitimacy were able to point to the encyclopedia as evidence that the regime was genuinely committed to traditional Chinese learning. The political utility of the project was as important as its scholarly value.

---

The military project was the third great Yongle enterprise.

Yongle conducted **five personal campaigns** into the Mongolian steppe between 1410 and 1424, attempting to break the surviving Mongol confederations that had reformed after the Yuan collapse. The Northern Yuan court — Toghon Temur's descendants — had splintered into several rival factions by the early fifteenth century, and various Mongol warlords were competing for paramount-leader status across the Mongolian heartland. Yongle's strategy was to march into the steppe with a large army, destroy whichever Mongol leader had most recently consolidated power, and force the Mongol nobility to submit nominal tribute to the Ming.

The campaigns were tactically successful in their immediate objectives. Yongle's forces marched as far north as the Onon River (in modern Mongolia, near the heartland of the original Mongol homeland) and defeated several major Mongol leaders. But the steppe was too vast for permanent occupation by a sedentary Chinese army; the Mongols simply withdrew when faced with a Chinese force, regrouped after the Chinese army returned south, and resumed their raiding within months. Yongle himself died on the return from his fifth campaign in August 1424, near the modern city of Wuzhumuqin, before reaching Beijing. His long-prepared sixth campaign was abandoned by his successor.

The Mongolian campaigns demonstrated Ming military reach but did not solve the northern frontier problem. The strategic lesson — that nomadic confederations could not be permanently destroyed by punitive sedentary expeditions — would be relearned, painfully, by every later Ming emperor who attempted aggressive northern policy. By the mid-fifteenth century, after the Tumu Crisis (Chapter 4), Ming northern policy would shift from offensive expedition to defensive Wall-building, and the offensive impulse of the early Ming would be permanently abandoned.

But the most spectacular project of Yongle's reign was not in the steppe. It was on the ocean. And it gets its own chapter.

---

The early fifteenth century in Eurasia is one of the great moments of post-medieval state-rebuilding, and the Ming under Yongle was its eastern anchor — the single largest, richest, most administratively sophisticated state on Earth at that moment, and the single most ambitious patron of imperial monumental projects.

# Chapter 3 — Zheng He's Treasure Fleet

Between 1405 and 1433, the Ming Dynasty conducted seven gigantic naval expeditions across the Indian Ocean. The fleets reached Java and Sumatra, India and Sri Lanka, the Persian Gulf and the Arabian Peninsula, and the East African coast at Malindi and Mogadishu. They were the largest naval expeditions launched by any state anywhere in the world before the eighteenth century. They preceded Vasco da Gama's voyage from Lisbon to Calicut by ninety years. The flagships were several times larger than anything the Portuguese or Spanish would build for another two centuries.

Then they stopped. The fleets were laid up. The shipyards at Nanjing were dismantled. The navigation records were eventually destroyed by the Ministry of War. The Ming never built another deep-water navy. China's naval projection effectively ended in 1433 and would not resume on a comparable scale for almost six hundred years.

This is one of the great counterfactual moments in world history — what would the modern world look like if the Ming had continued to project naval power across the Indian Ocean? — and it deserves a chapter to itself. The chapter is about the voyages, the man who led them, the ships and the science that made them possible, and the political-cultural reasons the Ming chose to stop.

---

The man who led the voyages was **Zheng He** (jeng HUH).

Zheng He was born around 1371 as **Ma He** (mah HUH), the son of a Hui Muslim family in **Yunnan** province in southwestern China. The family was prominent — his father and grandfather had reportedly made the Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca, suggesting both wealth and status — and the family name Ma was a Chinese version of Muhammad, marking the lineage as Muslim. Yunnan in the late fourteenth century was the last Yuan-loyalist province in China; when Hongwu's army conquered Yunnan in 1381, the ten-year-old Ma He was captured, castrated as part of the standard treatment of conquered Yuan officials' sons, and sent north as a slave-eunuch to be assigned to the imperial household.

The boy ended up in the household of Hongwu's fourth son, Zhu Di — the future Yongle Emperor — at the Beiping court. He grew up there as Zhu Di's personal servant, adopted the surname Zheng around 1404 (the name was bestowed by the new emperor in honor of military service during the Jingnan Campaign), and became one of Yongle's most trusted associates. By the time Yongle took the throne in 1402, Zheng He was thirty-one, physically imposing (Ming sources describe him as exceptionally tall and powerfully built), militarily experienced from the civil war, and unusually cosmopolitan — a Hui Muslim with Yunnan Mongol-frontier connections, who spoke Persian and possibly Arabic in addition to Chinese, and whose religious background gave him useful credibility in dealing with Muslim courts across the Indian Ocean trade network.

In 1405, Yongle made him Commander of the Western Treasure Fleet. The first voyage left Suzhou in the autumn of 1405. The seventh and last returned in 1433. Across those twenty-eight years, Zheng He commanded the largest naval expeditions in pre-modern history.

---

The fleets themselves require a careful description, because the dimensions are contested and the popular accounts often exaggerate.

Each voyage involved roughly 28,000 men aboard approximately 300 ships. The ships included multiple categories: warships, supply ships, water tankers, troop transports, horse carriers, and the largest category, the **baochuan** (bow-CHWAHN, "treasure ships") — the imperial flagship class.

The dimensions of the treasure ships are the most famous and most contested aspect of the entire program. Ming chronicle sources describe baochuan as up to **137 meters (450 feet) long** and **56 meters (185 feet) wide**, with nine masts and four decks, and crews of several hundred. If accurate, these dimensions would make them several times larger than any contemporary European vessel — Columbus's Santa Maria was about 19 meters (62 feet) long, Vasco da Gama's São Gabriel about 27 meters (88 feet) — and possibly the largest wooden ships ever built. Modern naval historians dispute whether ships of the stated length were structurally feasible; the engineering challenge of building a wooden hull strong enough to flex without breaking at 137 meters of length is severe, and the published structural calculations suggest the upper limit for traditional Chinese hull construction was probably significantly less. Archaeological excavations at the Nanjing shipyards have recovered rudder posts of dimensions consistent with very large vessels (one rudder post is over 11 meters long, suggesting a hull of perhaps 60 to 70 meters), and dry-dock foundations of unprecedented scale. The conservative modern reading is that the treasure ships were probably 60–70 meters (200–230 feet) long — substantially smaller than the chronicle dimensions, but still vastly larger than anything Europe could build for another two centuries.

Whatever the exact dimensions, the **scale of the fleets** is not in dispute. Twenty-eight thousand men is a small army. Three hundred ships is a substantial fleet by any pre-modern standard. The total fleet displacement, even at conservative ship dimensions, exceeded the entire combined naval power of all European states at the same date. The treasure voyages were not exploration in the European sense (the Ming was not seeking new lands — it knew the Indian Ocean trade network well from centuries of Chinese commercial contact, particularly during the Song and Yuan); they were imperial projection, on a scale calibrated to demonstrate Ming superiority to every state in the system.

The technology that made the voyages possible was substantial. Chinese shipbuilding had pioneered watertight bulkhead compartments — segmented internal walls that allowed a damaged section to flood without sinking the ship, an innovation that would not appear in European shipbuilding until the nineteenth century. The Ming had stern-mounted rudders (the European standard since the medieval period, but in larger and more sophisticated forms), the magnetic compass (invented under the Song, Tang-Song China Ch 6), and detailed navigational charts derived from a long Chinese tradition of coastal cartography. Star navigation tables prepared at the Nanjing observatory allowed positional fixes by the elevation of specific stars. The supply infrastructure included food storage adequate for 28,000 men on multi-year expeditions, freshwater management through both stored barrels and rain collection, and an advanced understanding of the Indian Ocean monsoon system that allowed the fleets to sail with prevailing winds in both directions.

---

The seven voyages can be summarized briefly.

The **first voyage** (1405–1407) sailed to Champa (in modern Vietnam), Java, Sumatra, Malacca, Sri Lanka, and Calicut on the Malabar coast of India. The diplomatic mission was to establish Ming authority at the major Indian Ocean trade ports and secure them as tributary states. The fleet defeated and captured a major pirate chief at Palembang in Sumatra and brought him back to Nanjing for execution.

The **second voyage** (1407–1409) repeated and extended the first, with stops at additional Indian ports.

The **third voyage** (1409–1411) was militarily more aggressive. The fleet intervened in a succession dispute on Sri Lanka, captured the Sinhalese king Vira Alakeshvara, and brought him back to Nanjing as a prisoner. The Yongle Emperor eventually pardoned and returned the king, after the Sri Lankan court promised tribute, which is the kind of imperial gesture that distinguishes Ming projection from European colonialism — the Ming wanted recognition and tribute, not territorial annexation.

The **fourth voyage** (1413–1415) reached the Persian Gulf, calling at Hormuz (Safavid Persia Ch 6 covers the later Hormuz history under the Safavids and English East India Company). The fleet returned with envoys from over twenty foreign states bearing tribute, and — most famously — with a giraffe brought from the Swahili port of **Malindi** (in modern Kenya). The giraffe was identified by Ming officials with the **qilin** (CHEE-lin), a mythical Chinese creature whose appearance was traditionally said to herald the reign of a sage-ruler. A famous Ming court painting (the *Tribute Giraffe with Attendant*, painted by Shen Du in 1414, now in the Philadelphia Museum of Art) shows the animal being led on a leash by an African handler — possibly the earliest extant Chinese depiction of an East African person and animal. The political symbolism was useful: a usurper-emperor whose legitimacy was contested at home received what could be officially interpreted as cosmological endorsement of his rule.

The **fifth voyage** (1417–1419) reached Aden in southern Arabia, returning the foreign envoys to their homes and collecting another wave of tribute.

The **sixth voyage** (1421–1422) sailed to Africa, with detachments reportedly reaching Mogadishu (in modern Somalia) and possibly further down the Swahili coast. (A persistent claim that the Ming reached the Americas in 1421, popularized by Gavin Menzies's 2002 book *1421: The Year China Discovered America*, has been comprehensively rejected by professional historians of pre-modern China and the Indian Ocean. The Menzies book is bunk. There is no evidence that any Ming vessel ever crossed the Pacific or rounded Africa, and there is substantial positive evidence that Zheng He's voyages were confined to the Indian Ocean trade network. The Ming knew about the Indian Ocean monsoons and exploited them; they had no equivalent knowledge of Pacific or Atlantic wind systems and no apparent reason to attempt crossings of either ocean.)

The **seventh voyage** (1431–1433) was launched after a long pause caused by political shifts at court (described below). It revisited many of the earlier ports, confirmed tribute relationships, and sent detachments as far as Mecca — where Zheng He's Muslim sailors were reportedly able to perform the Hajj on the imperial expedition's behalf, a remarkable diplomatic-religious moment for a Chinese imperial project. Zheng He himself died at sea, probably near Calicut, on the return trip in 1433. He was around sixty-two years old.

---

The political question — why did the voyages stop? — deserves direct attention, because the answer reveals one of the deepest structural tensions of Ming politics.

The voyages were a project of the **eunuch faction** at court. Zheng He himself was a eunuch, as were most of the senior commanders of the fleet. The administrative apparatus that supported the voyages — the shipyards at Nanjing, the supply networks, the personnel rosters — was managed through the imperial household, which is to say through the eunuch bureaucracy. The Yongle Emperor, who personally sponsored the voyages, was using the eunuch apparatus partly because it answered to him directly without filtering through the regular Confucian bureaucracy, and partly because the Confucian officials were skeptical of the entire enterprise.

The Confucian officials were skeptical for both ideological and practical reasons. Ideologically, the Confucian tradition emphasized agrarian governance, frugal court finance, and minimal state expenditure on prestige projects that did not directly benefit the rural population. The voyages were colossally expensive. They produced exotic tribute (giraffes, ostriches, lions, foreign envoys with strange clothes) but no tangible economic return — Ming exports of silk and porcelain were already reaching the Indian Ocean markets through private trade, and the official tribute exchanges generally cost the Ming more in return-gifts than they received in tribute. Practically, the officials wanted state resources directed to the agricultural infrastructure (river dikes, granaries, canals), not to building 70-meter ships to take giraffes to Beijing.

When the Yongle Emperor died in 1424, his son the **Hongxi Emperor** suspended the voyages within months — the new emperor was on the side of the Confucian officials, who had pushed the suspension hard during Yongle's lifetime and now had a sympathetic ear at the throne. Hongxi died after only nine months, but his successor the **Xuande Emperor** authorized only one more voyage, the seventh of 1431–1433, partly as a memorial to his grandfather Yongle and partly to confirm the established tribute relationships. After Zheng He's death and the fleet's return, the voyages were never resumed.

The political coup against the maritime project went further than just suspension. The records of the voyages — the navigation tables, the route logs, the ship designs — were progressively suppressed across the following decades. The standard account is that around 1480, the Ministry of War official **Liu Daxia** ordered the surviving navigation records destroyed to prevent any future emperor from reviving the voyages. This story may be exaggerated (the destruction was probably more piecemeal than the dramatic version suggests), but the underlying reality is documented: by the early sixteenth century, the Ming no longer had the technical capacity to build deep-water expeditions on Zheng He's scale, the shipyards had been dismantled or repurposed, and the maritime knowledge base had been allowed to lapse.

The decision to abandon the maritime project shaped Chinese state policy for the following four centuries. The Ming would maintain the official **Haijin** (HIGH-jin, "sea ban") edicts forbidding private overseas trade for most of the dynasty — the ban being honored mostly in the breach, since smuggling and unofficial trade continued at large scale, but the official posture being firmly against maritime engagement. The Qing dynasty that followed continued the same general policy. China would not project naval power on a comparable scale until the People's Republic began building a blue-water fleet in the 2000s, almost six centuries after Zheng He's last voyage.

---

The deeper question — why did the political coup against the voyages succeed so completely? — is one of the most-discussed counterfactuals in world history.

The standard answers focus on Confucian ideology (agrarian priorities, suspicion of merchant wealth, distrust of eunuch power), on fiscal pressure (the voyages were expensive at exactly the moment Ming finances were strained by the Beijing capital relocation and the Mongolian campaigns), and on strategic priorities (the northern frontier required attention, and the Ming made a deliberate choice to redirect resources from maritime projection to land defense). All of these are real factors and all are documented in the period sources.

But there is also a deeper structural point worth pausing on. The Ming voyages were not a commercial project; they were a state project. They did not generate revenue to fund themselves; they were funded by the imperial treasury. Their continuation depended entirely on continued political support from the throne. When that support evaporated — for the perfectly comprehensible reason that the next emperor was a Confucian-tradition agrarian moderate rather than an outward-projecting usurper-emperor — there was no commercial constituency capable of sustaining the project in the absence of imperial sponsorship. The Indian Ocean merchants (Chinese, Arab, Persian, Indian, Southeast Asian) who actually moved goods across the trade network continued doing so without any need for Ming naval protection; the imperial fleet had been imposed on top of an existing commercial network rather than being functionally necessary to it.

The contrast with the European maritime projects of the following century is instructive. The Portuguese voyages to the Indian Ocean in the late fifteenth century were sponsored by the Crown but operated by joint commercial ventures, with Portuguese merchants and the state sharing both costs and returns; the Dutch and English East India Companies of the seventeenth century (Safavid Persia Ch 6 covers the EIC's role at Hormuz) were corporate-state hybrids in which commercial profit motivation kept the maritime project running through political shifts at the home government. The Ming voyages had no equivalent commercial-political coalition. They were the Yongle Emperor's personal project; when his successors lost interest, they ended.

Whether things would have looked different if the Ming had continued is one of the great unanswerable questions of world history. Some economic historians have argued that a continued Ming naval presence in the Indian Ocean would have made Vasco da Gama's 1498 arrival irrelevant — the Portuguese would have found the trade network already controlled by a much larger Chinese imperial fleet and would have had no opening to establish their own coastal-fortress empire. Others have argued that the Indian Ocean trade network was deeply enough established by the early fifteenth century that no naval projection would have permanently controlled it, and the Portuguese would still have found their way in. The historical record contains the choice the Ming actually made (to stop) but does not contain the alternative.

What the historical record does contain is the cultural memory. Zheng He has become, in twentieth- and twenty-first-century Chinese popular culture, the icon of a possible China that engaged the wider world rather than retreating from it. His statue stands at Nanjing, at Jakarta, at several Indonesian and Malaysian Chinese-community sites along his routes. The People's Republic has invested heavily in promoting his memory as a counter-narrative to the standard Western framing of European maritime expansion as the inevitable arc of early-modern history. The 600th anniversary of the first voyage in 2005 was a major Chinese government commemoration. The treasure fleet has, in the modern Chinese imagination, become the symbol of what the Ming might have been if it had not turned inward.

It is also a reminder of what the Ming actually was for one extraordinary generation: the largest naval power on Earth, projecting force halfway around the world, with ships that nothing in Europe could remotely match.

---

The **Portuguese Atlantic exploration** under Henry the Navigator was beginning in the same decades — Henry founded his Sagres maritime academy around 1418, the Portuguese reached the Madeira Islands in 1419, the Azores in 1427, and Cape Bojador on the African Atlantic coast in 1434 (the year after Zheng He's last voyage). The **Korean Joseon dynasty** under King Sejong was developing the geobukseon (turtle ship) gunboat designs that would later defeat the Japanese at the Imjin War (Chapter 6). The **Polynesian navigators** had completed their two-millennium expansion across the Pacific (Polynesian Voyagers) and were settling into their final island cultures. In **Mali**, the great empire of the western Sahel was at its commercial peak. In the **Aztec** heartland, Tenochtitlan was completing its dominance of the Valley of Mexico (Aztec Empire Ch 5). The early fifteenth century is the moment when several civilizational centers were, simultaneously, making decisions about long-distance maritime engagement that would shape the next four centuries — and the Ming made the most consequential of those decisions in the most decisive manner.

# Chapter 4 — The Eunuch Empire

The Yongle Emperor died in 1424, on the return from his fifth Mongolian campaign. His son Hongxi reigned for nine months. His grandson Xuande reigned for ten years and authorized the seventh and last Zheng He voyage (Chapter 3). His great-grandson, the **Zhengtong Emperor** (jeng-TONG, "Orthodox Succession"), inherited the throne in 1435 at age six.

What followed was a 130-year period in which actual political power at court drifted progressively away from the throne and toward the eunuch bureaucracy that Hongwu had tried so hard to prevent (Chapter 1). The Tumu Crisis of 1449 was the most dramatic single moment of the period — a Chinese emperor was captured in battle by Mongol cavalry, the first such event since the Mongol Yuan conquest itself — but the underlying institutional drift was constant. By the end of the period, the regular Confucian bureaucracy was operating in the shadow of the Directorate of Ceremonial (the eunuch office that controlled access to the emperor), the Eastern Depot (the eunuch-run secret police), and the imperial workshops (the eunuch-controlled industrial-economic apparatus that produced everything from porcelain to silk to weapons for the imperial household).

This is the chapter about the eunuch empire — the institutional period that made the late Ming what it became, the Tumu Crisis that exposed the rot, the Great Wall reconstruction that was the period's most visible monument, and the Grand Canal economy that kept the dynasty solvent through it all.

---

The first eunuch to dominate Ming politics outright was **Wang Zhen** (wahng JEN).

Wang Zhen had been the boy Zhengtong Emperor's tutor before he became emperor. He retained a close relationship after the boy ascended the throne, and as Zhengtong grew up, Wang Zhen rose through the eunuch hierarchy to control the **Directorate of Ceremonial** (Si Li Jian) — the office responsible for handling memorials between the outer government and the inner palace, and increasingly the office that decided which memorials reached the emperor's desk and which were quietly filed without action. By the early 1440s, Wang Zhen was effectively running the Ming government, with the young emperor approving documents that Wang Zhen drafted and signing off on personnel decisions that Wang Zhen had made.

In 1449, Wang Zhen made the catastrophic mistake of his career. The Mongol leader **Esen Taishi** (eh-SEN TIGH-shih), the dominant figure of the Oirat (western Mongol) confederation, had been raiding the Ming northern frontier and demanding additional trade concessions. Wang Zhen persuaded the twenty-two-year-old Zhengtong Emperor to lead a personal punitive expedition against the Mongols — the first time a Ming emperor would campaign in person since Yongle. The expedition was hastily organized, with an army that Ming sources later claimed numbered 500,000 men (probably exaggerated but certainly very large), inadequately supplied, and led by Wang Zhen himself in his capacity as the emperor's chief political advisor.

The expedition was a disaster from the start. The army marched into Mongol territory in August 1449, found nothing because Esen had withdrawn rather than fight on Ming terms, and turned back toward Beijing as supplies ran low. Wang Zhen, deciding to detour the army past his hometown for a flattering display, slowed the retreat dangerously. Esen's cavalry caught the Ming army at the **Tumu** (TOO-moo) fortress on September 1, 1449, while it was reorganizing for water resupply. The Mongol attack was rapid and devastating. The Ming army, exhausted and poorly led, broke. Casualties were enormous — modern estimates suggest perhaps half the army was killed in a few hours, the rest scattered. Wang Zhen was killed by his own bodyguard, who reportedly judged that he had already destroyed the empire and might as well die with it. The Zhengtong Emperor was captured.

A captured Chinese emperor was an unprecedented diplomatic problem. Esen attempted to use the captive emperor as leverage to extract massive concessions from the Ming court. The Ming court — under the leadership of the brilliant Vice Minister of War **Yu Qian** (YOO chyen) — refused to pay. They formally enthroned the captured emperor's brother as the **Jingtai Emperor** (jing-TIGH), making Zhengtong's captivity diplomatically irrelevant. Yu Qian then organized the defense of Beijing itself against Esen's siege in October 1449. The defense held. Esen was forced to withdraw without taking the capital. The captured Zhengtong Emperor, now politically useless to his Mongol captors, was eventually released in 1450 and lived under house arrest in Beijing for seven years.

The story has a disturbing coda. In 1457, the displaced Zhengtong Emperor mounted a coup against his brother the Jingtai Emperor and reclaimed the throne (under a new reign name, **Tianshun**, "Heaven's Approval"). One of his first acts was to execute Yu Qian — the man who had saved Beijing from the consequences of his original capture — on trumped-up treason charges. Yu Qian's execution is one of the most universally condemned political killings in Chinese imperial history. Subsequent Ming and Qing emperors formally repudiated the verdict; Yu Qian's tomb at Hangzhou is a major pilgrimage site for the Confucian moral tradition.

---

The Tumu Crisis was the most visible single moment of the eunuch-dominated mid-Ming, but its institutional consequences were more important than the dramatic events.

The crisis demonstrated three things, each of which would shape Ming policy for the rest of the dynasty.

First, the **weisuo military system** (Chapter 1) was failing. Hongwu's hereditary military households, supposedly self-supporting and ready for service, had decayed across two generations. The army Wang Zhen led to Tumu was poorly trained, poorly equipped, and poorly motivated. It was a hereditary peasant militia rather than the professional force the Ming would need to face actual Mongol cavalry. Subsequent Ming emperors would gradually shift toward paid mercenary forces (mubing) as the weisuo decayed further, but the institutional transition was slow, expensive, and politically contentious.

Second, the **northern frontier** could not be permanently secured by offensive expedition. Yongle's five campaigns had not solved the Mongol problem; the Tumu Crisis showed that even one badly-organized expedition could end in catastrophe. Ming policy shifted toward a defensive posture, anchored on the rebuilt Great Wall (described below). The shift permanently abandoned the Yongle-era ambition of projecting Ming military power into the steppe.

Third, the **eunuch system** had become structurally impossible to dislodge. Wang Zhen was dead, but the institutional position he had occupied — chief eunuch of the Directorate of Ceremonial, with effective control over the flow of information to the emperor — remained. His successors slipped into the same position. Across the next 175 years, the chief eunuch of the Directorate would be the second-most-powerful person at court, and sometimes the most powerful, depending on the emperor's energy and competence. The institutional logic that had created the eunuch problem under the Hu Weiyong abolition (Chapter 1) continued to operate; the Tumu Crisis did not break it.

The chief eunuch who came closest to surpassing Wang Zhen in raw power was **Liu Jin** (lyoo JEEN), who dominated the court of the **Zhengde Emperor** (jeng-DUH, reigned 1505–1521) from 1505 to 1510. Liu Jin's tenure is usually treated by historians as the apex of pre-Wei Zhongxian eunuch corruption. He effectively ran the government while the teenage Zhengde Emperor pursued an enthusiastic life of debauchery and military play (the Zhengde Emperor is one of the most colorful Ming rulers — he built a private military pleasure-palace in the Forbidden City called the Leopard House, rode personally into combat against Mongol raiders under the assumed military rank of General Zhu Shou, and largely ignored normal governance). Liu Jin accumulated personal wealth that contemporary estimates put at roughly 250 million ounces of silver — possibly the largest individual fortune in pre-modern world history. He was eventually executed in 1510 after being accused of plotting to seize the throne; the punishment, **lingchi** (slow slicing), reportedly took three days to administer.

Liu Jin's wealth — even allowing for considerable exaggeration in the reported figures — illustrates the scale of eunuch corruption at its height. A single court official with no legitimate revenue source had assembled a fortune comparable to the entire annual tax revenue of major European states. The wealth was assembled through a combination of bribes, grants of state monopolies (salt, tea, mining), and outright extortion of provincial officials who needed to pass requests through the Directorate of Ceremonial. The Ming state had become, at the eunuch interface, something close to a pure rent-extraction apparatus — and the regular Confucian bureaucracy was institutionally incapable of doing anything about it.

---

The most visible monument of the mid-Ming period is the **Great Wall of China** that tourists visit today.

The Great Wall in popular imagination is often presented as a single ancient structure built by the Qin Dynasty under the First Emperor (Qin Dynasty Ch 4 covers the Qin-era Wall). This is not accurate. The Qin Wall was built of rammed earth, has mostly disappeared into the soil, and ran along a different route from the modern Wall. The famous brick-and-stone fortification with the watchtowers, battlements, and rolling stretches across the mountains north of Beijing — the Great Wall that appears on every Chinese tourism brochure — is almost entirely a Ming construction project, undertaken between roughly 1450 and 1600 in response to the strategic lessons of the Tumu Crisis.

The decision to rebuild the Wall was a deliberate strategic shift. After Tumu, the Ming court accepted that offensive expeditions into the steppe could not be sustained — Mongol cavalry could outpace any Ming army in the open plains, and the cost of supplying expeditions deep into Mongolian territory was prohibitive. The defensive alternative was to wall the entire northern frontier and force any attacker to either besiege fortifications (which was difficult for nomadic cavalry) or to penetrate the Wall at specific gates that could be defended by garrison forces. The Wall did not need to be impenetrable; it needed to channel attacks into chokepoints where the Ming could mass defensive forces.

Construction proceeded in stages over more than a century. The earliest substantial Ming wall sections were built in the 1450s and 1460s in Hebei and Liaoning provinces. The major reconstruction of the central sections — the picturesque mountain Wall north of Beijing, including the famous Mutianyu and Badaling sections that Western tourists visit — was concentrated in the mid-to-late sixteenth century, particularly during the reign of the **Wanli Emperor** (Chapter 5). The western extension to **Jiayuguan** (jya-yoo-GWAHN) in modern Gansu province, the Wall's Ming-era western terminus, was completed in stages through the late sixteenth century. The eastern terminus at **Shanhaiguan** (shahn-high-GWAHN, "Mountain-Sea Pass," where the Wall meets the Pacific) was built up as the major fortress facing the Manchurian frontier, the gate that Wu Sangui would eventually open to the Manchus in 1644 (Chapter 8).

The total length of the Ming Wall is contested; modern surveys have produced figures ranging from about 5,500 miles (the most commonly cited number) to over 13,000 miles (a 2012 Chinese government archaeological survey that included every fortification fragment that could be associated with the Ming defensive system). The construction effort was one of the largest in human history — millions of conscripted laborers, soldiers, and artisans worked on the project across the century and a half of major construction. The cost in human lives, particularly during the Wanli-era extensions, was enormous.

The Wall did not actually stop Mongol raids. Altan Khan invaded northern China in 1550, broke through the frontier defenses, and besieged Beijing for several days before withdrawing with massive plunder — a raid roughly comparable in audacity to Esen's 1449 Beijing siege a century earlier. The Wall slowed the Mongol raiding parties, channeled them into specific approaches, and made the raiders pay a higher cost in casualties for any given operation, but it did not exclude them from China. The Mongols continued raiding the northern frontier for the entire Ming period; the eventual modus vivendi was not the Wall but the **Longqing settlement** of 1571, in which the Ming recognized Altan Khan as a tributary ruler and opened official trade markets at the frontier — what the Mongols had been demanding all along.

The Wall's most spectacular failure came in 1644. The Manchu armies that overthrew the Ming did not, in the end, breach the Wall by force. They came through the Shanhaiguan gate, opened from the inside by the Ming general Wu Sangui (Chapter 8). The Wall held against Manchu attempts at frontal assault for years before Wu's defection; once the gate was opened, the Wall became irrelevant. The lesson — that fortifications cannot stop an enemy whose internal allies will let them through — would be relearned in many later contexts. The Ming Wall is now a UNESCO World Heritage site, the most visited monument in China, and one of the largest construction projects ever undertaken on Earth. It is also an enduring case study in the limits of defensive engineering against a determined adversary with internal political support.

---

The economic backbone of the mid-Ming was the **Grand Canal** and the rice it carried.

The Grand Canal had been built by the Sui dynasty in the early 600s (Tang-Song China Ch 1) and extended by the Mongol Yuan to reach Khanbaliq in the 1280s (Yuan Dynasty Ch 2). The Yongle Emperor had comprehensively rebuilt and re-cut the canal between 1411 and 1415 to support the new northern capital. The result was a continuous internal water transport network running roughly 1,100 miles from Hangzhou in the south to Beijing in the north, capable of carrying up to four million bushels of grain per year — the logistical artery that fed the Ming capital and remained the spine of Ming state finance.

The Canal had to operate continuously for the dynasty to function. Beijing, with a population of perhaps a million by the late Ming, did not produce nearly enough grain to feed itself; the surrounding north Chinese provinces produced wheat and millet but not the rice that the imperial court and its associated officials and military expected. The grain shipments came from the Yangtze delta — the most productive agricultural region in the empire — and moved north through the canal in the slow procession of grain barges that defined Ming logistics. The Canal Transport Bureau, staffed by hereditary canal workers, was one of the largest single state enterprises of the Ming period.

The Canal economy supported a thriving commercial network along its length. The Yangtze delta cities — **Suzhou** (SOO-joh), **Hangzhou**, **Yangzhou**, **Wuxi** — became some of the wealthiest urban centers in the world during the mid-Ming period. Suzhou by the late sixteenth century had a population of over a million, with extensive silk-weaving, embroidery, and book-printing industries. The Canal towns connected the wealthy Yangtze south to the political capital in the north, and the economic gravity of the late Ming was concentrated along this north-south axis to a degree that persisted into the modern period (the modern Beijing-Shanghai high-speed rail line follows roughly the same route as the Grand Canal).

The Canal also supported one of the most distinctive economic-political features of the mid-Ming: the **imperial workshops** (yuyong jian) operated by the eunuch bureaucracy. The Directorate of Ceremonial and its subsidiary offices ran enormous industrial operations — the **Jingdezhen porcelain kilns** producing the imperial blue-and-white wares (Chapter 7 describes the porcelain in more detail), the silk workshops at Suzhou and Hangzhou producing imperial robes and ceremonial textiles, the lacquer and jade and metalwork operations that supplied the court's ceremonial requirements. These workshops were enormous economic enterprises operated outside the regular fiscal system, with their own labor forces (often conscripted artisans serving long periods of corvée), their own raw-material supply chains, and their own sales channels for surplus production. The eunuch faction's institutional power rested partly on its control of this parallel economic apparatus.

---

A pause, on the institutional shape of the mid-Ming.

The mid-Ming century from Tumu (1449) to the rise of Zhang Juzheng (1572) is sometimes presented as a period of stagnation. This is partly accurate — the dynasty did not produce major institutional innovations during this period, did not expand its territory, did not significantly reform its administrative structure, and did not produce many emperors of great competence. The Zhengde Emperor's debauchery, the Jiajing Emperor's twenty-year absence from court to pursue Daoist alchemy in pursuit of immortality, the various Liu-Jin-style eunuch corruption episodes — these are the headline events of mid-Ming political history.

But the period was also one of institutional stability and economic growth, on a different reading. The Grand Canal economy worked. The Beijing capital was successfully maintained for over a century without major fiscal crisis. The Great Wall was built. The northern frontier was contained, expensively but effectively. The agricultural base of the empire continued to grow. The population recovered to and exceeded its Song-era peak. The commercial economy along the Yangtze became one of the most sophisticated in the world. The cultural production of the period — described in Chapter 7 — was extraordinary even by Ming standards.

The institutional pattern of the mid-Ming is one of an autopilot empire. The institutions Hongwu had built operated by their own momentum, with the eunuch bureaucracy filling the coordination role that the abolished Chief Minister had previously occupied, and the Confucian regular bureaucracy filling the technical-administrative role of running provinces and ministries. The system worked because the Chinese economic base was robust, the institutional infrastructure was substantial, and the threats to the dynasty were manageable. It failed when those conditions changed in the late sixteenth century — when the Imjin War (Chapter 6) and the rise of the Manchus created stresses the autopilot system could not absorb.

But for a long generation, the autopilot empire functioned. The next two chapters cover the late Ming era when the autopilot began to fail.

---

Cross-civ check: the mid-Ming century (1449–1572) was contemporary with the **fall of Constantinople** to the Ottomans in 1453 (Byzantine Empire Ch 12), the **Wars of the Roses** in England (1455–1487), the **Reconquista** completion in Spain (Granada 1492), **Columbus's voyages** to the Americas (1492 onward), the **Reformation** in Germany (Luther's 95 Theses 1517), the **Mughal founding** under Babur (1526), the **Aztec conquest** by Cortés (1519–1521, Aztec Empire Ch 8), the **Inca conquest** by Pizarro (1532–1533), the **Safavid founding** by Ismail I (Safavid Persia Ch 2), and the **Suleiman the Magnificent** Ottoman peak (1520–1566, Ottoman Empire Ch 5). The mid-Ming was the eastern anchor of one of the most transformative centuries in world history — a century during which the Eurasian-American world system was knit together for the first time and the early-modern global economy began to take shape. The Ming's relative inactivity during this period (the choice not to project naval power into the Indian Ocean, the choice not to engage with the Americas, the choice not to extend territorial control beyond its existing frontiers) was a strategic decision with very large long-term consequences.

# Chapter 5 — Single Whip and the Silver Globalization

In 1572, the **Wanli Emperor** (WAN-lee) inherited the Ming throne at age nine. His reign would last until 1620 — forty-eight years, the longest of any Ming emperor — and would span almost exactly the late Ming period during which the dynasty's commercial economy reached its peak, its silver-based monetary system was knit into the global economy, the great vernacular novels were written (Chapter 7), Matteo Ricci reached Beijing, and the Imjin War in Korea (Chapter 6) bankrupted the treasury just as the Manchu threat was rising in the northeast. The reign was simultaneously the cultural apex of late Ming China and the political prelude to its destruction.

The Wanli Emperor himself, after the first decade of competent rule under the regency of the Grand Secretary Zhang Juzheng, settled into one of the strangest patterns of imperial behavior in Chinese history: he simply stopped governing. From the late 1580s until his death, he refused to attend court, refused to fill vacant offices, refused to sign personnel decisions, refused to read most memorials. The dynasty drifted on its institutional momentum while the throne sulked in the inner palace. The Wanli work strike is one of the most-studied cases in the historical literature of what happens when an early-modern monarchy operates under a non-functional monarch for decades at a stretch.

This is the chapter about the late Ming silver economy, the global trade network it produced, the Wanli reign that funded all of it without governing any of it, and Matteo Ricci's arrival at the Beijing court. The Imjin War is its own chapter (Chapter 6).

---

The decade before the Wanli work strike was the most administratively vigorous of the late Ming, conducted by the Grand Secretary **Zhang Juzheng** (jahng joo-JENG).

Zhang Juzheng (1525–1582) was a brilliant career official from Hubei province who had risen through the regular Confucian bureaucracy and reached the Grand Secretariat (Neige) in the 1560s. When the boy Wanli Emperor took the throne in 1572, Zhang Juzheng became effective regent — chief tutor to the boy emperor, chief political coordinator, and chief reformer all at once. His tenure (roughly 1572–1582) was the most ambitious set of late-Ming reforms.

The reforms had three main components. First, **fiscal consolidation**: Zhang Juzheng audited the imperial accounts, identified massive arrears in tax collection, and forced provincial officials to make up the deficits or be dismissed. By the late 1570s the Ming treasury had a positive surplus for the first time in a generation. Second, **land surveys**: a comprehensive empire-wide survey of cultivated land in 1578–1580 identified vast quantities of land that had been quietly removed from the tax registers by wealthy households over the preceding century, restoring perhaps 30% more land to taxable status. Third, the **Single Whip Tax Reform**, the centerpiece of his fiscal program.

The **Single Whip Reform** (Yi Tiao Bian Fa, "Single Whip Method") was implemented gradually under Zhang Juzheng's leadership across the 1570s and formalized empire-wide in 1581. It consolidated the labyrinthine Ming fiscal system into a single payment, made in silver, per landholding. The previous system had required each rural household to make separate payments of grain (rice or wheat depending on region), corvée labor obligations (so many days of work per year owed to the state for canal maintenance, conscription, courier service, etc.), miscellaneous local fees, and special provincial assessments. The system was complicated, easy to manipulate, and difficult to administer fairly. The Single Whip replaced all of this with a single annual silver payment based on land area and quality.

The reform's logic was administrative simplicity, but its consequence was monetary revolution. The Ming had not previously been a silver-based economy. The Hongwu founding had attempted to use paper money (jiaochao) on the Yuan-era model, but the paper currency had inflated catastrophically through the early Ming and was effectively worthless by the mid-fifteenth century. Day-to-day commerce had reverted to copper coins (cash) for small transactions and silver bullion for large transactions, with no formal silver coinage but with weighed silver chunks (sycee) circulating as the de facto large-denomination currency. The Single Whip Reform took this informal silver-based commercial economy and made it into the official basis of state finance.

The effects were immediate and global. Chinese demand for silver, already enormous, became overwhelming after 1581. Every rural household across the most populous country on Earth needed to convert agricultural produce into silver to pay their taxes — and the Chinese silver mines produced nowhere near enough metal to meet the demand. The shortfall had to be made up by imports.

---

The silver imports came from two sources, and they were the gravitational center of the early-modern global economy.

The first source was **Spanish America**. The huge silver deposits at Potosí (in modern Bolivia) and at Zacatecas (in modern Mexico) had been discovered in the 1540s and were producing silver at unprecedented scale by the 1570s. Most of the silver crossed the Atlantic to Seville, where the Spanish Habsburg state taxed and minted it; from Seville it spread through European commerce, primarily to fund the Habsburg wars against the Dutch revolt and the Ottoman Empire. But a significant fraction — perhaps a third by the late sixteenth century — went the other direction. From Acapulco on the Pacific coast of Mexico, the **Manila galleon** carried silver west across the Pacific to **Manila** in the Philippines, where it was exchanged with Chinese merchants for Chinese silk, porcelain, and luxury goods. The Manila galleon ran annually from 1565 to 1815, the longest-running scheduled trans-oceanic trade route in pre-modern history.

The second source was **Japan**. Japanese silver mines at **Iwami** and **Sado** in the late sixteenth century were producing silver at scale, perhaps 20-30% of total global silver output for several decades. Japanese silver flowed across the East China Sea via several channels — direct smuggling between Japanese and Chinese merchants, official trade through the **Ryukyu Islands** (the Okinawa-based kingdom that maintained tributary relations with both China and Japan), and after 1557 through the Portuguese trading post at **Macao**, where Portuguese ships shuttled between Nagasaki and Macao and Goa carrying silver one direction and silk the other.

The combined silver flows were staggering. By the early seventeenth century, perhaps 10,000 tons of silver had flowed into China from these two sources combined — possibly a third or more of all silver mined in the early-modern world. The Ming economy was the destination for the largest single redistribution of precious metal in pre-modern human history. The Spanish Habsburg silver economy that funded the wars against the Dutch (Safavid Persia Ch 6 covers some parallel dynamics in the silk-for-silver trade with Iran) was the western pole of this system; Ming China was the eastern pole; and the Indian Ocean and Pacific commercial networks were the connecting tissue.

The economic consequences were profound. China's commercial economy was deeply monetized for the first time. Long-distance internal trade became dramatically easier because silver was a portable, durable, infinitely divisible medium of exchange. Specialized regional production for distant markets expanded — Jiangnan silk, Jingdezhen porcelain, Sichuan paper, Shanxi salt, Fujian sugar, Guangdong iron all developed integrated commercial networks. The late Ming commercial economy was, by the early seventeenth century, probably the largest and most sophisticated in the world.

The political consequences were almost as profound. The Ming state suddenly had access to silver tax revenues at a scale Hongwu could not have imagined. But the silver was also a vulnerability — disruptions to the Manila or Macao silver flows could create rapid fiscal crises, and the Ming state had no real control over either Spanish American mining output or Japanese silver production. When the Tokugawa shogunate restricted Japanese silver export in the early seventeenth century, when Spanish silver production declined after about 1620, the Ming fiscal crisis that followed contributed directly to the dynasty's fall (Chapter 8). The same silver flows that had built the late Ming commercial economy turned into the dynasty's strategic dependency — and when the silver dried up, the dynasty could not adapt fast enough.

The deeper structural point is worth pausing on. China under the Single Whip Reform had become integrated into a global economic system whose central commodity it did not produce and could not control. The state's fiscal viability now depended on the smooth operation of trade networks running from the Andes through Manila to the Yangtze delta — networks operated by Spanish galleons, Portuguese trading posts, Chinese and Hokkien private merchants, and Japanese silver miners, all outside the Ming's direct authority. This was, in retrospect, the moment when Chinese state autonomy stopped being possible in the way Hongwu had imagined it. The early-modern global economy had absorbed the Ming, and the Ming had not noticed.

---

Zhang Juzheng's reforms briefly stabilized the dynasty's finances and produced the late Ming silver economy. He himself died in 1582, in the middle of the great land survey, exhausted from a decade of overwork.

What happened next was a political reversal that exemplifies one of the chronic Ming problems. Zhang Juzheng had made enemies — many of them, accumulated over a decade of vigorous reform, fiscal pressure on wealthy provincial families, and ruthless personnel decisions. After his death, those enemies organized a posthumous campaign against his reputation. Within a few years, Zhang Juzheng had been formally disgraced, his honors stripped, his family's wealth confiscated, his sons exiled or driven to suicide. The reforms were not entirely undone — the Single Whip continued, because no one had a viable alternative — but Zhang Juzheng himself became a cautionary example for the next generation of officials, who took the obvious lesson: do not be too vigorous, do not make too many enemies, do not push reforms that depend on personal political capital you cannot maintain after death.

The Wanli Emperor, by then in his early twenties, had personally signed off on the destruction of his former regent's reputation. It was around this time that the emperor began his long withdrawal from active governance.

---

The **Wanli work strike** is one of the most extensively-studied episodes of Ming political history, partly because it lasted so long (roughly 1589 to Wanli's death in 1620 — over thirty years), partly because the documentary record is unusually good (the regular Ming bureaucracy continued producing memorials throughout the period, providing detailed evidence of what happens when an early-modern monarchy is run on autopilot for decades), and partly because the proximate cause was so personally trivial that the structural consequences are particularly stark.

The trigger was a succession dispute. The Wanli Emperor's first son, born to a low-ranking palace concubine in 1582, had a stronger claim to the throne by primogeniture than the Emperor's preferred third son, born to his favorite concubine **Lady Zheng** in 1586. The Confucian official class insisted that the eldest son be designated crown prince according to traditional Chinese succession law. The Emperor preferred his third son. The dispute became a multi-year political confrontation between the throne and the Confucian bureaucracy, conducted through escalating memorials, court arguments, and Imperial sulks. Wanli refused to designate any crown prince at all for years, fueled the speculation that he might overrule the bureaucracy, and finally — in 1601, after nearly two decades of dispute — capitulated and designated the eldest son as crown prince.

But by then, the work strike had set in. Some time around 1589, Wanli stopped attending court audiences. By the mid-1590s he had stopped reading most memorials. By the early 1600s he had stopped filling vacant ministerial offices — when a senior official died or retired, the position simply went unfilled, sometimes for decades. By the 1610s, large fractions of the regular bureaucracy were running on understaffed skeleton crews, with junior officials handling responsibilities they had no formal authority to discharge. The provincial governorships were unevenly staffed; the central ministries had ministers who had not had formal interviews with the emperor in years; the Grand Secretariat was filled with officials whose appointments had never been formally confirmed.

The system continued to function, after a fashion, because the institutional infrastructure was substantial. The Grand Canal still moved grain. The provinces still collected taxes. The provincial military commanders still defended their districts. The regular bureaucracy still produced its ritual outputs. But the central coordinating function that the throne was supposed to provide — the personnel decisions, the major policy choices, the responses to crises — was simply not being performed. When the Imjin War broke out in Korea in 1592 (Chapter 6), the Ming response was organized by the few senior officials still functioning, with minimal direction from the throne. When the Manchu confederation began consolidating in the northeast in the 1610s, the Ming response was similarly under-coordinated. The dynasty was running on its institutional momentum, and the momentum was slowing visibly with each year that the throne refused to engage.

There is one perfect detail worth pausing on. The Wanli Emperor's tomb at the Ming Tombs north of Beijing — the **Dingling**, the only Ming imperial tomb to have been excavated by modern archaeologists (it was opened in 1956–1958 in one of the most disastrous archaeological excavations of twentieth-century China, in which most of the recovered organic artifacts were destroyed by exposure before proper conservation methods could be applied) — contained the emperor's skeletal remains. Forensic analysis showed that Wanli had been physically incapacitated for much of his later reign; one leg was significantly shorter than the other, probably due to a chronic condition that had developed in his middle age, and would have made walking and standing extremely painful. Some modern historians have argued that part of the work strike was simple physical inability — the emperor was too ill to attend court ceremonies that required him to stand for hours in heavy ceremonial robes. The story is more complicated than pure royal sulkiness; but it is also true that nothing prevented him from delegating, from receiving officials privately, or from filling vacant offices through written orders. The work strike was substantially political in character, even if physical incapacity was a contributing factor.

---

The cultural and intellectual life of the late Ming, paradoxically, flourished during exactly the period of Wanli's withdrawal.

This is the chapter for one major late-Ming intellectual encounter: the arrival of the **Jesuit mission to China**, and particularly the figure of **Matteo Ricci** (mah-TEH-oh REE-chee).

The Catholic missionary order called the **Society of Jesus** had been founded by the Spanish soldier-mystic Ignatius of Loyola in 1540, in the wake of the Protestant Reformation. The Jesuits became the spear point of Catholic missionary expansion across the early-modern world — to Brazil, to India under the Portuguese, to Japan, and (from the 1580s) to China. The Jesuit missionary strategy was distinctive: rather than confronting the local culture, they would master it from inside — learn the language, adopt the local elite's clothing and manners, study the local intellectual traditions, and try to position Christianity as compatible with rather than opposed to local civilization. The strategy was controversial within the Catholic Church (the Dominicans and Franciscans favored a more confrontational approach) and would eventually lead to the Rites Controversy that closed the China mission in the early eighteenth century. But for over a century, it was the most sophisticated cross-cultural project in world history.

The Jesuit mission to China was founded in 1583 by **Michele Ruggieri** and **Matteo Ricci**, both of whom established themselves first in Macao and then at provincial Chinese cities in Guangdong. Ricci would become the dominant figure of the early mission. Born in 1552 in central Italy, trained at the Roman College in mathematics, astronomy, and theology, he arrived in China in 1582 at age thirty. He spent twenty years studying classical Chinese — eventually achieving the rare feat of foreign mastery of the language to a level where he could write substantive treatises in classical Chinese — and adapting himself to Chinese elite culture. By 1601 he had reached Beijing and was the first European granted permanent residence at the Ming court.

Ricci's approach was ingenious. He dressed in the robes of a Confucian scholar (after initially trying Buddhist monastic robes, which had identified him with a religious tradition the Confucian elite generally looked down on). He studied the Confucian classics deeply enough to argue Christian theology in their vocabulary. He emphasized the points of compatibility — the Confucian heaven (Tian) as analogous to the Christian God, the Confucian ethics of family and ritual as compatible with Christian moral teaching — while quietly setting aside the points of conflict (the Confucian veneration of ancestors, which Catholic doctrine considered idolatrous, would become the issue that broke the mission a century later). He used his European mathematical and scientific training as currency for cultural respect — translating Euclidean geometry into Chinese (the first six books of Euclid's *Elements*, translated with the Chinese scholar **Xu Guangqi** (shoo gwahng-CHEE) and published in 1607), demonstrating European-style world maps that displaced China from the center of the world (and were enormously controversial for that reason), discussing European astronomical methods that improved on Chinese calendrical calculations.

His Chinese-language treatises shaped the next two centuries of European understanding of China and Chinese understanding of Europe. **The True Meaning of the Lord of Heaven** (Tianzhu Shiyi, 1603) was the most successful Christian apologetic ever written in Chinese, arguing for a monotheistic understanding of ultimate reality in vocabulary that drew on Confucian and Daoist precedent rather than rejecting them. **The Great Western Mnemonic** introduced European memory technique to Chinese scholars. The world map he drew at Beijing in 1602 (the **Kunyu Wanguo Quantu**, "Map of the Myriad Countries of the World") was the first European-style world map widely circulated in China and remained influential well into the Qing period.

Ricci was personally received by the Wanli Emperor (or at least granted formal court status, since the work-striking emperor probably never actually met him in person), given a residence in Beijing, allowed to build a small chapel, and integrated into the city's intellectual life. He died in Beijing in 1610, was buried in a state-granted plot of land outside the city walls, and was succeeded by other Jesuits — including **Adam Schall von Bell** and **Ferdinand Verbiest** — who would continue the mission well into the Qing period and would eventually direct the imperial astronomical bureau.

The Jesuit mission was the most sophisticated cross-cultural intellectual encounter of the early-modern world. It produced genuine European understanding of Chinese civilization (Ricci's *De Christiana expeditione apud Sinas*, published in Europe after his death, was the first detailed European description of Ming China based on extensive direct observation), and genuine Chinese understanding of European mathematical and scientific methods (the European astronomical techniques the Jesuits introduced were used to reform the Chinese calendar in the 1640s and remained the basis of imperial calendrics for the rest of the Qing). It is also worth noting what it didn't produce: the Jesuit mission did not produce mass conversion in China the way the parallel Catholic mission produced in the Philippines and parts of South America. The Jesuit converts in late Ming China numbered perhaps a few thousand, mostly drawn from the educated elite (including the senior Ming official Xu Guangqi, who was baptized in 1603 and translated Euclid with Ricci); the Chinese population at large remained almost entirely uninfluenced. The mission was a high-cultural encounter, not a popular religious movement.

What the encounter shows, structurally, is that the late Ming intellectual elite was open to engagement with foreign ideas under the right circumstances — when the ideas came packaged with mathematical rigor, ritual respect, and a willingness to operate within Chinese cultural categories. The same Ming culture that had abandoned Zheng He's voyages and turned inward against maritime engagement was simultaneously engaged in active intellectual exchange with European scholars at the imperial capital. The myth of a Ming China sealed off from the world by the **Haijin** maritime ban needs to be balanced against the reality of Matteo Ricci writing in classical Chinese in Beijing while Spanish silver flowed in through Manila and Portuguese silk-and-silver shuttles connected Macao to Goa. The late Ming was vastly more globally engaged than the official rhetoric of cultural self-sufficiency suggested.

---

Cross-civ check: the Wanli reign (1572–1620) was contemporary with the **Spanish Habsburg** apex under Philip II (the king who built the Escorial, dispatched the Armada in 1588, and presided over the Spanish silver economy that supplied so much of Wanli China's monetary base); with the **Elizabethan** and early **Jacobean** periods in England (Shakespeare's career runs almost exactly parallel to Wanli's reign); with the **Mughal apex** under Akbar and Jahangir in India; with the **Ottoman classical age** continuing under Murad III and Mehmed III; with the **Tokugawa unification** of Japan (Tokugawa Ieyasu defeated his rivals at Sekigahara in 1600 and established the shogunate in 1603); with **Galileo's** early career in Italy (his telescope observations began in 1609); with the **Dutch revolt** against Spanish rule that would establish the Dutch Republic. Most strikingly, Wanli's reign was contemporary with **Shah Abbas the Great** in Safavid Iran (1588–1629, Safavid Persia Ch 4) — the two rulers presided over their respective empires at the same moment, both at the height of cultural achievement and commercial integration into the early-modern global economy. Abbas's Isfahan and Wanli's Beijing were the two great Asian capitals of the early seventeenth century, both fully integrated into the global silver trade, both producing some of the most sophisticated cultural production of the early-modern world, and both presiding over institutional structures that would unravel within a few decades.

# Chapter 6 — The Imjin War

In April 1592, the Japanese warlord **Toyotomi Hideyoshi** (toh-yoh-TOH-mee hee-deh-YO-shee) — the unifier of Japan after the Sengoku period of civil wars — landed an invasion army of 158,000 men at Busan on the southern coast of Korea. His stated goal was nothing less than the conquest of Ming China, with Korea as the staging route. The Korean army collapsed almost immediately. The Korean capital at Seoul fell within three weeks. The Japanese forces drove north and east across the peninsula, sacking Pyongyang in June and reaching the Yalu River — the Korean-Chinese frontier — by the autumn of 1592.

The Ming response was the largest military intervention of the late Ming period. Wanli-era Beijing dispatched expeditionary armies that fought a seven-year war on Korean soil, kicking the Japanese out of most of Korea by 1593, fighting them to a standstill through five years of negotiation and renewed combat, and decisively breaking their final 1597 invasion. The war ended in 1598 only because Hideyoshi died in Japan and the Japanese armies withdrew.

The Imjin War (named after the Korean lunar-calendar year of the invasion, **imjin** in Korean) is one of the largest pre-modern wars in East Asian history. It killed perhaps two million people. It devastated Korea so thoroughly that the country took most of the seventeenth century to recover. It exhausted the Ming treasury at exactly the moment the Manchu confederation was rising in the northeast (Chapter 8). And it demonstrated, in the cruelest way possible, how much the late Ming dynasty's military reach exceeded its institutional capacity to sustain that reach.

This is the chapter about the war itself, the strategic situation that produced it, and the consequences for all three states involved.

---

The strategic situation that produced the war began with the Japanese unification.

For most of the sixteenth century, Japan had been in the **Sengoku** (SEN-go-ku, "Warring States") period — a long civil war in which competing warlord families fought across the archipelago. The unification was completed in three stages by three successive figures. **Oda Nobunaga** (oh-da no-boo-NAH-gah) broke the back of the central rivals between the 1560s and his assassination in 1582. **Toyotomi Hideyoshi**, his most effective lieutenant, completed the unification by 1590, defeating the last major rival families. **Tokugawa Ieyasu** (toh-koo-GAH-wah ee-eh-YA-soo) would consolidate the unification after Hideyoshi's death in 1598, founding the Tokugawa shogunate that ruled Japan from 1603 to 1868 (Edo Japan covers this in detail).

Hideyoshi by 1591 had unified Japan but had a serious political problem. The unification process had concentrated enormous military power in the hands of the great daimyo (regional lords) who had submitted to him, and those daimyo retained their armies and their territorial bases. Hideyoshi could not safely demobilize them — they had nowhere to go and no other livelihood than warfare. He needed an external war to keep his fractious vassals occupied. He chose Korea as the route to his stated grand ambition, the conquest of Ming China.

The choice of Korea was militarily logical and politically catastrophic. Korea was the obvious overland route to China — the Korean peninsula reaches almost to the Manchurian frontier — and it had been the bridge through which Chinese culture, Buddhist religion, and political institutions had reached Japan for over a millennium (Ancient Japan covers this in detail). The Korean **Joseon dynasty** (founded 1392, contemporary with the Ming founding) was a Confucian-tradition state that maintained vassal-tributary relations with Ming China and was militarily weak; Hideyoshi assessed, accurately, that he could overrun Korea quickly. What he assessed wrongly was the Ming response: he assumed Beijing would either ignore the Korean war or send a token force that he could brush aside. In fact the Ming sent successive expeditionary armies that, however dysfunctional their internal organization, were large enough and persistent enough to fight the Japanese to a standstill.

---

The war divides into two distinct phases separated by an extended truce.

The **first invasion** (1592–1593) was the catastrophic Korean collapse and the partial Ming recovery. Hideyoshi's army of 158,000 men, organized in nine divisions under his senior daimyo commanders, landed at Busan on April 13, 1592. The Korean army was structurally unprepared: long peace under the Joseon dynasty had hollowed out the Korean military, and the Korean government had ignored repeated warnings from observant officials about Japanese rearmament. Busan fell on the first day. Seoul (then called Hanseong) fell on May 19. The Korean court fled north to Pyongyang, then abandoned Pyongyang for the northern frontier town of Uiju on the Yalu, and from there sent desperate appeals to Beijing.

The Ming response was initially small. A first relief force of 5,000 men was sent in July 1592 and was defeated at the second Battle of Pyongyang in September. The Ming court then dispatched a much larger force of perhaps 43,000 troops under General **Li Rusong** (LEE roo-SONG), which arrived in Korea in late 1592 and recaptured Pyongyang in January 1593. The Ming-Korean force then drove south and recaptured Seoul in April 1593. By mid-1593 the Japanese had been pushed back to a defensive perimeter around Busan and the southern coast.

The first phase also produced what is, even in cold-blooded military-history terms, the most spectacular individual achievement of the war: the naval victories of the Korean admiral **Yi Sun-sin** (YEE soon-SHIN). Yi Sun-sin commanded the Joseon navy from a base on the southern coast and conducted a series of engagements against the Japanese supply convoys between 1592 and 1598. His ships included the famous **geobukseon** (geo-BOOK-son, "turtle ships") — armored Korean galleys with iron-spiked roofs to repel boarders, multiple cannons, and high freeboard for defensive standoff combat. Yi's tactics emphasized concentrating his small fleet against fragments of the much larger Japanese supply effort, defeating each fragment in detail through superior firepower and discipline. His victories at the Battles of **Hansando** (July 1592, in which his fleet of 56 ships destroyed a Japanese fleet of 73 ships using a crane-wing tactical formation) and **Myeongnyang** (October 1597, in which his fleet of 13 ships defeated a Japanese fleet of perhaps 333 ships in the strait between Jindo and the mainland through tactical exploitation of tidal currents) are studied in modern naval academies as classic examples of inferior force defeating superior force through tactical brilliance.

Yi Sun-sin's naval campaign was strategically decisive. The Japanese ground forces in Korea depended on supply convoys from Japan; Yi's command of the Korean Strait disrupted those supply lines repeatedly. The Japanese army could not be reinforced or resupplied at the rate it needed. Combined with the Ming intervention on land, Yi's naval campaign forced Hideyoshi to accept a stalemate.

---

The **truce period** (1593–1597) lasted four years and was dominated by Sino-Japanese negotiation that produced no settlement.

The negotiations were one of the more comically dysfunctional diplomatic exchanges in pre-modern history. Both sides were lying to their own governments about what the other side had agreed to. The Ming negotiator **Shen Weijing** told the Beijing court that Hideyoshi had agreed to accept Ming suzerainty as a tributary king. The Japanese negotiator **Konishi Yukinaga** told Hideyoshi that the Ming had agreed to send a Ming princess as Hideyoshi's bride and to recognize Japan as the dominant power in Korea. When the actual Ming embassy arrived at Hideyoshi's court in 1596 carrying an imperial seal investing Hideyoshi as "King of Japan" — a formal grant of Ming tributary status, which was an enormous insult to a man who considered himself superior to the Ming emperor — Hideyoshi was furious. The negotiations collapsed. The Japanese launched the second invasion in 1597.

The **second invasion** (1597–1598) was less ambitious than the first — Hideyoshi's stated goal had narrowed from conquering China to securing the southern Korean provinces — but more brutal. Japanese forces conducted systematic massacres in southern Korea, partly as deliberate policy to depopulate the area and partly as the retaliation of soldiers who knew the war was losing political support back home. The famous **Mound of Ears** (Mimizuka) at Kyoto was assembled from the noses of perhaps 38,000 Korean and Chinese dead — Japanese commanders had been required to send back ear-or-nose proof of kills, since heads were too heavy to ship across the strait. The Mound still stands today in Kyoto and is one of the most physically grim memorials of pre-modern East Asian warfare.

The Ming response was substantial. Larger expeditionary forces were dispatched in 1597. Major land battles were fought at **Jiksan** in September 1597 (Ming-Korean victory) and **Sacheon** in October 1598 (Japanese tactical victory but strategic stalemate). Yi Sun-sin's naval victories continued.

The war ended on August 18, 1598, when Hideyoshi died in Japan of natural causes. The succession Council of Five Regents he had appointed in his last months ordered the Japanese armies in Korea to withdraw. The withdrawal was conducted in late 1598, with the last major Japanese forces fighting their way out at the **Battle of Noryang** in December 1598 — the battle in which Yi Sun-sin himself was killed, struck by a Japanese musket ball while pursuing the retreating fleet. (Yi Sun-sin's last reported words, as he refused to allow his death to be announced before the battle ended, were: "The battle is at its height — beat my drum and do not announce my death.") The Japanese forces withdrew. The seven-year war was over.

---

The casualties were on a scale that requires sober accounting.

Korean deaths from the war and its associated famines and epidemics are estimated at roughly 1 million people, perhaps 20% of the pre-war Korean population — one of the worst per-capita demographic catastrophes of the early-modern period. Chinese deaths from the Ming military campaigns were probably in the tens of thousands of soldiers, plus indeterminate numbers of civilian casualties in border provinces. Japanese deaths in Korea were perhaps 200,000 across the seven years. Total deaths probably reached 2 million.

The economic damage was equally severe. Korea's productive capacity was devastated — entire provinces were depopulated, cultivated land returned to scrub, urban centers were burnt out. The Korean government printing offices were destroyed and rebuilt; the **Joseon** dynasty that emerged from the war was structurally weakened in ways that persisted for generations (Joseon Korea covers the post-Imjin recovery and the consequences for Korean intellectual life). Japan's losses included the political defeat of Hideyoshi's faction; the Toyotomi family was destroyed at the Battle of Sekigahara in 1600 by Tokugawa Ieyasu, who had stayed home from Korea and emerged with his army intact — partly because the Korean adventure had broken so many of his rivals. The Tokugawa shogunate that would rule Japan for the next 250 years was the indirect beneficiary of the Imjin War's political wreckage of the Toyotomi camp.

The Ming consequences were the most consequential for the future of East Asia. The treasury was effectively bankrupted by the war. The standard estimate is that the Ming spent roughly 20 to 26 million ounces of silver on the campaigns — a substantial fraction of the entire annual silver tax revenue under the Single Whip system, accumulated as a war-debt that the late-Wanli treasury could not absorb. The financial pressure forced cuts in other budgets at exactly the moment those budgets were needed: northern frontier defense was scaled back just as the **Manchu** confederation was beginning to consolidate (Chapter 8 covers Nurhaci's rise during exactly this period), provincial military maintenance was deferred, and the inadequately-paid soldiers of the late Ming weisuo system began to desert in large numbers.

There is a structural argument worth making about the connection between the Imjin War and the eventual Ming collapse. The Manchus were not a force the Ming had to face down with superior resources to defeat; they were a force the Ming could have managed with a functional northern frontier policy. The northern frontier policy depended on an adequately-funded and adequately-paid army, on attentive coordination with the Mongol tribal allies who served as buffer states, and on competent provincial military administration in Liaoning and Hebei. The Imjin War destroyed all three. The same silver that should have paid for the northern frontier defense was, instead, being shipped to Korea to pay for the war against Hideyoshi. The same generals who should have been running the northern military commands were, instead, dying or being recalled for service in Korea. The same political attention that should have been monitoring Manchu consolidation was, instead, focused on Korean negotiations.

The Ming would not collapse for another forty-six years after the Imjin War ended. But the financial and military foundations that the dynasty would need to defend against the Manchus had been cracked, irreparably, by the seven-year Korean campaign.

Cross-civ check: the Imjin War (1592–1598) was contemporary with the **Spanish Armada** (1588) — the failed Spanish invasion of Elizabethan England that, in different ways, marked the limit of Spanish Habsburg projection. The two events make an instructive comparison: Hideyoshi's failed conquest of China (and Korea) and Philip II's failed conquest of England were both the product of unifying autocrats overestimating their reach in the late sixteenth century, both ended in strategic catastrophe for the attacker, and both reshuffled the political balance of their respective regions. The Imjin War was also contemporary with the **Wanli era** culture and the **Late Ming** intellectual flowering described in Chapter 5 and Chapter 7 — the contradictory image is of Ming China simultaneously fighting an enormous war in Korea, hosting Matteo Ricci in Beijing, producing the great vernacular novels, watching its silver-economy peak, and quietly setting the stage for collapse. The most striking parallel may be with the **Ottoman-Safavid wars** of the same period (Safavid Persia Ch 3 and Ottoman Empire Ch 5) — another set of brutal large-scale early-modern wars between major Asian states that exhausted the participants and reshaped the longer-term balance of power. The 1590s in particular were a decade of multiple large wars across the Eurasian world; they are one of the most underrated turning points in early-modern global history.

# Chapter 7 — The Late Ming Cultural Peak

A pause is in order before the dynasty ends.

The chapters of this book that have already covered the late Ming have emphasized what was failing — the work-striking emperor, the eunuch corruption, the bankrupting Korean war, the rising Manchu threat. All of that is real and all of it is the political-military reality the next chapter will describe in detail. But the late Ming was simultaneously one of the most extraordinary cultural and intellectual periods in Chinese history. The same decades that produced the Wanli work strike produced the four great vernacular novels of Chinese literature, the philosophical school of Wang Yangming, the Donglin Academy reform movement, the most sophisticated print culture in the world, the Ming blue-and-white porcelain that became the global luxury good of the early-modern era, and a body of late-Ming art, drama, and literature that would shape East Asian high culture for the next four hundred years.

This is the chapter for the cultural side of the late Ming. It is the second of the late-Ming chapters because the cultural achievements depended in large part on the silver-economy and commercial expansion described in Chapter 5 — but the cultural and intellectual production deserves to be considered as a single subject rather than scattered across other chapters where it would be incidental.

---

The four great vernacular novels of Chinese literature — **Romance of the Three Kingdoms**, **Water Margin**, **Journey to the West**, and **Plum in the Golden Vase** — were all produced or canonized during the Ming period. Three of them reached the form in which they are now read during the Wanli era specifically. The fourth is so closely associated with the late Ming that its anonymous publication in the early seventeenth century is treated as a key Ming literary event.

The novels collectively constitute one of the great literary achievements of pre-modern world literature, and they require some context.

Chinese literature before the Ming had been overwhelmingly dominated by classical-language genres — poetry, philosophical essays, historical chronicles, ritual texts — written in literary Chinese (wenyan), the formal written language that bore roughly the same relation to spoken Chinese that medieval Latin bore to spoken Italian. The classical-language tradition was sophisticated, technically demanding, and aristocratic; it required years of formal education to master, and its readership was effectively limited to the small Confucian scholar-official class. Vernacular-language fiction (xiaoshuo, "small talk") existed as an oral storytelling tradition and as written texts in vernacular Chinese (baihua, the language of everyday speech), but it was treated by the elite literary tradition as essentially worthless — entertainment for illiterate or barely literate audiences, not serious art.

The Ming changed this. Across the period, particularly during the late Ming commercial expansion, the vernacular novel was elevated into a serious literary form. Writers — many of them from the elite scholar-official class but operating outside the formal examination system — began to produce long, ambitious vernacular works of fiction that drew on the oral storytelling tradition but transformed it into something new. The novels were enormously popular. They were also, increasingly, taken seriously by literary intellectuals as worthy of critical attention. By the late Ming, sophisticated commentaries on the major vernacular novels were being produced by leading literary figures. The novel had become, for the first time in Chinese tradition, a respectable major form.

---

**Romance of the Three Kingdoms** (Sanguo Yanyi) is traditionally attributed to **Luo Guanzhong** (LWAW gwahn-JONG), a fourteenth-century author about whom essentially nothing is known. Its text reached the form in which it is now read in the Mao Lun edition of 1522. It runs to roughly 800,000 words across 120 chapters and dramatizes the three-way civil war of the **Three Kingdoms** period (220–280 CE), when the unified Han empire had collapsed and three rival successor states — **Wei**, **Shu**, and **Wu** — fought across northern, southwestern, and southeastern China for over six decades (Six Dynasties Ch 1 covers the actual history).

The novel is a heroic-historical fiction. Its best-known characters — the warrior **Guan Yu** (gwahn YOO, who became the patron god of Chinese martial brotherhoods after his death in 219 CE and is still worshipped in temples across the Chinese cultural world today), the strategist **Zhuge Liang** (joo-GUH lyahng, the master of intricate stratagems), the warlord **Cao Cao** (TSOW TSOW, the morally ambiguous unifier of the north) — became the defining figures of the Chinese cultural imagination of historical heroism. The novel's representation of the period is selective and often inaccurate (Cao Cao in particular is treated as a villain in ways that the actual historical record does not entirely support), but its impact on Chinese popular understanding of the Three Kingdoms period has been so total that the novel and the actual history have become difficult to separate. It established the conventions of Chinese historical fiction and remains one of the most widely read Chinese books of all time.

**Water Margin** (Shuihu Zhuan, "Outlaws of the Marsh") is traditionally attributed to **Shi Nai'an** (SHIH ny-AHN), another late-Yuan or early-Ming author about whom little is known. It reached its standard form during the Ming and tells the story of 108 outlaws who form a bandit fellowship at **Liangshan Marsh** during the Northern Song period. The 108 are a mixed group of disgraced officials, fugitive criminals, military deserters, and skilled artisans who have been driven outside legitimate society by various combinations of injustice and bad luck. They form a sworn brotherhood of equals, dispense violent vigilante justice against corrupt officials and oppressive landlords, and eventually negotiate a complex relationship with the Song state.

The novel's politics are ambivalent in ways that have made it permanently controversial. It celebrates anti-establishment violence in the service of justice; it also ultimately argues for accommodation with imperial authority on the bandits' own moral terms. The book has been variously read as a peasant-rebellion classic (a view favored by Mao Zedong, who reportedly identified strongly with several of its characters), as a Confucian moral treatise about loyalty under impossible circumstances, and as a celebration of male brotherhood that is uneasy with conventional family obligations. It is one of the foundational works of Chinese popular fiction and has been adapted into film, television, and games more times than any other Chinese novel.

**Journey to the West** (Xi You Ji), attributed to **Wu Cheng'en** (WOO chong-EN, a sixteenth-century minor official from Anhui) and published in 1592, is the most beloved of the four great novels and the one most accessible to modern readers. It is a fantastical retelling of the seventh-century pilgrimage of the Tang Buddhist monk **Xuanzang** (shwen-DZAHNG, the historical figure who traveled to India between 629 and 645 CE to retrieve Buddhist scriptures, and whose actual Records of the Western Regions remains one of the most important pre-modern accounts of Central and South Asia) to India to retrieve sacred texts. In the novel, the pilgrim monk is accompanied by three demonic-but-redeemable disciples: the trickster **Monkey King Sun Wukong** (sun woo-KONG, the most famous character in Chinese vernacular literature), the lustful pig demon **Zhu Bajie** (joo bah-JYEH), and the river spirit **Sha Wujing** (shah woo-JING).

The narrative is episodic — the pilgrims travel west across China and India, encountering demons, gods, and supernatural challenges in chapter after chapter. The framing is Buddhist allegory (the journey represents the soul's progression toward enlightenment), the cosmology is Daoist (with extensive imagined geography of Heaven, Hell, and the various spiritual realms), and the moral framework is broadly Confucian (the disciples must learn humility, persistence, and team loyalty). The result is a vast comic-allegorical-spiritual narrative that can be read by children for its slapstick adventure and by adults for its religious-philosophical depth — which is why it has remained the most popular Chinese vernacular work for four centuries. Modern adaptations include the Japanese animated series *Dragon Ball* (whose protagonist is explicitly modeled on Sun Wukong), countless Chinese television and film adaptations, and the influential 1942 Chinese film *Princess Iron Fan* that was the first Asian feature-length animated film.

**Plum in the Golden Vase** (Jin Ping Mei, jin ping MAY) is the fourth great novel and the most controversial. Published anonymously in the early seventeenth century (the standard attribution to a "Lanling Xiaoxiao Sheng" — "Scoffing Scholar of Lanling" — is almost certainly a pseudonym, and the actual author has never been identified despite extensive scholarly speculation), the book is a merciless social satire of late-Ming bourgeois life, narrated through the household of the corrupt merchant **Ximen Qing** (shee-MEN ching). It is also the first Chinese novel of explicit sexual realism. The book's frankness about sex, particularly its detailed depiction of the sexual life of Ximen Qing's wives and concubines, made it the most banned book in Chinese literary history — its full text was illegal in China for most of the period from the 1620s to the 1980s, and only expurgated editions were officially circulated.

Modern critics consider it a masterpiece. The book's psychological observation, its social satire, and its representation of late-Ming material culture (the descriptions of food, clothing, household furnishings, and commercial transactions are some of the most detailed in any pre-modern Chinese text) are extraordinary. The novelist **Patricia Sieber** has argued that *Plum in the Golden Vase* should be read as one of the foundational works of modern psychological fiction worldwide, comparable to the early modern European novel tradition that was developing in roughly the same period. The book's reputation has been rehabilitated in academic Chinese literature departments since the 1980s, and it is now widely taught at universities — though full unexpurgated editions are still relatively recent in the People's Republic.

---

Beyond the novels, the late Ming was a major intellectual period in Chinese philosophy.

The most influential Ming philosopher was **Wang Yangming** (wahng yahng-MING, 1472–1529). Wang Yangming worked in the Confucian tradition (he was a senior official by training and a successful military commander in his late career) but developed a distinctive school of neo-Confucian thought sometimes called the **School of Mind** (Xinxue). His central doctrines were the **innate moral knowledge** of every person (liangzhi, "good knowing") and the **unity of knowledge and action** (zhi xing he yi). The first held that every human being possesses an innate moral compass, so that the goal of moral cultivation is to clear away the obscurations that prevent us from acting on what we already know. The second held that genuine moral knowledge is inseparable from moral action — knowing the right thing is not real knowledge unless it produces the right action.

These ideas may sound straightforward but they were revolutionary in the Ming context. The dominant orthodox Confucian school was the **Cheng-Zhu** tradition founded by **Zhu Xi** (joo SHEE) of the Southern Song (Tang-Song China Ch 8), which emphasized rigorous textual study of the classics, careful observation of moral propriety in daily life, and a long process of cultivation through which the Confucian gentleman gradually developed moral wisdom. The Cheng-Zhu school was the orthodoxy taught in the imperial examinations and the official ideology of the Ming state. Wang Yangming's School of Mind, by contrast, suggested that the textual study and ritualized cultivation could be short-circuited — every person already had the moral knowledge they needed, and what was required was practice rather than scholarship.

The implications were politically and socially radical. Wang Yangming's school suggested that women, peasants, and uneducated commoners could in principle achieve sage-like moral wisdom — they did not need access to the classical education that was the gatekeeper of orthodox Confucianism. It also suggested that engaged action in the world (politics, business, military service) was a legitimate path to moral cultivation, not a distraction from it. Wang Yangming's followers — including the radical **Li Zhi** (LEE jih), who in the late sixteenth century argued for women's intellectual equality, defended unconventional sexual relationships, and challenged Confucian historical orthodoxy in ways that got him repeatedly arrested — pushed his ideas in directions that the Confucian establishment found increasingly threatening.

Wang Yangming's school spread across late-Ming China and into Korea and Japan. In Japan, his thought (called **Yomeigaku**, yoh-MAY-gah-koo) became one of the major intellectual influences on the late Tokugawa-period reformers who would lead the Meiji Restoration of 1868 (Meiji Japan). In Korea, his ideas were treated as dangerously heterodox by the orthodox Joseon Confucian establishment but found influential readers nonetheless. Wang Yangming is, in retrospect, probably the most influential single Chinese thinker of the Ming period, and his ideas would shape East Asian intellectual life well into the modern era.

The opposing late-Ming intellectual movement was the **Donglin Society** (dong-LIN, "Eastern Forest"), a moralistic neo-Confucian reform faction founded in 1604 by scholars based at the Donglin Academy in **Wuxi** in the Yangtze delta. The Donglin scholars were Cheng-Zhu orthodox in their philosophy — they were the heirs of Zhu Xi rather than Wang Yangming — and they directed their energy toward political reform rather than philosophical innovation. They attacked court corruption, eunuch power, and what they saw as the moral decay of late-Ming politics. Their conflict with the eunuch Wei Zhongxian during the Tianqi reign in the 1620s (Chapter 8) produced one of the most violent factional purges of the Ming, with many Donglin scholars tortured to death in 1625–1626.

The Donglin movement is one of the most remarkable late-Ming political phenomena. It was the first significant attempt in Chinese history to organize an opposition political movement — a faction defined not by personal loyalty to a particular leader but by a shared political-moral program — operating outside the regular bureaucratic structure but within the broader Confucian intellectual class. The Donglin scholars conducted their politics through public lectures at the Donglin Academy, through circulated written manifestos, through coordinated petitions and memorials to the throne, and through the cultivation of sympathetic provincial officials. They were destroyed by Wei Zhongxian's purge but their model — the academy-based reform movement — would recur in later Chinese political history.

---

The cultural production of the late Ming depended on infrastructure, and the infrastructure was the **printing explosion**.

Woodblock printing had been invented under the Tang and refined under the Song (Tang-Song China Ch 6). What changed in the late Ming was scale. Commercial publishing — driven by the growing literate population, the silver-economy purchasing power, and the falling cost of paper — became a substantial industry across the Yangtze delta cities and Fujian. By the late sixteenth century, China had probably the most extensive print culture in the world. Novels, drama, encyclopedias, manuals, ritual texts, examination cribs, illustrated guidebooks, religious texts, technical compendia, and erotic literature all circulated in cheap printed editions. The famous late-Ming illustrated woodblock-printed editions of the great novels and the major Yuan dramas (the Yuan Dynasty Ch 5 productions of Guan Hanqing and others were widely reprinted in the late Ming) are some of the most beautiful examples of pre-modern East Asian book production.

The economic structure was distinctive. Chinese woodblock printing required carving each page as a separate wooden block (rather than using the movable-type system that was simultaneously developing in Europe with Gutenberg). The block-carving was labor-intensive and expensive for the initial production, but once a set of blocks was complete, additional copies could be printed cheaply for the lifetime of the blocks. This meant that the economics favored long print runs of standard texts — novels, classics, examination guides, religious works — rather than the small-batch printing of pamphlets that European printing favored. The result was a Chinese print culture that was extensive in coverage but relatively static in content, with long-running editions of standard texts dominating the market.

By 1600 the late Ming reading public was enormous. Modern estimates of literacy in late-Ming China range widely but suggest perhaps 10–20% of adult males had functional literacy and another 10–15% had partial literacy adequate for reading simple texts. This was probably comparable to the most literate European societies of the same period (the Dutch Republic, parts of Protestant Germany) and substantially higher than the European average. The reading public supported not just classical scholarship but a thriving popular print market — the same novels that the elite literary critics were beginning to take seriously were circulating in cheap editions throughout the rural Yangtze region.

---

The most globally consequential late Ming cultural product, in terms of its impact on the rest of the world, was **blue-and-white porcelain**.

Ming blue-and-white porcelain from the imperial kilns at **Jingdezhen** (jing-deh-JEN) became the most globally traded luxury good of the early-modern world. The technique combined high-fired white porcelain (the white kaolin clay specific to the Jingdezhen region, fired at temperatures higher than European ceramic technology could achieve) with cobalt-blue underglaze decoration (the cobalt was originally imported from Persian sources, hence the late-medieval Persian and later Ottoman appreciation for the same blue palette). The combination of the brilliant white body and the rich blue decoration produced an instantly recognizable aesthetic that became the standard of porcelain everywhere it was traded.

The Jingdezhen kilns had been operating for centuries — they were already prestigious under the Song — but the late Ming was their commercial peak. The town by 1600 was producing massive quantities of porcelain for both domestic and export markets. The export trade ran primarily through Macao to Manila and Goa, then on to European markets, and through Quanzhou and other Fujian ports to Japan, Korea, and Southeast Asia. By the early seventeenth century, Ming blue-and-white porcelain was the standard luxury good of European wealthy households — appearing in Dutch still-life paintings, in the inventories of European royal collections, and (most influentially) as the model that Dutch potters at **Delft** were attempting to copy. The "Delft blue" ware that eventually became one of the iconic Dutch craft products was originally a deliberate imitation of Ming Chinese porcelain for European households who could not afford the real thing — a remarkable case of European craft tradition being founded on the attempt to reproduce a Chinese export good.

The export numbers are striking. By the 1620s, the Dutch East India Company alone was importing perhaps 200,000 pieces of Chinese porcelain per year. The total export volume across all European trading networks was probably in the millions of pieces annually. Combined with the Chinese domestic market, the Jingdezhen kilns were producing porcelain at industrial scale — one of the great pre-modern instances of mass production for global markets.

The cultural implications spread far beyond Europe. Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal courts all collected Ming porcelain as a luxury good (Safavid Persia Ch 6 covers the parallel Safavid carpet trade; the Persian and Ottoman cobalt-blue Iznik ceramics of the same period were partly inspired by Ming originals). Japanese tea masters incorporated Ming porcelain into the tea ceremony aesthetic. The porcelain itself became one of the early-modern world's truly global commodities — a product manufactured in central China that circulated, in essentially identical form, from London to Lima to Constantinople to Edo. The "blue and white" aesthetic that the Ming developed remains, four centuries later, one of the most recognizable visual styles in human material culture.

---

A pause, on the contradictions of the late Ming.

The cultural achievements just described were produced during the same decades the previous chapters have characterized as ones of political-military decline. This is not a coincidence; it is a structural feature of the late Ming. The same silver economy that bankrupted the treasury through the Imjin War supported the commercial publishing industry that produced the great vernacular novels. The same Wanli Emperor who refused to attend court was the patron of state-sponsored porcelain production. The same eunuch corruption that strangled the regular bureaucracy created the imperial workshops that produced some of the highest-quality late-Ming art. The cultural and the political worlds had drifted apart in ways that allowed each to follow its own logic.

The late Ming was, in this sense, an early-modern parallel to several other historical moments when great cultural production has accompanied or even preceded political collapse. The late Roman Empire of the fourth century produced a cultural-religious flowering (Augustine of Hippo, the early monasteries, the great mosaics) while the western state was disintegrating. The late Mughal Empire of the early eighteenth century produced enormous architectural and artistic achievement (the Lahore Fort, the Mughal miniature tradition's late peak) as the political center crumbled. The late Heian period in Japan produced *The Tale of Genji* and the foundational literature of Japanese vernacular culture as the imperial state was being captured by the warrior class.

The pattern suggests a generalization: cultural production does not require functional politics. It requires economic prosperity, an audience, and the institutional structures (workshops, print houses, academies, patronage networks) that allow specialized creative labor to operate. The late Ming had all three, despite the political dysfunction. When the political collapse finally arrived in the 1640s, it took most of the cultural infrastructure with it — the Ming-Qing transition is one of the most demographically destructive episodes in pre-modern Chinese history — but the cultural achievements of the late Ming had already been produced, printed, exported, and absorbed into Chinese (and global) cultural memory in ways that the political collapse could not undo.

The novels were still printed under the Qing. The porcelain was still produced. Wang Yangming's philosophy was still studied. Ricci's translations were still consulted. The Forbidden City was still standing, with new occupants. The Ming dynasty died; much of what the Ming had built lived on, in altered forms, under the Qing and the centuries that followed.

The **Spanish Golden Age** literary peak — Cervantes, Lope de Vega, Calderón — was simultaneous (Cervantes published *Don Quixote* in 1605; the same year Matteo Ricci published the first Chinese translation of European geometric optics in Beijing). The **Mughal court** under Akbar and Jahangir was producing the Mughal miniature tradition's classical phase. The **Safavid court** under Abbas the Great was producing the Isfahan school of miniature painting and the philosophical synthesis of Mulla Sadra (Safavid Persia Ch 5). The **Tokugawa Japan** of the early seventeenth century was producing the early literature of the Edo period. The early seventeenth century is one of the most extraordinary cultural moments in world history, with several major literary and artistic traditions reaching peak production simultaneously, and the late Ming was one of the most intensive of these.

# Chapter 8 — The Manchu Conquest

The Wanli Emperor died in 1620 after a forty-eight-year reign, the longest of any Ming ruler. The throne passed to his eldest son the **Taichang Emperor** (tigh-CHAHNG), who reigned for one month before dying — possibly poisoned, possibly killed by the Red Pill medication a court physician had prescribed for an unrelated ailment. The throne then passed to Taichang's eldest son the **Tianqi Emperor** (tyahn-CHEE), a barely literate teenager whose abiding interest was carpentry rather than governance. The eunuch faction at court — by then in its 175th year of accumulating institutional power — closed in on the new boy emperor. The chief eunuch who emerged as the dominant figure was **Wei Zhongxian** (way jong-shyen), and from 1620 to 1627 he would effectively rule the Ming as the most powerful eunuch dictator in Chinese history.

This is the chapter about the end. The Wei Zhongxian period; the rise of the Manchu confederation under Nurhaci and Hong Taiji; the Chongzhen Emperor's desperate attempt to save the dynasty; the peasant rebellion of Li Zicheng; the suicide on Coal Hill; Wu Sangui's defection at Shanhaiguan; the Qing founding; the Southern Ming resistance; and the long Ming legacy that survived the dynasty's collapse.

---

**Wei Zhongxian** had been a minor palace eunuch during the Wanli reign, originally a low-status servant who had reportedly castrated himself in adulthood to escape gambling debts (the standard route into the palace eunuch establishment was castration in childhood, so adult castration to enter the system was unusual and morally suspect by Confucian standards). He attached himself to the household of the Tianqi Emperor's wet nurse **Madam Ke**, who became one of the boy emperor's most trusted personal advisors after his accession. Through Madam Ke's influence Wei rose rapidly through the eunuch hierarchy, taking control of the **Directorate of Ceremonial** and the **Eastern Depot** secret police by 1623.

What Wei Zhongxian did with the institutional power was straightforward and brutal. He purged the regular bureaucracy of officials who opposed him, particularly the Donglin Society reformers (Chapter 7). The Donglin purge of 1625–1626 was one of the most violent factional eliminations of the Ming period — leading Donglin scholars were arrested by Wei's secret police, transported to Beijing, tortured to extract confessions, and executed. **Yang Lian** (yahng LYEN), the most prominent Donglin opponent of Wei, was tortured to death over forty-five days in 1625; his death produced a famous farewell letter to his sons that became one of the canonical texts of late-Ming Confucian martyrdom.

Wei Zhongxian also organized something that no previous Chinese eunuch had attempted: a personal cult. By the mid-1620s, provincial officials were erecting **shrines to Wei Zhongxian** across the empire — temples dedicated to the living chief eunuch as a quasi-divine figure, with statues, ceremonial offerings, and ritual prostration. Some of the shrines reportedly placed Wei's statue alongside or even in front of statues of Confucius. The Tianqi Emperor approved or tolerated the cult, partly because he was politically irrelevant in his own court and partly because the carpentry workshops in his apartments left him little time for politics.

The Tianqi Emperor died in 1627, at age twenty-one, possibly from a riverboat accident, possibly from the cumulative effects of years of unhealthy indoor living. His younger brother **Zhu Youjian** (joo yoh-JYEN) inherited the throne and took the reign name **Chongzhen** (chong-JEN, "Honorable and Auspicious"). Chongzhen was sixteen years old at his accession and was, by all accounts, a serious young man who understood exactly what Wei Zhongxian had been doing during his brother's reign.

Within months of his accession, Chongzhen moved against Wei. The chief eunuch was stripped of office in late 1627 and exiled. En route to his place of exile Wei was informed of new charges against him, realized that execution was inevitable, and hanged himself in a roadside inn. His associates were purged from the court; the Donglin scholars he had killed were posthumously rehabilitated; the personal-cult shrines were demolished.

The young Chongzhen Emperor had inherited a dying dynasty. His seventeen-year reign (1627–1644) would be a desperate attempt to save it, conducted by a man with the right instincts but the wrong personality for the task.

---

While the Wei Zhongxian crisis was unfolding at the Beijing court, the **Manchu confederation** was consolidating in the northeast.

The Manchus were a Tungusic-speaking people whose ancestral territory was the forested zone between the Yalu River and the Amur, in modern Manchuria. They were the descendants of the **Jurchen** who had founded the **Jin dynasty** in the twelfth century and conquered northern China from the Northern Song (Tang-Song China Ch 8). The Mongol conquest of the Jin in 1234 (Yuan Dynasty Ch 1 covers the campaigns) had broken Jurchen political unity, and for the next four centuries the Jurchen had existed as a fragmented collection of frontier tribes, sometimes nominally subject to the Ming, sometimes raiding the Ming frontier for plunder, never strong enough to threaten the Chinese state.

The unifier who changed this was **Nurhaci** (NUR-hah-chee, 1559–1626), a chieftain of the **Jianzhou Jurchen** living in the forests just north of the Ming Liaoning frontier. Nurhaci's family had a long history of complicated relations with the Ming — his father and grandfather had been killed by Ming forces in 1583, and the young Nurhaci had received Ming compensation that he used to build up his own forces. Across the 1580s, 1590s, and 1600s he progressively unified the Jurchen tribes, partly through military conquest and partly through political marriage alliances. By 1616 he had unified enough of the Jurchen that he proclaimed the **Later Jin dynasty** — explicitly modeling the new state on the historical Jin of the twelfth century.

Nurhaci's institutional innovations were what made the Manchu state militarily effective. He invented a written script for the previously oral Jurchen language (the Manchu script, modified from the Mongolian Uyghur script in 1599 and further refined by his son Hong Taiji). He organized his fighters into the **Eight Banners** (Baqi) — eight color-coded military-administrative units that were both army formations and units of household registration. The Eight Banners (organized initially in 1601, expanded to eight in 1615) were the foundational institution of the rising Manchu state. Each Banner contained companies of warriors with their attached households; the banner system tied military service, household registration, taxation, and ethnic identity together into a single administrative structure. Mongol and Han Chinese allies who joined the rising Manchu state were eventually organized into Mongol Eight Banners and Han Eight Banners parallel to the Manchu Banners.

In 1618 Nurhaci formally declared war on the Ming, listing seven grievances (mostly involving Ming border incursions and unpaid trade obligations). Over the following decade his forces took most of Liaoning from the Ming, defeating successive Ming relief armies and consolidating a substantial state along the Manchurian frontier. He died in 1626 at age sixty-seven, succeeded by his eighth son **Hong Taiji** (HONG TIGH-jee), who proved to be an even more capable consolidator.

Hong Taiji renamed the people from Jurchen to **Manchu** in 1635 (the etymology of "Manchu" is contested but probably derives from a Buddhist-religious title). In 1636 he renamed the Later Jin dynasty as the **Qing** (CHING, "pure" or "clear") — explicitly setting the new state up as a successor to the Ming rather than a revival of the historical Jurchen Jin. The Qing court adopted Chinese-style imperial ceremonies, recruited Han Chinese advisors and officials in increasing numbers, and prepared institutional infrastructure that could administer Chinese provinces if the Manchus succeeded in conquering them. Hong Taiji died in 1643, at age fifty-one, just before the Manchus would actually take Beijing. The succession passed to his five-year-old son the **Shunzhi Emperor**, with Hong Taiji's brother **Dorgon** (DOR-gon) as prince-regent.

Through the late 1620s and 1630s, the Chongzhen Emperor in Beijing was responding to the Manchu threat with a combination of frontier defenses (massive investment in fortifications at Shanhaiguan and along the Liaoning frontier), expensive military expeditions (none of which decisively defeated the Manchus), and obsessive personnel changes (Chongzhen executed seventeen chief ministers in his seventeen-year reign, alternating between contradictory strategic approaches as different advisors gained and lost his trust). The Manchu war was costing the Ming treasury enormous sums during exactly the years that the Spanish silver imports were beginning to decline (the late 1620s and 1630s saw the start of a global silver depression that contributed to fiscal crises across the early-modern world). The financial squeeze on the Ming was severe.

The financial squeeze translated into rural crisis. Ming peasants, taxed in silver under the Single Whip system, were facing grain prices that had collapsed (because silver was scarce and grain was abundant in the relatively peaceful Chinese interior) while their tax obligations remained constant in silver terms. A silver-payment tax that took 30% of a peasant's grain income in 1620 took 60% or more by 1635, simply because the silver-grain exchange rate had moved against the peasants. The pressure on rural China was enormous and rising.

---

The peasant rebellion that destroyed the dynasty broke out in **Shaanxi** province in the early 1630s.

Shaanxi was particularly vulnerable to the silver-fiscal squeeze because it was a marginal agricultural region where the silver tax burden was punishing even in good years. A series of bad harvests in the late 1620s and early 1630s, combined with the Ming government's reduction of the post-station courier service (a budget-cutting measure that threw thousands of formerly-employed couriers into unemployment), produced an explosive rural population of unemployed and starving men ready for rebellion.

The leader who emerged from this milieu was **Li Zicheng** (LEE dzih-CHENG, 1606–1645). Li Zicheng was a former post-station courier from Shaanxi who joined a rebel band in 1631 and rose through the bandit-warlord politics of the late-Ming northwest to become the dominant rebel commander by the mid-1640s. His army called itself the **Dashun** ("Great Shun") and fought under the ideology of overthrowing Ming corruption and restoring just government — a relatively coherent political-military program by rebel standards, with explicit promises of tax relief and the elimination of Confucian official corruption.

Li Zicheng's army swept across northern China through the 1630s and 1640s. He captured **Xi'an** in October 1643 and proclaimed himself emperor of the Shun dynasty. From Xi'an he marched east toward Beijing in early 1644. The Ming defenses crumbled almost completely in his path — provincial garrisons either surrendered without serious resistance or defected outright to the rebel cause. The Chongzhen Emperor, in Beijing, found that the imperial army he had spent years rebuilding was no longer mobilizable; most of the surviving Ming military strength was concentrated on the Manchu frontier under the general **Wu Sangui** at Shanhaiguan, hundreds of miles away from where it was now needed.

On April 25, 1644, Li Zicheng's army entered Beijing without significant resistance.

---

What happened that morning is one of the most viscerally remembered episodes of dynastic collapse in Chinese cultural memory.

The Chongzhen Emperor had spent the previous days desperately trying to organize a defense or arrange an evacuation. Neither was possible. The senior court officials had mostly fled or were paralyzed; the imperial guard had largely deserted; the Beijing populace was, in the standard pattern of cities about to be sacked, either cooperating with the incoming rebels or hiding. Late on the evening of April 24, Chongzhen reportedly killed his fifteen-year-old daughter Princess Changping with his own sword — telling her, according to the chronicles, "Why were you born into our house?" — and left her wounded but alive. He attempted to kill his other daughters but reportedly lost his nerve. He sent his three sons into hiding with eunuch attendants. He drank himself partway to oblivion. Then, before dawn on April 25, he walked out of the Forbidden City with one eunuch attendant, climbed **Coal Hill** (Jingshan, jing-SHAHN) — the artificial hill just north of the Forbidden City built from the soil excavated to dig the palace moat — and hanged himself from a pagoda tree at the summit.

He left a suicide note. The note (the version preserved in Ming Shi may be apocryphal but is the standard text) blamed his ministers for the catastrophe: "All my ministers have failed me. As I die the imperial diadem is gone from my head, my hair covers my face. Let the rebels carve my body to pieces but spare my people." His body was found later that morning by Li Zicheng's troops entering the Forbidden City.

The pagoda tree on Coal Hill (or its replacement; the original was reportedly cut down in the Cultural Revolution as a symbol of feudal monarchy) is still pointed out to visitors today as the spot where the Ming dynasty died. Coal Hill itself is now Jingshan Park, just north of the Forbidden City; visitors can climb to the pavilion at the summit and look down on the imperial palace from the same perspective Chongzhen would have had on his last morning.

The 276-year Ming dynasty had ended its reign over the imperial capital. Li Zicheng's Dashun regime occupied Beijing for forty-two days.

---

The events of those forty-two days were the hinge on which Chinese history would turn.

Li Zicheng's regime in Beijing was disorganized and brutal. His army, raised on a peasant-rebellion ideology of redistributing wealth from corrupt Ming officials, treated the surviving Beijing nobility and bureaucracy as legitimate targets for plunder. Senior officials were arrested, tortured, and forced to surrender hidden assets. The most famous casualty of the looting was **Chen Yuanyuan** (chen yu-ahn-YU-ahn), the favorite concubine of the Ming general Wu Sangui — Chen was reportedly seized by one of Li Zicheng's senior commanders, and the seizure became the personal trigger that moved Wu Sangui to defect.

The defection mattered because Wu Sangui commanded the **Liaodong army**, the main surviving Ming military force, garrisoned at the Shanhaiguan pass on the eastern end of the Great Wall where the Wall meets the Pacific Ocean. Shanhaiguan was the strategic gateway between Manchuria and the Chinese plain — the single most important fortress on the entire Manchu frontier, and the pass through which any Manchu invasion would have to come. Wu Sangui had spent years fighting the Manchus at Shanhaiguan; his army was the elite of late-Ming military forces, equipped with cannons and arquebuses, with extensive experience of Manchu cavalry tactics.

When Beijing fell in April 1644, Wu Sangui was on the march south with his army to relieve the capital — he was still moving when news arrived that Beijing had already fallen and Chongzhen was dead. Wu turned around and returned to Shanhaiguan to assess his options. He had three. He could submit to Li Zicheng's Shun regime; he could attempt to restore a Ming successor as a separate state in the south; or he could ally with the Manchus against Li Zicheng.

He chose the Manchus.

The decision was made in May 1644. Wu sent envoys to the Manchu prince-regent Dorgon proposing an alliance — Wu's army and the Manchu Eight Banners would jointly attack Li Zicheng, with the Manchus crossing the Wall through the Shanhaiguan gate that Wu would open from the inside. Dorgon accepted. On May 27, 1644, the combined Wu-Manchu army defeated Li Zicheng's Shun army at the **Battle of Shanhai Pass** in a hard-fought engagement on the open plain just south of the fortress. Li Zicheng retreated to Beijing, looted the city in panic, set fire to several of the imperial palaces, and abandoned Beijing on June 4. The Manchus entered Beijing on June 6.

On the day Dorgon's troops entered Beijing, the prince-regent went directly to the Forbidden City, occupied the imperial residence, and proclaimed the **Qing dynasty** as the legitimate successor to the Ming. The five-year-old Shunzhi Emperor was officially enthroned in the imperial palace shortly thereafter. The Qing presented themselves not as foreign conquerors but as restorers of order against the rebel Li Zicheng — they had, in their formal account, avenged the Chongzhen Emperor by destroying his rebel killers, and were now legitimately taking the throne the Ming dynasty had lost. The framing was politically essential and would shape Qing-Ming relations for the next 268 years.

Wu Sangui's name has been one of the most cursed in Chinese history ever since. His decision to let the Manchus through Shanhaiguan was the single most consequential individual act of the Ming-Qing transition, and it has been remembered — in standard Han nationalist tradition — as the betrayal that cost China two and a half centuries of foreign rule. Wu himself was rewarded by the Qing with vast personal estates in Yunnan, ruled them as a quasi-independent feudatory until his death in 1678, and then his son led the **Three Feudatories Rebellion** of 1673–1681 in a doomed attempt to seize power back from the Qing. The Qing crushed the rebellion. The Wu family was extinguished. Wu Sangui's place in Chinese moral history was settled.

---

The Ming did not actually end on Coal Hill in April 1644.

A series of **Southern Ming** loyalist regimes continued to claim the imperial title and resist the Qing for nearly two more decades. The first, the **Hongguang regime**, was proclaimed at Nanjing in May 1644 (just weeks after Chongzhen's suicide) under the Ming prince Zhu Yousong. The Hongguang court controlled most of southern China for about a year before Qing forces under Dorgon's general **Dodo** captured Nanjing in May 1645 and executed the prince. The next claimant, the **Longwu regime** at Fuzhou in Fujian, lasted from 1645 until Qing capture in late 1646. The third, the **Yongli regime** in the southwest, lasted longest — from 1646 until 1662, holding out in Guangxi, then Yunnan, and finally fleeing into Burma where the Yongli emperor was eventually captured and handed over to the Qing for execution in 1662.

The Southern Ming resistance also produced one remarkable maritime episode. **Zheng Chenggong** (jeng chong-GONG, 1624–1662), known to Europeans as **Koxinga** from the Hokkien pronunciation of his honorific title Guoxingye ("Imperial-Surname-Lord"), was the son of the Hokkien merchant-warlord Zheng Zhilong (one of the most successful figures of the late-Ming East China Sea smuggling networks). Koxinga became a Ming loyalist and led the most successful late-Ming naval resistance against the Qing through the 1650s, operating from a base in coastal Fujian and conducting raids that briefly threatened Nanjing in 1659. Driven from the mainland in 1661, he turned his fleet against the Dutch East India Company's outpost on **Taiwan** — at that point a Dutch colonial settlement based at Fort Zeelandia near modern Tainan — and after a nine-month siege expelled the Dutch from the island. He established the **Kingdom of Tungning** as a Ming-loyalist state on Taiwan; his descendants ruled the island until the Qing conquest of 1683.

The Southern Ming resistance is a substantial chapter of post-Ming history in its own right. What matters for this narrative is that the Ming dynasty's institutional collapse in 1644 was followed by nearly two decades of organized loyalist resistance, that the Qing did not fully control southern China until the suppression of the Three Feudatories in the 1680s, and that the final extinction of the Ming political project came not in Beijing in 1644 but in Burma in 1662 — when the captured Yongli emperor was executed, ending the formal succession.

---

What survived from the Ming, despite the collapse, was extensive.

**Beijing** survived as the imperial capital. The Qing dynasty kept the capital where the Ming had built it, kept the Forbidden City as their imperial palace (with relatively minor architectural modifications), kept the Temple of Heaven for their own annual sacrifices, kept the basic spatial-political organization of the city unchanged. The Beijing that visitors see today is, in its essential layout, the Ming capital that Yongle built — modified by the Qing, then by the Republic, then by the People's Republic, but with the original Ming foundations still visible underneath.

The **Forbidden City** survived as the imperial palace. The Qing made decorative changes (Manchu-language signage was added alongside the Chinese, certain Manchu shamanic ritual spaces were added in the inner palace) but kept the basic structure. The Forbidden City's continuity from Yongle's reign to its closure in 1924 is essentially unbroken. It is now one of the most visited monuments on Earth.

The **Great Wall** survived as the visible northern frontier. The Qing did not maintain it (the Manchus had no need for fortifications against their own former tribal homeland north of the Wall) but did not destroy it either. The Wall decayed across the Qing period, was rediscovered by Western tourists in the late nineteenth century, and was systematically restored in the second half of the twentieth century. It remains the most photographed monument in China.

The **four great vernacular novels** survived as the foundational works of Chinese popular fiction. They were continuously printed under the Qing, adapted into endless theatrical and operatic productions, and are still the most widely read Chinese books today. *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* and *Journey to the West* are taught in Chinese primary schools.

**Ming porcelain** survived as the global standard for porcelain art. Jingdezhen continued operating under the Qing (though the imperial wares of the Qing — particularly the famille-rose ceramics of the Yongzheng and Qianlong reigns — would eventually displace Ming blue-and-white as the most prized Chinese porcelain in collector markets). The Ming blue-and-white aesthetic remains, four centuries later, the visual style most associated with Chinese ceramic art in global imagination.

The **examination system** in its Eight-Legged Essay form survived as the institutional template of Qing civil service recruitment. The Qing maintained the Ming examination structure essentially unchanged from 1644 until the abolition of the examinations in 1905 — meaning that the system Hongwu had restored after the Yuan suspension and that the mid-Ming had codified in its formal essay structure ran continuously for over five centuries before the Qing finally abandoned it.

The **Mandate of Heaven** survived as the ideological framework of Chinese imperial legitimacy. The Qing presented themselves as legitimate inheritors of the Mandate from the Ming, conducted the formal commemorations of the dead Chongzhen Emperor (he was buried with Ming imperial honors at the Ming Tombs north of Beijing, the only Ming imperial tomb explicitly authorized by the conquering successor dynasty), and claimed the standard cosmological-political framework that every Chinese dynasty since the Zhou had used. The framing worked. By the early eighteenth century, the Qing was generally accepted as a legitimate Chinese dynasty rather than a foreign occupation, in a way that the Mongol Yuan never had been (Yuan Dynasty Ch 8 covers the comparable Yuan-Ming legitimacy question).

What did not survive was the Han ethnic-political identity that the Ming had embodied. The Qing was, like the Yuan before it, a foreign-conquest dynasty — the Manchu ruling class governed Han China for 268 years through a complicated set of ethnic-political arrangements (the Eight Banner system was preserved as the institutional anchor of Manchu identity throughout the Qing period, with Manchus enjoying legal privileges and military monopolies that distinguished them from the Han majority). The Han nationalist framework that would eventually overthrow the Qing in 1911–1912 explicitly treated the Manchu conquest as a foreign occupation comparable to the Mongol Yuan, and the early twentieth-century Republican movement self-consciously identified with the Ming dynasty against the Qing in much the same way the Hongwu Emperor had identified the Ming founding against the Yuan. The Ming-Qing-Republic-PRC sequence is, in its standard Han-nationalist reading, a story of the periodic recovery of Han Chinese self-government against foreign-conquest interregna.

---

A pause, on the deeper meaning of the Ming.

The Ming dynasty's place in Chinese cultural memory is unique among the major dynasties. The Tang and Song are remembered as cultural and economic peaks; the Han is remembered as the foundational Chinese imperial state; the Qin is remembered as the unifier; the Yuan and Qing are remembered as foreign-conquest interregna. The Ming is remembered as something more specific and more loaded: as the last fully Han Chinese dynasty, the dynasty whose architecture and material culture are the standard image of "traditional China" for both Chinese and foreign audiences, the dynasty whose collapse in 1644 marks the boundary between "Ming-style China" and the more complicated multi-ethnic Qing reality.

The image is partly accurate and partly mythological. The Ming was a Han Chinese dynasty in the sense that the ruling family and the Confucian official class were ethnically Han, but it was also a dynasty deeply engaged with the wider world — through the silver flows from Mexico and Japan, through the Macao-Manila trade networks, through Matteo Ricci at the Beijing court, through the wokou pirate-merchant networks across the East China Sea, through Zheng He's voyages to East Africa. The retrospective image of a Ming China sealed off from the world by maritime prohibitions misses how integrated the late Ming was into the early-modern global economy. The accurate image is something more interesting: a Han Chinese state that made deliberate political-cultural choices to maintain its formal autonomy from the wider world while remaining functionally enmeshed in global commercial and intellectual networks.

The Ming legacies that most define modern Chinese material culture — Beijing as the capital, the Forbidden City, the Great Wall, the four great novels, blue-and-white porcelain, the examination tradition, the Confucian-restoration ideological framework — are all things that the Ming deliberately built, restored, or canonized in conscious opposition to the Yuan period. The Ming was, in its own self-understanding, the restoration of proper Chinese civilization after a foreign interruption. The Qing inherited that restoration and operated within its institutional frame for two and a half centuries, even while remaining ethnically distinct from the Han majority. The People's Republic that eventually replaced the Qing inherited essentially all of the Ming's surviving institutional and cultural infrastructure, with the addition of various twentieth-century overlays.

The dynasty died in 1644. The China the dynasty built is, in most of its visible cultural-material features, the China the modern world knows.

---

A final note, on a parallel from this app.

The Safavid dynasty in Persia (Safavid Persia Ch 8) collapsed in 1722 — eighty years after the Ming — under structurally similar circumstances. A bankrupted treasury after major foreign wars; a politically weak emperor presiding over institutional decay; a peripheral tribal force (the Hotaki Afghans for the Safavids, the Manchus for the Ming) that the central state could not defeat with its degraded military; an institutional collapse that left a foreign-origin successor dynasty (the Afghans, then the Afsharids for Iran; the Manchu Qing for China) holding the imperial center. Both dynasties left enduring cultural legacies that vastly outlasted the political collapse — the Safavid Twelver Shia identity, the Ming cultural template — and both became, in retrospect, the defining periods of their respective civilizations.

The pattern is one worth pausing on. Major early-modern empires fell, across the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, to internal institutional decay combined with external pressure — the Ming, the Safavids, the Mughals (Mughal Empire), the Ottoman Empire (Ottoman Empire Ch 8), eventually the Qing itself. None of them fell because their cultural-civilizational achievements had been exhausted; they fell because their administrative institutions could not adapt to the new pressures of the early-modern global economy and the rising European maritime-commercial powers. The cultural achievements survived — sometimes outlasted by centuries — the political states that produced them.

The Ming is one of the clearest examples of this pattern. The dynasty's institutional history is a long demonstration of why a centralized imperial autocracy with an abolished prime ministership and a parallel eunuch shadow-government cannot, over centuries, produce reliable governance under successor emperors of varying competence. The dynasty's cultural history is a demonstration of how much can be produced when an institutional autopilot is operating in an economically dynamic and intellectually open society.

The autocratic emperor on Coal Hill killed himself because the institutions he had inherited could no longer save him. The novels were on the bookshelves anyway. The porcelain was in the workshops anyway. The Forbidden City was still standing. The Great Wall was still in place. The Manchus came through Shanhaiguan to take the empire, and they took an empire whose cultural infrastructure they could not have built and could not easily have destroyed. They settled into the Forbidden City. They commissioned new editions of the great novels. They patronized the Jingdezhen kilns. They maintained the examinations. They governed, for the next 268 years, the cultural world the Ming had made.

That is what survived.

Cross-civ check: the Ming-Qing transition (1644) was contemporary with several other great mid-seventeenth-century transformations across Eurasia. The **English Civil War** was at its peak — Charles I was defeated at the Battle of Naseby in 1645, the year after Chongzhen's suicide; he was executed in 1649 in a parallel act of monarchical termination. The **Thirty Years' War** in central Europe ended with the Peace of Westphalia in 1648, four years after Beijing fell. The **Mughal Empire** under Shah Jahan (the builder of the Taj Mahal) was at its cultural peak. The **Ottoman Empire** was beginning the long decline that would parallel the Qing collapse two and a half centuries later. The **Tokugawa shogunate** in Japan was sealing the country off from European trade through the Sakoku edicts of the 1630s and 1640s — exactly the maritime-isolation policy the Ming had nominally followed but never enforced. The **Russian tsardom** under Tsar Alexis was beginning the eastern expansion that would eventually bring Russian colonists into contact with Qing Chinese forces in the Amur basin within a generation. The mid-seventeenth century was a moment of multiple great state transitions across Eurasia, with the Ming-Qing transition as the largest in human-life terms of all of them. The Ming was one of the major early-modern empires that fell during this period of global crisis; what distinguishes its fall is the scale of the cultural inheritance it left to its conquerors and through them to the modern world. The dynasty died in 1644. China is still living in its ruins.

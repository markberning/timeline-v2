# Safavid Persia — How Iran Became Shia

**Goal:** Narrative for the Safavid dynasty (1501–1736 CE), the third great chapter in the Iranian civilizational thread after the Achaemenid–Parthian–Sassanid Persian Empire and the Islamic-era Persian afterlife. Across roughly 235 years, the Safavids did something no other Iranian dynasty ever attempted: they forcibly converted an overwhelmingly Sunni Persian population to Twelver Shia Islam, and the conversion stuck so completely that Iran is still a Shia state today. Along the way, they built the most beautiful city in the early-modern world (Isfahan), got crushed by Ottoman gunpowder at Chaldiran (1514), absorbed Georgian and Armenian slave-soldiers into a new ghulam army that broke the power of the founding tribes, lost it all to a small Afghan army in 1722, and were finally extinguished by a brilliant warlord named Nader Shah in 1736. The Safavids are why modern Iran exists as a distinct nation rather than just another Sunni Arab–Turkic province. Everything about the Islamic Republic of Iran — the clerical hierarchy, the Shia identity, the rivalry with Saudi Arabia, the Iran-Iraq border — runs back to this dynasty.

# Chapter 1 — The Safavid Order

The dynasty that turned Iran into a Shia country started as a Sunni mystical brotherhood in a small town nobody outside the region had heard of.

The town was **Ardabil** (ar-dah-BEEL), in northwestern Iran near the Caspian Sea, in the Persian-Turkic borderland of **Azerbaijan** (the historical region, not the modern republic, though the modern republic gets its name from it). The brotherhood was called the **Safaviyya** (sah-fah-VEE-yah), founded around 1300 by a charismatic Sunni Sufi sheikh named **Safi al-Din** (sah-FEE al-DEEN). And the dynasty that eventually grew out of this brotherhood is the **Safavid** dynasty, which takes its name from him.

This is one of the strangest origin stories in early-modern history. The founders of every other major Islamic empire of the period — the Ottomans, the Mughals, the Uzbeks — were soldiers and tribal warlords. The Safavids started as monks. By the time they reached the throne, they had transformed themselves into the most fanatical military force in the Islamic world, claimed descent from the Prophet Muhammad (probably falsely), reversed their original Sunni identity into a militant Shia one, and inspired their followers to charge Ottoman cannons believing they could not be killed. The transformation took two centuries. It is the precondition for everything else in this book.

---

To understand the Safavids, you have to understand **Sufism** (SOO-fizm), the mystical and devotional tradition within Islam.

Mainstream Islam, in both its Sunni and Shia branches, emphasizes religious law (the **sharia**, sha-REE-ah), correct ritual, and direct individual relationship with God through prayer. Sufism is a parallel tradition that emphasizes inner spiritual experience, the cultivation of the soul, and the role of a teacher — a **sheikh** or **pir** (peer) — who guides disciples on a path toward direct knowledge of the divine. Sufi orders, called **tariqas** (tah-REE-kahs, literally "paths"), formed around lineages of spiritual masters. Each tariqa had its own distinctive practices: certain forms of meditative chanting (**dhikr**, ZIK-r — "remembrance" of God's names), specific breathing techniques, sometimes ecstatic dance (the most famous example being the whirling dervishes of the Mevlevi order, founded by the Persian poet Rumi in the thirteenth century), and a master-disciple relationship that demanded absolute loyalty.

Sufism flourished across the Islamic world after the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century (Mongol Empire Ch 5 and Ch 7). The Mongols had wrecked the existing political order — sacking Baghdad in 1258 and ending the Abbasid caliphate — and the centralized religious authority that had legitimized the old order collapsed with it. Into this vacuum stepped the Sufi orders, which provided spiritual direction, social organization, charitable services, and political networks at the local level. By the time the Ilkhanate (the Mongol successor state in Iran, Mongol Empire Ch 7) collapsed in 1335, Sufi tariqas were arguably the most powerful non-state institutions across the Persian-speaking world.

This is the world Safi al-Din was born into.

---

**Safi al-Din** (1252–1334) was a Sunni Sufi sheikh, originally a disciple of an older Sufi master named Sheikh Zahid Gilani in Lahijan, on the Caspian coast. When Zahid died, Safi al-Din inherited the leadership of his tariqa, married Zahid's daughter, and moved the order's headquarters to his hometown of Ardabil. From Ardabil, he built the Safaviyya into one of the most influential Sufi orders in the Iranian world.

What is striking about the early Safaviyya is how mainstream it was. Safi al-Din was a Sunni Muslim of the **Shafi'i** (SHAH-fee-ee) school of jurisprudence — one of the four standard Sunni legal schools, the dominant one in much of the eastern Islamic world. He was on cordial terms with the Ilkhan court. The historian **Rashid al-Din** (the same Jewish-born physician-vizier who compiled the *Compendium of Chronicles*, the first true world history, Mongol Empire Ch 7) was a personal correspondent. Wealthy patrons donated land to the order. Pilgrims came to Ardabil to receive Safi al-Din's blessing. By the time he died in 1334 — coincidentally the same year the Ilkhanate began to fall apart — the Safaviyya was a wealthy, respected, politically connected Sunni Sufi establishment.

Nothing about Safi al-Din suggests his descendants would conquer Iran two centuries later, claim descent from the Prophet, declare themselves messianic figures, and force-convert millions of Sunnis to Shia Islam. The transformation happened in stages, and each stage was driven by external political pressure.

---

The first stage was geographic and political.

The Ilkhanate's collapse left Iran fragmented among rival successor dynasties (Mongol Empire Ch 7) — the **Jalayirids** in Iraq and western Iran, the **Chobanids** in northwest Iran, the **Muzaffarids** in the south, and various smaller principalities. None of these states had the strength to reunify Persia, and into this vacuum stepped a new conqueror: **Timur** (TEE-moor, also called Tamerlane in European sources), the Turco-Mongol warlord from Transoxiana who built one of the most violent empires of the medieval world between the 1370s and 1405. Timur's campaigns through Iran, India, the Caucasus, and Anatolia produced massacres on a scale not seen since the original Mongol invasions — pyramids of skulls outside cities that resisted, populations exterminated, entire regions depopulated. Timur was theoretically a Muslim and patronized Sufi orders, including the Safaviyya, but his political project was conquest, not cultural restoration.

When Timur's empire began to crumble after his death in 1405, his descendants — the **Timurids** — held on to a reduced version of his territory in Khorasan (eastern Iran and modern Afghanistan) for most of the fifteenth century. The Timurid capital at **Herat** (HEH-rat, in modern Afghanistan) became one of the cultural jewels of the Islamic world: Persian miniature painting reached new heights under masters like **Kamal al-Din Bihzad** (kah-MAHL al-DEEN bee-ZAHD), Persian poetry flourished in the work of **Jami** (the last great classical Persian poet), and Timurid architecture pioneered the soaring blue-tiled domes that would later define Safavid building. Much of what we think of as classical Persian high culture was codified at fifteenth-century Herat. The Safavids would inherit it almost entirely.

Meanwhile, in northwestern Iran, the Safaviyya order at Ardabil was changing.

---

The second stage was the conversion to Shia Islam.

Exactly when and how the Safaviyya shifted from Sunni Sufism to Shia Sufism is one of the messier questions in the historical literature. The standard scholarly reading is that the conversion was gradual, beginning in the late fourteenth century and accelerating through the fifteenth, driven by the order's shifting political alliances with various **Turcoman** (TUR-koh-man — Turkic-speaking nomadic tribal confederations) groups in Anatolia and Azerbaijan. Many of these Turcomans practiced a folk Islam that blended Sunni and Shia elements with pre-Islamic Turkic shamanic traditions. As the Safaviyya recruited disciples from these groups, the order's theology drifted toward Shia positions — particular reverence for **Ali ibn Abi Talib** (AH-lee), the Prophet Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law, whom Shia Muslims consider the rightful first successor to the Prophet, and for the line of imams descended from him.

The Safavid sheikhs also began to claim, retroactively, that their family descended from Ali himself. This claim was probably invented in the fifteenth century — Safi al-Din's actual genealogy almost certainly does not run through Ali — but it became central to Safavid legitimacy. If you are descended from the Prophet through Ali, you are not merely a Sufi master but a **sayyid** (SAY-yid), a member of the Prophet's bloodline, with a special claim to religious authority. The Safavids would eventually push this claim further: they were not just sayyids but the legitimate heirs of the Twelfth Imam himself, the **Hidden Imam** whose return Twelver Shia Muslims await as the apocalyptic redeemer.

The doctrinal claims and the political claims developed together. As the order grew, its sheikhs needed bigger justifications for their growing power.

---

The third stage was militarization.

The militarization happened under two sheikhs whose names matter: **Junayd** (joo-NAYD, sheikh from 1447 to 1460) and his son **Haydar** (HIGH-dar, sheikh from 1460 to 1488). Junayd was the first Safavid leader who actively raised armies, married into ruling houses, and sought a political throne. He was killed in battle in 1460 fighting the rulers of Shirvan (a region on the western Caspian coast). Haydar inherited his father's project and pushed it further — and gave the movement its visual signature.

The signature was a **red turban**. Haydar designed a distinctive twelve-pointed red headgear for his followers, the points commemorating the **twelve imams** of Twelver Shia Islam. The red turban was simultaneously a religious symbol, a military uniform, and a political badge — anyone wearing it was identifying as a Safaviyya disciple, a Shia, a partisan of Haydar, and a soldier ready to fight at his command. The Turcoman tribesmen who flocked to Haydar's banner were called **Qizilbash** (kih-zil-BASH), Turkish for "red-heads," after their distinctive headgear. The name stuck. The Qizilbash were the military backbone of the early Safavid project for the next century.

What made the Qizilbash extraordinary was the religious dimension of their loyalty. They did not fight for wages, plunder, or land alone. They fought because they believed their sheikh was a divinely guided figure — not just a Sufi master but a near-messianic incarnation of the divine, possibly the returning Hidden Imam himself. Some Qizilbash factions reportedly believed their sheikh was literally a manifestation of God in human form, a position that mainstream Islam — Sunni or Shia — would consider heretical to the point of blasphemy. The historian **Hans Roemer** described early Qizilbash religion as something closer to a millenarian cult than orthodox Shia Islam: ecstatic, charismatic, organized around personal devotion to a living messianic figure rather than around scripture and law.

This produced an extraordinary military asset. Qizilbash warriors charged into battle believing they could not be killed, that their sheikh's spiritual power would protect them, and that death in his service was a direct ticket to paradise. They were, in the literal sense, fanatics — but the fanaticism worked on the battlefield. Through the 1480s and 1490s, the Safaviyya order under Haydar's son **Ali** (briefly sheikh from 1488 to 1494) and then Ali's nephew **Ismail** built a fighting force that would shortly conquer Iran.

Haydar himself was killed in 1488 in another battle in the Caucasus. Ali was killed in 1494. The young Ismail — Haydar's grandson, Ali's nephew, six years old at the time — was smuggled to safety by loyal Qizilbash and hidden for the next five years among friendly families in the Caspian region. He emerged in 1499 as a thirteen-year-old leading a small army. By 1501, fourteen years old, he would conquer the city of Tabriz and proclaim himself **Shah** (SHAH — the Persian word for king, the same word the Achaemenid emperors had used 2,000 years earlier — Persian Empire Ch 1).

That is where the next chapter starts.

---

A pause, before the chronology takes over: the Safaviyya story is the kind of origin myth that sounds invented, and parts of it almost certainly were. The genealogy back to Ali is dubious. The claim of unbroken Shia identity from Safi al-Din onward is anachronistic. The accounts of Qizilbash religious extremism may be exaggerated by later orthodox Shia historians embarrassed by the founders' theology. Modern scholars are divided on how much of the early Safavid self-presentation reflects fifteenth-century reality versus sixteenth-century reconstruction. What no one disputes is that by 1500, the Safaviyya had transformed from a Sunni Sufi devotional order into an armed Shia messianic movement with thousands of fanatical Turcoman warriors, and that this transformation was the launch pad for one of the most consequential religious revolutions in Middle Eastern history.

A structural parallel worth pausing on: the two great Islamic empires of the early-modern Middle East — the Safavids and the Ottomans — were both born out of Sufi orders in the chaos that followed the collapse of the Mongol Ilkhanate. The Ottoman dynasty's earliest fourteenth-century Anatolian frontier emerged from heterodox dervish networks (the Babai movement and related groups), in much the same way the Safaviyya emerged from the Ardabil Sufi establishment. Both began as religious brotherhoods that armed themselves and turned political. They then faced the same institutional problem from opposite ends: how to build a military force loyal to the ruler personally rather than to the tribal leaders whose private armies had founded the dynasty. The Ottomans solved it through **devshirme** — recruiting Christian boys from the Balkans, raising them as Muslims, and creating the Janissary corps as a slave-soldier infantry with no kin ties to the territory they policed (Chapter 3). The Safavids would eventually solve it the same way at one remove, importing Caucasian boys and training them as **ghulam** household troops (Chapter 4). Both systems worked. The Ottoman version came earlier, was institutionally older when the rivalry began, and was one major reason the Ottomans hit Chaldiran in 1514 with a professional gunpowder army while the Safavids were still riding into battle on the basis of religious fanaticism.

Cross-civ check: while the Safaviyya was militarizing in the late fifteenth century, the **Ottoman Empire** (the rival Sunni Turkic empire that would shortly become the Safavids' nemesis) was at the height of its early expansion under **Mehmed II** (the conqueror of Constantinople in 1453) and **Bayezid II**. The **Mughal Empire** in India did not yet exist — it would be founded by Babur in 1526 — but Babur himself was a Timurid prince, distant cousin of the families the early Safavids were fighting. In **Spain**, the Catholic Monarchs Ferdinand and Isabella completed the **Reconquista** in 1492, expelling the last Muslim ruler from Granada the same year Columbus reached the Americas. In **China**, the **Ming Dynasty** was at mid-arc, with the great voyages of Admiral Zheng He completed and the country turning inward. The world that Ismail was about to step into was the early-modern world — gunpowder armies, intercontinental trade, religious confrontation across continents — and the Safavids would shape its eastern half.

# Chapter 2 — Ismail and the Shia Revolution

In 1501, a fourteen-year-old boy with red hair and pale skin, leading a few thousand Turcoman cavalry, captured the city of **Tabriz** (tah-BREEZ, the largest city in northwestern Iran) and proclaimed himself **Shah Ismail I** of a new dynasty. Within a decade, he controlled most of Iran. Within a generation, the country was forcibly converted from Sunni to Twelver Shia Islam, and the religious geography of the Middle East was permanently redrawn.

Ismail's revolution is one of the strangest political events in early-modern history. He was a teenager. His army was small. The country he set out to conquer was overwhelmingly Sunni and had been for nine centuries. He had no scholarly establishment to support the Shia conversion, no economic base outside what his Qizilbash followers could plunder, and no diplomatic recognition from any major foreign power. What he had was charisma, fanatical followers who believed he was a near-divine figure, and a willingness to use systematic violence on a population that had not asked for any of this.

It worked.

---

The political situation Ismail inherited was the chaotic aftermath of two centuries of Mongol-Timurid disintegration (Chapter 1). Iran in 1500 was divided among competing Turcoman tribal confederations.

The **Aq Qoyunlu** (ak koy-OON-loo, "White Sheep") were the dominant power, a Sunni Turcoman confederation that had defeated their rivals the **Kara Qoyunlu** ("Black Sheep") in the 1460s and ruled most of western Iran, eastern Anatolia, and Mesopotamia from their capital at Tabriz. The Aq Qoyunlu were going through their own succession crisis in the late 1490s, fragmenting into factions led by rival princes. To the east, the Timurid princes still held Khorasan and parts of Afghanistan, while the **Uzbeks** under **Muhammad Shaybani** (shy-BAH-nee) were pushing south from Central Asia, threatening to absorb the Timurid lands. To the west, the **Ottoman Empire** under Sultan Bayezid II watched the Iranian situation with mounting concern but did not yet intervene.

The Safaviyya order under Ismail's father Haydar had been on bad terms with the Aq Qoyunlu — Haydar had been killed fighting their allies — and the Aq Qoyunlu had imprisoned the boy Ismail's older brothers, eventually killing one of them. When Ismail emerged from hiding in 1499, the Aq Qoyunlu had just collapsed into civil war and the moment was ripe. He raised a Qizilbash army of perhaps 7,000 Turcoman cavalry, marched on the Aq Qoyunlu prince **Alvand**, defeated him at the Battle of **Sharur** in summer 1501, and entered Tabriz unopposed.

The first thing he did was declare Twelver Shia Islam the state religion.

---

This requires explanation. **Twelver Shia Islam** is the dominant branch of Shia Islam today — the official religion of Iran, a majority in Iraq and Bahrain, a substantial minority in Lebanon, Pakistan, and elsewhere. It takes its name from its core doctrine: that there were twelve divinely guided **Imams** (ee-MAHMS) descended from the Prophet Muhammad through his daughter **Fatima** and her husband **Ali ibn Abi Talib**, and that these twelve imams are the rightful spiritual and political successors to the Prophet. The First Imam was Ali himself (assassinated in 661 CE). The line passes through his son **Hussein** (who was killed at the Battle of Karbala in 680 CE, the foundational martyrdom of Shia Islam) down to the **Twelfth Imam**, **Muhammad al-Mahdi** (al-MAH-dee), who is said to have gone into **occultation** (a state of hidden existence) in 874 CE and will return at the end of time as the Shia messiah figure to establish justice on earth.

Sunni Islam, the dominant branch comprising roughly 85–90% of Muslims worldwide, rejects this entire framework. Sunnis hold that the proper succession to the Prophet was through the elected caliphs — Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and only then Ali — and that there is no special spiritual authority vested in Ali's descendants.

In 1501, Iran was, by the surviving evidence, somewhere around 80–90% Sunni. There were Shia communities — Twelver, Ismaili, and other branches — scattered across the country, and certain regions like Qom and Mazandaran had significant Shia populations. But Persia in general had been Sunni since the Arab conquest of the seventh century (Persian Empire Ch 10), and the major scholarly establishments at cities like Nishapur, Shiraz, and Isfahan were Sunni institutions teaching Sunni jurisprudence. The Safavids' own ancestor Safi al-Din had been a Sunni Sufi (Chapter 1).

What Ismail proposed in 1501 was therefore not religious reform, not religious tolerance, not religious pluralism. It was **religious revolution**. The state would be Twelver Shia. The official Friday prayer in every mosque would curse the first three caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman) as usurpers of Ali's rightful succession — a practice known as **tabarra** (tah-BAR-ah, "dissociation") that Sunnis considered intolerable blasphemy. Sunni religious scholars who refused to convert would be executed or driven into exile. Public Sunni religious practice would be banned. The country would be converted, on the timetable and by the methods Ismail's regime determined.

The conversion took the better part of a century to complete. It was nowhere close to complete in 1501. But the project began at Ismail's coronation, and it never stopped.

---

The methods of forced conversion deserve some attention, because they are often glossed over in popular accounts that emphasize Safavid art and architecture.

The basic mechanism was state violence. Sunni judges, preachers, and scholars who refused to convert were killed. Sunni mosques were converted to Shia mosques (which mostly meant adding the cursing of the first three caliphs to the Friday prayer and reorienting the educational curriculum). Sunni religious endowments — properties whose income supported mosques, schools, and charitable institutions — were confiscated and redirected to Shia institutions. Public displays of Sunni piety were banned and could be punished by death. In some cities, particularly **Tabriz** and **Herat** (the latter conquered in 1510), Ismail's regime conducted mass executions of Sunni clerics and notables. Death-toll estimates for Tabriz alone in the first few years of Safavid rule range widely in the scholarly literature — figures of 10,000 to 20,000 appear in various secondary accounts, with some scholars arguing for substantially lower numbers — but no surviving primary source supports a precise count, and the estimates that exist are extrapolations from chronicle descriptions of mass executions rather than firm tallies.

There was also a systematic ritual humiliation campaign. Public preachers, called **tabarra'iyyan** ("cursers"), were sent through the streets of conquered cities cursing the first three caliphs. Anyone who failed to join in could be killed on the spot. The historian **John Foran**, drawing on contemporary sources, describes scenes of Sunni inhabitants being forced at swordpoint to publicly curse caliphs whom they had been raised to revere. The cumulative psychological effect was enormous: within a generation, the social cost of being publicly Sunni had become so high that conversion was the only practical option for most of the urban population.

The conversion was therefore not a matter of theological persuasion. It was a matter of state power applied systematically over decades. The fact that it succeeded — that Iran is overwhelmingly Twelver Shia today, five centuries later, while Iraq and Anatolia (where Ismail's regime did not have the same staying power) remained majority Sunni — is testimony to how effective coercive religious conversion can be when sustained over multiple generations.

This is a hard thing for modern readers, raised on the principle of religious freedom, to absorb. The Safavid conversion of Iran was one of the most violent religious transformations in early-modern history, comparable in coercion (if not in body count) to the Spanish Inquisition's persecution of Jewish and Muslim converts in the same period. It is also the reason modern Iran exists. The Safavid state created the Twelver Shia identity that distinguished Iranians from the Sunni Arab and Turkic populations around them, and that distinct identity is what made Iran a coherent national entity rather than just another fragment of the Sunni Muslim world. The conversion is morally appalling and historically foundational at the same time.

The closest structural parallel to the Safavid project in this app is one that ran the opposite direction on coercion. **Ashoka** of the Maurya dynasty (Maurya Empire Ch 5–6), eighteen centuries earlier, used the machinery of the Indian state to propagate Buddhism — a minority religion in third-century-BCE India — as a state-sponsored faith. Ashoka, like Ismail, imported scholarly specialists (in his case Buddhist monks dispatched as missionaries to other regions), built new institutional infrastructure (rock and pillar edicts, monastery networks, pilgrimage circuits), and used royal authority to elevate a minority religion. But Ashoka tried to persuade rather than coerce — his edicts emphasize moral example, tolerance of other traditions, and the merits of Buddhist ethics, with no equivalent of the tabarra'iyyan or the executions in Tabriz. The Mauryan project did not survive Ashoka's dynasty: Buddhism remained important in India for several centuries but never became the majority faith, and within a few hundred years the Mauryan-era Buddhist establishment was in long decline. The Safavid project, conducted with state violence applied systematically over generations, did stick. The depressing inference is that for purposes of imposing a minority religion on a populated country, sustained coercion works better than persuasion. The Safavid Iran of 2026 is overwhelmingly Shia. The Buddhist India that Ashoka tried to build is not even a memory of itself.

---

The technical problem with the conversion was that there were almost no Twelver Shia scholars in Iran to staff the new state religion.

Twelver Shia Islam was a minority tradition with most of its scholarly centers outside Iran — at the shrine cities of **Najaf** and **Karbala** in Iraq (under Ottoman control), at **Bahrain** in the Persian Gulf, and at **Jabal Amil** (jah-BAL ah-MIL), a region in southern Lebanon that had been a Twelver Shia stronghold since the tenth century. Iran itself had Shia minorities but no Twelver scholarly establishment of any size. Ismail's regime needed jurists to issue rulings, preachers to staff mosques, theologians to argue against Sunni opponents, and teachers to train the next generation of Iranian Shia clerics.

The solution was importation. Ismail and his successors paid Arab Shia scholars from Jabal Amil and Bahrain enormous sums to migrate to Iran, settle in the new Shia centers at **Qom** and **Isfahan**, and train the Iranian clerical class that would propagate the new state religion. The migration began under Ismail himself and accelerated under Tahmasp I. Some of the most influential Safavid-era Shia clerics — including **Muhaqqiq al-Karaki** (mu-HAH-kik al-kah-RAH-kee), one of the architects of Safavid Shia jurisprudence in the 1530s — were Lebanese Arabs imported for the purpose. Their descendants intermarried with Iranian families and became the founding stock of Iran's Shia clerical establishment, the same establishment that would eventually produce Ayatollah Khomeini and the 1979 Islamic Revolution.

The mechanism is worth pausing on. The forced conversion of Iran was not, in the first instance, a popular movement. It was a state project that required imported labor — like building a factory before you have the workforce trained to operate it. The Safavid state created the demand for Shia clerical expertise, the Lebanese and Bahraini scholars supplied it, and a native Iranian Shia clerical class was trained over the following century to staff the system. By the late seventeenth century, the imported lineage had been effectively absorbed: the leading Safavid clerics were Iranian-born, trained in Iranian seminaries, and conducting their scholarship in Persian as well as Arabic. The state religion had become genuinely Iranian. But the seed had been imported.

---

Ismail's military conquests over the first decade of his reign extended the Safavid state across nearly the entire Iranian plateau.

In 1503 he defeated the last Aq Qoyunlu prince at the Battle of Hamadan and annexed western Iran. In 1504 he took Yazd and Kerman in central Iran. In 1507 he conquered the Caucasus principalities of Shirvan (where his grandfather Junayd had been killed). In 1508 he marched into **Iraq** and captured **Baghdad** — bringing the holy Shia cities of **Najaf** (the burial place of Imam Ali) and **Karbala** (the martyrdom site of Imam Hussein) under Safavid control. The Shia shrine cities became major Safavid pilgrimage destinations, lavishly patronized by the new regime.

In 1510 Ismail turned east and confronted the Uzbek leader **Muhammad Shaybani**, who had conquered the Timurid heartland and was threatening Iranian Khorasan. The two armies met at the **Battle of Merv** (MERV — an oasis city in modern Turkmenistan) in November 1510. Shaybani was defeated and killed; according to the bloodthirsty traditions of the period, Ismail had Shaybani's skull encased in gold and made into a drinking cup (the parallel to the Mongol drinking-from-skull tradition was probably deliberate — Ismail was claiming the political legacy of Genghis Khan as well as the religious legacy of Ali).

The Battle of Merv stabilized the eastern frontier briefly, and Ismail returned west to consolidate his rule. By 1510 he ruled an empire that extended from the Euphrates to the Oxus, from the Caucasus to the Persian Gulf — roughly the Iranian heartland he would pass on to his son. He had defeated the Aq Qoyunlu, the last Timurids, and the Uzbeks. He had declared Iran Twelver Shia, begun the forced conversion, imported Lebanese clerics, conquered the Shia holy cities, and established Tabriz as the Safavid capital.

He was twenty-three years old.

What he had not yet done was face the **Ottoman Empire**, the rival Sunni Turkic power to his west. The Ottomans had been watching Ismail's career with growing alarm, partly because of his religious revolution (which threatened Ottoman legitimacy as the leading Sunni state), partly because of his Qizilbash following inside Ottoman territory (the Turcoman tribes of eastern Anatolia were heavily Qizilbash and effectively pro-Safavid), and partly because of his sheer military success. In 1512, the Ottoman throne passed to **Selim I** (seh-LEEM, called **Yavuz** — "the Grim" — in Ottoman tradition), who was determined to settle the Safavid problem by direct military action. He would do so at Chaldiran in 1514, and the result is the subject of the next chapter.

Cross-civ check: Ismail's reign (1501–1524) overlaps almost exactly with the **High Renaissance** in Italy — Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Raphael, all working in Florence and Rome while Ismail was conquering Iran. **Martin Luther** posted his Ninety-Five Theses in 1517, three years after Chaldiran, beginning the **Protestant Reformation** that would fracture European Christianity. **Cortés** conquered the Aztec Empire (Aztec Empire Ch 8) in 1519–21. The **Mughal Empire** in India was founded by **Babur** in 1526, two years after Ismail's death. The early sixteenth century is one of the densest moments of imperial expansion and religious revolution in human history, and the Safavids are squarely in the middle of it.

# Chapter 3 — Chaldiran and the Frontier

The defining moment of early Safavid history happened on a flat plain in eastern Anatolia, on August 23, 1514. The battle was called **Chaldiran** (chal-dih-RAHN), and the Safavids lost it badly enough that the religious and political map of the Middle East was fixed for the next five centuries.

To understand Chaldiran, you have to understand what each side brought to the battlefield.

**The Ottoman Empire** under Sultan Selim I was, by 1514, the most militarily sophisticated state in the Islamic world. It had spent the previous century building a standing army centered on the **Janissaries** (JAN-uh-sair-eez, from Turkish *yeni çeri*, "new soldiers") — a corps of professional infantry recruited as boys from Christian populations in the Balkans, raised as Muslims, trained from childhood in military discipline, and equipped with the latest gunpowder weapons. The Janissaries were one of the world's first true standing professional armies. They drilled in unit tactics, trained with **muskets** (matchlock firearms that had become reliable enough for battlefield use by the late fifteenth century), and were supported by the most advanced Ottoman field artillery — bronze cannons cast in centralized state foundries and dragged to battlefields on heavy wagons. By 1514, the Ottomans had perfected a battlefield formation called the **wagon-fort** (Turkish *tabur cengi*) — a defensive perimeter of chained-together supply wagons, with cannons firing through gaps between them and Janissary musketeers firing from behind, that could withstand cavalry charges and shoot the attackers to pieces at distance.

**The Safavid army** under Shah Ismail was the opposite of all of this. The Safavids had no infantry, no muskets, no artillery, and no wagon-forts. Their entire military doctrine was built around the Qizilbash heavy cavalry charge: armored Turcoman horsemen, fanatically loyal, attacking in waves with lances and swords, relying on shock, mobility, and individual martial valor to break the enemy line. This had worked brilliantly against Aq Qoyunlu, Timurid, and Uzbek opponents, who were equipped with similar weapons and fought similar battles. It had not been tested against gunpowder.

Ismail also had a religious confidence problem. His followers genuinely believed he was a near-divine figure who could not be defeated. He had spent fifteen years winning every battle he fought. Chronicle accounts describe Qizilbash warriors in 1514 boasting that they would brush aside the Ottoman cannons with their swords. Ismail himself reportedly refused his commanders' suggestion to attack the Ottoman army before it could fortify its position — declaring that he would not strike like a brigand but would wait for Selim to deploy and meet him in fair battle. This was the kind of decision a leader makes when he believes God is on his side and the result is preordained.

---

The two armies met at Chaldiran, on the plain east of Lake Van, on August 23, 1514. Numbers are debated. Modern estimates suggest the Ottomans fielded roughly 60,000 troops — about 12,000 of them Janissaries with muskets, another several thousand cannoneers, and the bulk in supporting cavalry. The Safavids fielded perhaps 40,000 cavalry, almost all Qizilbash. The Ottomans had numerical superiority and decisive technological superiority. The Safavids had cavalry shock and religious fanaticism.

Selim deployed his army in the perfected wagon-fort formation: cannons and muskets behind chained wagons, Janissaries massed in the center, supporting cavalry on the wings. He waited. Ismail attacked.

The Qizilbash cavalry charged in two wings, attempting to envelop the Ottoman army. The right wing, led by Ismail himself, broke through the Ottoman left-wing cavalry and routed it — for a moment, the battle looked like another Safavid victory. But the left Qizilbash wing, charging the Ottoman right where the Janissaries and cannons were concentrated, ran straight into massed musket fire and artillery at close range. The Qizilbash cavalry — armored against arrows and swords, not against bullets — were torn apart. The cannon fired, the matchlocks volleyed, and the charge broke before reaching the Ottoman line. Within an hour the Qizilbash left wing was destroyed and the right wing, including Ismail himself, was caught in a counterattack.

Ismail was wounded, his horse killed under him, and only his bodyguards' efforts saved him from death or capture. Several of his most senior Qizilbash commanders were killed. His harem, traveling with the army in the customary fashion of Iranian kings, was captured — including one of his wives, **Tajli Begum**, who would later be ransomed back. The Safavid army was driven from the field. Selim's forces marched east unopposed, occupied Tabriz on September 7, 1514, looted the city, and held it for several weeks before withdrawing in late autumn because the Janissaries refused to winter so far from home.

The Safavids had lost the battle, lost the capital, and very nearly lost the dynasty. Ismail recovered Tabriz after the Ottomans withdrew, but the psychological damage was permanent. The aura of invincibility — the conviction among the Qizilbash that their sheikh-shah was divinely protected — was broken. Ismail spent the last decade of his life in mourning, drinking heavily, and reportedly refusing to lead troops into battle again. The chronicle accounts say he wore black, wrote melancholy poetry (he was an accomplished Turkic-language poet under the pen name **Khata'i**), and avoided the public ceremonies of kingship that had defined his early reign. He died in 1524 at the age of thirty-six, leaving a ten-year-old son to inherit the throne.

---

Chaldiran's consequences ran far beyond the battlefield.

First, it fixed the Ottoman-Safavid frontier in eastern Anatolia roughly where it would remain for the next four centuries. Eastern Anatolia stayed Ottoman; western Iran stayed Safavid; the border ran roughly along the modern Turkey-Iran boundary with some shifts to the north and south at various points. This is one of the most stable frontiers in world history — the line drawn by Selim's victory in 1514 is still very nearly the line drawn on maps today. The Ottoman Empire Ch 5 covers Selim's eastern campaign from the Ottoman side — for that narrative, Chaldiran secured the flank that allowed Selim to pivot south and conquer the Mamluks within three years.

Second, it established gunpowder as decisive in early-modern Middle Eastern warfare. The Safavids had been a pre-gunpowder cavalry empire facing a gunpowder Janissary state, and they had been crushed. Every successful Iranian government from Chaldiran forward would have to address this problem. Tahmasp I (Ismail's son) made halting progress on adopting muskets and cannons. Abbas I (Chapter 4) finally solved the problem by reorganizing the entire military around gunpowder weapons, with consequences that would let him recover most of what Ismail had lost.

Third, it ended the messianic phase of Safavid politics. Ismail had presented himself as a near-divine figure, possibly the returning Hidden Imam, whose followers could not be killed. Chaldiran demonstrated, in front of his entire army, that he could be defeated by ordinary mortals with better technology. The Qizilbash continued to fight for the Safavids for another century, but the religious extremism that had powered the original conquest faded. Subsequent Safavid Shahs would present themselves as legitimate Shia monarchs in the orthodox sense, descendants of the Prophet, defenders of the faith — but not as living gods. The shift made the dynasty more institutionally sustainable. It also made it more dependent on the Shia clerical establishment, which had its own ideas about the proper relationship between religion and state (Chapter 5).

---

**Tahmasp I** (TAH-mahsp, ruled 1524–1576) was ten years old when his father died, and his fifty-two-year reign was the longest of any Safavid king. The early years were a Qizilbash regency: rival tribal factions fought each other for control of the boy-shah while Ottoman and Uzbek armies invaded the empire from west and east. By the time Tahmasp emerged as effective ruler in his late teens, the situation was dire. The Ottomans had occupied Tabriz repeatedly. The Uzbeks under **Ubaydullah Khan** had launched five major invasions of Khorasan. The state treasury was depleted, the military was demoralized, and the imported Lebanese clerics were just beginning the long work of building Iran's Shia establishment.

Tahmasp's contribution was survival. He was not a great conqueror — he lost ground more often than he gained it — but he held the dynasty together long enough for the religious conversion to take root, the clerical establishment to develop, and the next generation of military and administrative reformers to come of age. He fought the Ottomans through three major wars (1532–1555, with the **Peace of Amasya** finally stabilizing the frontier in 1555), held off repeated Uzbek invasions in the east, and managed the Qizilbash factions through a combination of intermarriage, executions, and strategic favoritism that prevented any single tribe from dominating the court.

His most consequential strategic decision was relocating the Safavid capital. **Tabriz** had been the original capital under Ismail, but it was too close to the Ottoman frontier — captured and looted three times in the sixteenth century alone. In 1548, Tahmasp moved the capital inland to **Qazvin** (kahz-VEEN), in the northwestern Iranian highlands south of the Alborz mountains. Qazvin was further from Ottoman raiding range, defensible against Uzbek incursions, and located on the major trade routes connecting western Iran to Khorasan. It would remain the capital for fifty years, until Abbas I moved it again to Isfahan in 1598.

---

The cultural and religious project also accelerated under Tahmasp.

The **Shahnameh of Shah Tahmasp** — one of the most spectacular illustrated manuscripts in the history of book art — was produced at the Tabriz court between roughly 1522 and 1535. The **Shahnameh** ("Book of Kings") is the Persian national epic, written by the poet **Ferdowsi** around 1000 CE during the Samanid period (Persian Empire Ch 10). It is over 50,000 couplets long, telling the mythological and historical story of Iranian kingship from the creation of the world to the Arab conquest. Every Persian-speaking court before and after the Safavids commissioned its own illustrated copy of the *Shahnameh* — these manuscripts are some of the highest works of Persian art — but Tahmasp's was the most ambitious. It contained 258 full-page miniature paintings by the leading masters of the Tabriz school, including the great **Sultan Muhammad** (one of the most influential Persian painters of any era), and the production employed dozens of calligraphers, illuminators, painters, and binders over more than a decade. The completed manuscript was the size of a small piano, weighing roughly 75 pounds.

What is most interesting about Tahmasp's *Shahnameh* is what eventually happened to it. Around 1568, Tahmasp gave the manuscript as a diplomatic gift to the Ottoman Sultan **Selim II** — sending the greatest illustrated manuscript of the Persian national epic to the son of the man who had defeated his father at Chaldiran. The political message was complex: a peace offering, a display of cultural superiority, an acknowledgment that Persian culture transcended political enmity. The manuscript stayed in the Ottoman imperial library until 1903, when it was sold to a private European collector. It was eventually broken up — its individual paintings sold separately on the international art market through the twentieth century. As of today, individual pages of the Tahmasp Shahnameh hang in museums from New York to London to Doha, with the bulk of the surviving folios in the **Aga Khan Museum** in Toronto. The world's greatest illustrated Persian manuscript was partly disassembled by the international art trade in the twentieth century — a fate that says something about both the value of Persian art and the limits of preservation.

The **Tabriz school of painting** that produced this manuscript represented a major synthesis of earlier Persian painting traditions. It combined the **Herat school** style developed under the Timurids in the late fifteenth century — the flat compositions, brilliant colors, and elaborate landscape backgrounds of Bihzad — with new innovations in figure drawing, narrative complexity, and emotional depth. Persian miniature painting under the Safavids was one of the great visual arts of the early-modern world, comparable in sophistication to contemporary Italian Renaissance painting and operating on entirely different aesthetic principles. The **Mughal painting** tradition that would shortly emerge in India under Akbar (after 1556) drew its initial training from Safavid masters — including two Tabriz painters, **Mir Sayyid Ali** and **Abd al-Samad**, who migrated to Delhi after Tahmasp's son tried to reverse the Shia conversion (see below).

---

**Ismail II** is one of the strangest episodes in the Safavid story. He was Tahmasp's son, and after his father's death in 1576 he briefly attempted to reverse the Shia conversion entirely.

Ismail II had spent twenty years imprisoned by his father (the Safavids, like most early-modern dynasties, had a chronic problem with princely succession that they often solved by locking up the spare princes). When he emerged from prison in 1576 and took the throne, he was bitter, paranoid, and apparently sympathetic to Sunni Islam. During his eighteen-month reign he restored Sunni religious practice in many cities, persecuted Shia clerics who had prospered under his father, and reportedly considered formally restoring Sunni Islam as the state religion of Persia.

He never got the chance. He was found dead in his palace in 1577 — officially of natural causes, almost certainly poisoned, probably by his sister **Pari Khan Khanum** or by Shia clerics who had every motive to prevent the reversal. The episode is illuminating: in just seventy-five years, the Shia clerical establishment had become powerful enough that even a Safavid Shah could not openly oppose it without losing his life. The conversion had taken root.

After Ismail II's death, his elder half-brother **Muhammad Khudabanda** was placed on the throne. He was nearly blind and reportedly indecisive, and his eleven-year reign (1578–1588) descended into another round of Qizilbash factional warfare — exactly the kind of crisis the dynasty had faced after Ismail I's death seventy years earlier. The Ottomans took advantage and launched a major invasion (the Ottoman-Safavid War of 1578–1590) that captured Tabriz, Azerbaijan, and the Caucasus, forcing the humiliating **Treaty of Constantinople** in 1590 in which the Safavids ceded enormous territory.

It was at this moment — the dynasty's lowest point since Chaldiran — that a sixteen-year-old prince named **Abbas** seized the throne from his blind father and began the most consequential reign in Safavid history.

Cross-civ check: the long sixteenth-century reigns of Ismail I and Tahmasp I (1501–1576) overlap with the rise of the **Mughal Empire** in India under **Babur**, **Humayun**, and **Akbar**. Humayun himself spent fifteen years in Safavid exile after being driven from India by his brothers in the 1540s. Tahmasp gave him refuge at Qazvin, supplied him with a 12,000-strong Safavid army to reclaim his throne, and required in exchange Humayun's nominal conversion to Shia Islam and the cession of the strategic city of **Kandahar** (in modern Afghanistan). The Safavid-Mughal relationship would be marked by this combination of cultural intimacy (shared Persian language and high culture) and political competition (especially over Kandahar) for the next two centuries. Meanwhile, the **Ottoman Empire** under **Suleiman the Magnificent** (1520–1566) reached its peak power, the **Spanish Empire** under Charles V and Philip II conquered the Americas and Portugal, the **Tudor dynasty** consolidated England, and **Ivan the Terrible** founded the Russian tsardom. The sixteenth century is the moment when the early-modern world system took shape, and the Safavids were one of its major players.

# Chapter 4 — Abbas the Great and Isfahan

In 1588, a sixteen-year-old prince named **Abbas** seized the Safavid throne from his blind father Muhammad Khudabanda with the help of a Qizilbash faction that wanted a strong ruler. Over the next forty-one years, he transformed the Safavid Empire from a failing tribal confederation into one of the most sophisticated states in the early-modern world, recovered nearly all the territory lost to the Ottomans, broke the political power of the Qizilbash tribes that had founded the dynasty, and built a capital city that contemporaries called "**half the world**."

**Abbas I** (ruled 1588–1629), known to history as **Abbas the Great**, is the Safavid ruler everyone has heard of, and for once the reputation is fully deserved. He was a brilliant administrator, a ruthless politician (he had two of his own sons blinded and a third killed to prevent palace coups), a competent military commander, an obsessive city-builder, and a religious diplomat skilled enough to negotiate with the Catholic Pope while waging holy war against the Sunni Ottomans. His reign is the apex of the dynasty and one of the great moments in Iranian history.

He inherited a disaster.

When Abbas took the throne in 1588, the Ottomans were occupying western Iran, the Uzbeks were raiding Khorasan, the treasury was empty, the Qizilbash were fighting each other, and the Mughals had taken Kandahar. His first move was strategic retreat: in 1590, he signed the **Treaty of Constantinople** with the Ottomans on devastating terms — ceding most of Azerbaijan, the Caucasus, and parts of western Iran in exchange for a fifteen-year peace that gave him time to rebuild. The treaty was humiliating. It was also necessary. Abbas needed the Ottoman pressure off his western frontier so he could deal with the Uzbeks, the Qizilbash, and the structural reorganization of the state.

What he did with that fifteen years is the foundation of everything that followed.

---

The first project was the **ghulam revolution**.

The **Qizilbash** tribal cavalry — the founding military of the dynasty (Chapter 1) — had become a chronic political problem. The Qizilbash chiefs commanded their own tribal armies, governed major provinces as personal fiefs, intermarried with each other, and had repeatedly destabilized the state during succession crises. They had also been crushed at Chaldiran (Chapter 3) and were no longer competitive against Ottoman gunpowder armies. Abbas needed a military that was loyal to the crown rather than to tribal chiefs, equipped with modern weapons rather than lances, and answerable to the central state rather than to provincial power blocs.

The solution was the **ghulam** (GHOO-lahm) army. The word **ghulam** means "slave" or "servant" in Arabic, but the institution it referred to was something specific to the Islamic world: a military and administrative corps recruited from non-Muslim populations conquered or captured at the frontier, converted to Islam, trained from childhood in royal service, and made personally loyal to the monarch. The Ottomans had been doing this for two centuries with their Janissaries, recruited from Christian boys in the Balkans (Chapter 3). Abbas adapted the model.

The Safavid ghulam corps was recruited primarily from the **Caucasus** — Georgians, Armenians, Circassians — captured in raids, converted to Shia Islam, and trained in royal palace schools. Some came as boys taken in the **devshirme**-style boy levies; many more came as captives from the Caucasus campaigns Abbas conducted in the 1600s and 1610s. The captured boys were trained in horsemanship, military discipline, palace etiquette, Persian language, and Shia religion. The most promising were placed in elite musketeer or cavalry units. The most administratively talented were promoted to court positions, eventually rising to provincial governorships, military commands, and even the office of **vizier** (chief minister).

This was the structural innovation that made the ghulam system work: ghulams could not pass their offices to their sons in the way Qizilbash chiefs did. They served at the king's pleasure, owed everything to royal favor, and had no tribal power base to fall back on. The Georgian boy who became commander of the Safavid army served because Abbas had made him commander, and his Georgian relatives in their Caucasus villages had no say in the matter. This is exactly the kind of personal-loyalty mechanism that early-modern monarchs across Eurasia were building into their armies and bureaucracies — Janissaries in the Ottoman Empire, military slaves of the Mamluk sultanate before Ottoman conquest, eunuch administrators in Ming China — and the Safavid version worked with particular effectiveness.

The most famous example is **Allahverdi Khan** (ah-LAH-ver-dee), a Georgian boy captured in the Caucasus, converted, trained in palace service, and promoted by Abbas in the 1590s. Allahverdi rose to become commander-in-chief of the Safavid army, governor of the strategic province of Fars, and one of Abbas's most trusted advisors. He led the major Safavid victory over the Ottomans at **Sufiyan** in 1605 (which began the recovery of lost territory), recaptured Kandahar from the Mughals, and built the Si-o-se-pol bridge in Isfahan (more on which below). When he died in 1613, he was buried in a magnificent tomb at Mashhad, his career a model of what the ghulam system could achieve.

The ghulam corps grew to roughly 40,000 men by the mid-seventeenth century — a substantial standing army, equipped with muskets and cannons, drilled in modern infantry tactics, supported by artillery, and answerable directly to the Shah. The traditional Qizilbash tribal cavalry continued to exist as a secondary military force, but they no longer dominated the political system. Abbas had created a centralized monarchy on the early-modern European model, with a professional army loyal to the crown rather than to feudal lords. This was the structural foundation of everything else.

---

The military reform produced almost immediate strategic results.

In 1598, Abbas turned east and crushed the **Uzbeks** under their leader **Din Muhammad Khan** at the Battle of **Herat**, recovering Khorasan and the city of Herat (which had been under Uzbek occupation since 1588). In 1603, he launched the major Ottoman campaign that recovered most of the territory lost in 1590. The new Safavid army, with its musket-armed ghulam infantry and its bronze cannons, defeated the Ottomans at **Sufiyan** (1605), recaptured Tabriz, then Yerevan and the Caucasus. The **Treaty of Nasuh Pasha** (1612) and a follow-up treaty in 1618 restored most of the pre-1578 Safavid frontier. In 1622, with English East India Company naval assistance, Abbas captured the strategic Portuguese-held island of **Hormuz** at the entrance of the Persian Gulf (Chapter 6). In 1623, he captured **Baghdad** from the Ottomans, restoring Safavid control of the Shia holy cities of Najaf and Karbala for the first time since 1534. In 1624 and again in 1638, the Mughals and Safavids fought the chronic war over **Kandahar** that would continue into the next reign.

Within three decades, the dynasty that had been on the verge of collapse in 1588 had recovered nearly all its territory and projected power from the Caucasus to Kandahar. The transformation was complete by the late 1610s and the building program that crowned it was already underway.

---

The capital of the new Safavid state was **Isfahan**.

Isfahan is in central Iran, on the banks of the **Zayanderud** (zah-yahn-deh-ROOD, the "life-giving river" that flows out of the Zagros mountains and disappears into the central desert). It is one of the oldest cities in Iran, a pre-Islamic Sasanian city that had served as a regional capital under the Seljuks and other medieval dynasties. It is centrally located, defensible, with reliable water supply and good agricultural surroundings — strategic strengths that Tabriz and Qazvin could not match.

In 1598, Abbas formally moved the Safavid capital from Qazvin to Isfahan and began the most ambitious urban building program of the Safavid era. The project would consume thirty years and produce one of the most beautiful cities in the early-modern world.

The centerpiece was a vast new public square called **Naqsh-e Jahan** (nahkhsh-eh jah-HAHN, "Image of the World"). The square is approximately 1,750 feet long and 525 feet wide — the dimensions are deliberately chosen to be larger than any comparable public space in the early-modern world. (For context: Naqsh-e Jahan is roughly twice the size of St. Peter's Square in Rome, which is one of the largest comparable spaces in Renaissance Europe, and roughly seven times the size of Trafalgar Square in London.) The square was used for royal ceremonies, public festivals, military parades, polo matches, and the everyday commerce of the bazaar. The four sides were architecturally unified: the **Shah Mosque** at the south end, the **Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque** on the east, the **Ali Qapu palace** on the west, and the entrance to the **Grand Bazaar** on the north. Two-story arcaded shops surrounded the entire perimeter. The square was the political, religious, commercial, and ceremonial heart of the empire, expressed in a single architectural composition.

Each of the four anchor buildings deserves attention.

The **Shah Mosque** (now called **Imam Mosque** — every reference to "the Shah" in Iranian public space was renamed after the 1979 Islamic Revolution) is a massive congregational mosque built between 1611 and 1629. It is one of the most spectacular examples of Persian Islamic architecture: a four-iwan plan (a central courtyard with vaulted recesses opening on each side), a soaring 174-foot turquoise dome, twin minarets at the front entrance (and another pair at the rear), and several acres of polychrome tilework covering nearly every interior surface. The tilework alone is one of the great achievements of Islamic decorative art — a technique called **haft rang** (HAHFT RANG, "seven colors") in which polychrome glazes were fired onto large tile panels rather than assembled from small geometric pieces. This was an innovation specific to Safavid tilework and dramatically accelerated construction of large buildings. The Shah Mosque used haft rang panels at monumental scale to achieve naturalistic floral patterns, calligraphic inscriptions, and abstract geometric designs that could not have been produced quickly by traditional mosaic techniques.

The mosque also has a famous quirk: the entrance portal is oriented to the public square, but the prayer hall must be oriented to **Mecca** (the direction Muslims face during prayer, called the **qibla**, KIB-lah). The two orientations differ by about 45 degrees. The architects solved this by building a 45-degree rotation into the entrance vestibule, so that visitors enter from the square, pass through a transitional space, and emerge in the prayer hall facing Mecca. The transition is so smoothly handled that most visitors don't notice it. The **mathematician Sheikh Bahai** (Chapter 5) reportedly calculated the precise qibla direction.

The **Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque** on the east side of the square is a smaller but more architecturally refined building — Abbas's private royal chapel, never used for public Friday prayer. It was built between 1603 and 1619 and is considered by many specialists the apex of Safavid decorative art. The exterior dome is a distinctive cream color (rather than the usual Safavid turquoise) that shifts toward pink at sunrise and sunset. The interior tilework is more intricate than the Shah Mosque's, with a mesmerizing geometric ceiling pattern radiating from a central rosette. The mosque has no minaret and no courtyard — both omissions deliberate, since this was a private chapel, not a public mosque.

The **Ali Qapu palace** ("Sublime Gate") on the west side of the square was the imperial palace, a six-story tower with an open wooden terrace on the second level overlooking the square. Abbas and his successors used the terrace to watch polo matches, military parades, and the daily life of Naqsh-e Jahan from a position of imperial elevation. The upper floors contained reception rooms, a music chamber decorated with elaborate plaster niches cut in the shapes of vessels (designed to enhance acoustics for court concerts), and private royal apartments. The palace functioned as both a residence and a stage from which the Shah's relationship to the public square could be visually performed.

The **Grand Bazaar** entrance on the north side connected the square to the main commercial district of Isfahan, a covered market that stretched for miles through the old city. The bazaar was the economic engine of the capital, housing thousands of shops, dozens of caravanserais (the merchant inns where long-distance traders lodged with their goods), workshops for textiles and metalwork and ceramics, religious schools (madrasas), public baths, and tea houses.

The design of the entire complex was a piece of political theater. The Shah Mosque represented religion. The Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque represented the dynasty's private piety. The Ali Qapu represented royal power. The bazaar represented commerce. The square itself was the unifying public space where all four met, where the Shah could be seen by his subjects and his subjects could see each other in the architectural setting their ruler had built for them. It is one of the most successful pieces of urban design in the early-modern world, and it is essentially intact today — a UNESCO World Heritage site that you can still walk through, with the same buildings the same people in roughly the same spatial relationship Abbas designed five centuries ago.

The **Si-o-se-pol bridge** (see-oh-seh-POHL, "Bridge of Thirty-Three Arches"), built by Allahverdi Khan around 1602, completed the architectural ensemble of central Isfahan. It crossed the Zayanderud on a long stone causeway with — as the name says — thirty-three arches, providing infrastructure (the only major bridge over the river), water management (the bridge could be partially dammed to control river flow for irrigation), and social space (the upper promenade became one of Isfahan's favorite gathering places). The **Khwaju bridge** (kah-WAH-joo), built later in 1650 under Abbas II, completed the pair: a similar but more decorative stone bridge with teahouse rooms built into its piers, used as a pleasure garden and royal viewing pavilion as well as a crossing.

The Safavid bridges of Isfahan are some of the great pieces of early-modern infrastructure. They are also some of the most photographed buildings in modern Iran, the icons of the Safavid golden age that everyone who has been to Isfahan has stood on at sunset.

---

Isfahan grew enormously under Abbas and his successors. The city's population, perhaps 80,000 when Abbas moved his capital there in 1598, reached an estimated 500,000 to 600,000 by the 1620s — making Isfahan one of the largest cities in the world, comparable to Istanbul, Beijing, and (just barely) London and Paris. Contemporary census records (the figures are debated but the order of magnitude is reliable) describe the city containing 162 mosques, 48 religious schools, 1,802 caravanserais, and 273 public baths — a staggering urban infrastructure for a pre-industrial city. The proverb that contemporaries coined — *Isfahan nesf-e jahan ast*, "Isfahan is half the world" — was hyperbole, but it captured something real about the scale and beauty of what Abbas had built.

The proverb also captured something about the city's cultural diversity. By 1620, Isfahan was home to Iranian Persians, Turkic-speaking Qizilbash, Caucasian Georgian and Armenian ghulams, an Armenian Christian merchant community at **New Julfa** (Chapter 6), Indian Hindu traders working the Silk Road routes, English and Dutch East India Company representatives at their trading posts, occasional Catholic missionaries (Carmelites and Augustinians) sent by the Pope, and Jewish communities both old and new. The city was as cosmopolitan as any in the world. The proverb that the ulama probably found most uncomfortable about all this is that "half the world" included a lot of non-Shia.

---

Abbas died in 1629 at the age of fifty-eight, having reigned for forty-one years and presided over the recovery of his dynasty and the building of his capital. He was buried at **Kashan** (in central Iran) rather than at one of the great Shia shrines, in a relatively modest tomb whose lack of ostentation was probably deliberate. He had been one of the great early-modern monarchs — comparable in achievement to **Suleiman the Magnificent** of the Ottomans (whom he had matched militarily and surpassed culturally; Ottoman Empire Ch 5 covers Süleyman's reign and the parallel achievements in military recovery, architectural patronage, and administrative consolidation), to **Akbar** of the Mughals (his eastern rival, whose own architectural projects at Fatehpur Sikri were less ambitious than Naqsh-e Jahan), and to his contemporaries **Elizabeth I** of England and **Henry IV** of France in Europe.

He had also done something none of those monarchs had done: he had personally killed or blinded all of his potential heirs, leaving the throne to a grandson whose competence was untested.

That grandson was **Safi I**, and his reign would begin the long Safavid decline.

Cross-civ check: Abbas's reign (1588–1629) overlaps with the **Elizabethan and Jacobean periods** in England (Shakespeare's career runs almost exactly parallel to Abbas's reign), the **late Ming Dynasty** in China (about to collapse to the Qing in 1644), the **Mughal golden age** under Akbar and Jahangir in India, the **Ottoman classical age** under Murad III and Mehmed III, and the **European wars of religion** that would culminate in the Thirty Years War (1618–1648). Abbas was in active diplomatic correspondence with Pope Clement VIII, King Philip III of Spain, Queen Elizabeth I of England, and the Mughal emperor Akbar. The Safavid Empire under Abbas was a fully integrated participant in the early-modern world system, exchanging silk for silver, ambassadors for ambassadors, and ideas for ideas, with civilizations stretching from western Europe to East Asia.

# Chapter 5 — The Shia Clergy and the Isfahan School

The most consequential thing the Safavids built was not Naqsh-e Jahan Square. It was the institutional structure of Twelver Shia Islam — the clerical hierarchy, the seminary system, the relationship between religion and state — that emerged in Iran during the seventeenth century and has continued to define Iranian Islam to the present day.

This is the chapter that connects the Safavid past to modern Iran. The buildings of Isfahan are tourist attractions; the institutions described in this chapter are still running.

---

To understand what the Safavid clerical system became, you need to understand the doctrinal innovation at its core: the concept of the **mujtahid**.

Twelver Shia theology holds that the only fully legitimate religious-political authority on earth is the **Twelfth Imam**, the **Hidden Imam Muhammad al-Mahdi**, who went into occultation in 874 CE and will return at the end of time as the Shia messiah (Chapter 2). In the absence of the Imam, Shia communities need some form of religious guidance for everyday matters — questions of religious law, ritual, ethics, and theology — that the Imam himself cannot answer because he is hidden.

The early Twelver Shia tradition handled this through scholarly preservation of the Imams' teachings: collections of **hadith** (hah-DEETH — narrations attributed to the Prophet and the Imams), commentaries on those hadith, and direct application of the Imams' rulings to new questions by scholars who memorized the texts. This was the **Akhbari** (akh-BAH-ree, from *akhbar*, "reports" or "traditions") approach to Shia jurisprudence: religious guidance comes from the transmitted reports of the Imams, applied as directly as possible, with no independent reasoning by the contemporary scholar.

The competing approach was **Usuli** (oo-SOO-lee, from *usul al-fiqh*, "principles of jurisprudence"). The Usuli school held that contemporary scholars — qualified by training in legal reasoning, Arabic grammar, theology, and philosophy — could exercise **ijtihad** (ij-tih-HAHD, "independent reasoning") to derive new rulings from the foundational sources (Quran, hadith, consensus, reason) when the Imam's transmitted teachings did not directly address a contemporary question. A scholar qualified to exercise ijtihad was called a **mujtahid** (mooj-TAH-hid). And — crucially — Usuli theory held that ordinary Shia Muslims should follow the rulings of a living mujtahid, since the dead masters could not respond to new circumstances.

The implications of the Usuli position were enormous. If ordinary Shia must follow a living mujtahid, and if mujtahids are qualified by training rather than appointment, then the senior mujtahids of any given era effectively constitute a religious authority structure independent of the state. The mujtahid hierarchy is not appointed by the king. It is not subordinate to the king. It is parallel to the king, with its own legitimacy derived from religious training and recognized scholarly competence.

The eventual Usuli victory — completed in the eighteenth century, after the Safavid dynasty had ended — created the **marja-i taqlid** (mar-JAH-ee tahk-LEED, "source of emulation") system that defines modern Shia Islam. The most senior living mujtahids are called **marja**s. Each Shia Muslim formally chooses a marja to follow. The marja issues binding rulings on questions of religious practice, law, and ethics. The marjas collectively constitute a religious hierarchy with enormous social, financial, and political power — the power that ultimately enabled Ayatollah Khomeini, a senior marja, to lead the 1979 Iranian Revolution and establish the Islamic Republic of Iran.

The mujtahid system did not exist in this form when Ismail conquered Tabriz in 1501. It was built, gradually and contentiously, over the next two centuries — primarily by Lebanese Arab clerics imported by the Safavids (Chapter 2) and their Iranian disciples. The Akhbari-Usuli debate was not settled during the Safavid period; the Akhbaris were probably the dominant faction through most of the seventeenth century, with the Usuli school gaining strength in the late period and triumphing definitively in the eighteenth century at the shrine cities of Najaf and Karbala. But the institutional infrastructure that made the eventual Usuli victory possible — the seminaries, the patron-shrine networks, the sponsored translations of Arabic theological texts into Persian — was Safavid construction.

---

The institutional center of seventeenth-century Iranian Shia learning was **Isfahan**, the Safavid capital, where Abbas and his successors built or expanded a network of religious schools (**madrasas**) attached to the major mosques. The most famous were the **Madrasa-ye Chahar Bagh** (Four Gardens School, completed 1714 — covered in Chapter 7) and the **Madrasa-ye Jadda-ye Buzurg** (Great Grandmother School, founded by Shah Soleyman's mother). These schools trained the leading Iranian Shia clerics of the age, hosted advanced philosophical and theological debate, and produced the textual scholarship that would define Twelver Shia Islam for the following centuries.

The phenomenon centered on Isfahan was rich enough to deserve its own name in the historical literature: the **School of Isfahan**. It is one of the great moments in Islamic intellectual history.

The School of Isfahan was a philosophical movement that synthesized three earlier traditions. The first was **Peripatetic philosophy** — the Aristotelian tradition transmitted through medieval Islamic philosophers like **Ibn Sina** (Avicenna, 980–1037) and **al-Farabi** (c. 872–950). The second was **Illuminationism** (Persian *ishraqi*) — a mystical-philosophical school founded by **Suhrawardi** (sue-rah-WAR-dee, executed in 1191) that combined Aristotelian logic with Neoplatonic mystical insights about the nature of light, the structure of reality, and the soul's ascent to higher levels of being. The third was **Sufi mysticism**, the contemplative tradition we met in Chapter 1.

The School of Isfahan tried to synthesize all three. The result was a sophisticated philosophical system — at once rationalist (using Aristotelian logical analysis), mystical (insisting that the highest knowledge comes through direct intellectual intuition rather than discursive reasoning), and orthodox Shia (interpreting the Imams as cosmic principles as well as historical figures). It is one of the most ambitious philosophical systems in the history of Islamic thought, and it has been continuously studied at Iranian seminaries from the seventeenth century to the present.

The story of the school is best told through the man who was driven out of it.

**Mulla Sadra** (mool-LAH SAH-dra, full name Sadr al-Din Muhammad ibn Ibrahim Shirazi, c. 1572–1640) is the school's deepest philosopher and probably its most influential — and also the figure whose career most clearly exposes the tension at the heart of Safavid intellectual life. Born in Shiraz, he came to Isfahan as a young man to study under the leading philosophers of the new capital. He did the work expected of him, then began to push beyond it — and was driven out of the city, reportedly by hostile clerics who considered his metaphysics heretical and his teachings dangerous to public piety. He withdrew to the small village of **Kahak**, near Qom, where he lived in semi-rural retreat for somewhere between seven and twelve years (the exact dates are uncertain). It was at Kahak, far from the patronage and the persecution of the capital, that he wrote most of his major work. He was eventually rehabilitated enough to be invited back to Shiraz to teach, but the exile was the formative episode of his career and a perfect illustration of the chapter's central argument: that the same Safavid state that funded the most ambitious philosophical synthesis in early-modern Islam also produced a clerical establishment increasingly able to exile the philosophers it sponsored.

The philosophy that Mulla Sadra developed during his Kahak years was a metaphysical system called **al-hikma al-muta'aliya** ("Transcendent Theosophy") whose central doctrine was the **primacy of existence** over essence. The technical philosophy is hard to translate (Sadra's Arabic prose is famously difficult), but the core insight is roughly this: reality is fundamentally about existence, the dynamic process of being itself, rather than about the static essences or definitions of things. Existence has gradations — different levels of intensity — and beings move through these gradations in a continuous process of "substantial motion." The soul ascends through levels of being toward unity with God; the world is constantly in flux; the static categories of older Aristotelian philosophy mislead us about the nature of what is. Mulla Sadra's system has often been compared, within the Islamic philosophical tradition, to the great synthesizing systems of medieval Christianity (Aquinas) and modern European philosophy (Hegel) — figures whose works similarly tried to weld inherited rationalist traditions to a new theological or historical vision.

Mulla Sadra did not produce his system out of nothing. He was the culmination of a generational project, and the two figures who set up his work need to be introduced — partly because their careers show what it looked like to operate inside the Isfahan establishment rather than outside it.

**Mir Damad** (meer dah-MAHD, c. 1561–1631) was Sadra's most important teacher and the founding figure of the Isfahan school proper. A philosopher-theologian who combined Aristotelian metaphysics with Shia theology in elaborate Arabic treatises, he wrote in deliberately obscure prose that earned him the nickname "the Third Teacher" — Aristotle being the First, al-Farabi the Second. Mir Damad's most famous innovation was the concept of *huduth-i dahri* — eternal creation in time — a metaphysical position that tried to reconcile the Aristotelian doctrine of the eternity of the world with the Islamic doctrine of divine creation. The argument is technical and not easily summarized, but it set the philosophical agenda Sadra would inherit and radicalize. Mir Damad himself was a court favorite who died peacefully in royal service. The students he trained — Mulla Sadra preeminently — would not all be so fortunate.

**Sheikh Bahai** (BAH-hai, full name Baha al-Din Muhammad ibn Husayn al-Amili, 1547–1621) was the other senior figure of the same generation, the polymath par excellence of the Safavid court. He was Lebanese in origin (one of the Jabal Amil clerics imported during the conversion period — Chapter 2), and he served Shah Abbas as both religious advisor and practical engineer. His range was extraordinary: he was a senior Shia theologian, a mathematician, an astronomer, an architect, a hydraulic engineer, a poet (in both Persian and Arabic), and a designer of practical infrastructure. He calculated the qibla orientation of the Shah Mosque (Chapter 4). He designed the irrigation system that distributed water across Isfahan. He wrote treatises on arithmetic, algebra, astronomy, and theology that were standard textbooks at Iranian seminaries for centuries. He may have been the most learned individual of his century in the Islamic world, comparable to figures like Galileo and Kepler in Europe — and like them, equally at home in religion and in natural science. Bahai's career is the inside-track version of what Sadra's might have been: a Lebanese cleric absorbed seamlessly into the Safavid establishment, employed simultaneously by the dynasty and by the religious hierarchy, dying in royal favor.

The contrast across these three biographies is the chapter's argument in miniature. Mir Damad and Sheikh Bahai were establishment figures whose work the regime patronized and whose orthodoxy was unimpeachable. Sadra was their student, took their methods further, and was driven into rural exile by the next generation of clerics — the same clerical establishment the Safavids had built up to legitimize their rule, now strong enough to exile the empire's greatest philosopher. The same state that paid Lebanese clerics to publicly curse the first three caliphs (Chapter 2) also paid Mir Damad to teach and then tolerated the exile of his most brilliant pupil. The contradiction was real. It was also a preview: the clerical establishment that could exile Mulla Sadra in the 1610s was the same establishment that, under Majlisi at the end of the century, would be powerful enough to dictate religious policy to the dynasty itself.

**The Isfahan school and the Gundishapur precedent.** This was not the first time something like the Isfahan synthesis had happened on Iranian soil. The Sasanian-era **Gundishapur Academy** (Persian Empire Ch 9), founded in the third century CE and at its height under Khosrow I in the sixth, had performed almost exactly the same function — synthesizing Greek Aristotelian philosophy, Indian mathematics and medicine, and the Zoroastrian religious tradition into a working intellectual system that would outlast the dynasty that funded it. The Isfahan school, a thousand years later and under a different state religion, was the same civilizational phenomenon recurring: an Iranian dynasty using state patronage to weld together imported philosophical traditions (here Aristotelian, Neoplatonic, Sufi) with the dominant religion of the moment, producing an intellectual synthesis that would survive the dynasty's fall. Two empires apart, the same Iranian habit of building synthesizing intellectual institutions at the heart of the state.

---

Outside the philosophical schools, the Safavid clerical project also developed the **shrine pilgrimage network** that became the geographic infrastructure of Iranian Shia identity.

Sunni Islam has Mecca and Medina as its two great pilgrimage sites — both controlled, after 1517, by the Sunni Ottomans. Twelver Shia Islam has Mecca and Medina too (the Prophet himself is foundational to Shia as much as to Sunni faith), but it also has additional shrine sites associated with the twelve Imams and their families. Most of these sites are in Iraq: **Najaf** (the burial place of Imam Ali, the First Imam), **Karbala** (the martyrdom site of Imam Hussein, the Third Imam), **Kazimiyah** (in Baghdad, the burial place of the Seventh and Ninth Imams), and **Samarra** (the disappearance site of the Twelfth Imam). All four are under Ottoman control through most of the Safavid period.

The Safavids therefore developed parallel shrine networks inside Iran. The most important is the shrine of **Imam Reza** (the Eighth Imam, who died in 818 CE) at **Mashhad** (mahsh-HAD) in northeastern Iran. The shrine had existed for centuries, but the Safavids — beginning under Tahmasp I and continuing through Abbas I and his successors — expanded it into one of the largest religious complexes in the Islamic world. The shrine acquired massive endowments, multiple courtyards, monumental gateways, gold-plated minarets, and elaborate tilework. By the late Safavid period, Mashhad was effectively the alternative Mecca for Iranian Shia, with hundreds of thousands of pilgrims visiting annually.

**Qom** (KOHM), in north-central Iran, was developed as the second great Iranian shrine city. Qom is the burial place of **Fatima Masumeh**, the sister of Imam Reza, who died there in 816 CE. The Safavids expanded the shrine, built religious schools (the **Qom hawza**, hahw-ZAH — the seminary network) that drew students from across the Shia world, and established Qom as the center of Iranian Shia religious learning. The Qom hawza became the institutional descendant of the older shrine seminaries at Najaf and Karbala (which remained more prestigious through most of the period but were under Ottoman control), and it has grown continuously since. The Qom hawza is where **Ayatollah Khomeini** was trained in the twentieth century. It is where most senior Iranian clerics still receive their religious education today. The institutional continuity from the seventeenth-century Safavid expansion to the modern Islamic Republic is essentially unbroken.

A third major shrine, at **Ardabil** in northwestern Iran, was the original Safavid family shrine — the burial site of Sheikh Safi al-Din himself (Chapter 1). Abbas I expanded the Ardabil complex enormously, donating a massive library of manuscripts and decorative objects (including the famous Ardabil Carpet, Chapter 6), and the shrine became a pilgrimage destination connecting Safavid dynastic legitimacy to the religious identity of the country. The Ardabil shrine survives today and is also a UNESCO World Heritage site.

The point of the shrine network was to create a religious geography that was authentically Iranian, controlled by the Safavid state, and not dependent on Sunni-controlled Arab Iraq or Arabia. Mashhad replaced Mecca for Iranian Shia who could not (or would not) make the journey to Sunni-controlled Hejaz. Qom became the seminary city. Ardabil sanctified the dynasty itself. The cumulative effect was to ground Twelver Shia Islam in Iranian soil — to make Shia identity geographically Iranian rather than diasporic — in a way that helped the religion take root permanently after the dynasty that had imposed it was gone.

---

The relationship between the clergy and the state was always complicated, and it grew steadily more tense as the seventeenth century progressed.

The early Safavid clerical class had been almost entirely a state creation: imported Lebanese scholars paid by the regime, granted Iranian land and offices, and supporting the dynasty's claim to legitimate Shia kingship. By the mid-seventeenth century, a native Iranian Shia clerical establishment had developed enough independence to start asserting its own theological positions — including, increasingly, the position that the Safavid Shah was not himself a religious authority but only a temporal ruler who served the religious authority of the senior mujtahids.

The most assertive figure in this development was **Muhammad Baqir Majlisi** (mahj-LEE-see, 1627–1699), who served as the **Shaykh al-Islam** (the chief religious official) of Isfahan under the late Safavids and became the most influential cleric of the century. Majlisi was a militant Akhbari traditionalist who pushed Iranian Shia Islam in a more legalistic, more orthodox, less philosophically open direction. He persecuted Sufi orders (which he considered heretical), restricted Iranian Christians and Zoroastrians, suppressed popular religious practices that did not align with strict Shia legalism, and produced an enormous compilation of Shia hadith called **Bihar al-Anwar** ("Oceans of Lights") — 110 volumes, the most ambitious Shia hadith collection ever assembled. The *Bihar al-Anwar* is still consulted today as a primary reference work in Shia jurisprudence.

Majlisi's career marks the moment when the Iranian Shia clergy became fully institutionally independent of the state — a class of religious specialists with their own legal authority, financial endowments, educational institutions, and popular following, capable of dictating religious policy to a Safavid Shah who increasingly needed their support to legitimate his rule. The pattern that would dominate modern Iran — a king (or president) constantly negotiating with senior clerics for religious legitimacy — was established under the late Safavids and has continued, with various interruptions, ever since.

The Safavid Shahs of the late seventeenth century — Soleyman I and Sultan Husayn (Chapter 7) — were unable or unwilling to push back against this clerical independence. They allowed Majlisi and his successors to set religious policy, restrict cultural life, and persecute non-orthodox communities, without the kind of state pushback that earlier Safavid rulers might have applied. The result was a long-term shift in the balance of power between the dynasty and the clerical establishment that the Safavids themselves had created. By 1700, the Iranian Shia clergy was strong enough to outlast the Safavid dynasty entirely. When the Afghans destroyed the Safavid state in 1722 (Chapter 8), the clerical establishment survived. When Nader Shah deposed the last Safavid in 1736 (Chapter 8) and tried to broker a Shia-Sunni rapprochement, the clerical establishment opposed him and won. When the Qajar dynasty replaced the short-lived Afsharids in the late eighteenth century, they had to negotiate with the clergy on the clergy's terms. When the Pahlavi dynasty tried in the twentieth century to modernize Iran on a secular model, the clergy organized the resistance that eventually overthrew them. The chain runs from Majlisi to Khomeini, and the institutional infrastructure was Safavid construction.

---

The intellectual and religious flowering of seventeenth-century Iran was therefore not a side product of the Safavid state but its most enduring legacy. The buildings of Isfahan are spectacular but they are buildings. The clerical hierarchy, the seminary system, the legal-philosophical traditions, the shrine networks, the relationship between religion and state — these are still running, four centuries later, governing the lives of eighty million Iranians and influencing Shia Muslims worldwide.

The Safavids declared Twelver Shia Islam the state religion in 1501 because it served their political interests. Once established, the Shia identity outgrew them. By 1700, the Shia clerical class was strong enough to be independent of any specific dynasty. By 1979, it was strong enough to make itself the dynasty.

This is the deepest meaning of the Safavid revolution. They did not merely change Iran's religion. They built an institutional structure that turned religion into a permanent feature of Iranian state-society relations, a feature with its own self-perpetuating logic, capable of outlasting any of the regimes that nominally controlled it. The most important Safavid creation is the one that does not appear on any tourist itinerary.

Cross-civ check: the seventeenth-century philosophical flowering at Isfahan is contemporary with the **Scientific Revolution** in Europe — Galileo, Descartes, Newton, all working at roughly the same time as Mulla Sadra. The two intellectual revolutions were operating on entirely different premises: European thinkers were building the experimental methodology of modern natural science, while the Isfahan school was extending the Aristotelian-Neoplatonic-Sufi tradition into new metaphysical territory. The relationship between the two has been debated since the nineteenth century. The European tradition produced the technological transformation that would eventually overtake the Islamic world; the Iranian tradition produced a metaphysical depth that European philosophy lacked. Each got something the other did not. The world would have been different if they had met more directly.

# Chapter 6 — Trade, Carpets, and the World

The Safavid Empire under Abbas I was not just an Iranian state. It was a node in the early-modern world economy, exporting silk and carpets to Europe, importing silver from the Spanish Americas, hosting English and Dutch trading companies, and managing diplomatic correspondence with Catholic Habsburgs as well as Protestant Stuarts.

This chapter is about the commercial face of the Safavid state — the silk monopoly, the Armenian merchant network at New Julfa, the European trading companies, the recapture of Hormuz, and the cultural products (carpets, miniatures, ceramics) that became internationally recognized luxury goods. The economic story matters because it explains how Abbas funded the building program of Chapter 4 and the clerical infrastructure of Chapter 5. The Safavid golden age was built on silk and silver.

---

The commercial backbone was **silk**.

Iran had been producing silk since the Sasanian period (Persian Empire Ch 9), when sericulture (silkworm cultivation) had been imported from China along the Silk Road. By the sixteenth century, the principal silk-producing region was **Gilan** (gee-LAHN) and **Mazandaran** (mah-zahn-dah-RAHN) — the humid Caspian provinces of northern Iran where mulberry trees (the food source for silkworms) grew in profusion. Safavid Iran produced an estimated 700,000 to 1 million pounds of raw silk per year by the late sixteenth century, making it one of the largest silk producers in the world.

The economic geography of silk had, until the Safavids, run mostly through Ottoman territory. Iranian silk went west through Anatolia to Aleppo and Bursa, where it was sold to Italian and English merchants who shipped it to European markets. The Ottoman state collected substantial transit duties. The Iranian producer got a fraction of the eventual European retail price.

Abbas I attacked this system on multiple fronts. First, in 1599, he declared a **royal monopoly** on raw silk export — silk could only be sold to European merchants through a single state-controlled marketing apparatus, with all revenues flowing through the Safavid treasury. Second, he actively promoted alternative export routes that bypassed Ottoman territory. Silk could move south through the Persian Gulf, where European trading ships could pick it up at Hormuz or Bandar Abbas; or north through the Russian-controlled Caspian and Volga route, where it reached Europe via Moscow and Archangelsk. Third, he negotiated directly with European trading companies — particularly the English East India Company and the Dutch VOC (Verenigde Oostindische Compagnie) — to establish trading posts in Iranian ports.

The result was a substantial reorganization of the world silk trade in Iran's favor. By the 1620s, perhaps half of Iranian silk export was moving through non-Ottoman channels, and the Iranian state was capturing a much larger share of the eventual sale price. The silk monopoly was the single largest source of Safavid government revenue for most of the seventeenth century.

---

The mechanism that made the silk monopoly work commercially was the **Armenian merchant community of New Julfa**.

In 1604–1605, in the middle of his Ottoman campaigns, Abbas conducted one of the most ruthless population-engineering projects in early-modern history: he forcibly relocated the entire Armenian Christian population of the city of **Old Julfa** (on the Aras River, in the Caucasus borderland) across hundreds of miles of difficult terrain to a new settlement on the south bank of the Zayanderud River, just outside Isfahan. Population estimates vary widely: Armenian sources of the period give figures as high as 50,000 to 100,000, while many modern historians give substantially lower numbers — perhaps 10,000 to 20,000 — and treat the higher figures as the upper bound of a contested range. Many died on the march. Old Julfa was burned to deny it to the Ottomans (the standard scorched-earth tactic of the period) and the survivors were resettled in the new district called **New Julfa** (**Nor Jugha** in Armenian).

The brutal logic of the relocation was economic. The Armenians of Old Julfa had been one of the most successful merchant communities in the Caucasus — they had centuries of experience in long-distance trade, particularly in raw silk, and they had developed a network of commercial agents across the Ottoman, Mughal, Russian, and Mediterranean worlds. By moving them inside Iran, Abbas was essentially nationalizing their trading network. The Armenians were given commercial privileges, religious autonomy (they could maintain their own churches, schools, and Armenian Apostolic clergy), and effective control of the silk monopoly's commercial operations. In exchange, they served as the Safavid state's commercial agents to the rest of the world.

The arrangement was extraordinarily successful. Within a few decades, New Julfa was one of the wealthiest mercantile communities in Eurasia. New Julfan merchants operated trading houses in Venice, Marseille, Amsterdam, London, Aleppo, Istanbul, Surat, Madras, Manila, and as far away as Acapulco and Lhasa. They handled the bulk of Iran's silk exports and imported in exchange Spanish silver (mined in the Americas, shipped via Manila or the Levant), European luxury manufactures, and Indian cotton textiles. They built thirteen churches in New Julfa (the cathedral, **Vank**, with its remarkable frescoes blending Armenian, Persian, and European Renaissance styles, still stands today and is one of the most visited monuments in Isfahan), funded a printing press (the **Vank Cathedral Press**, founded in 1639, was the first printing press in Iran and one of the earliest in the Middle East), and developed a distinctive Armenian-Iranian merchant culture that has been studied in detail by economic historians like **Sebouh Aslanian**.

The New Julfa network is one of the great early-modern merchant diasporas, comparable to the Sephardic Jewish networks of the Mediterranean, the Gujarati Hindu networks of the Indian Ocean, and the Hokkien Chinese networks of Southeast Asia. It connected Iran to the world economy in a way that had not existed before and would not exist again after the network broke up in the eighteenth century. New Julfa is also one of the great moments of Safavid pragmatism: the same regime that was forcibly converting Sunni Iranians to Shia Islam (Chapter 2) maintained a protected Armenian Christian community at the heart of its capital because the Armenians were commercially valuable. The state served its economic interests over its religious ones when the two conflicted.

---

The Europeans the Armenians sold silk to were primarily the **English East India Company** and the **Dutch East India Company (VOC)**.

The European East India Companies were a new kind of commercial-political entity — joint-stock corporations chartered by their home governments with monopoly rights over Asian trade and effectively delegated military authority to defend their commercial interests by force. The English EIC was chartered in 1600. The Dutch VOC was chartered in 1602. By the 1610s, both were operating throughout the Indian Ocean and increasingly seeking to break the Portuguese commercial monopoly that had dominated Asian trade for the previous century.

The Portuguese had reached India in 1498 (Vasco da Gama's voyage) and quickly established a fortified network of trading posts (called *feitorias*) at strategic chokepoints across the Indian Ocean — Goa (in India), Malacca (in Southeast Asia), Mozambique (in East Africa), and most importantly **Hormuz** (the island controlling the entrance to the Persian Gulf, captured by the Portuguese commander Afonso de Albuquerque in 1507). The Portuguese had used Hormuz as the linchpin of their control over Persian Gulf trade for over a century, levying customs duties on all merchant vessels and reducing Iranian commerce in the Gulf to a Portuguese tributary.

Abbas wanted Hormuz back. The Portuguese were too well fortified, and the Safavids had no fleet capable of contesting Portuguese naval power. The English EIC, by contrast, did have a fleet — and they wanted to displace the Portuguese to capture the Iranian silk trade.

In 1622, an Anglo-Persian alliance made the deal. English EIC ships landed Iranian troops on Hormuz; the combined force besieged the Portuguese garrison; after several weeks of fighting, the Portuguese surrendered. Hormuz returned to Iranian control after 115 years of Portuguese occupation. The English East India Company received commercial privileges, including a substantial share of Hormuz customs revenues and exemptions from various duties. The new Iranian port of **Bandar Abbas** (literally "Port of Abbas") was developed on the mainland coast opposite Hormuz to serve as the primary Persian Gulf trading hub. The Persian Gulf, after a century of Portuguese dominance, returned to operating under primarily Iranian and English control — with Dutch competition added in the 1620s and 1630s.

The recapture of Hormuz is one of the most consequential strategic moments of the Safavid period. It restored Iranian control over the country's southern maritime trade, freed Iranian commerce from Portuguese transit duties, and established the foundation of the Iran-Britain commercial relationship that would later (much later, in unfortunate ways) shape the Iranian oil industry. Bandar Abbas remained a major Iranian port for the next two centuries, and it is still a working port today.

---

The other great Safavid export was the **Persian carpet**.

Persian carpet weaving has roots going back at least to the Achaemenid period (Persian Empire Ch 2) — the famous **Pazyryk Carpet**, found frozen in a Scythian tomb in Siberia and dated to the fifth or fourth century BCE, is widely considered the oldest surviving woven carpet in the world and is generally attributed to Achaemenid Persian production. The tradition continued through the Sasanian, Islamic, and Timurid periods. But the Safavids elevated carpet weaving to the highest level of state-sponsored art, comparable to Renaissance Italian painting in technical sophistication and significantly more widely traded as a commercial product.

Abbas I established **royal carpet workshops** at Isfahan, **Kashan** (in central Iran), **Kerman** (in southeastern Iran), and **Tabriz** in the early 1600s. Each workshop developed distinctive regional styles. The Isfahan and Kashan workshops produced the most refined court carpets, often in silk, with extraordinarily fine knotting (sometimes 800 to 1,000 knots per square inch), elaborate medallion-and-corner compositions, and rich color palettes including the deep madder reds and indigo blues that distinguish Safavid carpets. The Kerman workshops specialized in vase-pattern carpets with elaborate floral motifs. The Tabriz workshops continued the older Tabriz tradition of garden-pattern carpets.

The most famous individual Safavid carpet is the **Ardabil Carpet** — actually a pair of matching carpets, both commissioned around 1539–40 for the Safavid family shrine at Ardabil (Chapter 5). The carpets are massive — 34 feet by 17 feet — with a central medallion design surrounded by elaborate floral arabesques, lamp motifs (referencing the Quranic Verse of Light), and an inscription that gives the date and the name of the master weaver, **Maqsud of Kashan**. The knotting is approximately 300 knots per square inch — at the carpet's stated dimensions, that works out to roughly 25 million individual hand-tied knots (popular accounts often round this up to 26 million), requiring thousands of person-hours of skilled weaving labor. The carpets are arguably the most ambitious surviving textile objects from the early-modern world.

Both Ardabil Carpets survive, in damaged but largely intact form. The better-preserved one is in the **Victoria and Albert Museum in London** (purchased in 1893 after a complicated provenance trail through Iranian, Ottoman, and English hands). The other is in the **Los Angeles County Museum of Art**. Together they are the touchstone of all subsequent assessment of Safavid carpet art.

Persian carpets became internationally traded luxury goods on a massive scale during the Safavid period. They appear in Renaissance European paintings — most famously in seventeenth-century Dutch still lifes and interiors (Vermeer painted Persian carpets in several of his domestic scenes) — and in inventories of European royal and aristocratic households throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. (A common confusion is worth flagging here: the so-called "Holbein carpets" that appear in Hans Holbein's sixteenth-century portraits are Anatolian, not Safavid Persian — they were woven in Ottoman Turkey, and specialists use "Holbein" only as a shorthand for that earlier Anatolian pattern type. Persian carpets in European paintings are typically the later Dutch and Italian still-life examples.) The Safavid carpet was, in its time, what a Picasso painting is in ours: an instantly recognizable luxury object that signaled wealth, sophistication, and access to global commerce.

---

The other great visual art of the Safavid period was **miniature painting**.

The Safavid miniature tradition built on the Timurid Herat school (Chapter 1), the Tabriz school of the early Safavids (Chapter 3), and the broader heritage of Persian book painting. Under Abbas I, the dominant figure of the Isfahan school was **Reza Abbasi** (RAY-zah ah-BAH-see, c. 1565–1635), the leading court painter of the early seventeenth century.

Reza Abbasi's innovation was a shift toward more naturalistic portraiture, single-figure compositions, and sketch-like draftsmanship that broke with the dense, narrative-driven compositions of earlier Persian painting. His characteristic works are not illustrations of literary or historical scenes but stand-alone images: a young dervish reading, a courtier reclining, a hunter with his dog, a lover writing a letter. The figures are graceful, the lines fluid, the color palettes muted and atmospheric. Reza Abbasi's style influenced Persian painting for the next two centuries and provided much of the visual vocabulary that later spread to Mughal painting in India.

Reza Abbasi also represented the social position that visual artists occupied at the Safavid court: he was a salaried royal employee, the head of the imperial painting workshop, expected to produce works for royal commissions but also free to develop independent artistic projects. The Safavid court provided patronage on a scale comparable to the contemporary Mughal court and substantially greater than most Ottoman patronage of the same period (the Ottomans tended to fund architecture more than painting). Persian visual culture under the Safavids was operating at the highest level of the early-modern world.

Persian poetry of the Safavid period has been less celebrated than the painting and architecture, partly because it produced no figure of the stature of the great medieval poets — Rumi, Hafez, Sa'di, Ferdowsi — whose work defined the Persian poetic canon. The dominant Safavid-era style was called **Sabk-e Hindi** (SAHBK-eh HIN-dee, the "Hindi Style," because it was particularly cultivated by Persian-language poets at the Mughal court in India). Sabk-e Hindi was characterized by elaborate metaphorical conceits, philosophical and melancholic themes, and a high degree of intellectual complexity. Major Iranian Sabk-e Hindi poets included **Saib Tabrizi** (sah-EEB tab-REE-zee, c. 1592–1676), whose technical brilliance and prolific output made him one of the most quoted Persian poets in his own day. Modern Iranian literary opinion has rehabilitated the Sabk-e Hindi poets in recent decades, but for most of the twentieth century they were dismissed as overly mannered. The truth is somewhere in between: technical virtuosity at the cost of immediate emotional accessibility.

---

The cultural products of the Safavid Empire — silk textiles, carpets, miniatures, ceramics, illuminated manuscripts — became standard luxury imports in early-modern European royal and aristocratic households. They also flowed east to the Mughal court, where they shaped Mughal taste and provided technical training to Mughal artists. Persian-language poetry, philosophy, and scholarship dominated the literary culture of the Mughal Empire from Babur to Aurangzeb (1526–1707) — the Mughal court conducted its administration in Persian, patronized Persian poets and scholars, and considered Iran the senior cultural partner of the Persianate world.

The Safavid-Mughal relationship was therefore politically competitive (over Kandahar, where the two empires fought a series of wars across the seventeenth century — the city changed hands four times) but culturally cooperative. Persian artists, scholars, and merchants moved freely between the two courts. The two **Tabriz painters** Mir Sayyid Ali and Abd al-Samad migrated to Delhi after the chaos of Ismail II's reign and became founding figures of Mughal painting. The poet **Urfi Shirazi** wrote his major work at the Mughal court of Akbar. The Sabk-e Hindi style developed simultaneously in both empires. The cultural traffic was so intense that by the late seventeenth century, much of the most innovative Persian-language cultural production was happening at the Mughal court rather than at the Safavid court.

This is one of the structural features of the Persianate world that distinguishes it from the European national-cultural framework. Persian was the literary and administrative language of an enormous geographic region — from Anatolia through Iran into Central Asia and India — that contained multiple competing political states. A Persian poet, a Persian painter, a Persian scholar could move between courts as the political winds shifted, finding patronage wherever it was most generous. The Safavid-Mughal cultural exchange is a particularly intense example of this broader Persianate cultural mobility, but the pattern is general. Persian culture under the Safavids was a regional civilization, not a national one.

---

By 1629, when Abbas I died, the Safavid Empire was a fully integrated participant in the early-modern world economy. Silk flowed west through Armenian merchants to European markets. Silver flowed east from Spanish America to pay for it. English and Dutch ships called at Bandar Abbas. Carpets and miniatures decorated the homes of European aristocrats. The Safavid state was, on the commercial scale, one of the most important trading partners of Europe and arguably the second-richest state in the early-modern Islamic world (after the Mughal Empire under Shah Jahan, which had a much larger population and tax base).

The decline that followed is the subject of the next chapter, and the question is partly why the commercial integration that worked so well under Abbas could not be sustained. The answer involves bad rulers, declining military discipline, religious orthodoxy at the expense of intellectual openness, and ultimately the resurgence of Afghan tribal power on the eastern frontier. But the commercial integration was real, and the cultural-economic Iran that Abbas built was, for a period, one of the truly successful early-modern states. The decline destroyed the political structure but it did not destroy the cultural memory. Naqsh-e Jahan and Vank Cathedral are still standing.

Cross-civ check: the Safavid commercial integration of the early 1600s is contemporary with the rise of the **Dutch Golden Age** (Rembrandt, Vermeer, the VOC), the **English East India Company's** establishment in India (Surat 1612, Madras 1640), the **Manila galleon trade** that connected Spanish America to Asia via the Philippines, and the early **Mughal commercial expansion** under Jahangir and Shah Jahan. The early-modern world economy was being knit together at this moment, and Iran under Abbas was one of its central nodes — a richer, more sophisticated, more globally connected Iran than the country would be again until the late twentieth century.

# Chapter 7 — The Long Decline

In April 1629, Shah Abbas the Great died at his palace in Mazandaran. The throne passed to his eighteen-year-old grandson, who took the regnal name **Safi I** (sah-FEE) and reigned for the next thirteen years.

What followed was a slow institutional decay that lasted nearly a century. The Safavid Empire after Abbas was still one of the largest and richest states in the Islamic world, still ruling from Isfahan in palaces of extraordinary beauty, still collecting silk monopoly revenues and trading with European merchants. But the underlying foundations — the military discipline of the ghulam army, the administrative competence of the central court, the tax base in the productive provinces, and the assertive religious leadership of the early dynasty — were eroding. Each successive Shah was weaker than the last. Each generation lost more territory, more revenue, and more political will. By 1700, the Safavid Empire was a hollow shell whose decay had not yet broken into the open. By 1722, the shell would shatter.

This chapter is the long decline. It is unglamorous compared to the founding revolution of Ismail or the magnificence of Abbas's Isfahan, but the long decline is what most institutional histories of empires actually look like — slow, undramatic, internally driven, the kind of erosion that does not produce a single moment of crisis until the day everything finally collapses.

---

**Safi I** (ruled 1629–1642) was Abbas's grandson — Abbas had killed his own son and blinded two others (Chapter 4), leaving Safi as the only viable heir. Safi was eighteen at his accession, inexperienced, paranoid, and reportedly cruel. His early reign was dominated by a systematic purge of Abbas's senior officials, including the great Georgian ghulam commander **Imam Quli Khan** and his entire family — executed on suspicion of political ambition. The purges removed most of the military and administrative talent that had served Abbas, replacing competent veterans with younger officials whose loyalty was certain but whose capability was untested.

The military consequences appeared almost immediately. In 1638, the Ottomans under Sultan **Murad IV** launched a major campaign against Safavid Iraq — the same Iraq that Abbas had conquered in 1623 — and recaptured **Baghdad** after a forty-day siege. The fall of Baghdad in 1638 was a catastrophic loss for the Safavids. It ended Iranian control of the Shia holy cities of **Najaf** and **Karbala**. It cut off Iranian Shia from direct access to the major shrines of the imams. And it forced the Iranian state to negotiate from weakness with an Ottoman regime that had clearly recovered from the setbacks of Abbas's reign.

The result was the **Treaty of Zuhab** (zoo-HAHB), signed in 1639 and named for the small village (also called Qasr-e Shirin) where the negotiations took place. The treaty fixed the Ottoman-Safavid frontier with extraordinary precision: the Ottomans retained Baghdad and Mesopotamia (modern Iraq), the Safavids retained the Caucasus and the Iranian plateau, and the boundary ran roughly along the line of the modern Iran-Iraq border. The Treaty of Zuhab is one of the most enduring international borders in world history. The line drawn at Zuhab in 1639 is essentially the line that the United Nations recognizes today, and it has survived the collapse of both the Safavid and Ottoman empires, the British creation of modern Iraq in 1920, the Iran-Iraq War of 1980–88, and the American invasion of Iraq in 2003. There may be no other Middle Eastern frontier that has been as stable for as long. The Ottoman Empire Ch 8 covers the same treaty from the Ottoman side — readers of either TL encounter one of the most durable geopolitical facts of the modern Middle East.

The Treaty of Zuhab also marked the end of large-scale Ottoman-Safavid warfare. After 1639, the two empires settled into a tense but stable coexistence. The hot war of the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries was over. This relative stability was one of the things that allowed both empires to persist into the eighteenth century without major external pressure — but it also meant that neither could blame external enemies for the internal decay that was setting in.

---

**Abbas II** (ruled 1642–1666) was Safi's son, and his reign is generally treated as the Indian summer of the dynasty — a brief recovery before the deeper decline. Abbas II was capable, energetic, and reasonably ruthless in ways that made him an effective ruler if not a great one. He was nine years old at his accession and grew into adulthood under the regency of competent ministers; he assumed personal rule around 1654 and held the throne until his death from illness in 1666.

The signature achievement of his reign was the recapture of **Kandahar** in 1649. Kandahar had been lost to the Mughals in 1638 (Mughal emperor Shah Jahan, taking advantage of the Safavid disarray under Safi). Abbas II launched a major campaign in 1648–1649, besieged the city, and captured it after defeating Shah Jahan's son **Aurangzeb** (then in his forties and serving as a Mughal general). Aurangzeb mounted three subsequent campaigns to retake Kandahar — in 1651, 1652, and 1653 — and failed in all three. Kandahar remained Safavid for the rest of the seventeenth century. It was the last major Safavid military success.

Abbas II also continued the Isfahan building program. The **Khwaju Bridge** was completed in 1650 — the second of the great Safavid bridges over the Zayanderud, with its decorative pavilions and dam-like undercroft (Chapter 4). The **Chehel Sotoun** (cheh-HEL soh-TOON, "Forty Columns") palace was completed in 1647, a low pavilion in a formal Persian garden, with a wooden veranda whose twenty columns reflect in the front pool to make the "forty columns" of the name. The interior of Chehel Sotoun is decorated with large mural paintings depicting Safavid royal scenes — Abbas I receiving the Mughal emperor Humayun, Tahmasp I hosting various dignitaries, Shah Ismail at war. The murals are major works of seventeenth-century Iranian painting and represent a deliberate attempt by Abbas II to commemorate his own dynasty in visual form.

But Abbas II's reign also saw the beginning of the underlying decay. The **ghulam army** that Abbas the Great had built was no longer being properly recruited, trained, or paid. The captured Caucasian boys who had filled the corps in earlier generations were no longer being supplied at the same rate, partly because the Caucasus was less under Safavid control and partly because the central state lacked the administrative reach to manage the recruitment system. The cannons and muskets that had won the battles of Sufiyan and Hormuz were not being maintained or replaced. The frontier garrisons on the eastern approaches were understaffed, underpaid, and increasingly relying on local Afghan and Baluch tribal levies for security — levies whose loyalty would prove dangerous.

Religious orthodoxy was also becoming more aggressive. **Muhammad Baqir Majlisi** (Chapter 5) began his public career under Abbas II, and the conservative legalistic Shia Islam he championed was steadily displacing the philosophical and Sufi traditions of earlier Safavid culture. Sufi orders — including some that had been associated with the Safavid family for generations — were persecuted. Religious minorities (Christians, Zoroastrians, Jews) faced new restrictions. The intellectual openness of Mulla Sadra's generation was giving way to a narrower, more legalistic religious culture.

Abbas II died in 1666, probably of syphilis (the surviving symptom descriptions are consistent with late-stage syphilitic illness), at the age of thirty-three. His death is the moment when the long decline becomes visible.

---

**Soleyman I** (soo-lay-MAHN, ruled 1666–1694), born **Sam Mirza**, was Abbas II's eldest son and one of the worst rulers in Safavid history. He took the throne at age twenty, was reportedly an alcoholic by his late twenties, and spent most of his twenty-eight-year reign in the harem rather than the council chamber.

The historian **Roger Savory** describes Soleyman's reign as a model of institutional drift: the Shah did almost nothing, the central court did almost nothing of substance, and the empire ran on autopilot, governed by provincial officials whose competence varied wildly and whose loyalty to a king they rarely saw was correspondingly thin. There were no major military campaigns. There were no significant administrative reforms. There were no important new buildings (the architectural patronage of Abbas II's reign essentially ended). Nothing dramatic broke. Nothing was repaired either.

The European travelers who visited Iran during Soleyman's reign — including the French jeweler **Jean Chardin** (zhahn shar-DAN), whose ten-volume account of his Iranian travels in 1672–1677 is one of the most detailed European descriptions of Safavid Iran — left vivid pictures of a state that was visibly decaying despite its surface magnificence. Chardin's *Voyages en Perse* (published 1686) is a primary source for everything from court ceremony to commercial practice to popular religion in late seventeenth-century Iran. His account is mostly admiring of Iranian culture, but he is unsparing about the political situation: the Shah was incompetent, the senior officials were corrupt, the army was undisciplined, and the system was clearly heading toward crisis. (Chardin was a French Huguenot Protestant who later moved to England to escape the persecution that followed Louis XIV's revocation of the Edict of Nantes; his Iranian travels were undertaken partly in connection with the diamond trade and partly out of intellectual curiosity. His writings are a major source for European understanding of the Islamic world for the next century.)

The structural problem was the **harem succession system**. Safavid princes were no longer trained for active rule — earlier dynasties had sent princes out to govern provinces as young men, building administrative experience, but Abbas the Great had ended this practice (out of fear of princely revolts) by confining all royal princes inside the Isfahan palace. The princes were raised in the harem, educated by tutors and court scholars but without practical political experience, and emerged at the moment of succession with no skills for rule. Each generation of Safavid kings was therefore worse-prepared than the previous one. By Soleyman's generation, the system was producing kings who could barely function as administrators.

The harem itself became the de facto government. Real political power flowed through the network of senior eunuchs, royal wives, and queen mothers who controlled access to the Shah and managed the daily operations of the court. This was not necessarily catastrophic in itself — well-organized harem bureaucracies had governed effectively in Ottoman, Mughal, and Chinese contexts — but in late Safavid Iran it produced a court that was insular, factional, and disconnected from the practical demands of ruling a large empire. The Shia clergy under Majlisi filled the resulting vacuum, claiming more religious authority and progressively narrower religious orthodoxy. The provincial governors, with little oversight from the central state, became increasingly autonomous and corrupt. The army continued to decay.

---

**Sultan Husayn** (sool-TAHN hoo-SAYN, ruled 1694–1722) was Soleyman's son and the last effective Safavid king — though "effective" is a generous term for what he managed. He was reportedly devout, indecisive, and dominated almost entirely by the Shia clerical establishment, particularly by Majlisi (until Majlisi's death in 1699) and by Majlisi's successors. The historian **Laurence Lockhart**, in his classic 1958 study *The Fall of the Safavi Dynasty*, treated Sultan Husayn as the consensus low point of the dynasty — a king who combined personal piety with utter inability to govern.

Sultan Husayn's twenty-eight-year reign saw an acceleration of the trends that had begun under Soleyman. The army continued to decay; the treasury continued to bleed; the provincial governors continued to grow more autonomous; the religious persecutions continued to intensify. The Shah himself was preoccupied with religious devotions and large-scale construction at Isfahan — including the magnificent **Madrasa-ye Chahar Bagh** (Four Gardens School, completed 1714), one of the great Safavid religious buildings, which still stands as one of the most beautiful pieces of late Safavid tilework. While Isfahan was being beautified, the empire was disintegrating.

The provinces grew more restive. Persecution of religious minorities — Sunni Muslims (especially in the Caucasus and Afghanistan, where Sunni populations were forced to convert or face heavy taxation), Christians, Zoroastrians, Jews — produced widespread regional resentment. The Sunni populations of the eastern provinces (modern Afghanistan and Baluchistan) were particularly bitter. The Safavid governor of Kandahar, **Gurgin Khan** (a Georgian Christian convert serving as a Safavid official — the irony of a Christian-born Iranian governor persecuting Sunni Muslims is hard to overstate), conducted brutal campaigns against the Pashtun **Ghilzai** (gil-ZIGH) tribes around Kandahar in the early 1700s. The Ghilzai, like all Pashtuns, were Sunni Muslims, and they bitterly resented both the religious persecution and the political domination by a Christian-born official.

In 1709, the Ghilzai chieftain **Mir Wais Hotak** (meer WIGH-ees hoh-TAHK) led a revolt that killed Gurgin Khan and seized control of Kandahar. The Safavid government attempted to suppress the revolt but failed — sending three successive expeditions over the following years, all of which were defeated by the Ghilzai. By the time Mir Wais died in 1715, the Ghilzai had established an independent Sunni emirate in Kandahar with no realistic prospect of Safavid reconquest.

This was the moment when the long decline became existential. The Safavid army that could not subdue a single Pashtun tribal confederation in Kandahar was clearly not the same army that had captured Baghdad in 1623. The state was no longer capable of basic military operations on its own frontier.

The Ghilzai threat metastasized over the next several years. In 1717, the **Abdali** tribal confederation (the other great Pashtun tribal grouping, rivals of the Ghilzai) seized Herat from Safavid control. By 1720, the eastern provinces of the empire were effectively independent. And in 1721, Mir Wais's son **Mir Mahmud Hotak** (meer mah-MOOD) — the new Ghilzai leader, then in his early twenties — decided to push the issue further: he assembled an army of perhaps 20,000 Pashtun warriors and marched on Isfahan itself.

The army that intercepted him was the Safavid imperial force, supposedly the elite of the empire. They met at **Gulnabad** (gul-nah-BAHD), east of Isfahan, on March 8, 1722.

The Safavid army outnumbered the Afghans by perhaps two-to-one and was equipped with cannons and muskets. It was also poorly trained, poorly led, and poorly motivated. The Afghan force was smaller but battle-hardened, well-led, and fighting with the desperation of men who had little to lose. The battle lasted a single day. The Safavid lines collapsed under Afghan cavalry charges, the imperial cannons were captured, and the army fled back to Isfahan in disarray. The road to the capital was open.

What followed was the worst urban catastrophe in Iranian history.

Cross-civ check: the long decline of the Safavid Empire (1629–1722) overlaps with the **English Civil War** (1642–1651), the **Glorious Revolution** (1688), the late **Tokugawa shogunate** in Japan (the Edo period of stable Tokugawa rule), the **Manchu conquest** of Ming China (1644 and after), the rise of the **Romanov dynasty** in Russia, and the **Mughal apex** under **Aurangzeb** (1658–1707, reigning over the most extensive Mughal Empire ever assembled — and beginning the long Mughal decline that would parallel the Safavid one). The seventeenth century was the moment when the early-modern Eurasian state system reached its peak, with several large gunpowder empires reaching their territorial and economic maxima at almost the same time. The Safavid case is one example among several of how those empires began to decay simultaneously, partly under the weight of their own institutional structures and partly under the new pressures (commercial, military, ideological) generated by European expansion.

# Chapter 8 — The Afghan Catastrophe and Nader's Restoration

The fall of Isfahan in 1722 was the most violent end any major Islamic capital experienced in the early-modern period. The Mughal capital at Delhi survived multiple sieges. Ottoman Constantinople was never sacked between 1453 and the twentieth century. Even Baghdad, sacked by the Mongols in 1258 (Mongol Empire Ch 5), recovered relatively quickly. But Isfahan in 1722 was reduced from one of the largest cities in the world to a ruin, and the Iran that emerged from the catastrophe was a fundamentally different country.

This chapter is the end of the Safavid story — the Afghan invasion, the brief Hotaki occupation, the rise of Nader Shah Afshar, the formal end of the dynasty in 1736 — and the beginning of the Safavid afterlife in modern Iran.

---

After the disaster at Gulnabad on March 8, 1722, **Mir Mahmud Hotak's** Afghan army marched on Isfahan and laid siege to the city. The siege lasted six and a half months — from March to October 1722 — and produced one of the great urban tragedies of early-modern history.

Isfahan had a population of perhaps half a million people in 1722, including the surrounding suburbs (the city had declined somewhat from its peak under Abbas I but was still one of the world's largest cities). The Safavid government, paralyzed by indecision, made no serious attempt to organize a relief expedition. The provincial armies of Iran — what remained of them — either failed to mobilize, were defeated piecemeal by Afghan detachments, or simply melted away. The city was on its own.

The Afghan force was not large enough to storm Isfahan's walls — the Hotakis had perhaps 25,000 troops and the city had elaborate fortifications. But they could starve the city into surrender, and they did. Mahmud's army cordoned off Isfahan, cut the food supply lines from the surrounding agricultural districts, intercepted the relief caravans, and waited.

What followed inside the city was a famine of medieval intensity. Within two months food prices had risen by orders of magnitude. By summer 1722, the urban population was eating dogs, cats, leather, tree bark, and grass. By autumn, there are detailed European accounts of cannibalism in Isfahan — the Polish Jesuit Father **Krusinski**, who was inside the besieged city, left a primary-source account of the horrors. Estimates of deaths from starvation during the siege range from 80,000 to 200,000 people. The exact number is unknowable, but the order of magnitude is reliable: approximately a third to a half of the urban population died inside the walls before the city surrendered.

On October 23, 1722, Sultan Husayn personally rode out of Isfahan to the Afghan camp, dismounted, removed his royal aigrette, and placed the Safavid crown on the head of Mir Mahmud Hotak. Husayn formally abdicated the throne. Mahmud entered Isfahan unopposed and began an occupation that would last seven years.

The Afghan occupation was a continuing disaster. Mahmud and his forces were small in number relative to the population they were trying to control, and they responded to ongoing resistance with progressive escalation of violence. In 1725, Mahmud — by then mentally ill, possibly from syphilis or from the strain of governing a foreign capital with inadequate resources — ordered the systematic massacre of much of the surviving Safavid royal family and Iranian nobility, executing perhaps 3,000 people in a single afternoon at the Isfahan palace. He died shortly afterward, possibly of natural causes, possibly assassinated by his cousin **Ashraf Hotak** (ash-RAHF) who succeeded him.

The Afghan occupation also failed to control most of the country. The Safavid governor of **Khorasan** (the northeastern province), **Tahmasp Mirza** — Sultan Husayn's son, who had escaped from Isfahan before the siege — proclaimed himself **Tahmasp II** in 1722 and began organizing resistance from the provinces. Russian forces under **Peter the Great** invaded the Caspian provinces in 1722–1723, capturing Baku, Derbent, and the southern Caspian coast. Ottoman forces invaded from the west, capturing most of Azerbaijan, Armenia, Georgia, and parts of western Iran. The two foreign powers signed the **Treaty of Constantinople** in 1724, dividing the conquered Iranian territories between themselves while leaving Afghan-occupied central Iran as a residual rump.

By 1725, the Iran of Abbas the Great a century earlier was simply gone. The Afghans held the center. The Russians held the north. The Ottomans held the west. Tahmasp II claimed legitimate Safavid kingship from the northeast but commanded almost no military force. The empire had effectively dissolved.

---

The man who reassembled it was **Nader Shah Afshar** (NAH-der), one of the most extraordinary military commanders in Iranian history.

Nader was born around 1688 in **Khorasan**, in the eastern Iranian province, into the Turkic **Afshar** tribal confederation — one of the original Qizilbash tribes that had backed Ismail I two centuries earlier (Chapter 1). His early life was difficult: his father died when he was young, he and his mother were kidnapped by Uzbek slavers in his teens (he later escaped), he served as a low-level tribal warrior, and by his early thirties he was leading a band of Afshar irregulars that operated as bandits, mercenaries, or both. He was illiterate, by all accounts brutal, and unmistakably a military genius.

In 1726, Tahmasp II — the would-be Safavid Shah running the resistance from Khorasan — recruited Nader into his service. Nader rapidly became Tahmasp's principal general, then his de facto regent, then the actual ruler of Iran while Tahmasp held the formal title. Over the next decade, Nader conducted one of the most successful military campaigns in early-modern Asian history.

The campaign was systematic. First Nader expelled the Afghans. He defeated the Hotaki forces under Ashraf at the **Battle of Damghan** in October 1729 and again at **Murche-Khort** in November 1729, broke their occupation of central Iran, and chased them back to Kandahar. By 1730, the Afghan emirate had been destroyed and Iran had a unified government again under Tahmasp II — with Nader as commander-in-chief.

Then Nader turned on the Ottomans. In a series of campaigns from 1730 to 1735, he recovered most of the territory the Ottomans had seized during the Afghan crisis: the Caucasus, Azerbaijan, western Iran, and parts of eastern Anatolia. The Ottoman-Safavid frontier was effectively restored to roughly the Treaty of Zuhab line of 1639. The Russians, seeing which way the wind was blowing, signed the **Treaty of Resht** (1732) and the **Treaty of Ganja** (1735) returning the Caspian provinces to Iranian control.

Then Nader turned on the Safavids. In 1732, he had already deposed Tahmasp II (whom he considered drunken and incompetent) and replaced him with Tahmasp's infant son **Abbas III** as a puppet king under Nader's regency. In March 1736, Nader convened a council of Iranian notables on the **Mughan plain** in the Caucasus, formally deposed Abbas III, and had himself proclaimed Shah. The Safavid dynasty, after 235 years, was over.

The new dynasty was the **Afsharid**, named for Nader's tribe. It would last only sixty years and produce only Nader himself plus a series of successors who fought each other inconclusively after his death. But the immediate importance was structural: Nader had restored Iranian sovereignty, expelled foreign occupiers, reunified the country under a single government, and ended the dynasty whose institutional decay had caused the catastrophe in the first place.

---

Nader Shah's later career was meteoric and increasingly bizarre.

In 1738–1739, he invaded Mughal India. The expedition is one of the strangest military adventures of the eighteenth century. Nader's army crossed the Hindu Kush mountains, captured Kabul and Lahore, defeated the Mughal emperor **Muhammad Shah** at the **Battle of Karnal** (February 1739), and entered Delhi as a conqueror. The Mughal capital — at that point the largest and richest city in the world, the seat of the empire that controlled most of the Indian subcontinent — was occupied by an Iranian army.

What happened next was a calculated atrocity. After a riot in Delhi during which some of Nader's soldiers were killed, Nader ordered a general massacre of the city's population. The killing lasted roughly six hours. Estimates of the death toll vary widely in the literature, with no figure resting on a firm primary count — most scholarly sources fall in the 20,000 to 30,000 range, while contemporary chronicles and some later accounts give much higher figures (the 100,000–200,000 numbers that appear in some popular sources are at the high end of what was claimed at the time rather than a modern scholarly consensus). Nader's army then systematically looted Delhi for fifty-seven days, packing up the wealth of the Mughal capital and shipping it back to Iran. The loot included the **Peacock Throne** of Shah Jahan (the great gold-and-jewel throne that had been the centerpiece of Mughal imperial ceremony for a century), the **Koh-i-Noor diamond** (then probably the largest diamond in the world, later seized from the Sikh kingdom by the British and now in the British Crown Jewels), thousands of horses and elephants, and so much cash that Nader was able to suspend taxation in Iran for three years.

The 1739 sack of Delhi is one of the most consequential events in modern South Asian history. It crippled the Mughal Empire (which would never fully recover and would spend the following century in slow disintegration), opened the way for British East India Company political expansion (which began in earnest after the Battle of Plassey in 1757, less than two decades after Nader's invasion), and effectively transferred the wealth of the Mughal core to Iran. It also demonstrated, beyond any doubt, that Nader was the most successful military commander in eighteenth-century Eurasia.

The wealth he brought back to Iran did not save him politically. Nader spent his last years (1740–1747) increasingly paranoid, increasingly cruel, and increasingly disconnected from the country he had liberated. He had his eldest son **Reza Quli** blinded (on suspicion of plotting against him). He executed senior officials on minor pretexts. He raised taxes again to fund campaigns in the Caucasus and Central Asia. By 1747, he was so unpopular that his own bodyguard officers conspired to assassinate him — they cut him down in his tent in June 1747 in northeastern Iran. The Afsharid dynasty fragmented within a generation. Nader's nephew **Adel Shah** held part of Iran briefly; his grandson **Shahrokh Shah** ruled a reduced kingdom in Khorasan until 1796; the rest of Iran was contested among rival warlords for the next forty years until the **Qajar dynasty** finally reunified the country in the 1790s.

Nader's career is a separate story that belongs more properly to the Afsharid TL than to the Safavid one. What matters for the Safavid narrative is that Nader expelled the Afghans, defeated the Russians and Ottomans, and ended the Safavid dynasty — but did not undo the Safavid project. The Twelver Shia state Ismail I had built in 1501, the institutional clerical hierarchy that Majlisi had codified in the 1680s, the architectural ensemble at Isfahan, the carpet workshops at Kashan and Kerman, the Armenian merchant network at New Julfa — all of this survived the catastrophe and continued under Nader and his successors.

Nader himself attempted, briefly, to undo the Shia revolution. In 1736 at his coronation council, he proposed a religious settlement under which Twelver Shia Islam would be reclassified as a fifth Sunni school of jurisprudence (the **Ja'fari** school, named for the Sixth Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq, who had codified much of the early Shia legal tradition). The proposal would have ended the Sunni-Shia confessional divide, normalized relations with the Ottomans, and effectively rolled back the Safavid revolution at the doctrinal level. The Iranian Shia clergy refused. Their refusal was the moment when the institutional independence of the Iranian Shia clerical class — the institutional creation of the Safavids — definitively asserted itself against any attempt by the state to dictate religious policy. Nader could not force his settlement on a clerical establishment that had outgrown the dynasty that created it. The Twelver Shia identity of Iran was now permanent, regardless of which dynasty held the throne.

This is the deepest meaning of the Safavid afterlife. The dynasty died. The religion stayed.

---

The Iran that emerged from the eighteenth-century chaos was, in most cultural terms, recognizably the country the Safavids had built.

The state religion was Twelver Shia Islam. The clerical hierarchy was the marja-i taqlid system that the Safavid-era Usuli scholars had begun to develop and that would be fully institutionalized under the Qajars. The major shrine cities — Mashhad, Qom, Ardabil — remained the geographic anchors of Iranian religious identity. The intellectual traditions of the Isfahan school continued at the seminaries. The carpet industry continued in the same workshops. The Persian-language literary tradition continued unbroken. The country was poorer, smaller, and politically unstable for several decades after Nader's death, but the cultural identity that the Safavids had constructed persisted.

This cultural identity has now persisted, with various political vicissitudes, for five centuries. The **Qajar dynasty** (1789–1925) ruled Iran on essentially Safavid cultural foundations, with the addition of significant European political influence. The **Pahlavi dynasty** (1925–1979) attempted to push Iran in a more secular and Westernized direction, but their failure — the 1979 Islamic Revolution — was largely a victory for the institutional Shia clergy that the Safavids had created. The **Islamic Republic of Iran** (1979–present) is, in institutional terms, the most direct heir of the Safavid state — a Twelver Shia confessional polity governed by a clerical hierarchy whose authority derives from the marja-i taqlid system, which derives from the seventeenth-century Usuli mujtahids, which derive from the Lebanese Arab clerics whom Ismail I imported in the early sixteenth century.

The thread running from Ismail's conquest of Tabriz in 1501 to the Islamic Republic of 2026 is roughly: imported Arab clerics → trained Iranian Shia clerical class → late Safavid Usuli mujtahids → Qajar-era marja-i taqlid system → Khomeini and the 1979 Revolution → the current clerical-political structure of Iran. The dynasty whose name appears at the head of this chain ceased to rule in 1736. The institutional structure they constructed has continued operating, in one form or another, for nearly three centuries after their formal extinction.

---

What survived from Safavid Iran into modern Iran is therefore extensive: the religion, the clerical hierarchy, the shrine networks, the architectural heritage, the carpet industry, the literary language, the educational seminaries, and a national-cultural identity that distinguishes Iranians from their Sunni Arab and Turkic neighbors. The Safavid contribution to the modern world is greater in proportion to what is recognized about them than any comparably-sized early-modern empire. Most readers of world history have heard of the Ottomans and the Mughals. Fewer have heard of the Safavids. But the Safavids' core innovation — the construction of a permanent Twelver Shia state with an institutionally independent clerical hierarchy — is arguably more consequential for the present-day world than anything either of the other two early-modern Islamic empires accomplished.

The Iranian thread that began with Cyrus the Great in 559 BCE (Persian Empire Ch 1) and ran through the Achaemenid, Parthian, Sasanian, and Islamic-era dynasties (Persian Empire Ch 1–10) reached its most distinctive modern form under the Safavids. The Persian habit of absorbing whatever conquered it (Persian Empire Ch 10 — the absorption of Greek learning by the Sasanians, the absorption of Islam by the post-conquest Persian-speaking Muslim cultural world, the absorption of the Mongol conquerors by the Ilkhanate, Mongol Empire Ch 7) reached a new variation under the Safavids: instead of absorbing a foreign religion, the Safavids took a minority strand of Islam and made it the unique national identity of Iran, distinguishing Iranians from every other Muslim people in the region. Twelver Shia Islam, which existed before the Safavids as a scholarly tradition centered in Iraq and Lebanon, became under the Safavids an Iranian state religion — Iranian in its geography, its language, its institutional structure, and its political function. The conversion ran the other direction from the absorptions of earlier Persian history: instead of absorbing the conqueror, the Safavids absorbed a particular religious tradition into a new national identity.

The dynasty died in 1736. The country it built lives on.

Cross-civ check: the Afghan catastrophe of 1722 and the rise of Nader Shah are contemporary with the **early Hanoverian period** in Britain, the **regency of the Duc d'Orléans** in France, the **late Kangxi and early Yongzheng emperors** of Qing China (the Qing was at the height of its power as the Safavids fell), and the **Mughal decline** under Muhammad Shah Rangila (whose capital Nader sacked in 1739). The eighteenth century in Eurasia is the moment of the **decline of the Islamic gunpowder empires** (Ottoman, Safavid/Afsharid, Mughal) and the simultaneous **rise of European naval-commercial power**, particularly the British East India Company. The Safavid collapse and the Iranian eighteenth-century chaos are part of this larger pattern: the early-modern Islamic world losing relative position to Western Europe in ways that would shape the political and economic balance of the next two centuries. Iran emerged from the chaos under the Qajars in 1796 as a much weaker state than the Safavid empire it had been, and the relative weakness vis-à-vis Britain and Russia would define Iranian political life for the entire nineteenth century. But the cultural identity the Safavids built — the Shia state, the clerical hierarchy, the literary and architectural tradition — survived all of this, and it survives still.

The Safavids were the dynasty that made Iran Iran. Everything else is commentary.

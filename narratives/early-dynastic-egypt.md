# Predynastic and Early Dynastic Egypt — Before and Becoming the Pharaohs

**Goal:** Narrative for the first TL in the Nile Valley chain. Covers roughly 5000–2686 BCE — from the cattle-herding peoples of the drying Sahara, through the long predynastic centuries when "Egyptian" culture was being assembled out of farming villages along the Nile, through the unification under Narmer, through the eight kings of the First Dynasty, through the Second Dynasty's near-collapse and its rescue by Khasekhemwy. Ends on the eve of the Step Pyramid — the moment Djoser and Imhotep are about to invent monumental stone architecture and launch the Old Kingdom. The whole period is ~2,300 years long, almost as long as the entire run of dynastic Egypt that follows it. This is the foundation everything else rests on, and almost nobody knows what's in it.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — The Green Sahara and the Refugees

The desert wasn't always a desert.

If you went to North Africa nine thousand years ago and walked west from the Nile Valley, you would not be walking into the Sahara as we know it. You would be walking into a savanna with shallow lakes, slow rivers, grass growing up to your knees, herds of cattle and gazelle, hippos in the water, crocodiles on the riverbanks, and stands of acacia trees thick enough to throw shade. There were people living out there. They herded long-horned cattle, fished the lakes with bone harpoons, hunted wild game, and painted the rocks of their shelters with images of swimming humans, dancing figures, and animals they would later watch die out as the water disappeared.

This is not a thing most people know about, so it is worth saying clearly up front. The Sahara — the largest hot desert on Earth, the brown blank space that fills North Africa on every modern map — was, for most of the time period this book covers up to about 5000 BCE, a green and habitable landscape. Geologists call this period the **African Humid Period**, or sometimes the **Green Sahara** (terms that are used interchangeably to describe the same wet phase). It ran from roughly 9000 BCE to 5000 BCE, give or take a few centuries on either end depending on the region, and it ended with one of the most dramatic environmental collapses in human history. The desert came back, slowly at first and then catastrophically, and the people and animals who had been living in the Green Sahara had to get out — or die.

Most of them ended up beside the Nile.

This is where Egypt comes from. The pharaohs and the pyramids are not the beginning of the story. They are the *end* of a much longer story, in which the survivors of a drying continent crowded into one of the few places that still had reliable water and started building the civilization the rest of the world would eventually call ancient Egypt. Before there were pharaohs there were farmers. Before there were farmers there were refugees. Before there were refugees there was a green Sahara that nobody who could read or write was around to remember.

We are going to start with that Sahara, because without it nothing else makes sense.

---

The reason the Sahara was green is the same reason it is brown today: monsoons.

A **monsoon** is a seasonal wind that reverses direction with the changing of the year (the word comes from an Arabic word for "season"). In the modern world, the most famous monsoon is the South Asian one — every summer, warm air rising off the heated Indian subcontinent pulls in moisture-laden winds from the Indian Ocean, and those winds dump rain on India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh from June through September before reversing in winter. Africa has its own monsoon system, called the **West African Monsoon**, which works the same way: in summer, hot air rising off the Sahara pulls in moist air from the Atlantic and the Gulf of Guinea, and that moist air dumps rain on the southern edge of the desert before it runs out of water. Today, that rainfall mostly stays well south of the Sahara proper, watering the Sahel (the semi-arid grass belt south of the desert) and stopping there. But during the African Humid Period, the monsoon was much stronger — the rain belt pushed hundreds of miles further north, and the heart of what is now the Sahara got enough summer rain to fill lakes and grow grass.

Why was the monsoon stronger? The shortest answer is that Earth's orbit was wobbling.

Over very long timescales, the way Earth tilts and orbits the Sun changes in slow regular cycles. About 11,000 years ago, the tilt of Earth's axis and the timing of its closest approach to the Sun were both in configurations that made Northern Hemisphere summers significantly warmer than they are today. Hotter summers in North Africa meant a stronger pressure differential between the heated land and the cooler ocean, which meant a more powerful monsoon, which meant rain in the desert. The whole system was driven by tiny, glacially slow shifts in planetary geometry. As those shifts continued — as the orbital configuration drifted away from the optimum — the monsoon weakened. The rain belt slid southward. The lakes evaporated. The grasses died. The desert came back.

The collapse, when it finally arrived, did not happen smoothly. For most of the African Humid Period, the Sahara was getting drier in slow incremental steps that nobody alive at the time would have noticed within a single lifetime. But around 5500 BCE, the system seems to have crossed a tipping point — feedback loops kicked in (less vegetation meant less local moisture meant less rain meant less vegetation), and over the following few centuries the Green Sahara died off in a series of relatively rapid steps. By 5000 BCE the central Sahara was already arid enough to be dangerous. By 4000 BCE it was the desert we recognize on a modern map. By 3000 BCE — when the first pharaoh sat down on the throne of a unified Egypt — the central Sahara was as empty and as dry as it is now.

Imagine being a cattle herder in this story. Your father's father's father was a cattle herder in the same valley you grew up in. The waterhole has been getting smaller every year for as long as anyone can remember. The grass is patchier. The herd is thinner. Some years you can still graze in the old spots; some years you have to drive the cattle further than you ever did before. Everyone in the village knows it's getting worse. Some of the families have already left, walking east toward the rumor of a great river that nobody has ever seen but that is supposed to flood every summer no matter what the rains do. Some of the families are staying because their grandmother is buried under that rock and they're not going to leave her. Eventually the waterhole goes dry for a whole year and everybody who's still there has to make a choice.

This is the human face of climate collapse, and it played out over two thousand years across an area larger than the United States. Most of the people who made the long walk east are anonymous. Most of the herds that walked with them are anonymous. But they walked.

---

The clearest evidence we have that this happened — and that the people who made the walk brought a coherent culture with them when they did — comes from a place called **Nabta Playa**, in the desert of southern Egypt about 60 miles (100 km) west of the Nile.

If you visit Nabta Playa today, you will find a flat dusty pan in the middle of an absolutely empty expanse of sand and rock. There is nothing growing for as far as the eye can see. The nearest road is two hours away. The nearest town is half a day's drive. Standing in the middle of the Nabta Playa basin you cannot really conceive of why anyone would have come here, much less why anyone would have built anything here.

Six thousand years ago, this place was a lake.

The "playa" in the name is a geographer's word for a lake bed that fills only seasonally — during the African Humid Period, the Nabta basin filled up every summer when the monsoon rains came, and dried out every winter when the rains stopped. Cattle-herding people moved into the basin during the wet season to graze their herds on the temporary pastureland the lake supported, and moved out again when the lake disappeared. They did this for centuries. And around 4800 BCE, they did something extraordinary.

They built a calendar.

In the middle of what had been the dry lake bed, a group of these herders raised a ring of standing stones — flat slabs of sandstone wedged upright in the ground, set in a rough circle about 13 feet (4 meters) across. The stones are not large by Stonehenge standards (Stonehenge wouldn't be built for another 1,800 years) but they are deliberately arranged. Two pairs of stones inside the circle define sight lines. One sight line points to the rising sun on the summer solstice — the longest day of the year, which in southern Egypt is exactly when the monsoon was about to break and fill the lake. Another sight line aligns with the rising of certain bright stars at specific points in the year. The whole arrangement is a working astronomical observatory, used by people who needed to know, with reasonable precision, when the rains were coming.

Nabta Playa is the oldest known astronomical site in Africa. It predates Stonehenge by roughly two thousand years. It is approximately contemporary with the very earliest Mesopotamian temple platforms in southern Iraq, where the first cities of the Sumerian world were also just beginning to come into focus (Mesopotamia Ch 1). The fact that astronomy this sophisticated was being practiced in the Sahara before any pyramid was built, before any pharaoh existed, before any hieroglyph was carved — by cattle herders, in what was already turning into a desert — is one of the most under-appreciated facts about the deep past of African civilization.

Around the stone circle, archaeologists have found something else: pits with whole cattle interred under stone mounds. These are not animal corpses thrown into the trash. They are buried with care, with the kind of attention reserved for something sacred. The cattle were not eaten. They were sacrificed. The Nabta Playa people had a cattle cult — a religion in which the cow was a being with spiritual significance, important enough to be buried like a person under a stone tumulus.

This matters because Egyptian religion, three thousand years later, is full of cows. The goddess **Hathor** (HATH-or) is depicted with a cow's head, or as a woman with cow's ears and horns, and is one of the central deities of the pharaonic pantheon — patron of love, motherhood, music, drunkenness, foreign lands, and the western horizon where the sun sets. The bull god **Apis** (AY-pis) was worshipped at Memphis as the living embodiment of a divine cow-spirit, and a real living bull selected for specific markings was kept in a temple, fed the best food, paraded through the city on festival days, and mummified at death. The sky goddess **Nut** (noot) was sometimes pictured as a cow standing over the Earth. Cattle horns appear on the headdresses of queens. Cattle counts were the basic unit of Egyptian taxation through the Old Kingdom (we will meet this practice again in Chapter 7). The cow was, throughout Egyptian history, the single most religiously charged animal in the entire pantheon.

Nabta Playa is where that obsession comes from. The pharaohs did not invent their cow goddess in the Nile Valley. They imported her from cattle herders fleeing the encroaching dunes. The cattle cult walked east across the drying Sahara with the people who practiced it, and when those people landed beside the Nile and started farming, they brought the cows and the religion together.

---

So what did the Nile look like when the refugees arrived?

The Nile is the longest river in the world, depending on how you measure it, and it has the additional unusual property of running south-to-north for almost its entire length. (This is unintuitive enough that it is worth explaining: the headwaters of the Nile are in equatorial central Africa, in the highlands south of the Sahara, and the river flows downhill from those highlands all the way north to the Mediterranean Sea. Most people imagining a river in their head picture water flowing south, because most rivers in Europe and North America happen to do so. The Nile flows the other way. "Up" the Nile means south, toward the source; "down" the Nile means north, toward the sea. Egyptians used the river's direction as their cardinal compass — *south* was upstream and *north* was downstream, baked into the language, with the result that early pharaohs depicted on monuments are sometimes shown wearing the crown of "Upper" Egypt — meaning *southern* Egypt, the part nearer the headwaters.)

For the predynastic period, the only part of the Nile that matters is the last 700 miles or so, from where the river crosses out of what is now Sudan through the **First Cataract** at Aswan and then runs north through Egypt to the Mediterranean. (A **cataract** is a stretch of rapids and rocky channel where the river runs over hard bedrock — not a single waterfall but a long unnavigable obstacle that blocks boat traffic. There are six numbered cataracts on the Nile, all of them south of Egypt proper. The First Cataract at Aswan was Egypt's southern frontier for most of dynastic history. Everything south of it was Nubia, the subject of the Ancient Nubia book in this series.)

North of Aswan, the Nile runs through a narrow valley about 10 to 15 miles (16 to 24 km) wide, walled in on both sides by desert cliffs. The valley is green for the same reason it is green today: every summer, monsoon rains over the Ethiopian highlands swell the Blue Nile, which dumps a torrent of muddy water into the main Nile far upstream, and that torrent reaches Egypt as the **annual inundation** — the famous yearly flood that, until the building of the Aswan High Dam in the 1960s, used to coat the entire Egyptian floodplain in a fresh layer of fertile silt every September. That silt is what made Egyptian agriculture possible. It was free natural fertilizer, deposited every year without fail, in volumes large enough to keep the soil productive across forty centuries of intensive farming. The annual inundation is the central fact of Egyptian existence; everything else flows from it.

Around 5500 BCE, as the Sahara was crossing its tipping point, the Nile floodplain was already starting to fill up with people who had walked east. They were not sophisticated farmers yet. They were a mixture of refugee herders from the dying west, indigenous Nile fisher-foragers, and small bands moving down the river from south, north, and along the Mediterranean coast from the Levant (the eastern Mediterranean coastal region — modern Israel, Palestine, Lebanon, and coastal Syria — which was the Egyptian word for what we would now call the Middle East). All of them were converging on the same narrow strip of fertile land, and they were bringing with them, between them, the components of a complete agricultural package: domesticated wheat and barley from the Levant, domesticated sheep and goats from the same region, domesticated cattle from the Sahara, fishing technologies from the Nile itself. The Nile Valley in 5500 BCE was a melting pot in the most literal sense — different cultural traditions colliding inside a tight green ribbon and being forced to deal with each other.

Out of that collision, the first identifiable Egyptian farming villages took shape.

---

The earliest one we can put a name on is called the **Fayum A culture**, after the modern district where it was found.

The **Fayum** (fy-OOM) is a depression in the desert about 50 miles (80 km) southwest of modern Cairo, where a side branch of the Nile feeds into a natural basin and creates a permanent lake — not the same kind of seasonal playa as Nabta but a real standing body of water, surrounded by green farmland even today. In the predynastic period the lake was much larger than it is now, and its shores were attractive to settlers because you could fish the lake, hunt the marshes, and farm the strip of land between the water and the desert all at the same time. Around 5200 BCE, a community of these settlers started doing something nobody in Egypt had done before: they planted crops and stored the harvest.

The crops were emmer wheat and barley.

**Emmer wheat** is an old strain of wheat, the immediate ancestor of the modern bread wheats, that produces a hard kernel inside a tough husk. It was first domesticated in southeastern Turkey and northern Syria around 9000 BCE — that is to say, about 4,000 years before it shows up in the Fayum — and from there it spread west across the Mediterranean and east into Iran, Pakistan, and eventually the Indus Valley, where it would become the staple of another nascent civilization (Indus Valley Ch 1). When emmer reached Egypt, it found a climate that suited it perfectly: planted just after the autumn flood waters receded, ripening in the cool dry winter, harvested before the heat of summer set in. **Barley** is a cousin of wheat, even more drought-tolerant, and it was domesticated in the same region at the same time. Both crops moved together as a package across the prehistoric world. Both arrived in Egypt as cultural imports rather than independent inventions.

The Fayum A people did not just grow these crops. They did something slightly more impressive: they figured out how to store them.

If you want to be a settled farmer rather than a wandering forager, you have to solve the problem of what to do with a year's worth of grain between harvests. Grain is heavy, perishable, and attractive to mice, weevils, and thieves. The Fayum A solution was to dig storage pits — straight-sided holes in the ground, lined with woven basketwork or matting, sometimes capped with a flat stone — and bury the grain inside. Over a hundred such pits have been found at the major Fayum A sites, some still containing carbonized grain that survived the millennia because the pits got sealed and forgotten. The grain pits are the technological signature of the Fayum A culture and the moment Egypt crossed the line from foraging to settled agriculture. You cannot dig a hundred grain pits if you are still moving with the seasons. You have to commit to a place.

Around the same time, similar communities were taking shape further north in the Nile Delta. **Merimde Beni-Salame** (meh-RIM-deh ben-ee sa-LAH-meh, usually shortened to just Merimde) is the most thoroughly excavated of these — a sprawling settlement on the western edge of the Delta where, by about 4800 BCE, several hundred people were living in oval mud huts, growing wheat and barley, herding sheep and goats and a few cattle, and burying their dead inside the village in shallow graves. Merimde is unusual among early Egyptian sites in that the houses are densely packed, suggesting something closer to a real village than a scatter of family homesteads. Further south on the eastern Delta edge, a culture called **El-Omari** (al-oh-MAR-ee) was doing something similar: oval houses, grain pits, sheep, goats, the standard Neolithic farming kit.

(One word that is going to come up constantly in this book: **Neolithic** means "new stone" in Greek and refers to the period of human prehistory after agriculture and animal domestication had been invented but before metalworking became widespread. A "Neolithic" village is a stone-tool farming village. The end of the Neolithic in any given region is roughly when copperworking starts, which in Egypt happens around 4000 BCE and is the topic of the next chapter.)

The picture you should hold in your head for this earliest phase of Egyptian farming, around 5000 BCE, is something like this. The Sahara to the west is dying and emptying out. The Nile Valley is filling up with refugees from that collapse plus indigenous fisher-foragers plus small farming communities trickling in from the Levant. Different groups are settling different reaches of the river, each with their own pottery and burial customs. They are growing imported wheat and barley alongside their fishing and hunting. They are running cattle that they brought with them from the dying Sahara. They have a religion built around those cattle that traces back to the Nabta Playa stone circle and beyond. They are not yet anything we would recognize as "Egyptian" — there is no Egyptian state, no Egyptian writing, no Egyptian art style, no shared identity that connects the Fayum villager to the Merimde villager to the El-Omari villager to the cattle herder camped at a dry waterhole somewhere in the Western Desert. But the ingredients of all of those things are now present, in the same ribbon of land, at the same time, for the first time in history.

The mixing bowl is full. It is going to take another two thousand years to start producing a recognizable Egyptian civilization out of it. But the bowl is full.

---

It is worth zooming out for a moment to remember that none of this is happening in isolation.

At the same time the Fayum A farmers are digging their grain pits, the Sumerian world in southern Iraq is just beginning to coalesce into the first identifiable cultures of the Mesopotamian alluvium — the **Hassuna** and **Samarra** and **Halaf** cultures of northern Mesopotamia, and a little later the **Ubaid** culture of the south, the immediate ancestors of Sumerian civilization (Mesopotamia Ch 1). They are growing the same emmer wheat and the same barley, herding the same sheep and goats, building the same kinds of mudbrick villages. They are not Egyptian and they are not in contact with Egypt yet — there is no evidence of direct exchange between the early Nile farmers and the early Mesopotamian farmers in this period, and the two cultures were developing in parallel rather than in conversation. But they were drawing from the same Neolithic toolkit, which had spread west and south out of the original domestication zone in southeastern Turkey, and they were going to end up in the same general place — sedentary villages, social hierarchy, religion, craft specialization, the road to cities — at roughly the same time.

Further east, on the western edge of the Indus floodplain, a culture called **Mehrgarh** (MAIR-gar) had been farming the same wheat and barley package since about 7000 BCE and was now experimenting with cotton, copper, and dental drilling. They were the seed culture from which the Indus Valley civilization would eventually grow (Indus Valley Ch 1).

In the Yellow River valley of northern China, completely independently, a different Neolithic tradition was domesticating millet, raising pigs, and building villages of semi-subterranean houses (Ancient China Ch 1). They were on a separate track and would stay on it for thousands of years before the silk roads reconnected them with the western half of Eurasia.

Five regions of the world were running the same experiment in parallel: take a fertile river valley, add domesticated crops, add a few centuries of settled village life, and see what happens. In every one of those five regions, the answer turned out to be the same. Settled villages produce surplus. Surplus produces specialists. Specialists produce hierarchy. Hierarchy produces religion-backed elites, and elites build monuments. The first monuments produce the first cities, and the first cities produce the first kings.

But that arc takes time. In Egypt around 5000 BCE, none of that has happened yet. There are villages and grain pits and a memory of cattle in the Sahara, and that is almost all there is.

The next chapter is about what happens when those villages start producing something that is recognizably *Egyptian* — a distinctive material culture, distinctive burials, distinctive pottery, the first hints of social inequality, and the first artisans whose work survives well enough to be recognized across the millennia. That story starts on the east bank of the Nile, in a place called El-Badari, around 4400 BCE.

# Chapter 2 — Badarians and the Birth of an Egyptian Look

If you handed an archaeologist a single pottery sherd from anywhere along the Nile and asked them to tell you the moment "Egyptian" culture began — the first thing on the ground that you could pick up and recognize as belonging to the same tradition that would eventually build the pyramids — they would, almost without hesitation, point to the Badarian.

The Badarian was the first culture along the Nile that produced a material signature distinctive enough, refined enough, and widely shared enough that you can call it a coherent cultural tradition rather than just a regional grouping of villages. It runs from roughly 4400 to 3800 BCE — about six hundred years — and it is named for **El-Badari** (el-bah-DAR-ee), the modern Egyptian district where its first cemeteries were excavated by the British archaeologist Guy Brunton in the 1920s. Badari is on the east bank of the Nile in middle Egypt, about 200 miles (320 km) south of modern Cairo, and it sits in a stretch of the river where the floodplain widens out enough to support real farming villages with real surplus. The original Badarian sites were a string of cemeteries cut into a desert escarpment overlooking the cultivated land — graves dug into the dry sand at the edge of the green strip, where the bodies and the grave goods would survive intact for the next six thousand years.

What makes the Badarian recognizable is not any one thing. It is a package — pottery plus burials plus craft plus subsistence plus religion — that hangs together as a coherent way of being a Nile farmer, and the package shows up across enough of the Nile Valley, from the Faiyum down to as far south as the future Nubian border, that it is clearly not a single village's local style. It is a whole cultural region thinking in the same idiom.

The most famous element of that package is the pottery.

---

The signature Badarian pot is a small bowl or beaker, hand-built without a wheel, polished to a high shine, and fired in a way that turns the bottom of the pot a deep glossy red and the rim of the pot a deep matte black.

This sounds like a strange combination, and it took me a while to understand the trick. Here is how it works. **Pottery** is fired clay, and the color of the finished pot depends almost entirely on how much oxygen is present during the firing. If oxygen is plentiful — what potters call an **oxidizing** atmosphere — the iron in the clay turns red, and you get red pots. If oxygen is scarce — what potters call a **reducing** atmosphere — the iron stays in a different chemical state, and you get gray or black pots. Modern potters control this with the airflow on a kiln. Badarian potters did not have kilns. They fired their pots in shallow open pits, in a fuel of dung or twigs, and they figured out a clever workaround.

They stood the pots upside down, rim-first, in the firing pit. The bottom of the pot stuck up out of the fuel into the open air, where it got plenty of oxygen and turned a glossy red. The rim of the pot was buried deep in the fuel and the ash, where it was deprived of oxygen and turned a deep black. The transition between the two zones, at the height of the ash line, was sharp. The result was the famous **black-topped red ware**: a single pot, made from a single batch of clay, with a brilliant red lower body and a jet-black upper rim, the boundary between the two colors razor-clean.

It is technically beautiful. It also seems to have been religiously meaningful — though we have to be honest that we can't read the minds of people who didn't write things down, and any guess about what a Badarian pot meant to its maker is a guess. The leading theory, based on the fact that black-topped pots are concentrated in graves and not in everyday domestic contexts, is that the red-and-black scheme had something to do with the duality between the fertile floodplain (Egyptian "kemet," literally "the black land," referring to the dark silt deposited by the inundation) and the surrounding desert (Egyptian "deshret," "the red land," referring to the rust-colored sand and rock outside the floodplain). Black equals life, life equals the river, the river equals the Nile, and the Nile is at the bottom — so the upside-down pot in the firing pit was, in a sense, the cosmos in miniature, with the black band of life floating above the red expanse of the desert. This is a guess. But the same red-black duality runs all the way through later pharaonic art and language, and the pots are the earliest place we see it.

Even if the meaning is uncertain, the craft is not. Badarian black-topped ware is technically excellent. The walls are thin (some pieces are barely a few millimeters thick), the surfaces are burnished to a glassy shine, and the firing is consistently controlled. (To **burnish** a pot is to polish its surface, before firing, by rubbing it with a smooth pebble until the clay particles align and reflect light. Burnishing is slow, repetitive, manual work, and it produces a finish that no glaze can quite imitate.) Some pieces have a subtle rippled texture along the body — fingertip ridges left by the burnishing tool, deliberately preserved as decoration — and these "rippled ware" pieces are the highest-status pottery of the period, found mainly in the richest graves.

For comparison: at this same moment in southern Mesopotamia, the **Ubaid culture** was making coarser, plainer pottery with painted decoration, fired in early kilns at lower technical sophistication (Mesopotamia Ch 1). Mesopotamian pottery would catch up and eventually surpass Egyptian craft once the potter's wheel was invented around 3500 BCE, but in the early 5th millennium, the people on the Nile were the better potters in the Bronze Age world. They were also already producing pottery that was beautiful enough to bury, which tells you something about the value system.

---

The second great Badarian innovation is the cemetery itself.

A **cemetery** in the technical sense is a dedicated burial ground, separate from the living settlement, organized as a deliberate spatial arrangement of graves. This is not how all early human societies dealt with their dead. Many Neolithic peoples buried their relatives under the floors of the houses they had lived in, or in the corner of the yard, or in trash middens, or in caves that were also used for other purposes. The decision to dedicate a piece of ground exclusively to the dead, away from the living village, is a real cultural choice — and it implies several things. It implies that you expect to keep returning to the same spot. It implies that the relationship between the dead and the land is important enough to mark in space. It implies, eventually, that you can tell who is buried near whom and that those spatial relationships carry social meaning.

Badarian people made that choice. They buried their dead in shared cemeteries cut into the desert sand at the edge of the cultivated strip — close to the village, but on the other side of the boundary between the farmed black land and the unfarmed red land. The dead were laid on their left sides, knees drawn up to the chest in a fetal position, head pointing south, face pointing west. The orientation matters: by the time of the historical pharaohs, west would be the direction of the setting sun and the realm of the dead, and tombs would face west for that reason. The Badarian dead were already facing west three thousand years before any pharaoh. The convention had been settled before there was anyone alive who could explain why.

Most graves were simple oval pits, dug just deep enough to hold the body, sometimes lined with reed mats or animal hides. The body was usually wrapped in linen or the skin of a gazelle or goat, and the grave was filled with goods for the afterlife: pottery, food offerings, jewelry, cosmetic palettes, ivory combs, and sometimes small clay or ivory figurines.

It is the figurines that get archaeologists excited.

A **figurine** is a small handmade sculpture of a person or animal, usually under a foot in height. Badarian figurines are mostly women — stylized standing female figures, sometimes nude, sometimes with exaggerated hips or breasts, often with their arms folded across their chests or held at their sides. The faces are abstracted; the bodies are not. They are clearly ritual objects of some kind, but we don't know exactly what kind. The most popular guess is that they are fertility figures, related to childbirth or the fertility of the fields, but it is also possible that they represent specific dead women, or goddesses, or ancestors, or all three at once. They are made of unfired clay or carved ivory or polished stone. They appear in graves of women and men alike. They are the earliest sculpture from the Nile Valley that you can hold in your hand and recognize as religious art.

Alongside the figurines, Badarian graves often contain **cosmetic palettes**: small flat slabs of stone, usually green schist or siltstone, with a shallow depression worn into one face. These were used for grinding mineral pigments — green malachite (a copper ore that produces a brilliant green powder), black galena (a lead ore that produces black), and red ochre (an iron-rich earth pigment) — into the powdery cosmetics that Egyptians of every era wore around their eyes. The palette plus the pigment equals the eye paint, and the eye paint had practical and ritual functions both. Practically, the dark pigment around the eyes reduced glare from the desert sun and may have had antibacterial properties that protected against the chronic eye infections endemic to a culture that lived in dust. Ritually, the painted eye was associated, eventually, with the protection of the gods — the **Eye of Horus** in particular would become one of the most powerful protective symbols in Egyptian religion, appearing on amulets, sarcophagi, and the prows of ships for the next four thousand years. The Badarian palettes are the very earliest direct evidence we have of this practice. They are also, in their later evolved form, going to become the ceremonial palettes of the unification era — the same flat-stone-with-a-grinding-depression format, scaled up and elaborately carved, that the Narmer Palette will eventually be (Chapter 5).

Some Badarian graves are noticeably richer than others. They contain more pottery, more figurines, more imported goods, more elaborate burial wrappings, more space. A few outlier graves contain so much more that the gap between them and the average grave is clearly meaningful. **Social hierarchy** — the existence of haves and have-nots within a single community — is showing up in the archaeological record for the first time on the Nile, and it is showing up the way it almost always does: in the cemetery, where grave wealth is the most easily preserved record of what your village thought you were worth.

---

The third Badarian innovation, after pottery and cemeteries, is metal.

The Badarians worked **copper**. Not very much of it, and not very skillfully by later standards, but enough to count. Copper objects from Badarian graves are mostly small — beads, pins, rings, the occasional small chisel or awl — and they are mostly **cold-hammered**, which means the copper was beaten into shape with stone hammers without being melted first. Cold-hammering works for native copper (copper that occurs naturally in metallic form, as opposed to bound up in copper ores) because pure copper is soft enough to be reshaped under pressure. You take a nugget, beat it flat, fold it, beat it flat again, and gradually it turns into a sheet or a bead or a wire. The downside of cold-hammering is that it work-hardens the metal — the more you hammer it, the more brittle it gets, until eventually it cracks. The Badarians solved this with **annealing**: heating the work-hardened copper in a fire until it became soft again, then continuing to hammer. This is not yet smelting (extracting metal from ore by chemical reduction), and it is not yet casting (melting metal and pouring it into a mold). It is the first step on a technological road that, three thousand years later, will produce cast bronze statues of the gods at temple shrines and copper saws long enough to cut blocks for the Great Pyramid.

The copper itself probably came from **Sinai** — the desert peninsula east of the Nile delta, between Egypt and the Levant — where copper-bearing ores outcrop in several places along the western escarpment. By the dynastic period, Egyptian state expeditions to Sinai for copper and turquoise would be a regular institution, organized at royal expense, and we will meet them in Chapter 6. In the Badarian period, the trade was probably much more informal — small parties of travelers picking up nuggets of native copper from surface deposits and carrying them back to villages along the river. But the link was already established. From the very beginning, Egypt's metal supply ran east to the Sinai mines and back.

Copper is the start of the long Egyptian fascination with metal-as-status. By the late Badarian, copper objects in graves are clearly markers of wealth — the people buried with copper beads were not the same people buried without them. The copper trade was not creating commodities; it was creating elites.

---

The fourth Badarian innovation is linen.

**Linen** is cloth woven from the long fibers of the **flax** plant, a slim blue-flowered annual that grows well in cool wet conditions and was already being cultivated in the Levant by the time emmer wheat reached Egypt. Flax produces edible seeds (what we now call linseed) and stems full of long strong fibers that can be soaked, separated, spun into thread, and woven into cloth. Linen has several useful properties: it absorbs moisture and dries fast, it gets stronger when wet (where cotton gets weaker), it is naturally cool against the skin in hot climates, and it can be woven so finely that the finished cloth is almost translucent. For a culture living in the Egyptian summer, it is the perfect textile.

The Badarians wove linen at a level of skill that is, frankly, hard to believe for a culture this early. Linen scraps recovered from Badarian graves include fine plain weaves, twills, fringed edges, and pieces with deliberate decorative patterns woven into the fabric. The thread is consistent, the weave is even, and the finished textiles were clearly produced by experienced artisans working with proper looms. The earliest **horizontal ground looms** — flat looms staked into the ground, with the warp threads stretched between two beams — appear in Egypt around this time, and Badarian linen was almost certainly woven on them. These are the same kind of looms shown in tomb paintings of weavers thousands of years later. The technology, like so much else, was settled before history starts.

The Badarian dead were wrapped in linen. The Badarian elite were wrapped in *good* linen. By the time of the dynastic kings, the highest-status burials in Egypt would involve hundreds of yards of finely woven linen wound around the body — the basis of mummification, although true mummification with deliberate dehydration was still a thousand years in the future. The wrapping practice is older than the embalming. The Badarians started it.

---

So picture a Badarian village around 4000 BCE.

It sits on the edge of the Nile floodplain, at the boundary between the dark fertile silt and the dry red desert. It is a cluster of round or oval mudbrick houses, maybe a few dozen of them, with reed-thatch roofs and dirt floors. There is a granary or two, sheltered from the rain (such rain as there is) under a thatch awning. Cattle and sheep and goats are kept in pens at the edge of the village, and they are driven out every morning to graze the floodplain after the harvest. There are dogs around. There are children playing in the dust.

Most days, most of the adults are working the fields — sowing emmer and barley after the autumn flood pulls back, harvesting in the spring, threshing on a flat patch of beaten earth, storing the grain in the village pits, fishing the river when the work eases off. Some of the adults are doing other things. There is at least one potter, because the pottery is consistent enough to be the work of specialists. There is at least one weaver. There is probably someone who cold-hammers copper. There is at least one person who carves stone palettes. There is someone who makes the figurines. None of these people are full-time specialists in the modern sense — they all also farm or fish — but they are recognized as the village's experts, and the things they make get traded along the river to other villages that lack their particular skill.

When somebody dies, the body is washed, wrapped in linen, painted with cosmetics, and laid in a pit at the cemetery on the desert edge. Family members put offerings in the grave: a few favorite pots, a comb, a palette, sometimes a figurine, sometimes a bead necklace, sometimes (if the family is rich) a small copper ring or pin. The grave is filled in. The mourners walk back to the village. Life resumes.

Multiply that village by hundreds, scattered up and down the Nile from the Delta to the Nubian border, and you have the Badarian world.

---

Lower Egypt — the Delta and the area immediately south of it, the part of the river where it widens out into a fan of distributary channels before reaching the Mediterranean — was on a slightly different track in this period. The most important Lower Egyptian site is **Maadi** (MAH-dee), a settlement on the eastern edge of the future site of Cairo, occupied from about 3900 to 3500 BCE. Maadi has a different cultural signature from contemporary Upper Egyptian sites: the houses are different (some are partly subterranean, dug down into the ground), the burials are different (less elaborate, with fewer grave goods), the pottery is different, and the trade contacts are different. Maadi has imported Levantine pottery in quantities that Badarian sites do not, and it has copper that came from the same Sinai sources but reached Maadi by overland routes rather than by river.

The Maadi people were not Badarians. They were a parallel culture in Lower Egypt, in regular contact with the Levant via Sinai, somewhat less stratified than their Upper Egyptian contemporaries, and culturally distinct. We will see this north-south split repeatedly in the coming chapters. Lower Egypt and Upper Egypt were on different developmental paths through the entire predynastic period, and the eventual unification of the two halves under a single king will be hard-fought and hard-won precisely because the two halves had been doing things differently for centuries.

Around 3800 BCE, the Badarian phase shades into the next phase of Upper Egyptian culture, which archaeologists call **Naqada I** or the **Amratian**. The transition is gradual — Badarian pottery doesn't disappear so much as evolve into Naqada I pottery, the same villages keep being occupied, the same craft traditions continue. But something is changing in Upper Egypt during the late Badarian and early Naqada I that will accelerate dramatically over the next few centuries, and it is worth naming the change explicitly because it is the engine that drives the rest of this story.

The change is **scale**. Upper Egyptian villages start getting bigger. The richest graves start getting much, much richer than the poorest. A few specific places — Hierakonpolis in particular — start growing into something larger than a village, something that looks more like a town and is starting to look (if you squint) like a small city. Trade with distant places picks up. Specialized craft production picks up. Religion gets more elaborate. The first elite burials with imported goods from a thousand miles away start to appear. It is the beginning of what Egyptologists call the **Naqada Sequence** — the long centuries of Upper Egyptian development that will, by 3100 BCE, produce the first kings and the first unification of Egypt.

The Badarian was the foundation. The Naqada period is when Upper Egypt builds the first floors on top of the foundation, and Lower Egypt slowly falls behind. That is the next chapter.

Cross-civ check before we move on: at the moment the Badarian is shading into Naqada I, the Mesopotamian Ubaid culture is also entering its late phase, the irrigation networks of southern Iraq are starting to coalesce around proto-urban temple centers like **Eridu** (Mesopotamia Ch 1), and the city of **Uruk** is about to begin the explosive growth that will make it the first true city in the world. Mesopotamia is roughly half a step ahead of Egypt at this moment in the broader race toward urbanism. By the Naqada II period, Egyptian craftsmen will be importing the cylinder-seal idea, the niched-palace facade, and other Mesopotamian inventions across what may have been a real but indirect trade network running through Syria and the Sinai land bridge. Egypt was not going to grow up in isolation. Some of the most important things in the next chapter come from the east.

# Chapter 3 — Naqada I and II: Upper Egypt Pulls Ahead

By 3800 BCE the Nile Valley had two halves — Upper Egypt in the narrow southern valley and Lower Egypt in the wide northern Delta — and they were not developing at the same speed. Over the next thousand years, one half would sprint ahead while the other stalled, and the winner of that race would eventually swallow the loser and create the Egyptian state. This chapter is about how Upper Egypt won.

The thousand-year cultural tradition that archaeologists call the **Naqada sequence** is named for the modern village of Naqada (NAH-kah-dah), about 20 miles (32 km) north of Luxor on the west bank of the Nile, where Sir Flinders Petrie excavated a massive predynastic cemetery in 1894 and used the pottery sequence he found there to define the chronology. Petrie was the first archaeologist to figure out, by carefully sorting pots into groups based on small evolutionary changes, that you could put the predynastic period in chronological order even without dates. His Naqada sequence is still the backbone of how Egyptologists organize this era, and it divides the period into three phases: **Naqada I**, **Naqada II**, and **Naqada III**. This chapter covers the first two. The next chapter picks up at Naqada III, when actual writing and named kings show up in the archaeological record.

(Older books and museum labels sometimes call Naqada I the **Amratian** period and Naqada II the **Gerzean** period, after the villages where each phase was first identified. Modern Egyptologists mostly use the Naqada numbering instead. They mean the same thing.)

---

The Naqada I phase, roughly 3800 to 3500 BCE, is in many ways a continuation and intensification of the Badarian. Same kinds of villages, same kinds of farming, same kinds of cemeteries on the desert edge. The pottery is recognizably descended from Badarian pottery — black-topped red ware is still the dominant style, though the shapes get more varied and the proportions of the vessels stretch out. There is a new kind of decorated ware that Badarian potters didn't make: **white cross-lined pottery**, where designs are painted onto a polished red surface in white pigment after firing, often showing animals (hippos, antelope, elephants, fish), human figures, boats, and geometric patterns. The cross-lined pots are the first real narrative art the Nile has produced. Some of them show hunting scenes; some show what might be ritual processions; some show boats with cabins and oars. They are the earliest direct visual record of how predynastic Egyptians thought about themselves and their world.

The graves get richer and more stratified. The very richest Naqada I graves contain dozens of pots, hundreds of beads, ivory combs and pins, slate cosmetic palettes carved into animal shapes (turtles, hippos, fish, birds), copper objects, sometimes whole imported stone vessels. The poorest graves, in the same cemeteries, contain almost nothing. The gap between the richest and the poorest tomb is now wide enough that you cannot reasonably read it as anything other than a class system: a small number of households are accumulating wealth, displaying it, and burying it with their dead, while the great majority of the population is being buried with what they can scrape together. Egypt has chiefs now. They don't have crowns yet. But they have inheritances large enough to be visible from four thousand years away.

---

The most important Naqada I site — and the place where the developmental gap between Upper and Lower Egypt becomes clearest — is **Hierakonpolis**.

Hierakonpolis is the Greek name for an ancient Egyptian town that was actually called **Nekhen** (NEH-ken). Egyptologists use both names interchangeably, and you will see Hierakonpolis on most modern maps and Nekhen in most ancient texts. The Greek name means "City of the Hawk" or "City of the Falcon," and it refers to the town's patron deity: a falcon god who would eventually be identified with **Horus**, the divine prototype of the Egyptian king. Hierakonpolis sits on the west bank of the Nile in southern Upper Egypt, about 70 miles (110 km) downstream from Aswan, and the area around it has been almost continuously excavated since the 1890s. It is one of the most thoroughly studied predynastic sites in Egypt, and it deserves the attention. Almost everything that "ancient Egypt" eventually became can be traced in some form to choices that were being made at Hierakonpolis between 3700 and 3000 BCE.

Around 3700 BCE, Hierakonpolis was already significantly larger than any other settlement on the Nile. It covered something like 75 acres (30 hectares) of mudbrick houses, workshops, granaries, and ritual structures along a kilometer-long stretch of riverbank. Its population in the late Naqada I phase was probably between 5,000 and 10,000 people — not a city by Mesopotamian standards (Uruk would have ten times as many people at its peak, Mesopotamia Ch 2), but for the Nile Valley in 3700 BCE, this was a colossal concentration of human beings. Most of the rest of Upper Egypt at this time consisted of villages of a few hundred people each. Hierakonpolis was an outlier by an order of magnitude.

Why was it so big? Geography helped. The Hierakonpolis region had unusually wide stretches of cultivable floodplain on both sides of the river, plus access to the Wadi Abu Suffian — a desert wadi that ran west into the Eastern Sahara and gave the town's hunters and traders access to game, minerals, and the long-distance routes through what was already turning into desert. The town sat at a natural crossroads of riverine and overland trade, in a place where the agricultural carrying capacity could support a population large enough to specialize.

What it did with that advantage was build things nobody else was building yet.

The first thing it built was a **brewery**.

This sounds anticlimactic until you understand the scale. Beer in the predynastic Nile world was a staple food, not a luxury — it was made from coarsely ground emmer wheat and barley, partially fermented in shallow vats, mildly alcoholic, thick enough to drink as a meal, and rich in calories and B vitamins that the bread of the same period was often missing. Egyptians of every era drank beer. Workers were paid in beer. Temples offered beer to the gods. The pyramids were built by labor crews supplied with daily beer rations measured by the jar. Beer was so central to Egyptian existence that the standard Egyptian phrase for "to die" was sometimes "to depart for beer."

The brewery at Hierakonpolis, dated to around 3600 BCE, is the oldest large-scale beer production facility known anywhere in the world. It consists of a row of huge ceramic vats — each capable of holding tens of gallons — set into the ground in a dedicated production area, with hearths and grain-grinding stations alongside. The vats were large enough to produce significant volumes per batch — probably tens of gallons at a time — and the facility was clearly designed for continuous production rather than one-off brewing. That is industrial production, in the literal sense: it could not have been consumed by the brewer's family. It was being made for distribution — to feed laborers working on town projects, to provision ritual feasts, to supply the elite households that controlled the brewery in the first place. The presence of an industrial brewery is by itself proof that Hierakonpolis had a centralized economy and a working class large enough to need feeding at scale. Two thousand years before the pyramids, the same beer-and-bread provisioning system that would build the pyramids was already being practiced at Hierakonpolis.

Around the same time, the Hierakonpolis people built a **temple**.

The structure they built around 3500 BCE was a large rectangular enclosure of mudbrick and reed mats, with a central open-air courtyard, several inner chambers, and what appears to have been a wooden flagpole or totem pole at one end. It was associated with cattle burials, with offerings of imported flint tools, with caches of carved ivory figurines and ceremonial pottery. There is no mistaking it for a private house or a granary. It was built as a sacred space, and it is the oldest confirmed temple in Egypt — the great-great-grandfather, structurally and conceptually, of every later Egyptian temple from the Step Pyramid complex at Saqqara to the colossal pylons of Karnak. The architectural plan that the Hierakonpolis temple was using — open courtyard, screening walls, inner chambers, a tall pole or standard at the focal point — is the ancestor plan that all later Egyptian temples will iterate on for the next three thousand years.

We don't know exactly which god the Hierakonpolis temple was for. The town's later patron was Horus the falcon, and the strong likelihood is that the early temple was already a Horus shrine — possibly the very first place where the falcon was worshipped as a high god. If that's right, then everything later Egyptians believed about the divine kingship of the pharaoh as the earthly incarnation of Horus traces back to a mudbrick courtyard at Hierakonpolis around 3500 BCE.

---

And then there is the painted tomb.

In 1898, two Egyptologists named Frederick Green and James Quibell were excavating the Hierakonpolis cemetery and came across a burial they labeled, in their notes, as Tomb 100. It was the largest grave they had found at the site — a rectangular chamber dug into the desert sand, lined with mudbrick walls, big enough to walk around inside. The body and most of the grave goods had been looted in antiquity. But the walls, against all archaeological probability, were still painted.

The paintings on the walls of Tomb 100 are the oldest preserved figural mural art in Egypt. They date to around 3500 BCE — the very end of Naqada I, the cusp of Naqada II — and they show scenes that we can recognize as the seedbed of pharaonic iconography even though no pharaoh has yet existed. There are boats with cabins and rowers. There are hunters with bows and dogs. There are bulls and lions and antelope. There is at least one figure that appears to show what may be a **smiting pose** — a man standing over kneeling figures, one arm raised — though the paintings are badly faded and scholars disagree about the details of the composition. If the identification is correct, it is an early ancestor of the iconic pose that, three thousand years later, will appear on every temple pylon in Egypt as the standard depiction of pharaoh-as-victor. The visual vocabulary of kingship may be older than the kingship itself.

The figure in the smiting pose at Tomb 100 is not labeled with a name (there is no writing yet — that is the next chapter's story). But the implication is clear: somebody was buried in Tomb 100 who wanted to be remembered as a man who killed his enemies. That somebody was important enough to be buried in the largest tomb in the largest town on the Nile, and important enough that the artists who painted his tomb chose to depict him in the same pose that would later be reserved for kings. We do not have a name to attach to the body. But we have, at Hierakonpolis around 3500 BCE, the unmistakable signs of something that is no longer a chiefdom and is starting to look like a kingdom.

---

The Naqada II phase, roughly 3500 to 3300 BCE, is when the race between the two halves of Egypt stopped being close.

Everything that happened next — the innovations in craft, transport, agriculture, and trade — happened overwhelmingly in Upper Egypt. Lower Egypt's Maadi culture kept doing things the old way: small village clusters, modest hierarchies, mostly local trade networks. Upper Egypt was building something else entirely, and every new technology widened the gap.

Start with the pottery, because it tells you what people were thinking about. The old black-topped red ware fades out in Naqada II, and the new dominant style is a buff-colored ware decorated with painted scenes of boats, animals, and ritual processions in red ochre on a creamy background. These painted pots are some of the most beautiful predynastic objects to come out of Egypt, and their subject matter is revealing. The boats they painted have multiple cabins and oar-banks, the kind of vessel you would build only if you needed to move heavy cargo or large parties of people up and down the river. The processions show figures in distinctive headgear carrying staffs, leading captives, or making offerings. These are images of power and organized movement — the visual vocabulary of people who are running something, not just farming beside it.

The pots were not exaggerating. Naqada II craftsmen had started building **sewn-plank boats** (hulls made of cedar or acacia planks, fitted edge-to-edge, drilled with small holes, and sewn together with rope) that could carry heavy loads under sail or oar for long distances along the Nile. Earlier predynastic Egyptians had used reed rafts adequate for a short river crossing but useless for trade. The sewn-plank boat changed the economics of the valley: suddenly one town's surplus grain, one town's brewery output, one town's copper tools could travel a hundred miles downriver and come back with something worth more. Lower Egypt had nothing comparable.

At the same time, Upper Egyptian farmers were spreading **basin irrigation** across the floodplain. The technique was simple: build low earthen walls along the edges of natural depressions so that the September floodwater fills the basin and stays there for a month or two, soaking the soil deeply before draining away. Basin irrigation is much simpler than the canal-and-pump systems used in Mesopotamia (Mesopotamia Ch 2), but it requires coordination among neighboring villages to maintain the basin walls, and that coordination requires either a chief who can issue orders or a council that can make collective decisions. By 3400 BCE, large stretches of the Upper Egyptian floodplain were being managed as a coordinated system of basins. The people doing the coordinating were becoming the people who gave orders about everything else, too.

And then there is **faience**. **Faience** (fy-AHNCE) is a wonderful and weird material — not pottery exactly, but crushed quartz mixed with a small amount of natron (a naturally occurring sodium compound used as a flux), shaped into beads or amulets, then fired at high temperature. During firing, the natron migrates to the surface and forms a glassy glaze in brilliant turquoise to deep blue, depending on how much copper was added. Faience is the world's first deliberately manufactured glaze, and Egypt invented it. The blue-green color was loaded with meaning from the start — the color of the Nile in flood, the color of the sky, and eventually the color associated with rebirth and the afterlife. Egyptians would bury their dead with faience amulets for the next three thousand years. All of that symbolic loading begins in Naqada II, when the first beads come out of the first kilns at Hierakonpolis. Lower Egypt was not making faience.

The cumulative effect of boats, irrigation, and craft innovation was wealth, and the graves prove it. Naqada II elite tombs contain stone vessels carved from imported alabaster and basalt, gold ornaments, copper tools and weapons, ivory pendants, faience beads, and — most strikingly — small quantities of **lapis lazuli** (LAP-iss LAZ-yuh-lee), a deep blue stone whose nearest major source was the Sar-i Sang valley in the mountains of northeastern Afghanistan. To reach Egypt, lapis had to be quarried in Afghanistan, traded across Iran to Mesopotamia, traded across Mesopotamia to the Levant, traded across the Levant to the Sinai, and carried into Egypt — something like 3,000 miles (4,800 km) through a chain of intermediary trades. The only people who could afford to pay for the resulting bead were the very richest households on the Nile. Lapis is the first hard evidence that Upper Egyptian elites had become wealthy enough to participate in the proto-globalized trade networks of the Bronze Age. No Lower Egyptian graves contain lapis.

Gold tells the same story. The Eastern Desert between the Nile and the Red Sea was rich in alluvial gold (we will hear more about the Wadi Hammamat trade route in the next chapter). By the late Naqada II, gold appears in elite burials in quantities that suggest organized expeditions rather than casual gathering — somebody was sending crews into the Eastern Desert specifically to bring back gold. Egypt's reputation as the gold-producing power of the ancient world starts here, and it starts in Upper Egypt. The Delta had no Eastern Desert, no gold wadis, no expeditionary infrastructure. The gap was becoming a chasm.

---

Why did Upper Egypt pull so far ahead? Three theories compete, and probably all three mattered. First, geography: the Upper Egyptian floodplain is narrower and more clearly defined than the Delta, which concentrated population around fewer key sites and made coordination easier. Second, the Sahara migration: Upper Egypt may have absorbed more refugees from the drying western desert than the Delta did, and the cattle-herding traditions those refugees brought may have been more compatible with the kind of stratified, ritual-based society that produced kingship. Third, trade routes: Upper Egypt sat at the junction of the Nile, the Eastern Desert (gold), and the Red Sea coast (long-distance trade with Punt), while Lower Egypt's trade was mostly with the closer Levant and didn't generate the same wealth concentration. Whatever the mixture, by 3400 BCE the race was over. Upper Egypt had won, and the only remaining question was whether unification would happen by absorption or by conquest.

The next chapter is about how that final pull happened — about writing, about named kings, about the ceremonial palettes and royal cemeteries of the Naqada III period, and about the long set of regional conflicts that historians sometimes call the **Wars of Unification**. By the end of those wars, Egypt will have been welded into a single political unit under a single king, and the predynastic period will be over.

Cross-civ check: as Naqada II is winding down around 3300 BCE, **Uruk** in southern Mesopotamia is now a city of perhaps 40,000 people — the largest human settlement in the world by a wide margin (Mesopotamia Ch 2). Mesopotamian writing has just been invented (the earliest proto-cuneiform tablets are now being scratched out by Uruk accountants). Mesopotamian seal-cylinders, niched temple facades, and stylized hero-and-animal motifs are starting to appear in Egyptian elite contexts as imported curiosities. The two civilizations are now making contact, indirectly, through middlemen in Syria and the Sinai. Whatever the route, Mesopotamian ideas were now reaching the Nile, and some of them — especially the cylinder seal and the niched-facade form of monumental architecture — would be picked up and adapted by the very first kings of Egypt. The Nile Valley's first writing system (next chapter) is too different from cuneiform to be a copy, but the *idea* that you could record information by carving symbols on a hard surface may itself have been a Mesopotamian import, picked up by Egyptian elites who had seen it in action and decided to invent their own version.

---

# Chapter 4 — Naqada III: Writing, Kings, and the Road to Unity

By 3300 BCE the predynastic Nile world had two centers of political gravity, and they were on a collision course.

The southern center was the one we already know: **Hierakonpolis** (Nekhen), the city of the falcon, with its temple and its painted tomb and its industrial brewery, sitting in the fat green stretch of floodplain in southernmost Upper Egypt. The northern center was new — a town called **This** (or **Thinis**, both spellings appear in modern English; the ancient Egyptian name was something like *Tjenu*, pronounced roughly "CHEN-oo"), located somewhere in middle Upper Egypt, probably near the modern city of Girga, about 100 miles (160 km) downstream from Hierakonpolis. We don't know exactly where Thinis was — its actual ruins have never been definitively identified, and most scholars suspect it lies under modern farmland that has been continuously cultivated for thousands of years. What we do know is that Thinis was the political center for a powerful chiefdom that controlled the stretch of the Nile around **Abydos** (uh-BYE-doss), and Abydos is where the kings of Thinis were buried, in a sandy desert cemetery on the western edge of town.

Abydos is going to matter a great deal. By the late predynastic period it was already the most important royal cemetery in Egypt — the place where the kings of Thinis dug their tombs into the desert margin, in a corner of the cemetery known to modern archaeologists as **Cemetery U**. By the First Dynasty, every king of unified Egypt would be buried at Abydos. By the Middle Kingdom, it would be a national pilgrimage site associated with the cult of **Osiris** (the god of the dead and the underworld). By the New Kingdom, it would be a town with temples, processional roads, and religious schools attached to the royal tombs. The cult of Abydos as Egypt's holy of holies starts here, with the modest sand-lined burials of forgotten predynastic kings.

This chapter is about those kings — the men and (probably) women whose names have been recovered from the labels and pot marks of Cemetery U at Abydos and who were ruling parts of Upper Egypt in the centuries before any unified state existed. It is about the invention of writing, about the elaboration of art, about the long contest between rival Upper Egyptian centers that ended with one king on top of all the others. It is the period the archaeologists call **Naqada III**, and it runs from roughly 3300 BCE down to the moment of unification around 3100 BCE — about two hundred years, the shortest of the three Naqada phases, but the most important.

By the end of this chapter, Egypt will have writing, named kings buried in named tombs, ceremonial art commissioned by named patrons, and a single ruling dynasty that has destroyed all its rivals. The next chapter is about the man who finished the unification.

---

The most important single artifact of the Naqada III period is buried in a tomb labeled **Tomb U-j**, in the predynastic cemetery at Abydos.

Tomb U-j was excavated by a German team led by Günter Dreyer in the 1980s and 1990s, and the contents of it changed our understanding of how Egyptian writing began. The tomb itself is a brick-lined chamber complex with twelve rooms, dated to roughly 3250 BCE. It is the largest predynastic tomb yet found anywhere in Egypt, larger than any of the contemporary elite burials at Hierakonpolis. The man buried inside it was clearly a king, even though no king lists from later periods record a king of Egypt this early — he was a king of part of Upper Egypt at most, ruling from Thinis over the territory around Abydos, and his name has been reconstructed from later clues as something that sounds in our ears like **Scorpion**. Egyptologists call him **Scorpion I** to distinguish him from another and slightly later king, **Scorpion II**, whose famous ceremonial macehead we will meet in the next chapter. Scorpion I is the earliest Egyptian ruler whose name has come down to us. He probably ruled around 3250 BCE, more than a hundred years before the unification.

What was buried with him?

Two things. First, around 700 wine jars. The jars were imported, full, from the southern Levant — probably from vineyards in what is now Israel or Palestine — and shipped to Abydos in such quantities that they must represent either a massive trade relationship or the spoils of a successful military expedition. The wine itself is long gone, but residue analysis on the inside of the jars confirms it was wine, and pollen and seed evidence shows the grapes were Levantine. Scorpion I was being buried with one of the largest stockpiles of imported alcohol in the prehistoric world. He was clearly expecting to drink very well in the afterlife.

Second — and this is the historic part — the jars and the surrounding grave goods were tagged with **labels**: small flat tags of carved bone or ivory, each pierced with a hole at one corner so it could be tied to a jar with a string, each carved with a small group of symbols. The symbols are hieroglyphs.

Or rather, they are something we can recognize as the ancestors of hieroglyphs. They are stylized images — a falcon, a scorpion, a bull, an elephant, a serpent, what may be plant signs and architectural signs and numerical strokes — arranged in tiny clusters that almost certainly encode information about where the goods came from and how much there was. The labels are not yet a full writing system; they don't compose sentences, they don't conjugate verbs, they don't contain grammatical particles. But they are doing the basic work of writing, which is to use carved or drawn symbols to record specific information that can be read back later by somebody who knows the convention. The U-j labels are the earliest writing in Egypt by about two hundred years, and they push the start date of Egyptian literacy back to about 3250 BCE — almost exactly the moment when the first proto-cuneiform tablets were being inscribed in Uruk in Mesopotamia.

The two writing systems are not the same. Cuneiform uses a fundamentally different set of signs and a fundamentally different grammar. Hieroglyphic writing uses pictograms that are recognizably images of the things they name (a falcon sign for the word "falcon," at least to start with), and the system would later evolve to include phonetic signs that stood for sounds rather than meanings — so the same falcon sign could be used to write any word that started with the consonant sound the Egyptian word for "falcon" started with, regardless of meaning. This kind of phonetic-pictographic hybrid is the thing we now call **hieroglyphs** (a Greek word meaning "sacred carvings"), and it is a different kind of writing system from cuneiform in important technical ways. But the timing of the two systems' invention is so close that scholars have argued for decades over whether Egyptian writing was a totally independent invention or whether the *idea* of writing reached Egypt from Mesopotamia and Egyptian scribes built their own system from the ground up after seeing the Mesopotamian system in action.

The honest answer is we cannot prove either way. There is no Egyptian sign that can be shown to be a borrowed cuneiform sign. There is no Mesopotamian sign that can be shown to be a borrowed Egyptian sign. The two systems are technically independent. But the timing is suspicious enough — and the trade contacts well enough attested — that the **stimulus diffusion** theory (the idea that the concept of writing traveled from Mesopotamia to Egypt without any specific signs being copied) remains the most plausible reconstruction. Egyptian elites of the late Naqada II period saw or heard about the Mesopotamian innovation, decided that they could use something similar, and built their own from scratch. That is not the same as saying Egyptian hieroglyphs are derived from cuneiform. It is saying that the moment when Egyptian elites decided writing was something they wanted may itself have been triggered by exposure to a foreign idea.

Either way, the result is what matters. By 3250 BCE, Scorpion I's accountants are tagging his wine jars in the world's second writing system. By 3100 BCE, the unifier of Egypt will be carving his name on a ceremonial slate palette in fully formed hieroglyphs. Egyptian writing, once it starts, develops faster than any other ancient script. From the U-j labels to the Narmer Palette is only about 150 years. The whole conceptual leap — from picture-tag to phonetic script to royal monument inscription — happens in the same six or seven generations.

---

The other thing the Naqada III period invents is **monumental ceremonial art**.

We've already met the cosmetic palettes of the Badarian period — flat slabs of stone with shallow grinding depressions, used to grind eye-paint pigments, sometimes carved into animal shapes. By the late Naqada II and Naqada III, the palettes have started to evolve. They are getting bigger. The grinding depression is shrinking and being pushed toward one corner of the slab, while the rest of the surface fills up with elaborately carved figural decoration. By the very end of the predynastic period, some palettes are 24 inches (60 cm) across and have no functional grinding depression at all — they are pure ceremonial objects, designed to be displayed in temples or buried in royal tombs, with their entire surfaces covered in low-relief carving showing battles, processions, hunts, and ritual scenes.

These late predynastic **ceremonial palettes** are the immediate ancestors of the most famous artifact of the unification, the Narmer Palette (Chapter 5). Several of them survive — the **Hunters Palette**, the **Battlefield Palette**, the **Bull Palette**, the **Towns Palette** (sometimes called the Libyan Palette) — each carved with scenes of conquest and hunting, each apparently commissioned by some predynastic ruler as a memorial to a specific victory. We can't always read the symbols on them clearly enough to identify the ruler involved, but the iconography is consistent: a powerful figure (sometimes shown as a bull, sometimes as a lion, sometimes as a falcon-headed standard) crushing his enemies, taking captives, leading processions, and ruling cities. The palettes are political propaganda in carved stone. They are the visual side of the literacy revolution, and they show us the same world the U-j labels show us — a world where Upper Egyptian rulers were already commissioning art that celebrated their personal authority over rivals and conquered peoples.

Among the palettes, one of the most striking objects of the period is not a palette at all but a **knife**.

The **Gebel el-Arak knife** is a flint blade, beautifully made, with a carved ivory hilt, found in a tomb at the desert site of Gebel el-Arak in middle Egypt. It dates to around 3300–3200 BCE. The flint blade is a technical marvel — pressure-flaked from a single piece of stone with such precision that the cutting edge is still razor-sharp four thousand years later. But the famous part of the object is the ivory hilt, carved on both sides with miniature scenes that have made art historians argue about it for over a century.

One side of the hilt shows a battle: men in long boats fighting other men, with bodies floating in the water between the boats. Some of the boats have a flat, raised hull with a cabin at one end — these are recognizably Egyptian Nile boats. Other boats have a vertical, almost crescent-shaped hull profile that looks nothing like anything else known from Egyptian art — these look much more like Mesopotamian river boats from contemporary Sumerian seal carvings. The other side of the hilt shows a "master of animals" figure: a bearded man in long robes, standing between two rampant lions, holding them apart by their throats. This pose is not Egyptian. It is a classic Mesopotamian motif, used in Sumerian art for kings and gods, and it shows up at Gebel el-Arak in a context that suggests the artist had seen Mesopotamian seal-carvings and was deliberately quoting them.

Nobody quite knows what to make of the Gebel el-Arak knife. Possible interpretations include: (1) the knife commemorates an actual battle between Upper Egyptian and Mesopotamian-influenced parties on the Nile, (2) the knife is a status object made by an Egyptian craftsman who had been trained by, or had been studying the work of, Mesopotamian seal-cutters, (3) the knife is a foreign import that was adapted and re-decorated in Egypt for an Egyptian patron. What everybody agrees on is that the knife is direct evidence of significant contact between predynastic Egypt and Mesopotamia in the late Naqada II / early Naqada III period — contact significant enough that Egyptian elites were being buried with foreign-influenced art objects, and that Mesopotamian iconographic conventions were being incorporated into Egyptian visual culture.

The contact mostly seems to have flowed one direction: from Mesopotamia into Egypt. Egyptian art picked up the cylinder seal (a small carved cylinder rolled across wet clay to leave a continuous stamped impression — the standard Mesopotamian way of marking property and authenticating documents), the niched-palace facade (a particular ornamental treatment of mudbrick walls that mimicked the niched temple platforms of Sumerian cities), and elements of the master-of-animals iconography. But Egypt did not become a Mesopotamian colony. The borrowed elements got incorporated into a fundamentally Egyptian visual and political tradition, and within a generation or two they had been digested and re-expressed in a way that no longer looked Mesopotamian at all. By the time of the unification, only a few isolated motifs (the niched serekh frame for royal names, for instance — the device we will meet at length in Chapter 6) still carry the trace of their foreign origin.

---

While Upper Egypt was inventing writing, monumentalizing its art, and importing foreign ideas, its political life was getting bloodier.

By the late Naqada III, Upper Egypt was no longer a patchwork of independent chiefdoms. It had been consolidated, by force, into a small number of competing centers — and in the standard reconstruction (which is admittedly speculative but which fits the evidence as well as anything else), there were probably only two real powers left by about 3200 BCE: the kings ruling from Thinis and burying their dead at Abydos in the north, and the kings ruling from Hierakonpolis in the south. The two centers controlled stretches of the Upper Egyptian Nile that probably overlapped at the edges, and the historical pattern of all such situations applies: the two powers fought, they raided each other's territory, they took prisoners, and they commemorated their victories in carved palettes and rock art.

Several of the late predynastic ceremonial palettes — particularly the Battlefield Palette and the Cities Palette — show this conflict in iconographic form. The Battlefield Palette has carved figures of a giant lion and birds of prey tearing at the bodies of fallen enemies on a battlefield strewn with corpses. The Cities Palette shows a row of fortified towns being attacked by symbolic animal figures (lions, falcons, scorpions) representing different conquering rulers. The artistic convention is the same one that the Narmer Palette will use a generation later: powerful animal-totems standing in for the victorious king, anonymous broken bodies standing in for the defeated. The names of the victors are not always preserved, but the names of some of them have been recovered from contemporary inscriptions and pot-marks: figures that scholars have nicknamed "**King Iry-Hor**," "**King Ka**," and "**Scorpion II**." These were probably the last few rulers of the Thinite line before the unification — the predecessors and possibly the immediate ancestors of Narmer himself, the man who would finish the conquest.

What we do not have, even a hint of, is the perspective of the people on the losing end. There are no surviving texts from the Hierakonpolis side, no monuments commissioned by the kings of Hierakonpolis claiming victory over the Thinite north, no carved palettes celebrating the triumphs of a Hierakonpolis ruler. The archaeological record of Hierakonpolis itself shows the town continuing to be occupied through the unification and into the early dynastic period — it is not destroyed, it is not abandoned, the temple is still standing, the brewery is still running. What we do not see is a Hierakonpolis royal cemetery comparable to Cemetery U at Abydos. The kings of Hierakonpolis, if there were still any after about 3200 BCE, were not being buried with the kind of monumental tombs that their Thinite contemporaries were getting. The implication is that Hierakonpolis lost its independence sometime in the late Naqada III period — peacefully or not — and was absorbed into the growing Thinite kingdom. By the eve of the unification, the Thinite kings of Abydos had become the kings of all of Upper Egypt, and the only thing left to conquer was Lower Egypt.

There are also signs that, before the final unification, somebody had already started fighting. **Trade with Nubia** picks up dramatically in the late Naqada III, with Egyptian goods moving south past the First Cataract into Lower Nubia and Nubian gold and exotic goods moving north into Egypt (Ancient Nubia Ch 1 — the Nubian end of this exchange was the early A-Group culture, and the relationship was usually peaceful and lucrative for both sides until the early dynastic period when it turned violent). Egyptian rock inscriptions appear at Nubian sites for the first time in this period, often in the form of standardized royal names carved into cliff faces — claims of authority, or markers of expedition routes. The expansion is southward as well as inward.

By 3150 BCE, then, the political map of Upper Egypt has been simplified almost to the breaking point. There is one kingdom, ruled from Thinis. There is one royal cemetery, at Abydos, with the kings of the Thinite line buried in increasingly elaborate brick-chambered tombs. There is one writing system, derived from the U-j labels, now sophisticated enough to record royal names and titles in serekh frames. There is one art tradition, expressed in the ceremonial palettes and in the carved knives and macehead heads of the elite. There is one religion, centered on the falcon god of Hierakonpolis (now syncretized with the kings of Thinis as their divine patron — the kings of the Thinite line have started being called "**Followers of Horus**," meaning that the Horus falcon that Hierakonpolis worshipped has now been adopted as the protector and prototype of the Thinite kingship). And there is one obvious next step.

The next step is Lower Egypt.

The conquest of Lower Egypt is the subject of the next chapter. The man who finishes it is named Narmer. His face — or at least his name, his crowns, and his enemies — survives on a carved ceremonial palette that has become the single most famous artifact of predynastic Egypt and the conventional starting point of the dynastic king lists. With Narmer, the predynastic period ends and recorded history begins.

Cross-civ check before we go: at the moment Scorpion I is being buried at Abydos with his 700 wine jars and his ivory labels, the city of **Uruk** in southern Mesopotamia has just gone through its own period of explosive urban expansion and is the dominant power in the entire Near East (Mesopotamia Ch 2). Uruk's "colonial" trading posts have been established as far north as the upper Tigris and as far west as Syria. Mesopotamian cylinder seals, niched temple architecture, and proto-cuneiform writing are now the standard tools of Bronze Age statecraft from the Persian Gulf to the eastern Mediterranean. Egypt is the late arrival to this world, the second civilization to develop writing and the first to figure out how to use it for monumental royal propaganda. The Mesopotamian and Egyptian states are now developing in the same broad geopolitical neighborhood, and they will spend the next three thousand years influencing each other, fighting each other, trading with each other, and occasionally trying to ignore each other. The relationship that will eventually produce Cleopatra and the Ptolemies starts, dimly, in the Naqada III period, with a Sumerian master-of-animals figure carved on the ivory hilt of an Egyptian flint knife.

# Chapter 5 — Narmer Unifies Egypt

There is a particular slab of carved green siltstone, about 25 inches (64 cm) tall and shaped like a shield, that sits behind glass in the Egyptian Museum in Cairo. It has been sitting there since it was found, in 1898, by James Quibell and Frederick Green during their excavations at Hierakonpolis. If you visit Cairo and ask any museum guard where to find it, they will know what you mean. It is the **Narmer Palette**.

The Narmer Palette is the most reproduced, most analyzed, and most argued-about artifact of the entire predynastic period. It is conventionally treated as the very first monument of the unified Egyptian state — the moment when the first king put both crowns on his head and the dynastic age began. Whether that description is strictly accurate (it may simplify a longer, messier process) is a question worth dealing with. But we will start with what the object actually shows, because the object is spectacular, and some of what it shows has been misread so often that it is worth going through it panel by panel.

---

The palette is carved on both sides.

On the front (the "recto" side), a large figure dominates the upper register. He is wearing the **White Crown** of Upper Egypt — a tall, bowling-pin-shaped headdress that is one of the most distinctive objects in Egyptian iconography. He is holding a mace in his right hand, raised above his head, and with his left hand he is gripping a kneeling enemy by the hair. He is about to smash the enemy's skull. This is the **smiting pose** — the same pose we saw in the Painted Tomb at Hierakonpolis in Chapter 3, now carved in formal, polished relief by a master sculptor and inscribed with the name of the man doing the smiting.

The name is written in a **serekh** — a rectangular frame shaped like a palace facade, with the king's name written inside the top portion and the Horus falcon perched on top. Inside the serekh are two hieroglyphs: a catfish (*n'r*, pronounced approximately "nar") and a chisel (*mr*, approximately "mer"). Together they spell **Narmer**. The falcon on top of the serekh declares that the man named Narmer is the Horus king — the earthly manifestation of the falcon god Horus, the divine ruler of Egypt. Above the smiting scene, between two bovine heads (probably Hathor, the cow goddess) at the top of the palette, the name "Narmer" appears again in a simple cartouche-like frame. There is no ambiguity about whose monument this is.

The kneeling enemy is smaller — on Egyptian monuments, size always indicates importance, and the smitten are always smaller than the smiter — and beside his head there are hieroglyphs that may name him or may name his people. The standard reading identifies the victim as a representative of Lower Egypt, specifically the Delta. The tableau, taken as a whole, says: "Narmer, king of Upper Egypt, wearing the Upper Egyptian crown, conquers the Delta."

On the back (the "verso" side), the same Narmer appears again, this time wearing the **Red Crown** of Lower Egypt — a flat-topped headdress with a tall back-piece and a forward-curving wire or reed at the front. He is shown in procession, accompanied by attendants with standards, inspecting rows of decapitated enemies whose heads have been placed between their feet. Below the main scene, a bull (almost certainly another representation of the king — Egyptian rulers are frequently shown as bulls in predynastic and early dynastic art) tramples a fallen enemy and breaks through the wall of a fortified town.

The message: Narmer has won. He has conquered the Delta. He wears both crowns — Upper Egyptian on one side, Lower Egyptian on the other. He has inspected the dead. His bull-avatar is destroying the fortifications of the north. Egypt is unified.

---

Except. A few complications.

First complication: Narmer probably was not the first king to attempt unification. He may not even have been the one who finished it.

The ceremonial **macehead** of a king called **Scorpion II** — found at the same Hierakonpolis deposit where the Narmer Palette was discovered — shows a large figure wearing the White Crown of Upper Egypt, performing what appears to be an agricultural ritual (opening irrigation channels or plowing a field), attended by standards and bowing subjects. The figure's name is written as a hieroglyphic scorpion beside a small rosette sign. The macehead doesn't show the Red Crown of Lower Egypt, which has led most scholars to conclude that Scorpion II was a king who ruled Upper Egypt before Narmer and had not yet conquered the north. He was either Narmer's immediate predecessor or possibly his father. The macehead was deposited in the same temple at Hierakonpolis where the Narmer Palette was found, suggesting that both objects were votive offerings made by kings to the falcon god.

Scorpion II is not Scorpion I (Chapter 4). They are probably a century or more apart. But they may be related — the scorpion may have been the totem emblem of a particular family or lineage within the Thinite ruling house, reused by different kings in the same way that "Thutmose" or "Amenhotep" would later be reused by different rulers of the same dynasty.

Second complication: there was an even earlier predecessor whose name we barely know.

A serekh naming a ruler called **Ka** (or possibly **Sekhen**) appears on pots and jar sealings at Abydos, in contexts that predate Narmer but postdate Scorpion I. King Ka seems to have been another late predynastic ruler of the Thinite line, bracketed between Iry-Hor (an even more shadowy figure) and Scorpion II. We know almost nothing about him except that he existed, that he was buried at Abydos, and that he used a serekh with a Horus falcon just like Narmer's. The names Iry-Hor → Ka → Scorpion II → Narmer form a probable sequence of predynastic Thinite rulers, and between them they cover the final century or so of the predynastic wars — the century during which Upper Egypt conquered Lower Egypt and the unified state was created.

Third complication: the later Egyptian king-list tradition gives a different name for the first king.

The Egyptian priest **Manetho** (mah-NEH-tho), who compiled a history of the pharaohs for the Greek Ptolemaic rulers around 300 BCE, called the first king of the First Dynasty **Menes** (MEH-neez). Menes was credited with founding Memphis, uniting the Two Lands, and performing various founding acts that later tradition assigned to the first king. For most of the history of Egyptology, the identification of Menes was the central unsolved problem: who was Menes in real life? Narmer? Hor-Aha? Somebody else?

The current consensus, such as it is: **Menes is probably Narmer.** The name "Menes" may be a throne name (a formal name assumed at coronation, separate from the birth name) for the same king who was called Narmer in his serekh. A label from the tomb of Hor-Aha at Abydos appears to show the name "Meni" in a context associated with Narmer's successor, which could mean that the title/name was passed forward from Narmer to Hor-Aha, or that Hor-Aha was retroactively claiming credit for his father's unification. The identification is not certain. Nobody has found an artifact that says, unambiguously, "I am both Narmer and Menes, and I did all these things." But the weight of the evidence points to Narmer as the unifier and Menes as his legacy name.

---

What the Narmer Palette definitely does tell us — regardless of which king did what — is what the unification *meant*. It is not just a military victory memorial. It is a theological statement.

The two crowns are the core of the message. The **White Crown** (*hedjet*) and the **Red Crown** (*deshret*) are the visual emblems of Upper and Lower Egypt respectively — and from this point forward, every king of Egypt will be depicted wearing both, either separately on different occasions or combined into the famous **Double Crown** (*pschent*), which stacks the White Crown inside the Red Crown in a single headdress. The White Crown comes from the south, where it appears on predynastic rock art and pot paintings. The Red Crown comes from the north, where its earliest representations are found in Lower Egyptian contexts. The act of wearing both crowns is the act of claiming sovereignty over both halves of the country. It is the foundation of the Egyptian political system: the pharaoh rules the Two Lands, and the Two Lands are united in his person.

This duality is not just decorative. It is structural. Everything in Egyptian political theology comes in pairs after the unification: Upper and Lower Egypt, Red Land and Black Land, Horus and Seth (the gods of south and north), Nekhbet and Wadjet (the vulture and cobra, patron goddesses of Upper and Lower Egypt respectively), the Sedge and the Bee (the heraldic emblems of the two halves, which become part of the royal titulary). The dual structure is so deeply embedded in Egyptian kingship that it persists for three thousand years, long after any meaningful political distinction between Upper and Lower Egypt has ceased to matter. The Two Lands is the founding myth, and the myth is hard-coded into the Narmer Palette.

---

The other thing the Narmer Palette established is **Memphis**.

**Memphis** (Egyptian *Ineb-hedj*, "White Walls," later *Men-nefer*, from which the Greek name Memphis derives) was the capital city of unified Egypt, built at the junction point between Upper and Lower Egypt — at the apex of the Nile Delta, where the river stops being a single narrow channel and fans out into the web of distributary branches that reaches the Mediterranean. It is the obvious place to put a capital if you have just conquered both halves of the country: close enough to the Delta to control it, close enough to Upper Egypt to administer it, and sitting on the most important transportation hub on the entire river. Memphis was the political center of Egypt for most of the Old Kingdom and remained the largest city in Egypt (and often in the world) for centuries afterward.

The founding of Memphis is traditionally attributed to Menes/Narmer, and the story that Manetho tells is that Menes dammed a branch of the Nile to create the flat ground on which the city was built. Whether that is literally true is debatable — it may be a folk memory of an early irrigation or flood-control project — but what is not debatable is that Memphis was a planned foundation, built to be the capital of a unified state, at a site chosen for strategic rather than historical reasons. Thinis and Hierakonpolis were the old predynastic centers. Memphis was new. Putting the capital at Memphis was a deliberate statement that unified Egypt was something different from either of its predecessors.

Memphis is also where Narmer's successor, **Hor-Aha**, comes into focus. The name Hor-Aha means "Horus the Fighter," and the earliest evidence for his reign comes from a set of ivory labels and jar sealings found in his tomb at Abydos. One label shows a boat with a shrine, captioned with the name of the goddess **Neith** (a warrior goddess from the city of Sais in the western Delta — her cult center was deep in Lower Egyptian territory, and its prominence under the First Dynasty kings is a sign that the new dynasty was making active efforts to integrate the conquered north). Another label records a military campaign into Nubia — the earliest documented Egyptian attack south of the First Cataract. A third shows what may be a crocodile hunt in the Faiyum. The picture is of a king who is consolidating the new unified state by fighting on all its frontiers: north into the Delta, south into Nubia, west into the Faiyum desert.

Hor-Aha was either Narmer's son or his chosen successor. With his accession — if the traditional numbering is used — the **First Dynasty** begins. But the word "dynasty" is misleading if you think of it as a family name or a natural historical break. The First Dynasty is simply a numbering convention imposed on the king list by Manetho 2,800 years after the fact. In reality there may have been no clean break between Narmer and Hor-Aha — one king died, the next took over, and the basic institutions of the new unified state continued without interruption. What did change was scale. The next eight kings of the First Dynasty would rule over a territory that stretched from the First Cataract at Aswan to the Mediterranean coast, a unified Nile Valley state roughly 600 miles (960 km) long, with a population that may have reached one to two million people — the second or third largest state in the Bronze Age world, after Mesopotamia.

The pharaohs were not inventing a state from scratch. They were inheriting a set of institutions — serekhs, temples, irrigation systems, trade networks, craft specialists, an agricultural base, a writing system, a religion centered on the falcon god, a beer-and-bread provisioning economy, and a military tradition — that had been assembled over the previous two thousand years of predynastic development. The unification was the cap on the bottle, not the bottle itself. But the cap changed everything. After Narmer, after Memphis, after the Two Crowns, there is an Egypt — singular, self-conscious, administratively unified — in a way that there was not before. The pharaonic state is now operational.

The next chapter is about what the first dynasty's kings actually did with it — how they built the pharaonic system, where they took it, and what it cost to keep it running.

---

# Chapter 6 — The First Dynasty: Building the Pharaonic System

Eight kings in about 200 years. That is the First Dynasty — roughly 3050 to 2890 BCE, depending on which chronology you use. We have already met the first one, Hor-Aha, at the end of the last chapter. Now we are going to go through the rest of them, because the story of the First Dynasty is not just a story of who sat on the throne. It is a story of institutional construction. The unified state that Narmer and Hor-Aha created was a fragile thing — a military conquest held together by force and ceremony, with no infrastructure, no bureaucracy, and no guarantee that it would outlast the king who founded it. The First Dynasty is the period when the machinery of pharaonic Egypt was actually built: the royal titulary, the jubilee festivals, the provincial administration, the international trade routes, the legal and religious framework that would hold the state together for the next three thousand years. All of it gets assembled in these eight reigns, and it gets assembled by trial and error, with at least two serious crises along the way.

We know the kings by their serekh names. In order: **Hor-Aha**, **Djer**, **Djet**, **Merneith** (technically a queen regent, not a king, but she ruled and was buried as one), **Den**, **Anedjib**, **Semerkhet**, **Qa'a**. We know most of them from the same evidence: their tombs at Abydos, plus the ivory and wooden labels that were tied to grave goods inside the tombs, plus clay jar sealings stamped with their names. A few of them also left rock inscriptions at far-flung locations — Sinai, Nubia, the Eastern Desert — that tell us where they sent expeditions. The amount of information we have about any individual reign is still tiny by later standards, but it is enough to tell a story.

---

The most disturbing thing about the First Dynasty is the death toll.

When a king died, he was buried in a chamber cut into the desert at Abydos — a mudbrick-lined room, sometimes subdivided into multiple compartments, filled with grave goods and sealed with a sand-covered mound. Around the royal chamber, in neat rows, were **subsidiary graves**: smaller pits, each containing a single body, each apparently killed at the time of the king's funeral and buried alongside him to serve him in the afterlife.

This is **retainer sacrifice**. It is the organized killing of the king's household — servants, craftsmen, court women, dwarves, dogs, donkeys — at the moment of the king's death, so that his full court would accompany him into the next world. The practice existed in other early civilizations (the Royal Tombs of Ur in Mesopotamia, Mesopotamia Ch 4, had a similar but smaller tradition), and it is among the most brutal institutional facts of the early state. The people buried in the subsidiary graves did not volunteer. They were killed.

Under **Hor-Aha**, the first king of the dynasty, the tomb at Abydos had 36 subsidiary burials. Under **Djer**, the second king, the number exploded to **338**. Three hundred and thirty-eight men, women, and children killed at a single funeral. The bodies show no obvious wounds, which suggests poison or strangulation rather than cutting or clubbing. The subsidiary graves are uniform in size and layout, the bodies are laid out in the same contracted position as ordinary burials, and some of them have small grave goods of their own — beads, a copper tool, a jar. The whole thing was organized, systematic, and matter-of-fact. Nobody was trying to hide what they were doing.

After Djer, the numbers decline. **Djet** had about 174 subsidiary burials. **Den** had 136. **Qa'a**, the last king of the dynasty, had about 26. By the Second Dynasty, the practice had stopped entirely. The decline is gradual enough that it seems to reflect a real shift in ideology rather than a shortage of victims — the state had 200 years to decide that actually killing the royal household was a bad use of resources, and it eventually figured out substitutes. The earliest **servant figurines** — small carved or molded figures of humans, placed in the tomb to serve the dead — appear at the very end of the First Dynasty, and they grow more common through the Second and Third Dynasties. By the Middle Kingdom, the small figurines had become **shabtis** (shah-BTEEz): blue faience or stone statuettes inscribed with a spell that commanded them to answer for the dead when the gods called on the dead to do manual labor in the afterlife. The shabti is a magical proxy for a real human being. It replaced the retainer sacrifice so completely that later Egyptians seem to have forgotten the practice had ever existed.

Djer himself was later confused by Egyptian pilgrims with the god Osiris, and his tomb at Abydos was treated as the actual burial place of Osiris for much of the New Kingdom — one of the most famous pilgrimage sites in ancient Egypt, visited by thousands of pious Egyptians who had no idea that the 338 subsidiary graves they were walking over belonged to real people who had been killed to keep a king company.

---

The third king, **Djet** (sometimes written Wadj, or called the "Serpent King" after the cobra hieroglyph in his name), reigned relatively briefly but left behind one of the most beautiful objects of the early dynastic period: a limestone funerary stele found at Abydos, now in the Louvre, showing his serekh carved in crisp low relief — the Horus falcon perched on top, the cobra sign inside the palace-facade frame, the whole composition clean and spare and technically perfect. It is one of the earliest examples of the formal Egyptian relief-carving style that will dominate temple walls for the next three thousand years, and it is already fully mature. The conventions — profile view, flat background, hieroglyphic captioning, proportional scaling — are not being experimented with. They are being executed by someone who was trained in a workshop tradition that already knew exactly what it was doing.

Djet's brief reign is also when the First Dynasty's first real political crisis probably started. When Djet died, his son **Den** was still a child. Too young to rule. Too young to manage the rituals, the trade routes, the expeditions, the court. Someone had to run the country.

That someone was **Merneith**.

---

**Merneith** was Djet's wife and Den's mother, and she ruled Egypt as regent during her son's minority. She is the earliest woman in the documented history of the Nile Valley — and possibly in the documented history of the world — who can be demonstrated to have actually exercised sovereign political authority.

How do we know she ruled? Three pieces of evidence.

First: Merneith's name appears on contemporary king lists from the Abydos cemetery, in the same format and in the same position as the male kings. A clay seal impression found in the tomb of Qa'a, the last king of the First Dynasty, lists all the kings of the dynasty in order, and Merneith's name is included in the sequence between Djet and Den. If the scribes who made the seal had not considered her a ruler, they would not have put her there.

Second: Merneith was buried at Abydos in a tomb indistinguishable in size and plan from those of the male kings. It has subsidiary burials (about 40 — fewer than Djer's 338 but more than most later kings). It has the same kinds of grave goods: pottery, stone vessels, copper tools, ivory labels. Most royal women of the First Dynasty were buried in subsidiary tombs near their husbands. Merneith got her own royal-scale tomb in the main row. That does not happen unless the people who buried you regarded you as a king.

Third: a large mudbrick funerary enclosure, similar to the monumental cult buildings that other First Dynasty kings erected near their Abydos tombs, was built for Merneith. These enclosures (called **funerary enclosures** or sometimes "valley enclosures") were large rectangular walled courtyards, built on the desert plain near the cemetery, used for rituals associated with the dead king's ongoing cult. Building one for a queen regent is an extraordinary gesture of royal-scale commemoration.

Merneith's regency establishes a pattern that will recur throughout Egyptian history. When the male succession broke down — when a king died before his heir was old enough to rule — Egyptian tradition allowed a royal woman (usually the king's mother or principal wife) to govern as regent, with full royal authority, until the heir came of age. The pattern would produce Hatshepsut, Sobekneferu, Tausret, and (in a different mode) Cleopatra. It starts here, in the early First Dynasty, with a woman whose name we can read on a clay seal impression and whose authority we can infer from the size of her tomb.

---

Merneith's son, **Den**, was probably the most important king of the entire First Dynasty. He reigned for about 40 years — by far the longest reign of any First Dynasty king — and under him the machinery of the pharaonic state reached its first mature form.

What does "mature form" mean? It means the kit of institutions that we think of when we think of ancient Egyptian kingship was assembled and standardized during Den's reign. Specifically:

**The Two Ladies name.** Every Egyptian king had, by the time of the later dynasties, a set of five formal names, each associated with a different aspect of his authority. The oldest was the **Horus name**, written inside the serekh, proclaiming the king as the earthly embodiment of the falcon god. The second was the **nbty name** (pronounced something like "NEB-tee"), proclaiming the king under the protection of the **Two Ladies** — the vulture goddess **Nekhbet** of Upper Egyptian El-Kab and the cobra goddess **Wadjet** of Lower Egyptian Buto. (We met Wadjet and Buto in Chapter 2. The Two Ladies are the divine patrons of the two halves of the country, paired together to symbolize the unity of the state.) Den is the first king who is definitely attested with a nbty name alongside his Horus name. The nbty name added a second formal declaration — a second way of saying "I rule both halves" — to the royal titulary, and every king after Den would use it.

**The Heb Sed jubilee.** The **Heb Sed** was a royal renewal festival in which the king demonstrated his continuing fitness to rule by performing a series of rituals, the most dramatic of which was a ceremonial run between boundary stones. The festival was supposed to be celebrated first after 30 years on the throne, and then at shorter intervals thereafter. An ivory label from Den's tomb is the earliest depiction of the Heb Sed we have — it shows a figure (probably Den himself) running between two sets of markers, wearing the short jubilee kilt, accompanied by attendants and ritual objects. The Heb Sed would be celebrated by Egyptian kings for the next three thousand years, and it was so central to the ideology of divine kingship that pharaohs who hadn't reached their 30th year often celebrated it early, just to claim the renewal of authority it conferred.

**The smiting pose, formalized.** An ivory label from Den's tomb shows him raising a mace over a kneeling Asiatic enemy, with the caption "first time of smiting the East." This is the same smiting pose from the Painted Tomb (Chapter 3), the same one from the Narmer Palette (Chapter 5), now being depicted on a portable artifact attached to a specific product in a specific year. Den's version is the first to caption the image with a text identifying both the act and the target. The smiting pose would become the single most reproduced image in all of Egyptian art — every temple pylon, every obelisk base, every victory stele for the next three thousand years would feature some version of it. Den fixed the formula.

**Organized expeditions.** Den sent documented expeditions to the turquoise and copper mines in **Sinai** and left rock-cut inscriptions at Wadi Maghara — the earliest royal inscriptions in the Sinai peninsula. He conducted what his labels call the "first time of counting" — probably the first organized census of livestock and grain in the unified kingdom, the precursor of the biennial cattle counts that would be the basis of Egyptian taxation through the Old Kingdom. He fought campaigns against Asiatic peoples on the northeastern frontier, raided into Nubia, and maintained the trade relationship with **Byblos** (on the Lebanese coast) that supplied Egypt with the cedar wood it desperately needed for construction, shipbuilding, and coffins.

**The nome system.** By Den's reign the Egyptian state was divided into roughly 42 administrative units called **nomes** — 22 in Upper Egypt and 20 in Lower Egypt — each governed by a local official (later called a **nomarch**) who answered to the central government at Memphis. The nomes were probably not invented by the First Dynasty; they were more likely pre-existing tribal or chieftain territories that the unified state absorbed and standardized. But it was under Den that the nome system became a working administrative framework, with each nome required to deliver grain, labor, and recruits to the crown. The nome totems (the falcon of Nekhen, the hare of the Fifteenth Upper Egyptian Nome, the crossed arrows of Sais) appear on ivory labels from Den's reign, depicted as standards carried in royal processions — a visual reminder that the king ruled all 42 of them.

Den was, in other words, the first king of Egypt who looks like a pharaoh in the full sense. He had the titulary, the jubilee, the expedition system, the census, the provincial administration, the art conventions, and the building program. He did not just inherit a conquest. He organized it.

---

The end of the First Dynasty was less stable.

After Den, the throne passed to **Anedjib** (ah-NED-jib), whose reign appears to have been contested. His name was systematically erased from several monuments — an early form of **damnatio memoriae** (Latin for "condemnation of memory," the deliberate destruction of a dead person's name and image, usually by political enemies who wanted to erase them from history). The erasure suggests that Anedjib's legitimacy was disputed, possibly by a rival faction within the royal family or by officials who refused to recognize his authority. His tomb at Abydos is comparatively modest, with fewer subsidiary burials and less elaborate grave goods than his predecessors'. Something went wrong.

His successor, **Semerkhet** (seh-MER-ket), may have been an usurper. Semerkhet's name is missing from the Qa'a seal impression that lists the other First Dynasty kings, and some later king lists omit him entirely or add him with hesitation. He appears to have deliberately defaced Anedjib's monuments after taking power — the same damnatio memoriae that had been applied to Anedjib, now potentially directed by the man who overthrew him. If this reconstruction is correct, the late First Dynasty saw at least one violent succession: a king deposed and erased, his successor seizing the throne by force and being questioned by posterity.

The dynasty's final king, **Qa'a** (kah-AH), seems to have stabilized things. His tomb at Abydos was neatly built, with about 26 subsidiary burials and a rich collection of ivory and wooden labels that record festivals, Sinai expeditions, royal offerings, and a Heb Sed jubilee — the standard package of a king whose reign was running normally. Qa'a is important mostly for what happened after him: his successor, **Hetepsekhemwy**, moved the royal cemetery from Abydos to Saqqara and founded the Second Dynasty. But before we get there, one more piece of the First Dynasty picture needs to be set.

---

Across the river from Memphis, on the high desert plateau of **Saqqara** (sah-KAR-uh), the elite officials of the First Dynasty were building their own tombs — and some of those tombs were bigger than the kings' tombs at Abydos.

The First Dynasty **mastabas** (Arabic for "bench" — a flat-topped, sloping-sided rectangular tomb of mudbrick, the standard form of Egyptian elite burial before the pyramids) at North Saqqara are among the most impressive structures of the early dynastic period. They have elaborate niched facades (the same niched palace-facade design that decorated the serekh), multiple interior chambers filled with grave goods, sometimes subsidiary burials of their own, and footprints that in several cases exceed the footprint of the contemporary royal tomb at Abydos.

This has confused archaeologists for a century. Why were the king's officials being buried in tombs larger than the king's? Three theories compete. (1) The kings actually had *two* tombs — a ritual cenotaph (empty memorial tomb) at Abydos, the ancestral cult center, and a real working tomb at Saqqara, the capital's cemetery. (2) The Saqqara mastabas belonged to officials so powerful that they could outbuild the king in death, which tells us that the First Dynasty state was less centralized than it later became. (3) Some of the Saqqara mastabas are actually the real royal tombs, and the Abydos structures are the cenotaphs.

The question has never been fully resolved, and it may never be — the evidence is thin enough to support all three readings. What the Saqqara mastabas do tell us, unambiguously, is that the First Dynasty state had a wealthy official class capable of building massive mudbrick monuments, and that the Memphis-Saqqara area was already established as the administrative and funerary hub of the kingdom alongside the older ritual center at Abydos. The dual-site pattern — bureaucratic capital at Memphis, ancestral cult center at Abydos — would persist throughout the Old Kingdom.

The Saqqara mastabas are also architecturally important for another reason: they are the direct ancestors of the pyramids. The Step Pyramid of Djoser, the first pyramid in Egypt, was built at Saqqara about 200 years after the First Dynasty, and it was built by stacking six rectangular mastabas of decreasing size on top of one another to create a stepped monument. The architect, **Imhotep**, did not invent the idea from nothing. He was working in a cemetery full of mastabas, iterating on a form he could see all around him, scaling it up. The pyramid tradition begins with the mastaba tradition, and the mastaba tradition begins at Saqqara in the First Dynasty, in tombs built by officials of Hor-Aha, Djer, and Den.

---

One final thread before we leave the First Dynasty: the trade routes.

The First Dynasty established two long-distance trade relationships that would define Egyptian foreign commerce for the rest of the Bronze Age.

The first was with **Byblos** (BIB-loss), a port city on the coast of modern Lebanon. Egypt had almost no usable timber of its own — the Nile valley produced palm, acacia, and tamarisk, none of which was suitable for large construction, shipbuilding, or high-quality coffins. **Cedar of Lebanon** was the solution: tall, straight-grained, aromatic, and rot-resistant, available in large quantities from the forested slopes above Byblos. Egyptian ships crossed the eastern Mediterranean to Byblos, loaded up with cedar logs, and sailed back to the Delta ports. The trade was so important that by the Old Kingdom, Egyptians called any large seagoing ship a "Byblos boat," regardless of its actual destination. By the Middle Kingdom, two thousand years later, Byblos would become culturally semi-Egyptian — its kings using Egyptian titles and writing their names in hieroglyphs. In the First Dynasty, the relationship was purely commercial, but it was already indispensable.

The second was with **Sinai**. The southwestern tip of the Sinai peninsula, along the western escarpment, contained deposits of **turquoise** (a blue-green semiprecious stone prized for jewelry and inlays) and **copper ore** (essential for tools and weapons). First Dynasty kings sent armed expeditions across the desert from the eastern Delta to Sinai, mined the turquoise and copper, and brought them back. The expeditions left rock-cut inscriptions at **Wadi Maghara** (WAH-dee mah-GAR-uh) recording the king's name and the expedition commander's titles — Den, Semerkhet, and Anedjib are all attested here. These Sinai inscriptions are among the very earliest royal texts outside the Nile Valley, and they show the First Dynasty state projecting power and extracting resources from a desert frontier 150 miles (240 km) from the nearest Egyptian settlement.

The trade picture that emerges from the First Dynasty is a state that was already plugged into the broader Bronze Age world: cedar from Lebanon, copper and turquoise from Sinai, gold from the Eastern Desert, exotic goods from Nubia (ebony, ivory, incense, leopard skins), and wine and oil from the Levant. Egypt was not yet the empire it would become, but it was already a trade hub with reach, and the economic infrastructure that would finance the pyramids was being assembled.

The next chapter is about what happened when the system the First Dynasty built started to crack.

Cross-civ check: the eight kings of the First Dynasty (roughly 3050-2890 BCE) overlap with the tail end of the **Early Dynastic I** period in Mesopotamia, when the Sumerian city-states of Ur, Lagash, Nippur, and Kish were consolidating as independent kingdoms and starting to compete for regional dominance (Mesopotamia Ch 3). The two civilizations were not yet in direct political contact — there is no evidence of Egyptian embassies to Sumer or Sumerian embassies to Egypt in this period — but they were connected through the same trade networks that ran through the Levant and the Syrian interior. Lapis lazuli from Afghanistan still reached both ends of the Fertile Crescent. Cedar from Lebanon went east to Mesopotamia and south to Egypt. Ideas continued to flow. But the two civilizations were now developing on separate tracks, with different political structures, different writing systems, different religions, and different approaches to the problem of how to run a river-valley state. Egypt had one king, one river, one state. Mesopotamia had dozens of kings, a fan of rivers, and a permanent state of interurban competition. The two models would produce radically different political histories.

---

# Chapter 7 — The Second Dynasty Crisis

The Second Dynasty starts with a move and ends with a schism. In between, the pharaonic state that eight First Dynasty kings spent two centuries building came closer to shattering than it ever would again until the fall of the Old Kingdom. And the evidence for the whole crisis is so thin that the most important question — did Egypt actually split in two? — may never be answered.

Here is what we do know: a king named Peribsen replaced the Horus falcon on his royal serekh with the Seth animal. No Egyptian king had done that before. No Egyptian king would ever do it again. To understand why that single act of theology-as-politics mattered, we need to start at the beginning of the dynasty, when the cracks were still hairline-thin.

---

When **Hetepsekhemwy** (HEH-tep-SEH-kem-wee) came to power around 2890 BCE — either by succession from Qa'a or by a more violent transition that established a new ruling family — he did something none of the First Dynasty kings had done. He abandoned Abydos. The royal cemetery that had been the burial ground of every Thinite king since Scorpion I, the place where Hor-Aha and Djer and Den had been laid in the desert sand, the anchor of the Upper Egyptian ritual tradition — Hetepsekhemwy left it behind and was buried at Saqqara instead.

The move was as politically significant as anything in the Second Dynasty. Abydos had been the heart of Upper Egyptian identity since the late Naqada period. It was the cemetery of the kings who had unified Egypt. It was becoming the cult center of Osiris, the god of the afterlife. The fact that Hetepsekhemwy chose to be buried at Saqqara — the Memphite plateau, the capital city's own desert margin — says that the center of gravity of the Egyptian state had shifted permanently northward. Memphis was no longer just the administrative seat of a kingdom whose soul lived at Abydos. Memphis was the center, full stop.

Hetepsekhemwy's name tells the rest of the story. It means "**The Two Powers Are at Peace**" — a reference to Horus and Seth, the falcon god and the storm god, the divine patrons of (roughly) Upper and Lower Egypt respectively. In the normal course of things, an Egyptian king had a **Horus name** — his serekh was topped with the Horus falcon, identifying him as the earthly embodiment of Horus. Seth was the other pole of the divine balance: the wild, desert, chaotic counterpart to the orderly, fertile, civilized Horus. The mythology that later generations would tell says that Horus and Seth fought for the throne of Egypt after the death of Osiris, and that the gods decided in Horus's favor — which is why every king was the Horus king. But the mythology also insists that the conflict was resolved peacefully, with Seth given the desert and Horus given the fertile land.

Names that declare peace are usually chosen by people who have just ended a war. The strong implication is that the transition from the First Dynasty to the Second was not smooth — that the late First Dynasty turbulence around Anedjib and Semerkhet (Chapter 6) had not fully resolved, and that Hetepsekhemwy's name was a public announcement that the fighting was over and the country was reunified.

---

For a while, the announcement held. The third king of the dynasty, **Nynetjer** (nee-NET-jer), reigned for something like 35 to 40 years — long enough that the Palermo Stone (a later annal fragment recording early dynastic events in terse year-by-year entries) gives us our clearest picture of what a functioning Second Dynasty looked like from the inside.

The picture is monotonous in the best possible way. The main event Nynetjer's scribes recorded, year after year, was the **cattle count** (Egyptian *tnwt*, roughly "counting") — a biennial state inventory of all the cattle, sheep, goats, and other livestock in the kingdom, conducted every other year by officials dispatched from the central government. The count was both a tax assessment (the state took a percentage of the herd as revenue) and a dating system (years were not numbered sequentially but named after their most important event, and the cattle count was important enough to name a year after). An Egyptian scribe in Nynetjer's reign would not say "the fourteenth year of the king" — he would say "the seventh year of the count."

Regular cattle counts, biennial tax assessments, festivals celebrated on time, expeditions sent on schedule. Nynetjer celebrated a Heb Sed jubilee, held religious festivals at Memphis, and may have conducted military expeditions. The machinery the First Dynasty built was running exactly as designed — nome by nome, count by count, bread and beer flowing from the granaries. For four decades, the system worked.

And then the machinery broke.

---

After Nynetjer's long reign, the king list fractures. Names appear on some lists and not others. The later king-list traditions — Manetho's account, the Abydos king list, the Turin Canon — contradict each other in ways they almost never do for other periods, and Egyptian record-keepers were meticulous enough that their confusion is itself evidence of disruption. Rival kings may have ruled simultaneously from different parts of Egypt. The succession that Hetepsekhemwy's name had declared "at peace" was no longer at peace.

And then came **Peribsen** (PAIR-ib-sen), and the thing he did that no Egyptian king had done before.

---

This needs to be explained carefully, because it sounds like a minor religious detail and it was anything but.

The **Seth animal** is one of the strangest figures in Egyptian art. It has a long, downward-curving snout, tall rectangular ears, a stiff forked tail, and a lean body that doesn't quite match any real animal — scholars have proposed greyhound, aardvark, jackal, donkey, and okapi, among others, and none of them is a perfect fit. The most likely explanation is that the Seth animal was always a composite, a mythological creature deliberately designed to represent something that was not of this ordered world. Seth himself was the god of the desert, of storms, of chaos, of foreigners, of things that were dangerous and necessary but not civilized. He was not straightforwardly evil (that reading comes from much later Egyptian theology, and it is an oversimplification) — he was the indispensable other, the force that balanced Horus's order with chaos, the dry desert that surrounded the wet cultivated strip, the foreign lands that bordered Egypt. Every king from the very beginning had been identified with Horus. Seth was what the king was *not*.

And then Peribsen put Seth on his serekh.

The implications are enormous. By removing the Horus falcon and replacing it with the Seth animal, Peribsen was declaring himself as a king *of Seth* rather than a king of Horus. In the theological logic of the Egyptian state, this was not just a personal religious preference. It was a declaration about which god — which *half of the cosmic balance* — the king embodied. Since Horus represented Upper Egypt in much of the mythology and Seth represented Lower Egypt (or, in some versions, the desert lands versus the fertile lands), the change could mean that Peribsen was claiming legitimacy from a different power base than the Horus kings — possibly a Delta or northern faction that was asserting itself against the old Upper Egyptian ruling tradition.

This is one of those moments in ancient history where the evidence is too thin to support a confident narrative but too dramatic to ignore.

**Theory 1: Religious reform.** Peribsen was a sincere Seth devotee who wanted to elevate Seth above Horus in the royal cult, perhaps because he believed that the Horus-dominated theology had become corrupt or politically compromised. This theory treats the change as an internal theological dispute, like a Christian king switching from Catholicism to Protestantism.

**Theory 2: Civil war.** Egypt actually split during this period, with a Seth-named king (Peribsen) ruling Upper Egypt from the old Thinite/Abydos region and a Horus-named king (or kings) ruling Lower Egypt from the Memphis/Delta region. The Seth name was not a religious choice but a political one — Peribsen was declaring allegiance to the Upper Egyptian tradition against a rival northern dynasty. This theory is supported by the fact that Peribsen was buried at Abydos (the first king buried there since the First Dynasty — why go back to Abydos unless you had been pushed out of Saqqara?) and by the fact that his clay sealings are found almost exclusively in Upper Egypt.

**Theory 3: Compromise.** Peribsen adopted the Seth name as part of a negotiated settlement between northern and southern factions. The Horus kings were ruling from one capital and the Seth faction from another, and Peribsen's name was a deliberate signal that the power-sharing was working. This theory has the advantage of explaining why Peribsen's name was not erased from the king lists after his death (a Seth-named king who was simply a heretic or an enemy would normally have been damnatio'd by his successors, but Peribsen's name survives intact).

None of these theories can be proven with the evidence we have. But they all point to the same conclusion: the Late Second Dynasty experienced a genuine political-religious crisis — the most serious internal rupture Egypt had faced since the unification, 200 years earlier. The two-century-old system that Narmer built, that Den refined, that Nynetjer ran like clockwork — it cracked along the oldest fault line in Egyptian civilization, the one between the falcon and the storm god, between the fertile land and the desert, between order and the thing that order exists to contain.

Somebody had to put the serekh back together. That somebody arrived in the next generation. His name was **Khasekhemwy**, and his serekh has both Horus and Seth on top of it — the only serekh in all of Egyptian history with both divine patrons perched together on the palace-facade frame.

Cross-civ check: while Egypt was tearing itself apart over Horus and Seth, the Sumerian city-states of southern Mesopotamia were engaged in their own long-running internal conflicts — the wars between **Kish** and **Uruk** and **Ur** that would define Mesopotamian politics for the next several centuries (Mesopotamia Ch 4). In the Indus Valley, the transition from scattered farming villages to the first planned urban centers was just beginning — **Harappa** and **Mohenjo-daro** were not yet cities, but the villages in the Indus floodplain were growing, standardizing their pottery, and starting to experiment with the brick-and-drainage infrastructure that would define the mature Indus civilization a few centuries later (Indus Valley Ch 2). In the Yellow River valley of China, the Longshan culture was producing wheel-made black pottery and building the first rammed-earth walls around its settlements (Ancient China Ch 2). All four of the major Bronze Age civilizations were now in the same broad phase of political consolidation — the messy, contentious, sometimes violent process of turning village-level societies into state-level societies. Egypt just happened to be the one doing it most visibly, with a serekh-swap dramatic enough to show up in the archaeological record four thousand years later.

---

# Chapter 8 — Khasekhemwy and the Eve of the Pyramid Age

The man who saved the Egyptian state was named, originally, **Khasekhem** — "The Powerful One Appears." At some point during his reign, after he had accomplished the thing that needed accomplishing, he changed his name to **Khasekhemwy** — "The *Two* Powerful Ones Appear." The difference is a single hieroglyphic sign. The extra sign changes "powerful one" (singular) to "powerful ones" (dual). The man who started with one god ended with two.

Khasekhemwy was the last king of the Second Dynasty, ruling from about 2700 to 2686 BCE. He inherited a kingdom in crisis — the Horus-Seth schism described in the last chapter, with Peribsen's Seth-serekh hanging over the political landscape and the unity of Egypt in genuine question. By the time he died, the kingdom was restored, the schism was resolved, the dual divine patronage of Horus and Seth was inscribed on his royal monuments as a permanent fact, and his son (probably **Djoser**) was about to build the first pyramid in the history of the world. Khasekhemwy is the hinge. Without him, there is no Third Dynasty, no Step Pyramid, no Old Kingdom, no pyramids at Giza. The chain of events that produces the most famous monuments in human history runs through a king whose name most people have never heard.

---

We know more about Khasekhemwy than about any other Second Dynasty king, partly because he left more evidence and partly because his evidence is spectacular.

The most striking piece of evidence is a pair of **statues** found at Hierakonpolis in the temple deposit — the same deposit that yielded the Narmer Palette and the Scorpion Macehead. The statues show Khasekhemwy seated on a throne, wearing the White Crown of Upper Egypt, his face rendered with an unusual degree of individuality for this early in Egyptian art. His eyes are deeply set, his cheeks are broad, his expression is calm and fixed. He is not an idealized generic king. He is a specific man, carved by an artist who had looked at him.

The base of each statue carries an inscription: carved around the throne pedestal, in low relief, are figures of fallen enemies — sprawled bodies, piled corpses, broken limbs. Beside the figures are numbers. The most commonly read number is **47,209**.

Forty-seven thousand two hundred and nine dead northerners.

The number is probably exaggerated — ancient Near Eastern kings exaggerated their kill counts the way fishermen exaggerate their catches, and a body count of 47,209 in an era when the total population of Egypt was likely under two million implies a campaign of almost inconceivable brutality. But the political message is precise: Khasekhemwy fought a war against the north, and he won it decisively, and he wanted everyone who entered the temple at Hierakonpolis to know exactly how decisively he had won it. The enemy figures on the base of the statues are identified by their postures and attributes as people of the Delta — Lower Egyptians. The war of reunification was, like Narmer's unification 400 years earlier, a war of Upper Egypt against Lower Egypt. The country had to be re-unified by force.

Whether Khasekhemwy was fighting against a rival Horus king in the north, or against Peribsen's surviving faction, or against some combination of breakaway Delta chieftains who had taken advantage of the schism to go independent — we cannot say. The archaeological evidence tells us only that the conflict was large enough to warrant a body count in the tens of thousands and important enough that the king commemorated it with the most expensive art form available to him (stone statuary, which in this period was rare, technically demanding, and exclusively royal).

---

The resolution came in the name change.

After the victory — whether immediately after or after a period of consolidation — Khasekhem added the dual sign to his name and became **Khasekhemwy**. At the same time, he did something to his serekh that had never been done before and would never be done again. He put both gods on top.

The serekh of every Egyptian king before Peribsen had a Horus falcon on top. Peribsen replaced the falcon with the Seth animal. Khasekhemwy put **both** the falcon and the Seth animal on the serekh, facing each other, symmetrically balanced above the palace-facade frame. Below the two gods, an additional inscription was added: "The Two Lords are at peace in him." The whole device — the doubled name, the doubled gods, the explicit declaration of peace — is a piece of political theology so compact and so deliberate that it reads like a constitutional amendment in pictographic form.

What Khasekhemwy was announcing was not just that he had won the war. He was announcing that the fundamental structure of the Egyptian state — the dual Horus-Seth balance, the Upper-Lower partnership, the reconciliation of order and chaos within the person of the king — had been restored. Peribsen had broken the balance by choosing Seth alone. Khasekhemwy restored it by declaring both gods as co-patrons. The message was aimed not at the defeated northerners (who had already been killed in sufficient numbers to fill a statue base) but at the Egyptian elite, the priests, the nomarchs, the administrators who needed to be convinced that the crisis was really over and that the state was functioning again. "The Two Lords are at peace in him" means: you can go back to work.

It worked. After Khasekhemwy, the Horus-Seth crisis vanishes from the Egyptian record. No later king uses a Seth serekh. No later king needs to put both gods on his frame. The balance was settled, and it held. Seth would continue to be an important god in the Egyptian pantheon — he was the god who stood on the bow of the solar bark and fought the chaos serpent Apophis every night to protect the sun's journey through the underworld — but he was never again the primary patron of a sitting king. The Horus kingship that Narmer had inaugurated was restored, permanently.

---

Khasekhemwy's tomb at Abydos is the last piece of his story, and it is worth pausing on because it is the largest and most impressive royal burial of the entire Early Dynastic period.

It sits in the Abydos cemetery, in the same stretch of desert that had held the First Dynasty kings. Khasekhemwy went back to Abydos — the last king who ever would, as it turned out. His tomb is a massive stone-lined structure, roughly 223 feet (68 meters) long, with a central burial chamber of limestone (the earliest known stone-built royal chamber in Egypt) and multiple side rooms. The side rooms were filled with hundreds of stone vessels — alabaster, diorite, basalt — and copper tools, pottery, and food offerings. There are no subsidiary burials. The retainer sacrifice tradition that had peaked under Djer and declined through the rest of the First Dynasty was now fully extinct. The king was going to the afterlife alone, accompanied by objects and offerings rather than murdered servants.

The tomb's stone construction is the most architecturally significant fact about it. All previous royal tombs in Egypt — every tomb at Abydos, every mastaba at Saqqara — had been built of mudbrick. Mudbrick is cheap, fast, and easy to work, but it is also weak, permeable, and impermanent. Stone is expensive, slow, and difficult to work, but it is permanent. The decision to build a royal burial chamber out of fitted limestone blocks is a technological break — the first time anyone in Egypt had used cut stone for a load-bearing structure in a royal context. (The **Gisr el-Mudir** enclosure at Saqqara, dating to roughly the same period, also uses crude stonework and may have been slightly earlier, but it is an enclosure wall rather than a roofed chamber — the engineering demands are different.)

Khasekhemwy's tomb is the prototype of what came next. When **Djoser** — almost certainly Khasekhemwy's son, through his queen **Nimaathap** — ascended the throne as the first king of the Third Dynasty, he and his architect **Imhotep** built the **Step Pyramid** at Saqqara: a stack of six mastabas in dressed limestone, the first pyramid in the history of the world, surrounded by a complex of stone-built temples, courtyards, and ceremonial buildings that was larger than anything previously attempted in any material. The Step Pyramid is conventionally treated as the start of the Old Kingdom — the beginning of the age of pyramids, the golden age of pharaonic Egypt, the era when the Egyptian state deployed its full resources in the construction of monuments so enormous that they have defined the country's image for the rest of human history.

Every pyramid built after Djoser's — the pyramids at Meidum, at Dahshur, at Giza — was an evolution of the step-pyramid concept, itself an evolution of the mastaba concept, itself a refinement of the mudbrick burial chambers at Abydos where Hor-Aha and Djer and Den had been buried two hundred years earlier. The architectural lineage runs in a straight line from Khasekhemwy's stone chamber to the Great Pyramid of Khufu. Without the experimental stone construction at Abydos, there is no Step Pyramid. Without the Step Pyramid, there is no Giza. The First and Second Dynasties are not just a prelude to the Old Kingdom. They are the Old Kingdom's laboratory.

---

Khasekhemwy's queen, **Nimaathap**, is the dynastic bridge.

Nimaathap was not a ruling queen in the manner of Merneith. She was Khasekhemwy's principal wife and the mother of his heir, and her political importance is entirely biological: she carried the royal bloodline from the Second Dynasty into the Third. But her cult was maintained for generations after her death, and sealings from Djoser's reign call her "Mother of the King's Children" and "King's Mother." She was honored as an ancestress, and offerings continued in her name at Abydos long after the pyramid builders had moved the center of royal activity permanently to Memphis and Saqqara.

The Nimaathap connection is the one thread of human continuity that ties the Abydos kings to the pyramid builders. Khasekhemwy dies at Abydos. His wife and son are alive. His son moves to Saqqara, commissions the Step Pyramid, and launches the age of stone architecture that defines Egypt for the next millennium. The gap between the mudbrick-and-sand world of the Early Dynastic period and the cut-limestone world of the Old Kingdom is bridged, in the end, by a single generation — a mother and a son.

---

So what did the preceding 2,300 years produce?

Starting from the cattle herders of the dying Sahara, working through the Fayum grain pits and the Badarian black-topped pottery and the Naqada sequence and the Narmer Palette and the eight kings of the First Dynasty and the Seth crisis of the Second, the story that ends with Khasekhemwy's stone tomb at Abydos is the story of a civilization assembling itself from scratch. Every piece of the pharaonic toolkit — the writing, the religion, the art, the architecture, the administration, the trade routes, the farming, the mining, the military tradition, the divine kingship — was invented, tested, refined, and codified during the predynastic and early dynastic periods. The Old Kingdom that follows is spectacular, but it is spectacular because it inherited a complete and working civilization from the centuries that came before it.

The Saharan refugees brought the cattle and the religion. The Badarian farmers brought the pottery and the linen and the copper. The Naqada kings brought the hierarchy and the art and the first experiments with writing. Narmer brought the unification and the crowns. Den brought the administrative machinery. Khasekhemwy brought the stone.

And Djoser, inheriting all of it, stacked six mastabas on top of each other at Saqqara and produced the first man-made mountain in history.

That is the next book in this series. This one ends here, at the edge of the desert at Abydos, at a stone-lined tomb 223 feet long, where the last king of the Early Dynastic period lies with his copper tools and his alabaster vessels and his 47,209 dead — and behind him, the foundation that the pyramids will rest on.

Cross-civ final check: Khasekhemwy dies around 2686 BCE. At the same moment: the Sumerian city of **Ur** is about to enter its Early Dynastic III phase, with the Royal Tombs of Ur (Mesopotamia Ch 4) still a few centuries in the future — the Mesopotamian equivalent of the Abydos retainer sacrifices, at larger scale and with more gold. The **Indus Valley** civilization is entering its mature urban phase — Harappa and Mohenjo-daro are now fully planned cities with grid streets, fired-brick buildings, and the most sophisticated urban drainage system in the ancient world (Indus Valley Ch 3). **Ancient China** is still in its Longshan phase, a century or two away from the legendary Xia and the first historical Shang dynasty (Ancient China Ch 3). In the **Andes**, the city of **Caral** on the coast of Peru is a contemporary — one of the oldest urban centers in the Americas, with sunken circular plazas and monumental adobe platforms, built by a cotton-and-anchovy economy that had no pottery and no gold (Early Andean Ch 2). The world's early civilizations are all now in motion at the same time, all within a few centuries of each other, all working out the same basic problem of how to turn agricultural surplus into organized states. The Egyptian answer — one river, one king, one eternal stone tomb — was not the only answer. But it was one of the most durable. The pyramids that Djoser built are still standing.

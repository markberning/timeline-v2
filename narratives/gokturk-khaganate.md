# Göktürk Khaganate — The First People to Call Themselves Turk

**Goal:** Narrative for the Göktürk (or Türk) Khaganate, 552–744 CE — the steppe empire that gave the Turkic peoples their name. In two centuries the Ashina clan went from being Rouran ironworking vassals in the Altai Mountains to ruling the largest empire the steppe had yet seen, played the great-power game across all of Eurasia from Constantinople to Chang'an, got crushed by Tang Taizong, came back from the dead under Bilge Kagan, and finally collapsed in 744 — at which point the Turkic peoples scattered across Eurasia and produced, downstream, the Khazars on the Volga, the Bulgars on the Danube, the Seljuks in Iran, and the Ottomans in Anatolia. Every modern Turk and every Turkic country today traces its name to this dynasty.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

A note on names: the dynasty is variously called Göktürk (GERK-tewrk, "Sky Turk" or "Celestial Turk"), Türk (TEWRK), Kök Türk, or Türük in different scholarly traditions. The Old Turkic word the people used for themselves was *Türük* (TUR-uk); the *Gök* prefix ("blue" or "celestial") may have been added by later writers. This narrative uses "Türk" or "Gokturk" interchangeably and reserves "Turk" with no umlaut for the modern Turkic-speaking peoples in general. Chinese sources call them *Tujue* (TOO-jweh).

A note on pronunciations: Türk titles and personal names follow conventions inherited from Inner Asian Turkology. Approximate pronunciations are given on first use. Where a name has both a Türk form and a Chinese-source form (e.g., Ilterish / Kutlugh / Gudulu), the most-used English form is preferred.

A note on "first" claims: this TL is full of them — first people to call themselves Turk, oldest surviving Turkic-language text, first steppe empire to send embassies to both Persia and Byzantium. As with every TL in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of." The Türks were almost certainly not the first speakers of a Turkic language — the language family is older than the political ethnonym — but they were the first to organize a state under that name.

# Chapter 1 — The Steppe Inheritance

The dynasty that would name an entire language family started as the blacksmiths of somebody else's empire.

In the early sixth century, the Mongolian steppe was ruled by the **Rouran Khaganate** (ROW-ran, also spelled Juan-juan in older Chinese sources). The Rouran were the latest in a long line of nomadic empires that had occupied the same Mongolian heartland — first the **Xiongnu** (SHONG-noo, the original steppe superpower of 200 BCE to 90 CE, Xiongnu & Huns Ch 2), then the **Xianbei** (shee-EN-bay, who emerged from the Donghu remnants the Xiongnu had absorbed and dominated the steppe from roughly 100 to 350 CE), then the Rouran from about 402 to 552. Each empire inherited and refined the political template the Xiongnu had pioneered: a supreme ruler at the top, the steppe divided into eastern and western wings, a decimal military organization that could mobilize every adult male for war on short notice, and an annual tribal assembly where the ruler reviewed his troops and renewed his subjects' loyalty.

The Rouran were the first steppe rulers to take the title **khagan** (KHAH-gahn, also spelled qaghan, the great khan title that would later be claimed by Genghis Khan and every Mongol emperor after him, Mongol Empire Ch 2). The Xiongnu chanyu had been the supreme ruler of his confederation, but "chanyu" was a title specific to one steppe tradition. "Khagan" became the universal Inner Asian imperial title, and it was the Rouran who introduced it. By the time the Türks adopted it in 552, it carried over a century of legitimating weight.

Somewhere within the Rouran political world, in the southern **Altai Mountains** (al-TY, the high range that runs along the modern Russia-Mongolia-Kazakhstan-China four-corner area, separating the Mongolian steppe from Central Asia proper), there lived a clan called the **Ashina** (ah-SHEE-nah, meaning "Noble Wolf" in proto-Turkic). The Ashina were Rouran subjects, and their economic specialty was iron-working. They mined and smelted iron in the Altai, then paid annual tribute in finished iron goods to their Rouran overlords. Chinese chroniclers describing the Türk founding fifty years later refer to the Ashina specifically as the Rouran's "blacksmiths" — a caste of specialist artisans embedded within the larger nomadic political system.

The detail matters because iron-working in the sixth century was a high-value technology. The Ashina were not just any subject tribe; they were the ones who made the lance heads, the arrowheads, the stirrups, and the swords that the Rouran cavalry used to dominate the steppe. They also developed something that would prove decisive over the next several centuries: the **iron stirrup**, made standard equipment in their cavalry units. The stirrup gave a mounted warrior a stable platform from which to shoot a composite bow or wield a long lance, multiplying his combat effectiveness. The Türks were probably not the absolute inventors of the iron stirrup (the Xianbei may have used wooden or leather prototypes earlier), but they were the first steppe people to make iron stirrups standard issue — and within a few centuries, via Türk contact with Byzantium, the technology spread westward and transformed medieval European warfare.

---

A note before we go further. The reader is now arriving at the third major nomadic empire in this app's roster after the Scythians and the Xiongnu. There is going to be some shared vocabulary, and trying to re-explain everything would be tedious. So: the **Eurasian steppe** is the long belt of grassland stretching from Hungary in the west to Manchuria in the east, about 5,000 miles (8,000 km) end to end, too dry for forest and too wet for desert (Xiongnu & Huns Ch 1 covers the geography in detail). **Pastoral nomads** living on the steppe practice a way of life built around herding sheep, goats, cattle, and above all horses across seasonal circuits, living in round felt tents (called **ger** in Mongolian, **yurt** in Turkic), measuring wealth in livestock, and organizing socially around kinship and tribal bonds. The **composite bow**, made of horn, sinew, and wood glued together over months of work, was the universal weapon of the steppe — accurate at over 200 yards, fired from horseback at full gallop. **Tengri** (TEHNG-ree) was the eternal blue sky god worshipped by all the steppe peoples we have met so far and most of the ones we will meet, including the Türks and the later Mongols.

What was new with each successive steppe empire was political organization, not material culture. The Xiongnu invented the steppe political template; the Xianbei and Rouran refined it; the Türks took it to a new geographic scale; the Mongols (Mongol Empire Ch 2) eventually pushed it to its absolute limit.

---

The Türk founding myth, preserved by Chinese chroniclers who recorded Türk oral tradition in the seventh century, has the structure of the great steppe origin stories. There are actually two versions, and Chinese sources preserve both.

In the first version, the Ashina were the only survivors of a massacred tribe. Enemies attacked their village and killed everyone except a small boy whose hands and feet were cut off and who was thrown into a swamp to die. A she-wolf found him, fed him on meat, and eventually nursed him back to strength. When the original enemies returned years later to finish the job, the wolf carried the boy to a cave in the mountains, where she gave birth to ten boys. These ten boys were the founding ancestors of the ten Türk clans, and the line of one of them — named **Asena** (ah-SEH-nah, "noble wolf") — became the Ashina royal house.

In the second version, the wolf is the boy's actual mother by descent, not just a foster nurse. A noble Türk family of the high Altai had a daughter who descended from a wolf goddess; she gave birth to a litter of ten sons, half-human and half-wolf, who became the ten Türk clans. The wolf-headed standards that the Türk khagans carried into battle — gold-mounted standards topped with the head of a wolf — were the dynastic emblem that referred back to this myth, and the symbol persisted for centuries. Modern Turkish nationalism has revived the wolf as a national symbol; the **Bozkurt** ("Grey Wolf") iconography popular in modern Turkey traces back, through long historical detours, to the Ashina founding myth.

Cross-civ check: founding myths involving suckling wolves are not unique to the Türks. The Romans had **Romulus and Remus** (the legendary twin founders of Rome, suckled by a she-wolf after being abandoned in a basket on the Tiber, Ancient Rome Ch 1) — a story that almost certainly does not derive from Türk tradition (Rome's founding myth was firmly fixed by the third century BCE, eight centuries before the Türks emerged) but does suggest that wolves carry similar associations of fierceness and survival in many cultures. The structural parallel is striking: a bloodline of warriors descended from wolves, founding an imperial dynasty that comes to dominate its known world. The Romans made this story matter; the Türks made it matter; and in both cases the wolf-suckled-child motif provided a usable myth for political legitimacy.

---

Whether the wolf was metaphor or memory, the Ashina were a real clan with a real political ambition. By the early sixth century, under a leader named **Tuwu** (TOO-woo, also called Asianshad in Chinese sources) and then his son **Bumin** (BOO-meen, sometimes written Tumen), they had built up enough warriors and enough wealth to start playing politics within the Rouran khaganate. They married into other tribes, formed alliances with neighboring clans, and slowly accumulated a personal following large enough to threaten their overlords.

The break came over a marriage. Around 545, Bumin sent an envoy to the **Western Wei** (WAY) court — one of the divided Chinese states that had emerged after the collapse of the Han successor empires (and that the Six Dynasties Ch 7 covers in detail). The Western Wei rulers were members of the **Tuoba** (TWAW-bah) royal house, themselves of Xianbei steppe origin and culturally interested in maintaining good relations with their nomadic cousins. The Western Wei welcomed Bumin's envoy, exchanged gifts, and opened formal diplomatic relations.

For the Rouran, this was insubordination. Their Ashina vassals were not supposed to conduct independent foreign policy. The Rouran khagan **Anagui** (ah-nah-GWAY) sent a furious message to Bumin: he was a "blacksmith slave" (Chinese sources preserve the phrase) who should know his place. Bumin's response was to escalate. In 551, he requested a Rouran princess in marriage. Anagui rejected the request as another insult.

Bumin then turned to Western Wei and asked for a **Wei princess** instead. The Wei court agreed. The marriage went ahead in 551. Bumin now had a Chinese imperial princess as his wife (which gave him semi-imperial status by Chinese diplomatic convention) and a formal alliance with one of the wealthier Chinese states. He had also given the Rouran a public, deliberate provocation that demanded a response.

When the Rouran did not respond fast enough, Bumin attacked. In the spring of 552, he led the Ashina cavalry and their allies in a surprise assault on the Rouran field army on the Mongolian steppe. The Rouran were routed. Anagui committed suicide. The surviving Rouran fled west — and may, depending on which scholarly hypothesis you favor, have been the same people who appeared a few years later on the Hungarian plain as the **Avars** (AH-vars), the new steppe terror of the Byzantine Empire (Byzantine Empire Ch 4 and Ch 5). The Rouran-Avar identification is debated, but the timing and direction of the migration fit. If the identification holds, the Türk founding had a downstream consequence in central Europe within a single decade: the Rouran refugees built another steppe empire on the Hungarian plain and lasted there for two centuries.

Within a few months Bumin had taken the imperial title **qaghan** (the same title the Rouran had used) and proclaimed the founding of the **Türk Khaganate**. He was the first political leader in history known to have called himself and his people *Türk* — the ethnonym that, downstream, would be carried by every modern Turkic-speaker from Istanbul to Xinjiang.

---

Then Bumin died.

The new khaganate was a few months old. The Rouran were destroyed but the political order on the steppe was wide open. Bumin's eldest son, **Qara Issig Qaghan** (KAH-rah ee-SIG, also called Kelo or Issik Qaghan), inherited the throne and almost immediately died as well. The succession passed to a younger son, **Muqan Qaghan** (MOO-kahn), who proved to be one of the most effective rulers of the entire dynasty. Muqan would consolidate the eastern half of the empire across the next two decades.

Meanwhile, Bumin's brother **Istemi** (ees-TEH-mee, sometimes spelled Sizabul or Istami) was already pushing west. Istemi had been delegated control of the western wing during Bumin's brief reign and held the title **yabghu** (YAH-boo, the standard subordinate-khan title that designated the second-in-command of a steppe empire). Istemi's career would shape everything that followed: he turned the Türk Khaganate from a steppe-only power into a Eurasian power, allying with Sasanian Persia to crush the Hephthalites, opening direct contact with Byzantine Constantinople, and effectively founding the **Western Türk Khaganate** that his descendants would rule independently after the great split of 582.

Two brothers, two halves of an empire. The dual structure was inherited from Xiongnu practice (left and right wings under a single supreme ruler, Xiongnu & Huns Ch 2), but the Türks took it further than the Xiongnu had — the western yabghu was given near-equal authority over his territory, with his own court, his own diplomatic relations, and his own military campaigns. The system worked while one royal lineage held both halves of the empire and the senior khagan's authority was respected in both. It would become catastrophic as soon as rival branches of the Ashina dynasty contested the succession — which is what happened, on schedule, thirty years later (Chapter 5).

---

Cross-civ check: the Türk founding in 552 is almost exactly contemporary with the **Justinian** era in Byzantium (the great Byzantine emperor who reconquered Italy and North Africa, codified Roman law, and built the Hagia Sophia — Byzantine Empire Ch 3). Justinian's general Belisarius was fighting the Ostrogoths in Italy in the same years that Bumin was destroying the Rouran in Mongolia. The **Sasanian Empire** under Khusrau I was at the height of its power in Persia (Persian Empire Ch 9), and Khusrau would become the Türks' ally and then enemy within a few years. Northern China was divided into competing kingdoms — the **Northern Zhou** and **Northern Qi** — that would soon be reunified by the Sui Dynasty (Six Dynasties Ch 8). On the Indian subcontinent, the **Gupta Empire** (Gupta Empire Ch 8) was in its final decline phase. And the **Mayan civilization** (Maya Civilization Ch 5) was at its classical peak in Mesoamerica, simultaneous with everything happening in Eurasia and entirely unconnected to it. The mid-sixth century was a turning point on multiple continents simultaneously — and the Türk founding was one of the more consequential threads in the global pattern.

# Chapter 2 — Bumin Qaghan and the Founding

The new Türk state in 552 was a few thousand Ashina cavalry, a recently-acquired Chinese princess, the still-warm corpses of the Rouran ruling family, and a very large open question: now what?

Within thirty years the answer would be: an empire stretching 4,500 miles (7,200 km) from Manchuria to the Caspian Sea, larger than the Roman and Sasanian empires combined at their peaks, with embassies traveling to and from Constantinople and Chang'an, controlling the entire central Silk Road, and extracting 100,000 bolts of silk per year in tribute from the divided Chinese north. How the Türks got from one to the other in a single generation is the political miracle of this chapter.

---

The geographic heart of the new khaganate was the **Orkhon Valley** (OR-khon), a broad upland river basin in central Mongolia about 270 miles (430 km) west of modern Ulaanbaatar. The Orkhon River runs north through a fertile, defensible valley sheltered by the **Khangai Mountains** (KHAN-guy) to the south and west and the **Khentii Mountains** (KHEN-tee) to the east. It has reliable water, good grass for horse herds, and access to all the major migration routes across the Mongolian steppe. Every later steppe empire would establish its political center in or near the Orkhon: the Uyghurs would build their capital of Karabalghasun there in the eighth century, and Genghis Khan would build **Karakorum** (KAR-ah-KOR-um, Mongol Empire Ch 3) at almost exactly the same site in the thirteenth century. The Türks did not build a permanent city — they remained nomadic — but the Orkhon was where the khagan's summer encampment stood, where the great annual assembly met, and where the Ashina royal cemetery was eventually established.

The Orkhon's special status had a religious dimension. Türk tradition held that the broader Khangai region included a sacred mountain called **Otuken** (OH-too-ken, exact location debated — somewhere in the Khangai or possibly the eastern Altai). Otuken was the spiritual center of the Türk world, the place where the khaganate had to be based if the Türks were to remain strong. Bilge Kagan two centuries later (Chapter 7) would invoke Otuken as a programmatic argument: stay in Otuken, send caravans to China for what you need, but do not let your court drift into China itself. The geography of legitimacy was as fixed as the geography of pasture.

---

The political system Bumin founded looked superficially like the Xiongnu and Rouran systems before it: a supreme khagan at the top, the empire divided into eastern (senior) and western (junior) wings, a decimal military organization, and an annual tribal assembly. Below this surface, the Türks added two innovations that mattered.

The first was the **dual kingship** in a stronger form than the Xiongnu had used. Where the Xiongnu wing-kings had been chanyu's lieutenants with limited independent authority, the Türk **yabghu** of the western wing was effectively a co-ruler with his own court, his own diplomatic relations, and his own armies. Istemi running the western half independently while Muqan ran the eastern half independently was not a violation of the Türk political structure but its intended design. The system maximized the empire's geographic reach: one khagan could focus on China while another focused on Persia and Byzantium, and neither's attention was divided between fronts thousands of miles apart.

The cost of this design was instability at succession. As long as one royal generation held both halves of the empire and senior-junior relations were clear, the system worked. As soon as cousins started disputing whose branch held seniority, the system broke down — which is precisely what happened in 582 (Chapter 5). The Türk dual kingship was, in retrospect, the empire's structural design flaw.

The second innovation was **direct diplomatic engagement with settled empires as equals**. The Xiongnu had treated Han China as their main diplomatic counterpart, demanding tribute and ignoring the world beyond. The Rouran had been similarly China-focused. The Türks under Bumin and Istemi treated *all* the major Eurasian powers — Northern Zhou, Northern Qi, the Sasanian Persians, the Byzantine Romans, the Hephthalites, the lesser kingdoms of Sogdiana — as parts of a single diplomatic chessboard on which the khaganate played for advantage. This was unprecedented in steppe history. It required the Türks to build a diplomatic apparatus capable of managing simultaneous relations with multiple major powers in multiple languages — and to find an intermediary class capable of handling the actual mechanics of long-distance commerce and embassy travel. That class was the **Sogdians** (SOG-dee-anz), and Chapter 3 is about them.

---

Bumin himself ruled for less than a year before dying in late 552, possibly of natural causes related to age (Chinese sources do not record his birth date but suggest he was already in middle age at the founding). His eldest son Qara Issig Qaghan succeeded him and also died quickly. The throne then passed to Bumin's third son, **Muqan Qaghan**, who would rule from 553 to 572 and consolidate everything Bumin had begun.

Muqan was the warrior-administrator who turned the Ashina founding into an empire. Within his first decade he conquered the **Khitan** (KEE-tan, a Para-Mongolic-speaking people in eastern Mongolia and Manchuria who would much later found the Liao Dynasty in 916 and rule northern China for two centuries). The Khitan had been Rouran subjects; under Muqan they became Türk subjects. Muqan also subjugated the smaller tribes of southern Siberia — the **Yenisei Kyrgyz** (YEN-ih-say KEER-giz, Türk-speaking forest people of the upper Yenisei basin who would much later, in 840, destroy the Uyghur Khaganate that succeeded the Türks), the **Tieli** (TYEH-lee, the loose confederation of Türk-speaking tribes in northern Mongolia and the trans-Baikal region from which the later Uyghurs descended), the **Kurykan** (KOOR-uh-kan, in the Lake Baikal area), and various other smaller groups. By 565 essentially the entire Mongolian steppe and its margins were under direct Türk rule.

Then Muqan turned south to deal with China.

---

The China that Muqan faced was not the unified Han Empire that the Xiongnu had dealt with. It was the divided China of the **Six Dynasties** period (Six Dynasties Ch 1), specifically the late stage of that division. The Yangtze valley was ruled by a sequence of southern dynasties (the Liang, then the Chen) of mostly Han Chinese ethnicity. The North China Plain was divided between two rival dynasties of Xianbei steppe-descended ruling families: the **Northern Zhou** in the west (capital at Chang'an, modern Xi'an) and the **Northern Qi** in the east (capital at Ye, in modern Hebei). Both Northern Zhou and Northern Qi were militarily strong, economically wealthy, and locked in chronic warfare with each other.

Muqan's strategy was to play them off. He maintained simultaneous diplomatic relations with both dynasties, accepted tribute from both, and threatened both with invasion if either one stopped paying. Neither dynasty could afford to alienate the Türks because doing so would invite a Türk-supported attack from its rival. The result was that both Northern Zhou and Northern Qi paid annual silk tribute to Muqan — Chinese sources record the figure at 100,000 bolts of silk per year from each state, which would make Muqan's combined silk revenue roughly **200,000 bolts per year** at the peak, the largest tribute payment China had made to the steppe since the Han dynasty's heqin treaties seven centuries earlier (Xiongnu & Huns Ch 3).

The silk did not stay in Muqan's treasury. It moved west, through Sogdian merchants, into the Persian and Byzantine markets — financing the Türk diplomatic apparatus, paying for foreign luxuries the steppe could not produce, and funding the western expansion that Istemi was simultaneously conducting. The Türk Empire's economic base was, in a sense, the silk that the divided Chinese paid them to stay away. When China unified under the Sui in 589 and stopped paying, the Türks would suddenly face a fiscal crisis — but that is fifty years in the future from Muqan's vantage point.

---

What Muqan and Istemi were building together by the late 560s was something genuinely new. Earlier steppe empires had been regional powers; the Türks were a continental power. Earlier steppe empires had used trade as a sideline to raiding; the Türks made trade a central instrument of state policy. Earlier steppe empires had spoken to settled neighbors in the language of tribute; the Türks spoke in the language of multi-power diplomacy.

The historian **Étienne de la Vaissière** (working primarily in the early 2000s on Sogdian merchant networks) has argued that the Türk-Sogdian symbiosis was the structural innovation that made all this possible. The Türks provided military protection and overland security; the Sogdians provided commercial expertise, language skills (Sogdian was the lingua franca of the Silk Road), diplomatic experience, and access to markets from China to Constantinople. Neither side could have built a Eurasian empire alone. Together, they did.

By 575 the Türk Khaganate stretched from Manchuria in the east to the Volga in the west — about 4,500 miles end to end, larger by a wide margin than any empire that had existed up to that point in history. The contemporary Roman/Byzantine Empire, even with Justinian's reconquests, was perhaps 2,500 miles east-west; the Sasanian Empire perhaps 2,000. The Türks had built the largest political unit yet seen on the Earth, in roughly twenty-five years from a standing start, and they had done it by combining steppe military power with Sogdian commercial sophistication.

What they had not done was build cities, write down laws, maintain centralized treasury records, or develop any administrative apparatus more elaborate than the chain of personal loyalty that ran from khagan to wing-yabghu to clan chieftain to individual warrior. The empire was held together by Bumin's direct descendants ruling the two wings in cooperation. As long as that cooperation held, the empire worked. When it broke, the empire would shatter.

---

Cross-civ check: by 575, the Türk Khaganate was the largest political unit in Eurasia. The **Byzantine Empire** under Justin II (Justinian's nephew and successor) was suffering from the financial overstretch that Justinian's reconquests had caused (Byzantine Empire Ch 4). The **Sasanian Empire** under Khusrau I was at its peak (Persian Empire Ch 9). The **Six Dynasties** period in China was about to end with the Sui reunification (589). The **Gupta Empire** in India had collapsed and been replaced by smaller successor states (Gupta Empire Ch 8). In Mesoamerica the **Mayan classical period** was at its height (Maya Civilization Ch 5). In the Andes the **Tiwanaku and Wari** states were emerging (Andean Kingdoms Ch 1). The Türk Khaganate did not directly affect the Americas, but it touched everything in Eurasia from the Pacific to the Mediterranean, and the next chapters will trace those connections.

# Chapter 3 — The Sogdian Connection

The Türks were the muscle. The Sogdians were the brains.

This is an oversimplification, but it captures something true. The Türk Khaganate could not have run a Eurasian empire on its own — the Ashina had no city-building tradition, no native script, no merchant class, no diplomatic apparatus, no experience managing long-distance trade. What it had was cavalry. The Sogdians had everything else. The partnership between the two — steppe warriors and oasis traders — is one of the most successful symbiotic political relationships in history, and it is why the Türk Empire reached as far as it did.

This chapter is about the Sogdians: who they were, how they operated, and what they contributed to the khaganate. It is also about why the Türk decision to delegate commerce and diplomacy to a culturally distinct subject population worked so well — and why the model would be picked up, in modified form, by every later steppe empire including the Mongols (Mongol Empire Ch 6).

---

The **Sogdians** were an Iranian-speaking people from the oases of **Transoxiana** (trans-ox-ee-AH-nah), the strip of irrigated land between the **Oxus River** (modern Amu Darya) and the **Jaxartes River** (modern Syr Darya), in what is now Uzbekistan and Tajikistan. Their language, called **Sogdian** (SOG-dee-an), was a Middle Iranian language related to Persian but as different from Persian as Spanish is from French — close enough that an educated speaker could probably puzzle out the other, far enough apart that real communication required translation.

This is a place to make the language-family point explicitly, because the chapter is about to introduce several different language families and the relationships matter. **Iranian languages** (Persian, Sogdian, Bactrian, Khotanese, Pashto, Kurdish, and many others) belong to the broader **Indo-European** family, the same family that includes Greek, Latin, Sanskrit, English, German, Russian, and most of the languages of Europe and northern India (Persian Empire Ch 1 covers the Iranian language family in detail). **Turkic languages** (Old Turkic, Uyghur, Karakhanid, Khazar, Bulgar, Pecheneg, Kazakh, Uzbek, Turkmen, Azerbaijani, Anatolian Turkish — and the modern languages of all these names) belong to a completely separate family, the **Turkic family**, with no proven ancestral relationship to Indo-European at all. **Mongolic languages** (Para-Mongolic, Khitan, Mongolian, Buryat, Kalmyk) are yet another separate family. Türkic, Mongolic, and the **Tungusic** languages (Manchu, Evenki, etc.) are sometimes grouped as the **Altaic** macro-family, but the Altaic hypothesis is contested and modern linguistics increasingly treats them as separate families that share many features through long contact rather than common descent.

So when Türk khagans spoke Old Turkic to their warriors and Sogdian to their merchants and (through interpreters) Chinese to envoys from Chang'an, they were operating across three completely unrelated language families. The Sogdians were the empire's interpreters in both directions. Their merchants spoke Sogdian among themselves, Persian for trade with the Sasanians, Greek for trade with Byzantium, Chinese for trade with the Sui and Tang, and several Indian languages for trade with the Gupta successor states. Sogdian colonies stretched from Constantinople to Chang'an, and Sogdian itself was the lingua franca of the Silk Road for several centuries.

---

The two great Sogdian cities were **Bukhara** (boo-KAH-rah) and **Samarkand** (SAM-er-kand), both in modern Uzbekistan. Both had been around since at least the sixth century BCE under Achaemenid Persian rule (Persian Empire Ch 2), survived Alexander the Great's conquest in the 320s BCE (Greek-influenced Bactrian and Hellenistic kingdoms ruled Sogdiana for several centuries thereafter), passed under various Iranian and steppe successor regimes, and were reasonably prosperous mercantile centers when the Türks arrived in the 560s. After the Türk conquest, both became key administrative centers of the Western Khaganate's commercial network — and after the Arab conquest of Transoxiana in the early 700s, both would become the great cities of the Islamic world that the Islamic Golden Age (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6) made famous: Bukhara as the Samanid capital, Samarkand as the Timurid capital.

The Sogdian merchant network extended far beyond Bukhara and Samarkand. Sogdian colonies — physical neighborhoods of Sogdian merchants and their families embedded inside foreign cities — existed at:

- **Chang'an** (chahng-AHN, the Sui and Tang capital in central China)
- **Luoyang** (LWAW-yahng, the eastern Chinese capital)
- **Dunhuang** (DOON-hwahng, the western frontier oasis where the Hexi Corridor met the Tarim Basin)
- **Turfan** (TUR-fan), **Kucha** (KOO-chah), **Khotan** (HOH-tan), **Kashgar** (KASH-gar), and the other Tarim Basin oasis cities
- **Suyab** (SOO-yab, in modern Kyrgyzstan, the western Türk court city)
- **Constantinople** (the Byzantine capital), where a Sogdian merchant community is documented in the seventh century
- Various oases and trading posts across what is now Iran, Afghanistan, Pakistan, and the lower Volga

This was a true merchant diaspora, comparable in scale and reach to the later **Sephardic Jewish** and **Armenian** networks (the Armenians of New Julfa, who served the Safavid Empire in much the same role, are the closest functional parallel; Safavid Persia Ch 6 covers them). Sogdian merchant families had branch offices in dozens of cities, intermarried across the network to maintain trust relationships, and operated something like a multinational enterprise eight hundred years before the Italian banking houses or the Dutch East India Company.

The mechanism is worth understanding. Long-distance overland trade in the sixth century was high-risk and high-cost. Caravans took months to cross the steppe and the deserts. Goods were vulnerable to bandits, sandstorms, river floods, and political instability. To make a profit, a merchant had to know which goods sold where, what the going prices were, who could be trusted to deliver, and how to avoid losing the entire investment to one bad encounter. The Sogdian solution was the family network: a Bukhara merchant sending silk to Chang'an did not need to make the trip himself — he sent it via a relative at Dunhuang, who passed it to another relative at Chang'an, with each step covered by family obligation and the ability to pursue claims against family members if things went wrong. The network was a credit system, an information system, and an insurance system simultaneously, all built on kinship.

The Türks plugged into this existing Sogdian network rather than trying to build their own commercial apparatus from scratch. The arrangement was straightforward: the Türks provided military protection (their cavalry could escort caravans, their khagans guaranteed safe passage across Türk-controlled territory, and their political weight could pressure foreign powers to protect Sogdian merchants in their cities), and in exchange the Sogdians gave the Türks access to commercial revenue, diplomatic services, and writing. Sogdian script, adapted from the Aramaic alphabet, became the Türks' first written language. The earliest surviving Türk documents are in Sogdian script. Only in the early eighth century, with the Orkhon Inscriptions (Chapter 7), did the Türks develop a custom script for their own language.

---

The political consequence of the Türk-Sogdian symbiosis was that the khaganate's interests aligned with the Silk Road's interests. The empire had every reason to keep the trade routes open, to protect merchants, to maintain peace with neighboring powers when peace was profitable, and to fight wars only when they served commercial as well as political ends. This is a markedly different incentive structure from the Xiongnu's, whose chronic conflict with Han China had been driven primarily by the steppe's need for Chinese grain, iron, and silk, with very little commercial sophistication on the Xiongnu side itself.

The Türk-Sogdian model also had a clear strategic implication: the empire needed to control the Silk Road's chokepoints and keep them open. The chokepoints in the central section were the **Hephthalite Empire** to the south (which we will deal with in Chapter 4) and the **Tarim Basin** oasis kingdoms to the east. By 565 the Türks had subjugated or allied with both. This put them in control of the entire central Silk Road from the Chinese frontier at Dunhuang to the Iranian frontier at the Oxus — and brought them into direct contact with the Sasanian Persians.

The Sasanian Persians, however, did not want to buy Türk-Sogdian silk. The Sasanian state operated its own silk industry under royal monopoly and saw the Sogdian silk as competition that would undercut Persian textile producers. When Sogdian silk merchants tried to enter the Persian market, the Sasanian government turned them away. Worse, when Istemi sent a Sogdian-led embassy to negotiate with Khusrau I in 568, the Sasanians poisoned the envoys.

This was a serious provocation. The Türks were not yet ready for war with Persia, but they needed an alternative outlet for the silk that was piling up in their treasury (most of it tribute from China). The solution they found was geographically audacious and diplomatically unprecedented: send an embassy directly to Constantinople, bypassing Persia entirely, and negotiate a deal with Byzantium to buy Sogdian silk in the Mediterranean market. The man who led this embassy was a Sogdian merchant named **Maniakh** (MAH-nee-akh), and his journey is the subject of the next chapter.

---

Cross-civ check: the Sogdian merchant network in the late sixth century is comparable to a few other historical commercial diasporas. The **Phoenicians** (Phoenicia Ch 4) had operated a similar Mediterranean-wide network 1,200 years earlier. The **Sephardic Jews** of medieval Spain and Mediterranean would build a similar network 700 years later. The **Armenian merchants of New Julfa** under Safavid Persia (Safavid Persia Ch 6) would do something extremely similar 1,000 years later, including the same "merchant diaspora as state-aligned commercial network" structure. The **Hanseatic League** of medieval northern Europe was structurally similar but more formally organized as a city-league. What is unusual about the Sogdians is the scale (Constantinople to Chang'an, larger than any of the other comparable networks at their respective peaks) and the closeness of integration with the patron empire — the Türk Empire would not have functioned diplomatically or commercially without the Sogdians, and the Sogdians achieved a level of political influence inside the khaganate that few other merchant diasporas have matched.

# Chapter 4 — Diplomatic Acrobatics

In the late 550s, the Türks did something no steppe empire had ever done: they crushed a major settled enemy by allying with another major settled enemy. Then, within ten years, they turned around and sent an embassy to a third major settled enemy on the opposite end of Eurasia, deliberately to outflank the original ally.

This is the Türk diplomatic style at its peak. Three of the four major Eurasian powers — the Sasanian Persians, the Byzantine Romans, and the Hephthalites — appear in this chapter, and the Türks managed simultaneous relations with all of them, alternately allying and fighting and negotiating with whichever was most useful at the moment. The fourth major power, China, was divided into Northern Zhou and Northern Qi (Chapter 2 covered Muqan's tribute extraction from both). The chapter shows how the Türk khaganate worked the entire Eurasian system at once — a level of strategic reach no nomadic empire before them had attempted.

---

The first move was the destruction of the **Hephthalites** (HEFF-thal-ites).

The Hephthalites, sometimes called the **White Huns** by Greek and Persian sources (the "Hun" label here is a Western convention; they were not necessarily related to the Huns of Attila or to the Xiongnu, though scholarly opinion remains divided on the question — see Xiongnu & Huns Ch 6 for the broader Xiongnu-Hun debate), were a steppe people who had built an empire across Central Asia from roughly 450 to 560 CE. At their peak they controlled Sogdiana, Bactria (modern Afghanistan), parts of northern India, and the Tarim Basin — exactly the territory the Sasanians and Türks both wanted. The Hephthalites had also humiliated the Sasanians in the late fifth century: in 484 they had killed Shah **Peroz I** in battle, captured the Persian camp, and forced the Sasanians to pay tribute for nearly forty years afterward (Persian Empire Ch 9). Persian humiliation at the hands of the Hephthalites was an open wound that any Sasanian shah would happily heal.

In the early 550s, the Türks began pushing west under Istemi's leadership. By 555 they had reached the borders of Hephthalite territory in the central steppe. By 557 they were ready to make the Hephthalites a strategic priority. Istemi made the obvious move: he proposed an alliance with the Sasanian Persians, the only other power motivated to crush the Hephthalites.

The Sasanian shah at this point was **Khusrau I** (KHUS-row, reigned 531-579), the most successful Sasanian king of the entire dynasty (Persian Empire Ch 9). Khusrau had reformed the Persian tax system, professionalized the army, and was looking for a way to settle accounts with the Hephthalites without committing the entire Persian military to a long northern campaign. Istemi's offer was perfect: the Türks would attack from the north (where the Hephthalites' steppe frontier was), the Persians would attack from the south (where the Hephthalites' Iranian frontier was), and the two armies would crush the enemy between them. Khusrau accepted. The alliance was sealed by a marriage: Khusrau married Istemi's daughter (or possibly granddaughter — sources differ), making the Persian-Türk alliance a kinship bond as well as a political one.

The campaign was decisive and quick. Between 557 and 560 the Türks and Persians executed simultaneous attacks on Hephthalite territory. The Hephthalite king **Gātfar** was defeated, killed, and his army shattered. The Hephthalite Empire ceased to exist within three years of the alliance being formed. The Persian-Türk border was set at the **Oxus River** — the Persians took everything south of the Oxus (modern Afghanistan, Bactria, parts of northern Pakistan), the Türks took everything north of the Oxus (Sogdiana, the Tian Shan, the steppes north of the Caspian).

This was a textbook example of how the steppe and the settled world could combine to crush a rival neither could destroy alone. The Persians had tried for a century to defeat the Hephthalites and failed. The Türks were a young empire without the diplomatic credibility to negotiate a Hephthalite surrender. Together they crushed the Hephthalites in three years. The pattern would recur — Han China and the Wusun against the Xiongnu (Xiongnu & Huns Ch 4), the Tang and the Türks against later steppe enemies, eventually the Mongols and various settled powers against Khwarezm (Mongol Empire Ch 4) — but the Türk-Sasanian alliance against the Hephthalites was the cleanest sixth-century example.

---

Within ten years, the alliance broke down — and the Türk response is one of the great strategic moves in pre-modern diplomatic history.

The break came over silk. The Türks now controlled the Silk Road from Dunhuang to the Caspian and had vast quantities of Chinese silk in their treasury (mostly Northern Zhou and Northern Qi tribute, Chapter 2). The Sogdian merchants who handled Türk commerce wanted to sell this silk in Persian markets. The Persians refused. The Sasanian state operated its own silk industry under royal monopoly, and Sogdian silk would undercut Persian producers. Khusrau closed the Persian market to Sogdian merchants, and when Istemi sent a Sogdian-led embassy to negotiate, Khusrau had the envoys poisoned.

For the Türks this was both an economic loss (the silk was worthless without buyers) and a personal insult (the embassy had been sent under Türk diplomatic protection). Open war with Persia was not yet feasible — the Türks were still consolidating their conquests and the Sasanian army was the best-equipped force in the Middle East. But there was an alternative: bypass Persia entirely and sell the silk to the Byzantine Empire on the western side of the Sasanian world.

The geography was daunting. The standard route from Sogdiana to Byzantium went through Persia, which was now closed. The alternative route ran north of the Caspian Sea, through what is now southern Russia and Ukraine — territory occupied by various semi-independent steppe peoples, most of whom were nominal Türk subjects but in practice operated independently. This was a much longer, much more dangerous route, with no established commercial infrastructure. No Sogdian merchant had ever made the trip.

In 568, Istemi sent a Sogdian merchant named **Maniakh** to attempt the trip. Maniakh's mission was to reach **Constantinople**, present himself to the Byzantine emperor **Justin II** (Justinian's nephew and successor, ruling from 565 to 578), and propose a direct Türk-Byzantine alliance against the common Sasanian enemy, with associated commercial privileges for Sogdian merchants in Byzantine markets.

---

Maniakh's journey is one of the most remarkable diplomatic missions in pre-modern history, and we know about it in detail because the Byzantine historian **Menander Protector** (a sixth-century Byzantine official who wrote a continuation of the lost history of Agathias around 580 CE) preserved long extracts in his now-fragmentary chronicle. Menander's account is our primary source for the entire Türk-Byzantine relationship, and it is detailed, vivid, and ethnographically rich.

Maniakh traveled overland from Istemi's western court at **Suyab** (in the Chu River valley near modern Tokmok in Kyrgyzstan) up across the **Caucasus** mountains to the Byzantine Black Sea coast, then by ship to Constantinople. The trip took most of a year. He arrived in Constantinople sometime in 568. Justin II received him personally, accepted the proposed alliance, and agreed to send a Byzantine ambassador back to Istemi to ratify the agreement. The ambassador chosen was a senior Byzantine official named **Zemarchus of Cilicia** (zeh-MAR-kus, an experienced diplomat who had previously served on missions to other foreign courts).

In 569, Zemarchus traveled with Maniakh's returning embassy back to Istemi's court in the **Tian Shan** mountains. His detailed account of what he saw — preserved by Menander Protector — is the earliest extensive Western description of the Türk khaganate in operation, and it is worth quoting at length because of how much it tells us about Türk court culture.

---

Zemarchus's first encounter with the Türks was a **purification ritual**. Türk shamans (called **kam** in the Türk language) waved smoking branches of incense over the Byzantine envoys, leapt over fires with them carrying their luggage, and rang bells to drive away evil spirits before allowing them to enter the khagan's tent. The ceremony was a working diplomatic protocol: foreigners had to be ritually cleansed before approaching the sacred space around the khagan. Menander preserves the ritual in striking detail — it is one of the most vivid surviving descriptions of any sixth-century steppe shamanic practice.

The khagan himself — Istemi — received Zemarchus in a vast circular felt tent decorated with multicolored silks. Istemi sat on a wheeled gold throne (the Türks moved with their wagon-camp court even at full ceremony, and the throne could be wheeled into the tent for the audience). Behind him stood his bodyguard and his chief Sogdian advisors, including Maniakh. Zemarchus made the formal Byzantine greeting, presented his credentials, and proposed the alliance against Persia. Istemi accepted. The two empires were now formally at war with the Sasanians.

Then Istemi insisted on showing his guests Türk hospitality, which meant several days of feasting, drinking, and entertainment. The Byzantine envoys were given quarters in a specially-built tent next to the khagan's, where they were served what Zemarchus described as "barbarian food" (probably mutton stews, fermented mare's milk, and the staple steppe foods that Byzantine palates were not used to). They were made to drink prodigious quantities of **kumis** (KOO-mis, fermented mare's milk, the universal alcoholic drink of the steppe — mildly alcoholic, sour, often described by foreign visitors as tasting like spoiled yogurt). Wrestling matches and horse races were held in their honor. Türk warriors performed displays of mounted archery. Then the Byzantines were sent home with rich gifts and a Türk embassy of their own to ratify the alliance from Justin's end.

---

The alliance lasted about twenty years and was militarily inconclusive. The Türks raided Sasanian territory in the Caucasus several times in the 570s and 580s, occasionally with Byzantine coordination. The Sasanians fought back through proxies and through their own steppe alliances. No decisive battle was ever fought between Türk and Sasanian armies. By the late 580s the alliance had quietly lapsed, and after 591 — when Khusrau II of Persia was put back on his throne with Byzantine military support and the two empires temporarily reconciled — both powers lost interest in the Türks.

But the diplomatic precedent was huge. The Türks had demonstrated that a steppe empire could conduct independent great-power diplomacy across the entire Eurasian system, choosing alliances based on strategic interest rather than on geographic proximity. They had shown that the Sogdian merchant network could be repurposed as a diplomatic infrastructure, sending and receiving embassies across thousands of miles of hostile or neutral territory. And they had, briefly, tilted the balance of power in West Asia by adding their cavalry to the Byzantine side of the Sasanian-Byzantine struggle.

The Maniakh-Zemarchus exchange of 568-569 represents one of the earliest direct East-West diplomatic contacts in recorded history. There had been earlier indirect contacts — Roman gold reached Han China through Silk Road intermediaries (Xiongnu & Huns Ch 4); a Roman embassy from Marcus Aurelius reportedly reached the Han court in 166 CE — but those were trickles compared to the sustained, deliberate Türk-Byzantine relationship that Maniakh opened. The Sogdians made it possible. The Türks paid for it. Byzantium engaged with it. For about twenty years in the late sixth century, the great-power chessboard of Eurasia genuinely included a steppe nomadic empire as a top-tier player.

---

Cross-civ check: the late 560s is one of the most active diplomatic periods of the entire pre-modern era. While Maniakh was traveling to Constantinople, the **Lombards** were invading northern Italy (568, the same year), beginning the long process by which the Byzantine reconquest of Italy unraveled. The **Avars** (possibly the Rouran refugees who had fled the Türk founding in 552, Chapter 1) were establishing their khaganate on the Hungarian plain. The **Visigothic kingdom** in Spain was at its medieval peak. **Muhammad** would be born in Mecca two years later, in 570 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). The **Sui Dynasty** was a generation away from reunifying China (589). The Türks sat at the center of this Eurasian system, with relations stretching from Constantinople to Chang'an and embassies traveling in both directions. For a brief window, the steppe was the connective tissue of the entire Old World.

# Chapter 5 — The East-West Split

Then the empire fell apart.

Not all at once, and not for the same reasons twice — but the structural problem the Türks had built into their dual-kingship system finally came due, and the consequence was a fifty-year split that ended only when the Tang Dynasty crushed the Eastern Türks in 630. Between 582 and 630 the Türks fought each other more than they fought anyone else, the Sui and then Tang dynasties exploited every opportunity to play rival khagans against each other, and the empire that Bumin and Istemi had built shrank back from continental scale to regional scale.

---

The proximate cause of the split was a succession crisis after the death of **Taspar Qaghan** (TAH-spar, also called Tatpar) in 581.

Taspar had been a competent ruler, one of Bumin's sons, who succeeded Muqan in 572 and held the throne for nine years. He was the last khagan to maintain effective authority over both eastern and western wings of the empire simultaneously. When he died, the question was who got the throne — and it had multiple competing answers because the Türks did not have a clear succession rule.

Steppe succession traditions had always been ambiguous. The Xiongnu had used **lateral succession** (brother succeeds brother before the throne moves to the next generation), which created a growing pool of cousins who all had legitimate claims (Xiongnu & Huns Ch 5). The Türks inherited this ambiguity. By 581, three branches of the Ashina dynasty had legitimate-looking claims to the throne:

1. **Taspar's nephew** Anluo, who Taspar had designated as his preferred heir
2. **Bumin's grandson** Apa, whose father had been Bumin's eldest son and who therefore had the strongest claim by primogeniture
3. **Istemi's son** Tardu (TAR-doo, the western yabghu, who held effective independent power in the western wing)

The first two candidates were east-based and fought a quick civil war that produced a third compromise figure: **Ishbara Qaghan** (ISH-bah-rah), Anluo's elder brother, who took the throne in 582. The Apa faction was defeated and fled west, where Tardu welcomed them and used them as a pretext to assert his own independent authority over the western wing.

By 582 the empire was de facto split. Ishbara held the eastern wing from the Orkhon and ruled the Mongolian heartland. Tardu held the western wing from Suyab and ruled Sogdiana, the Tian Shan, and the steppes north of the Caspian. The two halves were nominally still one empire (Tardu acknowledged Ishbara as senior khagan) but were operationally independent and increasingly hostile to each other. The Altai Mountains became the rough boundary between them.

---

The split was not just structural; it was strategic catastrophe. As soon as the empire was effectively two empires, any third power could play one against the other.

The third power that took advantage was the **Sui Dynasty** of China.

The Sui Dynasty (589-618) was founded by **Yang Jian** (YANG jee-EN, posthumous name Emperor Wen of Sui), a Northern Zhou general of mixed Chinese-Xianbei descent who deposed the last Northern Zhou emperor in 581 and unified northern China under his own Sui regime. In 589 Sui armies crossed the Yangtze, conquered the southern Chen Dynasty, and reunified China for the first time since the Han collapse in 220 — almost four hundred years of division ended in a single decade (Six Dynasties Ch 8 covers the broader Sui reunification context; the planned Tang-Song China TL will cover the Sui in detail).

Yang Jian inherited a serious Türk problem. The previous Northern Zhou and Northern Qi dynasties had paid 200,000 bolts of silk per year combined to the Türks (Chapter 2). The unified Sui state could either continue the tribute payments — humiliating, costly, and not really sustainable politically — or stop them and risk a Türk invasion. Yang Jian chose to stop the payments and to prosecute an aggressive frontier policy designed to break Türk power before they could mount a major response.

His chief weapon was diplomacy. The Sui government supported the Apa faction against Ishbara, supported Tardu against Apa, supported Ishbara's son **Yong Yulu** against Apa, and generally encouraged every Türk faction to fight every other Türk faction. The Sui also offered titles, honors, and financial support to any Türk leader who would acknowledge nominal Sui suzerainty — and several rivalry-pressured khagans took the offer. By the late 580s the Türks had spent so much energy fighting each other that they could no longer mount a coordinated response to Sui aggression.

In 599, Tardu attempted a desperate consolidation: he claimed the title of sole **great khagan** and tried to reunite both halves of the empire under his rule. The bid lasted only three years. By 603 Tardu's subject peoples — the Tieli and other northern tribes — had revolted against him, the Sui had supported the revolts, and Tardu died in obscurity. The Western Khaganate continued under various Tardu descendants but never recovered the unity of the Bumin-Istemi era.

---

The Sui Dynasty itself collapsed less than a generation later. **Emperor Yang of Sui** (Yang Jian's son, ruled 604-618) was a megalomaniacal builder who launched extravagant projects — the Grand Canal, the rebuilding of the Eastern Capital at Luoyang, three failed invasions of Korea — that bankrupted the treasury and exhausted the population. By 615 the dynasty was visibly failing.

In that year the Eastern Türk khagan **Shibi** (SHEE-bee, ruled 609-619) saw his opportunity. Emperor Yang was on a frontier inspection tour at **Yanmen Pass** (YAHN-men, in modern Shanxi province), accompanied by a relatively small escort. Shibi mobilized his entire cavalry force and surrounded the imperial party at Yanmen. The siege lasted over a month. The emperor was trapped in a small fortress with limited supplies, watching his frontier garrison being burned out one by one. He sent desperate appeals for help to every Sui army within reach.

This was a deliberate Türk replay of the Xiongnu humiliation of Han Gaozu at Baideng eight centuries earlier (Xiongnu & Huns Ch 3). The Türks knew their history and were quoting it back at China. Shibi was demonstrating that even the unified Sui state could be humiliated by steppe cavalry if the steppe got organized again.

The Sui rescue eventually arrived. Reinforcements broke through Türk lines and Shibi withdrew. The emperor escaped — physically intact but politically destroyed. The Yanmen Incident hastened the Sui collapse: rebellions broke out across China, military commanders defected, and within three years the Sui Dynasty was finished. Emperor Yang was assassinated in 618 by his own bodyguards. The civil wars produced a new dynasty, the **Tang**, founded by the Sui frontier governor **Li Yuan**.

---

Li Yuan founded the Tang Dynasty in 618 — and he did it with Türk help.

This is one of the more uncomfortable facts in Tang historical memory, glossed over in later official histories but documented in contemporary sources. Li Yuan started his rebellion against the Sui in 617 from the frontier city of Taiyuan, in territory directly exposed to Eastern Türk raids. To prevent Shibi's cavalry from attacking his rear while he marched south on the Sui capital Chang'an, Li Yuan paid tribute to Shibi, accepted nominal Türk overlordship, and received in return Türk cavalry support and a guarantee of non-interference. The Tang founding rebellion would not have survived its first year without this Türk-tolerated start.

For Shibi, the arrangement was perfect: a Chinese rebel general was paying tribute and acknowledging Türk superiority, exactly the relationship the Türks wanted with all Chinese rulers. For Li Yuan, the arrangement was a humiliating necessity that he had every intention of repudiating as soon as he was strong enough. The Tang Dynasty would spend the next decade trying to wipe the slate clean of this debt — and would do so, in 630, with a thoroughness that ended the Eastern Türk Khaganate forever.

---

The decade between Tang founding (618) and the destruction of the Eastern Türks (630) was a slow walk toward catastrophe. Li Yuan reigned as Emperor Gaozu of Tang from 618 to 626, then was overthrown by his own son **Li Shimin** (LEE shee-MIN) in the **Xuanwu Gate Incident** of 626 — the most famous palace coup in Chinese history, in which Li Shimin killed his older brother and crown prince in an ambush at the Xuanwu Gate of the imperial palace and forced his father to abdicate. Li Shimin then took the throne as **Tang Taizong** (tahng tie-DZUNG), ruling from 626 to 649 and becoming, by general scholarly consensus, the most successful Chinese emperor of the entire post-Han period.

Tang Taizong had two priorities on the steppe frontier. First, he wanted to repudiate the humiliating tribute relationship his father had accepted with Shibi. Second, he wanted to break Türk military power for good, so that no future Tang emperor would face a Türk siege at Yanmen Pass.

His opportunity came faster than he had any right to expect. In 627 a brutal winter killed much of the Eastern Türk livestock — a catastrophe that had no Tang military involvement but devastated the Türk economy and weakened the khaganate's internal cohesion. The Tieli tribes, long resentful of Türk overlordship, used the crisis as a pretext to revolt. The reigning Eastern khagan, **Illig Qaghan** (ILL-ig, also called Xieli in Chinese sources, the same Illig who had been Shibi's brother and successor), was already losing control by the end of 629. Tang Taizong saw the opening and acted.

In the winter of 629-630, the Tang general **Li Jing** (LEE JEENG, one of the great Chinese military commanders of any era) led a 100,000-strong winter campaign across the frozen Gobi Desert — a military operation widely considered impossible by previous Chinese armies. The march itself was the masterpiece. Tang cavalry crossed terrain Chinese armies had never crossed in winter, moved with Mongolian-style speed, and surprised Illig in his Yinshan camp before he had a chance to organize a defense.

The battle was brief. The Türk army was routed, Illig was captured trying to flee, and within weeks the Eastern Türk Khaganate had ceased to exist as an independent state. The conquered Türk tribes were resettled in the Ordos region as Tang military auxiliaries. Illig was brought to Chang'an and put on display at the Tang court — the same humiliation in reverse that the Xiongnu chanyu Huhanye had performed before the Han emperor in 51 BCE (Xiongnu & Huns Ch 5). The first Türk Khaganate was over.

Tang Taizong then did something no Chinese emperor had ever done. The surrendered Türk and other steppe leaders, in a formal ceremony at Chang'an, proclaimed him **Tian Kehan** (TYEN keh-HAHN, "Heavenly Kaghan" in a half-Chinese, half-Türkic title). He accepted. Tang Taizong now held two parallel imperial titles: **Son of Heaven** in the Chinese tradition (the Mandate of Heaven that legitimated rule over the settled world) and **Heavenly Kaghan** in the steppe tradition (the kut from Tengri that legitimated rule over the nomads). No Chinese emperor before or after has held both titles. The combination made Taizong notionally the supreme ruler of both halves of Eurasia, settled and nomadic — a fusion of authority that the Tang would never quite institutionalize but that made the Tang Empire culturally cosmopolitan in a way no Chinese dynasty before it had been (and that the planned Tang-Song China TL will cover in detail).

---

The Western Türks held on for another generation. After Tardu's collapse, a sequence of weaker Western khagans ruled from Suyab and managed to maintain some independence as long as the Tang were busy with the Eastern Türks. Once the eastern problem was solved, Tang attention turned west. The Tang general **Su Dingfang** (SOO ding-FAHNG) led campaigns from 657 to 659 that destroyed the Western Türk Khaganate at the Battle of the Irtysh River, capturing the last Western khagan **Ashina Helu** (ah-SHEE-nah HUH-loo). The Tang then created a new administrative system to govern the conquered territory: the **Anxi Protectorate** in the Tarim Basin (covering Kashgar, Khotan, Kucha, Karashahr, and other oasis cities), and slightly later the **Beiting Protectorate** in Dzungaria (covering the steppe north of the Tian Shan).

By 660 the Tang Empire stretched from the East China Sea to the Aral Sea — about 4,500 miles east-west, the largest Chinese empire by territorial extent for the next thousand years (the planned Tang-Song China TL covers this peak). The Türks, who had been the largest empire in Eurasia eighty years earlier, were now Tang military auxiliaries serving their conquerors. The first century of Türk rule was over.

It was not, however, the end of the Türks. The Ashina dynasty would re-emerge from Tang domination in 682 to found a Second Khaganate (Chapter 7). The Western Türk fragments would reorganize as smaller khanates that would eventually feed into the Khazar Khaganate on the Volga and the Karakhanid state in Transoxiana (Chapter 8). The political ethnonym "Türk" had been planted; nothing would extract it now.

---

Cross-civ check: the Tang conquest of the Türks (630) is roughly contemporary with **Muhammad's flight from Mecca to Medina** in 622 and the early Islamic conquests beginning in the 630s (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). The Arab destruction of the Sasanian Persian Empire was happening at the same time as the Tang destruction of the Türk Empire — two of the great pre-modern empires falling within a decade of each other to expanding new powers from the east and the west. The Sasanian collapse opened Iran to Islam; the Türk collapse opened the steppe to Tang protectorate rule. Within a few decades the Arab armies would push into Transoxiana (Islamic Golden Age Ch 5) and the Tang armies would push north of the Pamirs, until the two finally met at the Battle of Talas in 751 (Chapter 8). The seventh century was the most consequential half-century in pre-modern Eurasian history, and the Türks were squarely at the center of it.

# Chapter 6 — Under the Tang

For half a century after 630, the Türks lived as Tang subjects.

This is not the most action-packed chapter in the book — there are no great battles, no founding figures, no spectacular building programs. It is a chapter about what happens to a steppe people when their political independence is taken away by a settled empire that is genuinely good at administration. The Tang Protectorate system was one of the most successful imperial governance experiments in pre-modern history, and it managed the Türks effectively for fifty years. Then it failed, in 682, and the Türks came back. Why it worked while it worked, and why it failed when it failed, is the structural story of this chapter.

---

The immediate Tang response to the 630 conquest was a debate about what to do with the conquered Türks.

Tang Taizong's senior advisors split into two camps. One camp, led by the Confucian scholar-official **Wei Zheng** (WAY JUNG, the most famous of Taizong's advisors and one of the most-quoted figures in Chinese political history), argued that the Türks should be removed from the frontier zone entirely — relocated south of the Great Wall into Chinese agricultural territory, where they would be assimilated into the Chinese population over a few generations and the Türk political identity would dissolve. Wei Zheng cited classical Chinese precedent: the Han dynasty had relocated subordinate populations to break their tribal cohesion, and the technique generally worked.

The other camp, led by the official **Wen Yanbo** (WEN yan-BWAW), argued the opposite: keep the Türks on the steppe, where they were geographically separated from the Chinese population and could continue to function as a buffer between Tang China and any future steppe threats from further north. Wen Yanbo cited a different classical precedent: dispersing conquered peoples often created refugee populations that destabilized the Chinese interior, while preserving them in their original territory under Tang oversight maintained the existing ecological and cultural balance.

Tang Taizong sided with Wen Yanbo. The conquered Türks were resettled in the **Ordos region** (the same Yellow River bend that the Xiongnu had contested with Han China eight centuries earlier, Xiongnu & Huns Ch 4), organized into prefectures under Tang administrative supervision but allowed to keep their tribal organization, their pastoral economy, and their Türk identity. Türk leaders were given Tang titles and salaries; some served in high positions in the Tang military. The captured khagan Illig was given an honorific Tang title and lived out his life in Chang'an as an ornamental court figure.

The system extended in modified form to other conquered steppe peoples. The **Tieli** confederation (the loose group of Türk-speaking northern tribes that included the Uyghurs, the Karluks, the Basmyls, and the Kyrgyz, among others) was organized into a series of subordinate prefectures under nominal Tang authority. The Türks of the Ordos paid no formal tribute but were expected to provide cavalry contingents for Tang military campaigns and to suppress raids by tribes further north. Türk warriors served in Tang armies as far afield as Korea (in the Tang-Goguryeo wars of 645-668) and Central Asia.

The arrangement was essentially the **Tang Protectorate System**, and it worked because it gave the Türks something to lose. A Türk leader who cooperated with the Tang got a salary, titles, recognition, and the security of operating under imperial protection. A Türk leader who rebelled lost all of these and faced the full weight of Tang military force, which between 630 and 682 was the most effective army in Eurasia. The cost-benefit calculation favored cooperation, and most Türk leaders most of the time chose cooperation.

---

The Western Türks were brought into the same system after Su Dingfang's 657-659 campaigns. The conquered Western territory was organized into the **Anxi Protectorate** (an-SHEE, "Pacifying the West"), based initially at Gaochang (modern Turfan) and later at Kucha. The Anxi Protectorate covered the entire Tarim Basin and projected Tang power north through the Pamirs into Sogdiana. Its main role was to keep the Silk Road open and protected.

The mechanism was the **Four Garrisons of Anxi**: Tang military bases at **Kucha** (the protectorate seat), **Khotan** (in the southern Tarim, the great Buddhist oasis kingdom that had developed a distinctive cultural synthesis under Indian and Iranian influence), **Kashgar** (at the western end of the Tarim, where the routes through the Pamirs began), and either **Karashahr** or **Suyab** (the fourth garrison's location shifted over time). Each garrison housed a few thousand Tang soldiers — small by Chinese standards but enough to maintain order in the oases and to project occasional force into Central Asia.

Slightly later, a parallel **Beiting Protectorate** (BAY-ting, "Northern Court") was established in **Dzungaria** (jun-GAH-ree-ah, the steppe basin north of the Tian Shan in modern northern Xinjiang). Beiting handled the steppe peoples of the western frontier — what was left of the Western Türk fragments, plus the various lesser tribes of the Ili Valley region. Together Anxi and Beiting governed a vast territory that had been, less than thirty years earlier, the heart of an independent Türk khaganate.

The **Sogdians** continued to operate the commercial network they had run under the Türks (Chapter 3). Tang protectorate rule was actually good for Sogdian merchants in some ways: it provided more reliable security, more predictable taxation, and access to the Tang capital at Chang'an, which under Taizong and his successors was becoming the most cosmopolitan city in the world. Sogdian colonies in Chang'an grew, Sogdian musicians and dancers became fashionable at the Tang court (Tang court culture in the seventh and eighth centuries was famously open to Central Asian cultural influences, and the planned Tang-Song China TL will cover this in detail), and Sogdian merchants made fortunes managing Silk Road trade under Tang protection.

The arrangement worked. For about half a century, the Tang governance of the former Türk territories was one of the most successful pre-modern imperial systems in operation anywhere in the world.

---

Then, around 670-680, things started to go wrong.

The first problem was the **Tibetan Empire**. The Yarlung dynasty of Tibet had unified the Tibetan plateau under Songtsen Gampo in the 630s and emerged as a major military power on China's western frontier. Tibetan armies began pushing into the Tarim Basin from the south in the 660s, fighting both the Tang and the surviving Western Türk fragments for control of the Silk Road oases. The Tang had to commit major military resources to the Tibetan front, which weakened their grip on the steppe to the north.

The second problem was internal Tang politics. Tang Taizong had died in 649 and been succeeded by his son **Gaozong** (gow-DZUNG), who reigned from 649 to 683 but spent the latter half of his reign incapacitated by stroke. Real power passed to his consort, **Empress Wu Zetian** (WOO dze-TYEN), a former concubine of Taizong's who had risen to become Gaozong's empress and then his de facto co-ruler. Wu Zetian would eventually proclaim her own dynasty in 690 (the only woman in Chinese history to rule in her own name) and is one of the most consequential and controversial figures in pre-modern Chinese politics. The transition period of her rise — roughly 660 to 690 — produced major political turbulence in the Tang court, and the steppe frontier was one of the casualties. Tang attention drifted, frontier garrisons were under-supplied, and the Türks noticed.

The third problem was generational. The Türk leaders who had personally surrendered to Tang Taizong in 630 were dying by the 670s. The new generation of Türk warriors had grown up under Tang protectorate rule, knew Tang military strengths and weaknesses, and resented the loss of independent Türk identity. The Tang policy of allowing the Türks to keep their tribal organization had preserved exactly the kind of social structure that could organize a revolt.

In 679 the first major Türk revolt broke out, led by a Türk noble named **Ashide Wenfu** (ah-SHIH-deh WEN-foo). The revolt was small and was suppressed within a year, but it demonstrated that Türk political identity had survived fifty years of Tang rule. Two years later, in 682, a more serious revolt broke out under a man named **Kutlugh** (KOOT-loog, his Türk name; Chinese sources called him Gudulu) — a junior Ashina noble who had been serving as a Tang frontier officer. Kutlugh would adopt the throne name **Ilterish Qaghan** (IL-ter-ish, "the One Who Gathered the People") and would re-found the Türk Khaganate.

That story is Chapter 7.

---

Cross-civ check: the half-century of Tang protectorate rule over the Türks (630-682) is one of the most consequential periods in pre-modern Eurasian history, even though it does not get the attention it deserves. The same period saw:

- The early **Islamic conquests** rolling east and west from Arabia (Islamic Golden Age Ch 2-3): the destruction of the Sasanian Empire (651), the conquest of Egypt (640) and North Africa (~670), the Visigothic kingdom in Spain (711)
- The **Byzantine Empire** under Heraclius and his successors fighting for survival against the Arab onslaught (Byzantine Empire Ch 6)
- The **Tibetan Empire** at its greatest extent under successive Yarlung kings
- The **Tang Empire** at its territorial maximum, extending Chinese influence into Korea, Vietnam, and Central Asia (Tang-Song China TL)
- In **Mesoamerica**, the Mayan Classic period continuing in full flower while Teotihuacan declined (Maya Civilization Ch 6, Teotihuacan Ch 7)
- In the **Andes**, the Wari and Tiwanaku states expanding toward their Middle Horizon peak

The seventh century was when the Mediterranean-centered Old World gave way to a more polycentric Eurasian system, and the Tang protectorate over the Türks was one of the structural pillars of that transition. When the protectorate failed in 682, the consequence was not just a Türk revival — it was the beginning of the Tang Empire's slow loss of grip on Central Asia, a process that would culminate in the An Lushan Rebellion of 755 and the permanent Tang withdrawal from the western steppe.

# Chapter 7 — The Second Khaganate and Bilge Kagan

In 682, seventeen Türk warriors gathered in a remote frontier valley and decided to restart their empire.

That, at least, is what Chinese sources record. The number "seventeen" may be poetic compression — Türk founding myths liked round numbers — but the basic shape of the story is consistent across sources: Kutlugh, a junior Ashina noble who had been serving as a Tang frontier officer, gathered a tiny core of Türk loyalists at the start of his revolt and grew it within a few years into a force capable of expelling the Tang from the Mongolian steppe and re-establishing the khaganate. The Second Türk Khaganate would last sixty-two years (682-744), produce the most articulate steppe political voice in pre-modern history (Bilge Kagan), and leave behind the oldest surviving texts in any Turkic language (the Orkhon Inscriptions). It is, in many ways, the chapter where the Türks become themselves.

---

The revolt's success owed everything to Tang internal turmoil during the regency of **Empress Wu Zetian**.

The Tang court in 682 was in crisis. Emperor Gaozong had died (in 683) and Wu Zetian was systematically removing potential rival Tang princes. Court attention was focused on succession politics. Frontier garrisons were under-supplied. The Tang general **Pei Xingjian** (PAY shing-JEN), who had successfully suppressed Ashide Wenfu's revolt three years earlier, was unavailable — he was dying of natural causes in 682. The Tang military response to Kutlugh's revolt was confused, uncoordinated, and consistently too late.

By 685 Kutlugh held the entire Mongolian steppe up to the Great Wall. He had taken the throne name **Ilterish Qaghan** ("the One Who Gathered the People" or "the Gatherer" in Old Turkic) and re-established the Ashina dynasty in the Orkhon Valley. The Tang protectorate over the eastern steppe had collapsed within three years. The Anxi Protectorate further west survived (it would last another century, eventually breaking up after the An Lushan Rebellion), but the Mongolian heartland was Türk again.

Ilterish then turned his attention to the structural problem: how do you build a steppe empire in a generation when nobody alive remembers how the previous one had worked? His solution was to recruit one of the few surviving veterans of the pre-Tang Türk political system — a man whose name became one of the most distinctive in pre-modern Inner Asian history.

---

**Tonyukuk** (TON-yoo-kook, his Old Turkic name; Chinese sources called him Yuanzhen) was already old when Ilterish recruited him around 685. He had been born sometime in the 640s, raised in Tang China, educated in Chinese as well as in his native Türk language and culture, and had served as a junior Tang official before defecting to the rebel cause. He brought to the Second Khaganate something no native steppe-raised Türk had: working knowledge of Tang administrative practice, fluency in Chinese, and a deep understanding of how settled empires governed their frontiers. He would serve as chief minister and senior strategist to three successive khagans across the next forty years — Ilterish, then Ilterish's brother Kapaghan, then Ilterish's son Bilge.

Tonyukuk's most distinctive contribution was strategic. He had thought carefully about why Türk power had collapsed before and how to prevent it from collapsing again. His conclusions, preserved in the autobiographical inscription he erected near the Tula River around 720 (the **Tonyukuk Inscription**, the earliest of the three Orkhon Inscriptions), are one of the most coherent pieces of political analysis from any pre-modern steppe culture.

His central argument was that the Türks should remain nomadic. Settling down in cities, taking up agriculture, adopting Chinese-style governance — every one of these moves looked attractive in the short term but in the long term destroyed the qualities that made the steppe peoples militarily superior to their settled neighbors. Tonyukuk had watched the conquered Sogdians become wealthy traders under various rulers but politically negligible; he had watched the various Tang-protectorate Türks lose their military edge while gaining titles; he had concluded that the only sustainable Türk strategy was to stay on the move, stay in the saddle, and stay out of cities.

He extended the argument to **religion**. Buddhism had been the great religious export from India to China and was making inroads among various steppe peoples — the Tarim oases were heavily Buddhist, and various Türk subordinate tribes had converted. Tonyukuk argued explicitly against Türk conversion to Buddhism. Buddhism, he wrote, made men gentle, made them avoid taking life, made them spend their time in monasteries instead of on horseback. A Buddhist Türk would be a weak Türk. The argument is the earliest articulate steppe critique of monastic religion, and it explains why the Türks (uniquely among the major steppe peoples of the period) largely rejected Buddhism. The later Uyghurs would break with this and convert to Manichaeism instead; the Mongols (Mongol Empire Ch 8) would eventually convert to Buddhism centuries later; but the Türks during their two-and-a-half-century existence stuck with Tengri sky worship and shamanic practice.

---

Ilterish died in 691, and the throne passed to his younger brother **Kapaghan** (KAH-pah-gahn, also called Mochuo in Chinese sources). Kapaghan was a more conventional steppe warrior than the politically sophisticated Ilterish, and his reign (691-716) was the military peak of the Second Khaganate.

Kapaghan extended Türk control over the eastern Khitan, raided deep into Tang territory in northern China, defeated the Kyrgyz and Bayirku tribes in the north, and at one point in the 690s forced the Tang court at Chang'an to plead for peace and negotiate the return of captured Tang officials. Wu Zetian, still on the Tang throne in her own name as the founder of her brief Zhou Dynasty (690-705), had to absorb a humiliating treaty that essentially recognized Türk parity with the Chinese state — the first such recognition since the Tang founding in 618.

Kapaghan was, however, increasingly unpopular within the Türk political world by the end of his reign. He had grown autocratic, suspicious of his nephews (Ilterish's sons), and prone to sudden violence against subordinates. His subject peoples — the Uyghurs, the Karluks, the Basmyls — were all preparing revolts by 715. In 716, on a campaign against the Bayirku tribes in northern Mongolia, Kapaghan was caught alone by a Bayirku ambush party and killed. His head was sent to the Tang court at Chang'an, where it was displayed as a war trophy.

The succession crisis that followed was brief but bloody. Kapaghan's son tried to take the throne but was killed within days by Ilterish's eldest son, **Kul Tigin** (KOOL tee-GIN), in a night raid on the imperial encampment. Kul Tigin then proclaimed his older brother **Bilge** (BIL-geh, "Wise") as the new khagan. Bilge took the throne name **Bilge Kagan** ("Wise Khagan") and would rule the Second Khaganate from 716 to 734.

Tonyukuk survived the transition and continued to serve as chief minister. The triumvirate of **Bilge Kagan, Kul Tigin, and Tonyukuk** — khagan, warrior-brother, and elder statesman — was the most stable leadership the Türks had ever produced, and the next eighteen years would be the cultural peak of the entire Türk era.

---

Bilge Kagan's eighteen-year reign was, by steppe standards, almost peaceful. There were occasional raids on Tang territory and brief campaigns against rebellious Tieli tribes, but no major wars. Bilge concentrated on internal consolidation: rebuilding the Türk political system after Kapaghan's volatile reign, settling tribal disputes, distributing wealth from Tang trade and tribute, and patronizing the cultural projects that would produce the Orkhon Inscriptions.

The Türks of Bilge's reign were prosperous. The Silk Road was running smoothly under Tang and Türk joint protection, Sogdian merchants were paying handsome commissions to the khaganate for security and access, and Tang silk flowed north in regular tribute payments. Bilge himself married a Tang princess in 734 (the marriage was negotiated through Tang diplomatic channels and Bilge accepted it as a sign of Tang recognition of his legitimacy). The Türk court at the Orkhon was the wealthiest steppe court that had existed since Modu's Xiongnu — and unlike Modu's court, it was deeply integrated into the broader Eurasian commercial system.

In 731, Bilge's brother and warrior commander **Kul Tigin** died, possibly of natural causes in his late forties. The grief was real and the political consequence was significant: Bilge had lost his most reliable military partner and the man who had originally secured his throne in 716. Bilge spent much of the next year preparing an elaborate funerary monument, including an inscription that would become one of the three Orkhon Inscriptions.

---

The **Orkhon Inscriptions** are the central cultural achievement of the Second Türk Khaganate, and they require their own attention.

There are three of them, all carved on stone steles in the Orkhon Valley between roughly 720 and 735:

1. The **Tonyukuk Inscription**, erected near the Tula River around 720 during Tonyukuk's lifetime, narrating his role in the 682 revolt and the rebuilding of the Türk state. Tonyukuk personally oversaw the carving — making this one of the few first-person royal autobiographies in the historical record where the subject was alive when the text was written.

2. The **Kul Tigin Inscription**, erected by Bilge in 732 in honor of his dead brother. The inscription contains both Bilge's words honoring Kul Tigin and Bilge's own programmatic statements about Türk political identity.

3. The **Bilge Kagan Inscription**, erected in 735 a year after Bilge's own death, the longest of the three and the most complete account of the Second Khaganate's history from a Türk perspective.

All three are written in **Old Turkic** language, using a custom alphabet called the **Old Turkic script** (also called the **Orkhon script**) — about 38 characters, descended ultimately from the Sogdian alphabet but heavily modified to fit Turkic phonology, with a visual style that resembles Germanic runes (the resemblance is coincidental, the result of similar techniques — sharp tools carving stone — producing similar angular letterforms). The script was probably developed in Tonyukuk's lifetime and represents the first writing system custom-designed for a Turkic language.

The Orkhon Inscriptions are the **oldest surviving texts in any Turkic language**. There are earlier Turkic-language documents in Sogdian script, but those are administrative and commercial texts. The Orkhon Inscriptions are literary: first-person royal autobiography, political philosophy, programmatic statements about Türk identity. They are, in their way, the steppe equivalent of the Roman emperor Augustus's *Res Gestae* — a ruler's own account of his deeds, carved in stone for posterity. They remain one of the most distinctive bodies of pre-modern political writing from any culture.

---

The most famous passage in the Orkhon Inscriptions is **Bilge's warning against Sinicization**, preserved in the Kul Tigin Inscription. The full passage is too long to quote here, but the structure is:

> "O Türk people," Bilge says, addressing his subjects directly across the centuries, "when you went toward the south, you almost destroyed yourselves... When you stayed in the Otuken forest and sent caravans, you had no troubles. The Chinese give us silk and gold and sweet words. By those silk and gold and sweet words, they draw the distant peoples to themselves. After they have settled close to them, they make them think bad thoughts. They do not let good and wise men come close. They corrupt one man, and through him they corrupt his whole tribe. Listening to those sweet words and accepting that gold and silk, you Türk people, perished by the tens of thousands. If you go to the southern lands, O Türk people, you will perish! If you stay in the Otuken country, sending caravans, you will live forever in your khaganate."

This is the earliest articulate steppe critique of integration with settled civilization. Bilge had grown up under Tang domination (he was born in the late 670s, had spent his childhood as a Tang protectorate subject, and remembered the loss of Türk independence personally). His argument is that Tang generosity was a trap: silk and titles bought Türk loyalty cheaply, drew Türk leaders into the Chinese cultural orbit, and slowly destroyed the political identity that had made the Türks militarily superior in the first place. The solution was to stay in the Otuken heartland — that is, in the Khangai-Orkhon region of central Mongolia — and to take what was needed from China through trade and tribute without letting the Türk court itself be drawn south.

The argument was not new (Tonyukuk's earlier inscription had made a similar case) but Bilge's version is the most articulate. It is also notable for being the **earliest surviving first-person voice from any steppe ruler** — the only direct testimony, in their own language, of how the steppe leadership of any pre-modern era thought about its political situation. Everything we know about Modu, Attila, Bumin, and Istemi comes from second- or third-hand accounts written by people from the settled civilizations they fought. Bilge alone speaks for himself.

---

Bilge died in 734, poisoned by his own minister **Buyruq Chor** (BOO-yook CHOR). Sources vary on the motive (palace politics, personal grievance, possibly external Tang intrigue) but agree on the manner: a slow poison administered over several days, with Bilge living long enough to identify his murderer and have Buyruq Chor and his entire clan exterminated before he himself expired.

Bilge's death deprived the Second Khaganate of its most stable leader. The successors were a series of weak khagans who fought each other for the throne and could not maintain the political unity Bilge had built. By the late 730s the Türk state was fracturing again. By 742 the subject peoples — Uyghurs, Karluks, Basmyls — were in open revolt. By 744 the dynasty was finished.

The end is the subject of the next chapter.

---

Cross-civ check: the Second Türk Khaganate (682-744) overlaps almost exactly with the great Islamic conquests under the **Umayyad Caliphate** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4) and the early **Abbasid Revolution** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6). The Umayyads conquered Sogdiana in 705-715 (effectively closing the Western Türk legacy in Central Asia, even though the Türks had not held that territory directly since the 660s), conquered the Visigothic kingdom of Spain in 711, and reached the gates of France at Tours in 732 — the same year Bilge erected the Kul Tigin Inscription. The **Tang Empire** was at its cultural peak under Emperor Xuanzong (Tang-Song China TL) but was approaching the fiscal and military crisis that would explode in the An Lushan Rebellion in 755. The **Byzantine Empire** under Leo III was fighting off the second great Arab siege of Constantinople (717-718) and recovering. In **Anglo-Saxon England**, **Bede** was writing his *Ecclesiastical History of the English People* (731). The eighth century is one of the moments when the Eurasian system shifted decisively, and the Türks were participants in the shift right up until the moment they collapsed.

# Chapter 8 — The Uyghur Take-Over and the Turkic Diaspora

In 744 a Türk khagan was killed in his own camp by one of his subjects, the imperial title passed to a different dynasty, and the Türk state ceased to exist. That was the political end. The cultural and ethnic consequences took several centuries to play out — and they reshaped most of Eurasia.

This chapter is about what happened after the Türks. Where the displaced Türk-speaking peoples went, what empires they built, and how the political ethnonym "Türk" — which in 552 had been the name of one Mongolian clan — eventually became the self-identification of two hundred million people across a continent. It is also about the four major successor traditions: the Uyghurs in Mongolia, the Karluks in Central Asia, the Khazars and Pechenegs and Bulgars in eastern Europe, and the Seljuks-then-Ottomans in the Middle East. Each one is its own story, but they all begin in the same place: the Türk collapse of 744.

---

The collapse itself was political, not military. By the early 740s the Second Khaganate was in chronic succession crisis (Chapter 7). After Bilge's poisoning in 734, the throne had passed through three weak khagans in eight years, each one less able to hold the empire together. The subject peoples — the Uyghurs in northern Mongolia, the Karluks in the Tian Shan, the Basmyls in the Altai — were growing in strength and becoming impatient with Ashina rule.

In 742 the three subject groups formed a coalition and revolted. The reigning Türk khagan **Ozmish** (OZ-mish) was killed in battle. His successor **Bomei Qaghan** survived for two more years before being killed in 744 by the Uyghur leader **Kutluk Bilge** (KOOT-look BIL-geh, no relation to the earlier Bilge Kagan despite the shared name). The Uyghur leader took the imperial title, declared the founding of a new dynasty, and the Ashina line — which had ruled the steppe for almost two centuries — simply vanished from history. There is no record of Ashina survivors continuing to claim the throne. The dynasty that had named the Turkic peoples was extinguished.

---

The **Uyghur Khaganate** (744-840) succeeded the Türks as the dominant power on the Mongolian steppe and ruled it for almost a century. The Uyghurs were Türk-speakers themselves — closely related linguistically and culturally to the Türks they had overthrown — but with several important differences in political style.

First, the Uyghurs converted to **Manichaeism** in 762, after their khagan **Bögü Qaghan** (BERG-oo) was introduced to the religion by Sogdian missionaries during a visit to the Tang capital at Luoyang (which the Uyghurs were occupying after rescuing the Tang from the An Lushan Rebellion). Manichaeism (a syncretic Persian religion founded by the third-century prophet Mani, combining Zoroastrian, Christian, and Buddhist elements into a dualistic system focused on the cosmic struggle between light and darkness) was a strange choice for a steppe empire — neither Tonyukuk nor Bilge would have approved. The Uyghurs were the only steppe empire in history to adopt Manichaeism as a state religion. The conversion gave them a distinctive cultural identity and tied them more closely to the Sogdian commercial network, but it also distanced them from the broader Türk-speaking world that remained committed to Tengri sky worship.

Second, the Uyghurs **built a city**. Their capital, **Karabalghasun** (KAR-ah-bahl-GAH-soon, in the Orkhon Valley near the future site of Genghis Khan's Karakorum), was a true urban center with permanent buildings, defensive walls, and a Manichaean temple complex. This was a sharp break from Türk practice, which had kept the khagan's court mobile and rejected city-building as a Sinicizing trap. The Uyghurs were essentially demonstrating that everything Tonyukuk had warned against — settlement, religious conversion, integration with sedentary culture — could in fact be done without immediate political collapse. (Long-term collapse was another matter.)

Third, the Uyghurs **rescued the Tang Dynasty** during the An Lushan Rebellion of 755-763. The Tang court, on the verge of being overthrown by the rebel general An Lushan (himself half-Sogdian and half-Türk, born of mixed parentage on the old Western Türk frontier), called on Uyghur cavalry to retake the lost Tang capitals at Chang'an and Luoyang. The Uyghurs delivered, restored Tang authority, and in exchange extracted enormous annual silk subsidies and effective parity-of-status with the Tang throughout the late eighth century. The relationship was enormously profitable for the Uyghurs but also entangling: every Tang internal crisis became an Uyghur problem.

The Uyghur Khaganate ended in 840, when the **Yenisei Kyrgyz** (the Türk-speaking forest people of the upper Yenisei basin in southern Siberia, who had been Türk subjects since Muqan's reign in the 560s) descended on the Mongolian steppe and destroyed Karabalghasun. The Uyghurs scattered. Some moved south into the Tarim Basin, where they founded the **Kingdom of Qocho** at Turfan and gradually converted from Manichaeism to Buddhism. Others moved further south into the Tang frontier zones. The Uyghur language and Uyghur cultural identity persisted; the modern Uyghur people of Xinjiang trace their descent and their name to this dispersed Uyghur population, though with substantial admixture from later Türk and other arrivals.

---

The **Karluks** (KAR-luks) had been one of the three subject peoples in the 742 revolt, alongside the Uyghurs and Basmyls. After the Türk collapse they migrated southwest, settling in the Ili River valley of modern Kazakhstan and eventually pushing into Transoxiana. They are one of the great underrated peoples of pre-modern history: a small Türk confederation that determined, through one battlefield decision in 751, the religious future of Central Asia.

The decision was at the **Battle of Talas** in July 751.

The Battle of Talas was a confrontation between two empires that should have known better than to fight each other so far from home. The **Tang Empire** was projecting power into Central Asia from the east through its Anxi Protectorate. The **Abbasid Caliphate** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6) — which had just overthrown the Umayyad dynasty in 750 and was at the start of its great cultural and territorial expansion — was projecting power into Central Asia from the west through Khurasan. Both empires were claiming overlapping influence in the Sogdian oases. In 750-751 a series of local disputes escalated into open war, and the two armies met at the **Talas River** in modern Kyrgyzstan.

The Tang army was commanded by the Korean-born Tang general **Gao Xianzhi** (GOW shyen-JR), a competent commander who had already won several Central Asian campaigns. He fielded perhaps 30,000 Tang troops plus around 20,000 Karluk auxiliary cavalry — the latter being former Türk subjects who had recently been absorbed into the Tang protectorate system. The Abbasid army under the general **Ziyad ibn Salih** had perhaps 40,000 Arab and Persian troops.

The battle was indecisive for the first four days. On the fifth day, the Karluks defected from the Tang side to the Abbasid side, attacking Gao Xianzhi's rear while the Abbasid main force pressed his front. The Tang army was shattered. Gao Xianzhi escaped with a few thousand survivors. Tang power in Central Asia never recovered.

The consequences of the Karluk defection were enormous. With the Tang army destroyed and Tang frontier governance broken (and the An Lushan Rebellion just four years away from completing the Tang collapse in Central Asia), the Sogdian oases passed under Abbasid Arab control for good. Sogdiana, Bukhara, Samarkand, Khwarezm, Ferghana — every one of them began the long conversion to Islam that would, over the next several centuries, transform Central Asia from a Sogdian-Iranian-Buddhist-Zoroastrian-Manichaean cultural mosaic into a uniformly Muslim region. The Karluks themselves converted to Islam in the tenth century and founded the **Karakhanid Khanate** (840-1212), the first Türk-speaking Muslim dynasty. Every later Türk-speaking Islamic state — Seljuk, Khwarezmian, Ottoman — descended in some sense from the religious watershed at Talas.

The single decision of one Türk-descended people on one battlefield in 751 effectively determined that Central Asia would be Muslim rather than Buddhist or Christian or anything else. The Karluks may not have understood the full implications, but their defection was one of the more consequential acts in pre-modern history.

---

Meanwhile, on the western steppe, other Türk-speaking peoples were building empires of their own.

The **Khazar Khaganate** (~650-969) had broken away from the Western Türks in the seventh century and established an independent state on the lower Volga, the Caucasus, and the Pontic steppe. The Khazars are one of the great oddities of medieval history: a Türk-speaking ruling class governing a multi-ethnic, multi-religious population (Slavic, Iranian, Greek, and others) that controlled the northern Silk Road and the Volga trade route to the Vikings (Viking Age Ch 5). Around 740, the Khazar royal court converted to **Judaism** — making the Khazars the only Jewish-ruling state in history outside ancient Israel. The conversion was almost certainly a strategic choice: the Khazars sat between the Christian Byzantine Empire and the Muslim Abbasid Caliphate and converting to either would have been a political surrender to one side. Judaism gave them a third option that committed them to neither great power. The Khazars blocked Arab northward expansion through the Caucasus for two centuries until the Rus prince **Sviatoslav** destroyed their state in 969 (the Rus, the proto-Russian Viking-descended state in Kiev, are covered in Viking Age Ch 5).

The **Bulgars** were another Türk-speaking people who had broken off from the Western Türk system. Under the leader **Asparuh** they crossed the Danube in 680 and founded the **First Bulgarian Empire** in what is now Bulgaria, conquering and ruling a much larger Slavic-speaking population. Over the next several centuries the Bulgar ruling class was gradually Slavicized — they converted to Orthodox Christianity around 864, switched to a Slavic written language, and lost their Türk identity entirely. The modern Bulgarians are predominantly Slavic-speaking and only a faint Türk political ancestry remains in the country's name. (A separate group, the **Volga Bulgars**, settled on the middle Volga and converted to Islam in 922; they were eventually absorbed into the Mongol successor states and became part of the ancestry of the modern **Tatars** of Russia.)

The **Pechenegs** dominated the Pontic steppe (the grasslands north of the Black Sea) from the ninth to the eleventh centuries, repeatedly defeating Byzantine and Rus armies before being absorbed by the **Cumans** (another Türk-speaking nomadic confederation) in the 1100s. The Cumans in turn were destroyed by the Mongol invasion of the 1230s (Mongol Empire Ch 5), with surviving Cuman populations fleeing into Hungary. Hungarian noble families today still preserve a distinct Cuman identity and the political vocabulary of the Hungarian state retains some Cuman-derived terms.

---

The biggest downstream consequence of the Türk collapse, however, came three hundred years later in Iran and Anatolia.

The **Seljuks** (SEL-jooks) were a Türk-speaking clan from the Aral Sea region who converted to Sunni Islam in the late tenth century. Under their leader **Tughril Beg** they migrated south into Iran in the 1030s, defeated the Persianate **Ghaznavid** dynasty at the Battle of Dandanaqan in 1040, and conquered Baghdad in 1055 (where they took the title of "Sultan" from the Abbasid Caliphate, which had become a ceremonial figurehead). The Seljuk Empire became the dominant Sunni Muslim power of the eleventh century — the empire that confronted the First Crusade, that sponsored the great theologian al-Ghazali, that built the most beautiful mosques and madrasas of medieval Iran (Islamic Golden Age Ch 8 covers the Seljuk era from the Persian side).

The Seljuks' most famous act was the **Battle of Manzikert** in 1071, where Sultan Alp Arslan defeated and captured the Byzantine Emperor Romanos IV in eastern Anatolia (Byzantine Empire Ch 9). The defeat broke Byzantine control of central Anatolia and opened the region to Türk settlement. Within a generation, hundreds of thousands of Türk-speaking nomads had migrated into the Anatolian highlands, founded a series of competing Seljuk emirates, and begun the slow process of converting Anatolia from Greek-speaking Christianity to Türk-speaking Islam. The cultural transformation of Anatolia (which had been a Greek-speaking Christian region for over a thousand years) into Türkiye (a Türk-speaking Muslim region) is one of the most dramatic ethnic-linguistic shifts in medieval history.

The fragments of Seljuk Anatolia eventually consolidated into a successor state called the **Sultanate of Rum** (room, the Türk-Arabic word for "Rome," meaning the formerly-Roman territory they had taken from Byzantium). Rum collapsed in the late thirteenth century under Mongol pressure, and from its fragments emerged the small frontier emirate of **Osman I** in northwestern Anatolia around 1299 — the founding of the **Ottoman dynasty** (Ottoman Empire Ch 1).

The Ottomans went on to take Constantinople in 1453, conquer the Mamluks of Egypt in 1517, besiege Vienna in 1529 and 1683, and rule a multi-continental empire from the Balkans to the Persian Gulf to North Africa until the dynasty was finally dissolved in 1922 (Ottoman Empire Ch 12 covers the dissolution). They are the latest, longest-lasting, and most influential of all the post-Türk Türkic empires. Their continuous self-identification as Türks runs back through the Seljuks to the Oghuz steppe confederations to the Western Türk subject peoples of the 700s to the Ashina founding in 552.

---

Pull back and look at the whole arc. In 552, a single Mongolian clan called itself Türk. In 2026, **two hundred million people** across **six independent countries** (Turkey, Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan, Turkmenistan) plus large minority populations in Russia, Iran, China, and many other countries call themselves Türks of one variety or another. The **Turkic language family** has more speakers than French, more speakers than German, more speakers than Italian. Every one of them traces the political ethnonym back through some chain of inheritance — Karluk to Karakhanid to Khwarezmian, Oghuz to Seljuk to Ottoman to modern Turkish, Pecheneg to Cuman to Hungarian Cuman, Volga Bulgar to Tatar — to the Ashina founding in 552.

The Ashina dynasty itself was extinguished in 744. The political institution they built lasted just under two centuries. But the name they invented for themselves became the master ethnonym for one of the world's largest cultural-linguistic families. It is one of the most successful brand-extensions in human history, and one of the strangest: the dynasty that named everyone is forgotten by almost everyone the name now belongs to.

---

The Türk legacy is also visible in the broader steppe political tradition that follows them. The Mongol Empire (Mongol Empire Ch 2) is in many ways a continuation of the Türk template: the same dual-wing organization, the same decimal military system, the same Tengri-based legitimating ideology, the same khagan title, the same use of Sogdian-descended administrative practice (the Mongols inherited the **Uyghur script**, descended from the Sogdian alphabet via the Türk Old Turkic script, as their own writing system). When Genghis Khan declared himself khagan in 1206, he was deliberately invoking a tradition that ran back through the Khitan Liao dynasty, through the Uyghurs, through the Türks, ultimately to Bumin in 552. The modern Mongolian nation, the Russian Federation's Türk-speaking minorities, the Central Asian republics, the Republic of Turkey — all of them are downstream consequences of decisions taken in the Orkhon Valley fourteen centuries ago.

---

A pause before closing. The Türks are an unusual entry in this app's roster because they are, in a sense, more important for what they started than for what they themselves accomplished. Their direct political existence lasted about 190 years and never produced cities, written law codes, theological systems, or distinctive material culture beyond what they inherited from the Xiongnu and Sogdian traditions. Their legacy is not the empire itself but the ethnonym, the language family, the political template, and the institutional practices that diffused outward across Eurasia after their collapse.

In this they resemble a few other historical pivot-points. The **Phoenicians** (Phoenicia Ch 7) gave the alphabet to the Mediterranean and then disappeared. The **Sogdians** (Chapter 3) ran the Silk Road for centuries and then lost their distinctive identity to Arabic-Islamic culture. The **Visigoths** (Ancient Rome Ch 9) destroyed the Western Roman Empire and then assimilated into the Iberian peninsula they had conquered. The **Akkadians** (Mesopotamia Ch 5) created the first empire in human history and then disappeared as a distinct people. In each case the historical legacy is structural, not material — the Türks built the road that ran from the Ashina founding to the Ottoman Empire, and the road outlasted them.

That is the Türk story. A small clan in the Altai, a he-wolf in a cave, a revolt against the Rouran in 552, a brother running west to crush the Hephthalites, an embassy to Constantinople, a fifty-year split, a Tang conquest, a Second Khaganate, a poisoned khagan, a name that became the name of two hundred million people. The first political community in history that called itself Türk, and the seed of every Türk-speaking community since.

---

Cross-civ check: the post-Türk Turkic diaspora is one of the great population-and-language stories of pre-modern history. By 1300 — five centuries after the Türk collapse — Türk-speakers had settled in a band across all of Eurasia: the **Kazakhs** and **Kyrgyz** on the Central Asian steppes, the **Uzbeks** and **Turkmen** in Transoxiana, the **Uyghurs** in the Tarim Basin, the **Tatars** on the Volga, the **Cumans** in Hungary, the **Pechenegs** absorbed into the Slavic populations of the lower Don and Dnieper, the **Anatolian Türks** building toward the Ottoman state. Add the **Mongols** — who were Türk-adjacent rather than Türk-speaking but inherited the entire steppe political template from the Türks — and the post-Türk world covers most of the Eurasian continent from China to the Carpathians. No other steppe people has had a comparable downstream impact. The Türks were the seedbed.

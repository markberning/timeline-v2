# Middle Horizon Empires — Tiwanaku & Wari

**Goal:** Narrative for the two highland Andean states that filled the gap between the Regional Development cultures (Moche, Nazca — Andean Kingdoms) and the Inca. From roughly 500 to 1000 CE, **Tiwanaku** on the southern altiplano and **Wari** in the central Peruvian highlands ran the first pan-Andean empires, sharing a religious vocabulary inherited from Chavín, inventing the planned administrative city and the imperial road network, and laying down almost every institution the Inca would later be famous for. They also did all of this without writing — no king-lists, no chronicles, not a single Tiwanaku or Wari ruler is known by name. This narrative is necessarily more archaeological than chronological.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

A note on names and pronunciation: the Andean Middle Horizon is a world of unfamiliar Quechua and Aymara place-names. **Tiwanaku** (tee-wah-NAH-koo). **Wari** (WAH-ree, also spelled Huari). **Pikillaqta** (pee-kee-YAHK-tah). **Cerro Baúl** (SEH-roh bah-OOL). **Pumapunku** (POO-mah-POON-koo). **Akapana** (ah-kah-PAH-nah). **Kalasasaya** (kah-lah-sah-SIGH-ah). **Conchopata** (kohn-choh-PAH-tah). **Ayacucho** (eye-ah-KOO-choh). **Khipu** (KEE-poo). **Suka kollu** (SOO-kah KOH-yoo). Pronunciations are given on first use of each name.

A note on "first" claims: the Andean record between 500 and 1000 CE is full of "firsts" — the first planned grid city in South America, the first imperial road network, the first khipu records, the first true tin bronze, the largest stone-cut blocks in pre-Columbian architecture. Each is qualified in context. As with every TL in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of," and what survives in the Andes is shaped by extreme preservation conditions: dry coastal climates protect organic materials, while the wet eastern slopes destroy them within years. Archaeological luck shapes what we can claim.

A second note on what we cannot do here. Most pre-modern empires come with at least the bones of a political narrative — a king-list, a foundation date, a defeat at a named battle. The Middle Horizon comes with none of that. Neither Tiwanaku nor Wari developed a writing system. Not a single ruler from either empire is known by name. There are no chronicles, no inscriptions, no native histories. Everything in the chapters that follow has been reconstructed from architecture, ceramics, textiles, burials, ice cores, sediment cores, and the early Wari knotted-string khipu records that we still can't fully read. Where the prose says "the rulers" or "the elite," it means the people whose existence is implied by the size of their tombs and the fineness of their grave goods. Where it says a leader did something, that's a guess. The chapters lean thematic for this reason — chronology does not carry the weight that it does in a written record civilization.

# Chapter 1 — After Moche & Nazca

By 600 CE, the brilliant regional cultures of the previous chapter (Andean Kingdoms) were all in trouble.

The **Moche** civilization on the north coast of Peru — the warrior-priests of Sipán, the builders of the largest adobe pyramids in the Americas, the makers of the most realistic ceramic portraits in the ancient world — had spent the late sixth century being battered by a sequence of catastrophic El Niño events. Floods washed out the irrigation canals that fed their cities. Decades of drought followed the floods. The southern Moche capital at the foot of the **Huacas de Moche** complex was abandoned. The northern Moche shifted their center of gravity inland to a defensive site called **Pampa Grande**, which itself was burned in an internal revolt around 700 CE. By 750, the Moche as a coherent civilization were gone (Andean Kingdoms, Chapter 8).

The **Nazca** culture on the south coast — the makers of the desert geoglyphs, the engineers of the puquios underground aqueducts — was hollowing out from a different cause. Long-term deforestation had stripped the **huarango trees** whose deep root systems held the desert valleys' subsurface water. As the trees disappeared, the wells went dry, the agricultural valleys lost productivity, and population shrank. By 600 CE, Nazca was no longer a regional power; by 750 it had been absorbed into the southern reach of an expanding highland state called Wari (Andean Kingdoms, Chapter 8).

The **Lima** culture on the central coast and the **Recuay** culture in the northern highlands were both in similar trouble — climatic stress, regional reorganization, declining political coherence. By around 600 CE, the entire western Andean coast was a patchwork of weakened polities.

This is the situation into which two new powers stepped, and the chapter you are about to read is in some sense the answer to the question: what do you build when the previous order has collapsed and there is room to start over?

---

The standard archaeological label for what came next is the **Middle Horizon**.

A "horizon" in Andean archaeology is a period when a single artistic style, religious system, or material culture spread across most of the central Andes, creating a layer of cultural uniformity visible in the dirt. There are three of them. The **Early Horizon** (roughly 900 to 200 BCE) is dominated by the **Chavín** religious complex (Early Andean Civilizations, Chapter 7). The **Late Horizon** (1450 to 1532 CE) is the Inca Empire. The **Middle Horizon**, sandwiched between the two regional periods, runs from roughly 600 to 1000 CE and is dominated by Tiwanaku and Wari.

The label is doing real work. Across central and southern Peru and into modern Bolivia, you can find Wari and Tiwanaku iconography on the same kinds of pottery, similar architectural plans, the same shared deity images. The two empires created a recognizable pan-Andean visual culture for the first time since Chavín a thousand years earlier. They did not, however, create a single political unit. There were two empires, two capitals, two different administrative styles, two different distributions of military and ceremonial power. Sorting out the relationship between them is the central archaeological puzzle of the period and the subject of Chapter 4.

For now, what matters is that the regional cultures of the previous chapter were replaced by something new: highland states with imperial reach, capable of projecting influence across hundreds of miles of altitude and ecology, building infrastructure that nothing on the coast had ever matched.

---

To understand how the Andean highlands could do this, you have to understand the **vertical archipelago**.

Almost no other civilization on earth lived in the kind of vertical landscape the Andes presented (Early Andean Civilizations, Chapter 1). The eastward gradient from the Pacific coast to the Amazon basin runs roughly 200 miles (320 km) horizontally and 20,000 feet (6,000 meters) vertically — a rise so steep that within a single morning's walk a llama caravan could pass through three or four entirely different climatic zones, each with its own crops, its own livestock, and its own seasonal cycle. In the **yunga** zone below 6,500 feet (2,000 meters) you grow coca, chili peppers, fruit, and cotton. In the **quechua** zone between 6,500 and 11,500 feet (2,000–3,500 meters) you grow maize, beans, and squash. In the **suni** zone between 11,500 and 13,000 feet (3,500–4,000 meters) you grow potatoes, quinoa, and other tubers. Above the **suni** is the **puna** (POO-nah), the cold high-altitude grassland where you cannot farm but you can pasture llamas and alpacas.

A single Andean village could not produce all of these things from its own land. So Andean communities developed, gradually, a strategy of **vertical archipelago** management: a village in the highlands would maintain colonies of its own people in distant ecological zones — a coca-growing colony down in the yunga, a maize colony in the quechua, a salt-mining colony out in the puna — all linked by reciprocal labor and product flow back to the central settlement. The colonies were not conquered territory. They were extensions of the home ayllu, staffed by relatives of the central village's residents, who returned home periodically with the fruits of their distant labor.

This was the basic Andean economic strategy from at least Chavín times. What Tiwanaku and Wari did was scale it. Where an individual ayllu (the extended kinship group, the basic Andean social unit) might maintain colonies in three or four distant zones, an empire could maintain colonies in dozens, across hundreds of miles, organized through state planning rather than family ties. The vertical archipelago at imperial scale is what Tiwanaku tried in the Moquegua Valley, the Cochabamba Valley, and the Atacama oases, and it is what Wari built into its road and storehouse network across central Peru.

The model is genuinely different from what almost any other ancient empire was doing. Roman, Han Chinese, and Persian imperial administration all assumed a relatively flat geography in which provinces were defined by territory you could march an army across. Andean imperial administration assumed a vertical geography in which the meaningful units were not territories but altitude bands, and the basic political problem was not securing a frontier but ensuring that maize from below and llama wool from above could move past each other on the same road. The road was vertical, not horizontal. The empire was a stack, not a map.

---

A note on what is missing from the rest of this book: writing.

Neither Tiwanaku nor Wari developed a writing system. Across the four-hundred-year span the chapters cover, no name of any king, governor, priest, or general has come down to us. There are no inscriptions on the Gateway of the Sun. There are no foundation tablets at Pikillaqta. There are no king-lists in the tombs at Huarmey. The earliest Wari **khipu** records — knotted-string accounting devices that the Inca would later inherit and refine — are present at Wari sites and clearly carry information of some kind, but the Wari khipus use a structural grammar (wrapped color-coded threads rather than the later Inca knotted decimal numerals) that modern scholars have not yet decoded.

This means that everything you are about to read in the next seven chapters is reconstructed from physical evidence: architectural floor plans, the distribution of pottery styles, the chemical signature of pigments, the isotope ratios in skeletons, the strata of debris at abandoned sites, the patterns of charred grain in storage rooms, the ice cores from the Quelccaya glacier high above the altiplano, the pollen records from sediment cores in Lake Titicaca. Where the prose says "the rulers" or "the elite," what it really means is "the people whose existence is implied by the size of their tombs and the fineness of their grave goods." Where it says a particular event happened in a particular year, the precision is provided by radiocarbon dating, which has a margin of error of decades. Where it describes a religious belief, the description is reconstructed from the iconography of figurines and stelae — never from a surviving text.

The Andean civilizational tradition produced everything you would expect from a major pre-modern empire — cities, roads, hierarchies, philosophy, ritual specialists, a state cult, an administrative class — except writing. This is unusual. Mesopotamia, Egypt, China, Mesoamerica, the Indus Valley, even the Mycenaeans and the Hittites — every other civilization that built cities and ran administrative empires also independently developed some form of writing or borrowed one from a neighbor. The Andes did not. The reasons are debated. Some scholars argue that the khipu system functioned as a writing equivalent for accounting and may have been more linguistically expressive than we currently recognize. Others argue that the elaborate iconographic programs on textiles, ceramics, and stelae served a quasi-textual function — encoded narrative meaning the way a complicated coat of arms might encode lineage. The honest answer is that we don't know why writing didn't develop in the Andes the way it developed everywhere else, and the consequence is that we know less about Andean political history than about almost any comparable civilization.

This shapes what the rest of the chapters can do. Where the Mesopotamia narrative could spend pages on Sargon of Akkad, his daughter Enheduanna, the Code of Hammurabi, and the Babylonian creation epic, the Middle Horizon narrative cannot name a single king. Where the Egypt narrative can quote Pharaohs from inscribed temple walls, the Tiwanaku narrative can only describe the temples themselves. Where the China narrative can reproduce Confucian arguments about the Mandate of Heaven, the Wari narrative can only point at the architectural standardization that suggests something like a state ideology was at work. The Andean record is rich in the things you can dig up and silent on the things you can read. The chapters that follow will be more thematic, more archaeological, less narrative than other chapters in this series — because the surviving evidence allows nothing else.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Andean Middle Horizon was forming between roughly 500 and 700 CE, the rest of the world was busy with its own transitions. In **Europe**, the Western Roman Empire had collapsed in 476 CE and the Mediterranean was reorganizing into Byzantine, Frankish, and Visigothic fragments. In **China**, the Sui Dynasty (581–618) reunified the country after centuries of division and was succeeded by the Tang (618–907), one of the great cosmopolitan empires of world history. In **the Middle East**, Islam was founded by the Prophet Muhammad in the 610s and the first Arab armies erupted out of Arabia in the 630s, conquering Persia and most of Byzantine Syria and Egypt within a generation. In **Mesoamerica**, the Maya Classic period (Maya Civilization, Chapter 5) was at its peak — Tikal and Calakmul fighting their long wars while Teotihuacan in central Mexico (Teotihuacan, Chapter 6) was just past its own peak and beginning the slow decline that would empty the city by 750 CE. The Maya were writing dynastic histories on stone stelae while the Wari were not writing anything. The Tang Chinese were producing Li Bai's poetry on paper while Tiwanaku was carving the Bennett Monolith without a single inscribed character. The Andean states were operating on the same imperial scale as their global contemporaries, but with a documentary record that more closely resembles the prehistoric than the historic.

This is where the next chapter starts.

# Chapter 2 — Tiwanaku Rising

Tiwanaku was a city built where it should not have been possible to build a city.

The site sits on the southern shore of **Lake Titicaca** (tih-tih-KAH-kah, the highest navigable lake in the world), at an elevation of roughly 12,500 feet (3,850 meters) above sea level. For comparison: the highest major city in modern North America is Denver at 5,280 feet. The highest town in the Alps is Trepalle at 6,690 feet. Tiwanaku is more than twice as high as Denver, almost twice as high as the highest Alpine settlement, and at an altitude where the air contains roughly 60% of the oxygen at sea level. People who travel to that altitude as adults experience headaches, nausea, sleeplessness, and severe shortness of breath for days or weeks. People born and raised at that altitude develop larger lung capacity, denser red-blood-cell counts, and a different pattern of resting metabolism — biological adaptations that took thousands of years to spread through the highland populations.

Tiwanaku was a city of 20,000 to 40,000 people at this altitude. It had monumental stone architecture, including the largest precision-cut stone blocks anywhere in the pre-Columbian Americas. It had a planned urban core surrounded by a productive agricultural hinterland. It ran an empire of colonies stretching hundreds of miles in every direction. And it did all of this in a place where merely surviving the climate is a notable achievement.

How is the only interesting question.

---

The story has to begin with **water**.

The **altiplano** — the high plateau of the central Andes that surrounds Lake Titicaca — is a cold, treeless, windswept landscape that rarely warms above 50°F (10°C) even in summer and routinely drops below freezing at night. The growing season is short. The crops that work are limited: potatoes, quinoa, oca, ulluco, mashua — all of them tubers or grains specifically adapted to high cold conditions, and all of them vulnerable to the killing frosts that can sweep the plateau on any night of the year. A single hard frost can destroy an entire potato crop. Subsistence agriculture at 12,500 feet is a constant negotiation with climate, with the negotiation routinely lost.

What made Tiwanaku possible was a technology called **suka kollu** (SOO-kah KOH-yoo, Aymara for "raised fields") — and the technology, which the Tiwanaku perfected but did not invent, predates the city by centuries. Around the lake basin, the surrounding plain is poorly drained and prone to flooding when the lake rises and freezing when nighttime temperatures drop. The suka kollu system addresses both problems with a single piece of landscape engineering. Long parallel raised earthen platforms, roughly 15 feet (4.5 meters) wide and several hundred feet long, are built up above ground level. Between the platforms, narrow water channels are excavated. Crops are planted on the raised platforms; water sits in the channels.

The clever part is not the layout but the thermal physics. Water has a much higher heat capacity than air or dry soil. During the day, the water in the channels absorbs solar heat and warms up. At night, as the air temperature plunges, the water radiates that stored heat slowly into the surrounding ground and the air directly above the platforms. The temperature on the platform tops stays 2 to 3°C warmer than the surrounding plain — exactly the difference between a fatal frost and an unharmed crop. The water also irrigates the platforms by capillary action through the soil, and the channels themselves can be stocked with fish, ducks, and edible algae for additional protein.

This is not a guess. In the 1980s, the archaeologist **Clark Erickson** worked with Aymara farming communities near the modern village of Huatta to rebuild a section of suka kollu fields using ancient techniques. The reconstructed fields produced potato yields three times higher than conventional dryland farming on the same plain. The ancient Tiwanaku agricultural system, dormant for nearly a thousand years, still worked. The yield improvement was not theoretical or marginal — it was decisive. Suka kollu turned the cold flood-prone Titicaca basin into one of the most productive farming zones in the entire Andes.

The mechanism matters because it explains why Tiwanaku could exist. A pre-modern city of 30,000 people requires enormous food surplus from a substantial surrounding agricultural population. Without suka kollu, the altiplano around Lake Titicaca could not have produced that surplus. With suka kollu — covering, at the city's peak, perhaps 80,000 acres of altiplano floor — it could.

---

The city itself was laid out around two great ceremonial structures and a sprawl of residential neighborhoods.

The first structure was the **Akapana** (ah-kah-PAH-nah), a seven-tiered platform pyramid roughly 600 feet (180 meters) on a side at its base and about 60 feet (18 meters) tall. The Akapana is sometimes described as the largest pre-Columbian structure in South America, though that distinction more accurately belongs to Wari-era coastal mounds. What makes the Akapana distinctive is not just its size but the elaborate stone drainage system built into its interior: a network of carved stone channels that carried water down from a sunken courtyard at the summit, through the building in a deliberate zigzag pattern, and out into surrounding distribution channels. The drainage system was not a practical necessity — there was no rain on the altiplano substantial enough to require it. It was symbolic. The Akapana was a model of a sacred mountain (an **apu**, AH-poo, the Andean term for the mountain spirits that controlled water and fertility), and the water flowing through the building's veins was a ritual reenactment of the way water flowed from real mountain peaks down to the altiplano fields below. The building was a working scale model of the cosmology that fed the city.

The second structure was the **Kalasasaya** (kah-lah-sah-SIGH-ah, Aymara for "place of the standing stones"), a walled rectangular enclosure measuring roughly 400 by 200 feet (120 by 60 meters), built from massive upright sandstone pillars set into the ground at regular intervals along the perimeter. Inside the enclosure, several monolithic stone figures stood in the central courtyard, including the **Ponce Monolith** (a 10-foot-tall stone figure holding a kero drinking cup and a snuff tablet) and, on the western edge, the famous **Gateway of the Sun**.

The Kalasasaya's eastern wall contains a precisely placed gateway. On the spring and autumn equinoxes, the rising sun shines through this gateway and casts a light beam across the enclosure to a specific point on the western wall. This is not coincidence. The Kalasasaya was an astronomical observatory of a kind, calibrated to mark the solar year. Other gateways and pillars within the complex appear to have marked the solstices, the major lunar events, and the rising and setting points of certain bright stars. Tiwanaku's planners, like most pre-modern monumental builders, embedded their religious calendar in the architecture itself, and the architecture was precise enough that the alignments still work today.

---

The most famous single object at Tiwanaku is the **Gateway of the Sun**.

It is a monolithic doorway carved from a single 10-ton block of dark gray andesite, roughly 9 feet (2.7 meters) tall and 13 feet (4 meters) wide, with a small rectangular opening cut through the center. The gateway stands in the northwestern corner of the Kalasasaya enclosure today, but archaeologists believe it has been moved at some point in the past — its original position is uncertain. What is not uncertain is what is carved on the upper lintel, which is one of the great pieces of Andean religious art and one of the most reproduced single images in pre-Columbian iconography.

The lintel shows a central figure standing frontally, facing the viewer, holding a staff in each hand. The figure's face is rectangular and stylized, with concentric circles for eyes and a downturned slit mouth. From its head radiates a sunburst headdress of feline and serpent figures, each emerging outward like rays. To either side of the central figure march 48 winged attendants — three rows of eight on each side — each carrying a staff or an offering, each rendered in the same stylized geometric vocabulary as the central figure. Below the central figure, a band of meander patterns and additional small figures runs across the rest of the lintel.

This central figure is the **Staff God**, and his appearance at the Gateway of the Sun is one of the most important single moments in Andean religious history. The Staff God image first appeared at Chavín de Huántar (Early Andean Civilizations, Chapter 7) on the **Raimondi Stele**, carved sometime around 500 BCE — over a thousand years before Tiwanaku. The Chavín Staff God was simpler: just the central figure, holding paired staffs, with an elaborate headdress. At Tiwanaku, the Staff God reappears with a sun-disk headdress, surrounded by his attendant figures, in a more elaborate but unmistakably continuous iconographic tradition. The same image then propagates from Tiwanaku into Wari art (with characteristic Wari modifications — see Chapter 7), and from Wari into the religious art of the Inca a thousand years later, where the same frontal pose, the same paired staffs, and the same radiating headdress appear in the figure of **Wiracocha** (wee-rah-KOH-chah), the Inca creator god.

This is genuinely remarkable. Across two thousand years and the entire length of the Andes — Chavín de Huántar in the northern highlands, Tiwanaku on the southern altiplano, Inca Cusco in the central highlands — the same basic divine image was preserved, modified, and transmitted as the supreme religious icon. There is nothing comparable in any other ancient civilization. The closest analogue might be the persistence of certain Indo-European storm-god images from the Hittite Tarhunna through the Greek Zeus to the Norse Thor, but even that lineage took different visual forms in different cultures. The Andean Staff God preserved its visual form, generation after generation, for longer than the entire history of Mediterranean civilization between Homer and the present day. What made this possible — what kept the image stable across a thousand miles and a thousand years — is unknown, but the persistence is one of the deepest features of Andean religion.

---

The third great architectural achievement at Tiwanaku is more controversial.

**Pumapunku** (POO-mah-POON-koo, "Door of the Puma" in Aymara) is a separate platform structure, slightly south of the main Tiwanaku ceremonial core. The platform itself is unimpressive — a low rectangular mound, originally about 550 feet (170 meters) on a side, with a sunken plaza on top. What makes Pumapunku famous, and infamous, is the masonry. The platform is built from precisely cut and interlocking stone blocks of andesite and red sandstone, some of them weighing over 100 tons (the largest, called the Plataforma Lítica, weighs an estimated 130 tons). The blocks are cut to fit each other with tolerances measured in millimeters. Many are H-shaped or T-shaped, with notches and tenons designed to interlock with adjacent blocks the way pieces of a jigsaw puzzle fit together. The cuts are flat enough that you can run a hand across two adjacent block surfaces and not feel the join. The corners are sharp enough that they appear, to a modern eye, to have been finished with diamond saws.

They were not. The Tiwanaku stoneworkers had no iron tools, no diamond blades, no power equipment. What they had was copper chisels, harder igneous stone hammers, sand and water for abrasive grinding, and (apparently) infinite patience. Modern masons working with period tools and techniques have repeatedly reproduced Pumapunku-style cutting, demonstrating that the precision is achievable through skilled hand-work — slowly. The Tiwanaku built Pumapunku with the same patient labor with which the Egyptians built the pyramids, the same patient labor with which medieval cathedral masons cut Gothic vaulting. The work is impressive precisely because it took so long.

Pumapunku has become a magnet for pseudoarchaeology — Erich von Däniken's 1968 *Chariots of the Gods* claimed the precision-cut blocks could not have been made by ancient hand tools and must therefore be evidence of advanced lost technology, ancient astronauts, or Atlantean refugees. None of these claims survive contact with the actual archaeology. The blocks are made of local stone, cut from quarries within a few miles of the site (the andesite from a quarry on the Copacabana peninsula, the red sandstone from quarries near Yunguyu, both transportable by raft across Lake Titicaca and then by sledge across the altiplano). The cutting techniques have been demonstrated. The precision is impressive but not impossible. Pumapunku is an extraordinary achievement of pre-modern human craftsmanship, and the impulse to attribute it to anything other than human craftsmanship is a deep insult to the Tiwanaku stoneworkers themselves.

The platform was probably the foundation for an upper gateway complex now lost — the H-shaped blocks would have interlocked to form vertical walls and lintels of a structure similar in concept to the Kalasasaya gateway but executed in much harder stone and on a much larger scale. The full structure is gone, dismantled at some point after Tiwanaku's abandonment, the cut blocks scattered across the platform surface in the heap that visitors see today.

---

The economy that supported all of this was the **llama caravan**.

Llamas — the only large domestic pack animal in the Americas before the Spanish — had been used by Andean people since at least 4000 BCE (Early Andean Civilizations, Chapter 3), but Tiwanaku was the first state to organize llama transport at imperial scale. A typical caravan might include 200 to 500 llamas, each carrying a load of 60 to 80 pounds (27 to 36 kg) over distances of hundreds of miles. The caravans moved freeze-dried potatoes (called **chuño**, choo-NYOH, the same product that highland farmers still produce today by exposing potatoes to night frost and daytime sun on the altiplano), dried llama meat (**charqui**, CHAR-kee, the source of the English word "jerky"), copper from the mines of the southern altiplano, **obsidian** for cutting tools from the highland sources, and finished textiles from the Tiwanaku weaving workshops outward to the colonies. Coming back inward, the caravans brought maize from the lower-elevation Cochabamba colonies, coca leaves from the eastern slopes, salt from the puna, and dried fish from the coastal Moquegua colony.

The caravan was not just transportation. It was the circulatory system of the Tiwanaku economy. The vertical archipelago described in Chapter 1 only worked if products from distant ecological zones could actually move between the colonies and the central capital, and the llama was the technology that made the movement possible. Without llamas, Tiwanaku's reach would have been limited to whatever a man could carry on his back across the altiplano — a few pounds, a few days. With llamas, the reach extended to multi-week journeys carrying multiple tons of cargo each.

By comparison: the contemporary Wari state, in the central highlands, also used llama caravans, but Wari had a more concentrated geography and a denser road network. Tiwanaku had a more dispersed colony system and longer caravan routes. Both were running the largest pack-animal logistics operations in the pre-Columbian Americas, and both worked because the llama can survive at altitudes where the European horse, the African camel, and the Asian ox would all die.

---

A pause for what we don't know about Tiwanaku political organization. There is no king-list. There are no royal tombs at Tiwanaku — the city has been excavated for over a century and no recognizable royal burial has been found. The ceremonial core is full of stone monoliths with carved figures (the Bennett Monolith, the Ponce Monolith, the Fraile Monolith), but none of them carries an inscription identifying the depicted person, and the iconography is consistent enough across these monoliths that they may all be representations of a single deity rather than portraits of individual rulers. The standard scholarly reading is that Tiwanaku was probably ruled by a hereditary elite organized around the dominant ayllu lineages, with religious specialists playing a major political role and the actual ruler (if there was a single ruler at any given time) operating more as a high priest than as a king in the Mesopotamian or Egyptian sense. None of this is well documented. The honest answer is that we don't know how Tiwanaku was governed beyond the broad fact that it was elaborately organized.

What we do know is that for roughly 500 years — from around 500 to 1000 CE — Tiwanaku was the dominant power on the southern altiplano. Its monumental architecture went up over decades. Its raised-field zone covered most of the lake basin. Its colonies stretched from the Pacific coast to the eastern Bolivian valleys to the Atacama oases of northern Chile. Its art style and religious iconography spread across the south-central Andes. And then, around 1000 CE, it stopped being the dominant power, abruptly enough that the next Chapter 8 will return to that question.

The next chapter shifts north and west, to the rival highland state that was doing something parallel and yet different in the Ayacucho basin of central Peru: Wari.

# Chapter 3 — Wari Founding

Wari was younger than Tiwanaku and stranger.

Where Tiwanaku grew slowly out of a centuries-old village tradition on the shores of Lake Titicaca, Wari erupted relatively suddenly into the Ayacucho basin of the central Peruvian highlands sometime around 550 to 600 CE. Where Tiwanaku grew through the elaboration of religious architecture and astronomical alignment, Wari grew through the construction of standardized administrative compounds whose floor plans look like the work of a single planning office. Where Tiwanaku ran an empire of distant kin-based colonies, Wari ran an empire of grid-built provincial capitals connected by a road network. The two states were contemporaries, drew on the same religious vocabulary, occupied roughly equal portions of the central Andes, and yet pursued the imperial project in measurably different ways.

The Ayacucho basin sits at roughly 9,000 feet (2,750 meters) — much lower than the Tiwanaku altiplano, mild enough to grow maize as well as the high-altitude tubers, well-watered by the rivers descending from the surrounding peaks. The basin had been settled for centuries by a regional culture archaeologists call **Huarpa** (HWAR-pah), known mainly from a distinctive painted ceramic style and a scatter of small to medium-sized villages. Around 550 CE, in the southern part of the basin, the Huarpa villages began to be reorganized into a much larger urban agglomeration centered on a previously minor site called **Wari** (or Huari, the modern Spanish spelling — both forms are used in the literature, with "Wari" preferred in recent English-language scholarship for both the empire and its capital). Within a generation or two, the city had grown to over 25,000 people and was projecting power far beyond the Ayacucho basin.

What changed?

---

The standard scholarly answer points to **Conchopata** (kohn-choh-PAH-tah), a smaller ceremonial site within the Ayacucho basin, where some of the most striking single artifacts of the Middle Horizon have been excavated.

At Conchopata, archaeologists have uncovered dozens of **giant ceramic urns** — the largest over four feet tall, capable of holding 30 gallons of liquid — that were ritually smashed and deposited in offering pits sometime in the late sixth century CE. The urns are painted in polychrome with elaborate iconographic programs, and the iconography is unmistakable: it is the **Staff God** of Tiwanaku, with all the same attributes — frontal pose, paired staffs, sunburst headdress, attendant figures — rendered in a clearly Tiwanaku-derived visual vocabulary but with characteristically central-Peruvian execution. The Conchopata urns are the smoking gun for one of the central facts about Wari: its religious vocabulary was imported wholesale from Tiwanaku, somehow, sometime around 550 to 600 CE, and the import event was elaborate enough to prompt the ritual smashing of giant urns in a public ceremony.

How exactly did Tiwanaku iconography get to Ayacucho, 500 miles (800 km) away across mountainous terrain? The most plausible mechanism is religious pilgrimage and elite emulation. The Ayacucho elite — the rising leadership of what would become the Wari state — appear to have made some kind of formal contact with Tiwanaku, either by sending pilgrims to the lakeside city or by hosting Tiwanaku ritual specialists on their own ground, and to have adopted the Tiwanaku Staff God complex as the religious legitimacy of their new political project. This is a familiar pattern in world history: a rising regional power adopts the prestige religion of an older established neighbor as a way of upgrading its own legitimacy. The Romans adopting Greek mythology, the Japanese adopting Chinese Buddhism, the Mughals adopting Persian high culture — all variations on the same move. Wari was doing the same thing with Tiwanaku iconography.

What Wari did not do was adopt Tiwanaku political institutions. The Wari state that emerged in the late sixth century looks nothing like Tiwanaku in administrative terms. Where Tiwanaku ran a colony-based empire focused on the Lake Titicaca core, Wari built a planned grid of administrative cities replicated across the territory it controlled. The grid was Wari's signature, and it appears almost as soon as the Conchopata urns are deposited.

---

The grid worked like this.

A typical Wari administrative compound is a rectangular enclosure, anywhere from 10 to 100 acres in area, surrounded by a high enclosing wall (often three or four meters thick at the base, several meters tall). Inside, the space is divided by interior walls into a grid of patio groups — clusters of small roofed rooms arranged around small open courtyards. Each patio group is roughly identical to its neighbors: same dimensions, same orientation, same room layout. The patios are typically arranged in repeating rows, and the corridors between them are narrow and easily controlled. Access to the compound is restricted to one or two gates in the perimeter wall.

The result is an architecture that screams central planning. There is nothing organic about a Wari compound. It looks designed — drafted, allocated, surveyed, laid out before any walls go up — and it looks exactly like the next Wari compound built two hundred miles away in a different ecological zone. The same template, applied identically, in city after city. This is a level of architectural standardization not seen anywhere else in the pre-Columbian Americas before the Inca, who would later replicate the model on a wider scale.

The function of these compounds has been debated, but the standard reading is that they served as **provincial administrative centers** — bases from which the Wari state collected tribute, organized labor, maintained storehouses, and conducted state-sponsored religious ceremonies. The repeating patio groups would have housed administrative officials, tribute storage rooms, workshop areas for state-controlled crafts production, and possibly residential quarters for visiting dignitaries. The high walls and restricted access suggest the compounds were also a piece of political theater — a visible demonstration of the central state's power, walled off from the local population, unmistakably foreign in form even when built in the middle of a long-settled valley.

The compounds appear across an enormous geographic range. **Pikillaqta** (pee-kee-YAHK-tah) in the Cusco basin, 350 miles (560 km) southeast of the capital. **Viracochapampa** in the northern highlands above Huamachuco, 350 miles (560 km) north. **Cajamarquilla** near the Pacific coast in the Rímac Valley by modern Lima. **Honcopampa** in the Callejón de Huaylas. **Azángaro** near modern Huanta. Each compound has the same template, applied to local materials and local site conditions, but unmistakably the work of the same imperial planning apparatus.

---

The most famous of these is **Pikillaqta** in the Cusco basin.

Pikillaqta covers about 120 acres (49 hectares) on a flat valley floor at 10,400 feet (3,170 meters) elevation, about 12 miles southeast of modern Cusco. Inside the perimeter wall, archaeologists have mapped over 700 individual rectangular rooms arranged in a rigorous grid of 501 patio groups, organized into four major sectors with high internal walls separating them. The structures stand two stories tall in places, and the second-story floors were originally accessed by removable wooden ladders rather than fixed stairs — a security feature that allowed each compound to be locked off from the next.

The site was built sometime around 650 CE, occupied for two or three centuries, and then deliberately abandoned around 900 CE. Many of the rooms appear never to have been finished — wall-plaster left half-applied, floors not fully laid down — suggesting the site may have been still under construction when the Wari imperial project entered its final phase. What was finished was occupied, used as a state administrative center for the Cusco region, and then sealed up and walked away from. The doorways of the abandoned structures were deliberately blocked with stone fill before the population dispersed, an unusual closure ritual that has parallels at other Wari sites.

The eerie thing about Pikillaqta is that it was built in territory that the **Inca** would later claim as their imperial heartland. Cusco — the city the Inca would build into the capital of their empire seven centuries later — sits 12 miles from Pikillaqta. The Inca cannot have failed to know about the older Wari compound. They certainly visited it; they probably understood what it had been; and the Inca administrative cities they later built across their own empire (Vilcashuaman, Huánuco Pampa, Tambo Colorado, dozens of others) were templated on Wari precedents in ways that would have been visibly obvious to the Inca planners. Pikillaqta is the prototype, and Inca Cusco is, in important respects, the production version.

---

The political-economic system that ran the Wari empire is generally called the **wamani system**, after the Quechua word **wamani** (wah-MAH-nee, "province" or "district") that the Inca later used for the same kind of territorial unit.

Wari territory was divided into wamani — the planned administrative compounds being the central place of each wamani — and each wamani sent labor tribute (**mit'a**, MEE-tah) to the central state. Mit'a was a specifically Andean institution: not slavery, not tax in money or goods, but rotating communal labor obligation. Each ayllu in each wamani owed the state a certain number of person-days of labor per year, with the labor being organized through community assemblies and supervised by state officials. The labor built the roads, maintained the storehouses, constructed the administrative buildings themselves, served in the army when the state had one, and produced state-controlled craft goods (state-quality textiles, state-quality ceramics) in the workshop sectors of the compounds. In exchange, the state provided religious ceremonies (the wamani compound usually included a state-sponsored shrine), guaranteed access to imperial storehouses in times of famine, and arbitrated disputes between communities.

This is essentially the same political-economic system the Inca would run, on a much larger scale, six centuries later. The Inca did not invent the wamani. They inherited it from Wari. They did not invent mit'a. They inherited it from Wari (and arguably from Tiwanaku as well — both highland states ran on mit'a). What the Inca did was scale the Wari template to cover a much larger empire, formalize it more rigorously, and project it across new territories. The institutional bones were Wari construction.

This is one of the central facts about Andean history that does not get its due in popular accounts. The Inca are usually presented as an imperial original — the first true Andean empire, the inventors of the road system and the storehouse network and the administrative geography. They were not. They were the inheritors of the Wari template, the second great execution of the same imperial model. Wari was the first. The Inca were the redux.

---

A note on what we know about Wari rulers: nothing.

As with Tiwanaku, Wari left no king-list. There are no royal tombs at the Wari capital that have been definitively identified as such. The single richest Wari elite burial found to date is at **El Castillo de Huarmey** on the north-central coast, excavated in 2012 by Polish archaeologists Miłosz Giersz and Patrycja Prządka-Giersz: an undisturbed mausoleum containing 58 high-status individuals, almost all women, in seated burial position with thousands of luxury offerings. The find was extraordinary — the richest pre-Inca burial assemblage ever recovered in Peru — and it has been interpreted as a royal mausoleum of some kind, possibly housing a powerful matrilineal dynasty. But none of the burials carries an inscription. None of the women is named. We know they were elite; we know they were buried with extraordinary wealth; we don't know what their political role was, what title they held, what relationship they bore to the central Wari state. The Huarmey burial is the closest we have to a Wari royal tomb, and it tells us only that elite women in Wari society could command burial honors comparable to elite men in other ancient civilizations.

This is consistent with a broader pattern in Wari society: the women who ran the chicha brewery at Cerro Baúl (Chapter 5), the women who staffed the workshop areas of the administrative compounds, the women buried with elite honors at Huarmey — Wari elite women appear to have held substantial economic and ritual authority in ways that were less common in the contemporary Mesoamerican and Eurasian empires. This is not speculation; it's documented in actual burial archaeology and workshop excavation, and the pattern is striking enough that it has reshaped scholarly thinking about pre-Inca gender roles in the central Andes.

---

The Wari capital itself — the city of Huari in the Ayacucho basin — was a sprawling urban center of perhaps 25,000 to 40,000 people at its peak, covering roughly 700 acres (300 hectares). The city's layout is messier than the planned provincial compounds: the central core is a dense cluster of patio groups and small plazas, while the periphery is more residential and less rigorously planned. The contrast is interesting. Wari built planned cities for everyone else, but lived in a partially organic capital itself — much the way a planning office might design model homes for the suburbs while the architects themselves live in older houses downtown.

Excavation at the Wari capital has been slow and incomplete. The site is large, the topography is irregular, and the structures are mostly built of fieldstone and adobe rather than the cut stone that survives well at Tiwanaku. Most of what is known about Wari governance comes from the provincial sites — Pikillaqta in particular — rather than from the capital itself. This is unusual. Most ancient empires are best documented at the capital. Wari is best documented in its provinces.

Cross-civ check: the contemporary **Tang Dynasty** capital at **Chang'an** (modern Xi'an) was the largest planned city in the world in the seventh century — a rigorous rectangular grid of over 100 city blocks within massive walls, designed to project Chinese imperial cosmology in physical form. The Wari planned compounds in central Peru, built at exactly the same time, are not in the same scale class as Chang'an — Pikillaqta would fit inside one of Chang'an's neighborhood blocks — but the basic concept is similar. Both were imperial states using rigorous architectural planning to project the central state's authority into peripheral regions. The Tang Chinese were doing it on the largest scale of any pre-modern civilization. The Wari, half a world away, were doing it independently, using the same architectural language of high walls and rectangular grids, with no contact between them and no shared antecedent. The convergence is one of the more interesting parallel inventions in world history.

---

# Chapter 4 — The Two Empires Side by Side

For roughly four centuries — from the late sixth century to the late tenth century CE — Tiwanaku and Wari shared the central Andes. They were geographic neighbors. They drew on the same religious vocabulary. They used many of the same agricultural and craft technologies. They occupied roughly equal swaths of territory, ran imperial economies of comparable scale, and shaped the cultural landscape of the entire south-central Andes between them. They were the two great powers of the Middle Horizon, and they were demonstrably contemporary.

What is remarkable is that we don't know whether they ever fought each other.

There are no known battles between Wari and Tiwanaku. There are no Wari burials of warriors killed by Tiwanaku weapons, and vice versa. There are no fortifications on the Wari frontier facing Tiwanaku, or on the Tiwanaku frontier facing Wari. There are no chronicles, since there is no writing. There is exactly one place in the entire Andes where Wari and Tiwanaku settlements existed close enough to each other to interact directly, and at that place — Cerro Baúl in the Moquegua Valley, the subject of Chapter 5 — the only documented event is a peaceful brewery shutdown ceremony.

This is genuinely strange. Most pairs of contemporary empires of comparable scale leave a record of conflict — at minimum, frontier skirmishes, defensive walls, diplomatic exchanges visible in trade records. Wari and Tiwanaku leave none of that. The two empires appear to have spent four centuries either ignoring each other entirely, or interacting in some elaborate diplomatic pattern that we have not yet recognized in the archaeological record.

---

Some structural comparison helps frame the puzzle.

**Geographically**, Tiwanaku was a southern altiplano power and Wari was a central highland power. The two cores were roughly 500 miles (800 km) apart, separated by mountainous terrain, multiple river drainages, and substantial unsettled puna grassland. Neither empire's effective administrative reach extended all the way to the other's core. Wari's southern frontier ran through the Ayacucho basin and into the Moquegua Valley on the south coast. Tiwanaku's northern frontier ran along the southern side of Lake Titicaca and into the Atacama oases. The two frontiers met, when they met at all, in the Moquegua Valley — and only in that one valley.

**Administratively**, Wari was a centralized state with planned provincial compounds and a road network linking them. Tiwanaku was a more dispersed state organized around colonies in distant ecological zones, linked to the lake-basin core by llama caravan rather than by paved road. Wari looks bureaucratic in its imperial structure; Tiwanaku looks more religious-confederational, with the colony settlements operating with more autonomy than the wamani provincial centers would have allowed.

**Religiously**, both empires worshipped versions of the Staff God (Chapter 7 will explore this in more detail), and both made extensive ritual use of the psychoactive plant **vilca** (VEEL-kah, *Anadenanthera colubrina*) and the **San Pedro** cactus (*Echinopsis pachanoi*). Both buried elite individuals with snuff trays, snuff tubes, and elaborate kero drinking cups. The shared religious vocabulary suggests either a common origin (probably the lingering Chavín-derived theological inheritance — Early Andean Civilizations, Chapter 7) or active sharing across the contemporary frontier, or both. The iconography is similar enough that for most of the twentieth century, archaeologists used the term "Tiahuanaco" indiscriminately for both empires' religious art, and only careful comparative work in the mid-twentieth century (notably by **Julio C. Tello**, the pioneer of Andean archaeology — Andean Kingdoms, Chapter 2) established that there were really two distinct artistic traditions.

**Economically**, both empires ran on the vertical archipelago and on the labor tribute mit'a system. Both produced large quantities of fine textiles, polychrome ceramics, and worked metal. Both used llama caravans for long-distance transport. Both stored agricultural surplus in centralized storehouses. The two economic systems were variations on a theme, not fundamental departures.

**Linguistically**, the standard scholarly hypothesis is that Wari is the historical reason **Quechua** (KETCH-wah) became the dominant language of the central Andes long before the Inca, and Tiwanaku is the historical reason **Aymara** (eye-MAH-rah) became the dominant language of the southern altiplano. Both hypotheses are plausible but not certain — neither empire left any written language for verification, so the linguistic geography is reconstructed from where Quechua and Aymara are spoken today and where they would have radiated outward from in the Middle Horizon. Quechua and Aymara are genuinely separate language families (as different from each other as English is from Greek), which suggests that Wari and Tiwanaku elites probably could not understand each other's everyday speech, even though they shared a religious vocabulary.

---

So what was the relationship?

There are essentially four scholarly positions, none of them definitively confirmed by the evidence.

**Position one** is that Tiwanaku and Wari were rival empires that maintained a tense armed peace through some combination of mutual deterrence, frontier diplomacy, and the geographic buffer between their cores. The lack of evidence for active warfare in this view is a sign of successful deterrence rather than absent conflict — the two empires kept careful distance from each other and managed the one shared frontier (Moquegua) through formal protocols. This view is consistent with the archaeology of Cerro Baúl, where Wari built a defensive mesa-top fortress in clear view of the Tiwanaku colony below but never attacked it.

**Position two** is that Tiwanaku and Wari were allies or partners, sharing a religious vocabulary by mutual consent and dividing the central Andes between them by some kind of negotiated arrangement. In this view, the lack of conflict is the natural result of an actual alliance, and Cerro Baúl is the diplomatic frontier post where the alliance was managed in person. This is harder to argue from the evidence — there's no clear sign of active alliance behavior either — but it cannot be ruled out.

**Position three** is that Tiwanaku and Wari operated in largely separate spheres and had no significant interaction with each other, the shared religious vocabulary being a residual inheritance from earlier Chavín-derived traditions rather than active contemporary sharing. In this view, the two empires were simply running parallel imperial projects in adjacent geographic spaces and never had occasion to engage seriously. This view has some support from how few sites show clear cultural mixing.

**Position four** is some combination of the above, varying by period — alliance early, rivalry late, mutual indifference in between, with the actual relationship probably more complex than any single label can capture. This is the position most working archaeologists hold today, in part because the evidence really is too thin to support any single neat narrative.

The honest summary is: we don't know. The Middle Horizon is the most populous archaeological period in the central Andes for which we have the least political-history data, because neither of the two great powers wrote anything down, and the diplomatic records that would have been kept on perishable materials (textile khipus, painted boards, oral tradition) have not survived.

---

What we can say is that the two empires looked outward in different directions and pursued imperial expansion through measurably different mechanisms.

**Wari** pushed north and west. Wari administrative compounds appear in the central highlands (the capital region), the northern highlands (Viracochapampa), the central coast (Cajamarquilla, Pachacamac), the south-central highlands (Pikillaqta in the Cusco basin), and as far north as the Cajamarca region in the upper Marañón. Wari did not push much south or east. The empire's geographic reach is roughly that of central and northern Peru, not extending into Bolivia or northern Chile. Wari expansion involved building administrative compounds in conquered or co-opted territory, integrating them into the road network, and running them through the wamani system. The expansion looks bureaucratic and somewhat top-down.

**Tiwanaku** pushed south and west. Tiwanaku colonies appear in the Moquegua Valley on the south coast (where the empire's reach met Wari's, at Cerro Baúl), the Cochabamba Valley in modern Bolivia, the **Atacama** oasis settlements of northern Chile, and as far south as the Tarapacá region. Tiwanaku did not push much north. The empire's geographic reach is roughly that of the southern altiplano, southern Peru, eastern Bolivia, and northern Chile. Tiwanaku expansion involved planting communities of Tiwanaku-affiliated people in distant ecological zones and maintaining them through the llama caravan system. The expansion looks more demographic and more bottom-up.

These two expansion patterns are complementary, not overlapping. Wari took the north and central. Tiwanaku took the south and the eastern slopes. Together they covered most of the central Andes, with relatively little direct geographic competition.

Whether this complementarity was deliberate (an alliance dividing the territory) or accidental (each empire just pushed in the directions it was best positioned to push) is one of the unsolved questions about the Middle Horizon. Either way, the result was that the two empires controlled, between them, a larger contiguous portion of the Andes than any single power had ever controlled before. The Inca, six centuries later, would simply re-conquer the same combined territory under a single political umbrella.

---

A pause for what the lack of writing means in practice. Other contemporary world powers — the Tang Chinese, the early Islamic caliphate, the Byzantine Empire — left behind diplomatic correspondence between empires, reciprocal embassy records, treaties, peace settlements, marriage alliances among ruling houses. We know who the Byzantine emperor was when he received an Abbasid caliph's letter; we know what the letter said; we know how he responded. None of this exists for Wari and Tiwanaku. There are no letters, no embassies, no treaties, no marriage alliances we can document. The two empires interacted, certainly — the shared religious vocabulary alone establishes that — but the *content* of the interaction is invisible to us. We have the architecture and the iconography. We don't have the conversation.

This is the basic limitation of Andean archaeology. The Middle Horizon was a fully imperial period in every measurable structural sense — large urban populations, planned administrative systems, long-distance road and caravan networks, integrated craft production and tribute economies, shared religious frameworks. But it produced no historical record in the modern sense. Everything we know about the period has been pulled out of the dirt. Everything about the relationship between Tiwanaku and Wari is inferred from physical proximity and material similarity, not from any actual statement either empire made about the other. The two great powers of the Middle Horizon simply did not say anything we can read about each other.

The single best window into how they actually interacted, when they actually interacted, is the one place where they lived within sight of each other for three centuries: the Wari frontier outpost of Cerro Baúl in the Moquegua Valley.

---

# Chapter 5 — Cerro Baúl

**Cerro Baúl** (SEH-roh bah-OOL, "Trunk Hill" in Spanish, named for its flat-topped silhouette) is a mesa in the Moquegua Valley of southern Peru, rising about 2,000 feet (600 meters) above the surrounding desert valley floor, with sheer cliffs on three sides and a difficult switchback path on the fourth. The summit covers roughly 25 acres of relatively flat ground, exposed to constant wind, with no water source. It is one of the most defensible sites in the central Andes and one of the most spectacularly impractical places anyone has ever chosen to build a city.

Around 600 CE, the Wari built a city on it.

Cerro Baúl is the southernmost outpost of the Wari empire, a frontier installation roughly 700 miles (1,100 km) from the imperial capital in Ayacucho. It was placed at the geographic edge of Wari's expansion and at the intersection of several important trade routes — the Moquegua Valley connects the south-central highlands to the Pacific coast and is one of the few places where coca-growing yunga zones, maize-growing quechua zones, and salt-producing puna zones are all within a day's walk of each other. Whoever controlled the Moquegua Valley controlled an unusually rich vertical archipelago in compact form.

The catch is that **Tiwanaku** had been planting colonies in the same valley since at least 600 CE.

Tiwanaku settlements at sites like **Omo**, **Chen Chen**, and **Cerro Echenique** — all located on the valley floor a few miles downhill from the Cerro Baúl mesa — were full-scale Tiwanaku colonies, with Tiwanaku-style ceramics, Tiwanaku-style architecture, Tiwanaku-style burials, and presumably Tiwanaku-affiliated populations. The Wari elite at Cerro Baúl built their fortress mesa-city in plain sight of these Tiwanaku colonies. From the summit of Cerro Baúl, the Tiwanaku settlements at the valley floor would have been clearly visible, and from the valley floor the Wari fortress on the mesa would have loomed overhead like a watchtower.

The two settlements existed within sight of each other for roughly three centuries, from about 600 CE to about 1000 CE. As far as the archaeological record shows, they did not fight. They did not intermarry to any significant degree. They did not exchange ceramics or building styles. They did not hybridize culturally. The Tiwanaku colonies stayed Tiwanaku; the Wari fortress stayed Wari; and somehow this state of armed peaceful coexistence persisted for about as long as the United States has existed.

---

The Wari built Cerro Baúl as a small but elaborate fortress city, with everything that an elite frontier outpost required.

The mesa top was leveled and laid out with the standard Wari architectural template: rectangular patio groups, internal grid layout, restricted access controlled by a single switchback path up the cliff. There was a ceremonial center with a small temple, residential quarters for the elite Wari population (probably never more than a few hundred people at any given time), workshop areas for state-controlled craft production, storage rooms for tribute goods, and — most distinctively — a substantial **brewery** for the production of **chicha** (CHEE-chah), the lightly alcoholic corn beer that was the central beverage of Andean ritual life.

The Cerro Baúl brewery is one of the most fully excavated and best understood industrial installations of the Middle Horizon. It was a long rectangular building with a series of large adobe-walled rooms, each containing rows of substantial ceramic vats for boiling, fermenting, and storing chicha. The brewery's production capacity has been estimated at several hundred gallons per batch — far more than the few hundred Wari elite residents on the mesa could possibly have consumed themselves. The brewery was a state-scale industrial operation, and its output was used for diplomatic feasting on a substantial scale.

Chicha in the Andes was not just an alcoholic beverage. It was the lubricant of political relationships. Andean political authority was constituted through reciprocal feasting: a leader who could host a large enough chicha feast, with enough food and drink and ritual elaboration, demonstrated the ability to mobilize resources at a level that bound the assembled guests into reciprocal obligation. The leader who hosted the feast became, by the act of hosting, the higher-status party in the relationship; the guests, by accepting hospitality, accepted a certain subordination. This is not modern speculation — it is documented in detail in Spanish chronicler accounts of how the later Inca state used feasting to consolidate political relationships, and the same mechanism was clearly at work centuries earlier.

So a brewery at Cerro Baúl producing several hundred gallons of chicha at a time was not a luxury. It was a piece of essential political infrastructure, used to host the regional dignitaries — including, almost certainly, Tiwanaku officials from the colonies down in the valley — at periodic state feasts that maintained whatever diplomatic accommodation the two empires had worked out for sharing the Moquegua Valley. The brewery was the embassy.

---

Excavation of the brewery has produced the single most evocative archaeological scene from the entire Middle Horizon.

Sometime around 1000 CE, as the Wari empire was approaching its final collapse (Chapter 8), the elite at Cerro Baúl held a final ritual feast. The brewery produced one last large batch of chicha. The participants — Wari officials, almost certainly some number of Tiwanaku guests as well — drank from elaborately painted ceramic **keros** (KEH-rohs, the standardized polychrome cups used for ritual chicha consumption). Substantial quantities of food were consumed. Then, in a closing ritual that has been carefully reconstructed from the archaeological deposits, the participants smashed their kero cups against the brewery walls. The fragments fell where they were thrown. The brewery itself was then set on fire. The roof beams collapsed. The brewing vats were left in place. The Wari elite walked down off the mesa and never came back.

Archaeologists found the brewery exactly as the Wari left it: the smashed kero fragments still scattered against the walls, the burned roof debris collapsed into the rooms, the brewing vats still in their original positions. Patrick Williams and Donna Nash, the archaeologists who have led the Cerro Baúl excavations since the late 1980s, were able to reconstruct the entire closing ceremony from the position of the smashed cups, the pattern of burning, and the orderly arrangement of the abandoned brewing equipment. Nobody had looted the site after the Wari left. The summit was inaccessible enough, and the regional political situation chaotic enough, that Cerro Baúl simply lay undisturbed for nine hundred years until the modern excavations began.

What was the closing ceremony commemorating? Almost certainly the formal end of the Wari political project at the southern frontier. The empire was contracting, the road network was becoming impossible to maintain, the Moquegua outpost was no longer sustainable. Rather than just abandoning the brewery — leaving it for whoever came along to occupy or strip — the Wari elite chose to formally close it down through a public ritual. Smashing the cups was an act of decommissioning: the brewery would never produce chicha for diplomatic feasting again, and the cups that had carried the chicha were destroyed to mark the end of that function. Burning the building was an act of consecration: the structure's role was complete, and it would be returned to the gods rather than left to the next political occupant.

The smashing-the-cup ceremony at Cerro Baúl is one of those moments where the silence of the Andean record briefly speaks. We don't know what the participants said to each other. We don't know what songs were sung or what speeches were given. But we know that they gathered, drank chicha together one last time, smashed the cups against the walls in a coordinated act, set fire to the building they had drunk in, and walked away. The act was elaborate enough to have required substantial planning, somber enough to have left no signs of subsequent looting or vandalism, and conscious enough to have produced the orderly final-deposit pattern that modern excavation has uncovered. It is a piece of political theater preserved in physical debris.

---

What does Cerro Baúl tell us about the Wari-Tiwanaku relationship?

It tells us, first, that the relationship was real. The two empires had at least one place — and possibly only one place — where they conducted face-to-face diplomatic relations on a sustained basis. The brewery at Cerro Baúl was producing chicha for diplomatic feasting throughout the three centuries of co-occupation, which means there were diplomatic feasts to feed. There were Tiwanaku officials traveling up the switchback path to attend Wari banquets. There were Wari officials going down to the valley floor to attend reciprocal Tiwanaku events. There was an ongoing protocol relationship between the two empires.

It tells us, second, that the relationship was managed without fighting. The defenses at Cerro Baúl were elaborate (the steep cliffs, the single switchback access, the perimeter walls on the summit), but they were never tested in combat. There are no Wari skeletons at Cerro Baúl with weapon trauma from Tiwanaku-style spears or maces. There are no Tiwanaku skeletons in the Moquegua Valley with weapon trauma from Wari weapons. The defenses functioned as deterrence, possibly, or as ritual statements of imperial identity, possibly — but not as actual battle infrastructure. The two empires kept the peace for three centuries.

It tells us, third, that the relationship was conducted through ritualized social interaction rather than political subordination. There is no evidence that one empire dominated the other in the Moquegua Valley. The Wari fortress was on the mesa; the Tiwanaku colonies were on the valley floor; both held their ground. The only sustained interaction we can document is the chicha feasting — a form of social interaction that, by its nature, expresses status hierarchy ambiguously enough that two contemporary empires could engage in it without either acknowledging subordination to the other. Both empires hosted; both empires were hosted; both empires went home.

This is a strikingly modern-sounding pattern of inter-state relations. Not alliance, not subordination, not conflict — simply ongoing diplomatic engagement managed through ceremonial protocol, between two powers that recognized each other as roughly equal and decided that the costs of the alternative arrangements were too high. The Wari and Tiwanaku in the Moquegua Valley invented, for their own purposes, something like the modern category of "peer-to-peer diplomatic relations." They kept it up for three hundred years. Then the climate broke, the empires collapsed, and the brewery's roof was set on fire.

# Chapter 6 — Wari Roads, Storehouses, Khipus

The administrative apparatus that ran the Wari empire was built around three pieces of infrastructure: the **road network** that connected the planned compounds, the **storehouses** (qollqa) that held tribute goods, and the **khipu** knotted-string records that tracked what was where. All three were Wari inventions in the central Andean context. All three were inherited, refined, and scaled up by the Inca half a millennium later. This chapter is about the imperial state mechanisms whose existence is implied by the architecture and the pottery — the bureaucratic technology of Andean empire.

---

The **Wari road network** was the first true paved road system in the central Andes.

What had existed before Wari was a patchwork of trails, llama caravan paths, and short paved sections built by individual coastal cultures (the Moche maintained good local roads in their valleys, for instance — Andean Kingdoms, Chapter 4). What Wari built, beginning sometime around 650 CE, was a coordinated network of paved roads connecting the imperial capital at Huari to the major provincial compounds and onward to the regional centers in their territories. The roads were typically 6 to 25 feet (2–8 meters) wide, paved with cut stone where the terrain allowed, lined with retaining walls on steep mountain slopes, fitted with stone staircases on the steepest sections, and supported by occasional way-stations called **tambos** (a word the Inca later adopted for the same kind of installation) where travelers could rest and llama caravans could be reorganized.

The total length of the Wari road system has not been precisely measured — the network is partially obscured today by later Inca and modern construction on the same routes — but the conservative estimate is several thousand miles of integrated paved road across central Peru. This is a major piece of pre-modern infrastructure. The Roman road network at its peak covered roughly 50,000 miles across Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East; the Wari road network covered perhaps a tenth of that across a much smaller geographic area, but with comparable engineering quality and the same purpose: moving administrators, soldiers, tribute, and information across an empire faster than the surrounding countryside could move itself.

Where the Roman road network was designed primarily for legions on foot and wheeled vehicles, the Wari network was designed for **llama caravans** and **runners** (and pedestrian traffic in general — the Andes had no wheeled vehicles, no pack horses, and no draft animals heavier than the llama, until the Spanish brought them centuries later). This shaped the road design: stone staircases that are useless for wheels but easy for llamas and humans, narrow sections through cliffs that would be impossible for carts, suspension bridges over gorges that swung gently underfoot. The Wari roads handled the load they were designed for, and the Inca later inherited and extended the same network — the famous **Qhapaq Ñan** (KAH-pak NYAN) Inca road system that the Spanish marveled at in the sixteenth century was, in its central Peruvian core, largely a refurbishment of the older Wari network, often built on top of the same physical roadbeds.

---

The **storehouse system** — called **qollqa** (KOH-yah, the Quechua word the Inca later applied) — was the other half of Wari's logistical infrastructure.

Each Wari administrative compound contained rows of small standardized storage rooms, typically rectangular, with tightly fitted doors and elevated stone floors to keep moisture out. The contents of these rooms have been recovered in various states of preservation: charred grain residues, dried potato chuño, freeze-dried llama meat (charqui), bundled textiles, stacked ceramic vessels, copper ingots and finished metal goods. The storehouses were not for the local population's everyday food needs. They were imperial reserves — tribute collected from the surrounding wamani, stockpiled for future redistribution as the central state determined.

The storehouse model served several functions simultaneously. It provided a buffer against famine: in years of poor harvest, the central state could redistribute stored grain to provinces in need, both relieving suffering and consolidating political loyalty. It provided supply for state activities: when the army marched (and Wari did have an army, though the evidence for major military campaigns is thin), the soldiers ate from the imperial storehouses along the route rather than living off the land. It provided diplomatic gifts: the kind of feasting infrastructure that Cerro Baúl maintained on a small scale for its frontier diplomacy was duplicated across the empire on larger scales for major ceremonial events. And it provided a visible symbol of state power: a Wari provincial center with full storehouses was demonstrably capable of supporting a major political event on short notice, and demonstrably authorized to extract tribute from the surrounding population at a level that could fill the rooms.

The Inca later scaled this system up enormously. The Inca storehouse complex at **Huánuco Pampa** in the central Peruvian highlands contained over 500 storage rooms; the complex at **Cotapachi** near Cochabamba in modern Bolivia contained over 2,000. The Spanish chroniclers who first encountered the Inca state in the 1530s were astonished by the scale of the storehouse system, which they had no European parallel for and which they correctly identified as one of the central pieces of Inca state power. What the Spanish did not realize is that the storehouse system was not an Inca invention. The Inca had inherited it from Wari, scaled it up by an order of magnitude, but kept the basic architecture and function intact.

---

The **khipu** system — knotted strings used as accounting and possibly textual records — is the third piece of the Wari administrative legacy, and it is the most controversial.

A khipu (KEE-poo, Quechua for "knot") is a primary cord with secondary cords attached, with knots tied at varying intervals along the secondary cords in patterns that encode information. The Inca khipu, as documented by Spanish chroniclers in the sixteenth century, was primarily a numerical record — a decimal accounting system in which the number, position, and type of knots on each cord encoded numerical values, with subsidiary cords encoding categories. Inca khipus were used to track tribute payments, population census data, military supplies, and other quantitative state information. Specialized khipu-keepers (**khipukamayuq**) maintained the records and could recite the contents of a khipu by reading the knots like text. Modern decipherment has confirmed the basic numerical structure of Inca khipus and continues to debate whether some Inca khipus also encoded narrative or linguistic content.

Wari khipus are older and structurally different. The earliest known khipu-like records come from Wari sites and predate the Inca version by several centuries. Wari khipus do not use the decimal knotted system that the Inca later perfected. They use a different structural grammar — wrapped color-coded threads tied around the main cord rather than knotted directly into it — and the encoding system has not been deciphered. Modern scholars (notably **Gary Urton** at Harvard, who has done much of the foundational work on khipu decipherment) have argued that Wari khipus carry information of some kind and represent the original ancestor of the Inca system, but exactly what kind of information is still under investigation.

What this means in practice is that the Wari empire was running a complex administrative state — collecting tribute, organizing labor, distributing supplies, maintaining a road network — without writing in the standard sense, but with some kind of recording system that allowed information to be stored, transmitted, and consulted across the imperial geography. The khipu was the database. The wamani officials who managed it were the administrators. The system was sophisticated enough to keep an empire running for several centuries.

This is actually a major intellectual achievement. The khipu represents an alternative solution to the problem that writing solved in other civilizations — how do you store information persistently across time and space, in a form that can be accurately consulted by people who were not present when the information was originally generated? Most other ancient civilizations solved this with writing — marks on clay, stone, papyrus, bone, or paper. The Andean civilizations solved it with knotted strings. The two solutions are not equivalent: writing is more flexible and more linguistically expressive than knotted strings, which is probably why writing-based civilizations developed richer literary, religious, and philosophical traditions than the Andes. But the khipu was sufficient for what the Andean state needed it to do, which was administrative record-keeping at imperial scale.

---

A note on what writing might have looked like in the Andes if it had developed differently. **Tocapu** (toh-KAH-poo) is the term Spanish chroniclers used for the small geometric symbols woven into elite Andean textiles — square or rectangular figures arranged in grid patterns on tunics, mantles, and other high-status garments. Wari tunics, in particular, are densely covered with tocapu, with the same symbols appearing across many different garments in patterns that suggest they had stable, recognized meanings. Some scholars have argued that the tocapu system was an emergent writing system — a vocabulary of woven symbols that could encode names, titles, lineages, or simple statements when arranged in particular sequences. This is contested. The honest scholarly position is that we don't know whether tocapu carried symbolic meaning beyond decorative patterning, and we don't have a key for decoding any specific tocapu sequence. If tocapu was proto-writing, it never developed past the proto stage. The Andes remained, formally, a non-literate civilization until the Spanish brought the alphabet in the sixteenth century.

What the Andes did develop was a sophisticated visual culture — iconography on textiles, ceramics, and stone — that carried information in ways no alphabetic civilization had to develop. The Staff God image you read about in Chapter 2, with its distinctive frontal pose and paired staffs, was a visual sign as universally recognized in the central Andes as the cross is in the modern Christian world. The standardized Wari tunic patterns identified the wearer's rank and affiliation as reliably as a modern military uniform identifies its bearer. The kero drinking cups, with their elaborate polychrome scenes, told the story of the chicha-drinking ritual being performed. None of this was writing in the strict sense, but all of it carried encoded meaning, and a competent member of Andean society could read this visual culture with the same fluency that a literate Roman could read Latin script.

---

The empire that ran on roads, storehouses, and khipus also ran on **language**.

The standard scholarly hypothesis is that Wari was the original vehicle for the spread of **Quechua** across the central Andes. Quechua is the largest indigenous language family of South America today, spoken by roughly 8 million people across Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Argentina, Colombia, and Chile. Modern linguistic geography of Quechua is essentially impossible to explain except as the lingering imprint of an earlier political imposition — the language is too widely distributed, across too many different ecological zones, to be the product of natural diffusion from any single point of origin. The political imposition that fits the geography best is Wari expansion in the seventh through tenth centuries.

The argument runs like this: Quechua originated as the dialect of one community in the central Peruvian highlands (probably in the Ayacucho region itself). When Wari expansion took the dialect's speakers into administrative roles across central Peru, Quechua became the administrative language of the empire — the language of the wamani officials, the road foremen, the storehouse managers. Provincial populations had to learn Quechua to deal with the imperial administration, even when their own home languages were different. Over generations, Quechua spread by absorption — local populations gradually adopted the imperial language and lost their original languages — and by the time the empire collapsed in the early eleventh century, Quechua was the de facto common language across central Peru. The Inca, six centuries later, found a Quechua-speaking population already in place across most of their imperial territory and adopted Quechua as their own administrative language without having to impose it. The Inca contribution was to spread Quechua further south and east, but the central Andean spread was Wari work.

This is the leading hypothesis. It has competition: some scholars argue Quechua was already widespread in the central Andes before Wari and that the empire merely consolidated existing linguistic patterns; others argue Quechua's spread is mostly Inca-era. The Wari hypothesis has the best fit to the linguistic geography but the weakest direct evidence (no Wari source ever wrote anything in Quechua or any other language). The honest answer is that Wari probably did spread Quechua, but the proof has been lost with the rest of the documentary record.

The corresponding hypothesis for Tiwanaku is that the southern altiplano empire spread **Aymara**. Aymara is the second major indigenous language family of the south-central Andes, spoken today by roughly 2 million people in Bolivia and southern Peru. Its modern distribution corresponds reasonably well to the historical reach of the Tiwanaku empire. The same argument applies: Tiwanaku's expansion through colonies and caravan networks in the southern Andes carried Aymara with it, and the language stuck after the empire fell.

What this means is that the linguistic geography of the modern central Andes — Quechua across central Peru and Bolivia, Aymara around Lake Titicaca — is essentially the fossil imprint of the Middle Horizon empires. The two empires died, the Inca came and went, the Spanish came and stayed, the modern republics formed and reformed, and through all of this the basic linguistic split has held: Quechua where Wari ran the wamani, Aymara where Tiwanaku ran the colonies. The two empires that shared the Middle Horizon also divided, between them, the language map of the modern central Andes.

# Chapter 7 — Tiwanaku Religion & Iconography

Earlier chapters have already introduced most of the elements of Middle Horizon religion. The Staff God on the Gateway of the Sun (Chapter 2). The Conchopata urns showing Wari adoption of Tiwanaku Staff God iconography (Chapter 3). The shared deity vocabulary across both empires (Chapter 4). What this chapter does is pull these threads together and look at the religious system as a whole — what the Middle Horizon empires worshipped, how they worshipped, and what made the religion stable enough to persist for centuries across two contemporary polities.

The short version: Middle Horizon religion was a continuation of the Chavín tradition (Early Andean Civilizations, Chapter 7) filtered through highland-state institutional patronage and elaborated with new ritual technologies. Three things define it. The **Staff God** was the central deity image. **Hallucinogens** were the central ritual technology. The **animal triad** of puma, condor, and snake was the central cosmological framework. None of these elements were Tiwanaku or Wari inventions. All of them had Andean antecedents going back at least a thousand years. What the Middle Horizon empires did was systematize, monumentalize, and propagate them at imperial scale.

---

The **Staff God**, you'll recall (Chapter 2), is a frontal figure standing upright, holding a tall staff in each hand, wearing an elaborate radiating headdress of feline and serpent figures.

The image first appears at Chavín de Huántar around 500 BCE on the **Raimondi Stele**, a 6-foot tall flat granite slab carved with the Staff God in classic Chavín kenning style — the kind of intricate geometric line-work that turns secondary features (claws, fangs, snakes, eyes) into independently legible smaller images embedded in the larger figure. The Chavín Staff God established the visual template: frontal pose, paired staffs, fanged face, serpent hair, radiating headdress.

A thousand years later, the same image reappears on the upper lintel of the Gateway of the Sun at Tiwanaku, recognizably the same deity but rendered in the cleaner and more geometric Tiwanaku style. The Tiwanaku Staff God has been simplified and rationalized: the kenning has mostly been stripped out, the headdress has become a sun-disk rather than a snake-and-fang nest, the attendant figures have been added to either side. The deity is still recognizably the same Andean Staff God, but the visual vocabulary has shifted toward greater geometric clarity.

A century or so after that, the same image reappears on the giant Conchopata urns at Wari, marking the moment when Wari adopted the Tiwanaku-derived religious vocabulary. The Wari Staff God is rendered in still another style — bold polychrome painting on ceramic rather than carved stone — but the basic iconographic elements are the same: frontal figure, paired staffs, sunburst headdress, attendant figures.

A thousand years after Wari adopted the image, the Inca were still using it. The figure of **Wiracocha** (wee-rah-KOH-chah), the Inca creator god depicted holding paired staffs in a gold plaque that hung in the Coricancha temple at Cusco, is recognizably the same Staff God image with the same basic attributes. The Spanish chroniclers who described the Coricancha in the 1530s did not know they were looking at a deity image transmitted in unbroken continuity from a stone slab carved two thousand years earlier in the northern Peruvian highlands. They were.

This kind of iconographic continuity across two thousand years is unusual in any civilization. The closest comparable case might be the persistence of certain Egyptian deity images — Horus, Anubis, Isis — across the three millennia of Pharaonic history. But even in Egypt, the deity images shifted substantially over time; the Old Kingdom Horus is visually very different from the Late Period Horus. The Andean Staff God preserved its visual template — frontal pose, paired staffs, fanged face, radiating headdress — across multiple successor civilizations and the entire central Andean geography, with surprisingly little drift in the basic iconographic vocabulary. What allowed this stability is unknown, but it is one of the deepest features of Andean religion.

---

The second central element of Middle Horizon religion is the systematic ritual use of **hallucinogenic plants**.

The Andes have an unusually rich pharmacopeia of psychoactive species. **Vilca** (VEEL-kah, *Anadenanthera colubrina*, also called cebil) is a tree of the southern altiplano whose dried seeds contain bufotenin and related tryptamine compounds; the seeds are ground into a snuff and inhaled, typically through bone or wooden tubes inserted in the nostril. **San Pedro cactus** (*Echinopsis pachanoi*) is a tall columnar cactus of the highlands whose flesh contains mescaline; the cactus is sliced, boiled into a brew, and consumed orally. **Coca leaves** (*Erythroxylum coca*) are not strictly hallucinogenic in everyday use but contain cocaine and related alkaloids that produce both stimulant and mild psychoactive effects. **Ayahuasca** (eye-ah-WAHS-kah, a brew of the *Banisteriopsis caapi* vine combined with leaves of the *Psychotria viridis* shrub, both Amazonian) was probably also used in the highland Middle Horizon, though the evidence is thinner.

The archaeological evidence for psychoactive plant use is unambiguous. Snuff trays and bone snuff tubes are among the most common high-status grave goods at both Tiwanaku and Wari elite burials. The trays are typically rectangular wooden boards with small carved figures around the rim, a flat surface for grinding the seeds, and a hollow on one end for the resulting powder. The tubes are decorated bone cylinders with one end shaped to insert in the nostril. The combination is unmistakable, and the parallel use of snuff trays across Andean cultures from the early Tiwanaku period through the Inca is one of the most stable features of regional religious practice.

The point of all this was visionary religious experience. The chemical effects of vilca snuff, San Pedro brew, and ayahuasca all produce, with regional variation, vivid altered states involving visual hallucinations, distorted bodily perception, intensified emotion, and (in many users) the feeling of communicating with non-human or supernatural beings. In Andean religious context, this kind of experience was understood as direct contact with the divine — the ritual specialists who consumed these substances were believed to be traveling, in their visions, to the spirit world to negotiate with the gods on behalf of the community. The vilca-induced vision of meeting the Staff God was the foundational religious experience that the iconography on the Gateway of the Sun was, in some sense, an attempt to make permanent in stone.

This is documented in the iconography itself. The Bennett Monolith (the largest carved stela at Tiwanaku, 24 feet tall, standing in the semi-subterranean temple) shows a frontal figure holding a kero drinking cup in one hand and a snuff tablet in the other — the ritual implements of chicha consumption and vilca inhalation, respectively, identified explicitly as the attributes of the depicted figure. The figure is probably a high-status priest or a deified ancestor, and the implements are not incidental. They define what kind of figure this is and what kind of practice authorizes his depiction at this scale. The Tiwanaku religious specialist was, by definition, the person who drank chicha and inhaled vilca in the right ritual contexts.

Cross-civ check: visionary plant use in religion is not unique to the Andes. **Soma**, mentioned obsessively in the Vedic Sanskrit hymns of early India (Vedic Period, Chapter 4), was apparently a psychoactive plant or fungus consumed by Brahmin priests in ritual contexts to produce ecstatic states. The **Eleusinian Mysteries** of ancient Greece almost certainly involved a psychoactive beverage (probably containing ergot alkaloids derived from infected barley) consumed by initiates. The **peyote** ceremonies of the Huichol and other indigenous peoples of northern Mexico are still practiced today. What is distinctive about the Andean case is the institutional embedding: Middle Horizon religion was not just decorated with occasional visionary use, it was structurally organized around it, with the ritual specialists, the iconographic vocabulary, and the burial assemblages all reflecting the centrality of the visionary experience to religious practice.

---

The third central element is the **animal triad**: puma, condor, and snake.

These three animals appear obsessively in Tiwanaku and Wari art. The puma — the apex predator of the Andean highlands — represents the **earth zone**, the domain of the living. The condor — the giant vulture whose wingspan can exceed 10 feet (3 meters), soaring at altitudes higher than any other bird in the Western Hemisphere — represents the **upper world**, the domain of the celestial and the sacred. The snake — particularly the constrictor and venomous species of the eastern jungle slopes — represents the **underworld**, the domain of the ancestors and the chthonic. Together, the three animals encode a vertical cosmology in which the universe is layered: snake below, puma in the middle, condor above.

This triadic cosmology is everywhere in Middle Horizon iconography. The Staff God's headdress typically includes both feline and serpent elements (and sometimes condor elements), encoding the triadic structure in the central deity image. The kero drinking cups often display the three animals in registers stacked vertically. The semi-subterranean temple at Tiwanaku is below the surrounding plaza level (snake zone), the Akapana platform rises above it (puma zone), and the Kalasasaya gateways open toward the celestial circuits of the sun and stars (condor zone). The architecture itself encodes the cosmology.

The Inca, six centuries later, would inherit the same triadic system — the puma, the kuntur (condor), and the amaru (snake) appear throughout Inca cosmology, in the same vertical-zone associations. The Inca city of Cusco was reportedly laid out in the shape of a giant puma, with the fortress of Sacsayhuamán as its head. The royal road system is sometimes depicted as a giant snake winding through the Andes. The condor was the bird of the royal lineage. All of this iconographic vocabulary was Wari and Tiwanaku inheritance, modified in details but preserved in fundamentals.

---

Where Tiwanaku and Wari differed religiously is mostly in **emphasis** rather than in **content**.

Tiwanaku, with its astronomical alignments and elaborate stone architecture, appears to have emphasized the celestial zone — the condor, the sun, the predictable cycles of the calendar. The Kalasasaya is an astronomical observatory; the Gateway of the Sun is iconographically a sun-disk; the Akapana drainage system is a model of celestial water descending to the earth.

Wari, with its more militarized administrative apparatus and its emphasis on bounded compounds, appears to have emphasized the earth zone — the puma, the territorial state, the human institutional reach across the wamani. Wari iconography has more puma imagery and more depictions of state ritual than Tiwanaku iconography does. The Wari ushnu platforms — the small raised stone stages that appear in the central plazas of Wari administrative compounds, from which the local governor presumably addressed assembled subjects — are explicitly puma-zone constructions: stages for human political theater set in the middle of the bounded administrative space.

Both empires used vilca and San Pedro. Both empires worshipped the Staff God. Both empires built around the puma-condor-snake triad. The differences are real but second-order. The first-order religious system was shared across the Middle Horizon, and the shared framework is one reason the two empires could coexist for so long without violent conflict — they recognized each other as practitioners of the same fundamental religion, even when the political and linguistic frameworks diverged.

This is the lasting contribution of Middle Horizon religion: it stabilized a shared central Andean religious vocabulary that both Tiwanaku and Wari practiced in their distinctive variations, and that the Inca later inherited and propagated across an even larger imperial space. The Inca religion that the Spanish encountered in the sixteenth century — the Wiracocha-Inti-Pachamama theological system, the puma-condor-snake cosmology, the chicha-and-coca ritual culture — was substantially the same religion that the Tiwanaku and Wari had been practicing six centuries earlier. The dynasty that propagated it was new. The religion was not.

# Chapter 8 — The Collapse

Sometime around 900 to 1000 CE, both the Tiwanaku and the Wari empires collapsed.

The collapses were almost simultaneous — within roughly a century of each other, plausibly within a few decades — and they almost certainly had a common cause: a multi-decade drought that struck the central Andes across the late ninth and tenth centuries. The drought is not a hypothesis. It is documented in two independent paleoclimate records: ice cores from the **Quelccaya Ice Cap** high above the southern Peruvian highlands (the largest tropical ice cap in the world, which preserves an annual layered record of precipitation going back thousands of years), and sediment cores from the bottom of Lake Titicaca itself (which preserve a similar layered record of precipitation, lake levels, and pollen distributions). Both records show a sharp downturn in precipitation beginning around 900 CE and persisting, with some variability, for roughly two centuries. By 1100 CE the climate had partially recovered, but the empires that the earlier wetter conditions had supported were already gone.

What the drought did is collapsed the agricultural systems that fed the empires.

---

For **Tiwanaku**, the trigger was Lake Titicaca itself.

The lake's water level depends on the balance between rainfall in the surrounding watershed and evaporation from the lake surface. In wet centuries, the lake rises and the surrounding low-lying plain floods (which is what the suka kollu raised-field system was designed to handle). In dry centuries, the lake shrinks and the surrounding plain dries out. The 900 CE drought drove the lake level down by roughly 40 to 50 feet (12 to 15 meters) over the course of several decades. The lake shoreline retreated. The water table beneath the surrounding fields dropped. The suka kollu channels — which depended on capillary rise from the elevated water table to irrigate the platform tops — went dry. The thermal-buffering effect of the channel water disappeared. The frost protection that had made highland agriculture possible at 12,500 feet collapsed.

Without the raised-field system, the altiplano around Tiwanaku could not produce enough surplus food to feed a city of 30,000 people. The city did not collapse violently — there are no signs of conquest, no destruction layers, no mass burials suggesting a single catastrophic event. It de-populated slowly. People moved out, presumably to lower-elevation valleys where dryland agriculture could still produce some crops. The ceremonial core was left intact. The Akapana, the Kalasasaya, the Gateway of the Sun, the Pumapunku platform — all of them were left standing. The city emptied around them. By 1100 CE, Tiwanaku was a ruin still consulted by pilgrims and respected as a sacred site, but no longer a functioning urban center.

---

For **Wari**, the mechanism was different but the underlying cause was the same.

The Wari empire ran on irrigation. The wamani provincial centers were placed in valleys where state-managed irrigation systems supported the agricultural population that fed the imperial apparatus. The 900 CE drought reduced the river flows that fed those irrigation systems, particularly in the more marginal frontier zones. As the irrigation systems failed, the agricultural surplus shrank. As the agricultural surplus shrank, the wamani compounds could no longer support themselves, much less send tribute back to the capital. As tribute flow contracted, the central state's ability to maintain the road network, the storehouses, and the diplomatic feasting infrastructure (the Cerro Baúl brewery being one example) eroded. The empire began to dismantle itself from the periphery inward.

The peripheral compounds were closed first. Cerro Baúl was decommissioned around 1000 CE in the smashing-the-cup ceremony described in Chapter 5. Pikillaqta in the Cusco basin was abandoned at roughly the same time, with its doorways deliberately blocked and its rooms sealed. The northern outpost at Viracochapampa was abandoned, with the construction apparently never even completed. As the periphery emptied, the central state at the Wari capital became less and less capable of sustaining itself. By the early eleventh century, the capital city of Huari was being dismantled in the same orderly way the peripheral compounds had been: doorways blocked, structures sealed, the population dispersed to smaller settlements in the surrounding valleys. The empire did not fall to invasion. It walked away from itself.

This is one of the more poignant features of the Wari collapse. There are no signs of warfare destroying the empire. There are no indications of internal revolt overthrowing the central state. The Wari elite appear to have made a series of orderly decisions to close the imperial infrastructure as the climatic conditions made it untenable. The compounds were sealed rather than just abandoned. The closing rituals were performed. The state apparatus dismantled itself with the deliberation of a corporation winding down its operations rather than a regime collapsing in chaos. When the work was done, the bureaucrats walked away from the buildings they had built, and the people who had served the imperial state went back to being members of local ayllu communities.

---

What followed the imperial collapse is called the **Late Intermediate Period** (roughly 1000 to 1450 CE) — the four centuries between the Middle Horizon and the Inca conquest.

The Late Intermediate is the most fragmented period in Andean political history. Without the unifying frameworks of Tiwanaku and Wari, the central Andes broke into smaller polities organized at the river-valley or regional scale. The largest and most successful of these was the **Chimú** (chee-MOO) kingdom on the north coast, which rose from the post-Wari fragments around 900 CE and built the adobe city of **Chan Chan** into the largest pre-Columbian city in South America (an estimated 60,000 people at its peak, covering roughly 8 square miles of dense urban construction). The Chimú inherited the Wari coastal infrastructure, particularly the irrigation systems, and ran an effective regional state that controlled the north coast for several centuries until the Inca finally conquered them in the 1470s.

The central coast was occupied by the **Chancay** culture, a smaller polity known for distinctive painted textiles, cloth dolls, and the unmistakable smiling-face Cuchimilco figurines. The southern coast and altiplano fragmented into a patchwork of smaller polities. The **Aymara kingdoms** around Lake Titicaca — the Lupaqa, the Colla, the Pacajes, and others — fought each other for control of the altiplano grazing lands until the Inca consolidated all of them in the fifteenth century. The Cusco basin, where Pikillaqta had been abandoned around 1000 CE, was occupied by a series of smaller chieftaincies (the Killke culture, archaeologically) that gradually consolidated into the early Inca polity sometime after 1200 CE.

The Late Intermediate is sometimes characterized as a period of decline — fragmentation, smaller political units, less impressive monumental architecture than the preceding Middle Horizon. This is partly true and partly an artifact of perspective. The smaller polities of the Late Intermediate were perfectly viable on their own terms; they just operated at smaller scale. What the Late Intermediate lost was the imperial integration that Wari and Tiwanaku had provided — the long-distance road network, the storehouse system, the wamani provincial structure, the cross-Andean religious framework. What it kept was the economic technologies (raised-field agriculture in the altiplano, irrigation in the coastal valleys, vertical archipelago colony networks at the local scale), the religious vocabulary (the Staff God remained the central deity image throughout the period), and the basic social institutions (the ayllu, mit'a labor tribute, the warrior-priest political model). The infrastructure of empire was dormant, but the components were all still there, waiting to be reassembled.

---

The reassembly happened in the Cusco basin, beginning sometime in the early fifteenth century, under a series of leaders whose names eventually become legendary: **Pachacuti** (PAH-cha-KOO-tee, ruled c. 1438–1471), **Tupac Yupanqui**, **Huayna Capac**. These leaders welded the small Killke-period chieftaincies of the Cusco region into the Inca state, then expanded the state outward across the central Andes in a campaign of conquest that took less than a century to absorb the entire territorial reach of the former Wari and Tiwanaku empires.

The Inca conquest is, in important respects, a redux of the Middle Horizon imperial project. The Inca built planned administrative cities (Vilcashuaman, Huánuco Pampa, Tambo Colorado) on the Wari template — rectangular grids, central plazas with ushnu platforms, internal patio groups, perimeter walls. The Inca built a road network (the famous Qhapaq Ñan) on top of the existing Wari road system, refurbishing and extending it. The Inca built storehouse complexes (qollqa) at the same kind of locations the Wari had stored at, scaled up by an order of magnitude. The Inca ran tribute through the wamani provincial system using mit'a labor, exactly the same way Wari had done. The Inca khipu accounting system was a refinement of the older Wari knotted-string records. The Inca state language was Quechua, which Wari had originally spread. The Inca religious framework — Wiracocha, the puma-condor-snake triad, chicha and coca and vilca, the mountain apus and the lake huacas — was the Middle Horizon religion essentially preserved.

What the Inca did was take the Wari template and execute it on a much larger scale, with fifteenth-century technologies and a more centralized political structure. The empire they built was the largest in the history of the pre-Columbian Americas — at its peak, in the early sixteenth century, it stretched from modern Colombia to modern Chile and contained perhaps 12 million people. But the institutional bones of that empire were not Inca inventions. They were Middle Horizon inheritance, dormant during the Late Intermediate Period, picked up and refurbished by the Cusco-based imperial revival.

This is the deepest legacy of the Middle Horizon. Tiwanaku and Wari did not just rule the central Andes for four centuries and collapse. They invented the institutional template for Andean empire, ran the first version of it, and then bequeathed it to the successor civilization that would eventually be encountered by the Spanish. When **Francisco Pizarro** (1478–1541, the Spanish conquistador who landed on the Peruvian coast in 1532 and captured the Inca emperor Atahualpa within the year) marched up the Inca royal road from the coast to the highland city of Cajamarca to meet the Inca emperor, he was walking on a roadbed that Wari engineers had laid down eight centuries earlier. He was passing storehouses that operated on a system Wari had invented. He was traveling through wamani provincial structures that Wari had designed. He was encountering, without knowing it, the second iteration of an imperial template whose first run had ended in deliberate orderly closure four centuries before he was born.

---

A pause for what does and does not appear in modern memory.

The Inca are world-famous. The conquistadors' chronicles described them in detail. **Machu Picchu** is one of the most-visited tourist sites on the planet. The Inca road system, the Inca terraces, the Inca Sun Temple at Cusco, the Inca emperor wearing his golden ear-spools — all of this is solidly lodged in the global cultural memory. Most reasonably educated people can name at least one Inca emperor (Atahualpa, usually) and know roughly when and how the empire fell.

Tiwanaku and Wari are not in the global cultural memory in any comparable way. Most reasonably educated people have never heard of either empire. The Gateway of the Sun is sometimes seen on travel posters but rarely identified by name. The Pumapunku is a fixture of pseudoarchaeology TV but almost never appears in standard world-history textbooks. Wari is practically invisible — even among visitors to Peru who know about the Inca and the Moche, very few know that there was an imperial state running the central Peruvian highlands seven centuries before the Inca, and that the Inca built directly on the Wari template.

The reason for the invisibility is partly about writing. The Inca were encountered by literate conquerors who wrote down what they saw, in detail, in Spanish books that have circulated continuously since the sixteenth century. Tiwanaku and Wari were not encountered by anyone literate. By the time the Spanish arrived in the 1530s, both empires had been ruins for four to five centuries. The Aymara residents around Tiwanaku in 1549, when **Pedro Cieza de León** (the Spanish chronicler who visited the site that year) asked about the ruins, told him the city had been built in a single night by giants, before the time of the Inca. This was the local oral tradition four centuries after the city's abandonment. It was not history in the modern sense, and it could not provide what the Inca chronicles provided: a continuous narrative of names, dates, events, and political relationships connecting the visible ruins to the human past. Tiwanaku and Wari were prehistoric to the Spanish. They have been prehistoric to most of world history ever since.

What rescued them, partially, has been modern archaeology. **Adolph Bandelier**'s work at Tiwanaku in the 1890s established the site as a serious pre-Columbian civilization rather than an Inca outpost. **Julio C. Tello**'s work in the 1920s and 1930s — Andean Kingdoms, Chapter 2 — distinguished Wari from Tiwanaku as separate empires rather than a single artistic horizon. The modern wave of Wari archaeology since the 1970s — particularly the work of **William Isbell**, **Gordon McEwan**, and **Anita Cook** — has reframed Wari as a true imperial state with planned administration rather than just a religious horizon. The 2012 discovery of the **Castillo de Huarmey** burial complex by Polish archaeologists Miłosz and Patrycja Giersz brought Wari back into the news cycle, briefly. The Cerro Baúl excavations by **Patrick Williams** and **Donna Nash** since the late 1980s have provided the central case study for Wari-Tiwanaku interaction. None of this has yet made Wari and Tiwanaku into household names, but the scholarly understanding of the Middle Horizon today is qualitatively different from what it was even a generation ago.

The hope, presumably, is that eventually the Middle Horizon will get the cultural recognition that the documentary record never gave it. The two empires that ran the central Andes for four centuries, invented the planned administrative city, built the original Andean road network, perfected raised-field agriculture, created the most precise stonework in the pre-Columbian Americas, and bequeathed almost every institution the Inca later got famous for — these empires deserve to be in the global cultural conversation alongside the contemporary Tang Chinese, the Byzantine Greeks, and the Carolingian Franks. They are not yet. Maybe they will be.

---

Cross-civ check: the simultaneous collapse of Tiwanaku and Wari around 900–1100 CE took place during one of the more dramatic climate-driven transitions in human history. The same period saw the **Maya Classic collapse** in the southern Yucatán — a cascade of city abandonments across the Petén lowlands between roughly 800 and 1000 CE, plausibly triggered by similar drought conditions to those that took down the Andean empires (Maya Civilization, Chapter 7). It saw the **Tang Dynasty collapse** in China in 907 CE, followed by the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period of fragmentation. It saw the climatic shift to the **Medieval Warm Period** in Europe, which favored Norse expansion to Iceland, Greenland, and briefly Newfoundland (Viking Age, Chapter 3). The end of the first millennium CE was a period of significant climate adjustment globally, and several major civilizations responded to it by collapsing. The Andean Middle Horizon is one of the more dramatic examples of the pattern. The Maya collapse, the Tang collapse, and the Tiwanaku-Wari collapse all happened within roughly two centuries of each other, on three different continents, with at least partly shared climatic causation. The first millennium of the Common Era ended with imperial demolitions on a global scale.

The empires that came back — the Inca in the Andes, the Postclassic Maya states in the Yucatán, the Song Dynasty in China — built on the institutional inheritance of the empires that had collapsed. The patterns repeat. The Andean version is less famous than the others but no less consequential. The Inca that the Spanish met in 1532 were the heirs of Wari and Tiwanaku, four centuries removed and a thousand years on from Chavín, executing the same Andean imperial template that the Middle Horizon had perfected and then walked away from.

That is the end of the Middle Horizon story, and the beginning of the next book in this series.

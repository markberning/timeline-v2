# Chapter 1 — The World Before the Raids

The horned helmet is a 19th-century opera invention.

Start with that, because it is the single most important fact about the Vikings that almost everyone gets wrong. The fearsome, bull-horned helmet that Wagner's costume designer Carl Emil Doepler put on the heads of his *Ring Cycle* singers in 1876 has nothing to do with anything anyone wore in the North Sea between 793 and 1066 CE. There is exactly one intact Viking-era helmet known to archaeology — the **Gjermundbu helmet** (YEHR-mund-boo), found in a Norwegian grave from the tenth century — and it is a plain iron skullcap with an eye-guard and a pointed crown. No horns. Horns on a battle helmet would catch sword strokes and rotate the helmet sideways to break the wearer's neck. Anyone who has ever swung a stick knows this. Doepler did not consult any sailors. He consulted a stage director.

The point is not pedantry. The point is that the actual Vikings — the people who actually lived this story — are more interesting than the costume. They were farmers who became sailors who became raiders who became settlers who became kings who became, eventually, the Russians, the Normans, the Icelanders, and a chunk of the genome of every English-speaking person reading this. They reached Baghdad. They reached Newfoundland. They sold each other's relatives in slave markets that stretched from Dublin to Damascus. They wrote poetry so technically dense that scholars still argue about how to translate it. They named one of their kings **Harald Bluetooth** (his teeth weren't actually blue — the nickname *blátǫnn* may refer to a dark dead tooth, or to a hereditary discoloration, or to a misreading of an old word for "dark-skinned"; nobody knows), and a millennium later the Swedish engineers who invented short-range wireless networking named the protocol after him because he had unified Denmark and Norway the way they were trying to unify computers and phones. The Bluetooth logo on every device on earth is a bind-rune of Harald's initials in the Younger Futhark alphabet.

This is the world we are walking into. It is more peculiar than the cartoon, and it is darker than the cartoon, and it ends with a Norman duke (himself a great-great-great-grandson of a Viking) winning the Battle of Hastings on a Saxon hilltop in October 1066, four hundred miles south of the coastal monastery where the whole thing started.

Before we go any further: a caveat. "First" in early-medieval history means "first surviving evidence of." The Viking Age was a literate age in a region that was overwhelmingly oral. The people we are about to meet wrote in runes, on stone and wood, in short formal inscriptions; they did not keep tax registers or court chronicles. Most of what we know about them comes from three kinds of sources: their victims (Christian monks who wrote in horror about being raided), their trading partners (Arab travelers and Byzantine bureaucrats), and themselves at one remove — the **Icelandic sagas**, which are prose narratives composed in the 12th and 13th centuries about events that had happened two or three hundred years earlier. The sagas are wonderful and they are also, in places, fiction. The archaeology is wonderful and it is also full of holes. Some of what we think we know about the Vikings is wrong. We don't always know which parts.

---

The setting is **Scandinavia**, the long northern peninsula that separates the North Sea from the Baltic. Today it holds Denmark (the flat, low country at the southern end), Sweden (the long forested middle), and Norway (the mountainous, fjord-cut Atlantic coast). Those three are Scandinavia proper (the North Germanic core, with closely related languages and a shared Norse descent), and they are not the same thing as the *Nordic countries*, a wider grouping that also takes in Finland and Iceland. The distinction matters here because Viking-Age Finland was Finnic (a different language family and a different people, Finno-Ugric rather than Norse) and sits largely outside this story, even though Swedish traders and raiders worked its coast. In the 700s CE these were not countries. They were patchworks of small kingdoms, free farms, and forested pagan backlands, governed by chieftains whose authority rested on personal loyalty and the redistribution of plunder. The total population of all three regions was probably around 1.5 to 2 million people — about the population of modern Phoenix, Arizona, spread over a region roughly the size of Texas plus California.

The geography shaped everything. **Norway** is a wall of mountains falling straight into the sea, fractured by long deepwater inlets called **fjords** (FYORDS, drowned glacial valleys carved by Ice Age ice and never refilled by sediment), and the only flat land is in narrow strips along the coast and at the heads of the fjords. You cannot grow wheat there reliably. You can grow oats and barley and you can keep cattle and sheep and you can fish, and that was the life of an ordinary Norwegian farmer. **Denmark** is the opposite: low, flat, sandy, the rolling glacial moraine of Jutland and a sprinkle of large islands (Zealand, Funen, Bornholm) commanding the entrance to the Baltic. Denmark could grow more grain than Norway, which is why Denmark would become the political center of the Viking world. **Sweden** sits between, a vast country of pine forest and lake systems, looking east toward the Baltic and the Russian river network rather than west toward the Atlantic.

The climate was, for once, on their side. The period from roughly 950 to 1250 CE is called the **Medieval Warm Period** (MWP), a stretch of mild northern temperatures that opened sea lanes and lengthened growing seasons across the North Atlantic. Iceland was actually warm enough to grow some barley. Greenland's coastal margins genuinely were green in summer. The pack ice retreated. Storms were no kinder than usual but the navigable season was longer, and the kind of long-distance ocean voyage that would have killed everyone in the year 600 was achievable by the year 900. The Vikings were lucky in their century. Their descendants in Greenland, who would be ground out by the colder **Little Ice Age** in the 1300s and 1400s, would be unlucky in theirs.

---

The basic unit of Norse society was the **longhouse**.

A longhouse is exactly what it sounds like: a long, narrow, single-room building, typically 50 to 100 feet (15–30 m) long and 15 to 20 feet (5–6 m) wide, with thick earthen walls, a peaked timber-and-turf roof, and a long central hearth running down the middle. Cattle and people often lived under the same roof, with the animals at one end and the family at the other — body heat from the cattle helped warm the human end during the worst of the winter, and the cattle benefited from being indoors rather than dead in a snowdrift. There were no chimneys; smoke rose to the rafters and seeped out through gable openings. There were almost no windows; the building was lit by the hearth and by oil lamps fed with seal blubber or fish oil. Inside, on either side of the central hearth, raised wooden platforms ran the length of the room, and these platforms served as bench, table, workbench, and bed. You sat on them by day, ate on them by night, and slept on them under woolen blankets and animal skins. Everyone in the household — owner, family, servants, slaves — slept in the same long room.

This is the texture of Viking domestic life. Smoky. Crowded. Smelly (the cattle, the lamps, the unwashed wool, the open fire). Built to keep the wind out. A longhouse was a fort against the climate, and a Norwegian winter is a serious adversary: the sun barely rises north of the Arctic Circle in December, temperatures sit below freezing for months, and the prevailing storm pattern is a sequence of Atlantic depressions delivering snow, sleet, and gale-force winds week after week. A house that did not keep its inhabitants warm was a house full of corpses by spring. The longhouse worked. Variants of it would be built across the entire Norse diaspora: in Iceland, in Greenland, briefly in Newfoundland, in the Hebrides, in Yorkshire. Wherever you find a long earthen rectangle with a central hearth, you are looking at the architectural fingerprint of a Norse household.

The longhouse was also the seat of authority. A **chieftain** (Old Norse *goði*, GO-thee, originally meaning something close to "the man who performs the rites") was a hybrid figure — part secular warlord, part priestly officiant — who hosted the seasonal sacrifices for his region, mediated lawsuits among the free farmers who owed him loyalty, and led them to war when raiding season opened. The role was hereditary in practice but never sacrosanct: a *goði* who could not perform the rites well, give out gifts generously, win lawsuits cleanly, or lead a successful raid would lose his following to a rival. He ran his hall, and the warriors who owed him loyalty came to feast there in the long winter nights, drinking ale and mead from horns, listening to **skalds** (court poets, Chapter 6) recite verses about ancestors and famous battles. A chieftain held authority not by coronation but by performance: he was generous (he gave out gifts, weapons, gold arm-rings, slaves), he was successful (he led raids that returned with plunder), and he was personally formidable (he could fight, and he had killed people). A chieftain who stopped being any of those things was replaced. Sometimes he was replaced by his son. Sometimes he was replaced by an axe in the back. The system was direct.

Above the chieftains, in the larger regions, sat the early kings — but the word "king" in 800 CE Scandinavia meant something much smaller than what it would mean in 1100 CE. A Norse **konungr** (KO-nungk, our word "king") might rule a single fjord system, or a cluster of islands, or a portion of Jutland, with revenues that would not impress a Frankish bishop. Real territorial kingship — the kind where a single ruler controls the entire country and collects systematic taxes — would only arrive in Scandinavia with Christianity and the writing technologies the Church brought with it (Chapters 5 and 7).

---

Norse society was built on **oaths**.

There were no written contracts. There were no notarized property deeds. There were no police. What there was, instead, was a dense web of personal sworn loyalty, witnessed by community and by the gods. A warrior swore an oath of loyalty to his chieftain. A chieftain swore an oath to his king. Two parties agreeing to a deal swore the deal in front of witnesses, sometimes with hands clasped over a sacred ring kept on the temple altar. Marriage was a contract between two families, sealed by oath and bride-price. Manumission of a slave was an oath. Adoption of a son was an oath. The legal world ran on memorized commitments enforced by reputation and the threat of violence. Break an oath, and you were *níðingr* (NEETH-ing-ger) — a person without honor, whom anyone could kill without consequence. The Norse word for an honorless oath-breaker survived into modern Scandinavian languages and into English in fossil form: an *outlaw* is someone outside the law — someone whose life is no longer protected by the oath-network of the community.

This is important context for everything that follows, because it explains why Viking armies could function. A Viking warband was not a state institution. It was a network of personal oaths radiating out from a war-leader (the war-band, or comitatus, originates in the Iron Age Germanic world; its Vendel-Age Scandinavian form, the hall, the ring-sword, and the sworn retinue, is the subject of the Vendel Age narrative). Men followed a chieftain because they had sworn to follow him, because he had given them gifts, because he was likely to give them more, and because killing him while their oaths still bound them would mean a permanent loss of standing among everyone who knew them. The whole structure was social, not bureaucratic. It bound the warband together more tightly than any payroll ever could, and it dissolved instantly when the chieftain died. This is also why Viking armies often disintegrated after a victory: the leader had been killed, or he had run out of plunder to distribute, and the oaths went slack.

---

Now, the central question. What is a "Viking"?

**The word "Viking" describes a thing you do, not a person you are.** This is a crucial distinction that almost every popular source gets wrong. The Old Norse phrase *fara í víking* means "to go on a viking" — to go on a raid, an overseas plundering expedition. A man who did this regularly was a *víkingr*. The word may come from the Old Norse *vík* (a bay, a creek, an inlet), suggesting "the men of the bays" — sailors who lurked in coastal hideouts. It may come from the Old English *wíc* (a camp). The etymology is contested. What is not contested is that "Viking" was a job description.

The vast majority of people in 800-CE Scandinavia were not Vikings. They were farmers, fishermen, blacksmiths, weavers, traders, slaves. They lived in longhouses, milked cows, hauled nets, raised children, and never went to sea in anger. The Viking minority — the men who went raiding — were a small and seasonal fraction of the population. A young man might do a season or two of raiding to win a marriage portion and then come home, marry, and farm for the rest of his life. A wealthy chieftain might lead raids for a couple of decades and then settle down to govern. The activity was bracketed within a normal agricultural life, not opposed to it.

This is why the modern phrase "Viking culture" is somewhat misleading, the way "pirate culture" would be a misleading way to describe 18th-century Britain. The Vikings were one (highly visible, highly violent) outgrowth of a broader Norse society that was mostly doing other things. When we talk about Viking religion, Viking ships, Viking law, Viking poetry, we are really talking about Norse religion, Norse ships, Norse law, Norse poetry — the cultural matrix from which the raiding minority sailed. The book is conventionally called the Viking Age because the raids are what wrote the Norse onto the world's literary record. Nobody on the Continent took notice of Norse weaving traditions. They took notice of Norse fleets coming up the river.

---

And the fleets are where everything begins, because the Viking Age does not happen without one specific piece of technology.

The **longship** is the keystone of the entire era. It is the reason a marginal frontier people on the rim of Christendom suddenly appeared everywhere from Iceland to the Caspian Sea within three generations. It is the reason the Carolingian and Anglo-Saxon defensive systems failed. It is the reason the Vikings could reach North America five hundred years before Columbus. Without the longship, none of the rest of this story happens.

The breakthrough was not invention; it was refinement. Northern Europeans had been building wooden boats with overlapping plank construction — called **clinker-built** in English, *klinkbygget* in Norwegian — for centuries before the Viking Age. Clinker construction means that each plank of the hull overlaps the plank below it (like clapboard siding on a house), and the planks are riveted together and caulked with tarred wool or animal hair. The hull this produces is light, flexible, and surprisingly strong. It bends with the waves rather than fighting them. A modern fiberglass hull is rigid; a clinker hull *flexes*, twisting up to several inches across its length in heavy seas, and that flex is the secret to taking a relatively light wooden boat across an ocean.

What changed in the late 700s was the addition of the **keel** as a deep, structural backbone — a single long timber, sometimes carved from one enormous oak, that ran the entire length of the boat and extended below the waterline as a rigid spine. The keel did three things at once: it kept the boat tracking straight in the water rather than skidding sideways under sail, it stiffened the hull longitudinally so it could carry a heavy mast, and it allowed the boat to take a square sail. Earlier northern boats had been rowed (the sailless clinker hull is the burial vessel at Vendel and Valsgärde, and the Vendel Age tells when and how the sail was finally added). Late-eighth-century longships could be sailed, with rowing as backup for windless days and harbor maneuvering. A square sail on a deep-keeled clinker hull is the package that made open-ocean voyages possible.

A Viking longship of the classic period — the kind that would attack Lindisfarne, sail up the Seine to Paris, ferry the Great Heathen Army across the North Sea, and reach Iceland — was typically 65 to 100 feet (20–30 m) long, 15 feet (4.5 m) wide, drew only 3 feet (90 cm) of water (which meant it could be beached anywhere with a sloping shore, and could navigate rivers and shallows that no contemporary Mediterranean ship could enter), carried a crew of 30 to 80 men, and could be rowed at about 5–7 knots and sailed at 10–15 knots in good wind. The hull was so light that the crew could carry the ship overland on rollers between river systems if necessary — a tactic the Rus' would use to portage between the Volga and the Don (Chapter 4). The ship was fast, shallow, oceangoing, river-capable, beachable, and portable. No other vessel in the world combined those qualities.

The two great surviving Viking ships found by archaeologists tell the story. The **Oseberg ship** (OH-seh-bayr), excavated in 1904 from a burial mound in southern Norway, dates to about 820 and was used as the funeral vessel for two high-status women buried in 834. It is shorter and lighter than a battle longship — probably a coastal craft, a yacht for a queen — but it shows the technology in flower: a deep keel, a tall mast step, elegant overlapping planks, and prow and stern carved in interlaced animals. The **Gokstad ship** (GOK-stad), found in 1880 in another Norwegian burial, dates to about 890 and is a true ocean-going longship: 76 feet (23 m) long, 17 feet (5 m) wide, with a mast amidships and 16 oar holes per side. A modern reconstruction of the Gokstad ship sailed across the Atlantic in 1893 in 28 days, proving that the technology was up to the job. The Vikings reached America in this kind of boat. They did not need anything else.

The longship was not just transport; it was also burial. Both the Oseberg and Gokstad vessels were funerary deposits — the most spectacular ship burials of the Viking Age — and the **Ladby ship** in Denmark and a scatter of smaller Norse ship burials show the same pattern: when a great chieftain died, you buried him in a boat. The ship carried the dead onward, the way it had carried the living to plunder. The metaphor was not subtle.

---

One more thing about the longship that gets understated: it was a slave ship.

The **slave trade** was central to the Viking Age economy. We will return to this in Chapter 7, when we get to the great Norse commercial centers at Hedeby and Dublin. For now, the foundational fact: a longship raid was, among other things, a labor-procurement expedition. When Vikings sacked a coastal village or a monastery, they took portable wealth (gold, silver, vestments, books — the books for their gem-encrusted covers, which they ripped off; the parchment was used to wrap fish), and they took people. Young men and women, mostly. The captives were tied, loaded into the boats, fed enough to survive the voyage, and sold at one of the great northern slave markets. For most of the Viking Age, the largest single market was Dublin (Chapter 3). For some Norse traders, the slaves went east instead of west, down the Russian rivers, ending in the markets of Constantinople and Baghdad in exchange for the **Abbasid silver dirhams** (the Islamic-world coinage) that flooded Scandinavia for two centuries (Chapter 4).

The romantic account of the Vikings — the brooding longship slicing through arctic mist, the fierce blue-eyed warrior with his battleaxe — leaves the slaves out. They are not optional. The economic system that sustained the raids depended on them. Roughly a quarter of the population of Viking Age Iceland, by some recent genetic estimates, was descended from women who had originally been Irish and Scottish slaves taken on raids — which is to say, the entire Icelandic founding population was substantially a slave-taking economy made flesh. The English word *thrall* (a slave, a person held in bondage) is a direct loan from Old Norse *þræll* (THRALL), and the Norse legal codes carefully distinguished the rights of free men, freedmen, and thralls. Slavery was not an embarrassment. It was a category of property, and it was the category that paid for the gold rings on the chieftain's arm.

---

So: the world before the raids. A patchwork of small kingdoms in a region where farming was hard and the sea was easy. A society organized around the longhouse, the oath, the chieftain's hall, and the warrior's loyalty. A pagan religious world (Chapter 5) that we will get to. A climate that was, just then, kinder than usual. And a single piece of marine technology — the deep-keeled clinker-built longship under square sail — that allowed, for the first time, a small band of armed men to step out of a Norwegian fjord on a spring morning and arrive on the coast of Northumbria a couple of weeks later, fully fed, fully armed, and fully prepared to take whatever they could find.

Cross-civ check: the Viking world that was about to explode (~793 CE) coincides with the **Carolingian Empire** at its peak under Charlemagne, the **Abbasid Caliphate** at its high tide in Baghdad under Harun al-Rashid (himself a contemporary of Charlemagne — they exchanged ambassadors), the **Byzantine Empire** struggling through the Iconoclast controversy, the **Tang dynasty** in China, the early **Heian period** in Japan, and the late stages of the Maya **Classic period** in the Yucatán. The longship arrived in a connected world. It was about to make the connections shorter.

# Chapter 2 — Lindisfarne and the Western Raids

On the morning of June 8, 793, Northumbrian monks at the monastery of Lindisfarne, on a tidal island off England's northeastern coast, saw something they had never seen before: dragon-prowed wooden boats running up the beach.

The chronicler Symeon of Durham, writing about three centuries later from monastic records, describes what happened next in the medieval scriptorial register that makes everything sound slightly more dramatic than it probably was: the heathens "miserably ravaged God's church on Lindisfarne with plunder and slaughter," dug up the altars, killed some of the monks, dragged others into the sea and drowned them, and carried off the rest in chains. The treasures of the church were stripped. The relics were desecrated. And then the boats vanished back into the North Sea as fast as they had come.

In purely military terms it was a small event. A handful of monks killed, a small island monastery sacked, perhaps a dozen captives taken. The total monetary value of the plunder was probably less than what a successful Frankish merchant cleared in a year. But Lindisfarne is the conventional starting date of the Viking Age because of what it meant, not what it took. **Lindisfarne** (founded 635 by the Irish monk Aidan, mother house of northern English Christianity, location of the most beautiful illuminated manuscript in Europe — the Lindisfarne Gospels, made around 715) was the most spiritually prestigious site in the Anglo-Saxon north (the Northumbrian golden age and Alcuin's letters of horror are covered in detail in the Anglo-Saxon England narrative; the Vendel-Age society that produced the raiders is the subject of the Vendel Age narrative). A raid on Lindisfarne was an attack on the foundational sacred geography of Northumbrian Christianity. And the raiders had not bargained, had not threatened tribute, had not negotiated. They had simply turned up out of an empty sea and taken what they wanted.

The Anglo-Saxon scholar **Alcuin of York** (AL-kwin, ~735–804, an English-born theologian who had moved to the court of Charlemagne and become the leading intellectual of the Carolingian Renaissance) wrote a horrified letter from Charlemagne's court when the news reached the Continent. "Never before has such a terror appeared in Britain as we have now suffered from a pagan race. Behold the Church of St. Cuthbert spattered with the blood of the priests of God, despoiled of its ornaments." Alcuin's letter is the Anglo-Saxon equivalent of the September 12 newspapers: the moment when a literate civilization realized that its assumptions about the geography of safety had been wrong. The sea, which had always been a barrier, was now a highway, and somebody on the other side was using it.

---

Why monasteries?

It is worth explaining the target selection, because **monasteries were the perfect targets** for the kind of force the early Vikings could project, and it is not an accident that most of the early raids hit them rather than secular settlements.

A monastery in 8th-century Britain or Ireland was, in modern terms, a combination bank, art gallery, university, and lightly defended manor house. Centuries of donations from kings, nobles, and pious commoners had piled up in the form of gold-and-silver liturgical vessels (chalices, patens, reliquaries), illuminated manuscripts bound in gem-encrusted covers, embroidered vestments shot through with gold thread, and stockpiles of food and ale. The monastery was the most concentrated unit of portable wealth in its region. And — this is the critical part — it was located, by religious convention, in remote places. Lindisfarne sat on a small tidal island visible from miles of empty coast. Iona was on a wind-swept Hebridean isle. Skellig Michael was a literal Atlantic crag. The whole architectural premise of early Insular Christianity was that monks should be apart from worldly society, in places where nothing ever happened, where the only neighbors were sheep and seabirds.

This was a wonderful theological choice and a catastrophic strategic choice. A coastal monastery was undefended (monks were unarmed, by their vows), located precisely where a longship could beach, contained portable concentrated wealth, and could be reached, looted, and exited within the same daylight tide. It was the eighth-century equivalent of a rural ATM with no security camera. The Vikings looked at this map and made the obvious calculation. For three decades after Lindisfarne, raid after raid would work the same playbook: a small fast fleet of two or three longships, an unscheduled landing on a remote stretch of coast, a quick strike on a known monastic target, and a withdrawal before any local force could assemble.

The Carolingians saw it coming and could not stop it. **Charlemagne** (Frankish king from 768, Holy Roman Emperor from 800, ruler of an empire stretching from the Pyrenees to the Elbe) ordered coastal defenses and a fleet around 800, but the Carolingian state was a land empire. It had no navy in any serious sense, no system of coastal watch towers, no warning network. The empire's military strength was in heavy cavalry and infantry mobilized from its great estates — slow to assemble, slower to deploy. By the time a Frankish levy had been raised in response to a Viking landing, the Vikings had been gone for three weeks. The very feature that made the longship deadly — its speed, its shallow draft, its ability to ascend rivers fifty miles inland and then escape on the next tide — was the feature that the existing defensive systems of Latin Christendom had no answer for. The Carolingian Empire was a cathedral built to withstand siege, not a coast guard.

Two years after Lindisfarne, the raiders hit **Iona** (eye-OH-nuh) in 795 — the great monastery off the western coast of Scotland founded by St. Columba in 563, the cradle of Irish-Scottish monasticism, the home of the Book of Kells (which the monks would eventually evacuate to Ireland for safekeeping). The raid on Iona was repeated. Vikings came back in 802, in 806 (when 68 monks were killed in a single attack), and finally in 825 — the **Second Sack of Iona** — when the abbot **Blathmac** (BLATH-mak) refused to reveal the hiding place of St. Columba's silver shrine and was tortured to death on the altar. The original Iona community largely abandoned the island after that and relocated inland to Kells in Ireland. The Book of Kells went with them, which is why it is called the Book of Kells today, and not the Book of Iona.

---

The raids escalated in tempo and ambition through the first half of the 9th century.

**Hedeby** (HAY-deh-bee) in southern Denmark grew explosively in this same period as Norse merchants channeled stolen wealth into a long-distance trade network we will return to in Chapter 7. The Frankish frontier was nibbled. Frisian coastal communities (the low coast of what is now the Netherlands) were raided repeatedly in the 810s and 820s. **Birka** in central Sweden, founded around 750 and prospering by the 820s, became the eastern counterpart to Hedeby — a Baltic trading hub where Slavic, Frisian, Arab, and Norse goods all changed hands. In 829, the Frankish missionary **Ansgar** (AHN-sgar, ~801–865, sent by Emperor Louis the Pious as the first Christian missionary to Scandinavia) sailed to Birka and built a small church there. The mission lasted, intermittently, for decades and converted very few people, but it was the first serious Christian foothold in Sweden. The conversion of Scandinavia (Chapter 5) was going to take another two centuries.

In 834, in southern Norway, two high-status women — one elderly, one in her fifties — were buried in the **Oseberg ship**, which would lie undisturbed in its turf-covered mound for over a thousand years until Norwegian archaeologists excavated it in 1904. Whether the older woman was a queen, a high priestess, a renowned witch, or all three is unknown. (Her companion may have been a sacrificial attendant — strangled to accompany her into the afterlife, in a pattern echoing the Scythian royal kurgans, Scythians Ch 3.) The grave goods — a sledge with carved animal heads, a four-wheeled wagon, looms, beds, kitchen tools, four ornate dog-sled-style sleighs, and the elegant ship itself — make the burial one of the richest finds of the Viking Age. The Oseberg ship is not a warship. It is a court vessel, a yacht, a thing for a great person to be seen in. It is also, today, the single most important physical artifact for understanding what Viking material culture actually looked like, because organic materials that would normally rot — wood, textiles, leather — were preserved in the waterlogged blue clay of the burial mound.

In 841, a Norse fleet established a permanent fortified beachhead — a *longphort* in Old Irish — on the south bank of the River Liffey at the mouth of an Irish harbor. They called the place **Dublin** (Old Norse *Dyflinn*, from the Irish *Dubh Linn*, "black pool," referring to a tidal pool in the Liffey). Dublin would grow within a century into the largest slave market in northern Europe (Chapter 7) and the political capital of a Norse-Irish kingdom that controlled the central Irish Sea for two hundred years. Other Norse longphorts followed: **Limerick**, **Waterford**, **Wexford**, **Cork**. Pre-Viking Ireland had no cities. The Vikings built Ireland's first cities, on the coast, with their own walls and their own laws, and the Irish were not consulted. The peculiar fact is that Ireland's modern urban backbone — Dublin, Cork, Limerick, Waterford, Wexford — is essentially the map of the original Viking longphorts. These were never Irish creations. They were Norse trading enclaves planted on Irish soil, and the Irish around them spent the 9th and 10th centuries alternately fighting and intermarrying with the foreigners. By the year 1000, "Norse-Irish" was a coherent ethnic category, with its own language, its own legal habits, and its own kings (Norse warlords with Irish wives and Gaelic-speaking children). The Battle of Clontarf in 1014 — when the Irish high king Brian Boru defeated a Norse-Irish coalition outside Dublin — is sometimes called the end of Viking Ireland, but the truth is that the Vikings were already half-Irish by then. They didn't leave; they melted in.

---

Then came the rivers.

The genuinely terrifying escalation of the 840s was the discovery, by Norse fleets, that they could sail up the rivers of Continental Europe and strike inland targets that had never had to think about coastal defense. **Paris**, by the standards of 845, was an inland city. It was 100 miles up the Seine from the sea. It was protected by no walls capable of stopping a serious assault, because it had not been seriously assaulted in the lifetime of anyone living. The walls it did have were Roman remnants, repurposed as a quarry by generations of locals. In March 845, a Viking fleet of 120 ships under a leader the Frankish chronicles call **Ragnar** (possibly the same legendary Ragnar Lothbrok later sagas would lionize, possibly someone else entirely whose name has been retroactively glued to the legend) sailed up the Seine, brushed aside Frankish resistance, and sacked Paris on Easter Sunday.

The shock to Frankish prestige was enormous. King **Charles the Bald** (Charlemagne's grandson, ruler of West Francia, the kingdom that would become France) paid an enormous **Danegeld** (DAYN-geld, "Dane-gold," tribute paid to make Vikings go away) of 7,000 pounds (about 2,500 kg) of silver — the first major Danegeld in Frankish history, though by no means the last. The Vikings took the silver and went home. They came back. The same year, 845, another Norse fleet sacked the newly founded archbishopric of **Hamburg** in northern Germany, forcing the missionary Ansgar to flee for his life. The Frankish Empire — ruler of half of western Europe, heir of Rome — was now paying its enemies to leave it alone and watching its archbishops abandon their sees.

Paris would be besieged again in 885–886 by an even larger fleet, perhaps 700 ships, under the joint command of several Viking warlords. This time the city had walls, and a young Frankish count named **Odo** held the bridge over the Seine at the Île de la Cité through nearly a year of siege. Odo's defense was so notable that he was elected King of West Francia in 888, displacing the Carolingians for the first time. The Vikings eventually withdrew up the Seine to plunder Burgundy after extracting another large Danegeld. The takeaway, for everyone watching: the Vikings could reach any river city in Christendom, the Carolingian state could not stop them, and the only reliable response was to pay them off.

In the 850s, the raiding fundamentally changed in character. **Overwintering** became normal: instead of sailing home to Scandinavia in the autumn, Viking fleets started to seize defensible river islands or coastal forts and dig in for the winter, raiding from a fixed local base year-round. This is the strategic shift that converts the Viking Age from a series of seasonal robberies into something much more dangerous — a permanent armed Norse presence operating from forward bases inside Continental and Insular territory. From an overwintered position, you could strike at greater depth, accumulate plunder more efficiently, and project force into a region in a way that mere summer raiding never could. The classic 9th-century Norse overwintering camps were on the Loire, on the Seine, on the Thames, and on a series of islands and estuaries up and down the European Atlantic. The Vikings were no longer guests. They were squatters, and they were heavily armed.

---

The most ambitious raid of the period demonstrates how far and how creatively the Norse were willing to operate.

In **859**, the Norse warlords **Hastein** (HAS-tane, also spelled Hasting or Anstign) and **Björn Ironside** (a son of Ragnar Lothbrok in the legendary tradition; whether he was a historical individual is debated) led a fleet of 62 ships out through the Strait of Gibraltar into the Mediterranean. This was unprecedented: a Norse fleet entering the inland sea where Romans, Byzantines, and Muslims had assumed they were the only naval powers. Hastein's fleet raided the coasts of Iberia (then partly under the Umayyad emirate of Córdoba, partly under the Christian kingdom of Asturias), spent a winter in the Camargue at the mouth of the Rhône, ascended the Rhône to plunder Provence, sacked the city of **Pisa** on the Italian coast, and raided as far as the southern coast of France and Morocco before turning around and heading back through Gibraltar in 862. By the time they returned to Brittany three years after they had set out, half their fleet was gone. The expedition was a disaster financially. It was also a statement: the Norse could go anywhere with a coast.

The Hastein expedition marks the apex of the long-range, free-lance Viking raid. After the 850s, the energy shifts: the raids stop being one-off plundering trips and start being conquests. The crews stop going home in the autumn. They send for their wives. They start to think about land.

The watershed is 865.

---

In the autumn of 865, a Norse force unlike anything that had come before — bigger, better-organized, and led not by a single chieftain but by a coalition of warlords — landed in **East Anglia** on the eastern coast of England. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, the contemporary court chronicle of Wessex, called it the **mycel hæþen here** — the **Great Heathen Army**. Modern scholars argue about the actual numbers (estimates range from 1,000 to 3,000 men, with later landings boosting the total), but everyone agrees that this was an order of magnitude beyond anything the Vikings had committed to England before. The army wintered in East Anglia, requisitioning horses from the local Anglo-Saxon king (who, lacking other options, complied), and then in 866 it marched north and took the city of **York** (Old Norse *Jórvík*, YOR-veek), the capital of the kingdom of Northumbria.

York fell on November 1, 866 — All Saints' Day. The Northumbrian kings Aelle and Osberht, who had been fighting each other, briefly reconciled and tried to retake the city in March 867. Both kings were killed. Northumbria became, effectively, a Viking kingdom.

According to a legend that the Icelandic sagas would tell three centuries later, the leadership of the Great Heathen Army was the sons of **Ragnar Lothbrok** — among them **Ivar the Boneless**, **Halfdan**, and **Ubba** — who had come to England specifically to avenge their father's death at the hands of King Aelle. Aelle, the saga says, was put to death by the **blood eagle** (a particularly horrible ritualized execution where the victim's ribs were cut from the spine and folded outward like wings, and the lungs pulled through the gap; whether the blood eagle was an actual practice or a poetic flourish from later Christian chroniclers describing pagan Vikings is one of the genuinely unsettled debates of Viking-Age scholarship). The historicity of Ragnar himself is contested — he is far more legend than man — but the historicity of his "sons" leading the Great Heathen Army is reasonably solid: contemporary sources do name **Ivar** and **Halfdan** as commanders, and **Ubba** appears in the Anglo-Saxon record. Whatever the truth about the patriarch, the family enterprise was real.

The Great Heathen Army would spend the next fifteen years reshaping the political map of England. East Anglia fell in 869 (the East Anglian king Edmund was tied to a tree and shot full of arrows, becoming Saint Edmund the Martyr, England's first patron saint before St. George took the role centuries later). Mercia, the central English midland kingdom, was carved up in 874. By 875, of the four traditional Anglo-Saxon kingdoms — Northumbria, East Anglia, Mercia, Wessex — three had been destroyed or reduced to vassal states. Only **Wessex** in the southwest still had a functioning native crown, and the Heathen Army was about to come for it.

The crown belonged to a 22-year-old named Alfred, who was about to become the most consequential king in English history.

Cross-civ check: the Great Heathen Army's invasion of England (865) coincides with the end of the **Tang dynasty's** stability in China (the Huang Chao Rebellion would erupt in 874), the height of the **Abbasid Caliphate's** intellectual flowering in Baghdad, the early **Pala dynasty** in Bengal, the **Heian court** flourishing in Japan, and the Maya **Terminal Classic** collapse in the southern lowlands. While Saxon kings died in the rain at Northumbrian battlefields, Baghdad scholars were translating Aristotle into Arabic. The world was bigger and stranger than either of them knew.

# Chapter 3 — Conquest and Settlement

In May 878, in the marshy fenlands of Somerset in southwestern England, a king who had been hiding in the swamps for four months emerged with a small army and won a battle that saved the English language.

That is not an overstatement. If **Alfred the Great** (king of Wessex from 871 to 899) had lost the **Battle of Edington** to the Viking warlord **Guthrum**, there would be no kingdom of England as a continuous Anglo-Saxon political entity. The whole of England would have become, within a generation or two, a Norse-speaking province of a wider Scandinavian world. The language we are reading right now — English, the Germanic-Romance hybrid that grew out of the Anglo-Saxon dialects of Wessex — would have been replaced by Old Norse the way Latin replaced Etruscan. None of this was inevitable. It came down to one battle in a swamp.

The setup: the Great Heathen Army had spent the early 870s reducing the rest of Anglo-Saxon England, and by 875 the remaining Norse force in England was led by Guthrum, a Danish chieftain who had carved out a power base in East Anglia. In January 878, Guthrum launched a surprise winter attack on Wessex — winter campaigning was rare in early-medieval Europe because armies dissolved without supplies, but Guthrum's force was based locally and could feed itself off Wessex farms. The attack caught Alfred at his hunting lodge at Chippenham during the Twelfth Night celebrations. Alfred barely escaped. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle describes him as fleeing with a small bodyguard "through woods and fen-fastnesses" to the Isle of Athelney, a small dry rise in the Somerset marshes, where he built a fort and waited.

The story that he burned the cakes — that, in hiding at a peasant woman's hut, he was so distracted by his thoughts that he failed to watch the cakes she had asked him to mind, and was scolded for letting them char — is from a later medieval chronicle and is almost certainly invented. What is not invented is that Alfred genuinely was reduced, in the spring of 878, to a refugee king with a bodyguard, hiding in a swamp, while the rest of his kingdom was occupied by people who wanted to kill him. He was 28 years old. He had been king for seven years. He had a hereditary illness (probably Crohn's disease, based on his contemporary biographer's medical descriptions) that gave him intermittent agonizing intestinal pain. And he was, somehow, going to win.

In the spring he sent runners to summon the levy of three Wessex shires: Somerset, Wiltshire, and Hampshire. The men came. In May 878, Alfred met Guthrum's army at **Edington** in Wiltshire. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle says Alfred "fought against the whole army and put it to flight, and pursued it as far as the fortress." Guthrum and his survivors were besieged in Chippenham for two weeks, ran out of food, and surrendered. The peace terms — the **Treaty of Wedmore**, signed at Wedmore in Somerset later that year — were extraordinary. Guthrum agreed to convert to Christianity. He stood as godfather to himself in Alfred's presence at his baptism, taking the Christian name **Æthelstan** (no relation to the later King Æthelstan of England, but a politically loaded choice — the name meant "noble stone"). Alfred and Guthrum then negotiated a partition of England: Wessex and western Mercia for Alfred, eastern and northern England for Guthrum and the Norse settlers.

That partition, formalized in a separate treaty around 886, created the **Danelaw** — the territory of England under Norse legal custom, stretching roughly from the line of the River Thames east of London north to the River Tees in Yorkshire, and including the cities of York, Lincoln, Leicester, Nottingham, Stamford, Derby, and Norwich. Within the Danelaw, Norse customary law applied: legal procedures, weights and measures, land-tenure conventions, and (crucially) place-names became Scandinavian. Modern English place-names ending in *-by* (a Norse word for "village" or "farm" — Derby, Whitby, Selby, Grimsby), *-thorpe* (a satellite settlement — Scunthorpe, Grimsthorpe), and *-thwaite* (a clearing — Bassenthwaite, Crosthwaite) are the linguistic fingerprint of the Danelaw, still legible on modern Ordnance Survey maps a thousand years later. England outside the Danelaw is a country of *-tons*, *-hams*, and *-hursts* — Anglo-Saxon endings. Inside the Danelaw, you cannot drive ten miles without hitting a Norse place-name. The map is a map of conquest.

The English language itself was permanently altered by the Danelaw. Old English and Old Norse were closely related Germanic languages — about as different as modern Dutch and German, mutually slow but possible to follow with effort. After three or four generations of bilingual contact in the Danelaw, English absorbed an enormous vocabulary from Norse. The pronouns *they*, *them*, *their* are Norse, replacing the original Old English *hi*, *hem*, *hera*. The everyday verbs *take*, *give*, *get*, *call*, *cast*, *die*, *want* are Norse. *Sky*, *skin*, *skull*, *egg*, *husband*, *window*, *knife*, *leg*, *root*, *cake*, *fellow*, *gift*, *law*, *outlaw* — Norse. The structure of English grammar simplified during this period, partly because the simplest mutual-intelligibility move when two related languages are in contact is to drop the case-endings that don't agree. The English language we are reading now is a creole of Anglo-Saxon and Old Norse, with French painted on top after 1066. Without the Danelaw, English would have ended up much closer to modern German. The Vikings who lost at Edington won the battle for the language anyway.

---

England was the loud half of the story. The Danelaw farmers, the Edington battle, the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle's blow-by-blow account — these are the parts of the Viking Age that medieval Christian Europe wrote down and remembered. But the same generations that produced Alfred and Guthrum also produced a quieter and more astonishing set of voyages, made by Norse men and women who never bothered Wessex at all. They sailed the other way: not south to plunder old kingdoms but west into empty water, looking for land nobody owned. The same longships that ferried the Great Heathen Army across the North Sea could, with a steadier nerve and a fairer wind, cross hundreds of miles of open ocean to islands no European had ever seen. While the southern half of the Norse diaspora was reshaping England, a different branch was doing something almost no one in the Mediterranean world believed was possible: settling the open North Atlantic.

The chronology of the North Atlantic settlement is, in outline:

- Around **825**, Irish monks (the **Papar**, "fathers" — tiny groups of Celtic anchorites looking for desert islands to be solitary on) reached **Iceland** but did not settle in numbers. The 12th-century Icelandic *Landnámabók* ("Book of Settlement") would later record them as the first arrivals.
- In **860**, a Norwegian named **Naddodd** sighted Iceland accidentally during a storm-blown passage to the Faroe Islands and named what he saw "Snowland."
- In **868**, **Floki Vilgerðarson** sailed deliberately to Iceland with three sacred ravens that he released at intervals to find the way (the first one flew back to the Faroes; the second flew up and returned; the third flew on, and Floki followed it). Floki gave the island its modern name, **Ísland** — Iceland — after a particularly hard winter. He was the first to overwinter there.
- In **874**, **Ingólfr Arnarson** became the first confirmed permanent Norse settler, founding the homestead that would become **Reykjavík** (RAY-kya-veek, "Smoky Bay," named for the volcanic steam vents in the area). The Landnámabók says he threw his **high-seat pillars** (carved wooden posts that flanked a chieftain's seat in a longhouse, treated as a sacred clan possession) into the sea as he approached the coast and built his hall where they washed ashore. The story — pagan ritual divination guiding the founding of a homestead — sets the tone for how Iceland was settled.

What followed, between 874 and roughly 930, is called the **Landnám** ("land-taking") period in Icelandic tradition. Approximately 9,000 to 20,000 Norse colonists — mostly from southwestern Norway, but also from Norse Ireland, the Hebrides, and the Northern Isles — arrived in waves and claimed every habitable patch of the island. By 930, the *Landnámabók* records, all the available land in Iceland was taken. The total population was probably about 60,000 by the year 1000.

A great deal of the immigration was driven by political pressure back home. **Harald Fairhair** (Harald Hárfagri, c. 860–933) — the first king to attempt to unify all of Norway under a single crown — had defeated his rival petty kings at the **Battle of Hafrsfjord** around 872 and demanded the submission of the surviving aristocracy. Many Norwegian chieftains preferred emigration to subjugation, and Iceland — empty, far away, and beyond Harald's reach — was the obvious destination. The Icelandic sagas would later mythologize this as "the freedom-loving Norwegians fleeing tyranny," which is partly self-serving but partly accurate. Iceland was a refuge for the kind of independent-minded chieftain who did not want a king.

So in 930, the settlers did something extraordinary: they convened a national assembly and declared a republic.

The **Althing** (Old Norse *Alþingi*, AL-thing-ee, "general assembly"), founded in 930 at **Þingvellir** (THING-veh-leer, "Assembly Plains," a dramatic geological site at the boundary of the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates, where a long fissure rift forms a natural amphitheater), is one of the longest-running parliamentary institutions in the world. The Icelanders met every June at Þingvellir for two weeks. They appointed a **Lawspeaker** (Old Norse *lögsögumaðr*, LERG-sergh-oo-mathr, "law-speaking man"), whose job it was to recite the entire body of Icelandic customary law from memory in three-year cycles, a third each summer (which is how a non-literate society stored its constitutional law: in the head of one specialist, refreshed annually). The Lawspeaker did not rule; he merely remembered. Disputes were arbitrated by panels of free farmers. Major decisions of the commonwealth — including the conversion to Christianity in 1000 (Chapter 5) — were taken by acclamation at Þingvellir.

This was not quite democracy in the modern sense (slaves and women had no voting power, and the system was dominated by a class of about 36 chieftains who held the *goðorð* offices), but it was a remarkable functional achievement: a stateless commonwealth running for 330 years on customary law, oral tradition, and the personal authority of the Lawspeaker, with no king, no standing army, no royal court, no taxation. The Icelandic Free State persisted until 1262, when civil wars among the leading clans drove the country into the arms of the Norwegian crown. Three centuries is a long run. By way of comparison, the **Roman Republic** lasted just under five centuries (Ancient Rome Ch 4), the United States is currently 250 years old, and the original Icelandic system held together for longer than the British constitutional monarchy has been in its modern form. Iceland was, in early-medieval terms, an experiment that worked.

The **Grágás** ("Grey Goose") laws — the body of customary law of the Icelandic Free State, named possibly for the goose-quill on which they would be written, possibly because the Old Norse word for goose connoted ancient wisdom — were finally committed to writing in 1117–1118, after Iceland had Christianized and acquired the Latin alphabet through the church. The Grey Goose laws are dense, precise, and procedurally elaborate: they regulate inheritance, dowry, manslaughter compensation, slave manumission, ship ownership, fishing rights, wood-cutting rights, cattle trespass, beached whales (a serious matter in a deforested island where any timber, even a piece of driftwood, was valuable), and the etiquette of formally calling on a neighbor to witness a legal transaction. They are also, between the lines, a portrait of an entire society that had decided to do without kings.

---

A note on something that does not get enough attention: a great many of Iceland's founding settlers were women, and several of them came as the heads of their own households.

The most famous is **Aud the Deep-Minded** (Old Norse *Auðr djúpúðga*, EWTH the deep-OOTH-ga; sometimes called Unn the Deep-Minded in different manuscripts), an Irish-Norse noblewoman whose career reads like a saga because it is one. Born probably in the Hebrides in the 830s, she was the daughter of a Norse chieftain and the wife of a Viking king of Dublin (Olaf the White). When her husband and son were both killed in different battles in Scotland and Ireland, Aud — by then in her sixties — gathered up her remaining family, freed her slaves, distributed dowries to her granddaughters, organized passage on a knarr (a wider, deeper Norse merchant ship for cargo and emigration, distinct from the slim warship-style longship), and led a household of perhaps fifty people across the open Atlantic from Scotland to Iceland in the late 880s. She established her family at Hvammr in western Iceland, became one of the most respected matriarchs of the early commonwealth, and converted to Christianity before she died. The Landnámabók devotes substantial space to her descendants, who included some of the most powerful Icelandic families.

Aud is not unique. The settlement records list other woman-headed households, female priests of the pagan gods (the female counterpart of the *goði* was the *gyðja*, GITH-ya), and women whose dowries gave them control of substantial estates. Norse society was patriarchal in the standard early-medieval sense, but it was not as patriarchal as its Mediterranean Christian contemporaries; women had significant property rights, could initiate divorce, owned the keys to the household stores (a literal Norse symbol of female authority — the keys were worn on a brooch), and could lead emigration parties when their husbands died. The Vikings did not invent female autonomy, but they preserved a Germanic-pagan version of it that the Christianizing Continent had largely buried under Roman legal categories.

---

Iceland was the first true open-ocean colony of the Viking Age. It was the first time a substantial population had been transferred across hundreds of miles of open Atlantic to a previously uninhabited landmass. This was not coastal hopping. Iceland is 600 miles (960 km) from Norway across some of the most dangerous water in the world — the Norwegian Sea, the Iceland-Faroes Ridge, the cold Atlantic depressions that come barreling east off Greenland. To sail to Iceland and back you needed the longship, the open-ocean sailing rig, the **sun compass** and **sunstone** for navigation in the absence of compasses (Chapter 4 notes the Norse may have used calcite crystals to find the sun's position on overcast days), the dead-reckoning skill of generations of fishermen, and a willingness to lose a quarter of your fleet to weather as a normal cost of business. The Norse had all of those, and they paid the price.

Iceland would, in turn, be the launchpad for the next move.

In 982, an Icelandic chieftain named **Erik the Red** (Old Norse *Eiríkr rauði*, EYE-reek-er ROW-thee) was outlawed from Iceland after killing several men in a property dispute. Outlawry was a heavy sentence: an outlaw could be killed by anyone without legal consequence, his property was forfeit, and he was to leave the country at the next sailing season. Erik had already heard rumors of a land to the west — sighted, supposedly, decades earlier by a Norwegian named Gunnbjörn during a storm-blown passage. Erik decided to use his three-year exile to find it.

He found it. The sailing was rough; he made landfall on the southwestern coast of what we now call Greenland and spent three years coasting and exploring. He named the place **Greenland** — *Grænland* — because, in his own quoted words from the *Saga of Erik the Red*, "men would be more readily persuaded to come there if it had a good name." This is the first explicit recorded act of marketing in Norse history. Greenland is not, in fact, particularly green. The southwestern coast does have summer pasture — meadow grasses and birch scrub in the protected fjords — and you can pasture cattle and sheep there during the brief Arctic summer, which is what the colonists did. But the interior is a continental ice sheet over a mile thick, and the coast outside the southwestern fjords is a rampart of black rock and floating ice. Erik had picked the one habitable strip and was selling it as if the whole island looked like that.

The marketing worked. In 985 (or possibly 986), a fleet of 25 ships set out from Iceland for Greenland. Fourteen ships made it through; the others were lost or turned back. The survivors founded two settlements: the **Eastern Settlement** (Eystribyggð) near the modern town of Qaqortoq, and the **Western Settlement** (Vestribyggð) further north near modern Nuuk. The total population eventually reached perhaps 2,000 to 3,000 at its 12th-century peak. The colonists farmed sheep and cattle, hunted seals and walrus (the walrus tusk was the Greenland economy's main export, a prestige material in Continental Europe — a chunk of carved walrus-tusk ivory could cross half of Europe in trade — and the small island chess set with figures of carved walrus-tusk known as the Lewis chessmen, found on a beach in the Outer Hebrides in 1831, is probably a Greenland export that washed up halfway home), and traded with passing Icelandic and Norwegian ships. They built turf-roofed longhouses and stone churches. They held annual assemblies. They went on hunts deep into the Arctic, leaving runic graffiti on rocks above the 73rd parallel.

Greenland would persist, against the steadily worsening climate of the Little Ice Age, until the late 1400s. The Western Settlement was abandoned around 1350; the Eastern Settlement vanished sometime between 1450 and 1500, leaving no clear written record of how. The last documented contact was a wedding at Hvalsey Church in 1408, recorded in a letter to the Icelandic bishop. After that, silence. When Hans Egede, a Danish-Norwegian missionary, reached southwestern Greenland in 1721, he found only ruins. The Greenland colony is the longest-lived European colony in the New World that ultimately failed: nearly five hundred years of pasturing sheep on the edge of an ice sheet, ended by climate, isolation, and the slow attrition of a population too small to replenish itself. It is also, in its own way, one of the great human stories of adaptation — a few thousand farmers held an Atlantic foothold through the Medieval Warm Period and could not hold it through the Little Ice Age. The lights went out one winter at a time.

But before they went out, the Greenlanders made one more move.

Around the year 1000, the year the Althing voted to Christianize Iceland, **Leif Erikson** — son of Erik the Red, born in Iceland, raised in Greenland — sailed west from the Greenland settlements toward an even further coast that traders had glimpsed years earlier. He coasted the eastern shore of what we now call North America and landed at three places he named **Helluland** ("Stone-Slab Land," probably Baffin Island), **Markland** ("Forest Land," probably Labrador), and **Vinland** ("Vine Land" or "Wine Land," probably the northern part of Newfoundland, where wild grapes do not actually grow but other vines do, and the name may reflect a misidentification of berries or a deliberate marketing flourish in the family tradition). At a bay on the northern tip of Newfoundland, the Norse built a small encampment.

The site was found in 1960 by the Norwegian explorer-archaeologists Helge and Anne Stine Ingstad, working from clues in the sagas. **L'Anse aux Meadows** (lans-oh-MEH-doh, French for "Bay of Meadows") sits on the windswept tip of Newfoundland — three turf-walled longhouses, a workshop, and a small smithy, dated by radiocarbon to around the year 1000, with iron nails forged from local bog iron in a Norse style and butternut shells indicating that the inhabitants had ranged at least as far south as New Brunswick. It is the only confirmed Viking site in North America. It is conclusive proof that the Norse reached the New World five centuries before Columbus.

Why did Vinland fail? The sagas describe escalating conflicts with the indigenous **Skraelings** (Old Norse *Skrælingjar*, SKRAY-ling-ar; a derogatory Norse term that probably referred to the Beothuk or other northeastern coastal peoples) — first trade, then trade gone bad, then violent skirmishes. **Thorfinn Karlsefni**, an Icelandic merchant, led the most ambitious colonization attempt around 1010 with 160 settlers and three ships. They lasted three years. **Freydis Eriksdottir** (Erik the Red's formidable, controversial daughter) led a final expedition that ended in internal Norse violence — the sagas tell a story of Freydis personally killing five women on the expedition with an axe, though whether this is history or saga drama is unresolved. By about 1015, the Vinland project had been abandoned. The Norse had reached America. They could not hold it. Without the population base to send hundreds of settlers, without the iron and steel weapons that gave 16th-century Spaniards a decisive technological edge, and against a substantial indigenous population that did not want them there, the Norse settlements in Newfoundland were small parties of lightly armed colonists facing the same problem the English at Roanoke would face six hundred years later. They left.

The lesson, looking back at the whole western expansion: the Vikings had reached the absolute limit of what their technology could do. From a longhouse in Norway in 793, Norse society had pushed across the open Atlantic, founded one of the world's longest-lived republics on a previously uninhabited island, established the first European colony in the New World, and bumped up against the edge of what longships and pre-industrial population numbers could sustain. The Vikings stopped not because they ran out of ambition. They stopped because they had run out of physics.

# Chapter 4 — East and the Rus'

While the western branch of the Norse diaspora was settling Iceland and bouncing off Newfoundland, a different and arguably more consequential branch was going in the opposite direction.

**Sweden** in the 9th century was less politically prominent than Norway or Denmark, but it had a geographic advantage that neither could match: it faced east, across the Baltic, into the headwaters of the Russian river system (the earliest hard evidence for this eastward push is the Salme boat-grave on Estonia, circa 750, roughly forty years before Lindisfarne and the first physically attested Scandinavian sea-raid). From a Swedish trading post on the Baltic coast, you could row a small boat into the Gulf of Finland, ascend the Neva to Lake Ladoga, work up the Volkhov to Lake Ilmen, and reach Novgorod — a distance of about 200 miles inland, navigable in days. From Lake Ilmen, you could portage south to the headwaters of either the **Dnieper** (NEE-per) or the **Volga**, the two great rivers that drained the East European Plain south to, respectively, the Black Sea and the Caspian Sea. By drag-and-row, the Swedish merchant-warriors discovered, you could connect the Baltic to two of the richest commercial worlds in 9th-century Eurasia: the **Byzantine Empire** centered on Constantinople, and the **Abbasid Caliphate** centered on Baghdad. The Swedes spent the next two centuries doing exactly that, and it changed both Russia and the Norse world forever.

Slavic-speaking and Finnic-speaking peoples on the Russian rivers called these eastern Vikings the **Rus'** (ROOS) — a term whose etymology is disputed, but which probably derives either from the Old Norse *Rōþskarlar* ("rowing men," the men who plied the rowing routes) or from the Finnish *Ruotsi* ("Sweden"), and which would, by an extraordinary linguistic accident, become the root of the words *Russia* and *Russian*. The country that would eventually call itself Russia is named after a Norse rowing crew. More on that in a moment.

The eastern Norse expansion ran in parallel with the western raids and settlements but was driven by a different logic. The west was about plunder and farmland. The east was about trade — specifically, about silver.

---

The Abbasid Caliphate in the 9th century was the wealthiest single political entity on earth. From its capital at Baghdad (founded in 762, the largest city in the world by 800 with roughly 700,000 inhabitants), the Abbasids minted enormous quantities of high-quality silver coins called **dirhams** — heavy, thin, broad coins struck with Quranic calligraphy and the issuing caliph's name and date. The Abbasid mints produced tens of millions of dirhams across the 9th and 10th centuries, and the dirhams flowed outward through trade as the dominant currency of the entire Islamic and Eurasian commercial world. They were the dollar of their century. And from about 800 onward, an extraordinary number of them flowed *north*, up the Volga, into the Norse world. The Islamic Golden Age Ch 8 covers the economic context — the Caliphate's silver mining, trade revenues, and monetary policy — that made this flood of dirhams possible in the first place.

Archaeologists have recovered hundreds of thousands of Abbasid silver dirhams from Viking-Age hoards across Scandinavia, the Baltic, and northern Russia. The largest hoards — from Gotland (a Baltic island that became the great Norse silver vault), from Hedeby, from Birka — contain tens of thousands of coins. The **Spillings hoard**, found on Gotland in 1999, contains about 14,000 silver coins (mostly Abbasid, some Sasanian, some Byzantine) weighing 67 kilograms (147 pounds) — the largest single Viking silver hoard ever recovered. Multiply that by the dozens of large hoards and the thousands of small ones, and the scale becomes clear: the Norse world imported, by some estimates, well over a hundred tonnes of silver from the Islamic Caliphate during the 9th and 10th centuries. That is a structural fact about the Viking economy. Most of the silver that the western raiders extracted from Frankish and English Danegelds was minor, in volume, compared to the eastern flow.

What did the Norse trade for the silver?

Furs (especially **sable**, **ermine**, and **fox**, prized at every point on the route — the medieval Mediterranean had no fur of its own to speak of, and the cold luxury market was dominated by the Russian boreal forest), **walrus ivory** from Greenland, **amber** from the Baltic shores, **honey**, **wax**, and — overwhelmingly — **slaves**. The Old Norse term *mansal* meant "the sale of human beings," and this was the central commerce of the Volga route. Norse raiders took captives in the western raids on Britain and Ireland, in the Baltic against the Slavs and Balts, in the Russian forests against the Finno-Ugric and Slavic peoples; the captives moved south down the Russian rivers in chains and were sold in **Itil** (the Khazar capital on the lower Volga) and **Bulghar** (a major Volga trading entrepôt) into the Islamic world — where the Abbasid demand for household servants, concubines, and slave soldiers sustained the market at scale (Islamic Golden Age Ch 8). Some ended up in Baghdad. Some ended up in Cordoba. The Arab geographer **Ibn Fadlan** (a 10th-century Abbasid envoy whose 922 travelogue describing his journey to the Volga Bulghars is one of the great primary sources for the Rus') described the Rus' merchants on the Volga in detail: tall, tattooed men carrying axes and seaxes; wealthy in dirhams; and accompanied by women in bondage whom they bought, sold, and abused under his appalled gaze. Ibn Fadlan also witnessed and described in horrifying detail the funeral of a Rus' chieftain on the Volga, including the strangling and ritual stabbing of a slave girl who had volunteered (or been volunteered) to follow her master into death. His account of the Rus' is one of the great pieces of 10th-century reportage. It is also, quietly, an inventory of what slavery looked like at the receiving end.

---

The political crystallization of the Rus' begins in the 860s, with a figure named **Rurik**.

According to the **Primary Chronicle** (the 12th-century compilation of Kievan Rus' historical traditions, written in Old Church Slavonic by Christian monks long after the events it describes), Slavic and Finnic tribes around Lake Ladoga and the Novgorod region invited a Varangian (Norse) chieftain named Rurik and his brothers to come and rule them, because "our land is great and rich, but there is no order in it." "No order" meant something concrete: the local tribes had been paying tribute to other Varangian raiders, then driven them off, then started fighting among themselves, with rival chiefs raiding each other's villages and no recognized arbitrator to settle blood-feuds or enforce treaties. What the chronicler is describing is a stateless region with no monopoly on violence — the same problem the Norse themselves had at home, only worse, because there was no shared kinship network or assembly tradition to fall back on. The remedy proposed was, in effect, to import a chieftain from outside who would be too high above the local quarrels to take sides. This is the classic invitation legend; whether anyone actually invited anyone is doubtful, and the Primary Chronicle's framing — "we asked the Vikings to come and govern us" — is suspiciously convenient for later generations of Rus' rulers who wanted to legitimize their dynasty. What is not doubtful is that around **862**, a Norse-led polity coalesced at **Novgorod** ("New City"), with Rurik or someone like him at its head, and that this polity would, within a generation, expand to dominate the entire Russian river system.

In **882**, Rurik's successor (or kinsman; the genealogies are fuzzy) **Oleg** (Old Norse Helgi) moved the Rus' political center south from Novgorod to **Kiev** on the middle Dnieper. Kiev was a much better commercial site than Novgorod: it sat on the main southern river, it commanded the trade route to Constantinople, and it had richer agricultural land around it. Oleg's seizure of Kiev — he is said to have killed the two existing Rus' chieftains there by tricking them into a meeting — and his declaration that Kiev would be "the mother of Rus' cities" is the conventional founding date of **Kievan Rus'**, the medieval state that would, over the next two centuries, evolve from a Norse trading enterprise into a Slavic Christian kingdom and ultimately into the cultural foundation of all three modern East Slavic nations: Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus.

The trajectory deserves a pause. It is one of the great cases in world history of an immigrant warrior elite assimilating into the population it ruled. The early Rus' rulers were Norse — their names were Norse (Rurik, Oleg, Igor, Olga, Svyatoslav, Vladimir all derive from Old Norse Hrørek, Helgi, Ingvarr, Helga, Sveinald, and Valdamar respectively), their warband ethos was Norse, their longships were Norse. By the third generation, they were marrying Slavic and Khazar princesses. By the fifth, they spoke Slavic at home. By the sixth, the dynasty was indistinguishable from the population it governed except by lineage. The Rurikid dynasty would rule the lands of the Rus' for the next seven centuries — until 1610 — and would govern, by the late medieval period, an empire that no early-medieval Norseman would have recognized as having anything to do with Scandinavia. The mechanism is one of the cleanest examples in history of how a small warrior elite can be culturally swallowed by the people it conquers: marry into them, learn their language, adopt their religion, and within a few generations the only thing Norse about you is the family tree.

---

Rus' diplomacy with **Constantinople** ran on a peculiar mix of raids and trade treaties.

Constantinople in 860 was the largest city in Christendom, a million-plus inhabitants behind the most formidable land walls in the world (the Theodosian Walls, three concentric defensive lines stretching across the peninsula on which the city sat — Persian Empire Ch 8 for the Byzantine context). The city sat at the junction of the Black Sea and the Mediterranean, controlled the Bosporus, and ran a Mediterranean trading economy that would make any Norse merchant's mouth water. The Vikings looked at this city, calculated, and went for it.

In **June 860**, while the Byzantine emperor **Michael III** was campaigning in Anatolia, a Rus' fleet of about 200 ships emerged out of the Black Sea, descended on the unprepared Bosporan suburbs, and plundered them for ten days. The city itself, behind its walls, was untouched, but the shock to Byzantine prestige was enormous — and it was reciprocated by the shock to Norse imagination. The Rus' had now seen Constantinople. The patriarch **Photius**, who watched the raid from the walls, preached two surviving sermons about it, calling the raiders "an obscure nation, a nation of no account, a nation ranked among slaves, unknown, but which has won a name from the expedition against us." The "nation of no account" had just become a player.

The raids became a recurring feature. Oleg led a major expedition against Constantinople in **907** that resulted in a celebrated trade treaty: Rus' merchants got the right to trade tax-free in Constantinople, to be lodged at imperial expense for six months a year, and to take a fixed monthly stipend of bread, wine, fish, and meat — extraordinary commercial privileges, the kind of thing the Byzantines did not extend to anybody. The treaty cemented the **Dnieper trade route** as the great Rus' artery to the Mediterranean. In **941**, **Igor of Kiev** (Oleg's successor) launched another major attack on Constantinople. This one ran into the Byzantine secret weapon — **Greek fire** (a flammable liquid mix, possibly petroleum-based, projected through bronze siphons mounted on the prows of Byzantine warships, which clung and burned even on water and could not be extinguished by normal means; the formula was a Byzantine state secret so closely guarded that it was forgotten by the 13th century and has never been definitively reconstructed). Igor's fleet was burned to the waterline. He limped home with a fraction of his ships and his men. But he was undeterred enough to mount another raid in 944, and the Byzantines, weary, signed a fresh and slightly less favorable treaty.

Igor was killed in 945 by the Drevlians (a Slavic tribe) when he tried to extort a second tribute from them. His widow **Olga** — a Norse-Slavic queen with a saga-worthy ferocity — took the regency for their young son and avenged him with a campaign so brutal it became legendary even in a brutal era. According to the Primary Chronicle, Olga buried alive the first Drevlian envoys, burned a second delegation in a bathhouse, killed 5,000 Drevlians at her husband's funeral feast, and burned down the Drevlian capital with the help of pigeons whose tails she set alight (the pigeons flew home to their roosts and ignited the thatched roofs of every house in town). The pigeon story is almost certainly fictional; the rest may be exaggerated; but Olga's campaign of revenge was real enough that the Drevlians ceased to exist as an autonomous people and were absorbed into the Kievan Rus'. She was a person you did not cross.

In about **957**, in one of the most consequential individual decisions of the Viking Age, Olga traveled to Constantinople and accepted Christian baptism. The Byzantine emperor Constantine VII Porphyrogenitus (who left a court-protocol manual describing her visit in detail) stood as her godfather. She returned to Kiev a Christian. Her son Svyatoslav refused to convert — his warband would have laughed at him, he said — but Olga had set in motion a process that her grandson would complete.

---

The grandson was **Vladimir**, and Vladimir's conversion in **988** is one of the great hinge moments in European religious history.

The Primary Chronicle preserves a wonderful (and almost certainly stylized) story about how Vladimir picked his religion. Faced with the question of which monotheism to adopt — Kievan Rus' was now wealthy enough to need a state religion, the old Slavic-Norse paganism was no longer adequate to a multiethnic territorial kingdom, and the neighbors all had options on offer — Vladimir is said to have sent envoys to investigate the great faiths. The Volga Bulghar Muslims were rejected because their ban on alcohol was "incompatible with the joys of the Rus'" ("Drinking is the joy of the Russians; we cannot live without it" is Vladimir's reported line; whether he actually said it or not, it is now permanently associated with him). The Khazar Jews were rejected because their having lost Jerusalem suggested God had abandoned them. The Western Roman Christians were rejected because their churches lacked beauty. The Byzantine Orthodox envoys, taken to a service at Hagia Sophia in Constantinople, sent back the report: "We knew not whether we were in heaven or on earth." Vladimir picked Orthodox Christianity.

The choice mattered. A different ruler picking Roman Catholicism would have placed Russia in the Latin West rather than the Greek East. A different ruler picking Islam would have integrated Russia into the Dar al-Islam. By choosing Constantinople over Rome and Constantinople over Baghdad, Vladimir aligned the Rus' permanently with the Byzantine cultural sphere — the Cyrillic alphabet (developed by 9th-century Byzantine missionaries Saints Cyril and Methodius for converting Slavs), Orthodox liturgy, Byzantine architectural styles, Greek-derived theological vocabulary, and the political theology of Caesaropapism that the Russian tsars would borrow wholesale a millennium later. Russia is Russian today in a culturally specific way because of choices made by a Norse-descended prince in Kiev in 988. The longshot is hard to overstate.

Vladimir followed his decision with mass action. According to the Chronicle, he ordered the wooden idol of the thunder god **Perun** (the Slavic equivalent of Thor) to be tied to a horse's tail, dragged down to the Dnieper, and beaten with sticks by twelve men until it could be thrown into the river. The population of Kiev was then ordered into the Dnieper for mass baptism. ("If anyone fails to come, let him be considered an enemy of the prince.") The conversion was top-down, fast, and not voluntary in any modern sense. But it stuck. Within a generation, Kiev had Byzantine-trained bishops, stone Orthodox churches, and a literate clergy producing the first Old Church Slavonic manuscripts.

---

There is one more eastern thread to follow, because it loops the Norse world all the way back to itself.

In 988, the same year Vladimir was baptizing Kiev, the Byzantine emperor **Basil II** was facing a serious revolt by Anatolian generals. Vladimir, as part of a marriage alliance that gave him the emperor's sister Anna, sent Basil 6,000 elite Norse warriors as imperial bodyguards. These men formed the founding cohort of what would become one of the most prestigious military units in the medieval world: the **Varangian Guard**, the emperor's personal household troops, recruited overwhelmingly from Scandinavia, the Rus' lands, and (later) Anglo-Saxon England. The Guard fought at Constantinople for nearly five centuries, until the city's fall to the Ottomans in 1453. They were paid in gold. They wore distinctive battle-axes. They spoke Old Norse on duty into the 13th century. And they served as the route by which dozens of Norse warlords made fortunes in Byzantine service and then went home.

The most famous was **Harald Sigurdsson** — better known to history as **Harald Hardrada** ("Hard-Counsel," HARD-rah-da, c. 1015–1066), half-brother of King Olaf II of Norway. After Olaf's death at Stiklestad in 1030 (Chapter 5), Harald, then 15 years old, fled east and joined the Varangian Guard in Constantinople. He spent the next decade fighting Byzantine wars in Sicily, Bulgaria, the Caucasus, North Africa, and Anatolia, accumulating an enormous personal fortune in pay and plunder. By the time he returned to Norway in 1045 and claimed his inheritance, he was one of the wealthiest individuals in the Norse world — and one of the most experienced soldiers. He would rule Norway for the next twenty years and die at Stamford Bridge in 1066 (Chapter 8) trying to take a kingdom that wasn't his.

Harald is the cleanest example of how the eastern and western branches of the Norse world were connected. A Norwegian king, a survivor of his half-brother's failed Christian campaign, fled east, joined the imperial guard of the most cosmopolitan city on earth, fought in Sicily within sight of North Africa, retired with a fortune in dirhams and Byzantine *nomismata* (gold coins), came home to claim a Norwegian crown, and died trying to take an English one. He carried a Norse longsword and a Constantinople pension into the same battle. The world was bigger than any one chapter could contain, and Harald saw most of it.

Cross-civ check: the Rus'-Byzantine dirham-flow age (~860–970) overlaps with the **Macedonian Renaissance** in Constantinople (a flowering of Byzantine art, scholarship, and military success), the **Buyid dynasty** in Iran (Persian Empire Ch 9 — Shia rulers controlling Baghdad while leaving the Abbasid caliph as a figurehead), the **Samanid dynasty** in Central Asia (the great patron of Persian-language poetry, Bukhara as a rival to Baghdad), the **Liao dynasty** in northern China, and the **Mayan** Postclassic transition in the Yucatán. The dirhams that paid for Norse longships originally came out of silver mines in Khurasan and Central Asia (Persian Empire Ch 9) and reached Scandinavia by river. The Viking Age was a Eurasian-scale economic system. It was not a local European drama.

# Chapter 5 — The Gods and the New God

The Norse worshipped a family of gods who fought monsters, drank too much, lied, schemed, slept with each other's spouses, knew that they were doomed, and went into the final battle anyway because dying well was the point.

Norse mythology, as we know it, comes to us from two main sources. The **Poetic Edda** is a collection of about 30 anonymous mythological and heroic poems, composed in Iceland and Norway in the Viking Age and the centuries immediately following, preserved in a single 13th-century manuscript called the **Codex Regius** (Latin: "Royal Book"), which spent four hundred years in private hands until it was rediscovered in 1643 by an Icelandic bishop, presented to the Danish king, and survived World War II in a Copenhagen vault. The **Prose Edda** is a 13th-century systematic textbook of Norse mythology written by the Icelandic statesman, scholar, and saga-writer **Snorri Sturluson** (1179–1241) for the use of skalds (court poets) who needed to understand the old myths in order to compose properly even after the conversion. Snorri is our best single source on Norse cosmology. He was also a Christian writing 200 years after the official conversion, working from oral tradition and surviving poems, and his accounts have been smoothed and rationalized in places that we cannot always identify. Caveat: the Norse cosmology described below is the system as Snorri wrote it down, which is the closest we can get, but the actual Viking-era pagan religious experience was almost certainly more local, more variable, and less neatly structured than Snorri's textbook makes it look.

With that caveat, here is the system.

---

The cosmos contains **nine worlds**, arranged around or upon a vast world-tree called **Yggdrasil** (IG-druh-sil, "Odin's Steed," because Odin is said to have hung on the tree to gain wisdom — more on that in a moment). Yggdrasil is an enormous ash tree whose branches extend over all the worlds and whose roots reach down into the underworlds. It is sustained by the **Well of Urd** (the Well of Fate), where the **Norns** (three female fate-spinners, vaguely paralleling the Greek Fates and the Slavic Rozhanitsy, who carve the destinies of gods and men into the wood of the tree) tend its waters. The tree is gnawed by serpents at its roots, plagued by a dragon named **Níðhöggr** at its base, and crowned by an eagle who drops gossip to a squirrel named **Ratatoskr**, who runs up and down the trunk carrying insults between the eagle and the dragon. This is a cosmology with a sense of humor.

The nine worlds are arranged on three rough levels — gods above, humans in the middle, monsters and the dead below — and most of the action in the surviving myths happens by characters traveling between them. The names that matter most for the stories are these:

- **Asgard** — the home of the **Æsir** (EYE-sir), the principal sky-gods (Odin, Thor, Tyr, and so on)
- **Midgard** — Middle-Earth, the world of human beings (Tolkien borrowed the word and the concept directly)
- **Jotunheim** — the home of the **Jötnar** (YERT-nar, often translated "giants" but really an older race of cosmological forces, often hostile, sometimes not, frequently the romantic partners of the gods)
- **Hel** — the realm of the unhonored dead, ruled by a goddess named Hel, half a beautiful woman and half a corpse
- **Muspelheim** — the world of fire, ruled by the fire-giant Surtr, who will burn the cosmos at Ragnarök

The other four — **Vanaheim** (an older lineage of fertility gods who fought and made peace with the Æsir), **Niflheim** (cold and mist), **Alfheim** (light-elves), and **Svartalfheim** (dark elves or dwarves) — fill out the system but rarely drive a story.

The principal gods of the Æsir:

**Odin** (in Old Norse *Óðinn*, OH-thin), the All-Father, god of war, wisdom, poetry, magic, and ritual ecstasy. Odin is one-eyed (he gave his other eye to drink at the Well of Mimir for wisdom). He hangs himself on Yggdrasil for nine days and nights, pierced by his own spear, in order to learn the secret of the runes. He has two ravens, **Huginn** ("Thought") and **Muninn** ("Memory"), who fly out across the world each day and return to whisper news in his ears. He has two wolves, Geri and Freki. He keeps a hall in Asgard called **Valhalla** ("Hall of the Slain"), where the slain warriors brought to him by the **Valkyries** (VAL-kuh-rees, "choosers of the slain" — armored female spirits riding through battle who select which warriors will die and join Odin) feast all night and fight all day in preparation for the final battle. Odin is the patron god of kings, of warriors, and of poets. He is also untrustworthy — he is the god most likely to break an oath, and the warriors who follow him know that he will eventually betray them, because he needs them dead and in Valhalla for his army at Ragnarök.

**Thor** (in Old Norse *Þórr*, THORE), the thunder god, son of Odin, the most popular deity in the Viking Age by a wide margin (Thor's-hammer pendants are by far the most common religious amulet in Norse archaeology, especially in the late pagan period when they appear as a deliberate counter-symbol to the Christian cross). Thor is a red-bearded, hot-tempered, immensely strong protector — of the gods, of humans, of the cosmic order against the giants. He drives a chariot pulled by two goats. He wields the hammer **Mjölnir** (MYERL-neer), which always returns to his hand after being thrown. Thursday is named for him.

**Freyr** (FRAYR) and his sister **Freyja** (FRAY-yah), the great Vanir gods of fertility, peace, and prosperity. Freyr controls rain and harvest. Freyja is associated with love, sexuality, gold, war (she gets half the slain; Odin gets the other half — a detail that gives a sense of her stature), and a magical art called **seiðr** (SAYTH, a kind of ritual divinatory magic that was generally considered women's work, and that was associated with shamanic practice; Odin himself could practice seiðr but it was considered effeminate for him to do so). Freyja drives a chariot pulled by two cats. Friday is named for her.

**Tyr** — the older war god, originally the principal sky-god of the proto-Germanic pantheon, demoted by the Viking Age to a supporting role behind Odin. He has only one hand, having sacrificed the other to bind the great wolf Fenrir.

**Loki** — not exactly a god, more of a **trickster** figure, the father of monsters (the wolf Fenrir, the world-serpent Jörmungandr, the goddess Hel). Loki is hilarious, treacherous, and ultimately catastrophic; he engineers the death of Odin's beloved son Baldur and is bound in a cave with a serpent dripping venom on his face until Ragnarök, when he will break free and fight against the gods.

And looming over the whole cosmology, the central feature that distinguishes Norse mythology from almost every other religious system in the world: **Ragnarök** (RAG-nar-erk, "Doom of the Gods" or "Twilight of the Gods").

The gods know that they are going to die. The Norns have foretold it. The whole cosmic order will end in a great battle at the end of time: the wolf Fenrir will break his chains and devour Odin; Thor will kill the world-serpent and walk nine paces and fall dead from its venom; the fire-giant Surtr will burn the world; the sun will be eaten; the stars will fall. After Ragnarök, a new world will rise from the sea, and a small remnant of gods and humans will repopulate it. But the present cosmic order — the gods we know, the heroes we know, the world we know — is doomed. The warriors in Valhalla are training for a battle they will lose. The heroism is in fighting anyway.

This is a religion of doomed nobility, and it shaped the Viking warrior ethic in concrete ways. A man fated to die cannot avoid his fate. The honorable thing is to face it well — to die with weapon in hand, with one's name on the lips of the next generation. (Whether the conventional Viking concept of Valhalla as "the warrior heaven" was as central to lived practice as the sagas suggest, or was sharpened in the post-conversion saga literature, is debated.) The poem **Hávamál** ("The Sayings of the High One"), the longest of the Eddic poems, contains lines that became proverbial: "Cattle die, kinsmen die, the self must also die; / I know one thing which never dies: / The reputation of each dead man." A reputation outlasts a body. Die well; live in your descendants' memory; that is what immortality is.

---

The lived practice of Norse paganism centered on **blót** (BLOTE) — sacrifice. A blót was a public ritual of animal slaughter (occasionally human, in extreme circumstances), conducted at major seasonal festivals to honor specific gods and seek their favor for the coming season. The slaughtered animal's blood was sprinkled on the altars, on the temple walls, and on the participants. The meat was cooked and eaten communally; the worshippers drank the blood (or, more commonly, an ale toasted in the names of the gods); and the rest of the carcass was burned. The chief blóts of the year:

- **Sigrblót** (Victory-Blót) in early spring, dedicated to Odin, asking for victory in the coming raiding season
- **Miðsumarblót** at midsummer, dedicated to Freyr, asking for a good harvest
- **Vetrnætrablót** (Winter-Nights-Blót) in October, beginning the winter half of the year
- **Jólablót** at midwinter (the origin of the word **Yule**, the great midwinter festival that Christianity would absorb and rebrand as Christmas)

The **Dísablót** was a midwinter sacrifice specifically for the **dísir** — collective female ancestral and fertility spirits. The Dísablót was particularly observed in Sweden, where the central royal cult site at **Uppsala** (uhp-SAH-la) hosted national-scale sacrifices into the late 11th century.

Adam of Bremen, a German cleric writing around 1075 (one of our key non-Norse sources for Viking-Age religion, though he is reporting at second hand from Saxon and Danish informants and is hostile to paganism), described the **Great Heathen Temple at Uppsala** in vivid and somewhat horrified detail: a wooden temple decorated with golden chains, containing three large wooden statues of Odin, Thor, and Freyr (Thor in the central position, with Odin and Freyr on either side); a sacred grove next to the temple where the bodies of sacrificial victims — humans, dogs, and horses — were hung from the trees; and a great festival every nine years at which 99 of each kind of victim (humans, dogs, horses, cattle) were sacrificed. Adam was repeating reports, and parts of his account — especially the human sacrifice details — may be exaggerated by his Christian informants. What is reasonably solid is that Uppsala was the central Swedish royal cult site, that animal sacrifice was a major part of Norse religious practice there, and that the temple persisted, against mounting Christian pressure, until the late 11th century. Uppsala was probably destroyed and replaced by a Christian cathedral around 1090.

The temple at Uppsala is the great late holdout of Norse paganism. By the time Adam was writing, every other major Scandinavian region had nominally converted. Sweden was the last domino, and its national royal cult held on for another century beyond the others.

---

The conversion of Scandinavia was not a single event. It was a 250-year process, full of false starts, reversions, forced baptisms, and political compromises. But the pivotal moments cluster in a roughly fifty-year window: the late 950s through the early 1000s, when most of the great Norse polities went officially Christian.

**Denmark** went first. **Harald Bluetooth** (Harald *Blátǫnn*, ruled c. 958–986) is the king who flipped Denmark, and he made sure everyone knew it. Harald was the son of **Gorm the Old**, the first historically attested king of a unified Denmark, and he was a politically capable, religiously pragmatic, and territorially ambitious ruler. Christianity had been seeping into Denmark for over a century, brought by Frankish missionaries (Ansgar in the 820s) and Christian merchants in the trading towns. By the 960s, Harald was under increasing pressure from the **Holy Roman Empire** (the German-led successor to Charlemagne's empire, then under Otto I) to convert his country, partly because Otto wanted to claim suzerainty over Denmark and a pagan kingdom was a legal target for crusade. Harald accepted baptism around **965** — the Saxon historian Widukind tells a vivid story about how a missionary named Poppo proved the Christian God's superiority by carrying a red-hot iron in his bare hand without injury, which convinced Harald, though the story is conventional and occurs in many medieval conversion narratives.

Harald commemorated his reign and Denmark's conversion with one of the most extraordinary monuments of the Viking Age: the **Jelling Stones** at the royal cult site of Jelling in central Jutland. Harald's father Gorm had erected an earlier, smaller runestone there for his queen Thyra. Harald topped his father's monument with a much larger one — a great **runestone** about ten feet (3 m) tall, on three faces of which is carved (in beautifully executed Viking-era runic script) the inscription:

> "King Harald ordered these monuments made in memory of Gorm his father and Thyra his mother. That Harald who won for himself all of Denmark and Norway, and made the Danes Christian."

On one face is carved a writhing serpent-and-lion battle scene (a Viking animal style of high quality). On another face is carved one of the earliest depictions of **Christ** in Scandinavia — Christ outstretched against a tangle of vines, simultaneously crucified and triumphant. The Jelling Stone is, in effect, **Denmark's baptismal certificate**: a public state monument announcing the country's conversion, in the language of pagan runestones, with Christian imagery, planted in the old royal pagan cult site, by the king who unified the country. It is a propaganda masterpiece, and it works as a propaganda masterpiece a thousand years later: tourist buses still stop there.

Harald's son **Sweyn Forkbeard** (Sveinn Tjúguskegg) revolted against Harald around 986 and exiled him; Harald died of his wounds the same year. Sweyn was a more pragmatic Christian than his father and a more aggressive king. He would, two decades later, become the first Danish ruler of England.

**Norway** went next, less peacefully. **Olaf Tryggvason** (995–1000), a great-grandson of Harald Fairhair, returned from a youth of raiding in the Baltic and the British Isles and seized the Norwegian crown. Olaf had been baptized in England (probably in 994, following a major Viking raid that ended in a peace and a Christianization deal with the English king Æthelred) and he came home a fervent and violent convert. Over a five-year reign he campaigned across Norway forcibly Christianizing region after region — destroying pagan temples, killing recalcitrant chieftains, threatening farmers with mutilation if they refused baptism. He founded the city of **Trondheim** as a new Christian Norwegian capital. And he sponsored conversion missions to Iceland, Greenland, and the Faroes from his court. Olaf was a Christian crusader-king in the literal sense, and his campaigns were ferocious enough to leave a permanent mark on Norwegian historical memory. He died at the **Battle of Svolder** in 1000, betrayed and ambushed by a coalition of Danish, Swedish, and Norwegian rivals, leaping into the Baltic in his armor rather than be captured. His campaigns had succeeded in Christianizing the coastal regions; the inland Norwegian valleys would take another generation.

Olaf's successor as Christian crusader-king of Norway was his (probably distant) kinsman **Olaf Haraldsson** (1015–1028, later canonized as **Saint Olaf**). The second Olaf was a more systematic ruler and a more brutal Christianizer than the first — he sent missionaries (or warbands) into the inland valleys to destroy idols and force baptism, codified the first Norwegian Christian laws, and consolidated royal authority around the new church. He was overthrown by a coalition of Norwegian chieftains backed by Cnut the Great of Denmark in 1028, fled east into exile, returned with an army to reclaim his throne, and was killed at the **Battle of Stiklestad** on July 29, 1030.

Stiklestad was a tactical defeat that became a strategic victory. Within a year, miracles were being reported at Olaf's grave. Within a generation, he had been declared a saint — Norway's first and only national patron saint. The cult of Saint Olaf spread rapidly across Scandinavia, the Norse diaspora, and as far as the Anglo-Saxon and Russian churches; medieval pilgrim routes across Norway converged on his shrine at **Nidaros Cathedral** (NEE-dah-ros, the medieval name for Trondheim, where Olaf's body was eventually interred). The man who had fought to impose Christianity by force became, in death, the saint whose cult sealed the conversion he had tried to win in life. The mechanism is not unique — the same dynamic created the cult of Edmund the Martyr in England — but it is one of the cleanest examples of how a violent Christianization could eventually become a beloved national identity.

---

**Iceland** converted in 1000, and the Icelandic conversion is one of the most remarkable in religious history because it was accomplished by a single act of constitutional procedure.

By the 990s, Iceland was split between an increasingly large Christian minority (converts from voyages abroad, contacts with Norway, missionaries sent by Olaf Tryggvason) and a still-dominant pagan majority. The two factions were on the edge of civil war. Pagan and Christian Icelanders refused to honor each other's legal proceedings. Marriages were collapsing along religious lines. Olaf Tryggvason was holding Icelandic hostages at his court in Norway and threatening to refuse trade to a non-Christian Iceland.

In the summer of 1000, the Althing met at Þingvellir under enormous pressure. The Lawspeaker that year was **Þorgeirr Ljósvetningagoði** (THOR-gay-ir LYOSS-vet-nings-go-thee, "Thorgeir the Priest of the People of Light-Lake"), a respected pagan chieftain. Þorgeirr was asked, by both sides, to render a judgment that would prevent the country from breaking apart. He withdrew under his fur cloak, lay down on the grass, and refused to speak or move for a day and a night. (No one knows what he was doing under the cloak. The traditional Christian interpretation is that he was praying or meditating. The traditional pagan interpretation is that he was performing **útiseta**, "outside-sitting," a Norse divinatory practice in which a person sat outside in silent communion with spirits to receive guidance. Modern scholars cannot say which it was.)

When Þorgeirr emerged from the cloak, he announced his ruling: Iceland would adopt Christianity as its public religion. All Icelanders would be baptized. Public pagan worship would cease. But — and this was the brilliance — private pagan practice would be tolerated. People could still worship the old gods at home, eat horse meat (a pagan ritual food banned by the church), and expose unwanted infants (a pagan custom that the church banned in principle). The compromise gave the Christian faction the public victory it needed and gave the pagan faction time to convert organically.

Iceland was therefore Christianized by parliamentary vote in 1000, brokered by a pagan Lawspeaker, with pragmatic concessions on both sides, with no executions, no battles, and no martyrs. The Icelandic conversion is the cleanest, most procedural, and least violent religious transformation of the Viking Age. It is also, not coincidentally, the conversion that most thoroughly preserved the cultural memory of the pagan past — Iceland would, three centuries later, produce the Eddas and the sagas, the documents in which Norse mythology survives. The country that converted gently kept its old stories. The countries that converted by sword forgot more.

---

**Sweden** held out longest. The Swedish royal kingdom did not formally Christianize until the early 11th century under **Olof Skötkonung** (995–1022), the first Swedish king to be baptized (around 1008). But pagan practice persisted at the great central temple at **Uppsala** for another seventy years, into the 1080s, when the temple was finally destroyed and replaced by a Christian church. The Swedes are a useful reminder that the Viking-Age conversion was not a single switch. It was a long, regional, generation-by-generation process, and pockets of organized paganism survived until well after the conventional 1066 end-date of the Viking Age.

The conversion mattered in concrete ways beyond the religious. **Christianity brought writing** — the Latin alphabet replaced runes for serious documentation, kings began to issue charters and laws, monasteries became centers of literacy, and Iceland in particular took to the new technology with extraordinary energy. Christianity brought **stone churches**, which were built on top of old pagan cult sites in a pattern repeated all across Scandinavia (the present-day cathedrals of Lund, Trondheim, and Uppsala all sit on or next to old pagan sacred sites). Christianity brought **diplomatic respectability**: a Christian Norse king could marry a Christian Frankish princess, sign treaties with Christian kingdoms as a recognized political peer, and integrate his kingdom into the broader European political order. The conversion was, in part, a process of accreditation. To be Christian was to be admitted to the diplomatic club of Latin Christendom. The Norse were tired of being outside it.

Cross-civ check: the great Norse conversion window (~960–1030) overlaps with the **Cluniac monastic reform** movement in Latin Christendom (the great spiritual renewal centered on Cluny in Burgundy that would shape European church politics for two centuries), the **Buyid** rule of Baghdad (Persian Empire Ch 9), the **Song dynasty's** founding in China (960), the **Fatimid Caliphate** at the height of its power in Cairo, and the **Toltec** ascendancy in central Mexico. Iceland's conversion of 1000 happened in the same year that Leif Erikson reached Vinland and that the Hungarian king Stephen I was crowned a Christian king of Hungary by the Pope. The whole western edge of the steppe and Atlantic worlds was being absorbed into Latin Christendom in the same generation. The Vikings' century-long conversion was one front of a continental religious shift.

# Chapter 6 — Skalds, Sagas, Runes

The Vikings were the most prolific creators of vernacular written texts in early-medieval Europe.

That sentence is almost the opposite of what most people assume. The popular image is of a non-literate culture, an oral world of beardy men shouting at each other across mead-halls. The reality is that Viking-Age Scandinavia produced more written texts in the local language — runestones, runic inscriptions on small objects, runic graffiti, eddic and skaldic poetry preserved in later writing — than any other corner of Europe in the same period. Latin Christendom in 900 wrote almost nothing in the vernacular; everything serious was in Latin. The Vikings, lacking Latin and lacking the political will to use it, wrote in their own language, in their own script, on their own surfaces. This chapter is about that body of text.

There are three traditions to cover: **runestones** (monumental writing on stone), **skaldic poetry** (formal court verse, oral, transmitted by memory through generations of professional poets), and **eddic poetry** (anonymous mythological and heroic verse, also oral, eventually written down in Iceland after the conversion). Each is a distinct technology of cultural memory. Together, they constitute one of the most distinctive vernacular literary traditions of the Middle Ages.

---

**Runes** were the writing system of the Norse and other Germanic peoples. The earliest runic inscriptions, in the **Elder Futhark** alphabet of 24 characters, date from around 150 CE — five centuries before the Viking Age proper. By the early Viking Age, the runic alphabet had been simplified and reformed into the **Younger Futhark** of 16 characters (the reform happened in stages around 700–800 CE). The Younger Futhark was an awkward writing system on purpose: 16 characters had to do the work of representing all the sounds of Old Norse, which meant some runes did double duty (the same rune for *p* and *b*, for example), and reading a runic inscription required context to disambiguate. The reform made the runes faster to carve but harder to read.

A runestone is a memorial monument: a standing stone, typically 5 to 10 feet (1.5–3 m) tall, carved with a runic inscription that commemorates a person — usually a male relative who has died abroad — and frequently mentions the journey he was on when he died. The classic runestone formula is: "X had this stone raised in memory of Y his [son/brother/comrade], who died in [foreign location]." Sweden has the largest single concentration of Viking-Age runestones — over 2,500 of them, the great majority erected between about 950 and 1100. They cluster in the Lake Mälaren region around modern Stockholm, the heartland of the Swedish commercial elite who sent their younger sons east to the Volga and to Constantinople.

The runestones are an extraordinary body of social-historical evidence. They tell us where Norsemen went (the inscriptions name **Garðaríki** — Russia; **Miklagarðr** — "the Great City," Constantinople; **Serkland** — the Caliphate; **Englaland** — England; **Langbarðaland** — the Lombard country, northern Italy), they tell us how they died (in battle, by drowning, by treachery), they tell us about family structures (women erecting stones for husbands, mothers for sons, brothers for brothers, sometimes joint stones for a whole family), and they tell us about the early reach of Christianity (later runestones often have crosses carved into them and end with prayers for the soul of the deceased).

The **Rök runestone** in Östergötland, Sweden, carved around 800, contains the longest known runic inscription — about 760 characters, on all five faces of the stone, in a mix of standard runes and several encrypted runic ciphers. The inscription was raised by a man named Varin in memory of his dead son Vaemod, and it references heroic legends, ancient kings, and what appears to be a riddle or cryptographic challenge. Modern scholars have argued for decades over the meaning. Recent interpretations have suggested that the inscription is responding to a climate catastrophe — possibly the **Late Antique Little Ice Age** of 536 onward — by invoking ancestral heroes against renewed climatic anxiety. Whatever its meaning, the Rök stone is the most ambitious piece of Viking-Age vernacular literature surviving on a non-perishable medium.

The **Jelling Stone** (Chapter 5) is the most politically loaded runestone — Harald Bluetooth's monument to his own kingship and Denmark's conversion, with its trilingual visual program of runic inscription, animal-style serpent-art, and Christian crucifixion imagery.

The **Berezan' runestone**, found on a small island at the mouth of the Dnieper in the Black Sea, was raised by a Swede named Grani in memory of his comrade Karl, who died on the way home from a Byzantine expedition. The Berezan' stone is, in geographic terms, the southernmost Viking-Age runestone yet found, and it nicely illustrates the eastern Norse reach: a Swedish memorial inscription, in Old Norse runes, on a Black Sea island within sight of the Anatolian coast.

The complete runestone corpus — about 6,000 inscriptions across Scandinavia and the Norse diaspora — is one of the largest bodies of vernacular epigraphy from any pre-modern European culture. The Vikings did not have parchment manuscripts. They had stones, and they made the stones speak.

---

**Skaldic poetry** (*skáldskapr*, SKAHLD-skahpr, "the craft of skalds") is the strangest, densest, and most technically demanding tradition of court poetry produced in any medieval European vernacular.

A **skald** was a professional court poet — usually Icelandic by the late Viking Age, since Iceland produced the great majority of skalds — attached to a king's or chieftain's hall. The skald composed praise-poems for the patron, commemorating his battles, his ancestry, his generosity, and his enemies. Skaldic poetry was paid; a successful skald received gold rings, weapons, ships, and land in exchange for verses. It was also performed, at feasts and assemblies, in front of the warband. A successful praise-poem was retold for generations and could establish or destroy a king's posthumous reputation. The political stakes were real.

What makes skaldic verse extraordinary is its formal density. The standard meter, **dróttkvætt** ("court-meter"), is one of the most metrically constrained poetic forms ever devised:

- Each stanza has eight lines
- Each line has six syllables
- Lines pair into couplets, with strict rules about which syllables alliterate with which (the alliteration patterns are mathematical)
- Internal rhymes within each line follow further strict rules (full-rhyme in even lines, half-rhyme in odd lines)
- The grammar is freely word-orderable to fit the meter, which means a skaldic stanza is often syntactically scrambled almost beyond recognition; understanding it requires unscrambling the word order

On top of this, the lexicon is dominated by **kennings** — compound metaphorical descriptors that substitute for ordinary words. A *kenning* is a two-element noun phrase that names something obliquely. Some examples:

- "the whale-road" — the sea
- "the swan-bath" — the sea
- "the storm of swords" — battle
- "the fire of arms" — gold
- "the feeder of ravens" — a warrior (because warriors leave corpses for ravens to eat)
- "Odin's mead" — poetry (from the myth in which Odin steals the mead of poetic inspiration from a giant)
- "the sky of the helmet" — the head
- "the icicle of blood" — a sword

Many kennings are nested: a sword is "the icicle of blood" or "the fire of arms," but in a kenning chain it might become "the icicle-of-blood storm" (battle), and a warrior in that battle might be "the feeder of the icicle-of-blood storm's swans" (the swans of battle being ravens, the feeder being the warrior). A complex skaldic stanza may contain six or seven kennings, all packed into 48 syllables, with the word order scrambled and the syntax distributed across multiple metrical clauses. Reading skaldic verse is a three-step process: unscramble the syntax, decode the kennings, identify what they refer to. A trained 11th-century skald could compose this on the spot in the patron's hall, and a trained audience could follow it.

Why this density? Two reasons. First, the formal difficulty was the point — skaldic verse was an explicit display of intellectual mastery, a way for a poet to demonstrate that he had spent years memorizing the Norse mythological corpus (because every kenning depended on the listener knowing the relevant myth) and the technical rules. A bad skald couldn't compose a single stanza. A good skald could improvise. Second, the density made memorization easier in a particular way: the alliteration and internal rhyme constraints were so rigid that a memorized stanza could only be reconstructed in one form, which meant the verses could be transmitted orally across generations without textual corruption. Skaldic poems composed in the 9th century were still being recited in the same wording in the 13th century, and the metrical lock made it possible. The form is the preservation technology.

The most famous skald — the one whose life and work the Icelandic sagas would later celebrate — was **Egill Skallagrímsson** (EH-gil skahl-ah-GREEMS-son, c. 910–990), a 10th-century Icelander who was simultaneously a fearsome warrior, a savage berserker, an awkward man, a tender father, a devoted son, and the greatest poetic genius of his generation. Egill killed his first man at age seven (over a children's ball game, according to his saga). He raided across the Baltic and England. He composed a legendary praise-poem called **Höfuðlausn** ("Head-Ransom") for his enemy King Erik Bloodaxe of York in a single night to save his own life — Egill had been captured by Erik, was due to be executed in the morning, and bought his head back with one of the great poems in the language. He also composed **Sonatorrek** ("On the Loss of My Sons"), a long lyric lament after the deaths of two of his sons, including drowning. Sonatorrek is one of the most personally moving poems in early-medieval European literature. *Egil's Saga*, written by Snorri Sturluson around 1230 from oral tradition and surviving Egill verses, is one of the masterpieces of the saga tradition. Egill is the proof of concept of the Norse poet-warrior: the same hands that swung the axe wrote the lyrics.

---

The **eddic poems** are different in character: anonymous, mythological, narrative, in much simpler meters than skaldic verse, transmitted orally and finally written down in Iceland in the late Viking and post-Viking period.

The Codex Regius (Chapter 5) preserves about 30 eddic poems, covering Norse mythology and heroic legend. The major mythological poems include:

- **Völuspá** ("The Prophecy of the Seeress"), the great cosmological poem in which a dead seeress, summoned by Odin, recites the entire history of the cosmos from creation to Ragnarök and the new world afterward. Völuspá is the single best literary source for Norse cosmology.
- **Hávamál** ("Sayings of the High One"), Odin's collected wisdom — a long string of proverbs, runic lore, courtship advice, and reflections on mortality, including the section in which Odin describes his self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil to learn the runes.
- **Vafþrúðnismál** ("Sayings of Vafthruthnir"), a wisdom-contest between Odin and a giant.
- **Þrymskviða** ("The Lay of Thrym"), the comic-mythological story of how Thor recovered his hammer from the giants by dressing up as the goddess Freyja and going through with a fake wedding.
- **Lokasenna** ("Loki's Quarrel"), in which Loki crashes a feast of the gods and insults each of them in turn, revealing scandals and weaknesses.

The heroic poems of the Codex Regius retell stories from the broader Germanic legendary tradition — the Volsungs, Sigurd the dragon-slayer, Brynhild the valkyrie, Atli (Attila the Hun) — and these stories would be reworked by other Germanic-speaking peoples into the German Nibelungenlied and ultimately, twelve centuries later, into Wagner's Ring Cycle. The Norse versions are older and stranger and contain the rawer version of the cycle.

The eddic poems are the foundation document of Norse mythology. Without them, we would know the Norse gods only from outsider sources (Adam of Bremen and the like) and from kennings in skaldic verse. The Codex Regius is the reason we can talk about Yggdrasil and the Nine Worlds and Ragnarök at all.

---

The third great Norse literary tradition — the **Icelandic family sagas** — is technically post-Viking-Age, written in the 12th and 13th centuries (after the conversion, after the Latin alphabet, after the establishment of monastic scriptoria in Iceland), but its subject is the Viking Age proper, and its authors were drawing on living oral tradition that ran continuously from the Settlement period to their own day.

The Icelandic family sagas are anonymous prose narratives — typically novel-length, in cool, terse, dialogue-heavy realist prose — that recount the lives of Icelandic chieftain families across the 9th, 10th, and 11th centuries. The most famous include *Egil's Saga* (about Egill Skallagrímsson, mentioned above), *Njál's Saga* (a sweeping multigenerational epic of feud and burning), *Laxdæla Saga* (a tragic love-and-feud story centered on a powerful matriarch, Gudrun Osvifsdottir), *The Saga of the Volsungs*, and the so-called *Vinland Sagas* — *The Saga of the Greenlanders* and *The Saga of Erik the Red* — which together describe the Norse voyages to Vinland that we covered in Chapter 3.

The sagas are extraordinary literature. They are written in a calm, distancing, almost cinematic prose that lets the reader judge character through action rather than authorial commentary. They are interested in the pressure-points of small communities: feuds that smolder for decades, marriages that fail across multigenerational fault lines, the way an oath made by a grandfather binds a grandson into a killing he doesn't want to do. They contain dialogue that feels modern. They contain character drawing of a sophistication that Western European literature would not match for another four centuries — the next time you find anything like the psychological realism of *Njál's Saga* in European prose, you are in 17th-century France.

Are they reliable history? Partially. The sagas were written 200 to 350 years after the events they describe, by Christian authors working in a literate court culture, drawing on long oral tradition that had certainly been reshaped along the way. The skeleton of named characters, family lines, and major events is largely confirmed by other sources (the runestones, the Landnámabók, foreign chronicles). The dialogue, the small-scale politics, and the precise sequences of events are partly oral memory and partly artful reconstruction. Modern scholars treat the sagas as a unique blend of remembered tradition, family lore, written record, and outright literary invention — useful, beautiful, but never to be cited as straightforward fact. They are the medieval Norse equivalent of historical novels: full of real people and real places, written about a period the author never lived through, and impossible to fully separate from the author's imagination.

The body of saga literature is enormous — approximately 40 family sagas, dozens of legendary sagas, the great kings' sagas (Snorri's *Heimskringla* — the saga of the Norwegian kings — runs to over 800 pages), and a series of bishops' sagas, contemporary sagas, and shorter tales. By volume of vernacular prose produced relative to population, Iceland in 1200 outproduced any European country; the only comparable medieval vernacular literary corpus is the Arabic literary explosion of the same period.

---

So: the Vikings wrote, and what they wrote was distinctive. Runestones for monumental memorialization. Skaldic verse for political praise and personal display. Eddic poetry for the mythological canon. Sagas (later) for family memory and historical reconstruction. The whole package is the literature of a culture that took oral tradition seriously as a technology — that built memorization aids into its meter, that used public stones as memorials when manuscripts were unavailable, and that, when manuscripts finally became available, produced a vernacular prose literature that the rest of medieval Europe would not match for centuries.

The popular image of the Viking as a non-literate brute is exactly backwards. The Viking world was built around words. The words just weren't in Latin.

Cross-civ check: the body of Norse vernacular literature (~800–1300) overlaps with the **Tang and Song Chinese poetic traditions** (the great age of Chinese lyric, when Li Bai and Du Fu and Su Shi are writing); the **classical Persian poetry** of Ferdowsi (whose Shahnameh, completed around 1010, is roughly contemporary with the Eddic poems' likely composition); the **Arabic poetic golden age** of the Abbasid court; and the late **Sanskrit court poetry** of medieval India. The Norse tradition's distinctiveness is not its existence — every literate culture had court poetry — but its medium (runic stone, oral memorization rather than parchment) and its formal density (skaldic dróttkvætt is in a class of its own).

# Chapter 7 — The North Sea Empire and the Long Reach

In **December 1013**, the Anglo-Saxon king of England, **Æthelred II** (often nicknamed "the Unready" — actually a mistranslation; the Old English *unræd* means "ill-counseled" or "no-counsel," a pun on his name *Æþelræd*, "noble counsel"), fled to Normandy with his wife Emma and their children. The kingdom he was abandoning had just been conquered by the Danish king **Sweyn Forkbeard**.

Three years later, in **1016**, Sweyn's son **Cnut** (the Anglicized form of Old Norse *Knútr*, KNOOT, often written **Canute** in older English) finalized the conquest, defeating the brief revival of Anglo-Saxon resistance under Æthelred's son Edmund Ironside. Cnut would rule England until 1035. He would also, simultaneously, rule Denmark (from his father's inheritance) and Norway (after he conquered it from Olaf Haraldsson in 1028). For a period of roughly seven years — 1028 to 1035 — Cnut was king of England, Denmark, and Norway at once, the ruler of the **North Sea Empire**, the most extensive single political unit ever to have a Norse-speaking ruler. He was not just a great Viking. He was, briefly, an emperor.

How did the Viking Age get from a 793 monastery raid to a 1016 multi-kingdom monarchy? Through about a hundred years of consolidating Scandinavian state formation, escalating Danegeld payments out of England, and a Norse military-economic complex that was both more sophisticated and more brutal than the early raiding period.

---

To get to 1016 we have to back up and walk through the century in order. The chapter follows that order: first the trading towns that made the Norse rich (the 800s), then the new Scandinavian states that organized them (the late 900s), then the second great wave of attacks on England (the 990s and 1000s), then Cnut's North Sea Empire (1016–1035). The thread running through all four scenes is the same — silver, slaves, and the gradual replacement of warband chieftains by territorial kings.

---

**Scene one: the trading towns, 800s onward.**

Long before Cnut, the economic muscle of Viking-Age Scandinavia was concentrated in a small number of trading towns that formed the commercial backbone of the entire diaspora. Three of these matter most.

**Hedeby** (HAY-deh-bee, also called *Haithabu*) was the largest. Located at the southern end of the Jutland peninsula at the head of the Schlei fjord, in what is now the German state of Schleswig-Holstein, Hedeby sat at the geographic chokepoint where North Sea trade met Baltic trade. The Danish king Godfred relocated merchants there from the trading town of Reric in 808 to consolidate Danish commercial control of the region; the move is recorded in the Frankish royal annals. Hedeby grew within a century into a town of perhaps 1,500 to 2,000 permanent residents (a large town by 9th-century European standards), with a defensive **semicircular rampart**, a network of plank-paved streets, hundreds of pit-houses and craft workshops, and a harbor ringed with quays. Hedeby produced glass beads, comb-makers' wares, iron tools, leather goods, and silver ornaments at a quasi-industrial scale, and it brokered exchange between the Frankish-Carolingian commercial sphere, the Norse Baltic, and (via the Russian rivers) the Islamic world. The Arab traveler **At-Tartushi** — a 10th-century Andalusian merchant whose surviving travelogue is one of the few outside descriptions of a Norse town — visited Hedeby in the 960s and left a horrified account: a noisy pagan town where wives could initiate divorce, women wore their eyes painted with kohl, and the inhabitants held a great seasonal feast in honor of Sirius (probably the autumn blót) at which they sacrificed cattle, dogs, and the occasional human being to honor their gods. He thought the town was awful. He also thought it was unprecedentedly large for a Northern European settlement.

**Birka** in central Sweden, founded around 750 on a small island in Lake Mälaren about 18 miles (30 km) west of modern Stockholm, was the eastern counterpart to Hedeby. Birka sat where the Baltic met the inland Swedish lake system and the headwaters of the trade routes east. It handled Slavic, Frisian, Arab, Anglo-Saxon, and Norse goods. Excavations of Birka cemeteries have produced thousands of graves containing Abbasid silver, Byzantine silks, Frankish swords, Anglo-Saxon coins, and Slavic pottery — a single woman's grave may contain artifacts from four distinct cultural zones thousands of miles apart. Birka was where the Volga route emptied into the Baltic. Its decline began around 970, probably because of shifting trade routes after the collapse of the **Khazar Khaganate** — a Turkic-Jewish steppe kingdom on the lower Volga that had been the gatekeeper of the Volga trade route for centuries. The Rus' prince Svyatoslav destroyed Khazaria in 968, after which the Volga route became less stable and the silver dirham flow began to dry up. The end of the Khazars killed Birka. The Swedish trading center moved to **Sigtuna**, a more inland, more Christian site, around 980.

**Dublin** (Old Norse *Dyflinn*) was the largest western Norse town and the linchpin of the Norse-Irish slave economy. By 950 it had a population of perhaps 5,000 — making it one of the largest cities in Atlantic Europe — surrounded by an earthen rampart, with a planned street grid, a working harbor, and a permanent slave market. The economic rationale of Dublin was specific and grim: Norse warbands raided Irish, Scottish, English, and Welsh coasts and brought captives to the Dublin market. Slaves were sold to Norse-Icelandic colonists (who needed labor for their farms), to Norse-Irish dynasties (who used them domestically and on land), to Continental European buyers, and — in the largest numbers — to merchants going east to feed the Islamic and Byzantine slave markets. The Dublin slave trade ran for nearly two centuries, from the 840s through the 1100s, and it was central to Viking Age economics. **Dublin Slave Market** had become a bottleneck institution: Norse fleets concentrated their captives there, Norse buyers arrived there, Continental and Mediterranean traders came there, and a single price-setting mechanism formed for human beings across the entire Norse commercial zone. This is not the Vikings as romantic seafarers. This is the Vikings as the operators of one of the most efficient slave markets in early-medieval Europe.

A note on the slave economy that the cartoon-Viking image leaves out: a substantial fraction — by some recent estimates one quarter to one third — of the founding population of Iceland was descended from female slaves taken in raids on Britain and Ireland. Genetic studies of modern Icelanders have found that about 60% of their female-line mitochondrial DNA traces back to women from the British Isles, while about 75% of their male-line Y-chromosome DNA traces back to Norway. The implication is straightforward and brutal: the Norse men who settled Iceland brought with them, in large numbers, women who had not chosen to come. The whole demography of Iceland is a population built on a slave-taking economy. The same dynamic operated, on smaller scales, throughout the Norse Atlantic. The rings of gold buried in chieftains' graves across the Viking world were, in part, monetized human beings.

The Dublin slave economy also functioned as the Atlantic mirror of an older Mediterranean pattern. The Phoenician city of Carthage had run Mediterranean slave hubs from about 800 BCE onward (Phoenicia Ch 7); a thousand years later, Dublin became the equivalent maritime entrepôt for the northern waters, fed by raid networks and feeding the Islamic and Byzantine markets via the Volga route. Different sea, different century, same business model: a fortified port with a harbor, a market, a price-setting authority, and a hinterland of raiders.

---

**Scene two: state formation, 950–980.**

By the second half of the 10th century, the wealth flowing back from the trading towns and the raids was hitting Scandinavia hard enough to reshape its politics. State formation was driven by three overlapping forces: the wealth coming back from raids and trade, the military pressure of unified neighbors (the Holy Roman Empire to the south, the Anglo-Saxon kingdom to the west, the Rus' to the east), and the unifying institutional infrastructure of Christianity (Chapter 5). Around 950–980, three large new royal projects took form.

In **Denmark**, **Harald Bluetooth** consolidated central royal authority not just by accepting Christianity (Chapter 5), but by undertaking some of the most ambitious public-works projects in Viking-Age Scandinavia. Around 975–980 he ordered the construction of a network of identical **ring fortresses** across Denmark — at **Trelleborg**, **Fyrkat**, **Aggersborg**, **Nonnebakken**, and **Borgring** — each a perfectly circular ramparted enclosure laid out with breathtaking geometric precision: identical diameters (ranging from 460 to 790 feet, 140 to 240 m), identical four-quadrant interiors with identical longhouse barracks arranged in symmetric squares, identical four-gate cardinal-direction entrances, identical proportional ratios between rampart, ditch, and inner courtyard. The ring fortresses were laid out by people who shared a single architectural plan and the precise instruments to execute it. They are unique in early-medieval Europe: nobody else was building anything like them. Whether they were primarily royal garrison strongpoints, military training centers for the conscript-based Danish royal levy, or central-place administrative nodes is debated. What is not debated is what they signal: a Danish kingdom capable of organized statecraft on a scale no previous Norse polity had achieved. Denmark in 980 was the first Scandinavian country with a real state. The ring fortresses are its concrete signature.

In **Norway**, the state-formation work begun by Harald Fairhair (Chapter 3) was completed, in fits and starts, by the two Saint Olafs, by Magnus the Good (1035–1047), and by Harald Hardrada (1046–1066). By the mid-11th century Norway had a unified crown, a Christian church, a developing cathedral system, and increasingly written laws. It was no longer a patchwork of fjord-kingdoms.

In **Sweden**, the consolidation was slowest. The royal authority around the great cult site at Uppsala remained nominally pagan, regionally limited, and informal in structure until the late 11th century. Sweden was the last Scandinavian country to fully Christianize, the last to develop a centralized monarchy, and the last to produce written laws.

---

**Scene three: the second wave on England, 980s–1016.**

The new Scandinavian states were better-organized than their predecessors, and what they organized first was a renewed assault on England. The second great wave of attacks, beginning in the 980s, produced the largest sustained tribute extraction in early-medieval European history.

By the time **Æthelred II** came to the English throne in 978 (at age 12, after his half-brother Edward had been murdered, probably with the connivance of Æthelred's mother — a fact that haunted Æthelred's reign), the political situation had shifted. Norse Scandinavia was no longer a patchwork of warbands. It was producing organized royal armies funded and led by kings like Sweyn Forkbeard. The English defenses, last seriously tested under Alfred a century earlier, had eroded. The Anglo-Saxon **fyrd** (the regional militia levy) was slow to assemble and unevenly equipped. The new Viking armies arriving in the 980s were faster, better-equipped, and operating with state backing.

The pattern repeated through the 990s and 1000s: a Danish or Norwegian fleet would arrive, raid coastal districts, defeat any local English force that came against it, and then accept a large **Danegeld** payment to leave. The first major payment was 10,000 pounds (about 3,600 kg) of silver in 991, after the Vikings won the **Battle of Maldon** on the Essex coast — a battle commemorated in one of the great surviving Old English poems, *The Battle of Maldon*, which celebrated the heroic doomed defense of the English ealdorman Byrhtnoth (he died on the battlefield, his bodyguards refused to flee, and the poem makes their last stand into a meditation on loyalty: "Heart shall be the harder, mind the keener, courage the more, as our strength lessens"). The Danegeld payments escalated. 16,000 pounds in 994. 24,000 pounds in 1002. 36,000 pounds in 1007. 48,000 pounds in 1012. By the end of Æthelred's reign, the English crown had paid out perhaps 250,000 pounds (90,000 kg) of silver to Viking armies — far more than the entire English royal annual revenue. The economic damage was crushing. The political damage was worse: every Danegeld payment confirmed that the English crown could not defend its own kingdom and that its subjects were paying a state tax to be transferred to its enemies.

This was, in mechanism, an old story playing out on a new sea. Eleven hundred years earlier and four thousand miles to the east, the **Han dynasty** in China had faced exactly the same problem with the steppe nomads of the Xiongnu confederation (Xiongnu-Huns Ch 3). After Han forces were humiliated at Baideng in 200 BCE, China settled into a system the Chinese called **heqin** — "harmonious kinship" — under which thousands of rolls of silk, large quantities of grain and gold, and an imperial princess every generation were sent north as tribute, in exchange for the chanyu's promise not to raid. The Han paid heqin to the Xiongnu for nearly seven decades. The English paid Danegeld to the Vikings for about three. The settled-state arithmetic was identical: a wealthy agricultural kingdom, a fast-moving military culture that could appear anywhere along an enormous frontier, and a calculation that buying off the raiders was cheaper than fortifying every coast or border. In both cases the calculation was wrong in the long run — heqin failed to stop Xiongnu raids, Danegeld did not stop Sweyn — and both societies eventually had to choose between conquest and being conquered. The English chose poorly.

The most infamous moment of Æthelred's reign was the **St. Brice's Day Massacre** on **November 13, 1002**. Æthelred, infuriated by the ongoing Danish raids and apparently fearing a fifth-column rising by the Danish settler population in eastern England, ordered the killing of all Danes within his kingdom on St. Brice's day. The chronicles report that the order was executed in some places with appalling thoroughness — at Oxford, Danes who took refuge in St. Frideswide's church were burned alive in it. The number of dead is unknowable; estimates range from a few hundred to many thousands, and the order was probably enforced unevenly across England (in the Danelaw itself, where the Danish-descended population was the majority, it was largely ignored; in Wessex and the south, where Danes were a vulnerable minority, it appears to have been carried out). One of the people killed in the massacre was **Gunhilde**, the sister of **Sweyn Forkbeard** of Denmark.

This was the trigger. Sweyn launched a sustained war of conquest against England that ran from 1003 to his death in early 1014. Æthelred fled to Normandy in 1013 (where his marriage to Emma of Normandy, sister of the Norman duke, proved consequential — that connection was the legal basis for William of Normandy's later claim to the English throne, Chapter 8). Sweyn died unexpectedly in February 1014, just weeks after being acclaimed king of England, and Æthelred briefly returned, but the situation was hopeless. Sweyn's son **Cnut**, then about 19, finalized the conquest in 1016 after a year-long civil war against Æthelred's son Edmund Ironside (so nicknamed for his armored ferocity). Edmund was killed (or possibly murdered) in November 1016, and Cnut became king of all England.

---

**Scene four: Cnut and the North Sea Empire, 1016–1035.**

Cnut, ruling England from 1016 and Denmark from 1018 (after the death of his elder brother Harald), was a different kind of Viking king. He was barely a Viking at all in the raiding sense. He was a Christian (he had been baptized as a youth), he was politically sophisticated (he kept many of the existing English administrative structures, retained many Anglo-Saxon nobles in positions of power, and married Æthelred's widow Emma of Normandy as a matter of statecraft, neutralizing both her Norman family and her sons by Æthelred), and he was a serious participant in mainland European politics. He attended the imperial coronation of the Holy Roman Emperor Conrad II in Rome in 1027 and walked away with formal acknowledgments of Danish royal status. He minted his own coins in England with a sophisticated minting infrastructure inherited from the Anglo-Saxon state. He governed England through a system of **earldoms** — large regional governorships — that consolidated previous arrangements. He was, in short, a competent king of a Christian kingdom, not a free-ranging raider.

In 1028, Cnut conquered Norway from Olaf Haraldsson (Chapter 5), adding the Norwegian crown to his portfolio. At this point — for about seven years, 1028 to 1035 — he was simultaneously king of England, Denmark, and Norway, plus suzerain of parts of Sweden and Pomerania. The **North Sea Empire** was a real political entity, with Cnut as its central ruler, governed by a system of earls, regional kings, and personal loyalty oaths. It was the largest state ever built around a Norse-speaking core.

It also did not last. Cnut died in 1035 at age 40. His sons quarreled. His Danish-English-Norwegian inheritance fragmented within seven years of his death. By 1042, Edward the Confessor — Æthelred's son by Emma — had reclaimed the English crown. By the same year, the Danish and Norwegian crowns had passed to other dynasties. The North Sea Empire was a single-generation construction. Its political infrastructure had not had time to outlast its founder. But the precedent — that a single Norse-derived ruler could govern all the major Scandinavian and English territories at once — was a permanent fixture of European political imagination. It was the closest thing Northern Europe had to a counterpart of the Carolingian Empire, and it was built by the great-grandson of a Viking pirate.

---

There is one more thread to pull before the final crisis of 1066. In Norway, Cnut's Norwegian conquest was reversed by Olaf Haraldsson's son **Magnus the Good** (1035–1047), who succeeded Olaf as king of Norway. Magnus shortly thereafter inherited the Danish throne in 1042 from **Harthacnut** (Cnut's son by Emma of Normandy and the last king of Cnut's English-Danish line, who died childless that year). For a brief moment Norway and Denmark were united again under Magnus, and Magnus was preparing to invade England to claim Cnut's old crown when he died in 1047 — possibly from a riding accident, possibly poisoned. Magnus was succeeded in Norway by his uncle, who had recently returned from the Varangian Guard in Constantinople with an enormous fortune, military experience that made him the most feared commander in Northern Europe, and a new nickname earned in his foreign service: **Hardrada**, "Hard-Counsel."

This was the man who would, twenty years later, sail to Yorkshire to claim a kingdom and die under a hail of Saxon arrows on the bridge at Stamford.

Cross-civ check: the consolidation phase of Viking-Age Scandinavia (~960–1066) coincides with the **Song dynasty** stabilizing China after the Five Dynasties period (Song China would become the wealthiest single state on earth by 1100), the early **Seljuk Turk** expansion across Persia and Anatolia (Persian Empire Ch 9), the **Fatimid Caliphate** at the height of its Egyptian power, the **Western Chalukya** dynasty in southern India, the Cluniac monastic reform shaping Latin Christendom, and the early stages of the medieval European agricultural and demographic boom that would sustain the high Middle Ages. Cnut's North Sea Empire was a peer of the Christian Holy Roman Empire and a player in the wider European order. It was also an outlier — there would not be a comparable Northern European maritime empire under a Scandinavian ruler again until the Danish crowns of the late medieval period, and even those would be different in character.

---

# Chapter 8 — The Year of Three Kings

In **January 1066**, the English king **Edward the Confessor** (the son of Æthelred II and Emma of Normandy, restored to the English throne in 1042 after the dissolution of Cnut's North Sea Empire) lay dying in Westminster, the new abbey he had built on a marshy island upriver from the City of London. He had no children. He had no obvious heir. He had been a deeply religious, politically ineffective, and possibly celibate king who left the practical governance of his realm to a network of competing earls, the most powerful of whom — **Harold Godwinson**, Earl of Wessex — was the brother of Edward's queen Edith and was, by 1066, the de facto regent of England.

When Edward died on **January 5, 1066**, the **Witenagemot** (WITT-en-uh-geh-moht, the council of leading English nobles and bishops, the closest Anglo-Saxon institutional equivalent of a royal council) elected Harold Godwinson king of England the next day. He was crowned at Westminster on January 6, the day Edward was buried. The choice was contested before the crown was even on Harold's head.

There were three live claimants to the English throne in January 1066:

**Harold Godwinson** himself, the new king, who had been chosen by the Witenagemot, was personally formidable, was the senior earl of the kingdom, and had been Edward's right-hand man for the previous decade.

**Harald Hardrada** of Norway, the former Varangian Guard commander, the most experienced military leader in Northern Europe, who based his claim on a tortuous chain of inheritance from the deceased Magnus the Good (who had been promised the English throne, in some accounts, by Harthacnut, the last king of Cnut's English line). The claim was thin in legal terms. Harald did not care; he had a fleet and an army, and he intended to take what he wanted.

**William, Duke of Normandy** (also called William the Bastard, and in the future William the Conqueror), great-grandson of the Viking **Rollo** — a Norse warlord granted the territory of Normandy by the Frankish king Charles the Simple in **911** in exchange for converting and defending the lower Seine. Rollo's descendants had governed Normandy ever since, gradually assimilating into French language and culture while retaining Scandinavian dynastic memory. William claimed that Edward had promised him the English throne during a 1051 visit, and that Harold Godwinson had personally sworn an oath in 1064 — under unusual and possibly coerced circumstances — to support William's claim. The Norman version of this oath, depicted later in the Bayeux Tapestry, shows Harold swearing on a chest of holy relics. The English version of events is silent or actively contradicts the Norman one. Whatever the truth about whose claim was strongest in legal terms, William's reaction to Harold's coronation was unambiguous: Harold was an oath-breaker and a usurper, and William would take England by conquest if he had to.

**Three men, three armies, one crown.** The year that followed was the climax of the Viking Age and the moment when it ended.

---

The first to move was Harald Hardrada.

In late summer 1066, Harald assembled a fleet of about 300 ships and roughly 9,000 men at the **Solund Islands** off the western coast of Norway. He was joined by **Tostig Godwinson** — Harold Godwinson's exiled younger brother, who had been Earl of Northumbria until his subjects revolted against his harsh rule in 1065, who had been expelled from England, and who had spent the past year in Flanders and Scotland looking for foreign backing to restore him. Tostig had visited Hardrada earlier in 1066 to propose a joint invasion. The two men sailed together for England in early September.

The invasion fleet rounded Scotland, raided the northeast English coast, and landed in the **Humber** estuary around September 18. Hardrada and Tostig advanced inland, defeated a hastily assembled English northern army at the **Battle of Fulford** on September 20 (just outside York, where the Earls Edwin of Mercia and Morcar of Northumbria — Harold Godwinson's brothers-in-law — were routed and probably suffered serious casualties), and accepted the surrender of York on September 24. Hardrada's army was now in possession of the second-largest city in England and the political center of the old Viking Kingdom of York.

Harold Godwinson was in the south of England, watching the Channel for William's expected invasion.

The news from York reached him around September 21. He moved fast. He gathered his housecarls (his elite household troops) and the thanes of southern England, and force-marched north along the old Roman road. The march was a logistical wonder of the period: roughly 200 miles (320 km) in five days, an average of 40 miles per day, possible only because much of the army was mounted and the road network through the East Midlands was Roman-built and well-maintained. By September 24 — the same day York surrendered — Harold Godwinson and his force were entering Yorkshire. He covered the last 8 miles to the village of **Stamford Bridge** on the morning of September 25.

Hardrada and Tostig had pulled most of their force back from York to a meeting point at Stamford Bridge, where they expected to receive hostages and tribute from the surrounding shires under the terms of York's surrender. They were not expecting an army. According to the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle and the much later (and more dramatic) Norse account in *Heimskringla*, many of the Norse troops had left their armor on the ships at Riccall, eight miles away. It was a hot late-September day. They were resting on the bank of the River Derwent. They saw, in the distance, what looked like a glittering sheet of moving metal — the English army marching up the road in full armor.

The **Battle of Stamford Bridge** on September 25, 1066, is one of the great forgotten battles of European history because it would be eclipsed three weeks later by the more famous battle that decided the kingship. But Stamford Bridge is, in its own way, the more telling of the two. The English caught the Norse half-armored and divided. There is a famous saga story of a single Norse warrior holding the wooden bridge over the Derwent against the entire English army for some time, killing forty Englishmen with an axe before being killed himself by an English soldier who floated under the bridge in a half-barrel and stabbed him from below. Whether this story is true (it appears only in the later saga sources, not in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle) the bridge was eventually crossed. The main battle on the eastern bank was a long, brutal, full-scale fight. Harald Hardrada took an arrow through the throat early in the battle and died. Tostig Godwinson was killed soon afterward. The Norse army was annihilated. Of the 300 ships that had come into the Humber, only about 24 were needed to carry the survivors home. The Norwegian invasion of England was finished. Harald Hardrada's body was buried in York, near the cathedral, in unconsecrated ground because of his pagan past — the Christian crusader-king of Norway, a Varangian Guard veteran of Constantinople, dead under a hail of English arrows in a Yorkshire pasture.

Stamford Bridge, **September 25, 1066**, is the battle conventionally dated as the end of the Viking Age. The reasoning is not that it ended Norse activity — Norse polities would continue, Norse kings would rule, Norse colonies would persist for another four centuries — but that it ended the structural pattern of the Age: large Scandinavian armies launching seaborne invasions of major European kingdoms in pursuit of conquest. Hardrada was the last great Viking conqueror of the classical model, and at Stamford Bridge he died a classical Viking death: spear, arrow, axe, in armored ranks on a meadow. After Stamford Bridge, the Scandinavian kingdoms turned inward; the new model of European political competition was Norman feudal warfare, not Norse longship raiding. The technology and the social structure of the longship-warband were obsolete.

But that judgment is in retrospect. From the perspective of Harold Godwinson, lying in his battle camp in Yorkshire on the night of September 25, the Viking threat had just been crushed. He had won. He could go home.

Then a rider came from the south.

---

Three days earlier, on **September 22**, a tide change at the mouth of the Somme had finally favored a Norman invasion fleet that had been waiting weeks for the right wind. **William of Normandy** had crossed the Channel with about 700 ships, 7,000 men, and 2,000 horses (a Norman cavalry-heavy army, a fundamentally different kind of force from anything the Vikings had ever fielded). William's fleet had landed at **Pevensey** on the Sussex coast on September 28, three days after Stamford Bridge. The Norman army had immediately moved up the coast to **Hastings**, where William fortified a base camp and began ravaging the surrounding countryside — burning villages, slaughtering livestock — to draw Harold south.

The strategy worked. Harold Godwinson, two weeks after one of the great victories in English military history, had to march south over 250 miles (400 km) to face a fresh Norman army. His own army was exhausted. His housecarls had taken serious casualties at Stamford Bridge. The southern fyrd had partially demobilized. Many of his troops were not arriving in time. But Harold had to act, because Norman raiding was destroying the southern English economy and the longer he waited, the worse the damage would be. He left London around October 11 with whatever forces he could assemble and met William's army at a ridge called **Senlac Hill** about 7 miles (11 km) inland from Hastings on the morning of **October 14, 1066**.

The **Battle of Hastings** has been described in detail in dozens of sources — the Bayeux Tapestry, the *Carmen de Hastingae Proelio* (a Latin verse account written within a year or two of the battle, probably by a Norman bishop), William of Poitiers' biography of the Conqueror (William of Poitiers was a Norman cleric who had been in William's household and wrote his *Gesta Guillelmi* around 1071–1077, the closest thing to an authorized Norman version), the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, and the Latin chronicle of John of Worcester (a 12th-century English monk who compiled an annalistic history drawing on lost earlier sources, generally read as the most balanced of the surviving accounts). The basic shape: English infantry holding a shield wall on the ridge, Norman cavalry repeatedly charging up the slope, neither side gaining decisive advantage through the morning, the Norman infantry and archers wearing down the English shield wall through the afternoon, the death of Harold Godwinson late in the day (an arrow through the eye, in the Bayeux Tapestry's famous depiction; possibly a hacking sword wound, in some textual accounts; possibly both) followed by the collapse of English resistance. Harold's two younger brothers, **Gyrth** and **Leofwine**, also died on the field. By dusk on October 14, William of Normandy was the master of the battlefield.

The whole defending core of the English aristocratic class was dead in two battles three weeks apart. There was no replacement available. By Christmas Day 1066, William had been crowned king of England in Westminster Abbey, on the same spot where Harold had been crowned eleven months earlier. The Norman conquest of England was complete in formal terms; the consolidation of Norman rule across the country would take another five years of vicious provincial campaigns. But England's Anglo-Saxon political class was effectively destroyed at Hastings, and over the next generation the Norman ruling class — speaking Norman French, building stone castles, holding land in feudal tenure — would entirely replace the Anglo-Saxon thane class as the governing stratum of English society. This is the conquest that brings about three quarters of the French vocabulary in modern English. It is also the conquest that ends the long Anglo-Saxon era and begins the medieval English kingdom.

The peculiar and wonderful irony is that William himself was a Viking. Not in name or language, but in blood. He was the great-great-great-grandson of Rollo — a Norse warlord who had taken Normandy from a Frankish king in 911, three generations before Cnut had taken England, ten generations before William's own reign. The Norman aristocracy who would, after 1066, rule England were the assimilated descendants of Vikings who had settled the lower Seine, learned French, become Christians, adopted continental cavalry warfare, and reinvented themselves as a French knightly nobility while retaining a residual Norse dynastic identity. The conquest that ended the Viking Age in England was led by a man whose ancestors had begun it. The Viking world had folded in on itself.

---

**1066** is the conventional end-date of the Viking Age. It is a usefully clean date, and the events of that year do mark a real structural shift. But it is worth taking a moment to look at what continued.

**Iceland** continued. The Icelandic Free State persisted for another 196 years after Stamford Bridge — through the great age of saga writing in the 12th and 13th centuries, until the Sturlung-era civil wars drove the country into Norwegian royal hands in 1262. The Norse colony in **Greenland** continued, perhaps for another 400 years; the lights only went out around 1450. The Norse-Irish kingdoms of **Dublin** and the Irish-Sea world continued; Magnus Barefoot of Norway would mount the last serious Norse military expedition into the Irish Sea zone in 1098–1103, dying in an Irish ambush on Ulster's coast.

**The Rus'** continued — and in fact reached a peak under **Yaroslav the Wise** (1019–1054), who codified the **Russkaya Pravda** (the foundational law code of medieval Russia, modeled in part on Scandinavian and Byzantine legal traditions), built the Saint Sophia Cathedral in Kiev (modeled on Hagia Sophia in Constantinople), and forged dynastic alliances across Europe. Yaroslav's daughters married the kings of France, Hungary, and Norway (Anne of Kiev became Queen of France; her signature in Old Cyrillic is still preserved on French royal documents). The Rurikid dynasty would govern Russia until 1610. Modern Russia is, in a real and unbroken sense, a downstream descendant of the eastern Norse expansion of the 9th century.

**The Varangian Guard** continued, though its character changed. After Stamford Bridge, displaced Anglo-Saxon nobles fled east across the Channel, across Europe, and into Constantinople, where they joined the Guard alongside the Rus'-Norse component. By 1100, the Varangian Guard was substantially Anglo-Saxon. By 1300, the cultural identity had shifted. The Guard finally dissolved when Constantinople fell in 1453 — having served the Byzantine emperors for 465 years, longer than the United States has existed.

**Norman** descendants of the Vikings continued conquering things long after 1066. Other Norman warlords took Sicily and southern Italy from the Byzantines and Muslims in 1060–1130, founding the Norman Kingdom of Sicily. Normans ran the First Crusade to Antioch in 1098. Normans served as elite mercenaries across the Mediterranean. The Viking diaspora's most successful descendants spent the next two centuries reshaping Mediterranean Europe.

So the end of the Viking Age is real but partial. The seaborne raiding and conquest model ended at Stamford Bridge and Hastings. The political, dynastic, and cultural legacies of the Norse expansion continued for centuries afterward — in Russia, in the Norman Mediterranean, in Iceland, in the Anglo-Saxon-Norman fusion that would eventually produce modern English political culture. The longship-borne Viking warband as a strategic actor in European politics ended in 1066. The downstream effects of the Viking Age have not yet ended; some are still running.

---

A final thought, returning to the helmet.

The Viking Age was 273 years long — from Lindisfarne in 793 to Stamford Bridge in 1066. That is shorter than the United States has existed, and noticeably shorter than the Roman Republic, the Tang dynasty, or the Pharaonic New Kingdom of Egypt. In that span, a marginal northern people sailing out of pre-state fjord-kingdoms reached Newfoundland, Constantinople, Baghdad, Rome, and the gates of Egypt; founded the political ancestors of Russia, Iceland, Normandy, and Norman Sicily; placed Christian kings on the thrones of Denmark, Norway, Sweden, England, and Kievan Rus'; created one of the densest vernacular literary traditions in pre-modern European history; established the slave-trading and dirham-flow trade networks that connected the Caliphate to the North Atlantic; built ring fortresses, longhouses, stave churches, and the longship; and ended their Age with the great-great-great-grandson of one of their own warlords riding up Senlac Hill to take the crown of England. It is one of the most successful three-century runs of any people in human history, and the absurd thing is that the popular memory of it is dominated by a costume detail invented by a German opera company in 1876.

The actual Vikings were less colorful and much more interesting. They were mostly farmers. They worked dirty, dangerous, profit-making jobs. They held themselves and each other to oaths because they had no state to enforce contracts. They wrote terse poetry with mathematical structure. They sold each other's relatives. They built parliaments. They reached parts of the world that no other Europeans of their era reached. They did all of this without horns on their helmets, and they would, every single one of them, have laughed at the picture.

Cross-civ check: the year of three kings (1066) coincides with the **Northern Song dynasty** at its commercial and demographic peak in China; the early **Seljuk Empire** consolidating Anatolia after the Battle of Manzikert (just five years later, 1071, when the Seljuks would shatter Byzantine military power and open the way for the eventual fall of Constantinople and the Crusades); the **Almoravid** rise in North Africa and Iberia; the **Chola dynasty** at the height of its naval reach across the Indian Ocean; and the founding of the **Toltec** capital at Tula in central Mexico. The world that the Vikings exited in 1066 was about to become the world of the Crusades, the high medieval European agricultural revolution, the rise of the universities, and the great commercial expansion of the Italian city-states. The Viking Age was over. The medieval world that replaced it was, in many of its most important components, the Viking Age's direct inheritance.

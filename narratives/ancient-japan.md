# Ancient Japan — 14,000 Years of Doing Things Differently

**Goal:** Narrative for the first TL in a Japanese chain. Covers roughly 14,000 BCE to 1185 CE — from the world's oldest pottery through the Jōmon hunter-gatherers, the Yayoi agricultural revolution, the Kofun tomb-builders, the wholesale adoption of Chinese civilization in the Asuka and Nara periods, the literary golden age of the Heian court, and the Genpei War that ended the classical era and created the samurai government. The central thesis: 14,000 years of doing things differently — the world's oldest pottery without farming, the most sophisticated court culture without a standing army, and a civilization that borrowed everything from China yet remained stubbornly, unmistakably Japanese.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — The World's Oldest Pots

Fourteen thousand years ago, someone on the Japanese archipelago made a pot.

That sentence does not sound remarkable. It is one of the most remarkable facts in human history. At the time that anonymous Japanese potter was shaping wet clay with their hands, pressing it into a rough vessel, and firing it in an open campfire, nobody else on Earth was doing the same thing — or at least, nobody else whose pots have survived. The oldest known pottery in the Near East dates to roughly 7,000 BCE. The oldest known pottery in Europe is later still. Chinese pottery from Xianrendong Cave may be roughly contemporary with the Japanese finds, and the dating is debated, but the Japanese claim is among the strongest in the world: fired clay vessels from multiple sites across the Japanese archipelago, dated by radiocarbon to approximately 14,000 BCE, older than farming, older than cities, older than almost everything.

A note about "oldest" before we go any further, because this book is going to use the word several times and the reader deserves honesty about what it means. "Oldest" in archaeology means "oldest surviving evidence found so far." The absence of older pottery elsewhere does not prove that nobody elsewhere was making it. Pottery is fragile. Archaeological sites are destroyed by erosion, farming, construction, and war. What survives is a fraction of what existed, and what gets excavated is a fraction of what survives. The Japanese archipelago happened to produce pottery very early, and that pottery happened to survive, and archaeologists happened to find it. Those facts are genuine. But claiming that Japan "invented" pottery is a stronger statement than the evidence supports. What the evidence supports is this: the people living on the Japanese islands were among the earliest potters anywhere on Earth, and they were doing it thousands of years before anyone in the great river-valley civilizations of Mesopotamia, Egypt, or the Indus Valley had figured it out.

What makes this especially remarkable is that those potters were not farmers.

---

The civilization that produced the world's oldest pottery is called **Jōmon** (JOH-mon), and it lasted for approximately 13,000 years — from around 14,000 BCE to roughly 300 BCE, which makes it one of the longest continuous cultural traditions in human history. To put that in perspective: the entire span of ancient Egyptian civilization, from the first pharaoh to Cleopatra, fits inside the Jōmon period about four times over. The Roman Empire, from Romulus to the fall of Constantinople, fits inside it roughly three times. The Jōmon people lived in the same archipelago, using variations of the same material culture, for longer than the entire recorded history of Western civilization.

The name "Jōmon" (meaning "cord-marked") comes from the distinctive decorative technique on their pottery: pressing twisted cords of plant fiber into wet clay before firing, creating patterns of ridges and grooves across the vessel surface. The term was coined in the 1870s by **Edward Sylvester Morse** (an American zoologist who excavated the Ōmori Shell Mounds near Tokyo in 1877, the first scientific excavation in Japanese history). Morse was looking for mollusks. He found an entire civilization instead.

The Jōmon were hunter-gatherer-fishers who lived in small communities of pit houses, hunted deer and wild boar, fished the extraordinarily productive waters surrounding the Japanese archipelago, and gathered chestnuts, walnuts, acorns, and wild plants from the dense forests that covered the islands. They did not farm. They did not herd domesticated animals (except dogs, which appear early in the Jōmon record). They did not build cities. By the standard definition used in most world history courses — settled agriculture, surplus production, social stratification, monumental architecture, writing — the Jōmon were not a "civilization" at all. They were foragers.

And yet they made pottery, lacquerware, jade ornaments, elaborate clay figurines, stone circles, and 500-person settlements with long-distance trade networks spanning hundreds of miles across open ocean. Everything about the Jōmon challenges the standard narrative that says civilization requires farming. The Jōmon did it without plows, without grain, without any of the agricultural package that powered the Neolithic Revolution in the Near East, China, and Mesoamerica. They found a different path to complexity — and it worked for thirteen millennia.

---

The Japanese archipelago itself is the reason the Jōmon could pull this off.

The islands stretch roughly 1,800 miles (2,900 km) from the subarctic north (Hokkaido, roughly the latitude of Montreal) to the subtropical south (the Ryukyu Islands, roughly the latitude of Miami). The four main islands — Hokkaido, Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu — run in a rough arc from northeast to southwest, separating the Pacific Ocean from the Sea of Japan. The total land area is about the size of California, but the geography is nothing like California: roughly 73% of Japan is mountainous, with narrow coastal plains, steep river valleys, and dense forest cover. The climate ranges from cold temperate in the north to warm maritime in the south, with heavy rainfall (Japan gets more rain than almost any other temperate country), warm ocean currents that bring rich marine life close to shore, and forests that produce abundant nuts, fruits, and wild plants.

This is a forager's paradise. The forests provided chestnuts and walnuts (both storable for winter), acorns (edible after leaching the tannins, a process the Jōmon mastered), and dozens of other plant foods. The coastline — Japan has roughly 18,500 miles (29,800 km) of coastline, one of the longest of any country in the world — provided shellfish, crabs, sea urchins, seaweed, and access to deep-water fish including tuna and bonito. The rivers brought salmon runs in the north. Deer and wild boar roamed the forests. The marine ecosystem off Japan's east coast, where the warm Kuroshio Current meets the cold Oyashio Current, is one of the most productive fishing grounds on earth. The Jōmon did not need to farm because the archipelago fed them.

This is not a unique insight. The **Pacific Northwest** coast of North America — another region of dense forest, rich marine resources, and heavy rainfall — also produced complex hunter-gatherer societies that built permanent settlements, carved elaborate art, held potlatch ceremonies, and never adopted agriculture. The pattern is the same: where marine and forest resources are rich and reliable enough, farming is not necessary for settled life. The Jōmon are the East Asian demonstration of this principle.

---

The Jōmon lived in **pit dwellings** (tateana-jūkyo, tah-teh-AH-nah JOO-kyoh) — houses dug roughly two to three feet into the ground, with wooden post frames and thatched roofs of grass or bark, centered on a hearth for cooking and warmth. A typical Jōmon pit house was about 15 feet (4.5 meters) across, big enough for a family of five or six. The sunken floor provided insulation against the cold (an important consideration in northern Japan, where winter temperatures drop well below freezing) and structural stability against the typhoons that regularly batter the archipelago.

By around 4000 BCE, some Jōmon communities had grown far beyond the scale of a typical forager band. The most spectacular example is **Sannai-Maruyama** (SAHN-nai mah-roo-YAH-mah), a site in northern Honshu (modern Aomori Prefecture) that was occupied continuously from roughly 3900 to 2300 BCE — sixteen hundred years in the same location. At its peak, Sannai-Maruyama housed up to 500 people in hundreds of pit houses and aboveground longhouses, with storage pits for chestnuts and nuts, burial areas, and massive six-post structures built from chestnut-wood pillars over a foot in diameter. The purpose of the large pillar structures is debated — watchtowers? ceremonial buildings? — but their construction required organized labor, timber management, and social coordination well beyond anything associated with simple forager bands.

Sannai-Maruyama also shows long-distance trade. Obsidian (volcanic glass, prized for making razor-sharp cutting tools) from sources in Hokkaido and the Izu Islands — the latter more than 350 miles (560 km) away by sea — has been found at the site. **Jade** from the Itoigawa (ee-TOI-gah-wah) region on the Sea of Japan coast, over 300 miles (480 km) distant, was carved into polished pendants and earrings — possibly the earliest jade-working tradition in East Asia, predating the jade cultures of Neolithic China. Amber, asphalt for waterproofing, and specialty stones for tools also moved through Jōmon trade networks that connected communities across the archipelago and required navigation of open ocean waters.

The Jōmon were not isolated villages scraping by on whatever the forest offered. They were networked communities with specialized production, long-distance maritime exchange, and enough surplus to invest in enormous construction projects and luxury goods. They were doing all of this without a single grain of cultivated rice.

---

Then there is the art.

Around 3500 BCE, potters in the Niigata region of central Honshu began producing what may be the most visually dramatic ceramics in all of prehistory. **Flame-style pottery** (kaen-doki, kah-EN DOH-kee) features rims that explode upward in writhing, flame-like projections — coils and loops and tendrils of clay that twist above the vessel like frozen fire. The vessels are large (some over a foot tall), elaborately decorated, and absolutely impractical for daily cooking or storage. The flamboyant rims would break with ordinary use, and the deeply sculpted surfaces would be impossible to clean. These were almost certainly ceremonial objects — used in feasts, rituals, or display rather than in daily food preparation.

Flame-style pottery has no parallel anywhere in the prehistoric world. At the same time, around 3500 BCE, the potters of Mesopotamia were producing simple, functional vessels — competent but plain. Egyptian pottery was utilitarian. Chinese Neolithic pottery was elegant but restrained. Nobody else was making anything remotely like the Jōmon flame pots. They represent an explosive investment of creative energy in clay, by people who had been making pottery for ten thousand years and had clearly decided that functional adequacy was not enough.

The other great Jōmon art form is the **dogū** (DOH-goo) — small clay figurines, typically five to ten inches tall, with exaggerated features: huge goggle-like eyes, swollen limbs, elaborate hairstyles or headdresses, and prominent breasts or bellies suggesting pregnancy. The most famous type, the **shakōki-dogū** (shah-KOH-kee DOH-goo), looks uncannily like a person wearing snow goggles or a space helmet — a resemblance that has fueled decades of pseudoarchaeological alien-contact theories, none of which have any scholarly support. The figurines were probably fertility symbols, ritual objects, or healing effigies (many are found deliberately broken, suggesting they may have been ritually "killed" to absorb illness or misfortune). Over 18,000 have been found across Japan, overwhelmingly from the northern half of the archipelago, and their production intensified dramatically in the later Jōmon period, when the culture was under stress from climatic cooling.

---

That cooling is the beginning of the end.

Starting around 2000–1500 BCE, the climate of the Japanese archipelago shifted cooler and drier. The dense nut-bearing forests that had sustained the Jōmon for millennia retreated, particularly in central and eastern Honshu. Salmon runs declined. The abundant marine resources of the warmer period became less reliable. Jōmon populations contracted, with settlement numbers dropping sharply in central Japan while communities in the north (which had always been adapted to colder conditions) persisted longer.

The Late and Final Jōmon periods (roughly 2000–300 BCE) show communities under pressure responding in a way that cultures under stress often do: they invested heavily in ritual. **Stone circles** appeared in northern Honshu — notably at Ōyu (OH-yoo) and Komakino (koh-MAH-kee-noh) — arrangements of boulders around central standing stones, some with associated burials and ceremonial deposits, likely used for seasonal gatherings or astronomical observations. Dogū figurine production intensified. Burial practices became more elaborate, with bodies placed in specific orientations and accompanied by ritual goods. The Final Jōmon was not a collapse — it was a slow contraction, a 13,000-year cultural tradition gradually thinning out as the environmental conditions that had sustained it changed.

When the end came, it came not from within but from across the sea. Around 900 BCE, and accelerating sharply around 300 BCE, people began arriving on the northern coast of Kyushu from the Korean Peninsula. They brought with them wet-rice agriculture, bronze and iron tools, and a way of life that was utterly different from anything the Jōmon had practiced. Within a few centuries, the Jōmon world would be transformed beyond recognition.

But it was never entirely erased. Genetic studies show that modern Japanese people carry significant Jōmon ancestry — mixed with the later continental arrivals. The Jōmon contribution to Japanese culture may go deeper than genes: some scholars argue that aspects of Shinto (Japan's indigenous spiritual tradition, which venerates nature spirits, sacred places, and the animating forces in trees, rocks, rivers, and mountains) preserve Jōmon-era spiritual practices that survived the Yayoi transformation and every subsequent cultural revolution. The oldest inhabitants of the Japanese archipelago left their mark not in monuments (they built none that survived) but in DNA, in spiritual intuitions about the sacredness of the natural world, and in the oldest pots on earth.

Cross-civ check: the Jōmon period (~14,000–300 BCE) spans nearly the entire timeline of human civilization elsewhere. When the Jōmon started making pottery, the last Ice Age was ending. By the time the Jōmon period ended, the Near East had passed through the invention of agriculture, the rise and fall of Sumer, the Akkadian Empire, Hammurabi's Babylon, the Hittite Empire, the Assyrian Empire, and the Persian conquest. Egypt had built the pyramids, risen, declined, and been conquered by multiple foreign dynasties. China had passed through the Shang and Zhou Dynasties and was deep in the Warring States period. Greece had gone through the Mycenaean age, the Dark Ages, and the Classical period — and would soon be conquered by Rome. The Jōmon outlasted them all, not by conquering or building, but by persisting in a way of life so well-adapted to their islands that it did not need to change until change was imposed from outside.

# Chapter 2 — The Yayoi Revolution

Around 900 BCE, and in a concentrated wave after about 300 BCE, the most dramatic transformation in Japanese history arrived on the beaches of northern Kyushu.

It did not arrive as an army. It arrived as rice.

**Wet-rice agriculture** (growing rice in flooded paddies fed by controlled irrigation) came to Japan from the Korean Peninsula, brought by migrants who crossed the roughly 120 miles (200 km) of the Korea Strait, probably in stages, settling first on the islands of Tsushima (tsoo-SHEE-mah) and Iki (EE-kee) in the strait itself, then on the northern Kyushu coast. These newcomers brought not only rice seeds and paddy-field technology but also bronze and iron tools, a different style of pottery, and a way of life built around settled farming, surplus production, and the social hierarchies that surplus inevitably creates.

The transition from Jōmon foraging to **Yayoi** (YAH-yoi) farming was the most transformative event in Japanese history before the Meiji Restoration in the nineteenth century. In the space of a few centuries, the Japanese archipelago went from a land of hunter-gatherer-fishers who had lived without agriculture for 13,000 years to a land of rice-farming villages organized into competing chiefdoms, armed with metal weapons, and fighting each other for control of the best paddy land. Everything that came afterward — the Yamato state, the emperor, Buddhism, the samurai, the shogunate, modern Japan — grew from roots planted in the Yayoi period.

The name "Yayoi" comes from a district in Tokyo where a distinctive style of pottery was first discovered in 1884. Compared to the exuberant flame pots and cord-marked vessels of the Jōmon, Yayoi pottery is restrained, functional, and elegant — thin-walled, smooth-surfaced, often reddish-brown, made for cooking rice and storing grain rather than for ritual display. The aesthetic shift from Jōmon to Yayoi is one of the most visually striking transitions in the archaeological record: from wild creativity to practical efficiency, from art for art's sake to tools for the harvest.

---

The critical question about the Yayoi transformation is: did new people arrive, or did existing Jōmon communities adopt the new technology?

The answer, based on decades of archaeological and genetic research, is: both, but with a heavy emphasis on migration. The Yayoi people were physically different from the Jōmon — taller on average, with narrower faces and different dental patterns, consistent with continental East Asian populations. Genetic studies confirm that a substantial influx of people from the Korean Peninsula mixed with the existing Jōmon population, with the continental genetic contribution becoming dominant in most of western Japan while Jōmon genetics persisted more strongly in the north (Hokkaido) and the far south (the Ryukyu Islands). The **Ainu** (EYE-noo), the indigenous people of Hokkaido who maintained a distinct culture into the modern era, are generally understood to carry the strongest direct Jōmon genetic heritage. The Ryukyu Islanders carry significant Jōmon ancestry as well.

The mechanism of transition varied by region. In northern Kyushu, where the continental immigrants arrived first, the replacement was rapid — within a few generations, Yayoi-style sites replaced Jōmon ones almost completely. Further east on Honshu, the process was slower and more mixed, with Jōmon and Yayoi communities existing side by side for centuries, trading, intermarrying, and gradually blending. In Hokkaido, the Yayoi package never fully penetrated at all — the island's cold climate was unsuitable for wet-rice farming, and Jōmon-descended cultures (eventually developing into the distinct Ainu tradition) persisted into the historical period.

The Korean connection is direct and well documented. The earliest Yayoi rice paddies, found at sites like **Itazuke** (ee-TAH-zoo-keh) and **Nabatake** (nah-bah-TAH-keh) in northern Kyushu, use techniques identical to contemporary rice farming on the Korean Peninsula. The pottery styles show Korean parallels. The bronze and iron that accompanied the Yayoi migration came from Korean and Chinese sources. Korean immigrants of this era (known collectively as **toraijin**, toh-RYE-jin, meaning "people who came across") would continue arriving in Japan for centuries, bringing wave after wave of continental technology, and their contribution to early Japanese civilization was enormous — a fact that the Ancient Korea narrative (Chapter 6) addresses from the Korean side.

---

Rice changed the rules of Japanese society completely.

In the Jōmon world, food came from the forest and the sea, resources that were abundant but could not be easily accumulated, stored, or controlled by elites. You could not monopolize a salmon run the way you could monopolize a granary. Rice was different. Rice could be grown in surplus, stored for years in raised granaries, measured, taxed, and redistributed. Whoever controlled the paddies and the granaries controlled the community. For the first time in Japanese history, wealth could be concentrated, and concentrated wealth meant concentrated power.

The archaeological evidence for this transformation is stark. Yayoi settlements show clear social stratification: some burials contain bronze weapons, jade ornaments, and Chinese mirrors, while most contain nothing. Settlements grew larger and became fortified — ditches, palisades, and watchtowers appear for the first time. The largest known Yayoi settlement, **Yoshinogari** (yoh-shee-noh-GAH-ree) in northern Kyushu, was a moated and palisaded town with watchtowers, raised-floor storehouses, and an elite burial precinct where leaders were interred in large jar coffins with bronze weapons and ornaments. Yoshinogari looks like a place that expected to be attacked — and the skeletal evidence from Yayoi sites across Kyushu confirms that it had good reason. Yayoi-period skeletons frequently show evidence of violent death: arrowheads embedded in bone, sword cuts on skulls, decapitated bodies in ditches. The Jōmon period shows remarkably little evidence of organized violence. The Yayoi period is drenched in it.

This pattern — rice agriculture producing surplus, surplus producing inequality, inequality producing warfare — is identical to what happened in Korea (Ancient Korea, Chapter 2), in China several millennia earlier (Ancient China, Chapters 2-3), and in every other part of the world where intensive agriculture replaced foraging. Japan compressed the transition into a few centuries. In the Near East, the shift from foraging to farming to warfare to states took over five thousand years. In Japan, it happened in less than a thousand.

---

Along with rice and iron came bronze — but the Japanese used bronze in a way that nobody else did.

**Dōtaku** (DOH-tah-koo) are bronze bells, some over three feet (one meter) tall, cast in the Kinai region (the area around modern Osaka and Nara, the political heartland of Japan for the next thousand years). They are elaborately decorated with scenes of daily life — harvesting, hunting, animals, buildings — and with geometric patterns. They were never rung. Most have no clappers. Many were buried on hilltops or in remote locations, deliberately hidden from daily life. They were ritual objects: offerings to something, prayers for something, connected to the agricultural cycle that now governed Japanese life. What exactly the dōtaku meant to the people who made them is unknown — there was no writing in Japan yet to explain — but they represent the first distinctively Japanese art form of the agricultural age, a creative response to continental bronze technology that had no equivalent in Korea or China.

The distinction between bronze and iron in Yayoi Japan is important. Japan received both metals at roughly the same time (unlike the Near East, where bronze preceded iron by nearly two millennia), but they served completely different functions. Bronze was ritual: bells, mirrors, ceremonial weapons. Iron was practical: hoes, axes, sickles, arrowheads, swords for actual killing. This functional split — bronze for the gods, iron for the world — mirrors the Korean pattern (Ancient Korea, Chapter 2) and suggests that the Japanese were not merely copying continental metalwork but making deliberate choices about how to use it.

---

In 57 CE, the first documented contact between Japan and China occurred. According to the Chinese **Book of the Later Han** (Hou Hanshu, a Chinese dynastic history compiled in the fifth century CE covering the Later Han dynasty from 6 to 189 CE), a delegation from the **Kingdom of Na** (NAH), one of several small polities in northern Kyushu, traveled to the Chinese capital and received a gold seal from Emperor Guangwu (gwahng-WOO). The seal was inscribed "King of Na of Wa, [vassal of] Han" — "Wa" (WAH) being the Chinese name for the Japanese islands, a name that the Japanese later considered derogatory (it can be written with a character meaning "dwarf" or "submissive"). In 1784, a farmer in northern Kyushu dug up a tiny gold seal that matched the Chinese historical description exactly. The **Gold Seal of the King of Na**, now one of Japan's most famous archaeological artifacts, is physical proof that Chinese historians were telling the truth about early Japanese diplomacy.

The Chinese records mention that "Wa" consisted of over a hundred small kingdoms, constantly warring with each other. This sounds about right for Yayoi Japan: a patchwork of competing chiefdoms, each controlling a few river valleys' worth of rice paddies, fighting their neighbors for land and resources, with no centralized authority and no political unity.

Then, in the third century CE, the Chinese records describe something that sounds almost mythical: a shaman queen.

**Himiko** (HEE-mee-koh) ruled a confederation called **Yamatai** (YAH-mah-tai) and sent envoys to the Chinese **Wei** (WAY) dynasty (one of the Three Kingdoms that divided China after the fall of the Han) in 238 CE. The Chinese text, the **Wei Zhi** (WAY JUH), describes Himiko as a ruler who "occupied herself with magic and sorcery, bewitching the people," who was served by a thousand female attendants, who was rarely seen in public, and who communicated with the outside world through her younger brother. When she died (around 247 CE), over a hundred attendants were sacrificed and buried with her. A male successor was chosen but the people would not obey him, so a thirteen-year-old girl named **Iyo** (EE-yoh) was installed as queen, and order was restored.

Himiko is the first historically documented ruler of Japan — the first Japanese person whose existence is confirmed by a written source outside Japan. Everything about her story is fascinating and almost everything about it is debated. Where was Yamatai? The Chinese text gives travel directions that are maddeningly ambiguous, and Japanese scholars have argued for over a century about whether Yamatai was located in northern Kyushu or in the Kinai region (the later Yamato heartland around modern Nara). If Kyushu, then Yamatai was a regional power, one of many. If Kinai, then Yamatai may have been the direct ancestor of the Yamato state that would eventually unify Japan. The debate is unresolved and may be unresolvable without new evidence.

What is clear from the Chinese account is that third-century Japan was a world of warring chiefdoms united, temporarily, by the personal charisma of a shaman-queen. The political principle that would define Japan for the next two centuries was already visible: authority derived not from military force alone but from spiritual power, from the ability to mediate between the human world and the divine. Himiko's shamanism echoes the shamanistic traditions of Korea (Ancient Korea, Chapter 2) and the broader spiritual world of northeast Asia. When the Yamato rulers later claimed descent from the sun goddess Amaterasu (Chapter 3), they were drawing on the same deep well of divine authority that Himiko had tapped.

Cross-civ check: the Yayoi period (~300 BCE to ~300 CE) coincides with the Qin unification and the Han Dynasty in China, the Maurya and post-Maurya period in India, the late Roman Republic and early Roman Empire in the Mediterranean, and the early Three Kingdoms period in Korea (when Goguryeo, Baekje, and Silla were forming from the confederation stage). Japan was receiving technology from Korea at the same time that Korea was receiving it from China — a cascade of cultural transmission from the Chinese heartland through the Korean peninsula to the Japanese islands that would continue for the next thousand years.

# Chapter 3 — The Tomb Builders

Around 300 CE, enormous mounds began rising from the plains of central Japan.

They are called **kofun** (KOH-fun, meaning simply "old mounds"), and they give their name to the entire era from roughly 300 to 538 CE: the **Kofun period**. Some were round. Some were square. But the most distinctive and largest were shaped like keyholes — a circular mound joined to a trapezoidal platform, visible from above as the silhouette of an old-fashioned key. The keyhole shape appears to be a uniquely Japanese invention: nothing like it exists in Korea, China, or anywhere else. Somebody came up with the idea, and for the next two and a half centuries, every powerful family in Japan adopted it as the standard form for elite burial.

The kofun were big. Not metaphorically big. Big in a way that demands comparison with the great monuments of the ancient world. The largest, the **Daisen Kofun** (DYE-sen KOH-fun) near Osaka, traditionally attributed to the semi-legendary Emperor Nintoku (NIN-toh-koo), is roughly 1,600 feet (486 meters) long and covers about 80 acres (32 hectares) — larger than the base of the Great Pyramid at Giza by area, larger than the Mausoleum of the First Emperor of China by volume of earth moved. It is surrounded by three moats and required an estimated 2,000 workers laboring for over fifteen years to complete. You cannot see its shape from the ground. Only from the air does the keyhole form emerge, a fact that was not fully appreciated until aerial photography in the twentieth century.

The Daisen Kofun is the largest tomb in the world by surface area. This is not hyperbole — it is a measured fact. And it was built by a polity that had no writing system, no coinage, no formal legal code, and no centralized bureaucracy. The kofun are monuments of pure political ambition: a way for powerful families to proclaim their status, anchor their lineage to a specific landscape, and associate their authority with the divine. If you can mobilize 2,000 laborers for fifteen years, you are not a village chief. You are something more — something approaching a king.

---

The political entity that built the great kofun is called the **Yamato** (YAH-mah-toh) court, and it is the direct ancestor of the Japanese imperial institution that exists today. The Yamato clan, based in the Kinai region (the fertile plain around modern Nara and Osaka), gradually established political dominance over much of Honshu and northern Kyushu during the fourth and fifth centuries CE, creating a network of subordinate clans united by marriage alliances, ritual authority, and the threat of military force.

"Dominance" needs qualifying. The Yamato court was not an empire in the Chinese or Roman sense. It did not have provinces, governors, or a centralized tax system. It was a primus inter pares arrangement — the Yamato clan was the most powerful among many powerful clans, and its authority rested on a combination of military strength, control of ritual (particularly the worship of the sun goddess **Amaterasu**, oh-mah-teh-RAH-soo, whom the Yamato claimed as their divine ancestor), and a network of marriage and gift-giving relationships with subordinate clans. The subordinate clans kept their own lands, armies, and local autonomy. They acknowledged Yamato leadership, supplied labor and warriors when demanded, and participated in the court rituals that bound the system together. But they were not provinces — they were allies, sometimes willing and sometimes resentful.

The Yamato rulers eventually took the title **tennō** (TEN-noh), "emperor" — though this title was probably not used until the seventh century and was projected backward onto earlier rulers by later historians. The traditional list of emperors, recorded in the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki (Chapter 5), begins with **Emperor Jimmu** (JIM-moo), who supposedly founded the imperial line in 660 BCE by descending from Amaterasu through a lineage that included gods, demigods, and semi-mythical heroes. The date is almost certainly fictitious — it appears to have been calculated to align with Chinese calendrical cycles — but the claim matters. By tracing the imperial line back to 660 BCE, the later Japanese court was asserting that the emperor's authority predated all foreign influence, that it came from the native gods of the Japanese islands, and that it was older than anything China could claim. The reality is that the Yamato court emerged as a significant political force sometime in the third or fourth century CE. But the mythology was doing political work that the reality could not.

---

The kofun themselves tell us things that no text records.

Around the mound perimeters, the Yamato and other powerful clans placed rows of **haniwa** (HAH-nee-wah) — terracotta clay figures, typically one to three feet tall, depicting warriors, horses, houses, boats, dancers, musicians, shamans, and birds. The haniwa were not buried with the dead inside the mound (unlike Chinese tomb figurines such as the Qin terracotta warriors). They were placed outside, on the mound slopes and around the base, forming a kind of ceremonial boundary or guardian ring.

The haniwa are invaluable because they show us what Kofun-period people actually looked like — or at least how they wanted to be depicted. Warriors wear armor made of riveted iron plates, carry swords and bows, and wear helmets with visors. Horses are equipped with elaborate saddle gear and armor. Women wear hairstyles and jewelry that suggest court fashion. The figures have simple, almost cartoon-like faces — round eyes, straight noses, no mouths or small slot mouths — but their clothing and equipment are rendered in careful detail. From the haniwa, we know that Kofun-period warriors used mounted combat, that horses were armored, that court dress included elaborate headgear, and that the military equipment of the period was heavily influenced by Korean models.

That Korean influence runs deep. The Kofun period was a time of intense interaction between the Japanese archipelago and the Korean Peninsula. **Baekje** (BEHK-jeh, one of the Three Kingdoms of Korea, occupying the southwestern part of the peninsula) was Japan's closest ally and most important source of continental technology. Waves of Korean immigrants, collectively called **toraijin** (toh-RYE-jin, "people who came across"), brought advanced skills in iron smelting, silk weaving, stoneware pottery (called **sueki**, SOO-eh-kee, which replaced the earlier Yayoi earthenware), horse breeding, and construction. The scale of Korean immigration during the Kofun period was substantial — some scholars estimate that as much as one-third of the aristocratic clans listed in later Japanese genealogies claimed Korean ancestry.

The **Gaya** (GAH-yah) confederacy (a group of small polities on the southern tip of the Korean peninsula, between Baekje and Silla) was another critical connection. Gaya produced some of the finest iron in East Asia, and Gaya iron was exported to Japan in large quantities. Japanese involvement in Korean politics during this period is documented but contested: the Nihon Shoki (a Japanese chronicle compiled in 720 CE, Chapter 5) claims that Japan established a colonial outpost called **Mimana** (mee-MAH-nah) in southern Korea, but most modern scholars, both Japanese and Korean, consider this claim exaggerated or fabricated. What is clear is that Japan and southern Korea were deeply interconnected — through trade, migration, military alliance, and marriage — to a degree that later nationalist historiographies on both sides have tried to minimize.

---

The most significant cultural import of the Kofun period was writing itself.

According to the Kojiki (compiled 712 CE, Chapter 5), a Korean scholar named **Wani** (WAH-nee) arrived from Baekje around 405 CE carrying the Confucian Analects (the foundational text of Confucian philosophy, compiled from the teachings of Confucius, who lived in China around 500 BCE) and the **Thousand Character Classic** (a Chinese text used to teach reading). Wani introduced Chinese characters to the Yamato court. The story may be legendary — a single scholar carrying civilization in his luggage is the stuff of myth — but the underlying reality is confirmed: Chinese writing arrived in Japan via Korean intermediaries during the late Kofun period, and it was Koreans, not Chinese, who taught the Japanese how to use it.

This matters because Chinese characters were spectacularly ill-suited to the Japanese language. Chinese is a tonal, monosyllabic, largely uninflected language. Japanese is a polysyllabic, non-tonal, heavily inflected language with a completely different grammatical structure. Using Chinese characters to write Japanese was like using a wrench as a hammer — it could be made to work, but it required constant improvisation. The Japanese would spend the next four centuries wrestling with this problem before finally developing their own writing systems (Chapter 6), and the tension between Chinese-character literacy and native Japanese expression would shape every aspect of Japanese literary culture.

Cross-civ check: the Kofun period (~300-538 CE) overlaps with the decline and fall of the Western Roman Empire (Rome fell in 476 CE), the Three Kingdoms and subsequent unification under Silla in Korea (Ancient Korea, Chapters 4-7), the Northern and Southern Dynasties in China, and the Gupta Empire in India. Japan during this period was roughly at the same stage of state formation as the Korean kingdoms: a patchwork of competing clans consolidating into larger polities, with the critical difference that Japan's island geography meant foreign invasion was far less likely. Korea had to fight off Chinese armies. Japan could borrow from China through Korea without facing Chinese soldiers. Geography gave Japan the luxury of choosing what to adopt and what to reject — a luxury that Korea, sitting on the edge of the Chinese world, rarely enjoyed.

---

# Chapter 4 — The Borrowed Civilization

In 552 CE (some sources say 538 CE — the discrepancy is itself an artifact of the messy early Japanese historical record), King Seong (SUNG) of Baekje sent a diplomatic gift to the Yamato court that would transform Japan more thoroughly than rice, bronze, or iron had. He sent a golden Buddha statue and a set of Buddhist sutras.

Buddhism had been born in India around 500 BCE, traveled east along the Silk Road to China around the first century CE, crossed into the Korean Peninsula by the fourth century CE, and now, carried by Korean diplomats and monks, it arrived in Japan. The journey from Siddhartha Gautama's Bodhi tree to the Yamato court had taken roughly a thousand years and covered over 3,000 miles (4,800 km). Buddhism was the most successful intellectual export in Asian history, and Japan would become one of its most creative interpreters — but not without a fight.

The arrival of Buddhism split the Yamato court into two warring factions. The **Soga** (SOH-gah) clan, one of the most powerful families at court, championed the new religion. They saw Buddhism as a source of continental sophistication, magical power, and diplomatic advantage — Buddhist temples were impressive, Buddhist monks were literate and educated, and adopting Buddhism would signal to China and Korea that Japan was a civilized state worthy of respect. The **Mononobe** (moh-NOH-noh-beh) and **Nakatomi** (nah-kah-TOH-mee) clans opposed it. They were the custodians of Japan's indigenous spiritual tradition (what would later be called **Shinto**, SHIN-toh, "the way of the gods"), and they argued that adopting a foreign religion would anger the native gods and bring disaster.

The dispute was settled not by theology but by politics and violence. When a plague broke out shortly after the Buddha statue arrived, the Mononobe blamed the foreign religion and threw the statue into a canal. When the plague persisted, the Soga retrieved it. The conflict escalated through the late sixth century until the Soga clan, backed by military force, destroyed the Mononobe in battle in 587 CE and established themselves as the dominant power at court. Buddhism was in, and so were the Soga.

The practical result was a characteristically Japanese compromise. Buddhism did not replace Shinto. Instead, the two traditions coexisted — and eventually fused into a syncretic blend that would define Japanese religion for the next millennium. Shinto shrines and Buddhist temples were often built side by side. Buddhist monks performed rituals at Shinto shrines. Shinto gods were reinterpreted as manifestations of Buddhist deities. The Japanese had borrowed a universal religion from the continent and made it local.

---

The figure who dominates the early Asuka period is **Prince Shōtoku** (SHOH-toh-koo), regent from 593 to 622 CE under his aunt, **Empress Suiko** (SWEE-koh) — Japan's first reigning empress. Shōtoku is the closest thing Japan has to a founding father: a visionary reformer who attempted to transform the Yamato court from a loose clan federation into a centralized state modeled on the Chinese empire.

Shōtoku's reforms were ambitious. In 604, he promulgated the **Seventeen-Article Constitution**, which was not a constitution in the modern sense (it established no branches of government, no legal procedures, no individual rights) but a moral code for officials, blending Buddhist compassion, Confucian hierarchy, and a pragmatic insistence on court harmony. Article One reads: "Harmony should be valued and quarrels should be avoided." Article Two urges reverence for the Three Treasures of Buddhism. Article Three demands obedience to imperial commands. The document reads like a management memo from a CEO who has studied philosophy — earnest, idealistic, and designed to impose order on a court that badly needed it.

Shōtoku also adopted the Chinese calendar system (enabling standardized timekeeping across the Yamato state), established a court rank system modeled on Chinese precedents, and most dramatically, sent an embassy to Sui China in 607 under the diplomat **Ono no Imoko** (OH-noh noh ee-MOH-koh) with a letter that opened: "From the Son of Heaven in the land where the sun rises, to the Son of Heaven in the land where the sun sets." The Sui Emperor Yangdi was reportedly furious at being addressed as an equal by what China considered a barbarian chieftain. The letter was diplomatically reckless and politically brilliant: it asserted that Japan's ruler stood on the same level as China's, a claim that no Korean kingdom had dared to make. Whether Shōtoku actually wrote the letter is debated, but the assertion of Japanese sovereignty it contained would remain a cornerstone of Japanese foreign policy.

Shōtoku's most enduring physical legacy is **Hōryū-ji** (HOH-ryoo-jee), a Buddhist temple complex near Nara that he founded in 607. Hōryū-ji contains the oldest surviving wooden structures in the world — buildings that have been standing for over 1,400 years, through earthquakes, typhoons, fires, and wars. The main hall and five-story pagoda at Hōryū-ji are masterpieces of early Japanese architecture, combining Chinese-derived column-and-bracket construction with Japanese woodworking traditions that achieved a structural resilience no other wooden buildings in history have matched. The pagoda's central pillar hangs from the top rather than resting on the ground, allowing it to sway with earthquake motion rather than resisting it — a structural principle that modern engineers have called a passive tuned mass damper, rediscovered and applied to skyscrapers in the twentieth century.

---

Shōtoku died in 622, and within a generation, the Soga clan's grip on power had become suffocating. In 645, a palace coup changed everything. Prince Naka no Ōe (NAH-kah noh OH-eh) and a court noble named Nakatomi no Kamatari (nah-kah-TOH-mee noh kah-mah-TAH-ree) assassinated the Soga clan leader Soga no Iruka (SOH-gah noh ee-ROO-kah) at a court ceremony — reportedly cutting him down in front of the empress while Korean ambassadors looked on in horror. The rest of the Soga were destroyed. Nakatomi no Kamatari was rewarded with a new surname: **Fujiwara** (foo-jee-WAH-rah). That name would dominate Japanese politics for the next four centuries (Chapters 6-7).

The coup's immediate result was the **Taika Reform** (TYE-kah, meaning "great change"), promulgated in 646. The reforms were modeled explicitly on the Tang Chinese bureaucratic system and attempted to centralize all land ownership under the emperor, establish a Chinese-style tax system, create a permanent capital, and organize the population into administrative units. On paper, the Taika Reform was a revolution: it proposed turning Japan from a collection of clan territories into a centralized empire modeled on Tang China, then the most sophisticated state on earth.

In practice, the reforms were only partially implemented. The powerful clans resisted giving up their land and their autonomy. The bureaucratic apparatus needed to run a Chinese-style centralized state barely existed. The reforms were a statement of intent — a declaration that Japan would model itself on China — more than a fully realized transformation. But the direction was set. Every subsequent reform for the next half century pushed further toward the Chinese model.

---

One crucial episode from this period shaped Japan's relationship with the continent for centuries. In 663, a combined Yamato-Baekje fleet sailed to Korea to help the collapsing kingdom of Baekje fight off a Silla-Tang Chinese alliance. At the **Battle of Baekgang** (BEHK-gahng), the Japanese and Baekje forces were annihilated — over 400 ships destroyed by superior Chinese naval tactics and firepower. The defeat was catastrophic. Japan lost its last military foothold on the Korean Peninsula and would not project military power onto the continent again for nearly a thousand years.

The Battle of Baekgang taught Japan a lesson that geography reinforced: the archipelago was better defended by water than by expeditionary armies. After 663, Japan turned inward, focusing on consolidating control over the home islands rather than pursuing continental ambitions. Thousands of Baekje refugees fled to Japan after their kingdom's destruction, bringing with them skills in architecture, metalwork, and Buddhist learning that accelerated Japan's cultural development. The fall of Baekje was a military disaster for Japan but a cultural windfall.

The reform momentum continued through the late seventh century. In 672, the **Jinshin War** (JIN-shin, a brief but violent succession dispute) ended with Prince Ōama (OH-ah-mah) seizing the throne as Emperor Tenmu (TEN-moo). Tenmu was a centralizer who strengthened imperial authority, promoted Buddhism even more aggressively, and commissioned the compilation of Japan's first national histories (which would eventually become the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki, Chapter 5). In 701, the **Taihō Code** (TYE-hoh) codified the Chinese-style administrative system into a comprehensive legal framework, establishing provinces, districts, centralized taxation, and a bureaucratic rank system. Japan now had, at least on paper, a government as sophisticated as any in East Asia.

The irony of the Asuka period is enormous. Japan had borrowed its religion from India (via China and Korea), its writing system from China, its government model from China, its architectural techniques from Korea, and its legal code from China. It had adopted a Chinese-style calendar, Chinese-style court dress, Chinese-style city planning, and Chinese-style court music. The Japanese court was, by 700 CE, one of the most thoroughly Sinicized institutions outside of China itself. And yet it never became Chinese. The Japanese took what they wanted, adapted it to their own purposes, and discarded what did not fit. Buddhism became Japanese Buddhism. Chinese writing became Japanese writing. Chinese government became Japanese government, with the emperor at its center rather than a Confucian mandate-of-heaven meritocracy. The borrowing was total. The transformation was equally total.

Cross-civ check: the Asuka period (~538-710 CE) overlaps with the late Roman Empire's transformation into the Byzantine Empire in Europe, the Sui and Tang Dynasties in China (the Tang being the civilization that Japan most aggressively copied), the Three Kingdoms unification under Silla in Korea (Ancient Korea, Chapters 6-7), and the early spread of Islam across the Near East and North Africa. Japan in this period was doing something remarkably similar to what the Germanic kingdoms of Western Europe were doing at the same time — adopting the institutional and cultural framework of a more advanced civilization (Rome for Europe, China for Japan) while retaining their own identity. The Taihō Code of 701 is roughly contemporary with the Visigothic legal codes in Spain and the Anglo-Saxon law codes in England — all attempts by recently organized kingdoms to codify authority using models borrowed from a more powerful neighbor.

---

# Chapter 5 — Nara

In 710, Empress Genmei (GEN-may) moved the Japanese capital to a brand-new city called **Heijō-kyō** (HAY-joh KYOH), and Japan entered the period that historians call the **Nara period** (710-794 CE), named after the city where the capital now stood.

Heijō-kyō was designed as a copy of Chang'an (CHAHNG-ahn), the Tang Chinese capital and the largest city in the world at the time (population roughly 1 million). The Japanese planners laid out a grid of straight streets dividing the city into symmetrical blocks, with the imperial palace at the northern end and a broad central avenue running south, exactly as Chang'an was organized. The resemblance was deliberate and obvious: anyone arriving in Nara who had visited Chang'an would have recognized the layout immediately. Japan was building a capital that announced, in every avenue and roofline, that it intended to be a civilized East Asian state on the Chinese model.

There was one significant difference. Chang'an was a walled city, defended against the steppe nomads who periodically threatened China's northern frontier. Nara had no walls. Japan's island geography meant it did not face the threat of mounted invasion — no Japanese city would be walled until the castle-building era nearly a millennium later. The Nara court could afford to build for beauty and prestige rather than defense, a luxury that continental powers rarely enjoyed.

Heijō-kyō at its peak housed perhaps 100,000 to 200,000 people — not remotely comparable to Chang'an, but one of the largest cities in the world outside China. It was Japan's first permanent capital (previous courts had moved with each new emperor, partly due to Shinto beliefs about death pollution contaminating the imperial residence). The decision to build a fixed capital represented a fundamental shift: the Japanese state was now institutional enough that it could survive in one place, anchored by buildings and bureaucrats rather than by the person of the ruler.

---

The Nara period's most enduring legacy is textual. In 712, a court attendant named **Ō no Yasumaro** (OH noh yah-soo-MAH-roh) completed the **Kojiki** (koh-JEE-kee, "Record of Ancient Matters"), Japan's oldest surviving written work. The Kojiki records the mythological origins of Japan — the creation of the islands by the divine couple Izanagi (ee-zah-NAH-gee) and Izanami (ee-zah-NAH-mee), the birth of the sun goddess Amaterasu, the descent of her grandson to earth, and the founding of the imperial line by Emperor Jimmu. It then continues with a semi-mythological chronicle of the early emperors, blending genealogy, legend, and poetry.

Eight years later, in 720, a larger and more ambitious work was completed: the **Nihon Shoki** (nee-HON SHOH-kee, "Chronicles of Japan"), Japan's first official national history, written entirely in classical Chinese (whereas the Kojiki used a mix of Chinese characters employed for their phonetic values in Japanese and straight Chinese). The Nihon Shoki covers the same mythological ground as the Kojiki but extends the historical narrative through 697 CE and includes alternative versions of many myths — a scholarly honesty that the Kojiki, which presents a single authorized version, lacks.

Why were these works compiled? The answer is political. The Yamato court needed a written foundation myth to legitimize its rule. Every civilized state in East Asia — China, Korea — had official histories tracing the ruling dynasty back to divine or cosmic origins. Japan, which had only recently acquired writing, had none. The Kojiki and Nihon Shoki filled that gap. They established that the emperor descended from Amaterasu, that the imperial line was unbroken since the age of the gods, and that Japan's political order was ordained by heaven. These claims were not neutral historical observations — they were state propaganda, composed by court officials under imperial patronage to serve imperial interests.

This is not a modern cynical reading. It is exactly what the texts are. And it does not diminish their value. The Kojiki and Nihon Shoki preserve myths, legends, poems, and cultural information that would otherwise be lost — including fragments of the oral tradition that predated writing and may reach back to Jōmon or early Yayoi-era religious practices. They are propaganda and treasure at the same time, just like every other ancient national origin text from the Hebrew Bible to Virgil's Aeneid.

---

The dominant figure of the Nara period is **Emperor Shōmu** (SHOH-moo, reigned 724-749), and his grand project was the most ambitious construction effort in Japanese history.

Shōmu was a fervent Buddhist who believed that the spiritual power of Buddhism could protect the nation from earthquakes, plagues, and political instability — all of which were abundant in eighth-century Japan. He ordered the construction of a network of **kokubunji** (koh-koo-BOON-jee, "provincial temples") across every province in Japan, each with a seven-story pagoda, and each serving as a spiritual anchor for the region. This was the religious equivalent of building a chain of military fortresses: Shōmu was garrisoning the country with prayer.

The crown jewel of this program was **Tōdai-ji** (TOH-dye-jee, "Great Eastern Temple") in Nara, and its centerpiece was the **Daibutsu** (DYE-boo-tsoo, "Great Buddha") — an enormous bronze seated Buddha statue, roughly 49 feet (15 meters) tall, cast from copper, tin, mercury, and gold collected from across the country. The casting of the Great Buddha was a national effort: it consumed most of Japan's copper and gold reserves, required 2.6 million laborers (according to the official account, probably an exaggeration but indicative of the project's scale), and took multiple attempts over several years to complete. The statue was dedicated in 752 at a ceremony attended by over 10,000 monks, with musicians and dancers from China, Korea, India, and Central Asia. The event was the most cosmopolitan gathering in Japanese history to that point.

The Great Buddha sat inside what was then the world's largest wooden building. The current **Daibutsuden** (Great Buddha Hall) at Tōdai-ji is a reconstruction from the eighteenth century and is itself the world's largest wooden structure — but it is actually only two-thirds the size of the original Nara-period hall. The original building was wider and probably taller. Nothing in the wooden architecture of Europe, China, or Korea at the time came close.

After Shōmu's death in 756, his widow, Empress Kōmyō (KOH-myoh), donated his personal collection of over 9,000 objects to Tōdai-ji, where they were stored in the **Shōsō-in** (SHOH-soh-in), a raised-floor log-cabin storehouse that has preserved the collection in extraordinary condition for over 1,200 years. The Shōsō-in collection is a time capsule of the eighth-century world: it contains Persian glass, Chinese Tang Dynasty mirrors, Central Asian textiles, Indian-influenced musical instruments, Korean metalwork, and Japanese lacquerware — evidence that Nara, at the far eastern end of the Silk Road, was connected to a trade network that stretched from the Mediterranean to the Pacific.

---

The Nara period's other great literary achievement was the **Man'yōshū** (mahn-YOH-shoo, "Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves"), Japan's oldest poetry anthology, compiled around 759. It contains over 4,500 poems by over 500 authors, from emperors and aristocrats to frontier soldiers and anonymous peasants. The Man'yōshū is remarkable for its emotional range — love poems, elegies, nature descriptions, political complaints, homesick soldiers' laments — and for its social range. No other ancient poetry collection of comparable size includes poems by commoners alongside poems by rulers. The greatest poet of the collection, **Kakinomoto no Hitomaro** (kah-kee-NOH-moh-toh noh hee-toh-MAH-roh), writing around 700, composed works of such emotional intensity and formal sophistication that he is still considered one of the greatest poets in the Japanese language.

The Man'yōshū also preserves an important transitional moment in Japanese writing. The poems are written using Chinese characters employed phonetically to represent Japanese syllables — a system called **man'yōgana** (mahn-YOH-gah-nah). This was the clumsy workaround for the fundamental problem of writing Japanese with Chinese characters (Chapter 3): since the two languages are completely unrelated, the only way to represent Japanese poetry was to strip Chinese characters of their meaning and use them purely as sound symbols. One character per syllable. It worked, but it was absurdly inefficient — imagine writing English by repurposing numbers as sounds (1 = "wun," 2 = "too," 4 = "for") and then spelling out entire sentences that way. The system was begging to be simplified, and within a century, it would be — with revolutionary consequences (Chapter 6).

The Nara period ended when the capital moved. The Buddhist clergy at Nara had become too politically powerful — one monk, **Dōkyō** (DOH-kyoh), nearly seized the throne in the 760s through his influence over the reigning empress. Emperor Kanmu (KAHN-moo, reigned 781-806) decided that the solution was to leave the monks behind. In 784, he began building a new capital away from Nara's temple districts, first at Nagaoka-kyō (abandoned after a series of bad omens and political murders) and then, in 794, at a site further north that he named **Heian-kyō** (HAY-ahn KYOH, "Capital of Peace and Tranquility"). The city would later be known by a different name: **Kyoto**. It would remain the imperial capital for over a thousand years.

Cross-civ check: the Nara period (710-794) coincides with the height of the Tang Dynasty in China (one of the greatest periods of Chinese civilization, and the model Japan was copying), the Unified Silla golden age in Korea (Ancient Korea, Chapter 8), the rise of the Abbasid Caliphate in the Islamic world, the Carolingian Renaissance in Europe (Charlemagne was crowned in 800, just six years after Kanmu moved the capital to Kyoto), and the Viking Age in Scandinavia. The Tōdai-ji Great Buddha, dedicated in 752, is roughly contemporary with the construction of the Umayyad Mosque in Damascus and the Abbey of Saint-Denis near Paris — all three representing different civilizations investing enormous resources in monumental religious architecture at approximately the same historical moment.

# Chapter 6 — The Heian Court

For two centuries after the capital moved to Kyoto in 794, the Japanese court turned inward and produced one of the most refined, self-absorbed, aesthetically obsessed, and literarily productive cultures in human history.

The **Heian period** (HAY-ahn, 794-1185 CE) is named after the capital, Heian-kyō, and its first two centuries (roughly 794-1000) represent the flowering of a distinctive Japanese civilization that had finally stepped out from under China's shadow. The Nara court had been an imitation of Tang China. The Heian court was something new — recognizably influenced by China, but transformed into something that no Chinese person would have recognized as their own.

The transformation was partly deliberate. In 894, the scholar and politician **Sugawara no Michizane** (soo-gah-WAH-rah noh mee-chee-ZAH-neh) recommended that the court cancel the long-standing practice of sending embassies to Tang China. The Tang Dynasty was in visible decline — racked by rebellions, weakened by warlordism, heading toward its collapse in 907 — and Michizane argued that the expense and danger of the embassy voyages (ships sank with distressing regularity in the Korea Strait) outweighed the benefits. The court agreed. The embassies stopped, and Japan began a period of relative cultural independence that would last for centuries.

Michizane himself met a bitter end. A victim of Fujiwara political intrigue, he was exiled from court in 901 and died in despair two years later. Then things got interesting. After his death, a series of plagues, fires, and lightning strikes hit the capital, and the court became convinced that Michizane's angry spirit was taking revenge. The solution was characteristically Heian: they deified him. Michizane became **Tenjin** (TEN-jin), the god of learning and scholarship, and thousands of shrines were built across Japan to appease his spirit. He is still worshipped today — Japanese students pray at Tenjin shrines before exams, making Michizane possibly the only government bureaucrat in history to become a god because his former colleagues felt guilty about firing him.

---

The Heian court revolved around two things: the Fujiwara clan and the written word.

The **Fujiwara** (foo-jee-WAH-rah) clan had been accumulating power since 645, when Nakatomi no Kamatari helped Prince Naka no Ōe overthrow the Soga and received the Fujiwara surname as his reward (Chapter 4). The Fujiwara strategy was elegant and ruthless: they married their daughters to emperors. The son of such a marriage would become the next emperor, but since he was also a Fujiwara grandson, the Fujiwara grandfather (or uncle) would serve as **sesshō** (SES-shoh, regent for a child emperor) or **kampaku** (KAHM-pah-koo, regent for an adult emperor), exercising real power while the emperor reigned as a symbolic figurehead.

In 858, **Fujiwara no Yoshifusa** (yoh-shee-FOO-sah) became the first non-imperial regent in Japanese history, setting the precedent. For the next two centuries, the Fujiwara monopolized the regency, the most important court positions, and the flow of revenue from tax-exempt estates. They did not seize the throne — they did not need to. They controlled the man on the throne, which was more efficient and less politically dangerous.

The peak of Fujiwara power came under **Fujiwara no Michinaga** (mee-chee-NAH-gah), who dominated court politics from 995 to 1027. Michinaga married four daughters to emperors and saw three of his grandsons become emperors in succession. At the height of his influence, he composed a poem at a banquet that captures the Fujiwara worldview with stunning candor: "This world, I think, is indeed my world. Like the full moon I shine, uncovered by any cloud." The poem was not modesty — it was an accurate description of his political position. Michinaga was the most powerful man in Japan, and the emperor was his puppet.

---

But the Fujiwara are not why people remember the Heian court. The literature is.

The single most important cultural development of the Heian period — and arguably the most important in all of Japanese cultural history — was the invention of **kana** (KAH-nah), the Japanese phonetic writing systems. By around 850, Japanese scribes had simplified the man'yōgana system (Chapter 5) into two streamlined syllabaries: **hiragana** (hee-rah-GAH-nah), a flowing, cursive script derived from simplified Chinese characters, and **katakana** (kah-tah-KAH-nah), an angular script derived from parts of Chinese characters. Each kana symbol represented one Japanese syllable (ka, ki, ku, ke, ko, sa, shi, su, se, so, and so on), and together the two systems could write any Japanese word phonetically.

Kana was a revolution. For the first time, Japanese could be written as Japanese — not as a tortured phonetic adaptation of Chinese characters, but as a direct representation of the spoken language. Chinese characters (called **kanji**, KAHN-jee, in Japanese) continued to be used for their meanings (and are still used in modern Japanese), but kana freed Japanese writers from the requirement of Chinese literacy. You no longer needed to master thousands of Chinese characters to put words on paper. You needed to learn about fifty kana symbols, and you could write anything you could say.

The monk **Kūkai** (KOO-kye), who founded the Shingon (SHIN-gon) school of esoteric Buddhism at Mount Kōya (KOH-yah) in 816, is traditionally credited with creating the kana syllabary — though the development was probably gradual and involved many hands. Kūkai was also one of the most brilliant minds of the Heian period: a poet, calligrapher, engineer, educator, and religious thinker who traveled to Tang China, studied esoteric Buddhism, and returned to establish a monastic tradition that remains one of the most important in Japanese Buddhism. His contemporary **Saichō** (SYE-choh) made a similar journey and founded the **Tendai** (TEN-dye) school at Mount Hiei (HEE-ay), northeast of Kyoto. Between them, Saichō and Kūkai established the two great Buddhist institutions that would dominate Japanese religious life for centuries — Tendai on Mount Hiei, Shingon on Mount Kōya, each with its own monks, temples, landholdings, and eventually its own armies.

---

Kana unleashed a literary explosion, and the explosion was largely female.

In Heian court culture, Chinese literary composition was considered a male accomplishment — men wrote official documents, histories, and serious poetry in Chinese. Women, who were generally not taught Chinese literacy (or were expected to hide it if they had it), wrote in Japanese using kana. This gendered division of literary labor had an extraordinary unintended consequence: it was women, writing in their own language rather than a borrowed one, who produced the masterpieces of Heian literature.

**Murasaki Shikibu** (moo-rah-SAH-kee SHEE-kee-boo), a lady-in-waiting at the court of Empress Shōshi (SHOH-shee, one of Fujiwara no Michinaga's daughters), completed **The Tale of Genji** (GEN-jee) around 1010. It is over 1,000 pages long, covers 75 years and three generations, features over 400 characters, and explores the romantic, political, and psychological life of Prince Genji and his descendants with a depth of character development and emotional complexity that anticipates the European novel by seven centuries. Scholars debate whether it should be called "the world's first novel" — the definition of "novel" is elastic, and earlier long prose narratives exist in other traditions — but no earlier work of fiction in any language approaches Genji's sustained psychological realism, its nuanced treatment of aging and loss, or its understanding that beauty and sadness are two faces of the same experience.

Murasaki Shikibu also kept a diary, which reveals a sharp, observant, and occasionally waspish personality. In one famous passage, she writes about her literary rival, **Sei Shōnagon** (SAY SHOH-nah-gon), the author of **The Pillow Book** (completed around 1002): "Sei Shōnagon has the most extraordinary air of self-satisfaction. But when we stop to examine those Chinese characters of hers, we find they are full of imperfections." The dig is specific — Sei Shōnagon showed off her Chinese literacy, which Murasaki considered vulgar — and it captures the competitive, knife-edged social world of the Heian court in a single sentence.

The Pillow Book itself is one of the most delightful works in Japanese literature: a collection of lists, anecdotes, observations, opinions, and gossip that essentially invents the essay form. Sei Shōnagon categorizes "Hateful Things" (a guest who stays too long), "Elegant Things" (a white coat over a violet waistcoat), "Things That Make One's Heart Beat Faster" (sparrows feeding their young), and dozens of other categories with a wit and precision that reads as startlingly modern. She invented the listicle a thousand years before the internet.

These two women, writing at the same court within a decade of each other, produced works that defined the Japanese literary tradition and that remain in print, in multiple translations, over a thousand years later. No other court culture of the early medieval world — not Abbasid Baghdad, not Byzantine Constantinople, not Carolingian Aachen — produced comparable female literary achievement. The Heian court's gendered division of literary labor was designed to keep women in their place. It accidentally gave them the tools to create the greatest prose literature in the pre-modern world.

---

The Heian court was also the period when Shinto, Japan's indigenous spiritual tradition, was formalized as an institution. In 927, the **Engishiki** (en-GEE-shee-kee) codified over 2,800 official Shinto shrines across Japan, establishing a formal hierarchy of shrines, specifying the rituals to be performed at each, and defining the relationship between the imperial house and the shrine network. Shinto had existed as a folk practice for millennia — the veneration of nature spirits (kami, KAH-mee), sacred places, and ancestral deities — but it had never been systematized. The Engishiki made it official: Shinto was the spiritual backbone of the Japanese state, with the emperor (descended from Amaterasu) at its apex and the shrine network extending imperial spiritual authority into every village.

The coexistence of Shinto and Buddhism in Heian Japan was remarkably smooth compared to religious conflicts elsewhere in the world. There were no Buddhist-Shinto wars, no heresy trials, no forced conversions. Instead, the two traditions interpenetrated until they became nearly inseparable: Buddhist temples maintained Shinto shrines on their grounds; Shinto gods were assigned Buddhist counterparts; the same worshippers prayed at both shrines and temples without sensing any contradiction. This syncretic accommodation, called **shinbutsu-shūgō** (shin-BOO-tsoo SHOO-goh, "the merging of kami and buddhas"), would last until the Meiji government forcibly separated the two traditions in the 1870s — over a thousand years of peaceful coexistence.

Cross-civ check: the Heian golden age (~794-1000 CE) coincides with the Abbasid Caliphate's zenith in Baghdad (where the House of Wisdom was translating Greek philosophy), the late Tang and Song Dynasty transition in China, the Viking expansion across Europe, and the collapse and fragmentation of the Carolingian Empire. Murasaki Shikibu was writing The Tale of Genji at approximately the same time that Leif Erikson was reaching North America, that al-Haytham was inventing optical science in Cairo, and that the Song Dynasty was developing movable-type printing in China. The Heian court, despite its self-absorption and its cultivation of a deliberately insular aesthetic world, existed in a remarkably creative historical moment — one of those periods when multiple civilizations were simultaneously producing their most distinctive cultural achievements without being aware of each other.

# Chapter 7 — Warriors Rising

The world the Heian court had built — refined, literary, obsessed with beauty and rank and the proper shade of sleeve showing beneath a robe — had a fatal structural weakness. It had no army.

That is an overstatement, but not by much. The Heian court had abolished the Chinese-style conscript army in 792 (it had never worked well — Japanese peasants made reluctant soldiers, and the expense of maintaining the system was enormous) and replaced it with nothing. Provincial defense was delegated to local strongmen. Tax collection was contracted out to estate managers. The enforcement of law outside the capital depended entirely on the willingness of local officials to enforce it, and local officials increasingly had their own priorities.

The result, over the tenth and eleventh centuries, was the gradual rise of a provincial warrior class that owed more to local loyalties than to the court in Kyoto. These warriors are the ancestors of the **samurai** (SAH-moo-rye, from the verb saburau, "to serve"), though the term itself would not come into common use until later. They were mounted archers and swordsmen, landholders who maintained private military forces, administered rural estates, and settled disputes through violence rather than court poetry. They were, in short, everything the Heian court was not.

The court needed these warriors. As Fujiwara power waned in the eleventh century — the clan's control depended on marrying daughters to emperors, and when no Fujiwara woman produced an heir, the system broke down — the court increasingly relied on two warrior clans to maintain order: the **Taira** (TYE-rah, also called Heike, HAY-keh) and the **Minamoto** (mee-nah-MOH-toh, also called Genji, GEN-jee — the same word as the novel's title, which is a literary coincidence). Both clans claimed descent from junior branches of the imperial family, which gave them the prestige to operate in the court's orbit. Both provided military muscle that the court itself could not supply.

---

The pattern of the eleventh century is one of the court's progressive dependence on warrior families.

The **Former Nine Years' War** (1051-1062), fought in the remote northern provinces of Honshu, illustrates the dynamic perfectly. The war began as a rebellion by the **Abe** (AH-beh) clan, a powerful family in the Mutsu region (the northernmost frontier of Yamato-controlled territory, facing the Emishi, the non-Yamato people of northern Honshu who had never been fully subjugated). The court sent a Minamoto commander, **Minamoto no Yoriyoshi** (yoh-ree-YOH-shee), to suppress the rebellion, but the campaign dragged on for twelve years and succeeded only when Yoriyoshi allied with another northern warrior family, the Kiyowara (kee-yoh-WAH-rah). The Minamoto won the war, but the court got the lesson: pacifying the provinces required warriors, and warriors who won wars accumulated followers, prestige, and independent power.

A follow-up conflict, the **Later Three Years' War** (1083-1087), further cemented Minamoto power in the northeast. The Minamoto were becoming a hereditary military aristocracy, passing down martial skills, warrior retainer networks, and an ethic of loyal service from generation to generation. The Taira, based more in the west and south, were building a similar power base. The stage was being set for a collision.

Meanwhile, the court still functioned — still wrote poetry, still performed rituals, still appointed officials — but its grip on real power was loosening. The **Fujiwara regency** had been partially displaced by a system called **insei** (IN-say, "cloistered rule"), in which retired emperors who had abdicated the throne continued to exercise political power from behind the scenes, free from the ceremonial obligations that constrained the reigning emperor. This system, which operated from the 1070s onward, showed that even within the court, the formal structures of government were being bypassed in favor of personal power arrangements.

In 1053, in the middle of this slow political dissolution, one of the most beautiful buildings in Japan was completed. **Byōdō-in** (BYOH-doh-in), specifically its famous **Phoenix Hall** (Hōō-dō, HOH-oh-DOH), was built by the retired regent **Fujiwara no Yorimichi** (yoh-ree-MEE-chee) on the site of his father Michinaga's villa in Uji (OO-jee), south of Kyoto. The Phoenix Hall is a masterpiece of Heian architecture — a delicate, symmetrical building that appears to float above a reflecting pond, with bronze phoenix ornaments on the roof and a gilded Amida Buddha inside. It is depicted on the Japanese 10-yen coin. It was built as a representation of the Buddhist Pure Land paradise, the heavenly realm where Amida Buddha awaits the faithful. That the most powerful family in Japan was building images of paradise at precisely the moment their political paradise was crumbling is an irony that the Heian court, with its exquisite sensitivity to the beauty of impermanence, might have appreciated.

---

The end of Heian political order came with sudden violence.

In 1156, a succession dispute within the imperial family spilled into armed conflict in the capital itself. The **Hōgen Rebellion** (HOH-gen) pitted two claimants to the throne against each other, and each side recruited Taira and Minamoto warriors to fight for them. For the first time, samurai fought in the streets of Kyoto, the sacred imperial capital where violence was supposed to be unthinkable. The Hōgen Rebellion lasted only a few weeks, but it shattered the pretense that the court could maintain order without warrior involvement. The poet-courtiers who had governed Japan through elegance and intrigue for two centuries discovered that the men with swords had been the real power all along.

The Hōgen Rebellion is the hinge point of Japanese history. Everything before it belongs to the classical era of court government. Everything after it belongs to the age of warriors. The chapter between those two eras is the Genpei War (Chapter 8).

Cross-civ check: the rise of the samurai class in the eleventh and twelfth centuries CE parallels the feudal transformation in Western Europe, where mounted knights were similarly displacing centralized royal authority and building local power through landholding, military service, and personal loyalty networks. The similarities are striking enough that historians have long debated whether Japanese "feudalism" and European "feudalism" represent the same social phenomenon or merely superficial parallels. The differences are real — the Heian court was never as weak as the post-Carolingian European monarchies, and the Japanese emperor retained a symbolic authority that European kings did not — but the structural parallels (central authority weakening, military landholders becoming the real power, a warrior ethic displacing a courtly one) are hard to ignore.

# Chapter 8 — The Genpei War

The conflict that ended the classical era of Japanese history began, as Japanese conflicts often did, with a court intrigue.

After the Hōgen Rebellion of 1156 (Chapter 7), the victorious faction included warriors from both the Taira and Minamoto clans. A follow-up conflict in 1160, the **Heiji Rebellion** (HAY-jee), resulted in a decisive Taira victory. The Minamoto leader was killed. His two young sons were spared — a decision that the Taira would eventually regret more than any other in Japanese history.

The older son, **Minamoto no Yoritomo** (yoh-ree-TOH-moh), was exiled to the remote Izu Peninsula in eastern Japan, where he spent twenty years building relationships with local warrior families, nursing his grievance, and waiting. The younger son, **Minamoto no Yoshitsune** (yoh-shee-TSOO-neh), was sent to a monastery and then escaped to the northern provinces, where he was taken in by a powerful northern warrior lord. These two brothers would grow up to destroy the Taira, create warrior government, and then destroy each other.

---

The man who dominated Japan in the years between the Heiji Rebellion and the Genpei War was **Taira no Kiyomori** (TYE-rah noh kee-yoh-MOH-ree), and his rise to power was without precedent in Japanese history.

Kiyomori was a warrior — a provincial military man from a family that had made its reputation fighting pirates in the Inland Sea. But after the Heiji Rebellion, he did something no samurai had done before: he entered the court and played the Fujiwara game. In 1167, he became **Daijō-daijin** (DYE-joh DYE-jin, Grand Minister), the highest government position in Japan — the first warrior ever to hold it. He married his daughter to the emperor, and when she bore a son, the infant was enthroned as Emperor Antoku (AHN-toh-koo). Kiyomori was doing exactly what the Fujiwara had done for two centuries: controlling the throne through marriage rather than seizing it.

But Kiyomori was not a Fujiwara. He was a warrior pretending to be a courtier, and the court resented him for it. His provincial warriors swaggered through Kyoto's refined neighborhoods. His family members occupied positions they were not qualified for. He moved the capital temporarily to Fukuhara (modern Kobe) to be closer to his maritime trade networks with Song China — a commercial venture that enriched the Taira but appalled the tradition-bound Kyoto aristocracy. Kiyomori's arrogance, and the Taira clan's rapid elevation from provincial soldiers to national rulers, created enemies everywhere: among the old Fujiwara courtiers, among rival warrior clans, among Buddhist monasteries whose lands the Taira had confiscated, and among the exiled and scattered remnants of the Minamoto.

---

In 1180, an imperial prince named Mochihito (MOH-chee-hee-toh) issued a call to arms against the Taira, and Minamoto no Yoritomo — who had spent twenty years in exile, building alliances and waiting for this moment — raised the banner of revolt in eastern Japan.

The **Genpei War** (GEN-pay, 1180-1185) is one of the great military narratives in world history, and it is the foundational epic of Japanese warrior culture. "Genpei" is a portmanteau of the Sino-Japanese readings of the two clan names — Gen(ji) for Minamoto and Hei(ke) for Taira. The war was fought across the length of Japan, from the eastern provinces to the western tip of Honshu, and it ended with the complete destruction of the Taira and the establishment of a new political order that would endure, in various forms, for nearly seven centuries.

Yoritomo was the strategist and politician — he stayed in his eastern base at Kamakura (kah-mah-KOO-rah), near modern Tokyo, organizing warriors, building alliances, and constructing the administrative apparatus of a new government. The actual fighting was done by his younger brother Yoshitsune, who turned out to be one of the most brilliant military commanders in Japanese history.

Yoshitsune's campaigns are the stuff of legend — literally. They were later romanticized in the **Tale of the Heike** (HAY-keh), the great medieval war epic that occupies roughly the same place in Japanese literature that the Iliad occupies in Western literature: a story of doomed warriors, tragic nobility, and the Buddhist truth that all glory is impermanent. The historical Yoshitsune was a tactical genius who won battles through audacity, speed, and a willingness to attack from directions that his enemies considered impossible.

At the **Battle of Ichi-no-Tani** (ee-chee-noh-TAH-nee) in 1184, Yoshitsune led a mounted charge down a near-vertical cliff face that the Taira considered impassable, attacking their fortified position from the rear while they were focused on a frontal assault. At the **Battle of Yashima** (yah-SHEE-mah), he attacked the Taira naval base from land during low tide. And at the final battle of the war, the **Battle of Dan-no-ura** (DAHN-noh-OO-rah) in 1185, Yoshitsune commanded the Minamoto fleet in a decisive naval engagement in the Shimonoseki Strait at the western tip of Honshu.

Dan-no-ura is the most famous battle in Japanese history. The Taira fleet, carrying the boy Emperor Antoku (Kiyomori's grandson, now eight years old) and the imperial regalia (the three sacred treasures that legitimized the emperor's authority — a mirror, a sword, and a jewel), faced the Minamoto fleet in a battle that turned when the tidal currents reversed, pushing the Taira ships into disarray. The Minamoto boarded and overwhelmed them. In the battle's most famous moment, the boy emperor's grandmother, the Taira matriarch Tokiko, took the child in her arms and leapt into the sea, drowning them both rather than allowing them to be captured. Other Taira followed. The sacred sword was lost to the waters of the strait and has never been recovered.

The Taira were destroyed. The few survivors who escaped the battle scattered into remote mountain villages, where their descendants, according to local tradition, lived in hiding for generations. Dan-no-ura entered Japanese cultural memory as the supreme example of the Buddhist concept of **mujō** (moo-JOH, "the impermanence of all things"): the Taira had risen from provincial warriors to rulers of Japan in a single generation, and they fell just as fast. The Tale of the Heike opens with a passage that every educated Japanese person can quote from memory: "The sound of the Gion Shōja bells echoes the impermanence of all things. The color of the sala flowers reveals the truth that the prosperous must decline."

---

The aftermath was swift and brutal. Yoritomo, who had orchestrated the war from Kamakura but never fought in it, was now the most powerful man in Japan. He rewarded his warrior followers with land grants and official positions. He established a new government in Kamakura, far from the court intrigues of Kyoto, that would later be formalized as the **Kamakura Shogunate** (SHOH-goo-naht, from shōgun, "generalissimo") — the first military government in Japanese history.

And then he turned on his own brother. Yoritomo was a politician, not a sentimentalist, and Yoshitsune's military genius and personal popularity made him a threat. Yoritomo accused Yoshitsune of insubordination, stripped him of his titles, and sent warriors to hunt him down. Yoshitsune fled north, was sheltered by allies, was betrayed, and in 1189 was cornered at a fort in the far north of Honshu, where he killed his wife and children and then committed suicide. He was thirty years old.

Yoshitsune became Japan's most tragic hero — the brilliant young warrior betrayed by his own brother, the embodiment of courage and loyalty destroyed by jealousy and political calculation. His story has been told and retold for eight centuries in plays, novels, films, and television dramas. In Japanese cultural tradition, there is even a term for the special sympathy directed toward a doomed, noble loser: **hōgan-biiki** (HOH-gahn BEE-kee, "sympathy for the lieutenant," from Yoshitsune's court title). Japan loves its tragic losers. Yoshitsune was the original.

---

The Genpei War marks the end of ancient Japan and the beginning of medieval Japan. The court in Kyoto continued to exist — the emperor continued to reign, courtiers continued to write poetry, and the Fujiwara continued to jockey for position — but real power had shifted permanently to the warriors. The Kamakura Shogunate would govern Japan for nearly 150 years, followed by two more shogunates (the Ashikaga and the Tokugawa), and the military government model would persist until the Meiji Restoration in 1868 — nearly seven hundred years of warrior rule.

The trajectory of Japanese civilization from this book's beginning to its end is extraordinary. In 14,000 BCE, the people of the Japanese archipelago were making pottery in a world without farming, without metals, without writing, without states. In 1185 CE, Japan had a literary tradition that included the world's first novel, a religious synthesis of Buddhism and Shinto that was unlike anything in the rest of Asia, architectural achievements (Hōryū-ji, Tōdai-ji, Byōdō-in) that rank among the finest in the world, and a political system that was about to pioneer something genuinely new — military government by a warrior class that ruled in the emperor's name but not under his control.

The central paradox of ancient Japan is that it borrowed everything and invented everything. Every major cultural element — writing, Buddhism, government institutions, metalworking, rice agriculture, city planning — arrived from outside, primarily from China via Korea. And yet the result was unmistakably, irreducibly Japanese. The Jōmon made pottery without farming. The Heian court created the novel without printing. The samurai built a government without overthrowing the emperor. At every stage, the Japanese took the received model and did something unexpected with it. Fourteen thousand years of doing things differently.

Cross-civ check: the Genpei War (1180-1185) is almost exactly contemporary with the Third Crusade in the Holy Land (1189-1192), where Richard the Lionheart and Saladin were fighting for control of Jerusalem. In China, the Southern Song Dynasty was in its middle period, culturally brilliant but militarily weak, about to be conquered by the Mongols. In Korea, the Goryeo dynasty was entering its own period of military government (the Choe military regime, beginning in 1170). The parallel with Korea is especially striking: both Japan and Korea developed military governments at almost exactly the same time, for almost exactly the same reasons — court cultures that had grown too refined and too weak to govern without warriors, warriors who discovered they could govern without the court.

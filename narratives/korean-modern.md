# Modern Korea — From Annexation to K-Wave

**Goal:** Narrative for Modern Korea (1910–2024 CE), the direct sequel to Joseon Korea. Across just 114 years the peninsula went through three of the most extreme political transformations of any country in modern history: 35 years as a Japanese colony, three years of total war that killed roughly three million people, and a permanent split into two hostile states whose subsequent paths diverged so completely that they now look like a controlled experiment in modern political economy. South Korea went from per-capita income lower than Ghana's in 1960 to OECD membership by 1996 to global cultural soft-power leadership by the 2020s. North Korea went from the more industrialized half of the peninsula in 1953 to a totalitarian dynastic state holding nuclear weapons. This is the story of how that happened — and of what Korean democracy has actually become.

These are not symmetrical regimes. The contrast is deliberately stark in this book and is also the historical truth.

# Chapter 1 — The Annexation Era

The Joseon dynasty ended at the stroke of a pen on August 22, 1910 (Joseon Korea Ch 8). The Japan-Korea Annexation Treaty, signed by the Korean prime minister **Yi Wan-yong** under Japanese duress and ratified by Resident-General **Terauchi Masatake**, formally transferred sovereignty over Korea to the Japanese empire. The 518-year-old Joseon royal family was reduced to a pensioned-off branch of the Japanese imperial house. Korea ceased to exist as an independent state and became, for the next thirty-five years, the Japanese colony of **Chōsen** (the Japanese pronunciation of "Joseon").

This chapter is about what those thirty-five years actually contained. The colonial period is the launching point for everything else in the book — the Korean state that emerged in 1948 was shaped, in opposition, by what the colonial regime had imposed; the people who built modern Korea had been raised under it; the wounds it left in Korean–Japanese relations are still open.

---

The Japanese colonial regime in Korea went through three rough phases.

The first phase, **military rule** (1910–1919), was outright military government. The **Governor-General** of Chōsen was always a senior Japanese army or navy officer, headquartered in a massive granite-and-marble Western-style building that the colonial regime erected directly in front of **Gyeongbokgung** (GYUNG-bohk-goong), the Joseon royal palace in Seoul. The placement was deliberate: walking up to the palace from the main southern boulevard, you were forced to walk around the colonial headquarters first. The architectural symbolism was the political program in miniature — the Joseon order had been swallowed and was now being literally physically displaced.

The Governor-General had absolute authority. Korean political parties were banned. Korean newspapers (except a handful of Japanese-controlled ones) were shut down. Korean teachers were replaced by Japanese ones in many schools. The Japanese gendarmerie patrolled rural areas with the legal power to flog Koreans on the spot for minor offenses. Resistance was met with summary execution and collective punishment.

Two specific colonial projects from this first phase deserve attention.

The **Land Survey of 1912–1918** was a comprehensive cadastral survey of all agricultural land on the peninsula. On paper it was a modernization measure — replacing Joseon-era informal land tenure with formal title registration. In practice it dispossessed huge numbers of Korean smallholders, because customary tenure that had operated for generations under the Joseon system did not produce paper documents that satisfied Japanese bureaucratic requirements. Land that could not be claimed under the new rules went to the colonial government, which then sold or leased it to Japanese settlers and to the **Oriental Development Company** (the colonial-era equivalent of the British East India Company, founded in 1908 to promote Japanese settlement and commercial development in Korea). By the late 1910s, roughly 40 percent of all arable land in Korea was owned by either the colonial state or Japanese settlers, and the vast majority of Korean farmers had become tenants on land their grandparents had owned.

The other early project was the **suppression of the Korean independence movement** that had been growing since the 1890s. Patriotic Korean societies were dissolved or driven underground. Independence activists were imprisoned, tortured, or fled abroad — to Manchuria, to Vladivostok, to Hawaii, to Shanghai. The most famous of the early exile communities formed in **Manchuria**, where former Joseon officers and yangban refugees built up partisan units that would harass Japanese forces along the Korean–Manchurian border for the next thirty years. The most famous independence martyr from this early period, **An Jung-geun** (Joseon Korea Ch 8), had assassinated **Ito Hirobumi** in Harbin in 1909; he was hanged in March 1910 just months before the formal annexation, and became the canonical Korean nationalist hero of the colonial era.

---

The second phase began in 1919, in response to the largest mass uprising in Korean history.

On March 1, 1919, thirty-three Korean nationalists — sixteen Christians, fifteen members of the Korean **Cheondogyo** religion (CHUN-doh-gyoh, "Religion of the Heavenly Way," a successor of the Donghak movement, Joseon Korea Ch 8), and two Buddhists — gathered at a restaurant in central Seoul and read out a **Declaration of Independence** drafted largely by the writer **Choe Nam-seon** (CHOY NAHM-sun). The declaration was lyrical in tone, demanding Korean self-determination on the principles announced by US President Woodrow Wilson at the Paris Peace Conference, and explicitly rejecting violent retaliation against Japan. The thirty-three signatories then telephoned the Japanese police and asked to be arrested.

The genius of the **March 1st Movement** was what happened next. A young schoolteacher in central Seoul read out the declaration to a crowd at **Pagoda Park**. The crowd took up the call. Within hours, parallel demonstrations were happening in dozens of cities across the peninsula. Within days, an estimated two million Koreans — roughly a tenth of the population — had taken part in protests, marches, and petition drives demanding independence. Korean students walked out of schools. Korean workers walked off jobs. Korean villagers gathered outside Japanese police stations chanting **"Daehan Dongnip Manse!"** (DAY-hahn DOHNG-nip MAHN-seh, "Long Live Korean Independence!"), the slogan that would become the rallying cry of the entire colonial-era resistance.

The Japanese response was savage. Police and military fired into crowds, ran over demonstrators with cavalry, and conducted mass arrests. Roughly 7,500 Koreans were killed and 15,000 wounded in the suppression, with another 45,000 arrested. The most notorious single incident was at the village of **Jeam-ri** (JEH-ahm-ree), in Suwon, where Japanese soldiers locked twenty-nine villagers inside a Christian church and burned it down, then gunned down anyone trying to escape.

The March 1st Movement did not achieve Korean independence. It did achieve three other things that mattered.

First, it forced the colonial regime to abandon outright military rule. The new Governor-General, Admiral **Saito Makoto** (sai-TOH mah-koh-TOH), introduced a much-publicized **"Cultural Rule"** policy in 1920 that allowed limited Korean-language newspapers, partially restored Korean schools, and softened the most overt symbols of military occupation. The substance of colonial rule did not change — Japanese economic exploitation continued, the surveillance state intensified — but the public face of the regime was less openly brutal for the next decade and a half.

Second, the movement produced the **Korean Provisional Government** in **Shanghai** (Korean: *Daehan Minguk Imsi Jeongbu*). Founded in April 1919 by Korean exiles in the French Concession of Shanghai, with **Syngman Rhee** (SING-man REE, 1875–1965) as its first President, it served as the institutional anchor of the independence movement for the next twenty-six years. The Provisional Government would relocate repeatedly — to Hangzhou, to Nanjing, to Changsha, eventually to Chongqing during the war — but it persisted, drafted Korean constitutions in exile, raised funds from Korean diaspora communities, and organized assassination cells and small-scale armed resistance. Its army-in-exile, the **Korean Liberation Army**, fought alongside Chinese Nationalist forces during the Second Sino-Japanese War. When liberation came in 1945, the Provisional Government would have legitimate (though contested) claim to be the Korean state — a claim that would matter enormously in the south's politics.

Third, March 1, 1919 became the symbolic founding moment of modern Korean nationalism. In modern South Korea, **March 1st** is a national holiday (*Samiljeol*). The current South Korean Constitution explicitly identifies the Provisional Government as the legal predecessor of the modern Republic of Korea. Even North Korea, which has spent seventy-five years constructing an alternative founding mythology centered on Kim Il-sung's Manchurian guerrilla activities (Chapter 4), still officially commemorates the March 1st anniversary. It is one of the few historical events both Koreas agree was the start of something.

---

The cultural rule period from 1920 to 1937 was the closest the colonial regime ever came to letting Koreans live recognizably Korean lives. Korean-language newspapers like the **Dong-A Ilbo** and the **Chosun Ilbo** (both still major papers in modern South Korea) began publishing in 1920. Korean literature flourished — modernist writers like **Yi Sang** (YEE SAHNG) and **Kim Yu-jeong** (KIM YOO-jung) published experimental fiction in Korean; the poet **Yun Dong-ju** (YOON DOHNG-joo) wrote the spare introspective verse that he would have published if he had not died in a Japanese prison in 1945. Korean Christianity grew rapidly during this period — the missionary networks established under the late Joseon (Joseon Korea Ch 8) expanded into a substantial Protestant minority with deep grassroots organization. The **Cheondogyo** religion mentioned above, the **Won Buddhism** movement (a reformist Buddhist sect founded in 1916), and a revival of indigenous **Korean shamanism** all expanded.

The cultural rule was also accompanied by deliberate Japanese economic restructuring of Korea. The colonial state built railways — the **Gyeongbu** line connecting Seoul to Pusan, the **Gyeongui** line connecting Seoul to Sinuiju on the Yalu River, the **Gyeongwon** line crossing east to Wonsan. By 1945, Korea had one of the densest railway networks in Asia outside of Japan itself. The state built ports, bridges, irrigation works, and electrical grids. It built coal mines and chemical plants in the resource-rich north. It built textile mills and small-scale manufacturing in the south.

It is important to be honest about what this infrastructure was and was not. Japanese colonial economic development in Korea was substantial in absolute terms. By the late 1930s, Korea was probably the most industrialized place in Asia outside of Japan proper. The railways, the ports, the electrical grid, the chemical plants — these all existed because the Japanese state built them.

It is equally important to be honest about why. The infrastructure was built to serve the Japanese empire, not to serve Korea. Korean rice was extracted from Korean farms at below-market prices and shipped to Japan; Korean industrial output served Japanese strategic needs; Korean labor was conscripted at colonial wages. The railways ran from the interior to the export ports; they did not connect Korean regions to each other. Korean entry into the colonial professional and managerial class was sharply limited; Japanese settlers staffed the upper levels of every important institution. Per-capita Korean consumption stagnated or declined during the colonial period despite the headline economic growth, because the growth was extractive.

Calling the colonial economy a "modernization" misstates the relationship. It was an extractive industrialization with some side benefits, conducted on terms that the Korean population had no voice in setting. The infrastructure was real; the political economy that produced it was a foreign imperial project.

The closest cross-civilizational parallel is **British colonial India** (medieval-india Ch 8 not yet shipped — see Mughal Empire and the late-Ottoman pattern, Ottoman Empire Ch 9). British India also built railways, ports, irrigation works, and a modern bureaucracy. India also exported cheap raw materials to the imperial center and was kept industrially constrained. India also produced a Western-educated indigenous elite that would lead its independence movement. Korea's colonial experience differed from India's in two respects: it was much shorter (35 years versus roughly 190), and Japan was an Asian neighboring power whose stated ideology was racial-civilizational kinship with Korea — making the claim that Korea was being "improved" particularly galling, because Japan was claiming brotherhood while extracting wealth as if from a stranger.

---

The third phase of colonial rule, from 1937 to 1945, was the most brutal.

When Japan invaded China in July 1937 (the Second Sino-Japanese War), Korea was militarized as a forward base. The colonial regime abandoned Cultural Rule and launched a deliberate program of **forced assimilation** (Japanese: *naisen ittai*, "Japan and Korea as one body"). The goal was no longer to manage a foreign Korean population — it was to absorb Koreans into the Japanese imperial body politic, on Japan's terms.

The assimilation program had several strands:

**Name changes (Sōshi-kaimei):** Beginning in 1939, Koreans were pressured (in many cases legally required) to adopt Japanese-style family names. Approximately 80 percent of the Korean population formally registered Japanese names. This was not just symbolic harassment — Koreans without Japanese names found it increasingly difficult to enroll children in school, hold government employment, or travel.

**Language suppression:** Korean was banned from public schools after 1938. Korean-language newspapers were shut down (the *Dong-A Ilbo* and *Chosun Ilbo* were forcibly closed in 1940). Speaking Korean in school could result in beatings; Japanese was the only permitted language of instruction. Generations of Koreans grew up partially illiterate in their own language.

**Shinto enforcement:** Koreans were required to worship at Japanese **Shinto** shrines, which the colonial state built across Korea — including a massive **Chōsen Shrine** complex on the slopes of **Namsan** mountain in central Seoul. Christian churches and Buddhist temples were pressured to add Shinto rituals to their services. Religious resistance was punishable as treason.

**Wartime forced labor (Chōyō):** From 1939, the Imperial Japanese government conscripted Korean men as industrial laborers in mines, factories, and construction projects across the Japanese empire. Estimates of the total number of Korean conscripts vary widely (modern South Korean government figures suggest roughly 800,000), and conditions were often brutal. The most notorious site is **Hashima Island**, an undersea coal mine off Nagasaki where Korean conscripts were worked in conditions survivors described as a prison camp. Hashima was added to UNESCO's World Heritage list in 2015 over Korean objections about the lack of acknowledgment of forced-labor history.

**The comfort women system:** From 1937, the Imperial Japanese Army established a network of military-supervised brothels — euphemistically called "comfort stations" (Japanese: *ianjo*) — across occupied Asia, staffed by women coerced or deceived into sexual slavery. Estimates of the total number of women run from 50,000 to 200,000; the majority were Korean (other women were Chinese, Filipina, Dutch-Indonesian, Japanese). The recruitment methods varied — some women were lured by promises of factory work, some were drafted into nominal labor service that turned out to be sexual slavery, some were straightforwardly kidnapped. Many died. Survivors who returned home faced lifelong stigma in conservative Korean society, and most never spoke publicly about what had happened to them until the early 1990s, when Korean activists organized the **Wednesday Demonstrations** outside the Japanese Embassy in Seoul that have continued every Wednesday for over thirty years.

The **comfort women** issue has become the most contentious single point in contemporary Japan-Korea relations. The Japanese government has at various points apologized (the **Kono Statement** of 1993, the **2015 Comfort Women Agreement** between Park Geun-hye's government and Shinzo Abe), but the apologies have been criticized in Korea as insufficient or politically conditional, and successive Japanese conservative politicians have publicly questioned the wartime army's responsibility — undermining whatever apologetic momentum the official statements achieved. The South Korean Constitutional Court ruled in 2011 that the Korean government had a constitutional duty to negotiate compensation for surviving comfort women on their behalf, and the issue continues to flare in inter-state relations every few years.

The historical record on the comfort women system is unambiguous. Imperial Japanese military documents demanding the establishment of comfort stations have survived. Veterans of the Japanese army, including some senior officers, have given on-record testimony about how the system operated. Korean and other Asian survivors began coming forward in the early 1990s and gave detailed corroborating accounts. The remaining historical disputes are over scale, over the precise legal status of recruitment (forced versus contracted), and over which civilian intermediaries were involved in coercion. The bare fact that the Imperial Japanese Army systematically organized military sexual slavery on a massive scale during World War II is not historically contested. Public denials are political positioning, not historical revisionism.

---

By 1945, the colonial regime had become a war economy in extremis. Korean rice was being shipped to Japan in such quantities that Korea's per-capita food consumption had collapsed; Korean men were being drafted into the Imperial Japanese Army as well as into forced labor; Korean textile mills had been converted to military uniform production; Korean students were being sent to die in Japanese-flagged warplanes as kamikaze pilots. Approximately 200,000 Koreans were serving in the Japanese armed forces by the war's end.

When American B-29s firebombed Tokyo in March 1945 and atomic bombs hit Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August, roughly 70,000 of the casualties were Koreans — wartime forced laborers and conscripts who had been brought to Japan precisely because they could be worked harder than Japanese citizens. The Korean atomic-bomb survivors, the **Hibakusha** in their Korean form, are an under-told story of the colonial period. Many of them remained in Japan after the war as part of the modern **Zainichi Korean** community.

Japan's surrender on August 15, 1945 ended thirty-five years of colonial rule. The end was abrupt. On August 14 the Korean population was officially still loyal subjects of the Japanese emperor; on August 16 the Japanese garrison was negotiating with Korean civic committees about how to keep order during the transition. Japanese settlers across the peninsula — there were nearly 800,000 of them in 1945 — packed up and either fled south to be repatriated through Pusan or, in the north, ran into Soviet occupation forces and lost everything they had built. The collapse of colonial rule, when it came, came in days.

But Korea did not get to be free. Two foreign powers had already decided otherwise.

That is where the next chapter begins.

# Chapter 2 — Liberation, Division, War

The events between Japan's August 1945 surrender and the July 1953 Korean War armistice are among the most consequential and most miscalibrated diplomatic and military episodes of the twentieth century. Korea was liberated, then divided, then plunged into a civil war that drew in the United States and China, killed roughly three million people on a peninsula of thirty million, and ended in a ceasefire line almost identical to the one it had started from. The two Koreas that we have today are the direct products of those eight years.

---

The **division at the 38th parallel** was decided in approximately thirty minutes.

It was August 10, 1945. Japan had not yet surrendered, but the Soviet Union had declared war two days earlier and Soviet forces were already entering northern Korea. American officials in Washington, scrambling to figure out which parts of the Japanese empire the United States could occupy before Soviet troops got there, asked two junior army officers — Colonels **Dean Rusk** (later US Secretary of State under Kennedy and Johnson) and **Charles Bonesteel III** — to find a line that would put the Korean capital of Seoul in the American zone. Rusk and Bonesteel found a National Geographic map, looked for a parallel of latitude that would do the job, and picked the **38th parallel north**. The line cut Korea roughly in half, with about two-thirds of the population in the southern American zone and the more industrialized north under Soviet occupation.

The Soviets accepted the proposal — partly because Stalin was being relatively cooperative in the immediate post-war moment, partly because the American zone was significantly smaller than the Soviets could have insisted on. American troops landed at **Incheon** on September 8, 1945. The 38th parallel was supposed to be a temporary administrative boundary for joint Soviet-American occupation; it was not supposed to be permanent.

It became permanent. The reasons were the standard Cold War reasons compressed into Korean particulars. The Americans and Soviets could not agree on the political composition of a unified Korean government — neither was willing to accept a regime aligned with the other side. The two-power **Joint Commission** set up to negotiate Korean unification met repeatedly between 1946 and 1947 and accomplished nothing. By late 1947 the United States had referred the Korean question to the United Nations General Assembly; the Soviet Union refused to allow UN-supervised elections in the northern zone; and by 1948 the two zones were on parallel tracks toward becoming separate states.

In the south, the **Republic of Korea** was proclaimed on August 15, 1948, with **Syngman Rhee** as its first president. Rhee — the same Syngman Rhee who had been president of the Provisional Government in Shanghai (Chapter 1) — was a Princeton-educated, English-fluent, virulently anti-communist nationalist who had spent the previous thirty years in American exile lobbying for Korean independence. He was also seventy-three years old, autocratic, and a poor administrator. The American occupation authorities backed him because he was the most prominent non-communist nationalist available; in retrospect this was a poor choice, but the alternative candidates were thin.

In the north, the **Democratic People's Republic of Korea** was proclaimed on September 9, 1948, with **Kim Il-sung** as premier. Kim Il-sung was thirty-six years old, the most capable of the Manchurian-guerrilla generation of Korean communists, and had been brought to Pyongyang in October 1945 in a Soviet uniform. He had spent the war years commanding a small Korean partisan unit on the Soviet-Manchurian border — under the name **Kim Song-ju** before he adopted the *nom de guerre* "Kim Il-sung," which he claimed had been used by an earlier legendary Korean guerrilla leader. The North Korean state would eventually erase the distinction between the legendary Kim Il-sung and the historical Kim Song-ju entirely, presenting the two as the same person.

Both states claimed sovereignty over the entire peninsula. Both states refused to recognize the other's existence. By the end of 1948, the United States had withdrawn most of its occupation forces from the south and the Soviet Union had withdrawn most of its forces from the north. Each Korean regime was responsible for its own security against the other. The peninsula had become a powder keg, and both sides were lobbying their respective superpowers for permission to settle the question by force.

---

Before the war, two episodes of mass political violence in the south set the tone of the new South Korean state.

The **Jeju Uprising** began in April 1948 on Jeju Island, off the southwestern coast. Local protests against UN-supervised southern elections (which Jeju residents saw, correctly, as institutionalizing the division of Korea) turned into armed insurgency by a small Communist guerrilla force. The South Korean government and the American occupation forces responded with a counter-insurgency campaign that killed roughly 30,000 people — about 10 percent of the island's population — over two years. The killings included deliberate massacres of civilians whom the security forces accused of harboring rebels. The Jeju Uprising was officially unmentionable in South Korean public discourse for the next forty-five years; the South Korean president **Kim Dae-jung** made the first formal apology in 1998, and the death toll has been openly investigated only since the early 2000s.

The **Yeosu-Suncheon Rebellion** in October 1948 was a mutiny by South Korean soldiers who had been ordered to deploy to Jeju and refused. The mutineers seized the southern coastal cities of **Yeosu** and **Suncheon**, killed local right-wing officials, and held out for several weeks before being suppressed by the South Korean army. In the suppression and in subsequent purges of the army, several thousand more people were killed.

These episodes mattered for two reasons. First, they showed that the new South Korean state would suppress its leftists with extreme violence — a pattern that would continue through the Korean War and the dictatorship years and that would fundamentally shape who got to be a legitimate political actor in modern South Korea. Second, the Korean War narrative is sometimes told as a clean story of communist invasion versus democratic defense, when the reality on the ground was that both Korean states had been engaged in low-level civil war against domestic opponents for two years before June 1950.

The Korean War, when it came, was therefore both a sudden invasion and the escalation of a war that had already been going on.

---

On June 25, 1950, at four in the morning, North Korean forces crossed the 38th parallel in a coordinated invasion. **Kim Il-sung** had spent the previous twelve months lobbying both **Joseph Stalin** (in person, in Moscow, in spring 1949 and again in March 1950) and **Mao Zedong** (in Beijing, May 1950) for permission to attack south. Stalin had finally agreed in early 1950, judging that the United States would not intervene to defend South Korea — partly because Secretary of State Dean Acheson had given a January 1950 speech in which he conspicuously omitted Korea from the American defensive perimeter in East Asia. Stalin's calculation was wrong. The decision to invade was Kim Il-sung's; the green light was Stalin's; the Chinese, who would end up bearing most of the foreign costs, had not been told the date.

The invasion was a catastrophe for the South. The North Korean army was equipped with Soviet T-34 tanks and trained by Soviet advisors; the South Korean army was a lightly armed constabulary force with no tanks and no anti-tank weapons. **Seoul** fell on June 28. By August, North Korean troops controlled approximately 90 percent of the peninsula and had pushed the South Korean army and the small American contingent into a defensive enclave around the southeastern port of **Pusan** — the **Pusan Perimeter** — which held only because of urgent American reinforcement, sea and air supremacy, and the topography of the Naktong River.

The United Nations Security Council, exploiting a Soviet boycott of the council (the Soviets were protesting that the Republic of China rather than the People's Republic was occupying the China seat), passed two resolutions in late June authorizing military intervention to repel the invasion. The intervention was mostly American — the United States provided about 90 percent of the foreign forces — but contingents from sixteen other countries (including Britain, Australia, Canada, Turkey, the Philippines, France, the Netherlands, Greece, and Ethiopia) participated, giving the Korean War the formal status of the first United Nations military operation. The American supreme commander was **General Douglas MacArthur**, the same MacArthur who had run the Allied occupation of Japan since 1945.

---

On September 15, 1950, MacArthur ordered an amphibious assault at **Incheon**, the port city on Korea's west coast about 150 miles behind the North Korean front line. The Incheon landing was militarily audacious — Incheon had massive tides (a 32-foot tidal range, among the largest on Earth, leaving very narrow windows when landing craft could approach the harbor) and was thought to be a near-impossible amphibious target. MacArthur insisted on it precisely because it was unexpected.

The landing succeeded. American Marines went ashore against light resistance, advanced rapidly inland, recaptured Seoul within two weeks, and severed the supply lines of the North Korean army still besieging the Pusan Perimeter. The North Korean army collapsed. By early October, UN forces had pushed past the 38th parallel into North Korea itself. **Pyongyang** fell on October 19. American troops reached the **Yalu River**, the border between Korea and Chinese Manchuria, in late October.

This was the high-water mark of the war for the South. It was also the moment MacArthur made his disastrous miscalculation. Chinese leader **Mao Zedong** had decided in early October that he could not allow American forces to consolidate control over a unified Korea hostile to China. The Chinese government had communicated this through the Indian ambassador K. M. Panikkar, who had relayed the warning to the United States. MacArthur and his intelligence chief, General Charles Willoughby, dismissed the warnings as bluff. They were not.

In late October 1950, approximately 300,000 troops of the Chinese **People's Volunteer Army** (Mao's euphemism for an undeclared intervention) crossed the Yalu River at night, infiltrated into the mountains of northern Korea, and waited. On November 25, they attacked. The Chinese intervention drove UN forces into the most catastrophic American military reverse since the surrender at Bataan. American Marines withdrew from the **Chosin Reservoir** (the **Battle of the Chosin Reservoir**, late November–December 1950) in subzero conditions through Chinese ambushes, abandoning equipment and dragging frostbitten wounded. UN lines fell back, Pyongyang was abandoned, Seoul was recaptured by Chinese and North Korean forces in January 1951.

This was the largest moment in modern Chinese military history before 1979. China had defeated the world's leading military power in a major land war and was now positioned as the dominant military force in East Asia — a position it had not held since the early Qing dynasty (Joseon Korea Ch 5).

---

The war ground on for two and a half more years.

UN forces under MacArthur's successor, **General Matthew Ridgway**, halted the Chinese advance by spring 1951 and recaptured Seoul (for the fourth time in eleven months). By June 1951 the front had stabilized along a line very close to the 38th parallel. From there the war became a slogging war of position — trench warfare reminiscent of the Western Front in 1916–1918, with bombardment, raids, and small-unit attacks across mountains both sides held with about equal stubbornness.

Two political episodes during the trench-war phase deserve mention.

**MacArthur was fired** in April 1951. The general had been openly campaigning for permission to expand the war into China itself — bombing Chinese cities, blockading the Chinese coast, using nuclear weapons, encouraging Nationalist Chinese forces on Taiwan to invade the mainland. President **Harry Truman** dismissed him, asserting the principle of civilian control over military strategy at the cost of his own popularity (MacArthur returned to a hero's welcome in the United States; Truman's approval rating dropped into the twenties). The dismissal was a quiet triumph for the constitutional separation of powers. A general acting on MacArthur's preferences in 1951 could conceivably have started a third world war.

**Truce talks** began in July 1951 at the village of **Kaesong** (later moved to **Panmunjom**) and dragged on for two years before producing an armistice. The talks were stalled mostly over the question of prisoner-of-war repatriation — North Korea demanded that all Chinese and Korean POWs be returned to communist control; the United States insisted that POWs who did not want to return should be allowed to defect. The eventual resolution allowed roughly 22,000 Chinese and Korean POWs to choose not to be repatriated, most of whom went to Taiwan or Brazil or elsewhere, in what was a substantial Cold War propaganda victory for the United States.

The **Korean War armistice** was finally signed on July 27, 1953, at Panmunjom. The signers were a North Korean general, a Chinese general, and an American general acting for the United Nations Command. Notably, no representative of South Korea signed; President **Syngman Rhee**, who wanted the war to continue until full unification, refused to authorize South Korean participation in the armistice. The armistice established a **Demilitarized Zone (DMZ)** roughly 2.5 miles wide running 155 miles across the peninsula along the existing line of contact — close to the 38th parallel but not identical to it. The armistice was a ceasefire, not a peace treaty. The two Koreas, technically, are still at war.

---

The Korean War's casualties are difficult to count precisely. Mainstream estimates put total fatalities at approximately three million people on a peninsula of thirty million — roughly 10 percent of the population. Of these, perhaps 600,000 to 1 million were South Korean civilians; another 1.5 million were North Korean civilians (the heaviest losses were in the north because the United States Air Force flattened it — by 1952 American bombers had run out of significant urban targets in North Korea and were bombing villages); roughly 215,000 were South Korean military, 400,000 North Korean military, 600,000 Chinese military, 36,000 American military, and several thousand other UN coalition military. The northern half of the peninsula was so comprehensively destroyed by the air campaign that General Curtis LeMay later estimated 20 percent of the North Korean population had been killed.

The physical destruction of Korea was extreme. Both Pyongyang and Seoul had each been captured and recaptured four times. Most major Korean cities were ruins by 1953. Per-capita GDP in both Koreas was lower in 1953 than at any point under Japanese rule. The northern half was more thoroughly bombed but had inherited the more industrialized colonial-era infrastructure; the southern half had less destruction but less to start with. Both were among the poorest countries on Earth.

This is the starting point for everything that follows. The two Koreas of the 1950s were both impoverished agricultural societies, both ruled by authoritarian governments, both carrying the trauma of fratricidal warfare, both surrounded by foreign garrisons (American troops in the south, Chinese troops in the north until 1958). They diverged later. They started in roughly the same place.

A cross-civilizational note: the Korean War was the first proxy war of the Cold War, and the only one in which American and Chinese troops directly fought each other. It also produced the first United Nations-flagged military intervention in modern history, established the precedent that East Asian regional conflicts could draw in nuclear-armed powers, and locked the Sino-American relationship into a mode of military hostility that would not be substantially modified until **Nixon's visit to China** in 1972 (Chapter 4). Compared to the previous American war (World War II) the Korean War is strangely under-remembered — there is no Korean War equivalent of the World War II memorial culture in American public memory, even though Korea killed more Americans per year of fighting than Vietnam later would. The forgetfulness is in itself a historical fact about American culture and one that Korean Americans have remarked on for decades.

---

Syngman Rhee survived the war as president and stayed in office for another seven years, ruling South Korea with mounting authoritarianism. He rigged the 1952 and 1956 elections, persecuted opposition politicians, and oversaw an economy that depended almost entirely on American foreign aid (which was running at about 14 percent of South Korean GDP in the late 1950s). In March 1960, Rhee's government rigged the vice-presidential election so blatantly that students poured into the streets in protest. The body of a high school student named **Kim Ju-yul**, killed by police and dumped in Masan Harbor, was recovered several weeks later — and triggered a nationwide student-led uprising known as the **April Revolution**. After several days of mass protest in central Seoul that left roughly 180 demonstrators dead, the United States embassy made it clear to Rhee that he no longer had American support, and the eighty-five-year-old president resigned and fled into exile in Hawaii.

The brief **Second Republic** (1960–61) that followed was a parliamentary democracy. It might have produced a stable Korean democratic system if it had been allowed to develop. It was not.

That is where the next chapter starts.

# Chapter 3 — Park Chung-hee and the Miracle on the Han

On the morning of May 16, 1961, a column of South Korean Marines and paratroopers crossed the Han River into central Seoul and seized the major government buildings. The coup was led by **Major General Park Chung-hee** (PARK chung-HEE, 1917–1979), a forty-three-year-old officer with an obscure pre-1945 history, a Communist Party affiliation he had narrowly survived during a 1948 army purge, and a political program of state-led national reconstruction that he had been developing in writing for years.

For the next eighteen years, Park would run South Korea. He would also engineer the most dramatic economic transformation any country has ever achieved over a comparable timespan. South Korea in 1961 was poorer than Ghana, South Sudan, or Liberia by per-capita income; by 1979 it was a middle-income industrial economy with global manufacturing exports. The transformation has a name: **the Miracle on the Han**, after the river that runs through Seoul. The cost has a name too: an authoritarian state that crushed political opposition, suppressed labor unions, tortured dissidents, and ruled by martial law and emergency decree.

Both ledgers are real. Both ledgers were the same project.

---

Park Chung-hee's biography is essential to understanding the politics of his rule.

He was born in 1917 in a poor village in **North Gyeongsang Province**, the seventh of seven children of a Joseon-era yangban family that had fallen on hard times. He attended Korean elementary school under the colonial regime, then trained as a teacher, then in 1940 enrolled in the **Manchukuo Imperial Military Academy** — the officer-training academy of the Japanese puppet state in Manchuria. He took a Japanese name (**Takagi Masao**) for his military service. He was commissioned as a junior officer in the **Manchukuo Imperial Army**, the Japanese-allied army that fought against Korean and Chinese resistance forces in Manchuria during World War II. He spent the last year of the war in the Manchukuo army; he was a colonial Korean serving the Japanese empire against fellow Koreans.

After Japan's surrender, Park returned to Korea, joined the new South Korean army, and was very nearly executed in 1948 in a purge of suspected Communist Party members within the officer corps. He had in fact joined a clandestine cell associated with the Yeosu-Suncheon Rebellion (Chapter 2). He saved his life by giving up the names of fellow Communist officers — an act that haunted his later political career. He was demoted and rehabilitated, served competently in the Korean War, and rose through the ranks of the postwar South Korean army to major general by 1961.

The reason this biography matters is that Park's political program was substantially shaped by what he had absorbed in Manchukuo. The Manchukuo state was a Japanese-backed experiment in **state-led developmentalism** — a model in which an authoritarian state used central economic planning, government-directed credit, infrastructure investment, and disciplined export-oriented industrialization to drive rapid catch-up with the West. The Manchukuo officials who designed this model — economists like **Hoshino Naoki** and bureaucrats like **Kishi Nobusuke** (who later returned to Tokyo and served as Japanese prime minister in the 1950s) — went on to design the postwar Japanese economic miracle. Park watched them at work as a junior officer. When he came to power in Korea, he applied a recognizably similar model, with adaptations: a state that picked national champions, allocated cheap credit through state-controlled banks, protected the domestic market, and pushed exports relentlessly.

The Korean economic miracle was, in this sense, the third wave of the Japanese-developed state-led developmentalist model — applied first to Japan itself, then to Manchukuo, then to Korea. The political ideology was not Communist (Park had renounced that affiliation). It was not free-market liberalism (Park found American free-market advisors useless). It was a third alternative: authoritarian state-directed industrial capitalism with a single-party-dominant political system. Korea's later democratic transition (Chapter 5) did not arrive as the gift of the developmentalist state; it arrived against the developmentalist state.

---

The mechanism of the Miracle on the Han was the **chaebol** (CHAY-bol, Korean: 재벌, "rich family") system.

A chaebol is a family-controlled industrial conglomerate, structured as a holding company with cross-shareholdings linking dozens of operating subsidiaries across multiple industries. The Park-era state did not so much create the chaebol as create the conditions in which a few of them could grow enormously fast.

The mechanism worked like this. The Park government identified a small number of strategic export industries — initially light manufacturing (textiles, plywood, wigs), then heavier industry (cement, steel, shipbuilding), then chemicals and electronics, eventually semiconductors and automobiles. It directed the state-owned banking system to lend at concessional rates to a small number of favored conglomerates that promised to enter those industries. It guaranteed export markets through trade agreements with the United States and Japan. It restricted imports so the chaebol could dominate the domestic market without foreign competition. It set ambitious export targets and rewarded conglomerates that hit them with more loans, more favored treatment, and more lucrative state contracts. It punished underperformance — chaebol that failed to deliver lost their political favor and could be forced into bankruptcy or absorbed into competitors.

The result was a dramatic concentration of industrial production in a small number of family-controlled groups: **Samsung** under the Lee family, **Hyundai** under the Chung family, **LG** (originally **Lucky Goldstar**) under the Koo family, **Daewoo** under the Kim family, **Lotte** under the Shin family, **SK** (originally **Sunkyong**) under the Chey family. These conglomerates and a handful of others dominated the Korean economy by the late 1970s. They still dominate it today — Samsung alone accounts for roughly 20 percent of South Korean stock market capitalization in the 2020s.

The chaebol system worked spectacularly well by macroeconomic measures. South Korean GDP grew at an average rate of about 9 percent annually from 1961 to 1996 — the longest sustained high-growth episode of any country in modern history except possibly post-1978 China. Per-capita income rose from $80 in 1961 (in current dollars, lower than per-capita income in Sub-Saharan Africa) to $11,000 in 1996. South Korea was admitted to the **OECD** in 1996, formally graduating into the developed-economy club — the first non-Western, non-island state to do so.

It also worked through systematic compression of labor rights, environmental standards, and political freedom. Korean workers in the export factories of the 1960s and 1970s worked twelve-hour shifts, six days a week, for wages that were a small fraction of equivalent Japanese or American wages. Independent labor unions were illegal or suppressed; the labor activist **Jeon Tae-il** (JUN TAY-il) burned himself to death in 1970 outside a Seoul textile factory while shouting "**We are not machines**" — an incident that became foundational for the Korean labor movement when it eventually emerged. Pollution from the chemical and steel plants devastated rivers and air quality. Anyone who organized against any of this was harassed, fired, blacklisted, or imprisoned. The South Korean intelligence agency, the **Korean Central Intelligence Agency (KCIA)**, became one of the most powerful institutions in the country.

---

Two specific projects from the Park era deserve attention.

The **1965 Treaty of Basic Relations with Japan** normalized diplomatic relations between South Korea and the country that had colonized it twenty years earlier. The treaty was deeply unpopular — university students staged the largest protests Park had yet faced, and his government had to call in the army to suppress demonstrations. The terms were politically humiliating: Japan paid $300 million in grants and $200 million in low-interest loans (later increased) as economic assistance, but did not formally apologize for the colonial period and did not separately compensate individual Korean victims of forced labor or sexual slavery (the Japanese position was that the lump-sum payment to the Korean government settled all wartime claims; this position is still contested in the 2020s).

Park accepted the treaty for economic reasons. The Japanese aid and the unblocked Japanese investment that followed were essential to the early phase of the Miracle. **POSCO**, the Pohang Iron and Steel Company that became one of the world's largest steel producers, was built mostly with Japanese reparations money under the leadership of former general **Park Tae-jun**. The Gyeongbu Expressway, the first expressway in Korea, was financed partly with Japanese loans. The 1965 treaty was a calculated trade of nationalist sentiment for industrial capacity, and Park believed correctly that the economic benefits would outweigh the political costs in the long run.

The **deployment of Korean forces to Vietnam** (1965–1973) was the second large external intervention. The United States paid the South Korean government to deploy roughly 320,000 troops to support the American war effort in Vietnam — the second-largest foreign contingent after the Americans themselves. The Korean **Tiger** and **White Horse** divisions developed reputations in Vietnam for combat effectiveness and for harshness toward civilians; specific massacres of Vietnamese villages by Korean troops have been investigated since the 1990s. Roughly 5,000 Korean soldiers died in Vietnam.

The Vietnam deployment served three purposes for Park. It cemented the alliance with the United States by getting Korean troops into a war the Americans needed help fighting. It provided combat experience and modern equipment for the Korean army. And — crucially for the Miracle — it generated foreign currency. American payments for Korean military service, contracting work, and supply procurement amounted to roughly $1 billion over the war's duration, equivalent to several percent of annual South Korean GDP at the time. The Vietnam War is one of the unsung financiers of the Korean economic miracle.

A parallel rural-development project deserves a brief mention. The **Saemaul Undong** (SAY-mahl OON-dong, "New Village Movement"), launched by Park Chung-hee in 1970, was a state-led campaign to modernize the Korean countryside through standardized infrastructure projects (rural roads, replacing thatched roofs with tile, electrification) and a regime-friendly ethic of self-help and discipline. Saemaul Undong is sometimes celebrated in modern South Korean memory as a successful rural development model and is occasionally exported to developing countries; it is also remembered, more critically, as a tool of authoritarian social mobilization that demanded compulsory village participation under the gaze of party officials. Both readings are partly true.

Park's industrialization also drove a quiet religious revolution. South Korea's Protestant Christian population grew from a few percent at liberation to nearly 20 percent of the population by the 1980s, propelled by Pentecostal megachurches that offered a prosperity-gospel theology adapted to the urbanizing industrial workforce. **Yoido Full Gospel Church** (yoh-EE-doh) under pastor David Yonggi Cho grew into the largest single megachurch in the world, with peak claimed membership over 800,000 by the 1990s. South Korean Protestantism became one of the most evangelically active in any country and has since produced the second-largest Christian missionary force in the world after the United States, sending Korean missionaries to (among other places) Muslim-majority countries and to mainland China. The combination of Pentecostal Protestantism and Park-era industrial discipline is one of the more underexamined synergies in Korean modernization.

---

The political form of the Park era hardened over time.

Park was elected president (in fairly narrow elections) in 1963 and 1967. By the late 1960s the rise of opposition leader **Kim Dae-jung** (KIM DAY-joong, 1925–2009, future president, future Nobel laureate) was becoming a serious political threat. Kim Dae-jung, a Catholic from the southwestern Jeolla region, was running on a platform of liberal democracy and engagement with North Korea, and was a credible electoral threat to Park's continued rule.

Park responded with two escalating measures.

In the 1971 presidential election, Park defeated Kim Dae-jung by approximately 950,000 votes out of 12 million cast — a narrow margin in what was already a partially manipulated election. The closeness convinced Park that a normal democratic system would not let him keep ruling.

In October 1972, Park declared martial law, dissolved the National Assembly, and rammed through the **Yushin Constitution** (YOO-shin — the word is the same as the Japanese **Meiji Restoration**'s *Ishin*, meaning "renovation," and the parallel was deliberate — Meiji Japan Ch 1). The Yushin Constitution gave Park indefinite terms (he could be re-elected by an electoral college that he himself appointed); the power to appoint a third of the National Assembly directly; the authority to rule by emergency decree; and effective immunity from judicial review. South Korea ceased to be a democracy in any meaningful sense in October 1972 and became an open dictatorship.

The Yushin period (1972–79) was the most repressive of Park's rule. Political opposition was criminalized. Kim Dae-jung was kidnapped from a Tokyo hotel in 1973 by KCIA agents and was nearly killed before American pressure forced his release. Anti-Yushin demonstrations were brutally suppressed. The labor activist movement was systematically dismantled. Christian dissident clergy and academic critics were arrested in waves — the **People's Revolutionary Party Incident** of 1975, in which the South Korean government framed eight men as North Korean spies and executed them within twenty-four hours of their conviction, has since been formally retried and overturned, and is one of the cleanest cases of judicial murder in modern Korean history.

The Yushin Constitution was Park's confession that the Miracle on the Han had not produced the political legitimacy he had hoped for. Eighteen years of state-led growth had transformed the economy, but the population had not become uncritically grateful. Educated middle-class Koreans, university students, Christian dissidents, and Jeolla-region opposition voters all wanted political freedom alongside the prosperity. Park's response was to suppress them harder.

---

By 1979 the Park system was under multiple stresses. The Carter administration in the United States was actively pressuring South Korea on human rights. The opposition was reorganizing under Kim Young-sam (the future president, see Chapter 5). A textile strike at the **YH Trading Company** in August 1979 had been crushed in a particularly brutal raid that left a young female worker, **Kim Kyung-suk**, dead — and triggered student-led demonstrations in **Busan** and **Masan** in October 1979 that reignited the anti-Yushin movement on a national scale.

In the inner circle of the Park government, two factions argued over how to respond. The hardliner faction, led by Park's chief security advisor **Cha Ji-cheol**, wanted to crush the demonstrators with military force "even if 30,000 die." The moderate faction, led by the head of the KCIA **Kim Jae-gyu** (KIM JEH-gyoo), argued for political concessions to defuse the unrest.

On October 26, 1979, at a private dinner in a KCIA safehouse in central Seoul, Kim Jae-gyu shot and killed both Cha Ji-cheol and Park Chung-hee. He later testified that he had been acting to save Korean democracy from a regime sliding into total catastrophe; the prosecutor's office testified that he had acted out of personal frustration over his declining influence. The truth was probably some mixture. Kim Jae-gyu was tried for treason, convicted, and hanged in May 1980.

Park Chung-hee's eighteen-year rule ended in a way that Park himself had probably not expected — assassinated by his own intelligence chief at a private dinner. The man who had executed eight innocent activists in twenty-four hours had himself been executed in roughly the same timeframe.

A reckoning, before the next chapter takes over: the Park Chung-hee question is still the single most contested in modern South Korean political memory. The political right considers him a national hero who built the modern Korean state. The political left considers him a dictator who tortured Korean citizens to industrialize on the backs of Korean workers. Most actual historical opinion, including Park's own daughter's later political career (Chapter 8), has had to live with both ledgers being true at the same time. The miracle and the dictatorship were the same project; they cannot be separated. South Koreans still vote, every five years, partly on which side of that ledger they emphasize.

# Chapter 4 — Two Koreas Diverge

By the late 1970s, the two Koreas had begun to look extraordinarily different from each other. The southern half was an industrial economy moving toward middle-income status; the northern half was a militarized command economy with a personality cult that had no real parallel in the modern world. This chapter is about how that divergence happened and why it went so much further than anyone had expected in 1953.

The fundamental fact to start with: in 1953, the northern half of the peninsula was the more economically developed half. The colonial-era industrial infrastructure (Chapter 1) had been concentrated in the north — the chemical plants, the major hydroelectric dams, the heavy-machinery factories. North Korean per-capita income in 1960 was probably higher than South Korean. The North had natural resources (coal, iron, hydroelectric potential) that the South lacked. The North had a more disciplined party-state apparatus and a more capable initial leadership. The conventional Cold War expectation in the late 1950s, even in some Western analytical circles, was that the communist North would economically outperform the capitalist South.

The reverse happened. By 1980 South Korean per-capita income was roughly twice North Korean. By 2000 it was roughly fifteen times. By 2020 it was probably forty times — though the North Korean numbers are increasingly unreliable. The divergence was the product of two parallel political programs running for forty years, and it deserves examination as a near-controlled experiment.

---

Start with the North.

**Kim Il-sung** ruled North Korea from 1948 to 1994 — forty-six years, the longest-running personal autocracy of the twentieth century outside of Generalissimo Franco's Spain. The political program he constructed was a synthesis of three elements: orthodox Stalinism (which he had absorbed in the Soviet Union before 1945), Mao Zedong-style mass mobilization (which he picked up from the Chinese intervention during the Korean War), and traditional Korean Confucian filial-piety politics (refashioned as personal devotion to the leader). The synthesis was given a name in the 1960s: **Juche** (JOO-cheh, often translated as "self-reliance" but more literally "the master subject" or "the standing-on-one's-own").

Juche as a doctrine had several components.

Politically, Juche meant absolute leader-centered authority. The Kim family and the **Workers' Party of Korea** were the unique embodiments of the Korean people's revolutionary spirit; opposition to either was definitionally counter-revolutionary. Over time, the doctrine evolved further: Kim Il-sung was identified not just as the leader but as a quasi-divine figure whose biography (much of which was embellished or invented) constituted the moral and historical foundation of the state. North Korean schoolchildren memorized lengthy passages of his speeches; North Korean homes were required to display his portrait; North Korean adults were required to wear lapel pins with his image. The cult of personality was deeper than Stalin's or Mao's by the 1970s, and would deepen further under Kim Jong-il.

Economically, Juche meant near-total autarky. North Korea's economic plans aimed to reduce dependence on foreign trade to the maximum extent possible, even at the cost of efficiency. Heavy industry, military production, and self-sufficient agriculture were the priorities. The state ran nearly the entire economy through central planning; private enterprise above the household level did not exist.

Diplomatically, Juche meant attempting to pivot between the Soviet Union and China without becoming dependent on either. Kim Il-sung played the Sino-Soviet split (which broke into the open in the early 1960s) to extract aid from both sides while committing to neither. By the 1970s North Korea had also developed a **Non-Aligned Movement** diplomatic identity, posing as a leader of postcolonial Asian and African states.

Culturally, Juche meant Korean ethnic exceptionalism. The Korean people were presented as uniquely virtuous, uniquely victimized by foreign powers, uniquely capable of charting their own destiny. The cultural content of Juche became increasingly racially flavored over time — by the 2000s North Korean propaganda was making essentially racial claims about Korean ethnic purity that would have been recognizable to Imperial Japanese ideologues circa 1940.

The result of forty years of Juche application was a country in which the state controlled nearly every aspect of public life, the leader was an object of religious veneration, and the population was kept ignorant of conditions in the outside world. North Korea has often been compared to George Orwell's **1984**, a comparison that would be obviously unfair if it were not so obviously accurate in many particulars. The state in *1984* had Big Brother, the Two Minutes Hate, the Newspeak, the rewriting of history, the Ministry of Truth, the constant surveillance, the corrective labor camps; North Korea has the Eternal President (Kim Il-sung was formally declared "Eternal President" after his death in 1994 — the country has no living president), public mass denunciation rituals, a deliberately impoverished official vocabulary, the **Workers' Party History Institute** that retroactively edits the historical record, the **Ministry of People's Security**, the **State Security Department**, and the **kwanliso** (관리소) prison camps that hold an estimated 80,000–120,000 political prisoners under conditions historians have compared to the Soviet Gulag at its worst.

The North Korean prison camp system — the **kwanliso**, sometimes translated as "control and management places" — has been documented in detail by escaped prisoners and intelligence services since the early 2000s. Inmates are held for political offenses, for being related to political offenders (the doctrine of **three generations of punishment** holds that the families of dissidents are also incarcerated), or for attempting to flee the country. Conditions involve forced labor under starvation rations, public executions, systematic torture, and rates of in-camp mortality that are difficult to verify but appear extremely high. The kwanliso are not a peripheral feature of the North Korean state. They are a core institution of how the regime maintains control.

Calling North Korea a totalitarian state is not Cold War rhetoric; it is descriptive fact. Calling it a dynastic state is not Cold War rhetoric either — power has passed from Kim Il-sung to his eldest son Kim Jong-il (1994) to Kim Jong-il's third son Kim Jong-un (2011), all of whom have been treated with quasi-religious reverence by the state propaganda apparatus. The closest historical parallel is probably the late Tokugawa shogunate (Edo Japan Ch 7) — a hereditary dictatorship with elaborate official ceremonies and an immobilized population — except that the late Tokugawa system was a stable rural society, while the Kim system has presided over deliberate famine.

---

The famine deserves its own treatment, but it belongs to Chapter 7. For now: by the late 1980s and early 1990s, the North Korean system was already obviously economically failing. Soviet aid had been declining since the late 1980s; Soviet collapse in 1991 cut off most of what remained. Chinese aid was reduced in the early 1990s as Beijing pivoted toward South Korea. Agricultural productivity collapsed. The country slid toward famine.

The South Korean trajectory ran the other way.

The story of post-Park South Korea begins, brutally, in May 1980.

---

**Chun Doo-hwan** (CHUN doo-WAHN, 1931–2021) seized power in two stages.

The first stage was the **Coup of December 12, 1979**. Six weeks after Park Chung-hee's assassination, Major General Chun and his **Hanahoe** (HAH-nah-hweh, "One Mind Society") faction of younger officers used the army's investigative authority over the assassination to depose the existing army chief of staff, install themselves at the top of the military hierarchy, and consolidate control over the senior officer corps. The civilian acting president **Choi Kyu-hah** retained nominal office but had no actual power.

The second stage was the response to the **Seoul Spring** of early 1980. After Park's assassination, university students and civic groups had begun demanding rapid democratization — direct elections, the abolition of the Yushin Constitution, the legal rehabilitation of dissidents. By April 1980 the protests were enormous. Chun Doo-hwan's military faction decided to crush the democratic movement before it could consolidate.

On May 17, 1980, Chun extended martial law nationwide, banned political activity, closed universities, arrested opposition leaders Kim Dae-jung and **Kim Young-sam** and dozens of others, and dispatched **Special Forces** units to several cities to enforce the new order.

In **Gwangju** (GWANG-joo, the largest city in southwestern Jeolla Province and a stronghold of opposition support for Kim Dae-jung), the response went off the rails almost immediately.

---

The **Gwangju Uprising** ran from May 18 to May 27, 1980.

Gwangju students protesting the martial law extension on May 18 were attacked by South Korean Special Forces troops with extreme brutality — soldiers beating, bayonet-stabbing, and shooting unarmed protesters in the streets and on university campuses. The attacks, which appeared deliberately designed to terrorize the city, instead unified it. Within days, ordinary Gwangju residents were joining the protests in massive numbers. Citizens raided armories for rifles. By May 21, the Gwangju protesters had armed themselves, fought a pitched street battle with the Special Forces, and driven the troops out of the city.

For the next six days, Gwangju was a self-governing city under citizen control. Hospital workers volunteered around the clock. Residents organized food distribution. A **Citizens' Settlement Committee** attempted to negotiate a peaceful resolution with the Chun regime. The atmosphere in Gwangju during those six days has been described by survivors as something close to a moral utopia — total strangers helping each other, looters being chased away by their neighbors, a mass civic improvisation that worked.

On May 27, 1980, the Korean army retook the city. The final assault on the city hall, where the citizen militia had made its headquarters, killed roughly 200 to 300 people in a few hours. Total deaths during the ten days of the uprising have been officially counted at approximately 200, but local Gwangju estimates and academic studies place the total at 600 or higher. The lower estimate is the count of formally identified bodies; the higher estimates account for missing persons and bodies disposed of by the military.

The Chun government, which controlled the press, blamed the uprising on North Korean infiltration and Communist agitators. It executed several uprising leaders and imprisoned hundreds of others. Kim Dae-jung was sentenced to death (later commuted under American pressure) on charges of having instigated the rebellion from his prison cell, where he had been held since May 17.

The Gwangju Uprising became the moral foundation of South Korean democracy.

It worked that way for several reasons. The uprising had been a clear case of citizens taking arms to defend democratic principles against a military coup; the moral asymmetry was unambiguous. The Chun government's lies about the uprising's causes were obvious to anyone with access to alternative information sources, which created a generation of educated South Koreans who had personally caught the state lying about a fundamental event. The Gwangju survivors and victims' families became a sustained organized political force advocating for accountability, which kept the issue alive through the dictatorship years. And the imagery of citizens defending their city against their own army's special forces was iconic in a way that resonated with the Korean memory of the colonial era — the same moral structure as the March 1st Movement (Chapter 1).

The Chun government succeeded militarily on May 27, 1980 and suppressed Gwangju. It did not succeed politically. The Gwangju Uprising would be the wound from which the Sixth Republic eventually grew (Chapter 5). Both Chun Doo-hwan and his designated successor **Roh Tae-woo** (ROH TAY-woo, 1932–2021) would, eventually, be tried for their roles in the Gwangju massacre and the December 12 coup, convicted of treason, and sentenced to long prison terms in 1996 (later pardoned). They are the only South Korean ex-presidents convicted of treason for taking power.

---

A note on the inter-Korean shadow war during this period. North Korea conducted dozens of covert operations against the South — assassination attempts, infiltration parties, kidnappings of Japanese civilians, the spectacular January 21, 1968 **Blue House Raid** in which a 31-man North Korean commando team got within 800 yards of the South Korean presidential residence before being intercepted. The most lethal episode was the October 1983 **Rangoon bombing** in Burma, in which North Korean agents detonated explosives at a memorial that visiting South Korean President Chun Doo-hwan was scheduled to attend; he was minutes late and survived, but four cabinet members and seventeen others were killed. A month earlier in September 1983 a Soviet fighter shot down **Korean Air Lines Flight 007** over the Sea of Japan, killing all 269 people aboard, including a US congressman; though not a North Korean operation, the episode underscored the militarization of North Pacific airspace at one of the most dangerous moments of the late Cold War.

The contrast between the two Koreas in the 1970s and 1980s — Park's authoritarian developmentalism in the South, Kim Il-sung's totalitarian Juche in the North — produced the divergence in economic and political outcomes that defines the modern peninsula.

Some structural reasons for the divergence are worth naming explicitly.

First, the South had a development model that could in principle work. State-led developmentalism, in conditions of secure property rights for the favored chaebol, an open American export market, and a population willing to accept extreme labor discipline, was capable of producing sustained high growth. The North's Juche autarky model could not. An economy that does not trade with the outside world is mechanically incapable of catching up to economies that do trade.

Second, the South was in a much better strategic position. American military protection meant the Park government could put almost all its budget into investment rather than defense. The North had to maintain its own military without comparable external defense, which absorbed an enormous fraction of national output. North Korean military spending in the 1980s and 1990s has been estimated at 25–30 percent of GDP — the highest figure ever sustained by any country in peacetime.

Third, the South was authoritarian but not totalitarian in the strict sense. Park Chung-hee crushed political opposition but did not try to control private family life, religious practice, or basic associational activity. Korean Christianity grew during the Park years. Korean Confucian family structures persisted. Korean folk traditions endured. The space for civil society was constrained but not abolished. North Korean totalitarianism abolished civil society. Without civil society, an economy degenerates into a small set of state institutions whose internal incentives become arbitrary.

Fourth, the South had real political contention from within. Even at its most repressive — the late Yushin period — Park's regime faced a Christian dissident movement, a labor movement, a university-student protest movement, a press that was constrained but not entirely captured, and an organized political opposition. These institutions provided continuous low-level pressure to liberalize, and they provided the personnel and the tactics that would eventually drive the 1987 democratization (Chapter 5). North Korea had no equivalent of any of these institutions.

The two Koreas diverged because they were running fundamentally different political and economic experiments under different external constraints. The fact that the diverging endpoints look so extreme is an artifact of how unusually pure each system was — the South's developmentalism was about as effective an authoritarian growth model as anyone has ever built, the North's totalitarianism was about as totally totalitarian as anything in modern history. Korea is the controlled experiment.

# Chapter 5 — Democratization

By the spring of 1987, South Korea was visibly approaching a political crisis. The economy had been growing at double-digit rates for years. The middle class was expanding. The student protest movement that had started after Gwangju (Chapter 4) had spread across every major university campus. The Catholic Church — under **Cardinal Kim Sou-hwan** (KIM soo-WAHN), the most respected moral authority in the country — was openly siding with the democracy movement. The labor movement, suppressed for decades, was reorganizing in factories around the industrial cities. The 1988 Seoul Olympics were thirteen months away, and the international press was increasingly focused on what would happen if the Chun government tried to suppress the democracy movement on television.

The detonator was a torture death.

---

In January 1987, a 22-year-old Seoul National University student named **Park Jong-chul** (PARK JONG-chul) was arrested for political activities and taken to the **Namyeong-dong** police interrogation center, where the South Korean security services conducted their most sensitive political investigations. During interrogation, Park was waterboarded by police officers attempting to extract the location of a fellow student activist. He drowned during the procedure.

The police initially attempted to cover up the death, claiming Park had collapsed of natural causes. A combination of a courageous medical examiner who refused to sign off on the cover story, and a Catholic priest who learned the actual circumstances and held a press conference, broke the story open. By February 1987 the entire country knew that the security services had tortured a student activist to death and had tried to lie about it.

The Chun government attempted political triage. A few low-level officers were arrested. Chun's chosen successor for the upcoming presidential election, retired General **Roh Tae-woo** (Chun's classmate from the Korean Military Academy and his collaborator in the 1979 coup), was nominated under tightly controlled circumstances. Chun announced that the existing constitution would not be changed before the 1987 election — meaning the next president would also be selected by a Chun-controlled electoral college rather than elected by direct popular vote.

This was the spark.

The **June Democracy Movement** began on June 10, 1987, the day Roh Tae-woo's nomination was announced. Massive demonstrations erupted in Seoul, Pusan, Daegu, Gwangju, and other major cities. Student groups had organized; the opposition political coalition under Kim Young-sam and Kim Dae-jung had organized; but what was new in June 1987 was the participation of the **white-collar middle class** — office workers in suits and ties who had never been politically active before, who joined the marches in central Seoul during their lunch breaks. The phenomenon was immediately noticed by international observers as evidence that the democracy movement had become national rather than student-and-dissident.

The June protests featured another iconic image: **Lee Han-yeol** (LEE HAHN-yul), a 21-year-old Yonsei University student, hit in the head by a tear gas canister fired at point-blank range by riot police on June 9. He was rushed to Severance Hospital but never regained consciousness; he died on July 5. The photograph of fellow student **Lee Jong-chang** holding the bleeding Lee Han-yeol — taken by photographer Jung Tae-won and reproduced across the world — became the visual symbol of the June Movement.

For two and a half weeks, Seoul and other cities were paralyzed by daily mass protests. The Chun government considered, and reportedly rejected, deploying the army to suppress the movement Tiananmen-style. (The decision is contested in the historical literature: some accounts say American pressure was decisive in keeping the army in barracks; some say Chun himself decided not to risk a Gwangju-scale massacre with the Olympics months away; some say the senior army leadership refused to obey orders to suppress.)

On June 29, 1987, Roh Tae-woo — under pressure from his own military faction and from the United States — announced the **June 29 Declaration**, accepting all of the democracy movement's main demands. Direct presidential elections would be restored. Press freedom would be restored. Imprisoned dissidents (including Kim Dae-jung) would be released. A new constitution would be drafted by a bipartisan commission and put to a referendum.

The June 29 Declaration was a complete capitulation by the Chun-Roh faction. South Korea was suddenly, after almost three decades of military dictatorship, on track for democratization.

---

The actual transition was messy.

The new constitution — the **Constitution of the Sixth Republic**, ratified by referendum in October 1987 — established a five-year non-renewable presidential term, a unicameral National Assembly, and a Constitutional Court with explicit power to overturn unconstitutional laws. The five-year non-renewable term was a deliberate response to the Park-Chun pattern of indefinite re-election; no Korean president since 1988 has served more than five years.

The first direct presidential election, December 16, 1987, was won by **Roh Tae-woo** with 36.6 percent of the vote. The opposition vote was split — Kim Young-sam took 28 percent, Kim Dae-jung took 27 percent, the maverick **Kim Jong-pil** (the surviving founder of the Park-era developmentalist apparatus) took 8 percent. Roh, despite being the candidate of the regime that had just been forced to capitulate, won because the democracy movement could not unite behind a single candidate.

This was a bitter outcome for the democracy activists. Kim Young-sam and Kim Dae-jung had spent two decades as fellow opposition leaders; if either had stepped aside, the other could have won outright. Their failure to unite handed the presidency back to the dictatorship's apparatus for one more term. South Korean political memory has not entirely forgiven either of them for it.

Roh Tae-woo's five-year term (1988–93) was a transition, not yet a full democracy. The military still had substantial influence. The intelligence services were still autonomous. The chaebol still operated under heavy regulatory protection. But the basic constitutional architecture worked. The 1988 Olympics were a global success. North-South dialogue began under Roh's **Northern Diplomacy** (Korean: *Bukbang Jeongchaek*), which normalized South Korean relations with the Soviet Union (1990) and China (1992) — the two communist powers that had backed North Korea in the war.

In 1992, **Kim Young-sam** won the presidency. He was the first non-military president in thirty-two years. His five-year term (1993–1998) is sometimes called the **Civilian Government** (*Munmin Jeongbu*). Kim Young-sam launched the most ambitious legal accountability project of the democratization era: the **Hanahoe** military faction was dismantled, the architecture of the Park and Chun dictatorships was systematically investigated, and Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo were arrested in 1995 on charges related to the 1979 coup, the Gwangju massacre, and the corruption of their administrations. Both were convicted in 1996 — Chun was sentenced to death (commuted to life imprisonment), Roh to 22 years. Both were pardoned in 1997 by outgoing President Kim Young-sam.

---

The Kim Young-sam presidency ended in catastrophe.

In late 1997, the **Asian Financial Crisis** swept across East and Southeast Asia. Thai baht devaluation triggered a chain reaction; investors fled emerging-market currencies; foreign capital that had been financing the rapid expansion of the Korean chaebol pulled out abruptly. The Korean won collapsed. Korean banks, which had borrowed dollars to lend won, faced sudden insolvency. The chaebol, which had taken on enormous foreign debt during the boom years, faced a wave of bankruptcies — including the catastrophic collapse of **Hanbo**, **Sammi**, **Kia Motors**, and most spectacularly **Daewoo** (one of the original five chaebol), whose bankruptcy in 1999 was the largest in world history at the time.

In December 1997, South Korea accepted a $58 billion International Monetary Fund bailout — the largest in IMF history at the time. The bailout came with extreme conditionality: structural reforms of the banking system, opening of capital markets to foreign investors, restructuring of the chaebol, layoff of millions of workers, and severe fiscal austerity. The Korean economy contracted by 5.5 percent in 1998. Unemployment quintupled. Suicides spiked.

The 1997 IMF crisis is one of the formative traumas of contemporary Korean memory, comparable in its national-psychological impact to the Korean War. The crisis broke the implicit social contract of the Miracle on the Han: the chaebol had failed, the developmentalist state had failed, and the foreign creditors had imposed terms that felt humiliating to a country that had just twenty years earlier been an aid recipient. There was a brief, much-celebrated **gold-collection campaign** in early 1998 in which roughly 3.5 million Koreans donated personal gold jewelry — about 227 tons total — to help service the country's foreign debt. The campaign is sometimes recalled as evidence of Korean national unity in a crisis. It is also sometimes recalled, more bitterly, as evidence of how far the system had pushed ordinary Koreans to bail out failures that were not theirs.

---

The political consequence of the 1997 crisis was the election of **Kim Dae-jung** as president on December 18, 1997. Kim Dae-jung — the longtime opposition leader from Jeolla Province who had been kidnapped by the KCIA in 1973, sentenced to death in 1980, and rebuilt his political career through the 1990s — became South Korea's first president to be elected from the opposition. The peaceful transfer of power from the formerly authoritarian establishment to a former dissident was a milestone for Korean democracy. Most Latin American transitions out of military rule had taken decades to produce a comparable handover; South Korea did it in ten years.

Kim Dae-jung's five-year term (1998–2003) had two defining themes: **economic restructuring** under the IMF program (which he implemented largely successfully — the South Korean economy returned to growth by 2000 and paid back the IMF bailout in 2001, two years ahead of schedule), and **engagement with North Korea**, which he called the **Sunshine Policy**.

The Sunshine Policy was named after one of Aesop's fables — the wind and the sun competing to remove a traveler's coat, where the wind only made the traveler clutch it tighter while the sun's warmth got him to take it off voluntarily. The doctrine was that hostility had failed to liberalize the North and might have entrenched it; that engagement, aid, and economic integration could over time make the regime more dependent on outside relationships and therefore more open. The policy had three concrete components.

First, **inter-Korean summits**. Kim Dae-jung met **Kim Jong-il** — Kim Il-sung's successor since 1994 — in Pyongyang on June 13, 2000. It was the first summit between leaders of the two Koreas since 1953. The optics were extraordinary: a former South Korean dissident the North Korean regime had once described as "a CIA puppet" embracing the Kim family successor. Kim Dae-jung was awarded the **Nobel Peace Prize** later that year for the achievement.

Second, **economic engagement**. The **Mt. Kumgang Tourism Project** — South Korean tourists visiting a designated mountain area in North Korea — operated from 1998 to 2008, when it was shut down after a North Korean soldier shot and killed a South Korean tourist. The **Kaesong Industrial Complex** opened in 2004 in the North Korean city of Kaesong, just across the DMZ; South Korean firms operated factories there, employing tens of thousands of North Korean workers, until the complex was closed in 2016 after a North Korean nuclear test.

Third, **family reunions**. The two Koreas had divided millions of families in 1945 and 1953. The Sunshine Policy organized intermittent reunion events at Mt. Kumgang and Pyongyang where elderly separated relatives — often eighty or ninety years old by the time of the reunions — were allowed to spend three days with relatives they had last seen as small children. The reunions were emotional, deeply consequential for participants, and politically significant; they were also tightly limited in scale (perhaps 20,000 family members total over twenty years, against millions still separated) and were repeatedly cancelled when relations deteriorated.

Kim Dae-jung's chosen successor, **Roh Moo-hyun** (ROH MOO-hyun, 1946–2009), won the 2002 election and continued the engagement policy through 2008, including a second inter-Korean summit in Pyongyang in October 2007. Roh was a self-taught labor lawyer from a poor rural background, the first Korean president without an elite educational pedigree, and his presidency was marked by an emotional populism and recurring conflicts with the conservative establishment. He killed himself in May 2009 by jumping from a cliff near his rural hometown while under prosecutorial investigation for corruption — an investigation his supporters considered politically motivated. The suicide intensified the country's already polarized politics and gave the Korean liberal movement a martyr figure that still shapes Democratic Party identity in the 2020s.

The Sunshine Policy's legacy is mixed. It did not denuclearize North Korea (Pyongyang's first nuclear test came in 2006, three years after Kim Dae-jung left office). It did not produce sustained inter-Korean reconciliation (Lee Myung-bak, the conservative president elected in 2007, suspended most Sunshine programs). It did produce the first sustained period of inter-Korean dialogue since the war, the first opening of North Korean territory to outside observers since the war, and the precedent that it was politically possible for a South Korean president to engage with Pyongyang without immediately destroying his coalition. The 2018 Moon-Kim summit (Chapter 7) was a direct descendant of the Kim Dae-jung playbook.

---

The North in this period was struggling. **Kim Il-sung had died** of a heart attack on July 8, 1994, leaving his son **Kim Jong-il** as successor. Kim Jong-il had been formally designated heir since 1980 and had spent fifteen years preparing for the role; the dynastic transition was managed without obvious instability, an unusual achievement in Communist successions. But his accession came in the worst possible economic moment. The Soviet collapse in 1991 had ended subsidized energy imports. Chinese aid was reduced. A series of bad harvests beginning in 1995 produced one of the worst famines of the late twentieth century — the **Arduous March** (Korean: *Gonan-ui Haenggun*), which killed an estimated 600,000 to 2.5 million people in a country of 22 million between 1995 and 1998. (Chapter 7 discusses the famine in detail.)

Kim Jong-il's response was to deepen the regime's military-first ideology — the **Songun** (SOHN-goon, "Military First") doctrine, which prioritized the army over every other institution. He also accelerated the nuclear weapons program that his father had started in the 1960s. By the early 2000s, North Korea was a country in which the population was being deliberately starved while the regime invested in nuclear weapons and luxury goods for the leadership. The mass disillusionment that this combination might be expected to produce in any other country was suppressed by the totalitarian apparatus described in Chapter 4. The regime survived.

The contrast between the two Koreas in 2000 — South Korea hosting World Cup matches and electing a Nobel Peace laureate, North Korea negotiating food aid in the shadow of a famine that had killed roughly 5 percent of its population — is the most extreme of the divergences. It is also the moment when the divergence became visible to outside observers as essentially permanent.

# Chapter 6 — K-Wave and Globalization

In December 2012, a music video by a Korean rapper named **PSY** (real name **Park Jae-sang**, born 1977) became the first YouTube video in history to reach one billion views.

The song was called **"Gangnam Style"**. The lyrics were entirely in Korean. The video featured PSY mimicking the lifestyle of South Korea's wealthy **Gangnam district** in southern Seoul — pretending to be in luxurious settings (a tour bus, a horse-riding stable, a yoga class) that were in fact being filmed in cheap or absurd substitutes (a children's pony, a parking garage, a bus full of tourists). The choreography included a horse-riding dance that was easy to imitate and that millions of people did imitate. President Barack Obama danced to it at the White House. UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon (a Korean) danced to it at the UN. Ai Weiwei made a parody version. The Saudi religious police banned it. It was the moment a Korean cultural product became unambiguously global, and the moment many non-Asian audiences first heard the term **K-pop**.

This chapter is about how Korean popular culture went from being a regional phenomenon to a globally recognized brand — and about the policy and industrial choices that made it happen, because the Korean Wave was not a spontaneous cultural movement but a deliberately engineered soft-power project.

---

The Korean wave is called **Hallyu** (HAHL-yoo, Korean: 한류, "Korean current/flow") — a term coined by Chinese journalists in the late 1990s to describe the surprise popularity of Korean television dramas in mainland China.

The first phase of Hallyu, in the late 1990s and early 2000s, was a regional Asian phenomenon. Korean dramas — formulaic romantic melodramas set in modern urban Korean settings — became wildly popular in mainland China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Japan, the Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia. The prototype was **"Winter Sonata"** (*Gyeoul Yeonga*), a 2002 KBS drama starring **Bae Yong-joon** that became a staggering hit in Japan after airing on NHK in 2003. Bae Yong-joon, nicknamed **"Yon-sama"** by Japanese fans, became one of the highest-paid foreign celebrities in Japan; the Japanese tourism industry organized "Winter Sonata pilgrimage tours" to filming locations on Korea's Nami Island, which became, briefly, a leading Japanese tourist destination.

The second phase, in the late 2000s and 2010s, was K-pop. Korean idol groups had been an industry product since the late 1990s, when the entertainment companies **SM Entertainment**, **YG Entertainment**, and **JYP Entertainment** began applying a manufactured-pop-star model adapted from Japanese **Johnny's Entertainment** and from American boy-band production. The Korean adaptation refined the model: trainees were recruited as young as eleven or twelve, signed to multi-year contracts, lived together in dormitories, trained six to twelve hours per day in singing, dancing, multi-language fluency (Japanese, Mandarin, and English), and stagecraft, and were eventually grouped into idol acts whose careers were micro-managed by the parent agency. The resulting product was extremely high-production-value pop music with elaborate choreography that drew heavily on contemporary American R&B and hip-hop while keeping Korean lyrical conventions and an Asian sensibility about visual aesthetics.

The first K-pop groups to reach broad international audiences were **Wonder Girls**, **Girls' Generation**, **Super Junior**, **TVXQ**, and **Big Bang** in the late 2000s. The K-pop "second generation" gave way to a "third generation" in the mid-2010s, dominated by **EXO**, **BTS**, **Twice**, and **Blackpink**. By 2017, **BTS** — a seven-member group from a smaller agency called **Big Hit Entertainment**, debuted in 2013 — had broken through to the American market and won their first Billboard Music Award. By 2020, BTS had topped the Billboard 200 album chart five times and become probably the biggest pop act in the world.

A side-bar that matters for understanding the K-wave's rapid spread: by 2003, South Korea had the **world's fastest internet**, with the highest broadband penetration rate of any country on Earth. The lead came from a deliberate state push for fiber-optic infrastructure during the post-IMF recovery years, combined with the ubiquity of high-density urban apartment housing that made fiber rollout cheap. The connectivity underpinned the country's later platform economy — the gaming industry around StarCraft and online RPGs, the early social-media platforms like Cyworld, and the streaming distribution that K-pop and K-drama needed to reach global audiences. South Korea was a high-bandwidth society at consumer scale before nearly anywhere else, which gave Korean creators a first-mover advantage in figuring out what online cultural products could become.

The third phase, in the late 2010s and 2020s, was Korean cinema and streaming-era television. **Bong Joon-ho's** thriller **"Parasite"** won Best Picture, Best Director, Best Original Screenplay, and Best International Feature at the 2020 Academy Awards — the first non-English-language film to win Best Picture in the Oscars' 92-year history. **"Squid Game"**, a Korean Netflix series created by **Hwang Dong-hyuk** and released in September 2021, became Netflix's most-watched show ever within four weeks of release, reaching 142 million viewing households. By the early 2020s, "Korean content" had become a globally recognized category in television, film, and pop music, and was treated by streaming platforms and global music labels as a strategic asset.

---

How did this happen?

A common Western telling of the story is that K-pop and Korean cinema represent organic globalization — talented Korean creators producing globally appealing work in the post-Internet age. This account is partly true but misses the policy dimension.

The Korean state has actively cultivated cultural export industries since the late 1990s, when **Kim Dae-jung's** government — partly because of the IMF crisis (Chapter 5), partly because of an explicit policy diagnosis that Korea needed soft-power assets to complement its hard manufacturing economy — substantially increased state subsidies for cultural production, training, and export. The **Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism** has run the **Korea Creative Content Agency (KOCCA)** since 2009 with a budget of several hundred million dollars per year for direct grants to drama producers, film studios, music labels, animation studios, video game developers, and webtoon platforms. State-funded cultural-export trade missions, market-access subsidies for Korean media in foreign markets, language-and-culture promotion (the **King Sejong Institute** network of Korean-language schools abroad), and intellectual-property protection efforts have all been pursued as deliberate industrial-policy goals.

Soft-power policy and developmental-state policy have been combined in a way that makes Korean cultural exports more like Korean industrial exports than like, say, American cultural exports. The Korean Wave is not Hollywood, which is a private-sector phenomenon that the United States government largely declines to subsidize. The Korean Wave is more like the Korean automobile industry: a private-sector activity in which the state has invested heavily, picked national champions, financed export campaigns, and treated international success as a national project.

This has implications. Hallyu's reach has been deepest in markets where the Korean government has invested most heavily in language and cultural promotion (Japan, Southeast Asia, China before the 2017 THAAD missile-defense fallout led Beijing to restrict Korean cultural imports). The Korean Wave's brand has been carefully managed at the state level — for example, when Korean cultural exports run into political issues (Chinese restrictions, Japanese revisionism, Western criticisms of K-pop's labor practices), the Korean government has often intervened diplomatically and through industrial policy rather than treating these as private-sector problems.

The Korean Wave is also genuinely a private-sector creative phenomenon. The big four entertainment agencies (HYBE, SM, JYP, YG) compete intensely. K-pop groups debut, succeed, decline, and disband on standard music-industry timetables. Korean film studios pursue both domestic and international audiences with conventional profit-driven calculation. Korean drama writers and directors have their own creative reputations. The state's role is to subsidize and promote, not to direct content. The result is a hybrid system that combines genuine creative achievement with state-sponsored amplification.

---

The fourth phase, ongoing in the 2020s, involves the question of what happens next.

Three patterns are visible.

The first is **Hallyu fatigue**. After thirty years of Korean cultural exports, some markets are showing signs of saturation — particularly in China, where state-level political tensions have throttled Korean content, and in Japan, where periodic political controversies (history, Takeshima/Dokdo) reduce Korean cultural appetite. K-pop's manufactured-idol-group model is also facing increasing internal critique inside Korea over its labor practices — long contracts, mental-health pressures on young trainees, the tendency to disband popular groups after seven years (the "K-pop curse"), the difficulty for Korean idols of having normal romantic lives or family lives during their performing years.

The second is **competition from other Asian content industries**. China has invested heavily in domestic streaming and music production over the past decade, and Chinese cultural exports are starting to find international audiences. Japan's animation and video game industries remain dominant in their genres. Thailand has produced major streaming hits in the late 2010s and 2020s. The K-wave was the first Asian cultural export wave to break into Western markets at scale; it will not be the last.

The third is the **migration of Korean creative talent to globalized platforms**. The biggest Korean creative successes of the 2020s have been streaming productions (Squid Game on Netflix; Pachinko on Apple TV+) where Korean directors and writers work with Western producers and budgets. The model is moving toward what might be called Korean-creator-driven, globally-financed production rather than purely domestic Korean industry. This is good for individual Korean artists' careers and good for the global reach of Korean storytelling. It complicates the original soft-power industrial-policy logic, because the state cannot subsidize what is no longer entirely a Korean firm.

---

A note on what the Korean Wave actually is and is not, before closing the chapter.

K-pop, K-drama, K-cinema, and K-beauty are commercial products. They have made Korea visible in global culture in a way nothing else has — Joseon dynasty pottery did not, Park Chung-hee's GDP figures did not, Hyundai cars and Samsung phones did and do but in a different register. They have generated a genuine generation of young people in dozens of countries who study Korean, visit Seoul, follow Korean fashion, eat Korean food, and consume Korean media. This is real soft power. It is the kind of soft power Japan had in the 1980s and 1990s and that Korea has eclipsed Japan in attaining among under-thirty audiences globally.

It is not a substitute for political institutions or international standing or military security. The two Koreas problem is not solved by Squid Game's success. The North Korean famine victims are not less dead because BTS sells out stadiums. The fundamental challenges of demographic decline, geopolitical pressure between China and the United States, and the unresolved 1953 armistice line are not addressed by cultural exports. Soft power is a multiplier, not a replacement.

This is a useful caveat to bear in mind because the celebratory framing of the Korean Wave can obscure the actual political situation. South Korea is not Switzerland. It is a frontline state across a militarized border from the world's most dangerous regime, in a region where Chinese-American competition is intensifying, with internal demographic and political problems that are not solved by being culturally fashionable.

The next chapter turns to North Korea. The chapter after that turns to the contemporary politics of South Korea. Together, they explain why the cultural success story is real and partial at the same time.

# Chapter 7 — The North Korean Question

This chapter is about North Korea — what the regime actually is, what it has done to its own population, and why every external strategy for changing it has so far failed.

The chapter is uncomfortable to write because the available facts are partly contested and partly horrifying. The historical baseline is that North Korea is a totalitarian state that has caused mass civilian deaths through its own policies, holds an estimated 80,000 to 120,000 political prisoners under prison-camp conditions that survivors have described as among the worst in the modern world, and is the only country to have left the **Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons** since the treaty came into force. None of these facts is Cold War rhetoric. All are documented in detail by international human rights bodies, by intelligence services from multiple countries, and by escaped North Korean defectors whose accounts have been corroborated against satellite imagery and against each other.

It is also uncomfortable because external policy has not figured out what to do about it, and pretending otherwise serves nobody.

---

The most consequential single event in modern North Korean history is the famine of the 1990s.

The **Arduous March** (Korean: *Gonan-ui Haenggun*, the regime's official name for the period — drawn from a Kim Il-sung guerrilla-era retreat, framed as a heroic ordeal) was a famine that ran roughly from 1994 to 1998 and killed an estimated 600,000 to 2.5 million people in a country of about 22 million. The lower estimates come from regime-friendly demographic reconstructions; the higher from analyses by South Korean and American researchers using mortality data from defector interviews and from satellite-derived agricultural production estimates. Even at the lower bound, the famine killed roughly 3 percent of the country's population. At the upper bound it killed more than 10 percent.

The famine was caused by a combination of factors. The Soviet collapse in 1991 ended subsidized energy imports — fuel for tractors, fertilizer for agriculture, electricity for industrial food processing. Chinese aid declined as Beijing pivoted toward South Korea (1992 normalization). North Korea's collective-farm system was already inefficient and dependent on heavy fertilizer inputs that the country could no longer afford or import. A series of summer floods in 1995 and 1996 destroyed harvests in key agricultural regions. The government's responses — reducing food rations to non-favored populations, prioritizing the army and Pyongyang loyalists, refusing to ask for sufficient outside aid until the catastrophe was already underway — converted a serious agricultural crisis into a mass-mortality event.

Survivors' accounts describe scenes that read like medieval famine narratives. Whole village populations died. Children starved on streets. Cannibalism was reported, including organized markets in human meat in some northeastern provinces. Hospitals had no medicine, no food, no electricity. Schools closed because students were too weak to attend. Workers were paid in cabbage and could not earn enough cabbage to live. The famine ended only when the regime allowed informal market activity (the **jangmadang** markets, see below) and when international food aid finally began to flow at scale in 1996–98.

The famine has shaped contemporary North Korea in two ways.

First, it created a generation that does not entirely trust the regime. The famine survivors are now in their thirties, forties, and fifties. They watched the state fail to feed them, watched the state continue to feed the elite, and learned that they would have to feed themselves. The informal **jangmadang** market system that emerged during and after the famine has become a permanent (though officially frowned upon) feature of North Korean life. Most North Koreans get most of their food, clothing, and household goods through informal markets rather than through the state distribution system. This is a large change from the totalitarian baseline of the 1980s and earlier. North Korea is no longer a fully totalitarian society; it is a totalitarian regime presiding over a partially marketized population that has learned not to depend on it.

Second, it produced a wave of defections — North Koreans who fled to China, then often onward to South Korea, where they became the largest source of testimony about North Korean conditions. Defector accounts, cross-checked against each other and against satellite data, have provided most of what the outside world knows about the kwanliso prison camps (Chapter 4), about famine-era conditions, about the practical details of life in the country. The defector population in South Korea was about 30,000 by 2020.

---

The other defining North Korean development of this period is the nuclear weapons program.

North Korea began nuclear research in the 1960s under Kim Il-sung and built a nuclear reactor at **Yongbyon** in the 1980s. The program accelerated in the 1990s under Kim Jong-il, culminating in North Korea's withdrawal from the **Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons** in 2003 and its first nuclear test in October 2006.

The North Korean program has gone through six tests as of the mid-2020s — 2006, 2009, 2013, twice in 2016, and once in 2017. The 2017 test, the largest, was claimed by Pyongyang to be a thermonuclear (hydrogen bomb) device with a yield estimated at over 100 kilotons — roughly six times the yield of the Hiroshima bomb. North Korea has also developed and tested intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs) capable of reaching the continental United States, including the **Hwasong-15** (2017) and **Hwasong-17** (2022).

The strategic logic of the North Korean nuclear program is straightforward. The regime watched the Soviet Union collapse, watched Saddam Hussein be deposed and killed (he had no nuclear weapons), watched Muammar Gaddafi be overthrown after surrendering his nuclear program (the **Libyan precedent**, frequently cited in North Korean propaganda), and concluded that nuclear weapons were the only reliable guarantor of regime survival. From the regime's perspective, the program has been entirely successful. North Korea has nuclear weapons; the regime has not been overthrown; American attempts to negotiate denuclearization have repeatedly failed.

External strategies for dealing with the program have all failed.

The **Agreed Framework** of 1994 was a Clinton-administration agreement under which North Korea was to freeze its plutonium program in exchange for fuel oil shipments and the construction of two light-water reactors. The agreement collapsed by 2002, blamed by both sides on the other.

The **Six-Party Talks** of 2003–09 attempted to negotiate a comprehensive denuclearization framework involving North Korea, South Korea, the United States, China, Russia, and Japan. The talks produced occasional partial agreements (the 2005 joint statement, the 2007 action plan) but no comprehensive settlement. North Korea conducted its first nuclear test during the talks.

The **strategic patience** approach of the Obama years (2009–17) explicitly accepted that no breakthrough was likely and focused on sanctions, deterrence, and denying Pyongyang the international standing it sought. This produced no progress toward denuclearization either, and during this period North Korea conducted four nuclear tests and dramatically expanded its missile capabilities.

The **Trump era** (2017–21) saw the most personally engaged American diplomacy with North Korea. President **Donald Trump** met **Kim Jong-un** in Singapore on June 12, 2018 — the first meeting between sitting American and North Korean leaders. The meeting produced a joint statement endorsing "denuclearization of the Korean Peninsula" without specifying terms, and the personal correspondence between Trump and Kim that followed (which Trump described as "love letters") generated photo-op diplomacy without concrete commitments. A second summit in Hanoi in February 2019 collapsed without an agreement when the United States and North Korea could not agree on the linkage between sanctions relief and verifiable denuclearization steps.

The **Biden era** (2021–25) returned to a more measured strategic-patience approach. The North Korean program continued. By the mid-2020s, North Korea had functional nuclear weapons, functional ICBMs, and an active program developing tactical nuclear weapons, hypersonic missiles, and submarine-launched missiles.

---

The outside world has, in practice, accepted that North Korea has nuclear weapons.

This is uncomfortable to say but true. The international community has not accepted it formally — North Korea is not a recognized member of the **Nuclear Suppliers Group**, the country is under heavy UN sanctions, no government accepts North Korean denuclearization claims at face value. But functionally the world has stopped expecting denuclearization. The strategic conversation has shifted toward managing the consequences — extended deterrence guarantees from the United States to South Korea and Japan, missile-defense investments, sanctions enforcement to slow the program's expansion, contingency planning for regime collapse. None of this is denuclearization.

The shift has implications. For decades, South Korean policy was premised on the assumption that North Korean nuclear weapons would eventually be eliminated through some negotiation. That assumption has quietly been replaced by a different one: that South Korea (and Japan) live next to a permanent nuclear-armed adversary, and need permanent deterrent capabilities, alliance arrangements, and crisis-management institutions to manage that fact. South Korean public-opinion polling has shown rising support since 2017 for an indigenous South Korean nuclear weapons program — a development that would have been unthinkable in the Sunshine Policy era and that is now within the boundary of mainstream conservative-party policy debate.

---

The succession from **Kim Jong-il** to **Kim Jong-un** in late 2011 was a reminder of how stable the dynastic regime is.

Kim Jong-il died of a heart attack on December 17, 2011. His third son **Kim Jong-un** — then in his late twenties, formally introduced to the public for the first time only the previous year, with no significant administrative experience and limited military background — took over. Most outside analysts in 2011 expected substantial difficulties: power struggles among the senior generals and party officials, possible factional conflict, possible reformist movement.

None of that happened. Kim Jong-un consolidated power within two years. He purged his uncle and former regent **Jang Song-thaek** (whom outside analysts had assumed would be the real power behind the throne), publicly executing him for treason in December 2013. He reportedly arranged the assassination of his half-brother **Kim Jong-nam**, a possible rival heir who had been living in semi-exile in Macau, by VX nerve agent at Kuala Lumpur airport in February 2017. He pursued a deliberate program of ideological consolidation around himself, including substantial new propaganda framing him as the ideological successor of Kim Il-sung (his grandfather, whom he physically resembles, in ways that probably are not entirely coincidental — there are persistent reports that Kim Jong-un has had cosmetic surgery to enhance the resemblance).

Kim Jong-un's regime has been more economically pragmatic in some respects than his father's. Informal markets have continued to expand. Limited foreign investment in special economic zones has been permitted. Pyongyang has been deliberately modernized as a showcase capital with new high-rise apartments, new luxury shops, and dramatic public-architecture projects. Whether any of this represents the beginning of a Chinese-style reform-and-opening-up is the perennial question; the answer so far is essentially no, because the regime continues to prioritize ideological control and military spending over broader economic development.

The dynastic regime now contains, by some accounts, a possible fourth-generation successor. Kim Jong-un has been seen at public events with his elementary-school-aged daughter **Kim Ju-ae**, in choreographed appearances that some analysts read as positioning her for eventual succession. Whether she actually becomes the next leader, or whether the regime collapses or transforms before her time, or whether the succession passes to one of her brothers (whose existence is not confirmed) — these are open questions that depend on whether the existing regime model lasts another twenty or thirty years.

---

The closing observation of this chapter is that North Korea's persistence is the largest strategic surprise of the post-Cold War era. The regime was supposed to collapse in 1989. It was supposed to collapse during the Arduous March famine. It was supposed to collapse after Kim Jong-il's death in 2011. It was supposed to collapse under the **Maximum Pressure** sanctions of 2017–18. None of these collapses happened.

The reasons appear to be a combination of: the totalitarian apparatus's continuing capacity to suppress internal dissent; the partial economic adaptation that the regime has tolerated since the famine, which has made the country less brittle than it was in 1995; Chinese strategic decisions to keep the regime alive (a North Korean collapse would produce refugee flows into Northeast China and would put a unified American-allied Korea on the Chinese border); and the regime's nuclear deterrent, which has effectively ended the possibility of forced regime change.

Outside observers should be careful about making confident predictions about North Korea's future based on outside-the-system analysis. The regime has out-survived three decades of expert predictions of its imminent demise. The most honest position is that the North Korean question is unresolved, that the regime has more durability than was expected, and that the tools available to outside powers — sanctions, deterrence, engagement, occasional summit theater — have not produced fundamental change in seventy-five years and are unlikely to do so in the next twenty.

Korea's twentieth-century division is, on present trends, going to be a 21st-century division as well.

# Chapter 8 — The Twenty-First Century

This final chapter is about contemporary South Korea — the country that K-pop, Samsung phones, and Netflix programming have made internationally familiar — and about what its democracy, its economy, and its demographics actually look like under the surface.

The argument of the chapter is that South Korea's success is real and its problems are also real, and that the celebratory international story leaves out the harder part.

---

Start with the economy.

South Korea in the 2020s is a high-income country with a globally competitive technology sector. **Samsung Electronics** is the largest memory chip maker in the world by a substantial margin and one of the two leading smartphone makers; combined with **SK Hynix**, South Korea controls roughly 60 percent of global DRAM production and over 50 percent of global NAND flash production. **Hyundai Motor Group** is the third or fourth largest automaker in the world. **LG Electronics** is one of the leading global brands in display panels, home appliances, and industrial equipment. **POSCO**, **Hyundai Heavy Industries**, **Samsung Heavy Industries**, **HD Hyundai Heavy Industries**, and **DSME** are major global producers in steel, shipbuilding, and offshore engineering.

The South Korean economy is also extraordinarily concentrated. The top ten chaebol account for roughly 80 percent of GDP. Samsung alone accounts for roughly 20 percent of stock market capitalization. The chaebol's dominance is structural — small and medium-sized enterprises in Korea operate largely as suppliers to the chaebol rather than as independent firms competing with them — and produces an ongoing political debate about whether the developmentalist legacy is now a drag on innovation rather than a source of it.

Three structural problems are visible.

The first is **demographic collapse**. South Korea's total fertility rate fell to **0.72** births per woman in 2023 — the lowest of any country ever recorded in the modern era, less than half the population-replacement rate of 2.1, and lower than even Japan's already-very-low rate of about 1.3. At a sustained TFR of 0.72, the South Korean population would halve every 25 years; the country has already entered absolute population decline, with deaths exceeding births since 2020. The causes are complex — extreme housing costs in Seoul, an overworked white-collar labor culture, the gendered structure of childcare (Korean women still bear primary responsibility for housework and childcare regardless of employment status), the high cost of private education that accompanies any child-rearing decision, the difficulty of work-family balance in a society that still expects intensive maternal commitment. The consequences are rolling — the country's productive labor force is shrinking, its pension system faces accelerating pressure, its long-term tax base is eroding, and the rural population is collapsing fastest, with hundreds of villages already abandoned.

The demographic problem is not merely a statistic. It is a coming inversion of the social contract that built the Miracle on the Han. The Park-era developmentalist state worked partly because it had an enormous young population to deploy in the export factories. The 1980s and 1990s middle class worked partly because of the savings glut produced by a young earning population funding a small retired population. The present South Korean welfare state assumes a working-age-to-retiree ratio that is collapsing year by year. The country will, on present trends, become one of the oldest societies in the world by 2050, with all the public-finance and political-stability questions that entails.

The second problem is **inequality and housing**. The Miracle on the Han was a relatively egalitarian growth story by international standards through the 1980s. The post-IMF restructuring (Chapter 5) shifted that. Korean income inequality has risen since 1997. Housing inequality has risen even more dramatically — Seoul housing prices, particularly in the **Gangnam** district, have risen so much faster than wages that an entire generation of younger Koreans has effectively been priced out of the city. The Korean expression **N-po sedae** ("the give-up-N generation") was coined in the 2010s to describe young Koreans who have given up on dating, marriage, children, employment, home ownership, and various other elements of conventional adult life because the economic conditions make them impossible. The phrase has gone through escalating numerical versions — *3-po*, *5-po*, *7-po*, *N-po* — as Korean cultural commentary has tracked the expanding list of life elements being relinquished.

The third problem is **political polarization**. South Korean democratic politics has settled into intensely competitive five-year alternation between the conservative party (currently the **People Power Party**, descendant of multiple earlier conservative formations stretching back to the Park-Chun apparatus) and the liberal party (currently the **Democratic Party**, descendant of the Kim Dae-jung opposition tradition). The competition is unusually personalized — every recent presidential election has been a referendum on the personal character of the leading candidates rather than on policy programs — and unusually litigious. The most recent four South Korean presidents (Lee Myung-bak, Park Geun-hye, Moon Jae-in, Yoon Suk-yeol) have all been criminally investigated or impeached or both during or after their terms. The post-presidential conviction rate is now one of the highest in the democratic world; only one South Korean president since the 1980s has avoided some form of criminal proceeding. The pattern reflects, in part, genuine corruption that the Korean prosecutorial system is correctly investigating; it also reflects, in part, the use of prosecutorial power as a political weapon by whichever side is in power.

---

The most consequential political event of the past decade was the **impeachment of Park Geun-hye** in 2016–17.

**Park Geun-hye** (PARK GUN-hyeh, born 1952) — the daughter of Park Chung-hee — had been elected president in December 2012 as the candidate of the conservative **Saenuri Party**. She was the first female president in Korean history and the first president to come from the family of a former dictator. Her presidency was conventional conservative until late 2016, when journalists and prosecutors uncovered an extraordinary corruption network involving her informal advisor **Choi Soon-sil** (CHOY soon-SHIL), the daughter of a quasi-religious figure named **Choi Tae-min** who had been Park Chung-hee's spiritual confidant in the 1970s.

The Choi Soon-sil scandal had multiple layers. Choi had been editing the president's speeches without holding any formal government position. Choi had been pressuring chaebol leaders to make donations to two foundations she controlled. Choi's daughter had received special admission to Ewha Women's University. Choi had been making policy decisions affecting Korean state institutions on the basis of her religious-mystical views. The image of South Korea's first female president taking instructions from her informal mystical advisor — combined with the sheer extent of the corruption network that Choi had built around the Blue House — produced the largest sustained protests in Korean history.

The **Candlelight Protests** ran from October 2016 through Park's removal in March 2017. Millions of South Koreans gathered weekly in central Seoul, holding candles, listening to speakers, eating street food, while pressing for the president's resignation. The protests were striking in three respects. They were enormous — single weekend gatherings drew over a million people. They were disciplined — there was essentially no violence, almost no property damage, and remarkably little confrontation with police. And they were sustained — week after week, in cold weather, under daily news coverage, the same crowds returned.

Park Geun-hye was impeached by the National Assembly on December 9, 2016, on a vote of 234-56 (with 28 of her own party voting for impeachment). The Constitutional Court unanimously upheld the impeachment on March 10, 2017, removing her from office. She was arrested two weeks later, indicted on multiple corruption and abuse-of-power charges, and eventually sentenced to 22 years in prison (later partially commuted; she was pardoned by Yoon Suk-yeol in December 2021 on humanitarian grounds related to her health).

The Candlelight Protests have been celebrated in subsequent years as a triumph of Korean democratic civic culture. They were also the moment when the post-1987 democratic consensus became visible as something settled — the country had used the constitutional impeachment process to peacefully remove a sitting president on grounds of corruption, in front of the world's cameras, without violence.

The presidential election that followed in May 2017 was won by the liberal **Moon Jae-in** (MOON JAY-in, born 1953), Kim Dae-jung's former chief of staff. Moon's five-year term included the Pyongyang summit (Chapter 7), the 2018 Winter Olympics in PyeongChang, the COVID-19 response (Chapter 6), and a substantial expansion of welfare programs.

---

Moon Jae-in's term ended in May 2022 with the election of **Yoon Suk-yeol** (YOON SOOK-yul, born 1960), a former prosecutor general who had become a conservative-party star by leading the corruption prosecutions of both Park Geun-hye and Lee Myung-bak. Yoon's election by 0.73 percentage points was the narrowest in Korean history. His term was marked from the start by extreme polarization and by chronic conflict with the National Assembly, which the opposition Democratic Party controlled.

The Yoon presidency reached its political crisis on December 3, 2024.

In a televised speech that night, Yoon declared **martial law**, accusing the National Assembly of obstruction, treason, and subversion of constitutional order. Military forces were deployed to the National Assembly building in central Seoul. The political situation was, briefly, terrifying. The last martial law declaration in South Korea had been Chun Doo-hwan's in May 1980 (Chapter 4); the imagery of soldiers occupying the National Assembly evoked the worst of the dictatorship years.

What happened next was decisive. The National Assembly's lawmakers — both ruling-party and opposition — rushed to the building. Citizens gathered outside in numbers that grew rapidly through the night. Some climbed the walls to enter the building. The Assembly's speaker, ruling-party chairman, and opposition leaders convened an emergency session at roughly 1 AM and voted 190-0 to overturn the martial law declaration, as the constitution allowed them to do. Yoon, faced with a unanimous Assembly resolution, withdrew the declaration approximately six hours after issuing it.

Yoon was impeached by the National Assembly on December 14, 2024, on a vote of 204-85, with 12 of his own party voting against him. The Constitutional Court considered the impeachment for several months. As of mid-2025, the question of whether Yoon would be permanently removed and what would follow constitutionally remained the central political question of the country.

The Yoon martial-law moment was, in one reading, the moment Korean democracy demonstrated its institutional resilience. The Assembly acted within hours; the citizens turned out; the military did not actually attempt to suppress the legislature; the impeachment process proceeded constitutionally. By those measures the system worked.

In another reading, the fact that a sitting Korean president declared martial law in 2024 — for the first time in 44 years — and tried to deploy soldiers against the legislature was the most alarming political moment in the country's post-democratization history. Korean democracy survived the test, but the test itself was a serious one, and the conditions that produced it (extreme polarization, chronic executive-legislative conflict, institutional distrust) have not been resolved.

The Itaewon disaster of late 2022 deserves a brief mention here as well. On the night of October 29, 2022, a Halloween crowd surge in a narrow alley in Seoul's Itaewon nightlife district killed 159 people, most of them in their twenties. Police preparation had been inadequate; emergency response was slow. The disaster was not a political event in the conventional sense, but it triggered an extended national mourning period and substantial political fallout, including the dismissal of the national police chief and a series of investigations that produced no senior-official accountability — a pattern that, fairly or not, was viewed as evidence that the Yoon administration was not handling public-safety crises well.

---

The closing observation of this book is about what Korean democracy has actually become.

The South Korean political system in the mid-2020s is real, durable, and contested. It has held competitive presidential elections every five years since 1987, with peaceful transfers of power between rival parties. It has used constitutional procedures to remove a president for corruption. It has refused to accept a martial law declaration from a sitting president. It has produced one of the most active civil societies in Asia, with regular mass protest, vigorous press, and a Constitutional Court that has shown itself willing to overturn unconstitutional state actions.

It is also stressed. Demographic decline is making the system progressively harder to fund. Polarization is making peaceful transitions of power feel less stable. Prosecutorial overreach has converted normal political disagreement into criminal litigation. The chaebol's economic dominance has become a barrier to renewed innovation. The population is aging faster than any major economy except Japan. The demographic crisis, the polarization crisis, and the geopolitical pressure between China and the United States are all simultaneous and interlocking.

If the Joseon dynasty's failure was its inability to modernize when the world demanded it (Joseon Korea Ch 8), the Republic of Korea's challenge is the inverse: it has modernized brilliantly, perhaps more completely than any country in modern history, and now has to figure out how to govern itself sustainably in the world that modernization made.

---

Korea has been on this peninsula for several thousand years (Ancient Korea Ch 1). It will, very probably, still be on this peninsula in another thousand years. What the Korean state will look like, whether the country will be unified or divided, what language the descendants will speak, what they will eat, who they will worship — these are questions whose answers history has shown to be genuinely uncertain across long enough time horizons.

What does seem reasonably durable is the underlying Korean civilizational thread. The Hangul alphabet that Sejong invented in the fifteenth century (Joseon Korea Ch 2) is now read by 80 million people every day. The Korean language is now studied internationally as a foreign language at a scale that would have stunned a Joseon-era yangban scholar. The Korean cuisine that started as peasant fare has become globally recognized. The Korean families and the Confucian patterns of their life — modified, contested, weakened in some respects, strengthened in others — still inflect how three generations of Koreans on either side of the DMZ live with each other.

The dynasty that ended in 1910 produced everything that the dynasty's successors have been arguing about ever since. The colonial regime that ended in 1945 produced the wounds and the infrastructure that the two Koreas have been working out ever since. The Korean War that ended in 1953 produced the division that the peninsula is still living with seventy-two years later. The Park-Chun authoritarian period that ended in 1987 produced the economy and the trauma that the present democracy is still negotiating with. The democracy that emerged in 1987 produced both the global cultural success and the deep institutional stresses that frame the country in 2024.

This is what Korean modernity has become: an extremely successful, deeply troubled, internally polarized, externally pressured, demographically declining democracy, on a peninsula whose other half is a totalitarian dynastic nuclear state, in a region where two superpowers are competing for influence, in a world where the Korean Wave is one of the few unambiguously joyful exports of the twenty-first century. The contradictions are simultaneous and the contradictions are real.

Korea always survives, the closing line of the previous book said. It still does. What it survives into is the next chapter, which has not yet been written.

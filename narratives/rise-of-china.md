# The Rise of Modern China — How the Poorest Country on Earth Became a Superpower in Forty Years

**Goal:** Narrative for the rise of modern China (1976–2024), the period from the death of Mao Zedong and the arrest of the radicals through Deng Xiaoping's reform and opening, the rural and then urban market revolution, the Tiananmen crisis, the post-1989 capitalist surge, WTO accession and the factory-of-the-world decades, the 2008 inflection, and the Xi Jinping turn toward concentrated power, technological ambition, confrontation with the United States, the COVID emergency, and a slowing, aging, indebted economy facing an uncertain future. This is the book the previous one in this chain handed off to: the Chinese Revolution ended in 1976 with Mao dead and the Cultural Revolution radicals in handcuffs, and what followed was not more revolution but the single fastest escape from mass poverty in human history, run by the same party that had caused the famine, without ever giving up its monopoly on power. It is the most consequential economic event of the modern era and one of the most politically contested subjects on Earth, and the book walks the actual sequence without flattening it into either the official story of national rejuvenation or the Western story of an authoritarian threat, because, like the dynasties before it, it is both at once.

**Status:** First draft, 15 chapters.

A note on names and pronunciation: this book uses pinyin, the romanization in standard use for the whole period (Xi Jinping is "shee jin-PING," Zhao Ziyang "jow dzuh-YANG," Hu Jintao "hoo jin-TOW," Zhu Rongji "joo rong-JEE," Shenzhen "shun-JEN," hukou "HOO-koh"). A note on contested numbers and contested words: this is the most politically live subject in the entire series, and almost every important figure in it — the Tiananmen death toll, the number of Uyghurs detained in Xinjiang, the COVID death toll, even China's own GDP and poverty statistics — is the object of deliberate, well-funded dispute between the Chinese government, its exiled critics, Western governments, and honest researchers working from deliberately incomplete data. The contested-claims rule of this series is applied here harder than anywhere else: where a number or a characterization is disputed, the text gives the range and says who disputes it and why, rather than picking the version that flatters one side. The same applies to loaded words. Whether China's actions in Xinjiang are a "genocide," whether Tibet was "liberated," whether the social credit system is the dystopia of Western imagination — these are framing disputes, and the book's job is to tell you what is and is not actually known and to be honest about where the evidence runs out.

A note on whose story this is: for forty years the Western frame was that economic liberalization would produce political liberalization, and China would converge on a democratic market society. It did not, and the failure of that prediction is itself part of the history. The Chinese state's own frame is a story of national rejuvenation after a century of humiliation, delivered by the unbroken leadership of one party. Both frames explain real things and both are also propaganda. The reality the book tries to hold is that the same system lifted roughly 800 million people out of extreme poverty and built the surveillance state of the 2010s, and that these are not two stories but one.

# Chapter 1 — Picking Up the Pieces

The previous book in this chain ended on September 9, 1976, when **Mao Zedong** died, and then again a month later, when his designated successor arrested Mao's wife and her three allies — the **Gang of Four**, the radicals who had run the Cultural Revolution (Chinese Revolution Ch 15). That arrest ended the Maoist era by force, but it did not answer the question this entire book is about: what do you do with a country of nearly a billion people that has just spent twenty-seven years being convulsed by its own government, that is poorer per person than most of sub-Saharan Africa, where the universities have been closed for a decade and the economy runs on ration coupons, and where the only legitimating idea anyone in power has is the thought of a man who caused the worst famine in human history? The answer this book traces is the most surprising in modern economic history, and it was not obvious in 1976. For two years it looked like the answer would be "more of the same, but quieter."

The man who arrested the Gang of Four, and who ran China for those two years, was **Hua Guofeng** (pronounced "hwah gwoh-FUNG"), a colorless provincial administrator Mao had elevated near the end precisely because he threatened no faction (Chinese Revolution Ch 15). Hua's problem was that his entire claim to rule rested on having been chosen by Mao, which meant he could not repudiate Mao without sawing off the branch he sat on. His formula, which became infamous, was the **Two Whatevers**: the party would resolutely uphold whatever policy decisions Mao had made and unswervingly follow whatever instructions Mao had given. This was a dead end and everyone serious knew it, because Mao's instructions were the reason the country was on its knees. Hua built giant Maoist projects, kept the portrait up, and produced a stagnant continuation of the system that had just failed catastrophically. He lasted about two years as the real leader and is the answer to a trivia question, and the reason he failed is the key to everything that follows: in post-Mao China, legitimacy could no longer come from being Mao's heir. It had to come from results.

---

The man who understood this was **Deng Xiaoping** (pronounced "dung shyao-PING"), and his return is one of the great political comebacks of the century. Deng was a revolutionary veteran, a survivor of the Long March, a man purged twice during the Cultural Revolution — once paraded in a dunce cap, his son left paralyzed after a fall from a building during a Red Guard interrogation — and rehabilitated for the third time in 1977 at the age of seventy-two. Deng never took the top title. He was never president or party general secretary or premier during his years of supreme power; at his peak his only formal post was head of the bridge association and chairman of a military commission. He ruled as **paramount leader**, an informal status resting on seniority, control of the army, and a network of loyalists in key posts — an arrangement worth noticing because it tells you the system had no constitutional mechanism for legitimate succession, a flaw that detonates in Chapter 5 and again, differently, in Chapter 11.

Deng's instrument against the Two Whatevers was not a coup but an argument about epistemology, which sounds absurd until you see it work. In 1978 a newspaper essay argued that **"practice is the sole criterion of truth"** — meaning that the test of a policy was whether it worked, not whether it matched Mao's words. This sounds like a philosophy-seminar point and was in fact a precision-guided political weapon: if reality, not Mao's quotations, decided what was true, then Hua's Two Whatevers had no ground to stand on, and policies could be judged by results. Deng's own version was earthier and became the slogan of the age: **"seek truth from facts,"** and, more famously, "it doesn't matter whether a cat is black or white, as long as it catches mice." The cat could be capitalist. That was the whole revolution in one sentence, and it is the deepest contrast in this book with the Maoist system it replaced, where the color of the cat was the only thing that had mattered and tens of millions had died over it (Chinese Revolution Ch 12–14).

The decisive moment came at a Communist Party meeting in December 1978 remembered as the **Third Plenum** (the third full meeting of the Central Committee elected in 1977). It is conventionally dated as the start of the entire reform era. There was no dramatic announcement; what happened was that the assembled leadership shifted the party's stated central task from "class struggle" — Mao's permanent revolution — to "economic construction," and endorsed Deng's people and Deng's pragmatism. **Boluan Fanzheng** (pronounced "bwoh-lwan fahn-JENG," roughly "eliminating chaos, returning to normal") was the umbrella term for what followed: the systematic reversal of Cultural Revolution verdicts, the rehabilitation of millions of purged officials and intellectuals, the rebuilding of courts and universities, and, in 1981, a formal Party **Resolution on Party History** that rendered the verdict the regime still officially holds — that Mao had been "70 percent right and 30 percent wrong." That arithmetic was not history; it was politics. It let the party bury the Cultural Revolution and the famine as Mao's "30 percent" while keeping the legitimacy of the revolution, the party, and Mao's embalmed body in its square. The contrast with the Soviet Union, which a decade later tried to confront its own Stalinist past honestly and dissolved in the process, is one this book returns to: the Chinese party studied that collapse obsessively and drew the lesson that you never, ever pull the founding thread.

---

One more thing happened in these two years that defines the limit of the whole story. In 1978–79, on a stretch of brick wall in Beijing, citizens began pasting up handwritten posters — the **Democracy Wall** — criticizing the Cultural Revolution, Mao, and the lack of freedom. For a few months Deng tolerated and even tactically used it, because its attacks on the Maoist old guard helped him. Then an electrician named **Wei Jingsheng** (pronounced "way jing-SHENG") put up a poster arguing that Deng's Four Modernizations (agriculture, industry, defense, science — the program inherited from Zhou Enlai, Chinese Revolution Ch 15) were worthless without a **Fifth Modernization**: democracy. Within months Wei was arrested and sentenced to fifteen years, the Wall was shut down, and Deng articulated the rule that has governed China ever since in the **Four Cardinal Principles**: economic reform was permitted, but the socialist road, the people's democratic dictatorship, the leadership of the Communist Party, and Marxism-Leninism-Mao Zedong Thought were not open to question. The deal, never written but never since broken, was set in 1979: the party would deliver prosperity, and in exchange it would keep an absolute monopoly on power. Everything in the next fourteen chapters is the working-out, the testing, and the hardening of that bargain.

# Chapter 2 — The Household Responsibility System

The reform era is usually told as a story about Deng and the cities and foreign capital, and those come. But the first and largest poverty reduction happened in the countryside, it happened fast, and it started not as a policy from above but as a crime committed by desperate farmers from below. This is the single most important thing to understand about how China actually escaped poverty, and it is the opposite of the planned, engineered story the regime tells about itself.

In December 1978, in a village called **Xiaogang** (pronounced "shyao-GAHNG") in poor, flat Anhui province, eighteen households were starving. Under the **people's commune** system, the structure Mao had built in the Great Leap Forward and that had caused the famine (Chinese Revolution Ch 12–13), land was farmed collectively: everyone worked the same fields, the harvest went to the state and the collective, and individual effort earned "work points" so loosely tied to output that the rational thing to do was the minimum. The result, twenty years running, was that one of the most fertile agricultural civilizations on Earth could not feed itself. The Xiaogang farmers did something that was, at the time, potentially a capital offense: they secretly divided the collective land among their own households, agreed each family would meet the state quota and then keep everything above it, and signed a secret contract — with fingerprints, because several could not write — pledging, in the version the village itself later made famous, that if any of them were executed the others would raise their children. By Xiaogang's own account the next harvest, on the same land with the same tools, was larger than the previous five combined; the precise figures come from later commemorative retelling and the story has hardened into national legend, but the pattern it illustrates is not in doubt.

This is the mechanism the whole rural reform runs on, and it is worth stating plainly because it is the answer to the question the famine chapter of the last book left open: nothing was wrong with Chinese agriculture except the incentive. When a family kept what it grew above the quota, output exploded — not because of new technology, irrigation, or seed, but because effort was now connected to reward. The system was named the **Household Responsibility System**, and the politically delicate point is that it was not invented in Beijing. It was invented, illegally, by farmers, tolerated by brave provincial officials (notably in Anhui and Sichuan, where the reformers **Wan Li** and **Zhao Ziyang** ran cover for it), and only afterward ratified and spread nationwide by Deng's leadership, which was wise enough to take the credit. Within six years collective farming was effectively gone and rural incomes had roughly doubled. The fastest single episode of poverty reduction in the early reform era had no foreign investment and no Special Economic Zones in it at all.

---

The rural reform had a second-order effect that mattered as much as the first. Once families could feed the country with far less labor than the commune had used, tens of millions of farmers were suddenly surplus to agriculture. They did not, at first, move to the cities — the **hukou** (pronounced "HOO-koh") household-registration system, a Mao-era institution that legally tied every Chinese person to their birthplace and its rations and services, made that nearly impossible (it returns, brutally, in Chapter 8). Instead the surplus labor went into **township and village enterprises**: small rural factories, owned by local collectives, making bricks, textiles, tools, processed food, anything. They were not state-planned and not quite private, they were ferociously competitive, and through the 1980s and early 1990s they were the fastest-growing part of the entire economy. They proved, before any Special Economic Zone had produced much of anything, that production organized for the market vastly outperformed production organized for the plan — a demonstration running on Chinese soil, with Chinese peasants, that no ideology could explain away.

The politics of this period had a brief, real openness to it that later got written out of the official story. Two men ran the reform machine under Deng. **Hu Yaobang** (pronounced "hoo yao-BAHNG"), the party general secretary, was a genuine liberal by the standards of the system — he drove the rehabilitations, encouraged intellectual freedom, and was personally incapable of the cynicism the job required. **Zhao Ziyang** (pronounced "jow dzuh-YANG"), the premier, was the brilliant technocrat who designed and ran economic policy. Both men believed, to different degrees, that economic reform would eventually require political reform, and both were destroyed for it — Hu in 1987, Zhao in 1989. The reader should hold their names. The arc from the Xiaogang fingerprints to Zhao Ziyang under house arrest for the last sixteen years of his life is the arc of Chapters 2 through 5: an economic miracle and a political dead end, growing from the same root.

# Chapter 3 — Special Economic Zones and the Open Door

The other half of Deng's reform, the half the world watched, was opening China to the global economy after thirty years of near-total isolation — first under Mao's autarky, then deepened by the Sino-Soviet split that had cut off even the Communist world (Chinese Revolution Ch 13). The instrument was geographic and deliberately small: fenced-off zones where the rules of the planned economy were suspended and foreign capitalism was allowed to operate, so that if it failed or contaminated the system the damage could be contained. This containment logic is the key to the whole open door. Reform was run as a series of walled experiments precisely because the leadership did not trust it and could not admit, ideologically, what it was doing.

In 1979–80 four **Special Economic Zones** were designated on the southern coast, the most famous of them **Shenzhen** (pronounced "shun-JEN"), a stretch of paddies and fishing villages across the border from Hong Kong. The site selection was not about local resources; it was about adjacency to capital. Shenzhen was next to Hong Kong, then a British capitalist enclave with money, factory know-how, global shipping links, and a population of Cantonese-speaking entrepreneurs many of whom had family on the mainland side. Xiamen faced Taiwan; Zhuhai faced Macau; Shantou was a major emigrant hometown. The zones were antennae aimed precisely at the **overseas Chinese** — the Hong Kong, Taiwanese, and diaspora business networks built up across the centuries this series has traced (the maritime trading Chinese of Srivijaya's world; the emigrant communities the Qing both used and distrusted, Qing Dynasty Ch 5). For the first decade, the overwhelming majority of foreign investment in China was not Western multinationals; it was overseas Chinese capital, moving factories a few miles across a border into a workforce that cost a tenth as much.

What the zones offered was simple and, in the Chinese context, revolutionary: a foreign company could build a factory, hire and fire workers, pay bonuses, import components duty-free, and export the product, all outside the plan. Shekou, an industrial zone inside Shenzhen, ran up a billboard with the slogan **"time is money, efficiency is life"** — a sentence that would have been a death sentence a decade earlier and was now state policy. The transformation was physically staggering. "**Shenzhen speed**," a building going up at a rate of a floor every few days, entered the language. A region of a few hundred thousand became, within a generation, a city of over ten million and one of the densest concentrations of electronics manufacturing on the planet. Set the pace against the rest of this series: it took Song-dynasty China centuries to build its commercial revolution, Meiji Japan roughly forty years to industrialize by state decree (Meiji Japan Ch 4), and South Korea about a generation to go from war ruin to industrial power (Korean Modern Ch 3). Shenzhen did the visible part in about fifteen years, because it was not inventing the industrial economy — it was importing one that already existed, next door.

---

None of this was ideologically comfortable, and the discomfort is part of the history, not a footnote to it. Through the 1980s a recurring conservative faction inside the party attacked the zones as exactly what they were — capitalist enclaves on socialist soil, foreign-controlled, breeding inequality and "**spiritual pollution**" (a 1983 campaign against Western ideas and culture, reined in within months) and "bourgeois liberalization." The question "is Shenzhen socialist or capitalist?" was not rhetorical; it was the live fault line of elite politics for fifteen years, and the reformers' answer was always evasive on purpose. They called it "socialism with Chinese characteristics," a phrase whose genius is that it means whatever the leadership needs it to mean. The honest description, which no one in power could say aloud, was that China was running state-supervised capitalism while keeping the Communist Party's monopoly on power — the exact combination the Western expectation said was unstable and would have to resolve, one way or the other. The book's recurring thesis, the contrast with India, sharpens here: India had democracy first and a slow, hesitant, half-reformed economy until 1991 (Modern India Ch 6 and Ch 10); China had economic reform first and no democracy at all, and for thirty years China grew far faster. Which sequence was right is the question the rest of this book, and the present, are still arguing about — and Chapter 5 is where the argument turned violent.

# Chapter 4 — The Urban Reforms and the First Strains

Rural reform was, comparatively, easy: break the communes, let families keep the surplus, stand back. Urban and industrial reform was the hard problem, and the way it half-succeeded and half-failed in the late 1980s produced the inflation, the corruption, and the political fracture that exploded in 1989. This chapter is the machinery of that explosion, and its central device has a dull name and enormous consequences: the **dual-track price system**.

In 1984 the party formally extended reform from the farms to the cities and the state-owned factories. The problem was that you could not simply free prices in an economy where the state still owned most heavy industry and where a sudden jump in the price of food and fuel could trigger urban unrest — the cities were where the regime lived. The solution, characteristically, was a hybrid: the **dual-track price system**, under which the same commodity — steel, coal, cement — had two prices at once. A factory's planned quota was bought and sold at the old, low, state-set price; anything produced above quota could be sold at the high market price. The theory was elegant: keep the plan stable while letting a market grow at the margin, and gradually let the market track swallow the plan track. In practice it worked, the economy grew fast, and it created the single most corrosive thing in 1980s China.

The corrosive thing was arbitrage, and you should understand it concretely because it is what people were actually furious about in 1989. If you could buy a truckload of steel at the planned price and sell it at the market price, you doubled or tripled your money for doing nothing — and the people positioned to do that were officials, and the children of officials, who controlled the allocation quotas. This was **guandao** (pronounced "gwahn-DAO"), "official profiteering," and it was not a few bad apples; it was the structural design of the transition handing a money-printing machine to the politically connected. The phrase "**princelings**" — the children of senior leaders, converting their parents' power into private fortune — enters Chinese political life here and never leaves it; it is the through-line to the anti-corruption purges of Chapter 11. Ordinary people watching prices rise while well-connected families visibly enriched themselves drew the obvious conclusion: the reform was making a corrupt elite rich. They were not wrong, and that grievance, more than abstract democracy, is what filled Tiananmen Square.

---

Then the machinery jammed. By 1988 the reformers, with Deng's backing, decided to break through to full market pricing in one push. The announcement that price controls would be lifted, made into an economy already running hot, triggered exactly what the gradualists had feared: a wave of **inflation** that hit double digits, panic withdrawals from banks, and panic buying — people converting cash into anything durable before it lost value. The leadership lost its nerve, reversed the price reform, and imposed an austerity "rectification." The reform program was now visibly stalled, the reformers were discredited, prices were still rising, corruption was rampant, and the party leadership had split openly between a market-acceleration camp around Zhao Ziyang and a retrench-and-control camp around the conservatives. This is the economic situation in the spring of 1989: not collapse, but a fast, unequal, inflationary boom that had enriched the connected and frightened everyone else, run by a leadership visibly at war with itself.

Over this sat a decade of unusual intellectual ferment that the official history later erased. The 1980s had a "**culture fever**": a flood of translated Western philosophy and fiction, packed lecture halls, fierce argument about whether Chinese tradition itself was the thing holding the country back. Its highest-profile artifact was a 1988 television series, *River Elegy*, watched by hundreds of millions, which argued that China's inward, agrarian, river-and-soil civilization had to open to the "blue" oceanic civilization of the modern world or die — a direct descendant of the New Culture Movement's argument seventy years earlier (Chinese Revolution Ch 3), and proof that the question that movement raised had never actually been resolved, only suppressed. Students who had grown up in this ferment, watching inflation and *guandao* and a stalled reform and a divided leadership, were primed. All they needed was an occasion to gather, and in April 1989 one arrived in the form of a funeral.

# Chapter 5 — Tiananmen 1989

This is the hinge chapter of the book, the moment the bargain set in 1979 — prosperity in exchange for a monopoly on power — was challenged head-on and defended with the army, and every subsequent chapter is shaped by how it ended. It is also one of the most contested events in modern history, contested down to the basic facts, and the contested-claims rule of this series has to be applied to it directly and stated up front: the Chinese state has spent thirty-five years erasing this event from its own history, the exile and Western accounts have their own pressures, and the most basic number — how many people were killed — is genuinely not known and is given here as a range, not a figure.

The occasion was a death. In April 1989, **Hu Yaobang** — the liberal party chief purged in 1987 for being too soft on the 1986–87 student protests (Chapter 4) — died of a heart attack. Public mourning for a disgraced reformer was the only legal way to make a political statement, exactly the mechanism by which mourning for Zhou Enlai had become a protest in 1976 (Chinese Revolution Ch 15), and students poured into **Tiananmen Square**, the vast ceremonial plaza at the center of Beijing where Mao had proclaimed the People's Republic in 1949 (Chinese Revolution Ch 9). The mourning became a sit-in, the sit-in became a movement, and the movement's demands were not, mostly, for Western multiparty democracy in the abstract. They were against **corruption**, against inflation, against *guandao* and the princelings, for press freedom, for dialogue, for the party to live up to its own stated ideals. The movement spread to scores of cities and drew in workers and ordinary citizens, which is precisely what made it lethal to the leadership: students could be lectured, but a worker-student alliance in the capital was the thing that had brought down dynasties.

In May, two things raised the stakes beyond control. The students launched a **hunger strike** in the square, which won enormous public sympathy and put the government in the position of letting young people starve on television. And the strike was timed to the state visit of the Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev — the first Sino-Soviet summit since the split (Chinese Revolution Ch 13), meant to be Deng's diplomatic triumph, with the world's press already in Beijing. The welcoming ceremony had to be moved and humiliatingly improvised because the square was full of protesters. The leadership split in the open. Zhao Ziyang, the premier and party chief, argued for conciliation and dialogue; the hardliners, with Premier **Li Peng** the public face, argued for force. Deng sided with force. Zhao went to the square in person, in what he knew was the end of his career, and told the students through a bullhorn, "We have come too late." He was purged within days and spent the remaining sixteen years of his life under house arrest, the highest-ranking casualty of the crisis and a deliberately erased non-person until his death in 2005.

---

Martial law was declared. On the night of June 3–4, 1989, the **People's Liberation Army** — the army of the revolution, the peasant army of the Long March and 1949 (Chinese Revolution Ch 6, 9) — was sent into the center of its own capital to clear it by force. The killing happened mostly not in the square itself but on the approach avenues, especially to the west, where the army shot its way through crowds of citizens trying to block the columns. The death toll is the contested number, and here is the honest state of it: the Chinese government's figure is roughly 200–300, almost all of whom it called rioters and soldiers; estimates from hospital records, the Tiananmen Mothers victims' group, foreign diplomats, and journalists range from several hundred to well over a thousand, with some early estimates far higher. The precise figure may never be known because the state that controls the records has spent thirty-five years ensuring it cannot be established and the event cannot be publicly mentioned inside China at all. What is not in dispute is that the state used the army to kill an unknown but substantial number of unarmed citizens in Beijing to end the protest, and that it worked. The single most reproduced image of the era is **Tank Man**, an unidentified man with shopping bags who stood in front of a column of tanks on Chang'an Avenue the next day; his identity and fate are unknown, and the image is so thoroughly censored inside China that many young Chinese have never seen it.

The consequences set the terms of the entire rest of this book. The party drew the lesson, explicitly, that political liberalization is an existential threat and economic growth is the thing that buys consent, and it would spend the next thirty-five years delivering the second to forestall the first — a bet whose strain is the subject of the final chapters. The West imposed arms embargoes and sanctions, China was briefly an international pariah, and for two or three years the entire reform project hung in the balance as the conservatives, vindicated, moved to roll it back. The reformers Hu and Zhao were gone. It looked, in 1990, as though China might freeze into a poorer, harder, more isolated version of itself. That it did the opposite — that within three years it launched the greatest economic boom in human history — is the most counterintuitive turn in the story, and it was the work of an eighty-seven-year-old man with no office taking a train south.

# Chapter 6 — The Southern Tour and the Capitalist Turn

For roughly two and a half years after the 1989 crackdown, China froze. The conservative faction, its warnings about where reform led apparently vindicated by the chaos in the square, was ascendant; the economy was deliberately cooled; the press talked about the dangers of "peaceful evolution" (the idea that the West would subvert socialism through trade and ideas rather than war); and across 1989–91 the entire Communist world outside China collapsed — the Berlin Wall, the Eastern European revolutions, and then, at the end of 1991, the dissolution of the Soviet Union itself. For the Chinese leadership this was not abstract. The country that had been the model for the entire system, the USSR that this series will eventually cover in its own right, simply ceased to exist, and its Communist Party with it. The lesson the Chinese hardliners drew was "tighten everything." The lesson Deng drew was the opposite and it is the most important strategic judgment in the book: the Soviet Union had died because it reformed politics without delivering economic goods, and the only thing that could save the Chinese Communist Party was to grow the economy so fast and so undeniably that prosperity, not ideology, became the basis of consent.

In early 1992, Deng Xiaoping, eighty-seven years old, holding no office, made what is called the **Southern Tour** — a trip to Shenzhen and the southern boom zones (Chapter 3), where he made a series of blunt, pointed remarks, carried at first only by the southern press because the central propaganda apparatus tried to bury them: that reform must accelerate, that whoever opposed it should "step down," that the test was not whether something was named socialist or capitalist but whether it raised production, living standards, and national power, and that "development is the only hard truth." It was an old man with no formal authority going over the heads of the entire central leadership by appealing directly to the regions getting rich, and it worked because those regions, and the provincial officials whose careers now depended on growth, mobilized behind it. Within months the conservative line was routed, and later in 1992 the Party Congress formally adopted the goal of a "**socialist market economy**" — the phrase that finally, officially, ended the thirty-year question of whether markets were ideologically allowed. The answer was yes, and the floodgates opened.

---

What followed was the takeoff. The early-to-mid 1990s saw an investment and construction boom, a surge of foreign capital now that the political question was settled, and a cultural shift captured in the slang word **xiahai** (pronounced "shyah-HAI"), "jumping into the sea" — officials, academics, and engineers quitting the safe state system to go into private business. "To get rich is glorious," a slogan attributed (probably apocryphally, the contested-quote rule applies) to Deng, was the spirit of the decade whether or not he said the words. The leadership that executed this was the new generation Deng had installed after purging Zhao: **Jiang Zemin** (pronounced "jyang dzuh-MIN"), the former Shanghai party boss elevated to general secretary as a compromise after Tiananmen, and the technocrat premier **Zhu Rongji** (pronounced "joo rong-JEE"), who ran the economy. Their signature structural achievement, easy to overlook and enormously consequential, was the **1994 tax-sharing reform**, which recentralized revenue collection to Beijing. Before it, the central government was fiscally weak relative to the provinces; after it, Beijing controlled the money. That fiscal machine is what funded the next two decades of state capacity — the infrastructure, the military, the surveillance state, the ability to run a giant stimulus in 2008. The boom is what people remember; the tax reform is what made the state that managed the boom.

**Deng Xiaoping died in February 1997**, at ninety-two, a few months before the event that was his last great design came due. He had outlived Mao by twenty-one years and had done something Mao never managed: he had built a system that could outlive its founder and transfer power without killing anyone at the top. The proof was that nothing happened. Jiang Zemin was already in charge; the reforms continued; there was no succession war. After two thousand years of Chinese history in which the death of the strongman meant crisis — the pattern this entire series has traced from the First Emperor through the Qing through Mao himself (Chinese Revolution Ch 15) — a paramount leader died and the machine simply kept running. That, more than any single reform, was Deng's achievement, and Chapter 11 is the story of the man who undid it.

# Chapter 7 — Joining the World

The decade from 1997 to 2002 is when China stopped being a large developing country that exported cheap goods and became structurally fused to the global economy in a way that would reshape both. Three things happened close together — the Hong Kong handover, the Asian Financial Crisis, and WTO accession — and the way the leadership handled all three came from the same source: the post-1989, post-Soviet conviction that economic results were the only thing standing between the party and the fate of its Soviet model.

On July 1, 1997, **Hong Kong** reverted to Chinese sovereignty, ending 155 years of British colonial rule that had begun with the First Opium War and the Treaty of Nanking (Qing Dynasty Ch 7). The terms had been set in the 1984 **Sino-British Joint Declaration** and the formula was **"one country, two systems"**: Hong Kong would keep its capitalist economy, common-law courts, free press, and a high degree of autonomy for fifty years, until 2047. For the regime this was the literal, ceremonial reversal of the opening humiliation of the entire previous book — the foreign powers who had dictated terms to a prostrate Qing handing the richest of their concessions back — and it was framed exactly that way. The bet underlying "one country, two systems" was that Hong Kong's freedoms could be quarantined behind a system boundary the same way the Special Economic Zones had quarantined capitalism (Chapter 3). Whether that quarantine could hold is Chapter 13, and the answer is that it did not.

Almost immediately the **Asian Financial Crisis** hit. Through 1997–98 the high-growth economies of Southeast Asia and then South Korea (the "miracle" economies, Korean Modern Ch 5) saw their currencies and markets collapse as hot foreign capital fled. China, with a largely closed capital account and a non-convertible currency, was insulated, and it made a decision that bought it enormous regional credibility: it did not devalue the yuan, which would have triggered a second round of competitive devaluations across the region. The contrast the leadership drew, and broadcast, was pointed: the open, liberalized economies had been wrecked by volatile global capital, and China's controlled, gradualist model had stood firm. It was a real argument and it shaped Chinese policy for a generation — a deep suspicion of fully open capital markets that persists today and is one reason the financial system never liberalized the way the trade economy did.

---

The hardest and least-remembered part of this period was domestic and brutal. Premier Zhu Rongji forced through the restructuring of the **state-owned enterprises** — the old socialist factories that employed urban China under the "iron rice bowl" of guaranteed lifetime jobs, housing, and pensions. The policy, "**grasp the big, let go of the small**," kept a few hundred strategic giants and cut loose or closed tens of thousands of the rest. The human cost was a wave of layoffs — *xiagang* — that put tens of millions of urban workers out of the only kind of job the system had ever promised them. This was the end of Maoist urban socialism, executed by the Communist Party itself, and it is one of the great untold mass dislocations of the era, absorbed with remarkably little organized resistance partly because the booming private and export economy was creating other jobs and partly because protest had been answered, definitively, in 1989. In the same years the state privatized urban housing, ending allocated work-unit apartments and creating overnight a private property market that would become the principal store of Chinese household wealth and, eventually, the economy's central vulnerability (Chapter 15).

The capstone was **accession to the World Trade Organization** in 2001, the single most consequential economic event in the book. WTO membership locked in China's access to the markets of the developed world, gave foreign investors legal security, and committed China to a body of trade rules. The result over the following decade was an export and manufacturing expansion with no precedent in economic history — exports roughly quadrupling in under a decade — and a corresponding hollowing of manufacturing employment across the developed world, the political aftershocks of which are still running through American and European politics. The Western governments that championed Chinese WTO entry did so on an explicit theory, stated at the time: that trade and prosperity would liberalize China politically. This is the central failed prediction of the era, and the book has to be honest that it failed: China integrated economically and grew more authoritarian, not less, and the gap between that expectation and what actually happened is itself one of the most important facts in modern history. The one episode that showed it most clearly in this very period was the 1999 banning of **Falun Gong**, a popular *qigong* spiritual movement, after ten thousand of its practitioners silently surrounded the leadership compound in Beijing; the state's reaction — total suppression of a non-political meditation group simply because it had demonstrated the capacity to organize millions outside party control — told you everything about which lesson 1989 had actually taught.

# Chapter 8 — The Factory of the World

The decade from roughly 2002 to 2012, under the leadership of **Hu Jintao** (pronounced "hoo jin-TOW," no relation to the liberal reformer Hu Yaobang of Chapters 2 and 5) and Premier **Wen Jiabao** (pronounced "wen jyah-BAO"), is the boom in its purest form: the years China became, in fact and not just in slogan, the factory of the world, lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty, and accumulated the contradictions — inequality, environmental ruin, corruption, a hollow political life — that the next leader would ride to power by promising to fix. It is, in the official telling, the golden decade. It is also the decade the system's costs came due, and both are true.

The engine had a human name, and the rule of this series is to describe the mechanism, not just the result: the **migrant workers**. The export boom did not run on robots; it ran on roughly 200 to 300 million people who left the countryside for the coastal factory cities and who, because of the **hukou** registration system (Chapter 2), were legally non-residents where they worked. A migrant from rural Sichuan building skyscrapers in Shenzhen or assembling iPhones in a Foxconn dormitory city could not enroll their children in the local school, access local health care, or claim local benefits, because their household registration was still rural and a thousand miles away. This was not an oversight; it was the design. It gave Chinese export manufacturing a vast supply of labor that was mobile, cheap, disenfranchised where it worked, and could be sent home in a downturn — the single biggest reason Chinese goods were unbeatably cheap, and a structural injustice that built modern China the way the disenfranchised labor of earlier industrial revolutions built theirs. The reader should hold the comparison the book keeps drawing: India in these same years was also growing fast, but as a democracy where the rural poor could vote, which made an Indian hukou impossible and Indian growth slower, messier, and less brutal to its own workers (Modern India Ch 10–12). Which of those was the better bargain is exactly the argument the two countries embody.

The scale of what this produced is genuinely hard to convey and the contested-claims rule applies even to the triumphs. The World Bank figure, repeated everywhere, is that China lifted roughly 800 million people out of extreme poverty over the reform era — the largest and fastest poverty reduction in human history, and broadly real, not propaganda. But the number depends heavily on where the poverty line is drawn and how much of it is the rural reform of Chapter 2 versus the export boom of this chapter, and serious economists argue about the decomposition. What is not in dispute is that a country poorer per person than most of Africa in 1978 had, within a single working life, built the physical infrastructure — ports, power, the world's largest high-speed rail network, whole cities — of a developed economy, and that hundreds of millions of people who were born in mud-floored poverty died, or will die, in heated apartments with refrigerators. That is the central fact that any honest account, including this one, has to put on the table before it puts anything else.

---

The costs were equally real and the Hu–Wen leadership's response to them was mostly to name them. Inequality, measured by the standard indices, rose to among the highest in the world; the coastal-inland and urban-rural gaps became chasms; the environment was wrecked on a scale that shortened lives in measurable millions; and corruption metastasized as the dual-track logic of the 1980s (Chapter 4) scaled up into a system where political power was the surest route to a fortune. Hu Jintao's answer was the slogan "**harmonious society**," an official admission that growth-at-all-costs had produced dangerous fracture — and largely an admission without a remedy, because the remedies (an independent press, courts, unions, accountable officials) were precisely the political liberalization 1989 had forbidden. The decade's defining scandal-genre — poisoned **infant formula** in 2008 that sickened 300,000 babies, contaminated everything, collapsing buildings — all traced to the same root: an economy moving faster than any mechanism of accountability the political system would permit. The early concealment of the **SARS** epidemic in 2003, a deadly coronavirus whose spread was worsened by officials suppressing the news, was the clearest single warning of where that combination led, and it is the direct rehearsal for Chapter 15. The system could build a high-speed rail network in a decade. It could not let a doctor tell the truth about a virus, and the same decade contained both facts.

# Chapter 9 — The Olympics and the Global Crisis

The year 2008 gets its own chapter because it is the inflection point of the entire story — the year China's arrival was staged for the world, the year the Western financial model that China had been told to emulate blew up, and the year China's response to that blow-up set it on the debt-fueled path that defines everything after. Compressed into twelve months are the triumph, the tragedy, the scandal, and the decision that mortgaged the future.

The triumph was the **Beijing Summer Olympics**. The opening ceremony in the **Bird's Nest** stadium in August 2008, watched by a global television audience that promotional figures put at several billion (a much-repeated, essentially unverifiable number), was the most expensive and elaborate in the history of the Games and was explicitly designed as a coming-out: the message, aimed at a domestic and global audience at once, was that the century of humiliation that opened the previous book (Qing Dynasty Ch 7–13; Chinese Revolution, the whole arc) was definitively over and China had resumed its place as a first-rank civilization. For the regime it was the emotional payoff of thirty years of the bargain — look what the trade has bought. The staging was flawless and the subtext was unmistakable, and it is worth taking seriously as the genuine national feeling it expressed, not dismissing as mere propaganda, because the resentment it was answering was real and a hundred years old.

The tragedy came three months before the Games. In May 2008 a magnitude-7.9 **earthquake** struck Sichuan province and killed nearly 70,000 people. It became politically explosive for one reason: a disproportionate number of the dead were schoolchildren, crushed when shoddily built schools collapsed while sturdier buildings nearby stood — "tofu-dreg" construction, the visible product of the corruption and unaccountable local power the Hu-era slogans had named but not fixed (Chapter 8). Parents who tried to investigate or protest were suppressed; the artist **Ai Weiwei**, who compiled a list of the dead children's names, was beaten and later detained (Chapter 10). The earthquake is the moment the costs of the model acquired a body count the public could see and count, and the state's instinct — to manage the grief and bury the accountability — previewed the next fifteen years.

---

Then, in September 2008, the Western financial system collapsed. The American mortgage crisis became a global crash, world trade seized, and within months Chinese exports — the engine of Chapter 8 — fell off a cliff, tens of thousands of factories closed, and an estimated twenty million-plus migrant workers lost their jobs and went home. For the first time since 1989 the bargain looked genuinely at risk: the prosperity half of "prosperity for a monopoly on power" was suddenly not being delivered, and the leadership knew exactly what unemployed millions and a broken promise had produced before.

The response was the most consequential economic decision in the book after WTO accession: a **four-trillion-yuan stimulus**, one of the largest fiscal interventions in history relative to the size of the economy, poured almost entirely into infrastructure and construction and financed by an explosion of lending through local-government financing vehicles and the property sector. In the short run it worked spectacularly: China kept growing through the global recession, pulled commodity exporters and the world economy along with it, and emerged in 2010 as the world's second-largest economy, having passed Japan — the country whose own miracle had been the model the whole East Asian path descended from, and whose post-1990 stagnation (Japanese Economic Miracle Ch 7–8) was the cautionary tale China told itself it would avoid. In the long run the 2008 stimulus built the trap the final chapters describe: it made growth dependent on ever-rising debt and ever-more construction, it inflated a property sector toward a quarter of the entire economy, and it bred the ghost cities, the local-government debt mountain, and the developer time bombs that come due in Chapters 10 and 15. 2008 is the year China saved the world economy and bought its own future on credit, and it did both with the same check.

# Chapter 10 — The Disaffected Decade and the Bo Xilai Affair

The years from 2009 to 2012 are the system's hangover and its hinge: the boom's costs were now undeniable, the stimulus had papered over them with debt, the internet had briefly opened a semi-public space in which the public could see and say what was wrong, and the impending leadership succession turned into the most dangerous elite crisis since 1989 — not a movement in a square this time, but a knife fight at the top. How that fight was resolved produced Xi Jinping, and the chapter has to explain it because nothing about the next decade makes sense without it.

The mood was the new fact. After 2008 the implicit promise of ever-rising prosperity-for-quiescence was visibly strained: the stimulus had inflated property prices beyond what ordinary workers could ever afford, the post-stimulus landscape filled with **ghost cities** (vast newly built districts with few residents, construction as an end in itself because building was what kept the growth numbers up), local governments were drowning in off-books debt, and the gap between the connected rich and everyone else had become the society's defining feature. For a few years there was an unprecedented place to say so. The microblog platform **Weibo**, launched in 2009, created a fast, national, semi-public square — the first in PRC history — where scandals, official lies, and disasters could go viral before the censors caught up. The 2011 **Wenzhou high-speed-rail crash**, and the authorities' attempt to literally bury a wrecked carriage while victims might still have been inside, became the era's defining online parable about speed, cover-up, and contempt — and it spread, uncontrollably, on Weibo before the apparatus shut the conversation down. The brief Weibo era is important precisely because it was brief: it showed that the demand for accountability had never gone away, only been denied an outlet, and it showed the leadership the same thing, which is why closing it became a priority of the next chapter.

The regime's nerves showed. In 2011, anonymous online calls for **"Jasmine"** gatherings, echoing the Arab Spring then toppling autocrats across the Middle East, produced almost no actual protesters but an enormous, panicky preemptive crackdown — police flooding the named street corners, dissidents detained, the word "jasmine" censored. The reaction was wildly disproportionate to the threat, and that disproportion is the tell: the leadership was watching long-stable authoritarian regimes fall in a chain reaction and was not at all confident the bargain would hold if it were ever seriously tested. The same year the dissident **Liu Xiaobo**, a Tiananmen veteran and lead author of **Charter 08** — a 2008 manifesto calling for constitutional democracy, signed by thousands — was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize while serving an eleven-year sentence, and the regime's fury (an empty chair at the ceremony, his widow under house arrest, his eventual death in custody in 2017) showed how absolutely the 1989 line still held twenty years on.

---

Into this came the succession crisis. China was due for its once-a-decade leadership transition in 2012, and the system Deng built (Chapter 6) still had no real mechanism for it beyond elite bargaining. The man who tried to force the outcome was **Bo Xilai** (pronounced "bwoh shee-LYE"), a charismatic "princeling" (the son of a revolutionary elder, exactly the category Chapter 4 introduced) running the megacity of Chongqing, where he had built a national profile on a populist program of Mao-nostalgic "red culture" mass-singing and a ferocious, lawless anti-mafia crackdown. Bo was campaigning, in a system where campaigning for the top is forbidden, for a seat at the apex. The affair detonated in 2012 when his police chief, **Wang Lijun**, fearing for his life, fled to a US consulate and handed over evidence that Bo's wife had murdered a British businessman, Neil Heywood. The scandal — murder, a defection, a princeling's naked power grab — was the largest elite rupture since Tiananmen, and it was resolved by Bo's purge, a life sentence, and the elevation of the man who emerged from the wreckage as the safe consensus candidate of an elite badly frightened by what it had just seen. That man was **Xi Jinping**, and the party that chose him in 2012 believed it was choosing a dull, reliable compromise. It was the single greatest misreading in modern Chinese politics.

# Chapter 11 — Xi Jinping Takes Power

The elite that installed **Xi Jinping** (pronounced "shee jin-PING") in 2012 expected a continuation: another consensus technocrat in the Jiang–Hu mold, presiding over collective leadership, term limits, and the quiet enrichment of the families at the top. Within five years he had concentrated more personal power than anyone since Mao, reversed the central political achievement of the Deng era, and turned the party from a machine for managing growth into an instrument of one man's will. This chapter is how he did it, and the mechanism — not just the fact — is the point, because it is a textbook case of using a real grievance to dismantle the system that produced it.

The instrument was the **anti-corruption campaign**, and its genius was that the corruption was completely real. The dual-track profiteering of the 1980s (Chapter 4) had, over thirty years, grown into a system in which the families of senior leaders controlled vast fortunes and entire industries, and ordinary Chinese knew it and hated it. Xi launched a campaign promising to hunt both "**tigers and flies**" — senior leaders and petty officials alike — and it was, by the numbers, the largest anti-corruption drive in the history of any modern state: well over a million officials disciplined, including a former member of the Politburo Standing Committee and security overlord, **Zhou Yongkang**, the most senior figure prosecuted in the PRC's history, plus top generals and the heads of state industries. Here the contested-claims rule matters: the campaign was simultaneously a genuine attack on a corruption that was strangling the country *and* the most effective purge of rivals in modern Chinese politics, and the honest reading is that it was both at once and that the two functions were inseparable — every tiger removed was both a thief and an obstacle. It made Xi genuinely popular with a public sick of princelings, and it made every official in China understand that his career and freedom now depended on personal loyalty to one man.

The ideological turn was equally deliberate. Xi's signature concept was the "**Chinese Dream**," the "great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation," explicitly fusing party rule to national pride and the century-of-humiliation memory that runs through this entire series — the same emotional engine Mao had used in 1949 (Chinese Revolution Ch 9), now attached to a rich and rising country rather than a poor and wounded one. Internally, a 2013 directive, **Document No. 9**, instructed the apparatus to combat "false ideological trends" — constitutional democracy, "universal values," civil society, an independent press, "historical nihilism" (honest discussion of the party's own past). It was the ideological blueprint of the era and the formal end of the brief Weibo openness of Chapter 10: the semi-public square was systematically closed, the 2015 **"709" crackdown** gutted the small community of human-rights lawyers who had tried to use the regime's own courts, and the space for any organized activity outside the party narrowed to nothing.

---

The capstone was the reversal of Deng's succession system. Deng's deepest institutional achievement had been the orderly, term-limited, collective transfer of power that let the system survive its founder's death (Chapter 6) — the solution to the two-thousand-year Chinese problem of what happens when the strongman dies. In 2016 Xi was designated the party's "**core**" leader, a status Hu Jintao never held. In 2017 his name and "Xi Jinping Thought" were written into the party constitution while he was still in office, an honor previously reserved, in that form, for Mao. And in 2018 the National People's Congress abolished the constitutional **two-term limit on the presidency**, clearing the way for Xi to rule indefinitely. This was the single most consequential political act in the book after Tiananmen, and its meaning is precise: it undid the one mechanism that had made post-Mao China different from imperial and Maoist China, and reintroduced exactly the problem Deng had solved — a system whose stability depends on one man and which has no agreed way to replace him. The chapter that began the series, two thousand years and many books ago, was about a dynasty that fell because legitimacy and succession had no stable form (Qing Dynasty Ch 13). In 2018 the most powerful state on Earth chose, deliberately, to reopen that question. One genuinely humane reform ran the other way and is worth recording: the **one-child policy** (the coercive one-child-per-family birth-control system imposed from 1980 and enforced with fines, mandatory IUDs, sterilizations, and forced abortions) was finally ended in 2015–16, not from liberalization but from demographic panic — the workforce was already shrinking, and the bill for thirty-five years of coercive birth limits, the subject of Chapter 15, had come due.

---

# Chapter 12 — The Belt and Road and the Tech Ambition

While Chapter 11 was the consolidation of power at home, this chapter is its projection outward and downward — China's bid to reshape the global economy in its favor and to build, at home, the most technologically sophisticated apparatus of social control ever attempted. These ran in parallel through the 2010s and they are the same project seen from two ends: a state confident enough to try to lead the world and anxious enough to want to see and score every one of its own citizens.

The outward project was the **Belt and Road Initiative**, announced by Xi in 2013: a vast, loosely defined program of Chinese-financed and Chinese-built infrastructure — ports, railways, highways, power plants, fiber — across Asia, Africa, Latin America, and into Europe. At its most ambitious it was the largest development-finance program since the American Marshall Plan that rebuilt postwar Europe, and its logic was multiple at once: an outlet for the construction industry and excess steel and cement the post-2008 economy could no longer absorb at home (Chapter 9), a way to secure energy and resource routes, a tool of strategic influence, and a statement that the international economic order would now have a Chinese pole. The Western critique that crystallized around it was "**debt-trap diplomacy**," the claim that China deliberately lent unpayable sums to seize strategic assets when borrowers defaulted, with Sri Lanka's 99-year lease of the Chinese-built **Hambantota port** as the totem case. The contested-claims rule applies squarely: careful research has found the deliberate-trap thesis mostly overstated — the debt problems are real but largely the ordinary result of bad projects and bad lending rather than a master plan — while the strategic-influence reality is also genuine. The honest statement is that Belt and Road extended Chinese power and entangled dozens of countries in Chinese finance, and that the cartoon version of it is not supported by the evidence, and both halves matter.

---

The domestic project had two faces. One was a genuine technological surge. China built, in roughly a decade, a near-cashless economy: **mobile payment** by QR code through two private super-apps, Alibaba's Alipay and Tencent's WeChat, went from novelty to near-universal, leapfrogging credit cards entirely. The "**platform economy**" — Alibaba, Tencent, a generation of giants and their founders, the English-teacher-turned-billionaire Jack Ma of Alibaba the emblem — was for about a decade the freest, most dynamic, most globally competitive part of Chinese life, and it coexisted with industrial policy (**Made in China 2025**, the explicit 2015 plan to dominate robotics, electric vehicles, aerospace, and semiconductors) and a methodical space program that landed the first probe ever on the far side of the Moon in 2019. This is the genuinely impressive China, and it is the same China as the other face.

The other face was the **surveillance state**. The 2010s saw the construction of the most comprehensive domestic-monitoring apparatus in history: hundreds of millions of cameras, facial recognition, big-data policing, and a patchwork of "**social credit**" blacklisting and scoring schemes. Here the contested-claims rule has to work hard against a specific Western myth: there was never, in the period of this book, a single unified national score that rated every citizen's life like a credit rating — that image is mostly a Western dystopian extrapolation. What actually existed was real and grim enough without exaggeration: fragmented but expanding blacklists that could bar people from flights, trains, schools, and jobs, with minimal due process, plus pervasive camera and data surveillance. And where the apparatus was turned on fully, in **Xinjiang**, it was not a metaphor. From around 2017 the state subjected the Uyghurs, a Turkic-speaking, mostly Muslim people of the far northwest, to mass internment in "re-education" camps, pervasive surveillance, family separation, forced labor, and birth suppression. The numbers and the word are both contested: estimates of those detained run broadly to around a million or more; the United States and several parliaments have formally called it "genocide," other governments and scholars argue for "crimes against humanity" as the more defensible legal characterization, and China denies it entirely and calls the camps vocational training. What the satellite imagery, leaked internal documents, and consistent survivor testimony establish beyond serious doubt is that a mass system of arbitrary detention and coercive control was built and run on an ethnic and religious group. This series has, across many books, described states absorbing and erasing peoples; this is that, documented in real time, by a state powerful enough to deny it to the world's face.

# Chapter 13 — Hong Kong and Taiwan

This chapter is about the two places where the "one country, two systems" promise of Chapter 7 met its test, and where the contradiction the whole book has tracked — a regime that delivers prosperity but cannot tolerate autonomous political life — became impossible to paper over. Hong Kong is where the promise was made and broken inside the book's own timespan. Taiwan is the unfinished business of the entire series, the place the losing side of the Chinese Revolution fled in 1949 (Chinese Revolution Ch 9) and never surrendered.

When Hong Kong returned in 1997 (Chapter 7), "one country, two systems" promised its capitalist economy, independent courts, free press, and a path toward democratic self-government would be preserved until 2047. For about fifteen years the quarantine mostly held. Then it visibly began to fail, in escalating stages. In 2014 the **Umbrella Movement** — a 79-day occupation of central Hong Kong demanding genuine universal suffrage for the city's leader, the protesters using umbrellas against police pepper spray — won no concessions but radicalized a generation and showed Beijing that the city would not quietly integrate. The 2015 abduction into mainland custody of five **Hong Kong booksellers** who sold gossipy political titles showed the system boundary was already porous from the mainland side. The breaking point came in 2019: a proposed law allowing extradition to the mainland triggered protests that, at their peak, put an estimated quarter to a third of the entire population into the streets, escalating over months into a broad, sometimes violent pro-democracy movement and the gravest sustained challenge to Beijing's authority since 1989.

Beijing's answer, in 2020, was the **National Security Law**: a sweeping statute, imposed directly by Beijing over the heads of Hong Kong's own legislature, criminalizing secession, subversion, terrorism, and "collusion with foreign forces" in terms broad enough to cover most opposition politics and most critical journalism. It worked, in the narrow sense that the protests ended, the opposition was arrested or exiled or silenced, the free press was gutted, and within a few years a wave of Hong Kongers emigrated, many on a British visa scheme created in response. "One country, two systems," designed as the model that was also meant to entice Taiwan, was effectively finished twenty-seven years before its fifty-year guarantee expired. The lesson Beijing drew was that the quarantine model had been naive and that control was the only safe state. The lesson Taiwan drew was the opposite and more consequential, and it is the hinge of the chapter.

---

**Taiwan** is where this entire series' Chinese arc has been heading and where it remains unresolved. The island has been governed since 1949 by the state the Nationalists set up after losing the civil war (Chinese Revolution Ch 9); over the following decades, and unlike the mainland, it underwent a genuine democratic transition and became, by the 2000s, a liberal democracy with its own elections, free press, and an increasingly distinct identity. The framework that had kept the peace was the deliberately ambiguous "**1992 Consensus**" — an agreement that both sides accepted there was "one China" while never agreeing what that meant. Through the 2010s that ambiguity eroded from both ends. On the island, the 2014 **Sunflower Movement** (students occupying the legislature against a trade pact that would deepen dependence on the mainland) turned Taiwanese politics decisively against integration, and in 2016 Taiwan elected **Tsai Ing-wen** of the independence-leaning party. On the mainland, Xi explicitly tied "reunification" to the national rejuvenation timetable and abandoned strategic patience for steadily escalating military pressure — record warplane incursions, large exercises, the open preparation of options. Watching Hong Kong's "two systems" be dismantled removed any incentive for Taiwan to accept the same offer, which is the central irony of the chapter: the crushing of Hong Kong, intended to demonstrate control, destroyed the model that was supposed to win Taiwan peacefully. The Taiwan question is the one major thread this series cannot close, because it is not closed: it is, as of the end of this book, the single most dangerous flashpoint between great powers on Earth, and how it resolves belongs to a history not yet written.

---

# Chapter 14 — The Wolf Warriors and the US Rivalry

For thirty years the organizing assumption of the global economy, and of American policy, was engagement: that integrating China into the world economy would make it richer, more like its trading partners, and a stakeholder in the existing order. Chapter by chapter this book has shown why that assumption failed. This chapter is about the period, roughly 2017–2022, when both sides finally admitted it had failed and the relationship flipped from engagement to open strategic rivalry — the defining geopolitical fact of the present and the frame in which everything in the final chapter happens.

The economic break came as a **trade war**. From 2018 the United States imposed tariffs on hundreds of billions of dollars of Chinese goods, citing the trade deficit, forced technology transfer, intellectual-property theft, and the explicit threat of the **Made in China 2025** industrial plan (Chapter 12) to American technological primacy. The deeper conflict, and the one that will outlast any particular tariff, was technological: a **chip war**, in which the US and its allies progressively cut China off from the most advanced semiconductors and, more decisively, the equipment and software needed to make them — an attempt to freeze China a generation behind at the technological commanding height, and the single most serious external constraint on the Chinese model. The flashpoint that made it concrete was the 2018 arrest in Canada, on US sanctions charges, of **Meng Wanzhou**, the chief financial officer of the telecom champion **Huawei** and the daughter of its founder, and China's retaliatory detention of two Canadians in conditions widely described as hostage-taking. Engagement was over; the language now was "**decoupling**," later softened to "de-risking," the deliberate partial unwinding of the economic integration that had defined the entire era of this book.

---

China's diplomatic posture changed to match, and the change had a name drawn from a movie. "**Wolf-warrior diplomacy**," after a jingoistic Chinese action franchise, described a new generation of diplomats who abandoned the patient soft-power cultivation of the Hu era for combative nationalism — picking public fights, amplifying conspiracy theories, hectoring host countries. It played well to a domestic audience raised on the rejuvenation narrative and badly almost everywhere else, hardening opinion against China across the developed democracies and accelerating the very encirclement it was reacting to. The hard-power scaffolding of that encirclement assembled in these years: the revived **Quad** (the United States, Japan, India, Australia), the **AUKUS** submarine pact, and a general realignment of Asian and European democracies toward balancing Chinese power.

Two confrontations showed the new logic. China rejected outright a 2016 international tribunal ruling against its expansive South China Sea claims and kept militarizing the artificial islands it had built across the disputed waters — a clear statement that it would not accept an international legal order it had not written. And in 2020, in the **Galwan Valley** high in the Himalayas, Chinese and Indian troops fought a brutal hand-to-hand clash that killed soldiers on both sides, the deadliest India–China violence in decades. The reader of this series should feel the echo: this is the same contested frontier, the same two civilizations, as the 1962 war China fought while its own people starved (Chinese Revolution Ch 13; Modern India Ch 6). The 2020 clash pushed India decisively toward the United States and into the Quad, completing a strategic encirclement and confirming the book's longest-running comparison — the two billion-person Asian giants, the democracy and the one-party state, that began their modern histories at the same point and have spent seventy years as the great natural experiment in how to lift a civilization out of poverty (Modern India, the whole modern arc). By the early 2020s they were on opposite sides of the defining rivalry of the century, and the experiment was still running.

# Chapter 15 — COVID and the New Era

The book ends where the present begins: with a virus, a lockdown, a protest, a third term, and an economy that stopped working the way it had for forty years. The COVID era is the stress test of the entire bargain this book has traced — prosperity in exchange for a monopoly on power — and it is the chapter where, for the first time since 1989, the strain showed in the streets, and where the prosperity half of the bargain visibly faltered. It is too recent for verdicts, and the contested-claims rule governs almost every sentence; the book's job here is to state clearly what is known and to be honest about how much is not.

The virus emerged in the central city of **Wuhan** in late 2019. The first act was a rerun of SARS (Chapter 8): local officials suppressed early warnings, including silencing the ophthalmologist **Li Wenliang**, who had tried to alert colleagues to a SARS-like illness, was forced to recant, caught the virus, and died in February 2020 — his death producing a wave of public grief and rage that the censors briefly could not contain, the closest thing to the Weibo moment of Chapter 10 the Xi era had allowed. Then the state pivoted to the other thing it could do: an unprecedented 76-day total **lockdown** of an eleven-million-person city, followed by a national **zero-COVID** apparatus of mass testing, contact tracing, snap lockdowns, and quarantine camps. For roughly two years zero-COVID genuinely worked at its stated goal — China's reported COVID death toll through 2021 was a tiny fraction of the United States' or Europe's, and that is broadly real, not only propaganda — and the regime broadcast it as definitive proof that its system was superior to the chaotic, death-tolerant democracies. This was the prosperity-and-competence bargain at its most triumphant.

---

Then it inverted, fast, and the inversion is the most important political event in China since 1989. The zero-COVID apparatus that had been a triumph in 2020 became, by 2022, a machine of arbitrary mass confinement with no exit: the two-month lockdown of twenty-five million people in **Shanghai**, China's richest and most cosmopolitan city, with food shortages, families separated, pets killed, and people sealed in their homes, broke the public's patience and the economy's momentum at once. In November 2022, after a deadly apartment fire in Urumqi in locked-down Xinjiang, protests broke out across multiple cities and elite universities, with demonstrators holding up blank sheets of white paper — the **white-paper protests**, the broadest open defiance since 1989, and at their edge a few voices did something almost unheard of since Tiananmen and called for Xi himself to step down. Weeks later, in December 2022, zero-COVID was abandoned almost overnight, with no preparatory vaccination drive for the elderly and no public warning, producing a vast, deliberately undercounted wave of deaths — credible estimates run to one to two million in a few months, against an official toll in the tens of thousands. In the space of a single year the same policy was both the regime's proudest evidence of superiority and a self-inflicted catastrophe ended by street protest, and both halves are the same story this whole book has told: a system that can mobilize like no other and cannot correct itself, because correction requires telling the leader he is wrong, and the entire architecture of the Xi era (Chapter 11) was built to make that impossible.

The book closes on an open question rather than a verdict, which is the honest place to close. In 2022 the 20th Party Congress gave Xi Jinping a norm-breaking **third term** and a leadership stacked with loyalists, and ended with his predecessor Hu Jintao, frail and apparently unwilling, led from the stage on live camera — an image read worldwide as the literal removal of the collective-leadership era Deng had built (Chapter 6, Chapter 11). And the prosperity half of the bargain is, for the first time in forty years, in doubt: the **property sector** that the 2008 stimulus inflated into a quarter of the economy (Chapter 9) cracked with the default of the giant developer Evergrande in 2021, with no obvious engine to replace it; youth unemployment hit records the state stopped publishing; the **population began to shrink** in 2022, decades earlier than forecast, raising the question of whether China will grow old before it grows rich. The previous book in this chain opened on the abdication of a child emperor and the question the Qing died on — how does an old, proud, enormous civilization absorb the power of the modern world without losing itself (Qing Dynasty Ch 13). In one lifetime the answer has gone from a starving, humiliated country carved up by foreigners to the second power on Earth, having lifted 800 million people out of poverty and built the most sophisticated apparatus of control in history with the same hands. Whether the bargain that did it — prosperity for a monopoly on power — can survive the end of the prosperity is the question the country is living right now, and it is the one place this series, which has always known how its stories ended, has to stop and admit that no one does.

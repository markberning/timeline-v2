# Joseon Korea — The Hermit Kingdom That Wasn't Hermit

**Goal:** Narrative for the Joseon dynasty (1392–1910 CE), the longest-lived dynasty in Korean history and one of the longest-lived in the world. Across 518 years, Joseon built one of the most thoroughly Confucianized societies that has ever existed, invented a phonetic alphabet that linguists now describe as the most scientifically designed writing system on the planet, beat back a Japanese invasion of 158,000 troops with what may have been the greatest naval commander in any navy in any century, knelt nine times before a Manchu emperor in the snow, retreated into deepening isolation under the nickname "Hermit Kingdom," and was finally extinguished by Japanese annexation on August 22, 1910 — the longest sustained run of any single dynasty in East Asian history. This is the dynasty that put Korean culture into its lasting modern shape: the alphabet (Hangul, still used today), the cuisine (kimchi, still eaten), the social grammar (the Confucian family hierarchy that even modern South Korean apartment-dwellers still navigate), the mountain temples and tile-roofed palaces visible in any tour of Seoul. Every single thing Westerners associate with traditional Korea was made or made permanent during these five centuries.

# Chapter 1 — The Founding

The dynasty began with a general who refused to follow orders.

In the spring of 1388, the Goryeo court — the dynasty that had ruled the Korean peninsula for the previous 470 years — issued a foolish command. Goryeo's last decades had been dominated by the great Mongol-era political struggle: was Korea aligned with the dying Yuan dynasty, the Mongol-founded regime that had subjugated Goryeo in the thirteenth century (Mongol Empire Ch 6), or with the new Ming dynasty that had just expelled the Mongols from Beijing in 1368? The Goryeo court was split. The pro-Yuan faction, hoping to recover territory the new Ming had claimed in the Liaodong Peninsula (the southern tip of Manchuria, just across the Yalu River from Korea), demanded the army march north and attack Ming positions.

The general assigned to lead this invasion was **Yi Seong-gye** (yee SUNG-gay, 1335–1408), the most successful military commander of late Goryeo. He had spent his career fighting Japanese pirates on the southern coast and Jurchen tribes on the northern frontier (the Jurchen were Tungusic-speaking forest people — a different language family from Chinese, Korean, or Mongolian, more closely related to Manchu — who lived north of Korea in what is now Manchuria). Yi was not enthusiastic about attacking the Ming. He thought the war was unwinnable, that the heavy summer rains would ruin a campaign, and that crossing the Yalu River into Ming territory would invite an annihilating counterattack on the Korean peninsula itself.

He marched north anyway, because that was the order, but when he reached the Yalu River island of **Wihwa**, he stopped. He held the army on the island, sent petitions back to the king asking for the order to be rescinded, and was refused. So in May 1388 Yi did the unthinkable: he turned the army around and marched on the Goryeo capital of **Gaegyeong** (modern Kaesong, in present-day North Korea).

This is called the **Wihwado Retreat**, and it was the founding act of the Joseon dynasty, four years before the dynasty actually existed. Yi seized the king, exiled him, installed a puppet, and over the next four years systematically destroyed his rivals and reorganized the country's land tenure. In 1392 he forced the abdication of the last Goryeo king and proclaimed himself the founding monarch of a new dynasty. His reign name would be **Taejo** (TAY-joh, "Great Founder"), the same temple name used by every founding king of every Korean dynasty for the previous millennium. The new dynasty was named **Joseon** (JOH-suhn) — a name with deep antiquarian appeal, since it had been the name of the legendary **Gojoseon** kingdom said to have ruled the peninsula in mythological prehistory (Ancient Korea Ch 2).

What Yi Seong-gye did in 1388–1392 is what generals do when the central government collapses around them. The unusual thing is that the dynasty he founded would last 518 years. Across the same five-century span, China would see the entire arc of the Ming dynasty (1368–1644) and the entire arc of the Qing dynasty (1644–1912) plus a few years; Japan would see the Muromachi shogunate, the Sengoku civil-war period, the unification under Hideyoshi, the Tokugawa shogunate (Ancient Japan Ch 8 — and the next Japan TL will pick this up), and the Meiji Restoration; Western Europe would go from the late Middle Ages through the Renaissance, the Reformation, the Enlightenment, the French Revolution, the Industrial Revolution, and into the early twentieth century. Joseon, meanwhile, would stay Joseon. One dynasty, one capital, one ideology, one alphabet, one royal palace complex, one method of selecting officials. The unusual thing about Joseon is not how it began. It is how long it lasted.

---

To understand why it lasted, you have to understand the ideology it was built on.

Joseon adopted **Neo-Confucianism** as its official state philosophy in 1394 — the first great act of Taejo's reign after the move of the capital. Neo-Confucianism is a specific philosophical school that had developed in China during the Song dynasty (960–1279) under thinkers like **Zhu Xi** (joo SHEE, 1130–1200), the synthesizer who organized the entire tradition into a coherent metaphysical system. It is not "old Confucianism plus some new stuff." It is a philosophical reconstruction of Confucianism that used Buddhist and Daoist metaphysical categories to give the old ethical teachings a comprehensive cosmological foundation.

The key ideas: the universe operates on **li** (lee, "principle" — the underlying patterns that govern all things) and **gi** (gee, also written "qi" in Chinese romanization — the material force or energy that gives things their substance). Human beings have an inherently good nature derived from heaven; moral cultivation is a matter of clearing away the cloudy bits of gi that obscure the inherent li. The proper organization of society mirrors the proper organization of the cosmos: hierarchical, ordered, ritually structured. The five core relationships — ruler/subject, father/son, husband/wife, elder/younger sibling, friend/friend — should each be governed by the appropriate moral norm (loyalty, filial piety, propriety, deference, trust). The classical Confucian texts, supplemented by Zhu Xi's commentaries, contain everything a person needs to know to live ethically and govern wisely.

This sounds abstract. In practice, what Neo-Confucianism produced was a society obsessed with ritual correctness, family hierarchy, and scholarly study. It produced civil service examinations (Chapter 3) that selected officials based on their mastery of Confucian texts. It produced an ancestor-worship system in which the heads of households performed elaborate seasonal sacrifices to dead parents, grandparents, and great-grandparents back four generations. It produced the **yangban** (YAHNG-bahn, "two orders" — the civil and military officials whose status was hereditary), Joseon's scholar-gentry class, who were exempted from taxes and military service in exchange for their dedication to ritual, scholarship, and government service.

Neo-Confucianism was not a religion in the Western sense — it had no creator god, no afterlife of judgment, no congregation, no clergy in the strict sense — but it occupied the same social space, organizing belief, behavior, ritual, and political legitimacy. And like any state ideology, it required something to displace.

What it displaced was Buddhism.

---

Goryeo had been a Buddhist state. For 470 years before Joseon, Buddhism had been the religion of kings, the patron of the arts, the owner of vast temple estates, the sponsor of the carved wooden printing blocks of the **Tripitaka Koreana** (the complete Buddhist canon — over 80,000 wooden printing blocks still preserved at Haeinsa Temple in southern Korea), and the institutional bedrock of Korean cultural life. Buddhist monasteries had owned a quarter of all arable land in the peninsula. Buddhist monks had served as royal advisors and battlefield commanders. Buddhist iconography had defined Korean art for centuries (Ancient Korea Ch 8).

Joseon broke this comprehensively.

The new dynasty's policy of **Buddhist suppression** began under Taejo and accelerated under his successors. Temple lands were confiscated. Monasteries were dissolved by the hundreds. The number of officially recognized Buddhist sects was reduced first to seven, then to two. Monks were banned from entering the capital city of **Hanyang** (HAHN-yahng, the new name for the city we now call Seoul). Buddhist clergy were stripped of their political role. The state ceased to recognize Buddhist ordination. Surviving temples were pushed out of population centers and into the mountains, where many of them still are today — the picturesque mountain temples of modern South Korea are not picturesque by accident, they are what survives of an institution that was deliberately exiled to the periphery.

This was as comprehensive a religious displacement as the Safavid conversion of Iran from Sunni to Shia Islam (Safavid Persia Ch 2), and it was carried out by similar mechanisms: state seizure of religious property, exclusion of the displaced clergy from political life, and the systematic substitution of an alternative ideology in every institution that had been religious. The Safavid project required imported Lebanese clerics to staff the new Shia state religion. Joseon had it easier — Korea already had a domestic Confucian scholar tradition to draw on, since Confucianism had been part of Korean elite culture since the Three Kingdoms period (Ancient Korea Ch 4).

The architect of the suppression was a man named **Jeong Do-jeon** (JUNG doh-JUHN, 1342–1398).

---

Jeong Do-jeon is the most important Joseon thinker most non-Korean readers have never heard of. He was Yi Seong-gye's chief political theorist, the man who more than anyone else designed the new dynasty's institutional and ideological framework. He drafted the legal code. He laid out the city plan of the new capital at Hanyang (the rectangular grid still visible in old central Seoul follows his design). He wrote a sustained polemical work, the *Bulssi Japbyeon* ("Critique of Buddhism"), arguing that Buddhism was philosophically incoherent, that its monastic life violated the natural order of family obligation, that its ascetic withdrawal from the world was a form of cowardly social parasitism, and that no proper society could be organized on its principles.

More radically, Jeong argued that the king should be a moral figurehead — a symbol of legitimacy and an exemplar of virtue — while actual day-to-day governance should be conducted by scholar-officials selected on the basis of competence and moral cultivation. The throne was sacred but the prime minister and his cabinet of bureaucrats should run the country. This was a recognizable strain of Confucian political thought (it traced back to the original Confucius and especially to Mencius in the fourth century BCE), but Jeong pushed it harder than any previous Korean theorist.

What this meant in practice was that the king should not act on his own initiative against the considered judgment of his ministers. Royal decrees should be subject to scholarly review. Royal personality should be irrelevant to royal function — a lazy or stupid king was no problem if the bureaucracy was strong. The court would have multiple layers of remonstrating officials whose explicit job was to tell the king when he was wrong.

This vision of constrained monarchy and bureaucratic supremacy was implemented over the next century in Joseon's administrative structure. It is one of the reasons the dynasty lasted 518 years: under this system, even a child king or a mentally unstable king could not destroy the state, because the state was not in his hands to begin with. (We will see the limits of this principle in later chapters — the system did not actually constrain Yeonsangun (Chapter 3) or completely prevent the Sedo crisis (Chapter 7) — but it meant the system could survive what would have wrecked many other monarchies.)

It is also one of the reasons Jeong Do-jeon was murdered six years into the new dynasty.

---

Taejo had eight sons by two wives. The succession question was therefore complicated, and Jeong Do-jeon's preference made it more so: backed by the queen, Jeong supported the youngest son, **Yi Bang-seok** (yee BAHNG-suk), as crown prince. The fifth son, **Yi Bang-won** (yee BAHNG-WON, 1367–1422) — who had actually been one of his father's most active military lieutenants in the seizure of power — was passed over.

Yi Bang-won did not accept this calmly.

In August 1398, Bang-won staged a palace coup. He killed Jeong Do-jeon, killed his half-brother the crown prince, killed his other young half-brother for good measure, and forced his shocked father Taejo to reconsider the succession. Taejo abdicated in disgust, retiring to his hometown of **Hamheung** (HAHM-hung, in the northeast), where he reportedly had a series of envoys executed when his son sent them to ask his permission for various ceremonial matters — an episode preserved in the Korean idiom *Hamheung chasa*, "messenger to Hamheung," which still means "person sent on an errand from which they will not return."

Bang-won did not immediately take the throne. He installed his elder brother **Jeongjong** (JUNG-johng) as a placeholder for two years, during which a second coup (the **Second Strife of Princes**, 1400) eliminated the last serious rival, the second-eldest brother. Bang-won then had Jeongjong abdicate in his favor and took the throne as **Taejong** (TAY-johng) in 1400.

This whole sequence — known in Korean as the **First and Second Strifes of Princes** — was bloody and traumatic and might easily have wrecked the new dynasty if the institutions had been less sturdy. It nearly wrecked Taejo, who never spoke to his fratricidal son again and lived out his last decade brooding in the mountains. It also produced one of the strangest features of the founding generation: the most brilliant intellectual of the new dynasty, Jeong Do-jeon, was dead in the dirt by 1398, killed by the man who would actually have to figure out how to run the country he had designed.

But Taejong was, despite the murder, an effective and farsighted ruler. He spent his eighteen-year reign consolidating royal authority, breaking the power of the founding generation's military supporters, reforming the tax system, and most consequentially preparing the smoothest possible succession for his son. In 1418 he abdicated voluntarily — a remarkable act in Korean royal history — passing the throne to his son **Sejong** while retaining shadow power as senior monarch for a few years. The Sejong succession was the foundation for everything that followed (Chapter 2).

---

A pause to take stock of the new capital, because the geography of Hanyang is the geography Joseon would inhabit for the next half-millennium.

The old Goryeo capital at Gaegyeong was rejected immediately by Taejo's geomancers as ill-fated. Geomancy (*pungsu* in Korean, **feng shui** in Chinese) was the study of how the energies of the landscape affected human fortune, and a new dynasty needed a city sited in a place of auspicious energy. After several false starts (including a brief move to a different site that was abandoned after a year), Taejo and his planners settled on the basin we now call Seoul: a roughly oval area of perhaps 10 square miles surrounded by an unbroken ring of mountains — Mount **Bugak** (BOO-gahk) to the north, Mount **Naksan** (NAHK-sahn) to the east, Mount **Namsan** (NAHM-sahn) to the south, and Mount **Inwang** (IN-wahng) to the west — with the **Han River** flowing east to west across its southern edge. This geography was geomantically near-perfect: protected, watered, defensible, with a clear axis of energy flow from the northern mountain (Mount Bugak) down to the river.

The city was named Hanyang. It would not be officially renamed Seoul until 1946, but the place is the same place, and most of the Joseon-era street layout still underlies modern central Seoul. The new dynasty built five major palaces in and around this basin, all of which still survive (in restored form) and all of which can be visited today: **Gyeongbokgung** (GYUNG-bohk-goong, 1395, the principal palace), **Changdeokgung** (chahng-DUHK-goong, 1405, the working palace after Gyeongbokgung was burned in 1592), **Changgyeonggung** (chahng-GYUNG-goong, 1418, the queen-mothers' residence), **Gyeonghuigung** (gyung-HEE-goong, 1623, the western secondary palace), and **Deoksugung** (DUHK-soo-goong, originally a royal villa, expanded into a palace in the late nineteenth century when Gojong took refuge there). The Confucian royal ancestral shrine, **Jongmyo** (JOHNG-myo, 1395), was built east of Gyeongbokgung to house the spirit tablets of the kings and queens; the rituals performed there continue today and are UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. The royal Confucian academy, **Sungkyunkwan** (SUNG-gyoon-gwahn, 1398), was built northeast of the palaces and remains in continuous operation as the lineal ancestor of modern Sungkyunkwan University.

The city wall ran for 11 miles around the perimeter of the basin, with eight gates — four major and four minor — that controlled access. Substantial portions of this wall still stand and can be hiked. The four major gates were named for the cardinal Confucian virtues: the South Gate (still standing as **Sungnyemun**, "Gate of Exalted Ceremony," and now a Korean national treasure) for propriety, the East Gate (**Heunginjimun**, "Gate of Rising Benevolence") for benevolence, and so on.

The whole layout was a Neo-Confucian statement in stone. The throne hall faced south, as Confucian rulers were supposed to. The palace gates were oriented along the geomantic axis. The royal shrine was placed on the east of the palace and the altar of grain on the west, in conformity with classical Chinese ritual prescriptions. Even the trees and ponds were placed to reflect cosmological correctness. The city was the dynasty's ideology rendered as urban planning.

Cross-civ check: while Yi Seong-gye was founding Joseon in 1392, the **Ottoman Empire** under Bayezid I was at the height of its early expansion, conquering most of the Balkans and starting to encircle Constantinople (Ottoman Empire Ch 1–2). The **Ming Dynasty** in China was 24 years into its reign, with the Hongwu Emperor consolidating his rule and exterminating his own founding generation in a series of bloody purges that make the Strife of Princes look mild. **Timur** the Turco-Mongol conqueror was campaigning across Iran, Iraq, and India, leaving the pyramids of skulls that are mentioned at the start of the Safavid story (Safavid Persia Ch 1). In **western Europe**, the Hundred Years' War was grinding through its later phases, and the Italian Renaissance was just beginning to find its earliest footing under figures like Brunelleschi (whose famous Florence cathedral dome would not be completed until 1436, when Sejong was already creating Hangul). In the **Americas**, the **Aztec Empire** was a generation old and rising rapidly to dominance over central Mexico (Aztec Empire Ch 1), and the **Inca Empire** under Pachacuti was about to begin its great burst of Andean expansion (Andean Kingdoms Ch 8). Joseon was being founded, in other words, in a world being remade everywhere at once — but the dynasty it founded would outlast every one of these contemporaries.

# Chapter 2 — Sejong the Great

The fourth king of Joseon is the only Korean monarch usually called "the Great" in English, and the call is not controversial.

**Sejong** (SAY-johng, born **Yi Do** in 1397, reigned 1418–1450) was Taejong's third son and an unlikely heir. He was the third son. He was a famously bookish child who reportedly read every Confucian classic and every Chinese history available, sometimes the same book a hundred times in a row. (His father once confiscated his books to make him exercise; the boy reportedly found one his father had missed and read it instead.) The conventional story is that Taejong's elder son, the original crown prince **Yangnyeong** (YAHNG-nyung), behaved so badly — drinking, womanizing, ignoring his Confucian instructors, generally embarrassing the family — that Taejong demoted him in favor of the bookish younger brother. The rejected elder later became a wandering Buddhist monk, by all accounts content with the trade. (Whether Yangnyeong's bad behavior was performed deliberately to free his more talented brother for the throne is a romantic theory popular in modern Korean drama; the actual evidence is consistent with both a princely self-sacrifice and simple princely incompetence, and it is impossible to distinguish them now.)

Sejong inherited the throne in 1418 at age twenty-one, with his father continuing as senior monarch for three more years. He reigned for thirty-two years, until his death in 1450. In those three decades he produced the most remarkable concentration of state-sponsored cultural and scientific achievement in any premodern East Asian reign — roughly equivalent in concentrated impact to what the Caliph al-Ma'mun had done in ninth-century Baghdad (Islamic Golden Age Ch 3), what Akbar would do in late-sixteenth-century India, or what Augustus had done for Roman literature in the first century BCE. The list is striking: a phonetic alphabet that solved a problem nobody else in East Asia had even attempted to solve; the first standardized rain gauge in the world; the first self-striking water clock in Korea; the first state-commissioned agricultural manual based on actual interviews with farmers rather than copied from Chinese sources; the first metal-cast typeface (in the Gyemija typeface of 1403 — actually under his father, but expanded under Sejong) capable of printing thousands of pages of text; the systematic mapping of the northern Jurchen frontier and the establishment of the modern Korea-China border; and a concerted scientific program in astronomy, music theory, agricultural science, and medicine.

Most of this was done through an institution Sejong founded called the **Hall of Worthies**.

---

The **Jiphyeonjeon** (JIP-hyun-juhn, "Hall of Assembled Worthies"), refounded by Sejong in 1420, was a state-funded research institute housed in Gyeongbokgung Palace. Its members were the brightest scholars in the country, recruited through the civil service exam and assigned full-time to scholarly projects rather than to administrative duty. They lived in or near the palace, had access to the royal library, drew government salaries, and were given specific research commissions by the king. Sejong personally participated in their work — the **Annals of the Joseon Dynasty** (the official daily record of the reign, see below) describe him sitting up past midnight discussing technical questions with his Hall of Worthies scholars.

The most famous member of the Hall of Worthies was probably **Jeong In-ji** (JUNG IN-jee, 1396–1478), who would later serve as a senior minister under three kings. Other key figures included **Seong Sam-mun** (SUNG SAHM-mun) and **Sin Suk-ju** (SHIN SOOK-joo), both linguistic scholars who would do much of the technical work on the Hangul project. The Hall produced the **Yongbieocheonga** (yong-BEE-uh-CHUN-gah, "Songs of Flying Dragons"), a 125-poem epic celebrating the rise of the Joseon dynasty and the first major literary work composed in the new alphabet; the **Worin cheongangjigok** (WUR-in CHUN-gahng-jee-gohk, "Songs of the Moon Reflected in a Thousand Rivers"), a Buddhist verse cycle in Hangul written by Sejong himself; the **Nongsa Jikseol** (NONG-sah jik-SUHL, "Straight Talk on Farming," 1429), the first agricultural manual based on Korean rather than Chinese practice; the *Hyangyak Jipseongbang*, a comprehensive medical reference focused on Korean herbs; and dozens of other government-sponsored books on history, ritual, music, and law.

This is the same institution-of-state-scholars idea that produced the Imperial Academy in Han China (Han Dynasty Ch 5), the House of Wisdom in Abbasid Baghdad (Islamic Golden Age Ch 3), the Translation Bureaus of Cordoba and Toledo, and the medieval European universities. Each civilization that wants to do science and scholarship at scale eventually figures out the same answer: pay the scholars, give them somewhere to work and access to good libraries, and let them go. The Hall of Worthies was Korea's version, and for thirty-two years it worked exceptionally well. (Sejong's grandson Sejo would dissolve it in 1456 after some of its members opposed his usurpation of the throne — one of the more depressing institutional self-inflictions in Korean history.)

---

The Hall's most famous product is **Hangul** (HAHN-gool, "Korean writing").

To explain why Hangul matters, you have to explain the problem it was designed to solve.

Korean is a member of the Koreanic language family — possibly distantly related to Japanese (the so-called "Altaic" hypothesis, now generally rejected by linguists), but for practical purposes a language isolate with no close living relatives. As different from Chinese as English is from Arabic, in linguistic structural terms. Korean has agglutinative grammar (long words built from stacked suffixes that mark grammatical relations), a vowel inventory completely different from Chinese, and a syllable structure that allows clusters Chinese cannot pronounce.

Korean was not, however, written in its own script before 1443. It was written in **classical Chinese characters** (called **hanja** in Korean), the same characters used to write Chinese itself. This created a fundamental mismatch. Educated Koreans learned to read and write classical Chinese as a separate language — like medieval European clerks learning Latin — and then composed all their formal writing in Chinese, even when the spoken language they used at home was Korean. There were various awkward scripts that used Chinese characters to spell out Korean sounds (the **idu** and **hyangchal** systems, see Ancient Korea Ch 7), but they were clumsy, inconsistent, and required the user to already know thousands of Chinese characters.

The result was that literacy was almost entirely confined to the yangban scholar class, because learning the 3,000–5,000 Chinese characters needed to read serious texts took decades of study available only to people whose families could afford it. The Joseon population was perhaps 5 million in 1443. The literate population was probably under 50,000. Government documents, legal codes, royal decrees, religious texts, agricultural manuals — everything was written in a language ordinary Koreans could not read.

Sejong saw this as a problem worth solving.

---

The **Hunminjeongeum** (hoon-MIN-jung-um, "The Correct Sounds for the Instruction of the People") was the alphabet Sejong personally designed, with assistance from Hall of Worthies scholars, and promulgated in 1443. He published an explanatory book in 1446 (now a UNESCO Memory of the World document) that included a famous preface in his own voice:

> "The sounds of our country's language are different from those of China and cannot be communicated through Chinese characters. Many among the ignorant common people, when they have things to say, are unable to express them in writing. Pitying this, I have newly created twenty-eight letters, wishing that everyone may easily learn them and use them to advantage in their daily lives."

This is, by any reasonable standard, the most explicitly democratic justification for a writing reform in any premodern document. Sejong is saying out loud that the existing writing system serves a small literate elite at the expense of the common people, and that the new system is being created specifically so commoners can write what they need to write. The political content is unmistakable.

The technical achievement is even more remarkable than the political one.

Hangul has fifteen consonants and ten vowels in its modern simplified form (the original 1443 alphabet had 28 letters total, including a few that have since fallen out of use). The consonants are not arbitrary symbols. Each one is a stylized diagram of the position the mouth is in when producing that sound. The letter ㄱ (g/k) shows the back of the tongue rising to touch the soft palate. The letter ㄴ (n) shows the tip of the tongue touching the alveolar ridge behind the upper teeth. The letter ㅁ (m) shows the closed lips. The letter ㅅ (s) shows the shape of the tooth-and-tongue configuration that produces a sibilant. The vowels are based on three primary symbols representing heaven (a dot or short stroke), earth (a horizontal line), and human (a vertical line), combined to produce the various vowel sounds.

The result is an alphabet that is simultaneously phonetically precise (it represents the sounds of Korean directly, with very few exceptions), structurally elegant (related sounds have related letter shapes), and easy to learn (Sejong's own preface to the Hunminjeongeum says, no exaggeration, that "a wise man can acquaint himself with these letters before the morning is over; even a stupid man can learn them in the space of ten days"). Modern linguists have widely praised it. The University of Chicago linguist Jared Diamond called Hangul "the world's best alphabet." Geoffrey Sampson's book *Writing Systems* devotes an admiring chapter to it, calling it "the most ingenious writing system ever invented." The Korean linguistic societies celebrate **Hangul Day** every October 9 as a public holiday.

The yangban hated it.

---

The yangban opposition to Hangul was sharp, sustained, and partly successful at slowing its adoption.

The most famous attack came from **Choe Manli** (CHOY MAHN-lee), a senior Hall of Worthies scholar who in 1444 submitted a long memorial to Sejong arguing that the new alphabet should be abandoned. Choe's arguments are revealing about the worldview the alphabet threatened: using Hangul instead of Chinese characters would alienate Korea from the civilized world (meaning China); it would let common people read books they had no business reading; it would make Korea look like a country of illiterate barbarians who needed a baby alphabet because they could not learn proper writing; it would undermine the institutional position of educated Confucian scholars whose legitimacy rested on their mastery of the inaccessible classical script. (Choe did not put it quite this baldly, but the subtext is unmistakable.)

Sejong's reply was sharp: he told Choe that he had clearly never thought hard about phonology, that the new alphabet was specifically designed for situations Chinese characters could not handle, and that the social benefits of mass literacy outweighed the concerns about cultural deference. Choe was demoted briefly. The alphabet survived Sejong's reign.

But its institutional position was never as strong as Sejong might have hoped. After Sejong's death the new alphabet was given the dismissive nickname **eonmun** (UN-moon, "vernacular script") and was treated as a low-prestige writing system suitable for women, novelists, Buddhist monks, and translations of Chinese classics aimed at semi-literate audiences. Government documents continued to be written in classical Chinese for the next 450 years. The yangban literary establishment continued to compose all serious literature in classical Chinese until late in the nineteenth century. Hangul was widely used — by women in private letters, by commoners for accounting and shopping lists, by Buddhist printers for religious tracts, by playwrights for popular pansori operas (Chapter 6) — but it occupied a low cultural position throughout Joseon.

What rescued Hangul was the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries. As Korean nationalism grew under the threat of Japanese annexation (Chapter 8), Hangul became the symbol of distinctive Korean national identity — *the* alphabet, as opposed to *the foreign script*. The Independence Newspaper began publishing entirely in Hangul in 1896. Korean independence activists in the early twentieth century made promotion of Hangul one of their core programs. By the time of liberation from Japan in 1945, Hangul was the official script of Korean nationalism, and both the new North and South Korean governments adopted it as the national writing system. South Korea now uses Hangul almost exclusively (with occasional Chinese characters in historical or scholarly contexts); North Korea uses Hangul exclusively. The five centuries of yangban suspicion have been forgotten. Sejong's bet that ordinary people deserved a writing system they could actually learn took five hundred years to pay off completely.

It paid off.

---

While Sejong was inventing the alphabet, he was also running the most active scientific program in fifteenth-century East Asia, much of it carried out by a single man.

**Jang Yeong-sil** (JAHNG YUNG-seel, c. 1390–after 1442) is one of the most extraordinary figures in Korean history, and his story is sometimes hard to believe even though it is well documented. He was born a *gwannobi* (a government-owned slave) in Dongnae, in the southeastern part of the country. His exact ethnic background is uncertain — some sources say his father was a Chinese immigrant — but his official social status was the lowest in the kingdom. Slaves did not receive formal education and could not hold government positions. Slaves made bronze implements and repaired tools, which is what Jang's father did, and what Jang grew up doing alongside him.

Jang Yeong-sil's mechanical talent was so extraordinary that local officials repeatedly recommended him for higher service. Eventually, around 1421, Sejong personally summoned him to the court, freed him from slavery, and assigned him to the royal observatory and instrument workshop. Over the next two decades Jang produced an astonishing range of scientific instruments:

- The **cheugugi** (CHU-goo-gee, 1441) — the world's first standardized rain gauge. A bronze cylindrical container of fixed dimensions was distributed to every county magistrate's office in the country, with instructions to record rainfall after every storm and report monthly to the Ministry of Works. The data was used to predict harvests, plan irrigation, and adjust tax assessments. Standardized rain gauges did not appear in Europe for another two centuries.

- The **jagyeongnu** (jah-GYUNG-noo, "Self-Striking Water Clock," 1434) — a sophisticated automated water clock that announced the hours by automatically striking gongs, drums, and bells through a system of brass balls released by floats. Smaller mechanical figures dressed as ministers appeared at appropriate hours to bow toward the throne hall. The jagyeongnu was the official state timekeeper, installed in the courtyard of Gyeongbokgung Palace and used to set the bells of the city gates that opened at dawn and closed at curfew.

- The **honcheonui** and **ganui**, two large bronze armillary spheres for tracking the positions of stars and planets. The astronomical readings were used to calibrate the Korean calendar — previously dependent on the Chinese imperial calendar, which was slightly miscalibrated for Korean latitude.

- A series of sundials of various designs, including the **angbuilgu** (ANG-boo-il-goo, the "upturned-bowl sundial"), a hemispherical sundial that could be read at any time of year by tracking the shadow against engraved hour and seasonal lines. Several of these were installed in public spaces in Hanyang for ordinary people to use.

- Improvements to metal type-casting that produced the **Gabin** typeface (1434), one of the most refined typefaces of the early Joseon printing industry.

The agricultural data, the astronomical observations, the time-keeping infrastructure, and the printing capacity were all integrated into a state apparatus aimed at producing useful knowledge — the most sustained scientific patronage program in fifteenth-century East Asia. (China under the Ming was actively reducing scientific work in this same period as it turned inward after the great voyages of Zheng He. Sejong's Korea was the leading scientific producer in the region by mid-century.) Jang Yeong-sil's name appears in the Sejong Annals dozens of times, always with his title and salary noted; he was eventually promoted to a high official rank that should have been impossible for a former slave. The institutional system that Sejong had built was capable of recognizing and rewarding extraordinary talent regardless of birth — at least when the king was personally pushing it. After Sejong's death the system reverted to its default mode, which is what made the brief flowering under Sejong so remarkable.

---

Sejong's military program was equally consequential, if less famous.

The northern frontier of Korea had been an unstable boundary for centuries. The territory north of the **Yalu** and **Tumen** rivers — what is now Manchuria, the Chinese-administered region of Jilin and Heilongjiang — was inhabited by **Jurchen** tribes (Tungusic-speaking forest people, ancestors of the Manchus who would conquer China in the seventeenth century). The Jurchen had no central political organization in the early fifteenth century — they were divided into dozens of competing tribes, alternately raiding Korean villages and trading with Korean officials. The exact line of the Korea-Jurchen frontier had wavered for centuries.

Sejong fixed it.

He sent his most capable generals — most importantly **Kim Jong-seo** (KIM JOHNG-suh) in the northeast and **Choe Yun-deok** (CHOY YOON-duhk) in the northwest — to drive the Jurchens north of the Yalu and Tumen and establish permanent military garrisons along the line. Between 1433 and 1443, Korean forces fought a series of expeditions against various Jurchen tribes, eventually establishing the **Six Forts** (along the Tumen River in the northeast) and the **Four Districts** (along the Yalu River in the northwest). Korean settlers were moved into the newly secured territories and granted tax exemptions. The Korea-China border that resulted is, with minor variations, the same border that exists today between the modern Korean states (North and South) and China — a remarkable case of a fifteenth-century military campaign producing a stable international border that has lasted nearly six hundred years.

The Jurchen, displaced and pushed back, would coalesce in the late sixteenth century under **Nurhaci** and become the **Manchus** who would invade Korea in 1627 and 1636 (Chapter 5) and conquer China in 1644. The same people who Sejong's generals had pushed off Korean territory would, three generations later, force Sejong's distant descendants to kneel and kowtow in the snow. The international frontier, once established, lasted; the people pushed beyond it did not stay pushed.

---

A pause, before moving forward: Sejong's reign is the kind of historical moment that generates legend, and a fair amount of what is written about him in popular accounts is legend. The image of a benevolent Confucian sage-king personally inventing an alphabet for his beloved subjects, sitting up nights with his Hall of Worthies, befriending a freed slave, conquering the northern frontier, and ushering in a golden age — this is the dominant image in Korean popular culture, and it has substantial basis in fact, but the substantial basis is layered with five centuries of admiring storytelling. Sejong was also a sometimes harsh ruler who allowed an absolutist political system to take its full course. The number of officials executed under his reign was non-trivial. The Buddhist suppression continued in his reign, which he supported even though he personally had complex views on Buddhism. The Jurchen campaigns were brutal, with significant civilian casualties on both sides. Modern Korean historians have done substantial work over the past few decades to recover the more complicated Sejong who lies beneath the icon.

What is undisputed is the institutional and cultural output: the alphabet that thirty million people now read every day; the rain gauges that produced six centuries of weather data; the agricultural manual that improved Korean farming for hundreds of years; the printing technology that made Korean books cheaper and more widely distributed than European books would be until the Reformation. Sejong's reign produced more usable, lasting, transformative cultural work than any other thirty-two-year period in Korean history. The legend overshoots the reality, but the reality is still dazzling.

# Chapter 3 — The Yangban Order

For roughly the next century and a half — from the death of Sejong in 1450 until the Imjin War in 1592 — Joseon settled into the shape it would keep. This chapter is about the social order that solidified during those years, because it is the social order that would define every subsequent Korean experience until the late nineteenth century.

The defining institution was the **yangban**.

The word *yangban* literally means "two orders" — the civil officials (the *munban*) and the military officials (the *muban*). Originally it referred specifically to people who held official posts. By the mid-fifteenth century, however, it had hardened into a hereditary status: the yangban were the families whose ancestors had held high office and who continued to dominate the civil service exam (the **gwageo**, see below). To be yangban was to be exempt from taxation, exempt from military service, and entitled to a degree of social deference verging on physical untouchability — a yangban could not be physically struck by a commoner without lethal consequences for the commoner, and could not even be addressed directly by a commoner without elaborate honorifics.

The yangban were not a tiny class. Estimates of the proportion of the Joseon population who counted as yangban vary enormously — some early-Joseon estimates put them at 5–10% of the population, with the proportion growing through legal manipulation and forged genealogies to perhaps 30–40% by the late dynasty. They were not a small aristocracy. They were a substantial broad-based status group, more like the European *gentry* than the *nobility*: not the very top of society but a diffuse status-conferring class with shared expectations of education, ritual behavior, and political voice.

Below the yangban were three other classes in the formal Joseon hierarchy:

- The **jungin** (JOONG-in, "middle people"), a small class of technical specialists — clerks, translators, doctors, court astronomers, painters at the Royal Painting Academy — whose work was essential but whose social status was below the yangban. Jungin status was hereditary and partly endogamous; their family lines often produced notable scientific and artistic talents (the great true-view painter Jeong Seon was jungin, see Chapter 6).

- The **sangmin** (SAHNG-min, "common people"), the vast majority of the population: peasants, artisans, fishermen, small merchants. Commoners paid the bulk of the kingdom's taxes, served the military corvées, and performed the labor that supported the yangban scholar class.

- The **cheonmin** (CHUN-min, "low people"), the lowest stratum: includes butchers, tanners, sorcerers, traveling entertainers, certain categories of criminals, and most importantly the **nobi** (NOH-bee), the hereditary slaves who made up perhaps a third of the early-Joseon population.

This four-class hierarchy is conventionally compared to the Indian caste system or to medieval European estates. It is not really like either. It was less elaborate than caste (only four classes, not thousands of jati subgroups) and more legally formal than European estates (Joseon law distinguished sharply by class status in ways that were not common in Western Christendom). It was also more permeable than the caste system in some respects — the Sejong-era promotion of Jang Yeong-sil from slavery to high office (Chapter 2) shows that mobility was possible — and much less permeable than the European estates in others, since slave status was hereditary and was very rarely escaped.

The most important fact about Joseon's class system is that it lasted. Roughly the same hierarchy that solidified under Sejong's grandsons in the mid-fifteenth century was still in place when the Gabo Reforms of 1894 (Chapter 8) abolished it on paper four and a half centuries later. The Joseon social order is one of the longest-running stable social systems in human history.

---

The civil service exam — the **gwageo** (GWAH-guh) — was the institutional mechanism that selected officials and maintained the yangban class.

The gwageo was modeled on the imperial examinations of Tang and Song China (Tang-Song China — coming in a future TL), but with distinctive Korean modifications. There were two primary tracks: the *munbang* (literary track) and the *muban* (military track). The literary track was vastly more prestigious. Within the literary track there were further divisions: the *saengwon* exam tested mastery of the Confucian classics; the *jinsa* exam tested literary composition. Candidates who passed either were considered "licensed scholars" and could enter the Sungkyunkwan academy or apply for low-level posts. The serious goal was the *daegwa* exam, which had three stages — provincial, central, and palace. The palace exam was overseen by the king himself, and the top three finishers in any year were lavishly celebrated, paraded through Hanyang, and assigned to elite court positions.

The pass rate for the highest stage was extraordinarily low. In a typical year, perhaps thirty-three candidates would pass the daegwa palace exam, out of tens of thousands who attempted it across the country. Most candidates spent decades — sometimes their entire adult lives — preparing without ever passing. Lifetime study was the yangban's vocation, and the gwageo was the pretext.

In theory, the gwageo was open to all freeborn men. In practice, it was almost entirely passed by sons of yangban families — for reasons that are obvious once stated. Preparing for the gwageo required twenty or more years of intensive Confucian education, which required a teacher, a library of expensive imported books, and the leisure to do nothing else. Commoners could not afford the time. The sons of nobi were legally barred. The sons of jungin had their own separate technical exams that produced jungin-level positions but did not lead to the high civil service. Even within the yangban class, certain provincial families were systematically excluded from top positions — most notably the families of the northwestern Pyeongan and Hamgyeong provinces, whose exclusion would eventually produce serious revolts (Hong Gyeong-rae's rebellion, Chapter 7).

The result was a meritocratic-looking system that actually reinforced hereditary class status. The gwageo guaranteed that the sons of officials would themselves become officials, generation after generation. It produced both extraordinary scholarly achievement (the level of textual literacy required to pass the higher exams was simply astonishing — candidates were expected to recite long passages from memory and produce eight-legged Chinese essays in formal style on a few hours' notice) and extraordinary stagnation (the curriculum was the same Confucian canon for five hundred years, with very little variation, and the exam questions tended to recycle the same problems generation after generation).

This is the place to mention that the parallel Chinese system, on which the gwageo was modeled, had similar effects but at greater scale. China's imperial examinations selected officials for an empire of 100+ million people; Joseon's gwageo selected officials for a country of perhaps 5–10 million. The result in both cases was a large, stable, classically educated bureaucratic class that could administer the country competently for centuries — and that proved spectacularly ill-equipped to deal with the industrial-age challenges of the nineteenth century, when both China and Korea found that their carefully selected Confucian scholar-officials had no idea how to negotiate with steamships or design factories.

---

Joseon women had a different experience of the Neo-Confucian order, and not a happy one.

The pre-Joseon position of Korean women had been relatively free by East Asian standards. Goryeo law had allowed equal inheritance between sons and daughters. A married woman generally retained her own family name and her own property. Widows could and routinely did remarry. Brides moved into the husband's household only after the first child was born; before that, the couple lived in the wife's family compound, with the wife's parents providing initial support. The kinship system was bilateral: a person traced descent through both maternal and paternal lines, and uncles on both sides were equally recognized as relatives.

The Confucianization of Joseon ground all this away over the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries.

The mechanism was the elaboration of patrilineal Neo-Confucian ritual. Confucianism teaches that the proper organization of the family is patrilineal — descent traced through the male line, ancestor worship organized by the eldest son, inheritance flowing primarily to sons, women incorporated into the husband's family on marriage. The Joseon court, advised by Neo-Confucian theorists, gradually rewrote the country's family law to match the Confucian ideal. Inheritance was rebalanced toward the eldest son. Widow remarriage was discouraged through a series of legal disabilities — a remarried widow's children were declared ineligible for the gwageo exam, which effectively closed the door for any yangban widow who hoped her grandchildren might hold office. The bride moved permanently into the husband's household at marriage, often as a teenager. Women were physically secluded in the inner quarters of yangban houses (the **anchae** or "inner room") and were not supposed to be seen by male visitors. Women's names were not even routinely recorded in family genealogies — daughters were listed simply as "the wife of so-and-so," or by the husband's office.

The change took roughly two centuries to complete. Modern Korean historians like **Martina Deuchler** (whose book *The Confucian Transformation of Korea* is the standard reference) have documented the slow, deliberate, multi-generational process by which a relatively flexible bilateral kinship system was hammered into a rigid patrilineal one through systematic legal change. By the late seventeenth century, Joseon had become more strictly patrilineal than China itself — the disciples having outdone the master.

This is not the only case where a colonized culture proved to be a more enthusiastic adopter of an imported ideology than the source culture. The Christianization of Spain in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries produced, in the Spanish Inquisition, a religious bureaucracy more aggressive than anything in contemporary Italy or France. The Buddhist suppression that the Joseon dynasty carried out (Chapter 1) was more thorough than anything in Buddhist-origin India. New converts, having something to prove, often go further than the originators. The Joseon yangban's enthusiastic implementation of patrilineal Confucianism is a case in point.

A handful of women managed to leave a public mark anyway. **Shin Saimdang** (SHIN sah-im-DAHNG, 1504–1551), the mother of the philosopher Yi I (see below), was an accomplished painter and calligrapher whose paintings of grasses and insects survive as among the finest small-scale art of the period — her face is now on the 50,000-won note, the highest-denomination Korean banknote, an unusual choice for a country whose currency mostly features kings. The poet **Heo Nanseolheon** (HUH NAHN-suhl-hun, 1563–1589) wrote in classical Chinese with such skill that her published work was considered the equal of any male poet of her generation, though she was severely limited in what she could publicly do as a yangban woman. The medical and divination specialist **Daejanggeum** (DAY-jahng-geum, early sixteenth century) became the first female royal physician — her name and her position are documented in the *Annals*, even though most details about her life are now uncertain (she is the basis for the famous twenty-first-century Korean drama). These were exceptional cases, and they remain exceptional.

The female experience of the Confucian order was, on average, severely diminished compared to the pre-Joseon period. This is part of the cost of the dynasty's social achievement. The yangban order was stable, ritually elaborated, and successful at what it set out to do — and it was built in part on the systematic legal reduction of half the population.

---

The slave system deserves separate treatment, because it was unusually large and unusually persistent.

The **nobi** were Joseon's hereditary slaves. Nobi status was inherited from either parent: if your mother was nobi, you were nobi; if your father was nobi, you were nobi (the rule varied by period and by the parents' specific status). There were two main categories:

- **Gwannobi** (GWAHN-noh-bee, "official nobi"): owned by the state. They served palace functions, provincial offices, military camps, and government workshops. Jang Yeong-sil (Chapter 2) was born a gwannobi in a provincial workshop. Gwannobi could rise to higher status through unusual talent, royal favor, or military service, though the vast majority did not.

- **Sanobi** (SAH-noh-bee, "private nobi"): owned by yangban households. They served as domestic servants, tenant farmers on yangban estates, and personal attendants. Major yangban households might own dozens or hundreds of sanobi. The wealthiest families could own thousands, organized into village-sized agricultural communities working family lands.

The proportion of the Joseon population that was nobi is debated, but figures of around one-third for the early dynasty are commonly cited, dropping to perhaps 10% by the late eighteenth century as legal reforms gradually freed various categories. This is a remarkably high slave proportion for any premodern state — comparable to or higher than the slave populations of classical Rome, contemporary Ottoman Anatolia, or the antebellum American South. Joseon was, on this measure, one of the most heavily enslaved societies in early-modern history.

Nobi were not chattel slaves in quite the American sense. They had legal personhood — they could marry (other nobi), own modest property, sue in some courts. They could not be killed at the master's whim. But they could be bought and sold, separated from their families, willed to heirs. Their children inherited their status. They were forbidden from taking the gwageo exam, holding office, or entering most non-yangban professions. They paid no taxes (the master took their labor instead) but had no legal independence.

The nobi system was gradually dismantled over the late dynasty. King **Hyeonjong** (HYUN-johng, reigned 1659–74) restricted some categories. King **Jeongjo** (Chapter 6) reduced the gwannobi rolls. In 1801, after Jeongjo's death, the new regime emancipated the remaining gwannobi (about 36,000 people) by burning the slave registers — a startling unilateral act of state. The sanobi system was not formally abolished until the **Gabo Reforms** of 1894 (Chapter 8), nearly a century later. The legal traces of nobi status — restrictions on certain occupations, prohibitions on certain marriages — lingered into the twentieth century.

This is a substantial dark side of the Joseon achievement. The Confucian order was elegant, scholarly, and stable. It was also built on the unfree labor of a third of the population, for centuries. Korean popular memory remembers the Hangul, the rain gauges, and Yi Sun-sin's turtle ships. It does not always remember the slave registers. It is fair to remember both.

---

The court politics of the late fifteenth and sixteenth centuries were dominated by an internal struggle among the yangban themselves, between two factions whose names are worth knowing.

The **Hungu** (HOON-goo, "Meritorious Officials") were the descendants of the founding-generation supporters of Taejo and Taejong. By the second and third generations after the founding, they had become a hereditary establishment with vast estates, royal in-law connections, and effectively unbroken control of the central court. They were Confucian, but their Confucianism was a state-power Confucianism focused on practical administration and institutional dominance.

The **Sarim** (SAH-rim, "Forest of Scholars") were a different stripe. They were provincial Confucians, descendants of late-Goryeo loyalists who had refused to serve the new dynasty, gathered at private academies in the south and west away from the capital. Their Confucianism was a moral-philosophical Confucianism focused on ethical purity, scholarly rigor, and the proper interpretation of the canon. They had spent the first century of Joseon outside the central court system, building their institutional bases at the new private academies (the **seowon**, see below).

In the late fifteenth century, beginning under King Seongjong (reigned 1469–94), the Sarim began to be promoted into central government posts. They were less compromised than the Hungu, more committed to Confucian principles, and useful as a check on Hungu power. King Seongjong systematically appointed Sarim scholars to the censorate (the office whose job was to criticize official misconduct), where they immediately began attacking Hungu corruption.

This produced four major bloody confrontations known as the **Literati Purges**:

- **First Purge (1498)**: King Yeonsangun, a paranoid and unstable ruler, was incensed by a draft of historical records that included veiled criticism of his great-grandfather's usurpation. He executed several Sarim officials, exiled others, and posthumously beheaded the corpse of the late Sarim master Kim Jong-jik. (This is the only known case in Korean history of a corpse being beheaded as judicial punishment.)

- **Second Purge (1504)**: Yeonsangun, having discovered the truth about his mother's execution years earlier (she had been a royal consort deposed and forced to drink poison), turned on the officials who had been involved or had failed to oppose it. Hundreds of officials were killed or exiled. Yeonsangun was deposed two years later in a yangban coup.

- **Third Purge (1519)**: King Jungjong, who had taken the throne after Yeonsangun's deposition, briefly favored an idealistic young Sarim scholar named **Jo Gwang-jo** (JOH GWAHNG-joh) and his program of radical Confucian reform. Jo's enemies fabricated an omen — by writing the words "Jo will be king" in honey on tree leaves so that ants ate the honey and made the words appear miraculously written — and turned the king against him. Jo was forced to drink poison.

- **Fourth Purge (1545)**: After the death of King Injong, factions surrounding the queen mothers of two competing princes turned on each other. Several hundred Sarim officials were killed or exiled.

The Literati Purges are the dynasty's first sustained period of internal political bloodshed since the Strife of Princes (Chapter 1), and they wasted some of the most talented scholarly minds of the period. Yet the system survived. The institutions Jeong Do-jeon had designed at the founding (Chapter 1) were sturdy enough to absorb even four catastrophic purges across half a century. By the late sixteenth century the Sarim had finally consolidated their position in the central government, the Hungu had been displaced, and Joseon court politics entered a new phase — the **factional wars** that would dominate the next two centuries (Chapter 7), as the Sarim themselves split into rival philosophical camps.

The structural irony is hard to miss. The Sarim won. They became the new establishment. And then they fractured along the same fault lines that had originally divided them from the Hungu. The pattern of insurgent scholar-purists displacing a corrupt establishment, becoming the new establishment, and then producing their own insurgent scholar-purists — this pattern would repeat across Joseon court history for the next four hundred years.

---

The intellectual heritage of the Sarim victory was the great philosophical flowering of mid-Joseon Neo-Confucianism, focused around two giants whose images are now on Korean banknotes.

**Yi Hwang** (yee HWAHNG, 1501–1570), scholar name **Toegye** (TWAY-gay), was Korea's most influential philosopher. His face is on the 1,000-won note. He spent most of his career in voluntary retirement at the country academy he had founded — **Dosan Seowon** (DOH-sahn SUH-won) in southeastern Korea — refusing repeated royal invitations to take high office and producing a steady stream of treatises that refined the Zhu Xi orthodoxy in distinctive ways. His most important work was the **Four-Seven Debate** (sa-chil ron), an extended philosophical correspondence with a younger scholar named **Gi Dae-seung** (GEE DAY-sung) about the metaphysical origin of the four moral feelings (commiseration, shame, deference, sense of right and wrong) and the seven natural emotions (joy, anger, sorrow, fear, love, hate, desire). Were the four moral feelings derived from li (principle), while the seven emotions were derived from gi (material force)? Or were both li and gi present in both? The debate sounds technical, and it was, but the underlying question was whether Confucian ethics had a metaphysical foundation distinct from ordinary emotional life. Yi Hwang's answer was yes; Gi's was no. The debate, conducted over more than a decade in beautifully written Chinese letters, is one of the great philosophical exchanges of the East Asian tradition.

**Yi I** (yee EE, 1536–1584), scholar name **Yulgok** (YOOL-gohk), was Toegye's younger contemporary and intellectual rival. His face is on the 5,000-won note. Yulgok was less metaphysically inclined than Toegye and more politically engaged. He served in high government office, advised the king on practical reforms, and famously proposed a **ten-thousand-soldier reform** — the recommendation that Joseon train and maintain a permanent standing army of 100,000 troops to defend against the brewing Japanese threat. The court rejected the proposal as alarmist. Twelve years after Yulgok's death, the Imjin War vindicated him appallingly (Chapter 4).

Yulgok's philosophical contribution was a more monistic reading of Neo-Confucianism: he argued that li and gi were inseparable, that any concrete thing always involved both at once, and that the proper goal of moral cultivation was the harmonization of li and gi within the individual. The disagreement between Toegye and Yulgok is sometimes summarized as Toegye emphasizing transcendent moral order (li) and Yulgok emphasizing immanent material reality (gi). Their respective followers split into rival philosophical lineages that became the basis of the later factional divisions (Chapter 7) — the Easterner faction trace their philosophical lineage to Toegye, the Westerners to Yulgok, and the eighteenth-century Old Doctrine and Young Doctrine factions both descend from Yulgok-influenced lines. Joseon politics would be shaped by the metaphysical disagreements of these two sixteenth-century philosophers for the next three hundred years.

---

The institutional infrastructure that supported all this scholarship was the **seowon** network.

A *seowon* (SUH-won) was a private Confucian academy combining three functions: an advanced study center, a memorial shrine to revered Confucian masters, and (eventually) a political base for a particular philosophical lineage. The first seowon was founded in 1543 at **Sosu Seowon** (SOH-soo) in southwestern Korea, by a provincial official memorializing an early Korean Confucian master. The model spread rapidly. By the seventeenth century there were roughly 200 seowon across the country; by the nineteenth century, over 600.

The seowon were the institutional power base of the Sarim. They provided the academic credentialing, social networks, and ritual continuity that kept the Sarim philosophical lineages alive across generations. They also accumulated tax exemptions, slave grants, and land donations on the same model as Buddhist temples in earlier eras — the Sarim, having displaced the Buddhist establishment in central political life, gradually rebuilt the same kind of local landed institutional power base under a different ideology.

This is why the Daewongun (Chapter 8) would, in the 1860s, gut the seowon system as part of his attempt to centralize royal authority. The seowon had become too powerful, too tax-exempt, and too independent of the central state. Of the over 600 seowon that existed in mid-century, the Daewongun left only 47 standing. The rest were dissolved, their lands confiscated, their political base broken. Nine of the surviving seowon are now UNESCO World Heritage sites, including Dosan Seowon (where Toegye taught) and Sosu Seowon (the first one). They are some of the most beautiful surviving examples of Joseon-era architecture.

The Joseon yangban order was being established in a world that had not yet been globalized — and it would have to confront the globalized world in the nineteenth century without much preparation.

# Chapter 4 — The Imjin War

In April 1592, a Japanese fleet of perhaps 700 transport ships landed roughly 158,000 troops on the southern Korean coast at Busan. They were led by some of the most experienced battlefield commanders in Japanese history. They were armed with European-style matchlock muskets — the **arquebus**, called *teppo* in Japanese — that Portuguese traders had introduced to Japan a half-century earlier. They had no idea what they were walking into.

This was the **Imjin War**, named for the year-cycle character *imjin* in the Korean sexagenary calendar (the same character that names the year 1592). It is sometimes called the **Japanese Invasions of Korea** in English, since the war came in two phases: the initial invasion of 1592–93, a stalemated truce from 1593 to 1597, and a second invasion in 1597–98 that ended only when the original instigator died. Across six and a half years it killed an estimated one to two million Koreans, devastated the agricultural economy, burned the great palaces of Hanyang to the ground, and triggered a civilian guerrilla resistance that would leave deep marks on Korean political memory ever after. It was also the only major Japanese military project of the early-modern period and one of the largest amphibious operations of the sixteenth-century world — comparable in scale to the Spanish Armada (1588) and Henry VIII's Boulogne expedition (1544), but larger than either.

The man who started it was a peasant.

---

**Toyotomi Hideyoshi** (toh-yoh-TOH-mee hee-day-YOH-shee, 1537–1598) was the second of the great unifiers of late-sixteenth-century Japan, after his master **Oda Nobunaga** (Ancient Japan Ch 8 — and a future Japan TL will deal with him properly). Born to a peasant family in Owari Province, Hideyoshi rose through the warrior class on raw talent, becoming Nobunaga's most able general and eventually inheriting the unification project after Nobunaga was killed in 1582. By 1590 Hideyoshi had brought all the warring **daimyo** (DAI-myoh, "great names" — the regional Japanese warlords) of Japan under his control, ending the **Sengoku** ("Warring States") period that had wracked Japan for over a century.

This left Hideyoshi with a problem: he had several hundred thousand professional soldiers with nothing useful to do at home. The same daimyo armies that had spent the previous century fighting each other would, with idle hands, be tempted to start fighting each other again. Hideyoshi needed an external war.

He chose Korea. The official justification was extravagant: Hideyoshi proposed to conquer not just Korea but China itself, install himself as emperor in Beijing, and possibly extend Japanese rule into India. (He apparently meant this seriously, at least in 1591–92.) The actual military rationale was that Korea was a stepping stone to China and a relatively soft target. Joseon had not fought a major war in two centuries. Its military was undertrained, its arsenals were undersupplied, and its political leadership had repeatedly rejected Yi I's recommendation to maintain a professional standing army (Chapter 3). Korean diplomats had even reported back from Hideyoshi's court that the man was a buffoon making impossible threats — though one of the two ambassadors had actually reported correctly that Hideyoshi was deadly serious and his armies were enormous, only to be ignored by a court that wanted to hear the easier message.

The Joseon court realized its error very quickly.

---

Within three weeks of landing, the Japanese had taken Busan, marched north through the central peninsula, and reached the Joseon capital. King **Seonjo** (SUN-joh, reigned 1567–1608) fled north toward the Chinese border, with his entire court trailing behind him in undignified retreat. Hanyang fell on June 12, 1592, almost exactly two months after the initial landings. Pyongyang fell shortly after. By the end of the summer the Japanese controlled most of the populated southern and central regions of the peninsula.

The collapse of the Joseon land army was complete and humiliating. The standing forces had been small and badly equipped. The mobilization plans for emergency defense did not function. Most of the country's senior generals were either captured or killed in the early battles. Nine-tenths of Korean cities and towns south of Pyongyang fell without serious resistance. The Japanese, in their letters home, professed surprise at how easy the invasion was proving.

The Joseon court fled to **Uiju** (oo-EE-joo) on the Yalu River, almost in Chinese territory, and began begging the Ming for emergency aid. The Ming would eventually intervene (see below), but in the summer and fall of 1592 the Korean state was collapsing.

What saved it was a combination of three things: a navy nobody had expected, civilian guerrilla resistance from below, and Ming Chinese intervention from above.

---

The naval salvation came from one man.

**Yi Sun-sin** (YEE soon-SHIN, 1545–1598) was, in 1591, a relatively obscure mid-rank officer of the Joseon navy, recently appointed commander of the **Jeolla Left Naval Command** (the small naval district covering the southwestern part of the peninsula). He had been a competent but unremarkable officer for most of his career — repeatedly demoted for failing to flatter superiors, twice court-martialed and stripped of rank, then reinstated. His 1591 appointment to the Jeolla naval district was a kind of professional rehabilitation. He used the year between the appointment and the invasion to do something almost no other Joseon officer was doing: he prepared.

Yi rebuilt the Jeolla Left fleet from scratch. He drilled his crews aggressively. He stockpiled gunpowder and provisioned his ships for extended campaigns. Most consequentially, he commissioned the construction of a new class of warship — the **geobukseon** (GUH-book-suhn, "turtle ship") — that would become legend.

The geobukseon was a modification of the standard Joseon **panokseon** (heavy oared galley with a flat-deck crew platform) into something more like a floating tank. The crew platform was covered with a curved roof of thick oak planks, studded with iron spikes pointing upward to prevent boarding. The roof was sometimes plated with iron sheeting (the extent and exact configuration of the iron plating is debated; modern reconstructions show varying interpretations of the contemporary descriptions). The bow was decorated with a fierce dragon head that could be rigged to emit smoke or fire from sulphur burners. The ship's broadsides bristled with cannon ports — perhaps eight to ten cannons per side — capable of firing the heavy iron-tipped wooden bolts and stone shot that would prove devastating against the lighter Japanese transports.

The geobukseon was not the entire Joseon naval advantage. The standard panokseon was already a heavier, more cannon-equipped vessel than anything in the Japanese fleet, which mostly consisted of armed transports designed for coastal sailing. Korean naval gunpowder was of higher quality than Japanese gunpowder. Korean ship construction used heavier oak with iron nails compared to the Japanese cypress with wooden pegs. And Korean naval doctrine emphasized standoff cannon engagement, while Japanese naval doctrine emphasized boarding and hand-to-hand combat — a doctrinal difference that would prove catastrophic for the Japanese once Korean cannons started firing.

The crucial factor was Yi himself. He combined extraordinary tactical imagination, an unusual willingness to engage in detailed reconnaissance before battle, and a kind of personal calm that his crews found inspiring. He kept a daily war diary, the **Nanjung Ilgi** (NAHN-joong il-gee, "War Diary"), which survives and is read in modern Korea as a national treasure — both a primary historical source and a literary classic. The diary entries record battles, supply problems, cabinet politics, family matters (he was tortured by news of his mother's death and his son's death during the war), and personal exhaustion in straightforward, unembellished prose. It is the most intimate document we have of any senior commander of the period in any country.

---

Yi Sun-sin won every major naval engagement he fought. There were many.

The first was at **Okpo** (OHK-poh) in May 1592, where Yi destroyed twenty-six Japanese ships at the cost of one wounded sailor. He followed it with a series of smaller engagements through the early summer, picking off isolated Japanese squadrons.

The **Battle of Sacheon** in late May was the geobukseon's combat debut. The new ship type appeared, fired its broadsides, drove off boarders with the iron spikes, and destroyed thirteen Japanese vessels. Yi was wounded in the shoulder by a musket ball that he reportedly had cut out of the wound himself the next day. He remained on station.

The defining victory came at **Hansan Island** in August 1592. A Japanese fleet of seventy-three warships under **Wakizaka Yasuharu** (one of Hideyoshi's most experienced naval commanders) was destroyed almost completely. Yi used a feigned retreat to lure the Japanese fleet out of a narrow channel into open water, then formed his ships into a **crane-wing formation** (a curved line that could converge from both sides on the enemy center) and turned and devastated the Japanese with concentrated broadside fire. Forty-seven Japanese ships were sunk; perhaps 9,000 Japanese sailors and marines were killed or drowned. Korean losses were minimal.

Hansan was decisive in a way single battles rarely are. It severed Hideyoshi's seaborne supply line. The Japanese army on land was now cut off from reinforcement and resupply by sea, just as the campaigning season was about to turn into autumn and the Ming Chinese were preparing to intervene. Hideyoshi reportedly raged at his subordinate generals about the loss but had no way to compensate for it. From this point on, the Japanese strategic position deteriorated.

---

Court politics intervened.

In 1597, halfway through the war, Yi Sun-sin was framed by political rivals at court (using accusations partly orchestrated by a Japanese double agent), demoted to common foot soldier, and tortured in prison. His replacement, **Won Gyun** (WON GYOON), promptly led the Joseon fleet into a disastrous trap at the **Battle of Chilcheollyang** in summer 1597. Of the 134 Joseon ships engaged, 122 were destroyed. Won Gyun himself was killed. The naval advantage that had saved Korea in 1592 was wiped out.

Yi was hastily reinstated as admiral. He was given the surviving thirteen ships.

The Japanese fleet of 133 warships approached the **Myeongnyang Strait** in October 1597, expecting to round the southern tip of the peninsula and resume the supply route to the new Japanese offensive in the southwest. Yi positioned his thirteen ships at the narrowest point of the strait, where the tidal current ran at perhaps eight knots — the strongest tidal current in Korean waters. He waited for the tide to turn, then engaged.

The **Battle of Myeongnyang** is one of the most lopsided naval victories in history. The narrow channel allowed Yi to engage only a small portion of the Japanese fleet at a time. The strong current disrupted Japanese formations and prevented them from concentrating their numbers. Korean cannon fire was, again, devastating. Thirty-one Japanese ships were sunk, including the flagship of the Japanese commander. The remaining Japanese fleet broke off and retreated. Korean casualties: approximately ten dead. Korean ships lost: zero.

Myeongnyang ranks alongside the Battle of Salamis (Persian Empire Ch 5) and the destruction of the Spanish Armada as one of the most consequential outnumbered naval victories in the history of warfare. The 13:133 ratio is approximate but real. Modern Korean popular culture has made Myeongnyang a national legend — the 2014 Korean film *The Admiral: Roaring Currents* about the battle was the highest-grossing Korean film of all time at its release. The location of the battle has been studied by naval historians from multiple countries; the tactical exploitation of the tidal current is studied at modern naval academies.

Yi died fourteen months later, on December 16, 1598, at the **Battle of Noryang** — the final naval engagement of the war, when a combined Joseon-Ming fleet caught the last retreating Japanese squadrons and destroyed roughly half of them. Yi was shot by a Japanese musket bullet during the engagement. According to the standard account, his last words were a request to his nephew not to announce his death until the battle was won, lest his men lose heart. The battle was won. He died on the deck.

The Imjin War ended within weeks. Hideyoshi himself had died three months earlier in Japan, and his designated heirs lost interest in continuing the Korean expedition. The Japanese armies began their final withdrawal.

---

The naval war has tended to overshadow the equally important land war, but the land war was where most Koreans actually fought and died.

The defining feature of the land war from late 1592 onward was civilian guerrilla resistance. Across the country, yangban scholars, Buddhist monks, and ordinary farmers organized themselves into local self-defense forces called **uibyeong** (oo-EE-byung, "Righteous Armies"). The uibyeong typically operated from mountain fortresses, harassed Japanese supply convoys, picked off isolated garrisons, and occasionally fought set-piece engagements when they could mass enough fighters. They were not a coordinated national resistance — they were dozens of separate local efforts loosely affiliated with the displaced central government — but in aggregate they made it impossible for the Japanese to fully consolidate their hold on the conquered territories.

The most famous uibyeong commander was **Gwak Jae-u** (GWAHK JAY-oo, 1552–1617), a yangban from southeastern Korea who fought in distinctive red robes and earned the nickname "Hong-uijeonggun" ("Red-Robe General"). Gwak's irregular forces conducted a sustained campaign of ambushes and night raids in Gyeongsang Province that tied down Japanese forces for years. Buddhist monk-soldiers, organized by figures like the elderly monk **Hyujeong** (HEW-jung) and his disciple **Yujeong** (YEW-jung), formed disciplined units that fought in major engagements as well as guerrilla actions. The presence of Buddhist monks in the war effort is one of the more interesting paradoxes of the period: an institution suppressed by the Joseon state for two centuries (Chapter 1) suddenly mobilized to defend the same state when foreign invaders threatened.

The most consequential land battle was the defense of **Haengju Fortress** in February 1593. **General Gwon Yul** (GWON YOOL), with about 2,300 Korean soldiers and a substantial contingent of Buddhist monks, defended a hilltop fortress northwest of Hanyang against a Japanese force of approximately 30,000 troops. The Japanese launched nine separate assaults on the position over a single day. Each one was repulsed with the help of a primitive multiple-rocket-launcher weapon called the **hwacha** (HWAH-chah).

The hwacha was a wheeled cart mounting a rectangular grid of 100 to 200 rocket-tube holes. Each hole held a **singijeon** ("magic machine arrow"), an iron-tipped arrow with a small gunpowder rocket motor at its tail. When the propellant was ignited, all the singijeon fired simultaneously in a single dense volley, raining down on Japanese formations from a distance of several hundred meters. The hwacha at Haengju, combined with Korean cannon fire from the walls and downhill rolling of stones, made the Japanese assaults appallingly costly. By nightfall, the Japanese had taken roughly 10,000 casualties and abandoned the attack. Joseon casualties were perhaps a tenth of that.

Haengju is sometimes described as one of the world's first uses of mass rocket artillery in combination with conventional gunpowder weapons in field warfare. (The Chinese had used rocket weapons earlier, but not in this kind of mass-volley configuration.) It was one of the engagements that turned the war.

---

The Ming Chinese intervention was the third leg of the Korean salvation.

The Ming dynasty in 1592 was 224 years old, settling into the mature middle phase of its long arc, and it had two reasons to intervene in the Korean war. First, Korea was a tributary state of Ming China — the formal vassalage relationship had been re-established under the Joseon founding (Chapter 1) — and a tribute state in distress was a Ming responsibility. Second, and more practically, Hideyoshi had explicitly stated his intention to conquer China after Korea. The war was, from the Ming perspective, not a foreign distraction; it was a forward defense of the Chinese frontier.

A Ming expeditionary force of perhaps 40,000 troops under General **Li Rusong** (LEE roo-SONG) crossed into Korea in early 1593. Li recaptured **Pyongyang** in February 1593 in a brutal three-day siege that involved Korean troops and the Ming artillery. The Japanese forces, denied resupply by Yi Sun-sin's naval blockade and now confronting professional Ming infantry from the north, fell back from Pyongyang and concentrated in fortified positions in the southern coastal regions. The war settled into a stalemate that would last four years before Hideyoshi's renewed offensive in 1597.

Ming participation in the war was extraordinarily expensive. The cost is variously estimated at 8 to 25 million **ounces of silver** — sums equivalent to several years of total Ming state revenue. The fiscal strain contributed to the Ming dynasty's progressive financial weakening that would help trigger its collapse a half-century later (the Ming would fall to internal rebellion and Manchu conquest in 1644). It is not an exaggeration to say that the Ming saved Korea at a cost that helped end the Ming. The Joseon court would maintain a fierce gratitude to the Ming for the rest of the dynasty — a gratitude that would shape the disastrous decision-making of the 1620s and 1630s when the Manchu Jurchen began rising and demanding that Joseon switch allegiances (Chapter 5).

---

The cost of the Imjin War to Korea was catastrophic.

Estimates of total Korean dead vary widely, but figures of one to two million seem reasonable for the seven-year war and its immediate aftermath — out of a pre-war Joseon population of perhaps eight to ten million. Vast tracts of agricultural land went out of production. Granaries, irrigation systems, and rural infrastructure were destroyed across the southern provinces. The pre-war land registers — which had been the basis of taxation and military service — were lost in the fighting; for decades after the war, the Joseon state operated without accurate population or property data and lost massive tax revenue as a result.

The cultural cost was equally severe. Gyeongbokgung Palace was burned (probably by retreating Joseon officials and frustrated Hanyang residents during the panicked 1592 evacuation, before the Japanese even arrived) and was not rebuilt for two and a half centuries. The royal observatory was destroyed. Many of the records of the early dynasty were lost. The **Sungkyunkwan Academy** was burned. The **Bulguksa** temple complex outside Gyeongju (the masterpiece of Unified Silla architecture, see Ancient Korea Ch 8) was burned, with its wooden structures destroyed though the stone foundations and pagodas survived. The countryside lost much of its surviving Buddhist heritage.

The human and cultural transfer to Japan was, paradoxically, one of the most consequential legacies of the war — for Japan. As they retreated, the Japanese armies systematically captured and forcibly deported Korean potters, papermakers, weavers, printers, and other craftworkers. Estimates of the total number of Koreans abducted to Japan range from 50,000 to 100,000. The captured potters were settled in northern Kyushu, where they founded the **Arita** porcelain industry (whose distinctive blue-and-white wares became one of Japan's first major export products to Europe in the seventeenth century) and the **Satsuma** ware tradition. The transfer of Korean ceramic knowledge essentially launched modern Japanese porcelain. The Japanese tea-ceremony tradition that valued the rough, naturalistic Korean stoneware bowls (the *Ido* and *Kintsugi* aesthetic that would later influence twentieth-century international design) emerged in this same period as Japanese collectors prized the Korean potter-made ware.

This is not a pleasant compensation. Korean cultural transfer to Japan happened because skilled craftsmen were dragged from their homes at swordpoint. Modern Japanese ceramic museums sometimes acknowledge this history in their exhibition labels; modern Korean nationalism remembers it bitterly. But it also did something culturally productive: the Korean potters in Kyushu founded family lineages that produced ceramics in Japan for the next four centuries, some of them still active today, and the cross-cultural fertilization (in both directions) shaped East Asian ceramic art for generations.

---

The Imjin War defined Korean political memory for the next four centuries. The war produced the founding myths of modern Korean military identity (Yi Sun-sin remains arguably the most revered historical figure in modern Korea — his statue stands in central Seoul and his face is on the 100-won coin). It seared into Korean elites a permanent suspicion of Japan that would shape the response to nineteenth-century Japanese expansion (Chapter 8). It demonstrated the value of civilian uibyeong resistance — a model that would be invoked again under Japanese colonial rule in the early twentieth century. It also, paradoxically, deepened Korean dependence on Ming China at exactly the moment China was about to enter terminal decline.

The dynasty had survived. It survived in worse shape than it had begun. And forty years later it would have to do it again, against a different enemy, with even worse results.

---

# Chapter 5 — The Manchu Trauma

The Imjin War had wrecked southern Korea. Recovery took most of a generation. The court of King **Gwanghaegun** (GWAHNG-hay-goon, reigned 1608–23), Seonjo's son and successor, focused on rebuilding the agricultural base, restoring the tax registers, and patching together the shattered state apparatus. Gwanghaegun was an unusually competent ruler — a product of the wartime regency, where he had effectively governed the southern provinces while his father fled north — and the recovery during his fifteen-year reign was substantial.

Gwanghaegun's most consequential policy was a foreign-policy balancing act in the north.

By the 1610s, a new threat was rising in the Manchurian forests. The **Jurchen** tribes that Sejong's generals had pushed off Korean territory two centuries earlier (Chapter 2) had been gradually consolidating under a brilliant chieftain named **Nurhaci** (NUR-hah-chee, 1559–1626). Nurhaci had unified most of the Jurchen tribes by the 1610s and proclaimed his own state — the **Later Jin** (named to invoke the earlier Jin dynasty that had ruled north China before the Mongol conquest). In 1618 he formally renounced his vassalage to the Ming and declared war on China.

The Ming demanded that Joseon, as a Chinese tributary state, send troops to help fight the Jurchen invasion. Joseon's gratitude for Ming aid in the Imjin War (Chapter 4) made it politically impossible to refuse outright. Gwanghaegun's solution was a careful piece of duplicity: he sent a Korean expeditionary force of 10,000 men under **General Gang Hong-rip** (GAHNG HONG-rip), but with secret instructions to surrender at the first opportunity and avoid actually fighting. At the **Battle of Sarhu** in 1619 — the Ming's catastrophic defeat at the hands of Nurhaci's Jurchens — General Gang surrendered his command and explained to Nurhaci that Korea wanted no quarrel.

This was effective realpolitik. It preserved the Korean army, kept Joseon out of an unwinnable war, and bought a few years of peace with the rising Jurchen power. It was also viewed by hard-line Ming-loyalist yangban officials at court as treasonous betrayal of the Ming benefactors. In 1623 these officials staged a coup, deposed Gwanghaegun, and installed his nephew **Injo** (IN-joh, reigned 1623–49) as a more reliably pro-Ming alternative.

The new Injo regime explicitly repudiated Gwanghaegun's policy of accommodation with the Jurchens and re-affirmed Korean loyalty to the dying Ming. This was the decision that produced the Manchu invasions.

---

The **First Manchu Invasion** came in 1627.

Nurhaci had died in 1626. His son **Hong Taiji** (HONG TAI-jee, 1592–1643) inherited the Later Jin throne and decided that the new pro-Ming Korean court at Hanyang needed to be neutralized before he could launch a major campaign against China. In January 1627, Hong Taiji dispatched a force of perhaps 30,000 cavalry under his cousin **Amin** (ah-MIN) into northern Korea.

The campaign was rapid and one-sided. Amin's cavalry crossed the Yalu in deep winter, brushed aside the local Korean defenses, and reached **Pyongyang** within weeks. King Injo and the court fled to **Ganghwa Island** (a defensible offshore base in the Yellow Sea, which had been the refuge of choice for Korean kings under invasion since the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century). Negotiations followed. The Korean court agreed to recognize the Later Jin as a "brother state," send tribute payments, and break formal diplomatic ties with the Ming. The Jurchen forces withdrew. The 1627 campaign was, by the standards of seventeenth-century warfare, almost surgical.

But the Korean court did not actually break with the Ming. Injo's officials interpreted "brother state" as a nominal arrangement that did not require severing the deeper Ming alliance. Tribute payments to the Later Jin were sometimes sent and sometimes withheld. Korean officials continued to use Ming era-names in court documents (a gesture of continued loyalty) and continued to receive Ming envoys.

In 1636, with the Ming visibly collapsing and his own state now formally renamed the **Qing dynasty**, Hong Taiji decided to make Korea pay properly.

---

The **Second Manchu Invasion** of 1636 was different.

Hong Taiji crossed the frozen Yalu in person in December 1636 with a force of 120,000 troops — including substantial Mongol auxiliaries (the eastern Mongols had been incorporated into the Qing political system) and Manchu Bannermen. The objective this time was not nominal vassalage but full submission — Korea was to repudiate the Ming completely, accept Qing suzerainty unconditionally, send hostages from the royal family, and convert its diplomatic relationships to Qing-centered terms.

The campaign moved much faster than 1627. The Qing forces ignored the fortified positions on the northern frontier and drove straight for Hanyang. Within ten days they were at the gates of the capital. Injo's intended refuge on Ganghwa Island was cut off by the speed of the Qing advance — instead the king and a portion of the court took refuge in the mountain fortress of **Namhansanseong** (NAHM-hahn-sahn-SUHNG), about 15 miles southeast of the capital.

Namhansanseong was a defensible fortress on a 1,500-foot peak, ringed by stone walls following the contours of the mountaintop. With supplies for a few weeks' siege, it was a temporary refuge while Korean forces gathered. They never gathered. The various provincial reinforcements that should have come to relieve the king were defeated piecemeal by Qing cavalry detachments. Within Namhansanseong, supplies dwindled. The winter cold was severe — temperatures probably below 0°F at night. Officers and soldiers froze to death on the walls. Food rationing was reduced to almost nothing. The court inside the fortress argued bitterly between the **Cheokhwapa** ("Reject Peace" faction) and the **Juhwapa** ("Accept Peace" faction) — between hard-line officials who urged the king to fight on regardless of consequences and pragmatic officials who urged immediate surrender to spare the country worse devastation.

After 47 days of siege, with the king's supplies almost exhausted and a Qing victory in the field at Ganghwa Island (where the queen and crown prince had been captured), Injo agreed to surrender.

---

The surrender ceremony at **Samjeondo** (SAHM-jun-doh, "The Three Junction Crossing") on January 30, 1637, is one of the most psychologically traumatic episodes in Korean political memory.

Injo descended from Namhansanseong, dressed in plain white commoner's robes (a deliberate symbol of stripped status), and approached Hong Taiji's military camp on the southern bank of the Han River. There, before assembled Qing nobles and Korean court officials, the King of Joseon performed the **sambae gugodu** — three kneelings and nine prostrations, the formal ritual of submission used by Chinese emperors' tributary subjects when receiving the imperial presence. Injo dropped to his knees three times, and on each occasion struck his forehead against the ground three times in sequence. Nine head-strikes against the cold ground, in the presence of foreign barbarians, before what was supposed to be the highest sovereign of the Korean state.

Hong Taiji watched, accepted the surrender, and dictated terms. Korea would now be a vassal of the Qing rather than the Ming. Two of Injo's sons — Crown Prince **Sohyeon** and his younger brother **Hyojong** — would go to Qing as hostages. Massive annual tribute would be paid. Korean troops would assist in future Qing campaigns. Korean political documents would use Qing era-names rather than Ming ones. The Qing erected a stele commemorating the surrender at the site, naming it the **Samjeondo Monument**, that survives to this day on the south bank of the Han River — a reminder that this is what happened, that no amount of subsequent embarrassed amnesia can erase.

The Qing armies withdrew. Korea retained nominal independence, with Injo on his throne and the Joseon administrative system intact. But the country was now a tributary of a foreign barbarian dynasty (or so the yangban regarded the Manchus), the king had personally kowtowed nine times to a foreign emperor, and the long Korean fantasy of cultural superiority over the Manchu-Jurchen peoples beyond the northern frontier was permanently shattered.

---

The trauma was not just political. It produced a sustained ideological crisis in Korean Confucian thought.

Confucian doctrine held that the proper hierarchy of nations was civilizational: China, as the Middle Kingdom, was the source of civilization; Korea, as a fully Confucianized neighbor, was the most successful student; barbarians at the periphery were inferior. The Manchus, as forest-dwelling Tungusic-speaking former-Jurchens, were unambiguously barbarians by this scheme — and now Korea was their tributary. This was not merely inconvenient; it threatened the entire conceptual scheme of Korean identity within the East Asian order.

Korean Confucian intellectuals responded with a defensive posture that would shape elite ideology for the next two and a half centuries: they declared that the Ming dynasty was the rightful Chinese sovereign, that the Qing was a usurper barbarian state, and that Joseon Korea was the only remaining authentic Confucian civilization on earth. This doctrine, sometimes called **sojunghwa** (SOH-joong-HWAH, "Little Middle Kingdom"), had Korea taking on the cultural mantle of fallen civilization — Korea was now the true heir of Chinese culture, the last bastion of authentic Confucianism while the actual Chinese capital was occupied by barbarian Manchu emperors.

This ideological position had practical consequences. Joseon court documents continued to use Ming era-names long after the Ming had ceased to exist (privately, in court records, alongside the official Qing dating). Annual sacrifices were performed at a special shrine — the **Daebodan** (DAY-boh-dahn, "Altar of Great Recompense") — built inside the royal palace specifically to memorialize the Ming Wanli Emperor (who had sent troops to save Korea in 1592). Korean envoys to Beijing were required to wear traditional Korean court dress (which preserved earlier Ming-era styles) rather than adopt Qing-style queue hairstyles or banner clothing — a quiet but consistent gesture of refusal. Korean intellectuals talked about the sojunghwa as a kind of underground identity, the truth maintained against the falsehood of the visible political order.

This is one of the more interesting cases of ideological resistance through cultural conservation in early-modern history. Joseon could not militarily defy the Qing — the 1636 lesson had been comprehensive — but it could maintain a parallel ideological universe in which the Manchus were not really legitimate and Korean culture preserved the true tradition. The same mechanism would later help Korean nationalism survive Japanese colonial rule (Chapter 8).

---

The Crown Prince Sohyeon hostage episode is one of the most poignant subplots of the Manchu trauma.

**Sohyeon** (SOH-hyun, 1612–1645) was Injo's eldest son and the designated crown prince. After the 1637 surrender, he was sent as hostage to the Qing capital — first at Mukden in Manchuria, then at Beijing after the Qing conquest of China in 1644 — for a total of eight years. His brother **Bongnim Daegun** (later King **Hyojong**) was sent with him.

Sohyeon's experience in Mukden and Beijing transformed him intellectually. He was treated reasonably well by the Qing court — he had personal access to senior Manchu officials, was taken on military campaigns as an observer, and was permitted considerable correspondence with home. He observed Qing military organization closely. He met Western Catholic missionaries, particularly the German Jesuit **Johann Adam Schall von Bell** (yo-HAHN AH-dahm SHAHLL, 1591–1666), the imperial astronomer at the Qing court, who taught Sohyeon Western astronomy, gifted him books on Western mathematics, and gave him a small celestial globe and a Chinese-language Catholic catechism. Sohyeon became personally interested in Catholicism, though he never converted.

When Sohyeon returned to Hanyang in early 1645 — after the Qing had finally permitted him to come home — he brought back books on Western science, Catholic religious texts, and a sophisticated understanding of why the Manchus had won and how the Joseon court might rationally adjust its outlook.

His father refused to see him for weeks. When the king did receive his son, the audience was brief and cold. Two months after his return, Crown Prince Sohyeon died suddenly. The official cause was illness. Contemporary court rumor and modern scholarship both lean toward poisoning — most likely by his father, or by court officials acting at the father's behest, who feared that Sohyeon's Qing-friendly and Western-curious orientation would undermine the sojunghwa ideology around which post-1637 Korean identity had been organized. Sohyeon's wife was executed shortly after on flimsy charges. Sohyeon's three young sons were exiled to a remote island, where two of them died.

We do not know with certainty what happened to Sohyeon. The Joseon court record on this episode is incomplete and the surviving documents are deliberately ambiguous. But the pattern — sudden death, hasty burial, execution of the wife, exile of the heirs, refusal to investigate — is very hard to read as anything but a political assassination ordered or condoned by the king. The brilliant, internationally educated, scientifically curious crown prince who had spent eight years in barbarian captivity and come home with new ideas was a threat. He was eliminated.

His younger brother Bongnim Daegun, who had stayed quieter about his Qing-era observations, succeeded as **Hyojong** in 1649. Hyojong reigned for ten years. His major project was an elaborate plot to launch a revenge expedition north against the Qing — the **Northern Expedition** that Joseon trained matchlock musketeers and stockpiled weapons for, but never actually launched. Hyojong died in 1659 with the expedition still in the planning stage. The revenge plot quietly faded.

---

The Manchu trauma also intensified Joseon's foreign-policy isolationism in a way that earned the country its lasting nickname.

After 1637, Joseon adopted a **sea ban policy** — sometimes called the **Hermit Kingdom** policy in nineteenth-century English — that systematically restricted foreign contact. Private overseas trade was banned. Foreign ships approaching Korean coasts were turned away or attacked. The only authorized foreign presences were the **Waegwan** (WAY-gwan, "Japan House") in Busan, where a small number of Tsushima-clan Japanese merchants were permitted to live and trade under tight restrictions, and the annual Korean tribute embassies to Beijing, which traveled along a fixed road and were not permitted independent contact with Chinese populations along the way. Even Catholic missionary contact, when it began later (Chapter 6), was systematically suppressed.

This isolation was not absolute. Korean envoys to Beijing brought back Western books and ideas (sometimes via Jesuit contact at the Qing court). Japanese merchants at the Waegwan brought European goods and information. Shipwrecked European sailors who washed up on Korean shores were sometimes interrogated and detained for years (the Hendrick Hamel case below). But by the standards of seventeenth-century Asia, Joseon was extraordinarily closed — more isolated than Tokugawa Japan (which at least permitted limited Dutch trade through Nagasaki), more isolated than Qing China (which permitted European trade in southern ports), almost certainly the most isolated state in early-modern East Asia.

The reasons were multiple. The Imjin War had been a Japanese intrusion. The Manchu invasions had been a continental intrusion. The yangban political establishment had concluded that foreign contact brought catastrophe and the only safe response was withdrawal. The sojunghwa ideology reinforced this by treating Korea as the last preserve of authentic civilization that needed protection from corrupting outside influences. The result was two and a half centuries of careful, deliberate isolation.

---

The most famous case of accidental foreign contact during the isolation period was the **Hendrick Hamel** shipwreck.

In August 1653, a Dutch East India Company yacht named **De Sperwer** (the Sparrowhawk), bound from Batavia (modern Jakarta) to Nagasaki, was driven off course by a typhoon and wrecked on the south coast of **Jeju Island**. Of the 64 crew members, 36 survived. The senior surviving officer was a 23-year-old named **Hendrick Hamel** (HEN-drik HAH-mel), who served as the expedition's accountant.

The Joseon authorities did not know what to do with the survivors. They could not be returned home — Joseon had no diplomatic relations with the Dutch — but executing them was problematic, and exporting them through China was not permitted under the strict Sea Ban. The Joseon court eventually decided the easiest solution was simply to keep them. The Dutch were transferred to Hanyang, presented at court, assigned military service in the royal guard, and effectively held as the king's involuntary guests.

Hamel and his shipmates spent thirteen years in Korea, slowly being moved between provincial postings as their numbers dwindled to about twenty by the mid-1660s. In September 1666, Hamel and seven others escaped by stealing a small boat and sailing to Japan. The Japanese authorities at Nagasaki, surprised but cooperative, eventually returned them to Batavia and from there to the Dutch Republic.

Hamel wrote a detailed report of his thirteen years in Korea — partly to claim his back wages from the Dutch East India Company, partly because he was an interesting man with a remarkable story. The book was published in Dutch in 1668 as the **Journal of Hendrick Hamel**. It was the first detailed European account of Korea ever published, and it remained the most informative European source on Korea for the next two hundred years. Hamel described Korean dress, food, government, Buddhism, the climate, the language, the political system, Korean attitudes toward foreigners — the whole texture of seventeenth-century Joseon life as observed by an outside European witness. Modern Korean historians use his account as one of the most detailed primary sources for everyday life under King Injo and his successors.

The remaining Dutch crew were eventually allowed to depart in 1668, after Japanese diplomatic intervention. Their thirteen-year detention was the longest involuntary residence of European travelers anywhere in East Asia in this period, and the Hamel case remained the principal European point of contact with Korea until the nineteenth century opened the country forcibly (Chapter 8).

# Chapter 6 — Silhak and Late-Joseon Culture

The half-century of recovery after the Manchu trauma — roughly 1660 to 1720 — was followed by a long late-dynasty period of substantial cultural achievement that the political histories tend to neglect because the political surface of the country in these years was visibly decaying. The institutional politics of late Joseon were faction-ridden and stagnant (Chapter 7). But beneath the surface, an intellectual movement and an artistic flowering were producing work that would shape Korean modernity.

The intellectual movement was **Silhak** (SHEEL-hahk, "Practical Learning").

The word *silhak* covers a loosely organized scholarly tendency rather than an organized school. The basic complaint was that mainstream Joseon Neo-Confucianism had become absorbed in metaphysical disputes (the four-seven debate of Toegye and Yulgok, Chapter 3, and similar controversies that successor scholars continued for generations) at the expense of practical knowledge. The Silhak scholars argued that scholarship should produce something usable — better farming methods, better administrative procedures, better public-health practice, better understanding of the actual material world — and that pure metaphysical disputation, however philosophically refined, was a form of intellectual luxury the country could no longer afford.

The Silhak movement was therefore not a rejection of Confucianism but a redirection of Confucian scholarship toward practical application. Many Silhak scholars were devout Confucians; what they wanted was a more useful Confucian scholarship.

The founding figure was **Yu Hyeong-won** (YOO HYUNG-WON, 1622–1673), scholar name **Bangye** (BAHNG-gay). Yu spent twenty years in voluntary rural retirement in the southwestern province of Jeolla, writing a sweeping reform proposal called the **Bangye Surok** (BAHNG-gay SOO-rohk). The Bangye Surok argued for the redistribution of land away from the great yangban estates to actual cultivators, the abolition of slavery (a remarkably radical proposal for the seventeenth century), the establishment of a meritocratic civil service open to all classes, the rationalization of the tax system, and the reform of the military system. Yu Hyeong-won was not in a position to actually implement any of this; he was a private scholar with no court position. But his writings, slowly circulated in manuscript among sympathetic scholars, became the founding document of the Silhak tradition.

---

The Silhak tradition flowered most spectacularly in the late eighteenth century under King Jeongjo's patronage.

**King Jeongjo** (JUNG-johng, reigned 1776–1800) was the grandson of King Yeongjo and the son of the murdered Crown Prince Sado (Chapter 7). He came to the throne after a difficult childhood in which his grandfather had killed his father by sealing him in a wooden rice chest, and his accession survived a major assassination plot orchestrated by the same noble faction that had been responsible for his father's death.

Jeongjo was, by any reasonable measure, the second-best king of the Joseon dynasty after Sejong. He was scholarly, politically capable, willing to confront the entrenched factional system, and personally invested in promoting the Silhak intellectual current. He founded the **Gyujanggak** (GYOO-jahng-gahk, "Pavilion of the Royal Library") in 1776 inside Changdeokgung Palace, modeled on the Hall of Worthies of Sejong's reign (Chapter 2). The Gyujanggak was both a royal library — eventually housing tens of thousands of books — and a research institute. Jeongjo recruited talented young officials of mixed factional background to staff it, used it as a counterweight to the entrenched yangban political establishment, and personally participated in its scholarly work.

Several of the great Silhak figures were Gyujanggak scholars or royal favorites. The most important were **Park Ji-won**, **Park Je-ga**, **Hong Dae-yong**, and above all **Jeong Yak-yong**.

---

**Park Ji-won** (PAHK JEE-WON, 1737–1805), scholar name **Yeonam** (YUN-ahm), was the great Silhak essayist. He accompanied the 1780 Joseon embassy to Beijing — one of the regular tribute embassies that constituted Joseon's main official foreign contact — and used the trip to produce his masterpiece, the **Yeolha Ilgi** (YUL-hah il-GEE, "Jehol Diary"), a 26-volume travelogue covering the embassy's journey across northern Korea, through Manchuria, and into the Qing summer capital at Jehol (modern Chengde). The Yeolha Ilgi is unlike any other Korean book of the period. It mixes journalism, natural observation, philosophical reflection, satirical fiction, technical description, and impassioned argument for Joseon to adopt Qing techniques in agriculture, water management, transportation, and manufacturing. Park Ji-won had no interest in the sojunghwa fantasy that the Qing were barbarian usurpers — what he saw on his trip were paved roads, brick buildings, sophisticated water mills, productive farms, and a manufacturing economy generations ahead of Korea's. He came home to argue that Joseon should learn from this rather than pretending it did not exist.

The Yeolha Ilgi is also extraordinary as literature. Park Ji-won wrote in a fluid, witty, observational prose style that broke deliberately with the formal classical Chinese of conventional yangban writing. He included satirical fictional sketches alongside straight reportage. He included passages in vernacular Korean phrasing transcribed phonetically. The book was read with delight by sympathetic intellectuals and with horror by orthodox Confucians who considered its style undignified and its enthusiasm for Qing culture treasonous. The Yeolha Ilgi is now considered one of the great works of Joseon literature.

**Hong Dae-yong** (HONG DAY-yong, 1731–1783), an earlier traveler to Beijing in 1765, made his lasting contribution in cosmology. After meeting Jesuit astronomers at the Qing court — including the German Jesuit **Ignaz Kogler** — and acquainting himself with telescopic astronomy, Hong returned to Korea and wrote a treatise called the **Damheonseo** (DAHM-hun-SUH) in which he argued that the earth rotates on its axis once every twenty-four hours (rather than the heavens revolving around a fixed earth) and speculated that there might be intelligent beings on other planets, since the universe was probably structured similarly throughout. These conclusions were entirely his own — Western astronomy had not yet established Copernican rotation as orthodoxy and had certainly not endorsed extraterrestrial life — and they represent a remarkable leap in pre-telescopic Korean cosmological thought. Hong argued from first principles and from analogy: if the earth was a sphere (which Western astronomy had established), then the apparent motion of the heavens was probably the earth's rotation rather than the universe's rotation, since rotation of the small body was simpler than rotation of the immense one.

---

**Jeong Yak-yong** (JUNG YAHK-yong, 1762–1836), scholar name **Dasan** (DAH-sahn), was the greatest Silhak polymath and one of the most extraordinary intellectual figures in Korean history.

Dasan's range was astonishing. He served as a senior official under Jeongjo, designed the pulley crane that lifted the massive granite blocks for Hwaseong Fortress (see below), wrote treatises on smallpox vaccination (which he learned about from Jesuit-influenced sources in Beijing), produced reform proposals on land tenure and tax administration, did important work in textual criticism of the Confucian classics, wrote major studies of Korean geography and history, and produced a steady stream of poetry and personal essays. The corpus of his collected works runs to over 500 volumes — by far the largest single-author body of work in Joseon scholarship.

Dasan's career was effectively destroyed by his connection to Catholicism. He had been baptized as a young man (around 1784, when Catholic ideas first reached the Joseon yangban through Jesuit-influenced contacts in Beijing — see below). When Jeongjo died in 1800 and the Andong Kim faction (Chapter 7) seized power, Dasan was caught up in the **Sinyu Persecution** of 1801 along with hundreds of other Korean Catholic converts. His brother Jeong Yak-jong was executed. Dasan himself was exiled to a small village in the southwestern province of Jeolla, where he spent the next eighteen years in rural isolation. He used the exile to write. The bulk of his collected works was produced during these eighteen years of internal banishment.

The Korean intellectual tradition has tended to treat Dasan's exile as a tragic waste of a great mind that should have been at the center of national policy-making. The Silhak movement that he had inherited and extended was effectively decapitated when he was sent into exile. The reform proposals he wrote in Jeolla were not implemented for another century. The combination of Dasan's case and the broader Catholic persecutions essentially ended Silhak as an active political movement. By the time Korea was forced to confront Western pressure in the mid-nineteenth century (Chapter 8), the intellectual tradition that might have provided a basis for orderly modernization had been largely silenced.

---

The Hwaseong Fortress is the physical monument to Jeongjo's reign and to Dasan's engineering genius.

**Hwaseong** (HWAH-suhng, "Brilliant Fortress") is a fortified city wall surrounding the city of **Suwon** (SOO-won), about 18 miles south of Seoul. King Jeongjo commissioned its construction in 1794, partly as a fortified administrative center for southern access to the capital, partly as a memorial to his murdered father Crown Prince Sado (whose tomb is just south of Hwaseong, and whom Jeongjo had hoped to honor by making Suwon a kind of secondary capital).

The construction took 33 months — extraordinarily fast for a 3.5-mile defensive wall with four major gates, multiple bastions, command towers, and supporting infrastructure. The speed was achieved through several technological innovations:

- **Pulley cranes designed by Jeong Yak-yong**, which could lift the massive granite blocks used for the wall's foundations using mechanical advantage rather than the gangs of human labor that traditional Korean construction had required. Dasan's cranes drew on Western mechanical principles he had learned through Catholic-influenced sources.

- **Standardized brick manufacture**, with the bricks fired at on-site kilns rather than transported from distant production centers.

- **Detailed centralized planning** that documented every brick, beam, and piece of stone in the project. The construction record book — the **Hwaseong Seongyeokuigwe** ("Records of the Construction of Hwaseong Fortress") — survives, and it is so comprehensive that Korean restorers in the twentieth century used it to faithfully rebuild sections of the fortress that had been damaged.

- **Wage labor rather than corvée**, which Jeongjo introduced specifically for this project. Workers were paid actual wages rather than being conscripted as unfree labor, with the goal of demonstrating that paid skilled labor was more efficient than corvée. The experiment succeeded — workers paid in cash worked harder and faster than corvée laborers, and the Hwaseong project became a demonstration that Joseon's traditional labor system was economically irrational.

Hwaseong is now a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most visited historical destinations in South Korea. Substantial portions of the original 1796 construction survive, and the fortress can be walked along its walls, visited at the gates, and toured at the inner palace grounds. It is one of the most beautiful Korean buildings of the eighteenth century and arguably the high-water mark of Joseon civic architecture.

---

The other great cultural achievement of late Joseon was a flowering of distinctly Korean visual art.

For most of the dynasty, Korean painting had followed Chinese conventions — landscape paintings depicted imagined Chinese mountains and Chinese-style scholar pavilions, even when the painter had never seen China and never would. This was the educated yangban convention. In the eighteenth century, two great painters broke this convention.

**Jeong Seon** (JUNG SUN, 1676–1759), scholar name **Gyeomjae** (GYUM-jay), pioneered **jingyeong sansu** ("true-view landscape") painting. Instead of imagined Chinese mountains, Jeong painted actual Korean mountains: Mount Geumgang on the east coast (now in North Korea), Mount Inwang behind Gyeongbokgung in Seoul, the Korean coastal cliffs and waterfalls and pine forests. He developed a distinctive technique using sharp vertical brushstrokes to capture the angular granite peaks of the Korean landscape — a different visual texture from the rounded Chinese hills of conventional painting. His **Album of Mount Geumgang** (1734) is one of the foundational works of Korean landscape painting. His masterwork, the **Inwangjesaekdo** ("View of Mount Inwang After Rain," 1751), shows Mount Inwang from his rooftop in central Seoul as he watched the rain clear after a storm — a real place, a real day, a real moment, captured in a way that Korean painting had never quite captured before.

The true-view tradition was a kind of visual version of what the Silhak intellectuals were doing in scholarship: turning attention to actual Korean reality rather than imagined Chinese ideal. It is not a coincidence that Jeong Seon and the leading Silhak scholars were near-contemporaries and that their projects shared intellectual roots.

**Kim Hong-do** (KIM HONG-doh, 1745–1806), scholar name **Danwon** (DAHN-won), was the great genre painter of late Joseon. Danwon's specialty was scenes of commoner life: a wrestling match in a market square, a one-room schoolyard with a teacher swatting an inattentive student, a blacksmith's forge, a roof-tiler at work, a group of farmers eating lunch in a field. The scenes are observational, witty, full of small human details — a bystander laughing at the wrestling match, a child crying at the school, an apprentice yawning in the smithy. Danwon's paintings are an unmatched visual record of eighteenth-century Korean daily life. His album of genre scenes is one of the most beloved works in Korean art.

**Shin Yun-bok** (SHIN YOON-bohk, 1758–1813), scholar name **Hyewon** (HEY-won), pushed even further. Hyewon's genre scenes depicted yangban men with kisaeng (entertainer) women, scholars at illicit drinking parties, secret lovers meeting under the moon. The paintings are sensual, sometimes explicitly erotic, often gently mocking the gap between yangban Confucian rectitude and yangban actual behavior. Hyewon was eventually expelled from the royal painting academy (the **Dohwaseo**) on charges of indecency. He continued to work privately for elite patrons. His best-known work, the **Album of Genre Paintings**, is a National Treasure of South Korea.

The combination of Danwon and Hyewon represents a Joseon art that for the first time was looking at Korean society directly — not as a Confucian morality play, but as a human spectacle worth seeing. The same observational impulse that produced the true-view landscapes of Jeong Seon and the practical-knowledge scholarship of the Silhak movement was producing, in painting, the first really sustained visual record of how Koreans actually lived.

---

A note on Joseon ceramics, since they are one of the things Korea is now most famous for and they followed an unexpected trajectory.

Joseon ceramic taste shifted decisively in the early dynasty, away from the elaborate **Goryeo celadon** (the jade-green glazed stoneware that had been the great glory of the previous dynasty, prized at courts as far away as China and Japan, see Ancient Korea Ch 8) and toward the rougher **buncheong** (BOON-chung) stoneware of the early Joseon period and then toward **baekja** (BAYK-jah, white porcelain).

Baekja is austere and visually plain — pure white porcelain with minimal decoration, often only a single brushstroke of cobalt blue. The aesthetic preference for plainness was a Confucian visual statement. White was the color of mourning and ritual purity. Elaborate decoration was associated with Buddhist excess. The proper yangban serving vessel was a plain white porcelain bowl, with restraint as its visual virtue.

The **moon jars** (Korean *dalhangari*) are the most famous expression of the Joseon white porcelain aesthetic. These large round jars — typically 16 to 20 inches across, bulbous and asymmetrical, made by joining two thrown bowls at their rims — have become objects of intense modern collector interest. The British potter Bernard Leach, who lived in Korea in the early twentieth century, popularized them in the West. Modern Korean ceramic artists continue to produce them. The Joseon moon jar's modest, slightly imperfect, weighty white presence has come to symbolize a particular kind of Korean aesthetic minimalism that influenced twentieth-century Japanese and Western design.

The same century that produced the elaborate cobalt-decorated porcelain of Qing China produced, just across the border, the deliberately understated white porcelain of Joseon — two different responses to similar ceramic technology, expressing two different cultural aesthetics. Joseon's white porcelain is now arguably the most internationally recognizable Korean visual style.

---

The introduction of Catholicism to Korea is one of the most distinctive episodes in the history of Christianity in Asia, because it happened without any missionary involvement.

In 1784, a young yangban scholar named **Yi Seung-hun** (yee SUNG-hun, 1756–1801) accompanied his father on the regular tribute embassy to Beijing. While in Beijing, he sought out the resident Jesuit missionaries — Catholic priests had been at the Qing court since the time of Matteo Ricci in the early 1600s — and was baptized by Father Louis de Grammont. He took the baptismal name Peter (after the apostle whose name means "rock" in Greek). On his return to Korea, he brought back Catholic books, religious medals, and an explicit project of organizing a Korean Catholic community.

Within a few years, an underground Korean Catholic church had organized itself, entirely without foreign missionary involvement. The congregation grew rapidly — perhaps several thousand by the late 1790s, including some prominent yangban (the Silhak scholar Jeong Yak-yong and his brothers were among the early converts, though Dasan himself partially recanted under pressure) and many commoners and women. Korean Catholics held secret meetings, read Catholic texts in classical Chinese (since no Catholic literature was yet available in Korean), and developed their own indigenous religious practice without any priest present in the country.

This is, as far as anyone has been able to establish, the only case in the history of Christianity where a substantial church was founded by indigenous lay converts without missionary involvement. The standard pattern of Christian expansion is missionary-led: a foreign priest arrives, preaches, and gradually accumulates converts. The Korean case ran in reverse: lay Korean converts wrote to the Beijing diocese asking for a priest. The first priest sent — the Chinese-born Father **Zhou Wen-mo** (Korean: **Ju Mun-mo**) — arrived secretly in 1795 and ministered to a Korean church that had already been organized for over a decade.

The Joseon court reacted with predictable horror. Catholicism violated multiple Confucian principles: it required ancestor worship to be abandoned (replaced by belief in a single creator god); it claimed the absolute spiritual authority of foreign priests over the Korean state; it was an alien religious importation comparable to Buddhism, which the dynasty had spent four centuries suppressing. From the late 1790s onward, the Joseon government conducted increasingly severe persecutions of Korean Catholics.

The largest were the **Sinyu Persecution** of 1801 (which killed around 300 Catholics including Father Ju Mun-mo, and exiled Jeong Yak-yong), the persecutions of 1839 (which killed several French missionaries who had by then secretly entered the country), and the great **Byeong-in Persecution** of 1866 (which killed around 8,000 Korean Catholics and nine French missionaries — by some counts the largest single-year religious persecution in nineteenth-century Asia). Many of the executed Korean Catholics were later canonized as saints by the Vatican; **Andrew Kim Taegon**, the first Korean priest, was beheaded in 1846 at age 25 and is now the patron saint of Korean clergy.

The persecutions ultimately failed to eradicate Korean Catholicism. The church survived underground, kept alive by lay catechists, and emerged into the open after Korea was forced to accept religious toleration in the late 1880s (Chapter 8). Modern South Korea has roughly 5 million Catholics — about 11% of the population — and the descendants of the first Korean Catholic generations of the 1780s and 1790s still constitute important elements of the Korean Catholic community today.

The persecutions also had a political function. They were used by conservative court factions (Chapter 7) as a pretext to attack Silhak scholars whose intellectual openness to Western ideas made them vulnerable to charges of Catholic association. The combination of the Catholic persecutions and the suppression of Silhak effectively closed Joseon's intellectual openness to Western knowledge for the better part of the nineteenth century — at exactly the moment when openness to Western technical and scientific knowledge would have been most useful in confronting the gunboat diplomacy that was about to arrive (Chapter 8).

---

Cross-civ check: while late-Joseon culture was producing its great moments — Jeongjo's Gyujanggak, Hwaseong Fortress, Dasan's exile-era writing, Jeong Seon's true-view landscapes, Kim Hong-do's genre scenes — the **American Revolution** had just produced the United States Constitution (1787); the **French Revolution** had begun (1789); **Catherine the Great** was completing the Russian expansion across Siberia and into Alaska; **Edo Japan** under the Tokugawa shogunate (Ancient Japan Ch 8) was producing its parallel cultural flowering of ukiyo-e woodblock prints, kabuki theater, and Confucian-influenced popular fiction; the **Qing Empire** under the Qianlong Emperor was at its territorial peak and beginning to slip into the slow decline that the nineteenth century would expose; the **Mughal Empire** in India was nearly extinguished, with the **British East India Company** consolidating its rule across the subcontinent (the **Battle of Plassey** in 1757 having given the Company effective control of Bengal). The world Joseon was choosing to ignore was about to come knocking on the gates very hard.

# Chapter 7 — Faction Wars and the Slow Decline

Joseon court politics from the mid-sixteenth century onward were dominated by sustained, sometimes bloody factional struggle. This chapter is about why.

The factional pattern emerged in 1575 — the founding moment of what became the **Eastern–Western division**. Two senior officials, **Sim Ui-gyeom** (SIM oo-ee-GYUM) and **Kim Hyo-won** (KIM HYOH-WON), feuded over a personnel appointment in the censorate. Each had supporters at court. Sim's house was in the western quarter of Hanyang; Kim's was in the eastern. Their respective factions came to be called the **Westerners** (*Seoin*) and the **Easterners** (*Dongin*) after the geographic accident of where the two men lived. Within a generation, the geographic origin had been forgotten and the factions had hardened into ideological camps with their own doctrinal positions, recruitment networks, and political agendas.

The Easterners traced their philosophical lineage to **Yi Hwang (Toegye)** and emphasized the metaphysical primacy of *li* (principle). The Westerners traced their lineage to **Yi I (Yulgok)** and emphasized the practical interplay of *li* and *gi*. The original metaphysical disagreement (Chapter 3) had now become a political identification. Easterners and Westerners married within their own faction. They sent their sons to seowon affiliated with their philosophical lineage. They held different interpretations of how Confucian ritual should be performed at court. They competed for high office in the censorate, the Ministry of Personnel, and the seats around the king.

Both Easterners and Westerners then split further. The Easterners divided into **Northerners** and **Southerners** in the 1580s. The Northerners further divided into **Greater Northerners** and **Lesser Northerners** in the 1610s. The Westerners eventually divided into **Old Doctrine** (*Noron*) and **Young Doctrine** (*Soron*) in the 1680s. The Southerners further subdivided. By the eighteenth century there were at least four major factions and several minor ones, with overlapping rivalries and shifting alliances.

This sounds like ordinary party politics. It was much worse than ordinary party politics. The factions were intermarried over generations, recruited through the seowon system, traced their philosophical lineages back to the great mid-sixteenth-century Confucian teachers, and held positions on technical questions of ritual that they regarded as touching the deepest moral foundations of the state. When factions came to power they tended to purge their rivals — not just exile them, but execute them and posthumously revoke their honors. When their rivals returned to power, they did the same. The corpses accumulated.

---

The two great cases that defined eighteenth-century factional politics were the **Yesong Ritual Controversies** of 1659 and 1674.

These will sound, to a modern reader, like the most absurd political disputes possible. They were not absurd to the participants.

The first Yesong (the **Giha Yesong**) erupted in 1659, when King Hyojong died and the question arose: how long should the dowager queen (Hyojong's stepmother, who outlived him) wear mourning? Confucian ritual law specified different mourning periods for different relationships. Was Hyojong, as the second son of King Injo who had inherited the throne after his older brother Sohyeon died, to be treated as the eldest son for mourning purposes (which would require three years' mourning) or as a younger son (which would require one year)? The Westerner faction argued for one year (treating Hyojong as a younger son who had irregularly succeeded). The Southerner faction argued for three years (treating Hyojong as a legitimate eldest-son successor). The court adopted the Westerner position. Several Southerner officials were exiled.

The second Yesong (the **Gabin Yesong**) came in 1674, when Hyojong's queen — who had been a daughter-in-law of the same dowager queen — also died. The same question recurred: how long should the dowager queen wear mourning for her dead daughter-in-law? Was the daughter-in-law to be treated as a primary daughter-in-law (eldest son's wife — one year) or a secondary daughter-in-law (younger son's wife — nine months)? This time the Southerner position won. Westerner officials were exiled and several were executed.

This was not a polite academic dispute. The mourning periods were rituals that had to be performed publicly and that had to be performed correctly. Performing them incorrectly was an insult to the dead, a violation of cosmic order, and a falsification of the political legitimacy of the line of succession. The two Yesong controversies turned on the technical question of how the line of succession of the Joseon throne should be ritually understood — which is to say, on the question of whether Hyojong's reign was a legitimate continuation of the dynasty or an irregularity that needed to be acknowledged in the mourning code. The metaphysical-political stakes were genuinely high.

But the actual public disputes consumed enormous court energy and produced multiple rounds of purges. Modern Korean political history sometimes uses the Yesong as a kind of comic illustration of how detached late Joseon court politics had become from anything most people would recognize as practical concerns. There is justice to the criticism. There is also something to be said for the older interpretation: the controversies were not absurd if you took the Confucian metaphysics of the state as seriously as the participants did, and Joseon's whole legitimating framework rested on that metaphysics.

---

The eighteenth-century version of factional politics was dominated by two competing camps with confusingly similar names: the **Old Doctrine** (*Noron*, NOH-ron) and the **Young Doctrine** (*Soron*, SOH-ron).

The Old Doctrine and Young Doctrine were both descendant Westerners — that is, both had traced their philosophical roots back to Yi I (Yulgok) — but had split in 1683 over the proper response to a particular factional dispute. By the early eighteenth century they were the two major court factions, and the political question of every reign was which faction had the king's ear.

The Old Doctrine generally favored a strong central monarchy, conservative Confucian orthodoxy, suspicion of intellectual novelty, and the maintenance of the existing yangban structure. The Young Doctrine was more reformist, more open to compromise across factional lines, and more willing to incorporate Southerner perspectives. Neither was associated with anything we would recognize as a modern political program — both worked within the same Confucian framework — but they represented genuinely different orientations toward how the state should be run.

King **Sukjong** (SOOK-johng, reigned 1674–1720), an extraordinarily long-reigning monarch (46 years), used factional manipulation as a deliberate tool of royal control. He shifted his favor between the Westerners and the Southerners (and later between the Old Doctrine and Young Doctrine) based on his political needs, exiling the disfavored faction one year and recalling them the next. The result was a series of rapid-cycle purges — historians count five major political reversals during Sukjong's reign alone — that kept any single faction from becoming permanently entrenched but also wasted a great deal of administrative talent.

---

King **Yeongjo** (YUNG-johng, reigned 1724–1776) was the longest-reigning Joseon king (52 years on the throne) and the king who tried hardest to break the factional pattern.

Yeongjo's policy was called **Tangpyeong** (TAHNG-pyung, "Impartiality"). The basic idea was that the king should appoint officials from both Old Doctrine and Young Doctrine in roughly equal numbers, refusing to favor either faction, and that ministerial cabinets should be constructed with cross-factional balance. The Tangpyeong policy was an attempt to break the all-or-nothing pattern of factional warfare by forcing both sides to participate in government simultaneously.

It worked, partially. The frequency of major purges declined sharply during Yeongjo's reign. Cross-factional cabinets did manage to coexist, even if the underlying factional resentments did not disappear. The Tangpyeong policy gave Joseon its longest period of relative court stability since the early-dynasty founding generations.

But Yeongjo's reign was marked by an extraordinary personal tragedy that overshadowed everything else: the murder of his own son, Crown Prince **Sado**, by being sealed in a wooden rice chest.

---

The death of Crown Prince Sado is one of the most psychologically disturbing royal episodes in any premodern monarchy.

**Crown Prince Sado** (SAH-doh, born 1735, died 1762) was Yeongjo's only surviving son and the designated heir. By all accounts he was an unusually intelligent and physically capable young man as a child. As an adult he developed what modern psychiatrists would diagnose as a severe psychiatric disorder — possibly schizophrenia, possibly bipolar disorder with psychotic features, possibly something else, the diagnostic categories being too imprecise across two and a half centuries. The symptoms were dramatic: episodes of violent rage during which he killed servants (the historical record documents Sado personally killing somewhere between 100 and 600 attendants over his last decade — even the lower number is appalling); episodes of paranoid delusion; episodes of inappropriate behavior in public ceremonies; an obsessional fear of formal court dress that left him unable to put on his crown-prince robes without psychological breakdown.

Yeongjo, who was already in his sixties and had hoped for a competent successor, gradually lost patience. The relationship between father and son deteriorated catastrophically. Yeongjo would publicly humiliate Sado at court rituals; Sado would respond with episodes of self-destructive rage. Court factions used the conflict for their own purposes — the Old Doctrine generally supported Yeongjo, while the Young Doctrine and Southerner factions tended to support Sado. The royal psychiatric tragedy became a factional political crisis.

In the summer of 1762, Yeongjo decided he had to act. The traditional method of forcing a royal family member's death — sending a poisoned cup of wine — was unavailable, since killing an heir to the throne required public execution under the law and Yeongjo could not bring himself to issue a formal execution order. He chose another method. On a hot July day, Yeongjo ordered Sado sealed inside a wooden rice chest (the *dwiju*, a heavy wooden grain-storage container roughly four feet on each side) in the courtyard of Changgyeonggung Palace. The chest was nailed shut, lashed with rope, and left in the summer sun. Sado lived for eight days inside it before he died of dehydration and heat exposure.

The episode left a permanent scar on Korean royal history. Sado's wife, **Lady Hyegyeong** (heh-GYUNG), wrote a memoir — the *Hanjungnok* ("Memoirs Written in Silence") — that survives as one of the most important first-person accounts of any royal court in East Asian history. Lady Hyegyeong's account is unsparing about her husband's mental illness, about her father-in-law's cruelty, and about her own complicated feelings of grief and survival. It is essential reading for anyone trying to understand the human cost of late-Joseon royal politics.

The aftermath was profound. Sado's son **Jeongjo** (Chapter 6) eventually became king, and his reign was haunted by the determination to vindicate his murdered father. Jeongjo built Hwaseong Fortress (Chapter 6) partly as a memorial to Sado. He built a mausoleum complex for his father just south of Hwaseong. He dedicated himself to reform partly as compensation for the political dysfunction that had killed his father. Jeongjo was, in this sense, perhaps the first reform-minded Korean king in centuries to be motivated by genuine personal trauma rather than purely by abstract political principle.

Jeongjo died in 1800 at age 47 — quite suddenly, possibly poisoned (the suspicion has lingered for two centuries), with much of his reform agenda incomplete.

What followed was the long collapse known as **Sedo politics**.

---

**Sedo jeongchi** (SEH-doh JUNG-chee, "in-law clan politics") was the political system of nineteenth-century Joseon, and it was the system that destroyed the dynasty's ability to respond to the challenges of the modern world.

The mechanism: when Jeongjo died in 1800, his successor was the eleven-year-old **Sunjo** (SOON-johng, reigned 1800–1834). Power was held during Sunjo's minority by the regent dowager queen, **Queen Jeongsun**, and after her death by Sunjo's wife's family — the **Andong Kim** clan. When Sunjo died, his successor was eight-year-old **Heonjong** (HUN-johng, reigned 1834–1849), with power held by the **Pungyang Jo** clan (his maternal grandfather's family). When Heonjong died without an heir, the succession went to a distant relative **Cheoljong** (CHUL-johng, reigned 1849–1863) — a young man who had been raised in poverty on a remote island as the descendant of an exiled royal line — and power immediately reverted to the Andong Kim clan.

For most of the period from 1800 to 1863, in other words, the actual government of Joseon was conducted not by the king (who was repeatedly a child or an inexperienced adult) but by the in-law clans of his mother or wife. The Andong Kim and Pungyang Jo families operated the state as a private patronage system. Office positions were sold for cash. Gwageo exam results were rigged in favor of clan-affiliated candidates. Tax revenues were systematically diverted into clan coffers. The provincial bureaucracy decayed as locally appointed officials extracted maximum revenue from the peasantry to remit to their clan patrons in Hanyang.

The consequence was rural devastation. Tax collection became increasingly arbitrary and brutal. Granary loans (the *hwangok* system, which had originally been intended to provide low-interest loans to farmers in lean years) were converted into a mechanism of mass extortion — local officials would force peasants to take loans they did not want, then collect repayment plus interest on imaginary loans they had never actually issued. The rural population grew increasingly impoverished, indebted, and desperate. Land was concentrated in fewer and fewer hands. Famines became more frequent and more severe.

---

The first major sign that the Sedo system was producing outright revolt was the **Hong Gyeong-rae Rebellion** of 1811–1812.

**Hong Gyeong-rae** (HONG GYUNG-RAY, 1771–1812) was a yangban scholar from the northwestern province of **Pyeongan** (PYUNG-ahn, the area around modern Pyongyang). The northwestern provinces had long been excluded from high government office on the grounds that they were less Confucianized and produced less reliable scholars than the central provinces — a discrimination that had been maintained for centuries even when northwestern candidates passed the gwageo with high scores. Combined with the Sedo-era tax extortion, the regional discrimination had produced sustained northwestern resentment.

In December 1811, Hong Gyeong-rae raised a rebellion in the small county of **Gasan** with a few hundred followers. The movement spread rapidly. Within weeks, several northwestern counties were under rebel control. Hong proclaimed an alternative regime, denounced the Andong Kim corruption, and demanded redress for northern grievances. At the peak of the rebellion in early 1812, several thousand rebels controlled a substantial portion of the northwest.

The Joseon government took several months to mobilize an effective response. The royal army, severely degraded by decades of Sedo-era neglect, struggled to raise an effective force. Eventually a 5,000-soldier expedition was assembled under more competent commanders, marched north, and besieged the rebels in the walled city of **Jeongju** (JUNG-joo). The siege lasted four months. In May 1812, government forces tunneled under the city walls and detonated a gunpowder mine that breached the defenses. The city fell. Hong Gyeong-rae was killed in the final assault. Several thousand rebels were executed.

The Hong Gyeong-rae rebellion was the largest internal revolt Joseon had faced since the Imjin War, and it was a clear warning that the Sedo system was generating unsustainable popular pressure. The court's response was not to reform the system but to suppress it more harshly and to forget about northwestern grievances. The northwestern resentment would resurface later in the century in the Donghak movement (Chapter 8).

---

Cross-civ check: while the Sedo politics were destroying Joseon governance in the early nineteenth century, the **British East India Company** was completing its conquest of the Indian subcontinent (the **Anglo-Maratha Wars** ended in 1818); the **Qing Empire** was suffering its first humiliating military defeat by Western powers in the **First Opium War** (1839–42); the **Ottoman Empire** was undergoing the **Tanzimat Reforms** in an attempt to modernize before it was dismembered (Ottoman Empire Ch 8); the **United States** was completing its westward expansion through the Mexican-American War (1846–48); the **Russian Empire** was probing eastward into Central Asia and southward toward Iran (Safavid Persia's successor Qajar dynasty was being subjected to Russian pressure simultaneously with Joseon's analogous experience). The world was being remade by industrial-age power politics. Joseon was almost entirely unprepared, and the Sedo system had ensured it would have no resources to adapt.

---

# Chapter 8 — Opening, Annexation, End

The decline of Joseon into Japanese annexation took about fifty years to complete. The opening, in retrospect, was visible from a long way off.

---

The **Donghak** (DONG-hahk, "Eastern Learning") movement was founded in 1860 by a destitute yangban scholar named **Choe Je-u** (CHOY JEH-OO, 1824–1864). Choe had been raised in the impoverished southwestern province of Jeolla, had failed the gwageo, and had spent a decade in religious seeking — Buddhist meditation, Daoist practice, even some exposure to Catholic literature. In 1860 he experienced what he described as a divine revelation in which **Hanullim** (HAH-nool-im, "Lord of Heaven," a traditional Korean shamanistic deity-name) commanded him to teach a new doctrine that would save Korea from Western religious corruption.

The doctrine was deliberately syncretic. Donghak combined Confucian ethics, Buddhist meditative practice, Daoist cosmology, and traditional Korean shamanism into a single religious system. The name "Eastern Learning" was a deliberate counterpoint to "Western Learning" (*Seohak*), the Korean term for Catholicism. Choe Je-u was preaching that Korea should resist Western religious importation by drawing on its own indigenous spiritual resources, blended into a new synthetic faith.

The political content of Donghak was even more important than the religious content. Choe taught that "all human beings are equal," that the rigid yangban-commoner-slave hierarchy was a violation of Heaven's intention, that the corruption of officials was a moral abomination requiring purification, and that the revelation he had received was meant for the common people first. This was effectively a Korean millenarian movement with explicit social-revolutionary content. Within four years, Donghak had spread across the southwestern provinces, attracting impoverished peasants, dissatisfied yangban, displaced gwageo failures, and discharged soldiers.

The Joseon government recognized the political danger immediately. Choe Je-u was arrested in late 1863, charged with sedition, and executed by decapitation in 1864. His designated successor **Choe Si-hyeong** (CHOY SHEE-hyung) went underground, organized the surviving congregations into a tighter network, and continued teaching for the next thirty years. The Donghak movement did not die. It went underground and grew. In 1894 it would explode into full rebellion (see below).

---

Meanwhile, the long isolation was being broken from the outside.

The **Heungseon Daewongun** (HUNG-sun DAY-WON-goon, 1820–1898) — birth name Yi Ha-eung, father of King Gojong — became regent in 1864 when his ten-year-old son ascended the throne. The Daewongun was, by Sedo-era standards, a remarkable politician. He systematically broke the power of the Andong Kim and Pungyang Jo clans, gutted the seowon system (Chapter 3), purged corrupt officials, rebuilt Gyeongbokgung Palace (which had been a charred ruin since the Imjin War, see Chapter 4), restored some royal authority, and re-energized the central state.

He was also rigidly committed to isolation. His policy slogan was carved on stone steles erected across the country: **"Western barbarians have invaded; if we do not fight, we must make peace, but advocating peace is selling out the country."** The Daewongun believed that the only way to preserve Korean sovereignty was to refuse all foreign contact, build up military strength, and execute anyone who collaborated with foreigners.

This produced two early military confrontations with Western powers, both of which Joseon won militarily, and both of which set up worse problems later.

In 1866, the armed American merchant steamer **General Sherman** sailed up the Taedong River to Pyongyang demanding trade rights. Local Korean officials denied the demand. The American crew opened fire on Korean civilians, took hostages, and refused to leave. Eventually local forces under **Park Gyu-su** (PAHK GYOO-soo) — Park Ji-won's grandson, and later one of the architects of Korean modernization — captured the ship by setting it on fire with burning rafts. All twenty-four crew members died. The General Sherman incident became a Korean victory of sorts: the foreign barbarians had been defeated. It also planted a grudge that would lead to American military reprisal five years later.

In late 1866, France retaliated for the recent execution of nine French Catholic missionaries (in the Byeong-in Persecution) by sending seven warships and 600 marines to **Ganghwa Island** under Rear Admiral Pierre-Gustave Roze. The French marines occupied parts of the island for several weeks, burned the Joseon **Oegyujanggak** (OY-gyoo-jahng-gahk, "Outer Royal Library") that had stored important state documents, and looted 297 royal manuscripts from it. (These manuscripts ended up at the Bibliothèque Nationale de France in Paris, where they remained for nearly 150 years before being repatriated to Korea in 2011 — a long story with diplomatic resonances of its own.) The French were eventually beaten back by Joseon land forces and withdrew. Another Korean military victory; another grudge planted.

In 1871, the United States sent five Navy warships under Rear Admiral John Rodgers to demand reparations for the General Sherman and to force open Korean ports. The American expedition seized two Joseon forts on Ganghwa Island in a brief but bloody battle that killed 243 Korean defenders against three American dead. Despite this lopsided military result, Joseon refused to negotiate. The Americans waited at anchor for some weeks, then withdrew with nothing accomplished. A third Korean defensive victory.

These three episodes — General Sherman, the French expedition, the American expedition — confirmed the Daewongun in his belief that Western powers could be successfully resisted by force. The conclusion was largely correct in the short run. Joseon defenses were not yet so degraded that they could not repulse small Western expeditionary forces. The conclusion was disastrously wrong in the long run, because it discouraged any serious effort to reform the Korean military or modernize Korean institutions during the brief window when such reform might still have been possible.

---

The Daewongun's regency ended in 1873 when his son, King **Gojong** (GOH-johng, born 1852, reigned 1864–1907), came of age and asserted personal rule. Gojong's reign was largely shaped by his queen — **Queen Min** (MIN, 1851–1895), a woman of formidable political intelligence — who became the dominant force at court. The Min faction systematically undid much of the Daewongun's program, brought Min relatives back into office, and adopted a more flexible foreign policy that was open to limited modernization.

It was too late.

In 1875, Japan sent the gunboat **Unyo** (a small Western-style warship purchased from Britain) to Korean waters and provoked an incident at Ganghwa Island (the same fortress that had held off the French and Americans). The Japanese demanded reparations, treaty negotiations, and the opening of Korean ports.

The result was the **Treaty of Ganghwa**, signed February 27, 1876. Three Korean ports — Busan, Wonsan, and Incheon — were opened to Japanese trade. Japanese citizens were granted extraterritoriality (immunity from Korean law). Korea was declared "an independent nation" — a phrase carefully designed by Japan to detach Korea from its tributary relationship with Qing China and to make it a candidate for Japanese influence.

The Treaty of Ganghwa is sometimes called Korea's "perry moment," after the American Commodore Perry whose gunboats had forced Japan to open in 1854. The parallel is exact, and almost certainly deliberate: Japan had learned the lesson of being on the receiving end of gunboat diplomacy and was now applying the same playbook to Korea, just one generation later. The mimicry is one of the more striking patterns in nineteenth-century East Asian history. Japan modernized faster than Korea, and the consequence was that Japan got to be the imperial power and Korea got to be the colony.

---

The next two decades — from 1876 to 1894 — were a period of accelerating crisis as multiple foreign powers and multiple internal factions all maneuvered for advantage in Korea's politically chaotic situation.

The **Imo Incident** of 1882 was the first major flare-up. Korean soldiers of the traditional military, who had not been paid for over a year while a new Japanese-trained "Special Skill Force" (the *Byeolgigun*) was given new uniforms and modern equipment, mutinied. They attacked the Japanese legation, killed seven Japanese instructors, killed Queen Min's relatives in court positions, and briefly restored the Daewongun to power. The Qing government, alarmed at the prospect of either Japanese intervention or anti-Min forces winning, sent 4,500 troops who arrived faster than the Japanese, arrested the Daewongun, and forcibly returned him to China for several years of detention. The incident left Qing troops permanently stationed in Hanyang and gave the Qing government direct involvement in Joseon affairs that it had not exercised in centuries.

The **Gapsin Coup** of 1884 was the second flare-up. A group of pro-Japanese reformers led by **Kim Ok-gyun** (KIM OHK-gyoon, 1851–1894) and **Park Yeong-hyo** (PAHK YUNG-hyo) staged a palace coup with Japanese backing, killed several conservative ministers, and proclaimed a fourteen-point modernization program — the abolition of class distinctions, the establishment of modern budgeting, the reorganization of the military, the modernization of the legal system. Three days later, Qing troops led by the young general **Yuan Shikai** (you-AHN SHEE-kai, who would later become the first president of the Republic of China after the Qing dynasty's fall) crushed the coup. Kim Ok-gyun fled to Japan. He was eventually assassinated in Shanghai in 1894, his body dismembered and returned to Korea where it was further mutilated as a public display.

The Imo and Gapsin episodes hardened the Sino-Japanese rivalry over Korea. Both the Qing and the Japanese now regarded Korea as a strategic stake. The two empires established an uneasy condominium under the **Convention of Tianjin** (1885), under which both agreed to withdraw their troops from Korea but each could send troops back if invited by Joseon, with each obligated to notify the other if it did so. The arrangement was a powder keg, awaiting only an excuse for either power to send its troops back in.

The excuse came in 1894.

---

The **Donghak Peasant Revolution** of 1894 was the largest peasant uprising in Joseon history.

The Donghak movement (see above) had been growing underground for thirty years. By the early 1890s, agricultural conditions in southwestern Korea were catastrophic — drought, locusts, official extortion, repeated tax demands. In January 1894, a Donghak preacher named **Jeon Bong-jun** (JUN BONG-JOON, 1855–1895) led a peasant revolt against an exceptionally corrupt magistrate named Jo Byeong-gap. The revolt spread rapidly. Within months, Donghak rebel forces controlled most of Jeolla Province and were threatening to march on Hanyang.

The rebel demands were radical but not unreasonable: end official corruption, lighten taxation, abolish slavery, redistribute land, expel foreign influence. The Donghak rebels were not simply destructive — they organized administrative councils in the territories they controlled, distributed food, and conducted negotiations with the central government.

The Joseon court panicked. Unable to trust the regular army to defeat the rebels, the Min faction asked Qing China for military assistance. Three thousand Qing troops landed at Asan in early June 1894. Under the Convention of Tianjin, the Japanese government was notified — and immediately sent its own much larger force of 8,000 troops, which landed at Incheon and marched on Hanyang.

Once Japanese troops were in Korea, war between Qing and Japan became inevitable.

---

The **First Sino-Japanese War** lasted seven months — from August 1894 to April 1895 — and was a comprehensive Japanese victory. Japan crushed Qing forces on land and at sea, sinking the Chinese fleet at the **Battle of the Yellow Sea** (September 1894), driving the Qing army out of Korea, and then invading Manchuria itself. The **Treaty of Shimonoseki** in April 1895 ended the war: China formally renounced its tributary relationship with Korea, ceded Taiwan and the Pescadores Islands to Japan, paid an enormous indemnity, and lost its status as East Asia's dominant power.

Korea was the prize. With Qing influence eliminated, Japan was now the dominant external power on the peninsula, and Japanese pressure for reform escalated. The pro-Japanese cabinet that took power after the Donghak revolution issued the **Gabo Reforms** of 1894–95: the slave system was abolished, the gwageo civil service exam was abolished, the four-class hierarchy was abolished, men and women were declared equal in remarriage rights, and a modern Western-style cabinet government was established. These were sweeping changes — in some sense the most radical legal reforms in Joseon history — but they were imposed under Japanese pressure rather than emerging from internal Korean reform processes, and many Koreans viewed them with suspicion as foreign-imposed Westernization.

The Gabo Reforms also failed to save the dynasty. They were the modernization that came too late.

---

The political climax of the immediate post-Sino-Japanese-War period was the **assassination of Queen Min** on October 8, 1895.

Queen Min, who had been pro-Japanese in earlier years, had pivoted sharply toward Russia after the Sino-Japanese War — calculating that the new Japanese dominance was more dangerous than the older Qing tutelage and that Russia might be a useful counterweight. The Japanese government, particularly Minister to Korea **Miura Goro** (mee-OO-rah goh-ROH), regarded the queen as the principal obstacle to Japanese influence in Korea.

Miura organized her murder.

Before dawn on October 8, 1895, a group of about fifty Japanese assassins — a mix of Japanese army personnel, civilian *soshi* (right-wing ruffians), and Korean collaborators — broke into Gyeongbokgung Palace, killed several palace guards, found the queen in her chambers, killed her with swords, dragged her body to the palace garden, and burned it. King Gojong was held under arrest in his own palace for several weeks. International outrage was substantial — the British, American, and Russian governments protested — but Japan suffered no real consequences. Miura was recalled to Japan, briefly tried, and acquitted on a technicality.

The Min assassination is the moment the Joseon dynasty effectively lost its claim to sovereignty. A foreign power had murdered a Korean queen inside the royal palace and faced no meaningful punishment. The clear message was that Joseon could not protect its own monarchs. Gojong, terrified, fled to the Russian legation in February 1896 and lived there for over a year — running the country, in effect, from a foreign embassy on Korean soil.

---

In 1897, after returning from his Russian-legation refuge, King Gojong proclaimed himself Emperor of the **Daehan Jeguk** (DAY-hahn JEH-gook, "Korean Empire"). The proclamation was a bid for sovereignty equal to the Qing and Japanese emperors — Korea was now formally an empire, no longer subordinate to any other power.

The Korean Empire lasted thirteen years. It was never really independent. Throughout the period from 1897 to 1910, Russian, Japanese, and to a lesser extent American interests competed for influence at the Korean court. Gojong played one foreign power against another — sometimes Russian-leaning, sometimes Japanese-leaning, sometimes hopefully American-leaning. None of the foreign powers actually wanted to defend Korean sovereignty; they wanted to position themselves to absorb Korea on advantageous terms.

The decisive contest was the **Russo-Japanese War** of 1904–05, the first modern war won by an Asian power against a major European power. Japan crushed Russian forces in Manchuria and at sea (the **Battle of Tsushima** in May 1905 destroyed the Russian Baltic Fleet that had sailed halfway around the world). The **Treaty of Portsmouth** in September 1905 — mediated by US President Theodore Roosevelt, who later won the Nobel Peace Prize for it — gave Japan a free hand in Korea. The Americans, in a separate informal agreement (the **Taft-Katsura Memorandum**), recognized Japanese control of Korea in exchange for Japanese recognition of American control of the Philippines.

Korea was alone.

---

The **Eulsa Treaty** of November 17, 1905, formally established Japanese control over Korean foreign policy. Five Korean ministers — the **Five Eulsa Traitors** — signed the treaty under direct Japanese pressure. Emperor Gojong refused to sign personally but was unable to prevent it. The treaty stripped Korea of its right to conduct foreign affairs and installed a Japanese **Resident-General** (the first being **Ito Hirobumi**, see below) with supervisory authority over the Korean government.

Korean reaction was widespread and emotional. The newspaper editor **Jang Ji-yeon** (JAHNG JEE-yun) wrote a famous editorial titled "Today We Cry" lamenting the treaty's signing. Several prominent Korean officials committed suicide in protest — the most famous being the senior official **Min Yeong-hwan**, who left a suicide note urging continued resistance and slit his throat in his home. Demonstrations and small armed uprisings broke out across the country.

Gojong made one last effort. In 1907 he secretly dispatched three envoys — **Yi Sang-seol**, **Yi Jun**, and **Yi Wi-jong** — to the **Second Hague Peace Conference** in the Netherlands, with instructions to denounce the Eulsa Treaty before the international community. The envoys traveled across Russia and reached the Hague, but the conference's organizers, under Japanese pressure, refused them an official hearing. One envoy, Yi Jun, died at the Hague (probably suicide; the Korean version of events says assassination, but the evidence is consistent with despair). The Hague mission failed.

Japan used the Hague mission as a pretext to depose Gojong. The Japanese Resident-General forced Gojong to abdicate in favor of his mentally limited son **Sunjong** (SOON-johng, 1874–1926). This was the last Joseon king. He had no real authority. Japanese officials made every meaningful decision.

---

In October 1909, a Korean independence fighter named **An Jung-geun** (AHN JOONG-GUN, 1879–1910) traveled to **Harbin railway station** in Manchuria, where the former Japanese Resident-General **Ito Hirobumi** (ee-TOH hee-roh-BOO-mee, 1841–1909, the architect of the Eulsa Treaty and arguably the most important Japanese statesman of the early Meiji period) was scheduled to arrive to meet with the Russian Minister of Finance.

When Ito stepped off the train, An Jung-geun shot him three times at close range with a Browning automatic pistol. Ito died within minutes. An Jung-geun was immediately arrested by Russian guards and turned over to Japanese authorities. He was tried in a Japanese court in occupied Liaodong, sentenced to death, and hanged in March 1910. Throughout his trial and imprisonment he was unrepentant, declaring that he had killed Ito as an act of resistance against Japanese imperialism in Korea, and he wrote an unfinished treatise titled "On East Asian Peace" arguing for a confederation of Korea, China, and Japan as equals against Western imperialism.

An Jung-geun is now a hero in both North and South Korea. His memorial in Harbin (built recently in cooperation with the Chinese government) and his calligraphy (he was a fine calligrapher in classical Chinese, and several pieces written in his prison cell survive) are objects of genuine veneration. He is one of the figures who, in the historical memory of modern Korea, redeems the otherwise humiliating final years of Joseon — a Korean who acted, who succeeded in striking down one of the architects of his country's destruction, and who died with dignity.

His action did not prevent the annexation. It probably accelerated it.

---

On August 22, 1910, Japan formally annexed Korea. The **Japan-Korea Treaty of 1910** transferred sovereignty entirely from Korea to Japan, dissolved the imperial government, and made Korea a Japanese colony. The Korean prime minister **Yi Wan-yong** (yee WAHN-yong) signed for Korea; Resident-General **Terauchi Masatake** signed for Japan. Emperor Sunjong was reduced to a figurehead under house arrest. The Joseon dynasty ended after 518 years.

Korea would be a Japanese colony for the next 35 years, until Japan's defeat in World War II in August 1945. The colonial period — its brutality, its exploitation, its forced labor, its sexual slavery (the so-called "comfort women"), its attempt to suppress Korean language and culture, its industrial transformation of the peninsula, its complicated legacies — is the subject of the next TL in the chain (Korean Modern). Joseon ends here.

---

A reckoning, before closing the dynasty.

Joseon lasted 518 years. By any reasonable measure that is one of the longest sustained runs of any single political system anywhere in human history. The Roman Empire — counting from Augustus to the fall of the Western Empire — lasted about 500 years. The Ming dynasty in China lasted 276. The Tokugawa shogunate in Japan lasted 264. The Romanov dynasty in Russia lasted 304. The Bourbon dynasty in France lasted 200. Joseon outlasted all of them, by significant margins in most cases.

What did the dynasty actually produce?

It produced an alphabet that thirty million people now read every day. It produced a civilian-led bureaucratic state with sophisticated mechanisms of accountability, even if those mechanisms ultimately failed in the nineteenth century. It produced the most extensive systematic dynastic record (the Annals of the Joseon Dynasty, 1,893 volumes) in any premodern East Asian state. It produced a series of remarkable technological innovations under Sejong — the rain gauge, the water clock, the metal type printing — that for a brief moment made fifteenth-century Korea the leading scientific producer in East Asia. It produced philosophers (Toegye, Yulgok) whose work was studied seriously in Tokugawa Japan and, to a lesser extent, in Qing China. It produced one of the great naval commanders in any country in any century (Yi Sun-sin), whose six-year defense of the Korean peninsula against Japanese invasion stands as one of the great military achievements of the early-modern world. It produced an intellectual movement (Silhak) that, had it been allowed to flower fully under Jeongjo's grandsons rather than being suppressed in the Catholic persecutions, might have provided a basis for orderly modernization. It produced a body of visual art (true-view landscapes, genre paintings, white porcelain) that has shaped twentieth-century Korean and international aesthetic sensibilities. It produced a Confucian social order so durable that elements of it can still be detected in modern South Korean family life, business culture, and educational practice.

It also produced four major literati purges, the deliberate Confucianization of Korean women into legal and social diminishment, a slave system that held a third of the population at the dynasty's height, the Imjin War that killed up to two million Koreans and burned the country to the ground, the Manchu surrender ceremony at Samjeondo, the rice-chest murder of Crown Prince Sado, the Sedo politics that hollowed out the state apparatus across the early nineteenth century, and the catastrophic failure to modernize that ended in the 1910 annexation.

Both ledgers are real. The dynasty was simultaneously one of the most achieved and one of the most failed political systems of the early-modern world, and the achievements and failures cannot really be separated — they came from the same Confucian elite scholarly culture, the same bureaucratic stability, the same long historical inertia. Joseon was good at being Joseon for a very long time. It could not become anything else when the world demanded it.

---

The Korean people survived the dynasty's end. They survived 35 years of brutal Japanese colonization. They survived the trauma of national partition in 1945. They survived the Korean War of 1950–53. They survived the dictatorship years of the 1960s through 1980s. The two Koreas now exist on the same peninsula their Joseon ancestors inhabited, speaking the alphabet that Sejong invented, eating versions of the food that Joseon farmers grew, organized to greater or lesser degrees by the Confucian family categories that Jeong Do-jeon helped impose.

The dynasty died. Korea did not. Korea always survives.

That is the story.

# Timurid Empire — The Skull-Pyramid Builder Who Founded a Renaissance

**Goal:** Narrative for the Timurid Empire (1370–1507 CE), the Central Asian dynasty founded by Timur (TEE-moor), the limping Turco-Mongol warlord that European sources called Tamerlane (TAH-mer-lain). Timur conducted one of the most violent imperial conquests of the medieval world, building skull pyramids outside cities that resisted, sacking Baghdad twice, ending the Tughlaq dynasty at Delhi in 1398, capturing the Ottoman sultan Bayezid I (BYE-eh-zeed) at Ankara in 1402, and dying in 1405 on his way to invade Ming China. His son Shah Rukh and grandson Ulugh Beg (oo-LOOG BEG) then turned Herat (HEH-rat) and Samarkand (SAM-ar-kand) into the cultural laboratories of the Persianate world: a great observatory, the most accurate star catalog in the world before the telescope, the Bihzad (bee-ZAHD) miniature painting tradition, and the architectural vocabulary that the Mughal Empire would inherit intact when Babur — the last Timurid prince — was driven from his ancestral lands by Uzbek conquerors in 1505 and founded a new empire in India in 1526. The Timurid story is the most direct sequel to the Mongol Empire (Mongol Empire Ch 5 and Ch 7) and the most immediate prequel to the Safavid (Safavid Persia Ch 1) and Mughal worlds. Honest accounting of the brutality is non-negotiable. Honest accounting of the cultural achievement is non-negotiable too.

The skull pyramids were real, the chronicle accounts of mass massacres at Isfahan and Delhi and Damascus are well-documented, and the agricultural and demographic damage to the central Islamic world took centuries to recover. The cultural achievement at fifteenth-century Herat was also real. Both things are true at once. That is the Timurid story.

# Chapter 1 — Tamerlane's World

The empire that Timur built sat astride two civilizational worlds that the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century had violently knit together — the settled Persianate world of Iran and Khorasan to the south, and the nomadic Turkic-Mongol steppe to the north — and the joining was uneasy. Both worlds were in flux when Timur was born around 1336, and the political vacuum produced by their joint instability is what made his career possible. To understand Timur, you have to understand the wreckage that produced him.

---

The wreckage was the legacy of the **Mongol Empire** (Mongol Empire Ch 1–8). In the early thirteenth century, **Genghis Khan** (Mongol Empire Ch 1) had united the Mongol tribes and led them out of the steppe in the most rapid imperial expansion in human history. By 1260 the Mongols controlled an empire stretching from Korea to Hungary. By 1294, when **Kublai Khan** (Mongol Empire Ch 6) died, the unified Mongol Empire had fragmented into four successor states ruled by rival branches of Genghis's descendants: the **Yuan** in China (Mongol Empire Ch 6), the **Ilkhanate** in Iran (Mongol Empire Ch 7), the **Golden Horde** in the Russian steppe (Mongol Empire Ch 8), and the **Chagatai Khanate** (CHAH-gah-tye) in Central Asia.

The Chagatai Khanate is the one Timur was born into. It is named for **Chagatai** (CHAH-gah-tye), Genghis Khan's second son, who received Central Asia — roughly the territory between the Aral Sea and the Tarim Basin — in his father's division of the empire. The Chagatai khans ruled Transoxiana (the rich agricultural region between the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers, in modern Uzbekistan), Khorasan, and the eastern steppe from the 1220s onward. Their core territories included the great Silk Road cities of **Samarkand** (SAM-ar-kand) and **Bukhara** (boo-KHAR-ah), which had been sacked during the original Mongol conquests in 1220 but had been rebuilt as Mongol-administered centers.

The Chagatai story through the fourteenth century is a slow-motion collapse, useful to walk through because it explains the world Timur stepped into. The early Chagatai khans had ruled as steppe nomads, refusing to settle in the conquered cities and treating Transoxiana as grazing territory rather than as agricultural taxation base. Through the early fourteenth century, two factions developed within the khanate: a "settled" faction that wanted to rule from the cities, intermarry with the local Persian-speaking Muslim population, and convert to Islam; and a "nomadic" faction that wanted to maintain steppe traditions and resist the lure of urban civilization.

The settled faction won, partially. In 1334, the Chagatai khan **Tarmashirin** (tar-mah-SHEER-in) formally converted to Islam and tried to reorganize the khanate as a Persianate Muslim state on the model of the Ilkhanate. The nomadic faction revolted, killed him, and the khanate fractured. By the 1340s the Chagatai realm had split into two pieces: a western half (sometimes called Transoxiana or Mawarannahr, and centered on the agricultural cities of Samarkand and Bukhara) and an eastern half (called Moghulistan, in the Tian Shan range and modern Xinjiang). The western half was nominally ruled by Chagatai puppet khans whose actual power was wielded by local emirs and tribal warlords; the eastern half remained more nomadic and sent periodic raids into the western settled lands.

This is a useful comparison to set up. The Chagatai struggle between settled-Muslim and nomadic-steppe identities resembles the struggle that the **Yuan dynasty** (Mongol Empire Ch 6) was having in China at the same time, where Mongol rulers in Beijing were similarly debating whether to assimilate as Chinese-style emperors or maintain steppe identity. It also resembles the struggle that the **Ilkhanate** in Iran (Mongol Empire Ch 7) had resolved a generation earlier when Ghazan Khan converted to Islam in 1295. And it resembles, on the other end, the struggle the **Golden Horde** (Mongol Empire Ch 8) was having in the Russian steppe, where Mongol khans were converting to Islam under Berke and Uzbeg while their Russian subjects remained Orthodox Christian. Across the Mongol successor world, the same question — to settle or to remain nomadic, to convert or to maintain steppe paganism — was being answered differently in each fragment of the original empire. The Chagatai answer was: both, badly, with constant civil war.

By the 1340s the political authority of the Chagatai khans was a fiction. Real power in Transoxiana belonged to a constellation of competing tribal emirs — leaders of clans like the **Barlas** (BAR-lass), the **Jalayir**, the **Suldus**, and the **Arlat** — who fought each other for control of the rich agricultural land around Samarkand, raised their own private armies, and treated the Chagatai khans as ceremonial figureheads to be installed and deposed at will.

This was the world Timur was born into.

---

The exact date of Timur's birth is not certain. The standard date is **April 8, 1336**, given by the Timurid court historian **Sharaf al-Din Yazdi** in the **Zafarnama** ("Book of Victory"), the official Timurid history compiled in 1424 — but Yazdi was writing nearly a century after the event, his sources were partly oral, and he was producing a deliberately propagandistic text that emphasized auspicious astrological alignments. The best estimate is that Timur was born sometime between 1330 and 1340. The standard date 1336 is probably close enough.

He was born in **Kesh** (later called **Shahrisabz**, "Green City"), a town about 50 miles south of Samarkand in modern Uzbekistan. His clan was the **Barlas** — a Mongol clan that had been settled in Transoxiana for over a century since the original Genghisid conquests, and that had Turkified linguistically (the Barlas spoke a Turkic dialect, not Mongolian, by Timur's day) while retaining Mongol tribal identity and aristocratic lineage. The Barlas were not one of the dominant emirate clans of the period, but they were respectable: minor aristocracy in the Chagatai system, with grazing rights and tribal followers, but no political power at the khanate level.

Timur's father was **Taraghai**, a minor Barlas chief who appears in the chronicle accounts as a respectable but undistinguished tribal leader. His mother was **Tegina Begim**. The family was Sunni Muslim by Timur's generation — the Persianate religious culture had absorbed the steppe Mongols of Transoxiana over the preceding century, in the same gradual mechanism that had turned the Ilkhanate's Mongols Muslim during Ghazan Khan's reign. The conversion was not pure ideological adoption but the slow accumulation of intermarriage, Sufi missionary influence, and the practical advantages of speaking the language of the people you were taxing.

Most importantly: Timur was **not a direct descendant of Genghis Khan**. The Barlas clan claimed shared distant ancestry with the Borjigin (Genghis's clan) through a putative common Mongol ancestor, but the line was far enough back that no court genealogist could plausibly claim Timur as a Genghisid in the senior line. This fact would constrain Timur for the rest of his career. By the steppe customary law (the **Yasa** of Genghis Khan, Mongol Empire Ch 1), only direct male-line descendants of Genghis could legitimately claim the title **Khan**. Timur could conquer, he could rule, he could massacre — but he could never legally call himself Khan. He worked around this constraint his whole life. The workarounds shaped the structure of his empire.

A note on the names: throughout the Persian-language sources, Timur is called **Timur** (TEE-moor — the Turkic word for "iron"). After his leg injury (more on which shortly), he became **Timur-i Lang** ("Timur the Lame"), often shortened to **Timur Lang**. European sources transliterated this through Latin and Italian into **Tamerlane**, the form Christopher Marlowe used in *Tamburlaine the Great* (1587), and the form most English-language general readers know. Modern scholarship uses Timur. We will use Timur for the man and his dynasty (the Timurids), with occasional reminders that Tamerlane is the same person.

---

The next thirty years of Timur's life are sketchy in the surviving record. Yazdi's Zafarnama gives a hagiographical account of a precocious warrior-leader gathering followers from his teens onward. The earlier and more candid **Tuzukat-i Timuri** ("Memoirs of Timur"), which purports to be Timur's own dictated autobiography, is now generally considered a sixteenth-century forgery and not reliable. What can be reconstructed from contemporary chronicle fragments and from later Timurid sources, read critically, is roughly this:

In his teens and early twenties, Timur led a small band of Barlas tribal followers, conducting cattle raids and seasonal sheep-stealing operations in the borderlands south of Samarkand. The line between "minor tribal warlord" and "bandit" in the fourteenth-century Chagatai world was almost meaningless — successful young Barlas chiefs built their followings the same way: a small armed band, a successful raid, the loot distributed generously, more followers attracted by the prospect of more loot, more raids. Timur was good at it. By his mid-twenties he had a band of perhaps a few hundred men.

Sometime around 1363, when Timur was in his late twenties, he was seriously wounded in a skirmish in **Sistan** (sis-TAN, in southeastern Iran). The chronicle accounts vary: one tradition says he was hit by an arrow in the leg and shoulder during a sheep-rustling raid, another says he was wounded in a battle with a local emir. The injuries were real and permanent. His right leg was crippled — he walked with a noticeable limp for the rest of his life — and his right arm was partially withered, with reduced function. The nickname **Timur-i Lang** ("Timur the Lame") dates from this period.

We know the injuries were real because of an unusual line of evidence. In **June 1941**, Soviet anthropologists led by **Mikhail Gerasimov** opened Timur's tomb at the Gur-e-Amir mausoleum in Samarkand and examined the skeleton. The remains were of a man about 5'7" (170 cm), muscular, in his late sixties at death, with a healed fracture of the right leg femur and damage to the right elbow consistent with an old wound — exactly matching the chronicle descriptions. Gerasimov also produced a forensic facial reconstruction that has become the standard image of Timur in popular sources. (A famous and probably apocryphal story attached itself to the exhumation: a local tradition reportedly warned Gerasimov that opening Timur's tomb would unleash a curse, and Nazi Germany's invasion of the Soviet Union began three days after the exhumation, on June 22, 1941. Gerasimov was a working anthropologist and not given to mysticism, but the coincidence of dates is striking enough that the story is now part of the Timur mythology. The remains were reburied with full Islamic rites in November 1942 — and the Soviet victory at Stalingrad began the following month. Make of all this what you will.)

The injuries didn't stop Timur from leading armies. They did permanently shape his physical movement and probably his self-image. Contemporary observers describe him as a man who ignored physical pain to a degree that struck them as superhuman — riding through campaigns that broke other men, sleeping outdoors in winter conditions in his sixties, refusing to be carried in a litter even when his condition deteriorated. The lameness was a constant reminder, and a constant repudiation, of physical limitation.

---

The political path that took Timur from raiding party leader to ruler of Transoxiana ran through one figure: his brother-in-law and partner **Amir Husayn** (hoo-SAYN — not to be confused with **Husayn Bayqara**, the much later Timurid ruler we will meet in Chapter 8).

Amir Husayn was the grandson of a previous emir of Transoxiana and had a stronger hereditary claim to leadership than Timur did. In the 1360s, he and Timur formed an alliance: Husayn provided the political legitimacy and the senior tribal connections, Timur provided the military leadership and the personal followers. Together they fought a series of wars against the **Moghul** (or Eastern Chagatai) khans who had been raiding Transoxiana from the east, and against rival local emirs.

The alliance was always tense. The chronicle accounts describe repeated quarrels, brief separations, and reconciliations forced by external threats. The decisive break came in 1370. By then, Timur had built his own personal following large enough that he no longer needed Husayn, and Husayn — a less competent leader — was losing political support. Timur turned on him: he besieged Husayn at the fortress of **Balkh** (BALKH, in modern Afghanistan), captured him, and executed him. The execution was conducted by an unnamed third party — Timur was careful never to claim direct responsibility for killing his former ally, though it was understood that he had ordered it. With Husayn out of the way, Timur was the dominant military figure in Transoxiana.

In April 1370, Timur captured **Samarkand** and held a tribal assembly (a **kurultai** — the same Mongol institution that had proclaimed Genghis Khan as ruler in 1206, Mongol Empire Ch 1) at which he was acknowledged as sovereign of Transoxiana. He was around thirty-four years old. He had risen from minor Barlas tribal chief to ruler of one of the richest agricultural regions in Central Asia in roughly fifteen years. He held that position for the next thirty-five years, until his death in 1405, and used it as the staging ground for an empire that eventually reached from Anatolia to Delhi.

But he had a problem that no military victory could solve: he was not a Genghisid.

---

The legitimacy puzzle is the single most distinctive feature of Timurid politics, so it deserves a careful unpacking. We will return to it constantly.

In the Mongol political tradition that had governed Eurasia since 1206, the title **Khan** (and especially the higher title **Khaqan**, "Great Khan" or "Khan of Khans") was reserved by customary law for direct male-line descendants of Genghis. The rule was backed by the prestige of the **Yasa**, Genghis's customary legal code (Mongol Empire Ch 1), and by 150 years of universal practice across the Mongol successor world. The Yuan emperors were Genghisids. The Ilkhans were Genghisids. The Golden Horde khans were Genghisids. The Chagatai khans were Genghisids. Even rebels and rival contenders within these states had to be Genghisids to claim the title.

Timur was not. By the rules of the system he inherited, he could conquer territory and exercise power, but he could not legitimately call himself Khan. To do so would be a usurpation that would alienate the steppe constituencies whose loyalty he needed.

He worked around the constraint in several ways simultaneously. The first workaround was the **puppet khan**. Throughout his reign, Timur formally ruled as the deputy of a Genghisid puppet — first **Suyurghatmish** (SOO-yer-gat-mish, ruled nominally 1370–1388), then **Sultan Mahmud** (1388–1402), both descendants of the Chagatai line. The puppet khans had no actual power. They appeared at ceremonial events, their names were inscribed on coins, official decrees were issued in their name, and friday prayers were said for them. But the actual government — the army commands, the tax collection, the diplomatic correspondence — was Timur's. The arrangement was a transparent fiction that nobody in the Eurasian political world was fooled by, but the form was preserved. Timur could legitimately claim that the rightful Genghisid khan was on the throne, and that he himself was simply the chief military officer.

The second workaround was Timur's own title. He never called himself Khan. His official title was **Amir** ("commander," from the Arabic) or **Amir-i Kabir** ("great commander"), with the additional honorific **Sahib-Qiran** ("Lord of the Auspicious Conjunction" — a reference to a particular astrological alignment at his birth that Yazdi's chronicle interpreted as marking him as a world-conqueror). These were lesser titles than Khan, but they were not constrained to Genghisids and Timur could legitimately use them.

The third workaround was marriage. Around 1370, Timur married **Saray Mulk Khanum** (sah-RYE MOOLK kha-NOOM), a Chagatayid princess descended from Genghis Khan in the senior line. This marriage gave Timur the title **Guregen** (gher-AY-gen) — "son-in-law of the Khan" — which was the closest approximation to Genghisid status a non-Genghisid could obtain. Guregen was the title Timur used most often on his coinage and in his chancery documents. Saray Mulk Khanum became Timur's senior wife, the chief lady of his court, and the namesake of the great mosque he built at Samarkand (now called the **Bibi-Khanym Mosque** — Bibi-Khanym being a Persian rendering of "lady" applied to Saray Mulk Khanum). She survived Timur and lived on into Shah Rukh's reign.

The fourth workaround, developed gradually, was forged genealogy. Timurid court historians invented an elaborate genealogy connecting the Barlas clan to a putative Mongol ancestor shared with Genghis Khan in the deep ancestral past. The forgeries were transparent — modern historians regard them as obviously fabricated — but they served a political purpose. By the second generation of Timurid historiography, the official line was that Timur and Genghis were cousins of cousins, sharing common Mongol ancestry, and that Timur's rule was therefore a legitimate continuation of the Genghisid project rather than a usurpation. The Mughal court historians (working a century and a half later, in India) inherited and elaborated these genealogies into still more grandiose forms.

All of this is worth dwelling on because it shapes how the empire actually worked. Timur could not simply rule as a sovereign Khan in his own right. He had to conduct an elaborate political theater in which a ceremonial Chagatai khan sat on a ceremonial throne, while Timur the "great commander" exercised actual power. Diplomatic correspondence had to be routed through the puppet's chancery. Court ceremony had to acknowledge the puppet's nominal precedence. This was true throughout Timur's reign, not just at the beginning. He never escaped the legitimacy gap. He grew the empire to stretch from Anatolia to Delhi while still formally serving as the deputy of a fiction.

The consequences for the dynasty were structural. After Timur's death, the puppet-khan convention gradually eroded — Shah Rukh did not consistently use puppet khans, and his successors abandoned the practice. But the underlying problem, the absence of Genghisid legitimacy, persisted. It was one of the reasons the Timurid Empire never quite achieved the political solidity of its Genghisid predecessors. The Uzbeks who eventually conquered the Timurid heartland in 1500–1507 (Chapter 8) were Genghisids in the senior Jochid line, and they used the legitimacy advantage explicitly against the Timurids.

The point: the most violent conqueror of the medieval world spent his entire career working around a constitutional rule he could not break. The rule itself was a survival from the Genghisid moment a century and a half earlier. Mongol political theory continued to constrain post-Mongol politics long after the unified Mongol Empire had ceased to exist as a functioning state.

---

A pause, before the campaigns begin: the world Timur stepped onto in 1370 was a world that had already been transformed by the Mongols and was still digesting the transformation. The cities he would build at Samarkand and the architecture his successors would patronize at Herat drew on Persianate building traditions whose continuity reached back through the **Ilkhanate** (Mongol Empire Ch 7), the **Seljuk Turks**, the **Samanids** of Bukhara, and ultimately to the **Sasanian Empire** (Persian Empire Ch 9). The administrative systems he inherited at Samarkand were Persian-Islamic systems descended from the **Abbasid caliphate** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1), with Mongol-era modifications. The literary culture he patronized was the Persian-language poetic tradition that had been refined under the **Samanids** and **Ghaznavids** in the tenth and eleventh centuries. None of this was Mongol — it was the Persianate civilization the Mongols had conquered and then absorbed (Mongol Empire Ch 7, the Ilkhanate's transformation under Ghazan Khan).

The Mongol contributions to Timur's world were political and tribal: the Yasa code, the kurultai assembly, the Genghisid legitimacy framework, the tribal-confederation army structure, and a habit of imperial scale that pre-Mongol Persianate states had not really pursued. The Persianate contributions were administrative and cultural: the bureaucracy, the language of high culture, the architectural vocabulary, the religious establishments, the agricultural taxation systems. Timur synthesized both — combining Mongol political theater with Persianate administrative practice, Mongol military tradition with Islamic religious legitimacy, Turkic tribal identity with Persian high culture. The synthesis was awkward, often contradictory, and never fully resolved during Timur's lifetime. It would be Timur's son Shah Rukh who would let the Persianate side gradually win, producing the cultural florescence that defined the later Timurid period.

Cross-civ check: Timur's birth in 1336 places him squarely in the fourteenth century — the same period as the **Hundred Years' War** in Europe (Battle of Crécy 1346, Black Death 1347–51), the **Ming dynasty's** founding in China (1368, two years before Timur captured Samarkand), the high-medieval **Mali Empire** under Mansa Musa in West Africa (ruled 1312–1337), the **Vijayanagara Empire's** rise in southern India (founded 1336, the same year as Timur's traditional birth date), and the **Aztec foundation** of Tenochtitlan in 1325 in central Mexico (Aztec Empire Ch 1). The Black Death that swept Europe and the Middle East in 1347–51 also reached Central Asia — some scholars argue the demographic damage from the plague contributed to the political fragmentation that gave Timur his opening, though the evidence is thin and the mechanism is hard to prove. The mid-fourteenth century is one of the great inflection points of human history, with empires rising and falling on five continents simultaneously, and Timur's career falls right inside it.

# Chapter 2 — The Conqueror's Method

What made Timur a world-historical figure rather than just a regional warlord was the systematic terror he applied to the cities and peoples he conquered, combined with the scale of military operations he was willing and able to conduct. From his consolidation in Transoxiana in 1370 through the early 1390s, he developed a method of imperial conquest that was distinctive even by the brutal standards of medieval warfare. The method had military components, political components, and propaganda components. They worked together. They worked well enough that for thirty-five years, almost nothing in the Eurasian world was strong enough to resist him.

This chapter is about that method.

---

The military component started with the army.

Timur's army was a hybrid force. The core was Turco-Mongol cavalry on the steppe model: light horse archers in the Mongol tradition (Mongol Empire Ch 2), supplemented by heavy cavalry in armor for shock action. The cavalry was supplied by the tribal levies of the Chagatai confederations — Barlas, Jalayir, Suldus, and others — and supplemented by recruited steppe nomads from the Kipchak and Moghulistan regions. Tactically the army operated in the classic Mongol fashion: mobility-first, with feigned retreats, encircling maneuvers, and concentrated arrow volleys to break enemy formations.

What was new compared to the Genghisid armies was the integration of substantial infantry forces and siege capabilities for taking walled cities. By the 1380s, Timur had developed siege engineering teams — captured Persian and Iraqi engineers, later supplemented by Indian craftsmen brought back from the 1398 Delhi campaign — capable of building catapults, mining tunnels under walls, and constructing siege towers. He also experimented with early gunpowder weapons, though gunpowder artillery was not yet decisive in Central Asian warfare and would not become so until the early sixteenth century. The Janissary musketeers and field artillery that the Ottomans would deploy at Chaldiran in 1514 (Safavid Persia Ch 3) and the matchlocks that Babur would use at Panipat in 1526 (Chapter 8) were the technologies that finally displaced the Mongol-style cavalry system. Timur was operating in the last generation of pure cavalry-based imperial warfare.

The army's logistical reach was extraordinary. Timur conducted campaigns on three continents. He would assemble at Samarkand, march two thousand miles to Damascus, fight a major battle, and return to Samarkand within a single campaign cycle. This required an enormous baggage train, prepositioned supply depots along the routes, careful seasonal scheduling, and an administrative machinery for keeping the army fed, watered, and paid across distances that European medieval armies could not have managed. The expertise to run this logistics machinery was inherited largely from the Mongol Empire's earlier century of practice — the Yam relay system (Mongol Empire Ch 4), the steppe-distance estimation traditions, the tax collection apparatus that funded the campaigns.

What made Timur's campaigns different from Genghis Khan's was their reversibility. Genghis and his successors had conquered to incorporate territory into a unified Mongol state. Timur conquered, looted, sometimes massacred, and then withdrew. He did not garrison the cities he took with permanent occupying forces. He did not impose Timurid administrators on conquered provinces in any systematic way. The pattern was: appear with overwhelming force, demand submission, accept tribute or punish resistance, install a local client regime, and march on. This was raid-and-loot warfare conducted at imperial scale. It produced enormous short-term wealth for the conquerors but left the conquered regions in a state of administrative chaos that often persisted for decades.

The reasons for this pattern are debated. One factor is the simple administrative limit of any single ruler: Timur could not be in three places at once, and he chose to spend his time on the next campaign rather than on long-term administration of the previous one. Another factor is the nature of his coalition: his army was made up of tribal contingents whose loyalty depended on ongoing successful campaigns and shares of loot, and a ruler who stopped campaigning would have lost his political base. A third factor is the legitimacy gap discussed in Chapter 1: Timur could not legitimately claim the title Khan and so could not credibly claim sovereignty over conquered territory in the way a Genghisid could. Conquering and withdrawing was politically safer than conquering and ruling.

Whatever the reasons, the consequences were enormous. The agricultural base of the central Islamic world — the irrigated farmlands of Iraq, southern Iran, and Khorasan — was repeatedly devastated by Timurid campaigns and never properly rebuilt during Timur's lifetime. The decline in agricultural productivity, demographic collapse in major cities, and disruption of long-distance trade caused by these campaigns is documented in the chronicle records and in modern archaeological surveys. Some Iranian regions did not return to their pre-Timur populations for two or three centuries. The economic damage Timur did to the world he conquered was, in the long run, larger than the wealth he extracted from it.

---

The terror component was the skull pyramid.

The practice of stacking the heads of massacred populations into pyramids outside the walls of cities that had resisted is documented in Timurid sources, in non-Timurid Persian chronicles, in Arab Mamluk sources, and in European accounts (Clavijo, who visited Samarkand in 1404, mentions seeing displays of skulls; later European travelers report similar sights). The practice was not invented by Timur — Mongol armies under Genghis Khan and his successors had occasionally done similar things, and pre-Mongol Central Asian warfare had its own traditions of severed-head display — but Timur made it systematic and ostentatious in a way that previous practitioners had not.

The basic mechanism was: a city that resisted, surrendered after a siege, and was then occupied would be subjected to a population massacre, after which the heads of the dead would be collected and stacked into pyramidal towers near the city's gates. Each tower would contain perhaps thousands or tens of thousands of skulls (the chronicle figures vary widely and are not modern-style census counts). The towers were left in place as a permanent monument to the punishment.

Documented cases include **Sabzevar** in eastern Iran (1383), where the city's defenders were massacred after a prolonged siege; **Isfahan** in 1387, where a tax revolt against a Timurid garrison was punished with a general massacre of perhaps 70,000 people; **Tikrit** in northern Iraq (1393); **Aleppo** in Syria (1400); **Baghdad** in 1401, where the chronicle accounts say each Timurid soldier was ordered to bring at least two heads and the resulting pyramid was one of the largest Timur ever built; and **Damascus** in 1401. There were others. The Isfahan massacre is particularly well-documented because Isfahan was an important Persian-Islamic cultural center and the massacre's effects on the city's intellectual life were noted by contemporary Persian chroniclers who had no reason to exaggerate Timurid atrocities.

The numerical scale of these massacres is hard to pin down. The chronicle accounts give round figures (70,000 at Isfahan, 90,000 at Baghdad, comparable numbers elsewhere) that are clearly not based on modern-style census methods — they are estimates, and they have a tendency to round up to dramatic-sounding numbers. Modern historians have argued for both somewhat lower figures (treating chronicle numbers as exaggerations) and somewhat higher figures (when the chronicle accounts are corroborated by archaeological survey data showing depopulation). The order of magnitude — that Timurid massacres killed people in the tens of thousands at multiple cities, totaling somewhere in the hundreds of thousands of victims across his career — is well-established. The exact body count is not, and probably cannot be.

What is the point of pausing on this? Two things. First, the brutality was not incidental to Timur's career — it was central to his political method. The skull pyramids were a deliberate signaling device. The message they sent was: resist Timur and your city will be destroyed; surrender promptly and your city will be spared. The Damascus, Aleppo, Baghdad, and Delhi examples all happened to cities that had been warned and had refused to submit. The message was understood. Cities that heard about the previous massacres were significantly more likely to open their gates without a fight, and Timur preferred this — a city that surrendered yielded its tribute and craftsmen with much less effort than a city that had to be stormed.

Second, the brutality is what produced the cultural achievement. The skilled craftsmen — weavers, calligraphers, tilemakers, miniature painters, architects, engineers — who built Samarkand into one of the great cities of the medieval world were almost all forcibly deported from the cities Timur had massacred. Indian stonemasons from Delhi were marched up to Samarkand to build the Bibi-Khanym Mosque. Damascus weavers were resettled to produce textiles for the Timurid court. Tabriz miniature painters were brought to work on Timurid manuscripts. The cultural enrichment of Samarkand in the 1390s and 1400s was directly caused by the cultural impoverishment of every other major city Timur conquered. He took the artistic capital of the Islamic world and concentrated it forcibly in a single capital.

The Renaissance at Herat under Timur's son Shah Rukh (Chapter 6) inherited this concentrated craft capacity. The Bihzad miniature tradition, the Jami poetic tradition, the architectural style that the Mughals carried to India — all of this rests on a deportation infrastructure that Timur built through systematic terror across thirty years. There is no clean way to separate the cultural achievement from the violence that produced it. They are the same project.

This is unpleasant. It is also true.

---

The political component, alongside the puppet khans (Chapter 1), was a system of provincial administration through trusted family members and tribal allies. Timur installed his sons and grandsons as governors of major provinces — **Miran Shah** (Timur's third son) in northwestern Iran, **Umar Shaykh** (Timur's second son) in Fars, **Shah Rukh** (Timur's fourth son, the eventual successor) in Khorasan, and **Pir Muhammad** (a grandson) in Afghanistan and parts of India. The princes had real authority within their provinces but reported to Timur and could be overruled or replaced at any time. The system worked while Timur was alive; it produced a multi-sided succession war the moment he died (Chapter 5).

The senior tribal emirs of the Chagatai confederation — particularly the surviving Barlas, Jalayir, and Suldus chiefs — were given high military commands and shares of conquered loot but were carefully prevented from accumulating independent provincial power bases. Timur was careful to rotate emirs between commands, mix tribal contingents in his armies, and intermarry his family with rival emirate clans. The result was a court structure in which loyalty to Timur personally was more important than tribal affiliation. After his death, this carefully managed balance fell apart immediately.

---

The first major external campaign was against **Tokhtamysh** (TOKH-tah-mish), khan of the **Golden Horde** (Mongol Empire Ch 8). The Tokhtamysh war is worth a careful walk-through because it illustrates Timur's method in a particularly clear case.

**Tokhtamysh** was a Genghisid prince of the Jochid line — a direct descendant of Genghis Khan through Genghis's eldest son Jochi, the founder of the Golden Horde dynasty (Mongol Empire Ch 8). In the 1370s, the Golden Horde was going through a chaotic period of civil war and dynastic instability. Tokhtamysh was a contender for the Horde throne but had been driven out of the Jochid territories by his rivals. He fled south to Transoxiana in the late 1370s and asked Timur for protection.

Timur saw an opportunity. By backing Tokhtamysh's claim, he could install a Genghisid client on the Golden Horde throne, put one of the four major post-Mongol successor states in his debt, and gain influence across the entire Russian steppe without having to conquer it directly. He provided Tokhtamysh with troops and supplies. Tokhtamysh used this support to defeat his rivals and seize the Golden Horde throne in 1380, then went on to consolidate his rule by sacking **Moscow** in 1382 (an event the Russian chronicles record with horror) and reasserting the Horde's authority over the Russian principalities for another generation.

The arrangement worked beautifully — for Timur. He had created a Genghisid client without expending much of his own resources, and Tokhtamysh duly acknowledged his gratitude in diplomatic correspondence through the early 1380s.

Then, in the late 1380s, Tokhtamysh turned. The standard scholarly interpretation is that as Tokhtamysh consolidated power he began to see Timur not as patron but as rival — the Golden Horde's traditional sphere of influence included parts of the Caucasus and northeastern Iran that Timur was conquering, and a Genghisid khan ruling the Horde could not credibly accept being subordinate to a non-Genghisid amir. Tokhtamysh raided the northern frontier of Timur's empire in 1387, then launched a major invasion of Transoxiana itself in 1388.

Timur's response was overwhelming. In 1391, he led a Timurid army north into the Russian steppe — one of the most ambitious logistical operations of the medieval world, traversing approximately 1,500 miles (2,400 km) of steppe terrain in a single campaign — and defeated Tokhtamysh at the **Battle of the Kondurcha River** in June 1391, in what is now the Russian region of Samara. Tokhtamysh escaped but lost his army.

Tokhtamysh recovered. Three years later, in 1395, Timur had to do it again. This time he marched even farther — through the Caucasus and up the Volga — and crushed Tokhtamysh again at the **Battle of the Terek River** in April 1395. He then sacked the Golden Horde capital of **Sarai** (sah-RYE), pursued Tokhtamysh's retreating forces deep into the Kipchak steppe, and systematically destroyed the trading cities along the Volga and lower Don. The Golden Horde never recovered. Tokhtamysh fled to Lithuania, was eventually killed in 1406, and the Horde fragmented into smaller successor states (the Crimean Khanate, the Kazan Khanate, the Astrakhan Khanate, the Nogai Horde) over the following century.

The strategic consequences were enormous and largely unintended. The Tokhtamysh wars eliminated the Golden Horde as a coherent political force. This in turn freed the Russian principalities — particularly **Moscow** — from the constant pressure of Horde tribute and military intervention that had constrained Russian state-building for 150 years. The rise of a unified Russian state under the Muscovite princes in the fifteenth century, completed by **Ivan III** in the 1480s when Moscow formally repudiated Mongol overlordship, was substantially enabled by the destruction Timur visited on the Golden Horde in 1391–95. Timur did not intend this — he wanted a docile Genghisid client on the Horde throne, not a permanent power vacuum that allowed Moscow to grow strong — but it is one of his most important historical legacies. He inadvertently created the geopolitical conditions for the rise of Russia.

A useful comparison: this is the same kind of long-tail strategic consequence that the original Mongol invasions had produced. Genghis Khan's destruction of the Khwarazmian Empire (Mongol Empire Ch 3) created the conditions for the rise of the Ilkhanate (Mongol Empire Ch 7). The Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 (Mongol Empire Ch 5) ended the Abbasid caliphate (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1) and created the conditions for the Sufi-network political restructuring that would eventually produce the Safavid Empire (Safavid Persia Ch 1). Imperial conquest in this period regularly produced strategic side effects that lasted for centuries. Timur's wars against the Golden Horde are a textbook example.

By the mid-1390s, Timur had eliminated the Golden Horde as a threat from the north, conquered Persia (the subject of Chapter 3), and was free to turn his attention to the south and west. The 1398 Indian campaign and the 1402 confrontation with the Ottomans — the events that made him a household name in European history — were still ahead. Chapter 4 will cover them.

Cross-civ check: Timur's emergence in the 1370s overlaps with the **Ming dynasty's** consolidation in China under the Hongwu Emperor (founded 1368, the same decade Timur captured Samarkand), the **Aztec ascension to dominance** in the Valley of Mexico (Aztec Empire Ch 4–5), the **Catholic Schism** in Western Europe (the rival popes at Rome and Avignon, 1378–1417), the **rise of Vijayanagara** as the dominant Hindu kingdom of southern India (Vijayanagara was founded in 1336, the same year as Timur's traditional birth date), and the **Mali Empire's** late medieval prosperity in West Africa under successors of Mansa Musa (Mali Empire Ch 5–6). The late fourteenth century is a moment of broad imperial competition across Eurasia, with the post-Mongol world reorganizing into new state systems, and Timur is one of the major reorganizers.

# Chapter 3 — Persia and the Caliphal Heartland

Through the 1380s and 1390s, Timur conducted a systematic conquest of the Iranian plateau, Iraq, and the southern Caucasus — the heartland of the post-Mongol Islamic world that the **Ilkhanate** had ruled until its collapse in 1335 (Mongol Empire Ch 7). The conquest was conducted as a series of multi-year campaigns, punctuated by returns to Samarkand for resupply and reorganization. The result was the destruction of the successor states that had emerged after the Ilkhanate's fall — the **Muzaffarids**, the **Jalayirids**, the **Sarbadars**, and various smaller dynasties — and the absorption of their territories into the Timurid Empire.

It was also the most demographically and economically destructive phase of Timur's career. The agricultural infrastructure of the Iranian plateau and Iraq was devastated by the campaigns; the cities were repeatedly sacked; and the central Islamic world emerged from the Timurid wars in a state of long-term decline that did not begin to reverse until the Safavid revival a century later (Safavid Persia Ch 4).

This chapter is about the Persian campaigns — and particularly about the two sacks of Baghdad, which were the most consequential single events of Timur's career for the long-term history of the Islamic world.

---

A note on the political geography first.

When the **Ilkhanate** collapsed in 1335 (Mongol Empire Ch 7), the Iranian and Iraqi territories it had ruled fragmented into a constellation of regional successor states. The major ones were:

- The **Jalayirids** (ruled from Baghdad and Tabriz), descendants of an Ilkhanid emir who had seized power in Iraq and northwestern Iran. They were the largest of the successor states and held the most strategically important territories.
- The **Muzaffarids** (ruled from Shiraz and Isfahan), a Sunni dynasty that controlled most of central and southern Iran.
- The **Chobanids** in northwestern Iran (a rival faction descended from another Ilkhanid emir, defeated by the Jalayirids in the mid-fourteenth century).
- The **Sarbadars** (ruled from Sabzevar in Khorasan), a small but ideologically distinctive Shia movement that had built a quasi-republican state in eastern Iran.
- The **Karts** of Herat (a vassal dynasty that had held Khorasan under nominal Ilkhanid suzerainty).
- The **Injuids** of Shiraz (briefly, before being absorbed by the Muzaffarids).
- Various smaller local dynasties in the Caucasus, the Caspian provinces, and the Persian Gulf coast.

None of these states was strong enough to resist Timur. They were also unable to coordinate against him. By the 1380s, Persia was a politically fragmented but culturally rich region with no unified defense, ripe for systematic conquest by a determined outside power. Timur was that power.

---

The first phase of the Persian conquest, in 1381–1387, focused on Khorasan and the Caspian provinces — the territories closest to Timur's base in Transoxiana. He took Herat in 1381 from the Kart dynasty (who became Timurid clients), then proceeded west through Mazandaran and Gilan. The Sarbadar state at Sabzevar in eastern Iran resisted briefly and was punished with the first major Timurid massacre in Iran in 1383. The chronicle accounts say 2,000 men were buried alive — though the figure is uncertain — and the city's defensive walls were demolished.

The second phase, in 1386–1388, brought Timur into central and southern Iran. The Muzaffarid dynasty was the main opposition. Timur defeated several Muzaffarid armies, took Isfahan, Shiraz, and most of Fars and central Persia, and incorporated the region into his expanding empire under client rulers.

The **Isfahan massacre of 1387** was the most notorious incident of this phase. Isfahan had surrendered without a fight to Timur's representatives in 1387 and accepted a Timurid garrison. A few weeks later, the city's population revolted against the Timurid tax-collection unit and killed approximately 3,000 of Timur's soldiers in a single night. Timur's response was a general massacre. The chronicle figures give 70,000 dead — modern historians regard the order of magnitude as plausible even if the precise figure is uncertain — and the heads were stacked into a pyramid outside the city walls.

The Isfahan massacre was the model for what became standard Timurid practice. A city that resisted from the start might be massacred and burned. But a city that surrendered, then revolted, would be punished with particular brutality — because the revolt represented a betrayal of the surrender agreement and could not be tolerated as precedent. The signal was: do not surrender if you intend to revolt later. The next time Timurid representatives showed up at your gates, they expected the surrender to be permanent, or they would burn you down twice.

---

The third phase of the Persian campaigns — and the one that mattered most for the long-term history of the Islamic world — was the conquest of Iraq.

**Baghdad** in 1393 was a much-diminished city. The original Mongol sack in 1258 (Mongol Empire Ch 5) had killed the last Abbasid caliph, ended the eastern Abbasid caliphate as a functional institution (Islamic Golden Age Ch 8), and reduced the city from one of the largest in the world to a regional capital. Through the Ilkhanate period and the Jalayirid succession, Baghdad had partially recovered — by the 1390s its population was perhaps 100,000–150,000, far below the million-plus peak of Abbasid times but still substantial — and it remained the cultural and religious center of the eastern Arab Islamic world. The Jalayirid sultan **Ahmad** (AH-mad, ruled 1382–1410) had ruled Baghdad and most of Iraq for over a decade and had built up an effective regional administration.

Timur attacked Baghdad in October 1393. Sultan Ahmad fled west to Mamluk-ruled Syria, abandoning the city. Baghdad surrendered without much resistance and was occupied by Timurid forces. Timur did not order a general massacre at this point — the city had submitted promptly — but he extracted enormous tribute, looted the wealth of the Jalayirid court, and imposed a Timurid garrison.

He also did something politically significant. He installed a member of the **Sayyid line** of the Abbasid family — descendants of the last Abbasid caliphs — as a ceremonial caliph in Baghdad. This was a transparent political theater. The actual eastern Abbasid caliphate had ended in 1258. The Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt had maintained a parallel "shadow caliphate" in Cairo since the 1260s, with their own line of Abbasid puppets, but no one took these caliphal lineages as legitimate religious authorities in the way the original Abbasid caliphs had been. By installing his own Sayyid puppet caliph at Baghdad, Timur was claiming a religious legitimacy parallel to the Mamluk Cairene caliphate — a political move designed to demonstrate that the senior Sunni religious authority in the Arab world had recognized Timurid rule.

This was an Islamic-world version of the same legitimacy game Timur was playing on the steppe with the Chagatai puppet khans (Chapter 1). On the steppe, a Genghisid puppet khan provided steppe legitimacy. In the Arab heartland, an Abbasid puppet caliph provided Sunni religious legitimacy. The arrangements were transparent fictions but they served Timur's political needs.

The Sayyid puppet caliph at Baghdad did not last. After Timur withdrew, the Jalayirid sultan Ahmad returned and the local political arrangements reverted to whatever the situation on the ground produced. But Timur had made his point. He could install religious legitimacy as easily as he could massacre populations. The two practices were complementary parts of the same political project.

---

The second sack of Baghdad, in 1401, was the catastrophic one.

After the 1393 conquest, Sultan Ahmad had returned to Baghdad and reasserted Jalayirid rule under Timurid suzerainty. Through the late 1390s, while Timur was occupied with the Indian campaign (Chapter 4) and the Tokhtamysh wars, Ahmad gradually reasserted independence and began to support enemies of the Timurid Empire. By 1401, with Ahmad in open opposition and Baghdad effectively functioning as an independent city, Timur returned to settle the issue.

The 1401 siege lasted six weeks. The city resisted vigorously, partly because Ahmad's forces had repaired the walls and stockpiled supplies, partly because the population — having survived the 1393 Timurid occupation and seen what it cost — had no illusions about what surrender would mean. The siege ended with a Timurid storming of the city in July 1401.

What followed was the second sack. Timur ordered each of his soldiers to bring a minimum of two human heads. The accounts describe a systematic massacre of the urban population — soldiers, civilians, men, women, children — that lasted for several days. The chronicle figures put the dead at 90,000 (some accounts say 100,000), and the heads were stacked into a pyramid that was, by all surviving descriptions, one of the largest such structures Timur ever built.

The 1401 sack is the moment when Baghdad effectively ceased to be a major city for several centuries. The infrastructure was destroyed. The administrative class was killed. The agricultural hinterland was devastated by the troops requisitioning supplies. Surviving inhabitants fled into the countryside and to neighboring states. The city's population fell to perhaps 20,000–30,000 by the early fifteenth century — a fraction of its pre-Timur level. Baghdad would not recover its political importance until the Ottoman conquest in 1534 incorporated it into a long-running empire that could rebuild the urban infrastructure systematically.

The destruction of Baghdad in 1401 is sometimes treated as a continuation of the 1258 Mongol destruction — and there is a real continuity, in the sense that both events are major demographic blows to the same city — but the comparison can mislead. The 1258 destruction ended an institutional structure (the eastern Abbasid caliphate) but the city itself recovered substantially over the following century. The 1401 destruction occurred at a moment when the city was already weakened and pushed it into a much longer period of marginality. The combination of the two — Mongol sack followed by Timurid sack 143 years later — is what reduced Baghdad from "former imperial capital recovering its position" to "regional town with no political weight" for several centuries.

The deeper consequence was for the Arab Islamic world's intellectual life. Baghdad had been the center of the **Islamic Golden Age** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1–4) — the great translation movement, the Bayt al-Hikma library, the philosophical and scientific establishment that had produced Ibn Sina, al-Razi, al-Khwarizmi, and the foundational works of medieval Islamic intellectual life. The 1258 sack had damaged this; the 1401 sack effectively ended what survived. The center of gravity of Islamic intellectual culture shifted permanently — eastward to Persianate centers (eventually to Timurid Herat itself, then to Safavid Isfahan), westward to Mamluk Cairo, and to Ottoman Istanbul after 1453. The Arab core of the early medieval Islamic world never recovered the cultural primacy it had held in the eighth through twelfth centuries. Some of this would have happened anyway through other historical processes — but the Timurid sacks of Baghdad were a major accelerator.

---

The Persian campaigns also had a structural cultural consequence: the systematic deportation of skilled craftsmen to Samarkand.

After every major sack — Sabzevar, Isfahan, the Iraqi cities, eventually Damascus and Delhi — Timur's standard practice was to spare and deport the population's skilled craftsmen. Weavers, calligraphers, miniature painters, tilemakers, masons, metalworkers, glassmakers, papermakers, bookbinders, mathematicians, astronomers, theologians, scribes — all of these specialists, when identified among the surviving population, were marched to Samarkand to work on Timur's building projects, populate the workshops of his court, and contribute to the cultural infrastructure he was building in his capital.

The arithmetic was simple: by the late 1390s, Samarkand had absorbed the craft elite of half a dozen major Persian and Arab cities. The cultural enrichment of Samarkand was the cultural impoverishment of every other major city Timur had conquered. Tabriz lost much of its miniature-painting establishment. Shiraz lost its calligraphy schools. Damascus lost its weaving guilds. Baghdad lost what was left of its scholarly community after the 1401 massacre. All of these specialists ended up in Samarkand, working on the Timurid building program and training a new generation of Samarkand-based craftsmen.

The cultural florescence of fifteenth-century Herat under Shah Rukh (Chapter 6) and fifteenth-century Samarkand under Ulugh Beg (Chapter 7) — the Bihzad miniature tradition, the great architectural projects, the astronomical observatory — rests on this concentrated craft capacity. The capacity itself was built through forced relocation across thirty years of imperial conquest. There is no clean separation between the cultural achievement and the deportation mechanism that produced it. Both are part of the same Timurid project.

A pause: this is an uncomfortable historical pattern, and it is worth not flinching from. The relationship between imperial violence and cultural flowering is a recurring theme in world history. The agricultural surpluses extracted by the **Maurya Empire** funded **Ashoka's** Buddhist patronage (Maurya Empire Ch 5–6). The wealth taken from sacked cities funded **Roman** monumental architecture (Ancient Rome Ch 3). The forced labor of conquered Mesoamerican peoples funded **Aztec** temple-building (Aztec Empire Ch 5). The slave-trade profits funded the Renaissance European art markets that produced Italian Renaissance painting (more on the Italian Renaissance comparison in Chapter 6). The Timurid case is a particularly direct version of this pattern: the cultural achievement and the violence are not just historically contemporary but mechanistically linked. The deported craftsmen were the cultural achievement, and they were deported because their cities had been destroyed.

You can deplore this pattern. You should deplore this pattern. But you should also see it clearly. Pretending that the Timurid Renaissance happened on its own merits, separately from the campaigns that built it, is a sanitization that prevents you from understanding either part of the story.

---

By 1395, with Persia subjugated, the Golden Horde shattered, and the Caucasus under Timurid control, Timur had built one of the largest empires in Eurasian history. He had also conducted some of the most systematic violence the medieval world had seen. The next phase of his career — the southern and western expeditions to India, Syria, and Anatolia in 1398–1402 — would extend the empire to its maximum reach and produce the moments by which European history would remember him.

Cross-civ check: the Persian campaigns of the 1380s and 1390s overlap with the **late Yuan dynasty** in China (collapsing into the Ming Dynasty in 1368, immediately before Timur's rise), the **Castilian Reconquista** advancing southward against Granada in Spain, the **Lithuanian-Polish union** through the marriage of Jadwiga and Jogaila in 1386 that produced the largest Christian state in eastern Europe, and the **Aztec Triple Alliance** consolidating control of the Valley of Mexico (Aztec Empire Ch 5). Across Eurasia, the late fourteenth century was an era of regional consolidation and imperial competition; Timur's Persian conquest was simultaneous with similar consolidation projects in China, the Iberian peninsula, eastern Europe, and Mexico. The Timurid achievement was distinctive in scale and brutality, but the broader pattern — a single ruler unifying a fragmented region through military conquest — was the dominant political form of the period.

# Chapter 4 — India and Anatolia

Timur's reputation in European history rests almost entirely on two events: the 1398 sack of Delhi, and the 1402 capture of the Ottoman sultan **Bayezid I** (BYE-eh-zeed) at the Battle of Ankara. Both happened in a four-year period at the height of his career, both produced enormous wealth for the Timurid state, and both had consequences that shaped the next century of Eurasian history. They also produced the most lurid material in the Timur mythology — the elephants captured at Delhi, the Ottoman sultan in a wheeled cage at Ankara — and that material has dominated the European literary representation of Timur from Christopher Marlowe in 1587 to the present.

This chapter is about the southern and western campaigns of 1398–1402, what happened, and what they meant.

---

The Indian campaign of 1398 was Timur's most logistically ambitious operation to date. The march from Samarkand to Delhi covers approximately 1,500 miles (2,400 km) one-way, crossing the Hindu Kush mountains in northern Afghanistan (a major mountain range that effectively separates Central Asia from the Indian subcontinent, with passes that climb above 12,000 feet — the same range Babur would cross 130 years later, Chapter 8), the Indus river valley, and the plains of Punjab. Timur conducted the operation in a single campaign cycle — leaving Samarkand in spring 1398, fighting at Delhi in December, and returning to Samarkand by spring 1399 — which required careful seasonal scheduling to avoid the Indian monsoon, the central Asian winter, and the worst of the Hindu Kush snow conditions.

The military justification for the campaign was the standard Timurid framing: the Delhi Sultanate under the **Tughlaq dynasty** had been ruling as Sunni Muslims over a majority Hindu population, and Timur claimed (the chronicle accounts give several variants of the speech) that the Tughlaqs had been insufficiently zealous in promoting Islam. The actual motivation was loot. India was famously rich, the Delhi Sultanate was politically weak, and Timur needed the resources for ongoing campaigns in Persia and the planned western expedition.

The Delhi Sultanate had been the dominant Muslim power in northern India for nearly two centuries (founded 1206, the same year Genghis Khan was proclaimed in Mongolia — there is no causal connection, but the simultaneity is striking). The Tughlaq dynasty (1320–1414) was the third major dynasty of the Sultanate. Under its founder **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** in the early fourteenth century the Sultanate had extended deep into south India, but by the 1380s it had lost most of those territories and was reduced to a much smaller territory in Punjab and the Ganges plain. The reigning Sultan in 1398 was **Nasir-ud-Din Mahmud Tughlaq**, a young and politically weak ruler in the middle of an internal succession dispute. The Tughlaq state was structurally vulnerable.

Timur's army crossed the Indus in September 1398 and advanced through Punjab, sacking towns and gathering loot as they went. The defending forces — provincial governors and local lords commanding fragments of the Tughlaq army — were defeated piecemeal. By early December the Timurid army was outside Delhi.

The **Battle of Delhi**, on December 17, 1398, was a one-day affair. Sultan Mahmud's army, including a large corps of war elephants in elaborate armor, deployed outside the city walls. Timurid cavalry skirmished with the elephant force; Timur reportedly ordered camels loaded with flammable material driven toward the elephants and set on fire, panicking the elephants and breaking the Tughlaq line. The Tughlaq army collapsed, Mahmud fled, and Timur entered Delhi as conqueror.

What followed was not the orderly tribute-extraction Timur had originally planned. The chronicle accounts (including Timur's own apologetic version in the official Timurid history) describe a multi-day general massacre that Timur said he had not ordered — soldiers began plundering individual houses, fighting broke out between Timurid troops and Delhi residents who tried to defend their property, and the violence spiraled out of control. By the time Timur's commanders restored discipline several days later, much of the city had been looted, large sections had been burned, and a significant fraction of the population was dead.

The exact death toll is unrecoverable. Contemporary chronicle estimates range widely; many give six-figure numbers (100,000 or higher) but these should be read as orders of magnitude rather than census counts. What is well-attested is that Delhi was effectively uninhabitable for years afterward — contemporary accounts describe streets choked with corpses, the city's water supply contaminated, surviving residents fleeing to surrounding regions, and the urban infrastructure largely destroyed. The historian **Yahya bin Ahmad Sirhindi**, writing several decades later, said that for two months no birds were heard in Delhi — a literary trope, but one that probably preserves something of the actual desolation.

Timur withdrew from Delhi in early January 1399, marching back to Samarkand with an enormous baggage train of looted wealth. He also took with him a substantial corps of Indian skilled craftsmen — particularly stonemasons, who were destined to work on the great congregational mosque he was building at Samarkand (the Bibi-Khanym Mosque, completed 1404). He took the surviving Hindu war elephants, which became part of his subsequent campaigns and were used to dramatic effect at Ankara in 1402. The total loot — gold, silver, textiles, jewels — was enormous, sufficient to fund Timurid government operations for years.

---

The political consequences of the Delhi sack ran longer than Timur intended.

The Tughlaq dynasty did not formally end in 1398 — Sultan Mahmud returned to Delhi after Timur's departure and nominal Tughlaq sultans continued in office for another sixteen years — but real Tughlaq political authority was destroyed. The provincial governors of the former Sultanate effectively went their own ways, the central administration collapsed, and the dynasty became a ceremonial fiction maintained by whichever provincial lord happened to control Delhi at the moment. The Tughlaqs were formally replaced in 1414 by the **Sayyid dynasty** (a confusing name, since this Sayyid dynasty has no connection to the Sayyid puppet caliph Timur installed at Baghdad — different region, different family, same Arabic word meaning "descendant of the Prophet"), which was followed in 1451 by the **Lodi dynasty**, which was finally swept away by Babur at Panipat in 1526 (Chapter 8). The 128-year arc from Timur's sack to Babur's victory is one continuous story — the gradual hollowing-out of a once-great Sultanate that had been mortally wounded by the Timurid invasion and never recovered.

Timur's grandson **Pir Muhammad** (peer moh-HAH-mad) was installed as Timurid governor of Punjab and parts of India in 1399, but his position was weak and the Timurid presence in India faded within a few years. The 1398 expedition was therefore a raid, not a conquest. The wealth was extracted, the Delhi Sultanate was crippled, and then the Timurids withdrew.

The Mughal claim to Indian sovereignty 128 years later under Babur (Chapter 8) ran partly through this 1398 precedent. Babur, a direct descendant of Timur, framed his 1526 invasion as the recovery of territories that his ancestor had legitimately conquered — a flimsy legal argument but one that mattered in early-modern Islamic political thought. The 1398 sack and the 1526 conquest are part of the same dynastic continuity.

---

After the Indian campaign, Timur returned to Samarkand for a year of consolidation, then turned west for what would become his last major external campaign. The objective was the eastern Mediterranean — specifically the **Mamluk Sultanate** of Egypt (which controlled Syria from Damascus) and the **Ottoman Empire** (which controlled Anatolia from its capital at Bursa). Both were major Islamic powers. Both had refused to acknowledge Timurid suzerainty. Both had to be punished.

The Syrian campaign came first. Timur invaded Mamluk Syria in 1400, defeated a Mamluk army outside Aleppo, and stormed the city. Aleppo had been a major commercial and intellectual center for centuries — a key node on the long-distance trade routes connecting the Mediterranean to Mesopotamia and Iran — and the sack devastated its commercial infrastructure. The chronicle accounts describe the standard Timurid massacre and skull-pyramid, with the additional detail that the great congregational mosque was used as a stable for Timurid horses (a religious provocation Timur would not have done at a Sunni mosque in his own territory but apparently considered acceptable in a city he had just conquered).

The army then pushed south to **Damascus**, the great Mamluk regional capital. Damascus was besieged through the winter of 1400–1401 and surrendered in March 1401 after the Mamluk garrison's counterattacks failed and supplies inside the walls ran out. Timur extracted enormous tribute, demanded the deportation of the city's craftsmen — Damascene weavers, metalworkers, and tilemakers, all famous across the medieval Islamic world for their skills — to Samarkand, and ordered the sack to proceed. The chronicle accounts describe widespread destruction; the Great Mosque of Damascus (the Umayyad Mosque, one of the oldest and most architecturally important mosques in the Islamic world) suffered fire damage during the sack, though it was restored afterward.

It was during these surrender negotiations that Timur met **Ibn Khaldun** (ib-n hahl-DOON, 1332–1406), the Tunisian historian and political theorist who was at that moment serving as a senior judicial official of the Mamluk regime in Damascus. Ibn Khaldun is one of the most important medieval historical theorists; his **Muqaddimah** ("Introduction" — a long preface to his world history that has been read as a foundational text of historical sociology) had been completed in 1377, and he was already a famous intellectual in his own time. He met Timur multiple times in early 1401 and recorded his impressions in his autobiography. The meetings are one of the best primary-source descriptions of Timur we have.

Ibn Khaldun was struck by Timur's intelligence. The chronicle accounts of Timur sometimes give the impression of a brutal warlord with limited political subtlety; Ibn Khaldun's first-hand description gives a different picture. Timur was, according to Ibn Khaldun, sharp, well-informed, intellectually curious, knowledgeable about the political situations of distant regions, and capable of conducting sustained intellectual conversation about questions of history and dynastic succession. He asked Ibn Khaldun about the political geography of North Africa (which Ibn Khaldun knew well), about the dynastic history of the Mamluks, and about Ibn Khaldun's own theoretical writings. He was, in Ibn Khaldun's account, a recognizably sophisticated political mind — albeit one operating in service of an unflinching willingness to use mass violence.

This is worth flagging because the Marlovian image of Timur as pure brutish conqueror is misleading. Timur was an intellectual operator as well as a soldier. He maintained an elaborate court historian establishment (Yazdi's Zafarnama and other official histories were written under direct Timurid patronage). He made political decisions on the basis of careful analysis of opponents and circumstances. The brutality was a chosen instrument of policy, not an absence of policy. Ibn Khaldun saw this clearly. Modern historians who have read Ibn Khaldun's account carefully tend to agree.

After the Damascus sack, Timur withdrew north — back through Aleppo, into the Anatolian frontier zone, and toward his next objective: the Ottoman Empire.

---

The **Ottoman Empire** in 1400 was a rising power. Founded around 1300 in northwestern Anatolia by **Osman I** (Ottoman Empire Ch 1), the Ottoman dynasty had spent the fourteenth century expanding through the Balkans and absorbing other Anatolian principalities. By the late 1390s, under Sultan **Bayezid I** ("the Thunderbolt," ruled 1389–1402), the Ottomans had:

- Defeated a major Crusader army at the Battle of Nicopolis in 1396, halting Western European efforts to roll back Ottoman expansion in the Balkans.
- Conquered most of Anatolia from the rival Turkish principalities, unifying the peninsula under Ottoman rule for the first time.
- Begun a long blockade of **Constantinople** with the apparent intention of taking the Byzantine capital (which would in fact fall to Bayezid's great-grandson Mehmed II in 1453, Byzantine Empire Ch 12 and Ottoman Empire Ch 4).
- Established a formidable military system based on the **Janissary** corps (Ottoman Empire Ch 3) — Christian boys from the Balkans, recruited as slaves, raised as Muslims, and trained as elite infantry.

Bayezid was widely considered the most successful Sunni Muslim ruler of his generation. He was also, like Timur, an outsider ruler trying to legitimize a non-traditional dynasty — the Ottomans had no Genghisid lineage, no caliphal connection, no Abbasid sanction, only their military success and their growing reputation as the Islamic world's frontier defenders against Christendom.

The collision between Timur and Bayezid had been building for years. Timurid expansion westward was bringing Timur's territories into direct contact with Ottoman territories in eastern Anatolia. Several of the Anatolian Turkish princes the Ottomans had conquered had appealed to Timur for support against Bayezid; some Ottoman vassals in eastern Anatolia were defecting to Timur. Diplomatic correspondence between the two rulers had grown increasingly hostile. Bayezid's letters reportedly addressed Timur with insulting forms of address; Timur's responses warned of consequences.

By the spring of 1402, Timur's army was in eastern Anatolia, and Bayezid was assembling his forces to meet it.

---

The **Battle of Ankara** was fought on July 28, 1402, on a plain east of the Anatolian city of Ankara (then a regional center of moderate size, now the capital of modern Turkey). The military details:

Timur's army numbered perhaps 140,000 to 200,000 (the figures are disputed) and included substantial cavalry forces, elephants from the Indian campaign, and a heterogeneous mix of Turco-Mongol contingents, Persian troops, and conscripts from conquered territories. Bayezid's army was somewhat smaller, perhaps 85,000 to 120,000, but included the elite Janissary infantry, Ottoman feudal cavalry (the *sipahi*), Anatolian Turkish vassal contingents, and Serbian heavy cavalry under the Serbian prince **Stefan Lazarević** (a vassal of the Ottomans by treaty after Nicopolis).

The Anatolian Turkish vassal contingents were a structural weakness. Many of these troops had been incorporated into the Ottoman army less than a decade earlier, after the Ottoman conquest of their home principalities, and several of their commanders had been in clandestine correspondence with Timur. When the battle opened, the Anatolian contingents on the Ottoman left wing defected to the Timurid side and turned on their former comrades. The Ottoman left wing collapsed.

The center held longer. Bayezid's Janissaries fought the Timurid center to a standstill through most of the day, and the Serbian heavy cavalry on the Ottoman right wing actually pushed Timur's left back. But the collapse of the Anatolian left wing allowed Timurid cavalry to swing around behind the Ottoman center, and by late afternoon the Ottoman army was being attacked from front and rear. Bayezid attempted to break out with his bodyguard but was surrounded and captured.

Sultan Bayezid I was taken alive — the first time a major Ottoman sultan had been captured in battle, and the first time any major Sunni Muslim sovereign had been taken prisoner in this manner. Several of his sons were killed in the battle; others escaped. The Ottoman army was destroyed.

---

The captivity of Bayezid is one of the famous episodes of medieval history, and the chronicle accounts of it are colorful enough that some sorting through is needed.

The widely-known story — that Bayezid was kept in a wheeled cage and humiliated at Timur's banquets, with his wife forced to serve naked at the feasts — has a mixed historical pedigree. The cage story originates in late Persian and Ottoman chronicle accounts and is repeated in the European literature; modern Ottoman historians have generally treated it as embellished or anachronistic. The contemporary accounts that survive (including the Timurid official sources) describe Bayezid as held under house-arrest conditions, traveling with Timur's court but not in a cage, and treated with the standard ceremonial respect due a captured but defeated sovereign. He was given quarters appropriate to his rank, attended by his own retainers, and consulted on matters of policy. Timur is reported to have lectured him about his political mistakes.

What is well-documented: Bayezid's psychological state deteriorated through the months of captivity, his health declined, and he died on **March 8, 1403**, eight months after the battle, while still in Timurid custody. The cause of death is variously reported as illness, an apoplectic stroke, or — in some accounts — suicide by self-administered poison. He was buried in the Ottoman capital at Bursa under a tomb that survives.

The version of the captivity story Christopher Marlowe used in *Tamburlaine the Great* (1587) — Bayezid in an iron cage, used as Timur's footstool when mounting his horse, finally smashing his head against the bars in despair — is dramatic but not historical. It draws on the Italian and Latin sources transmitted through Renaissance Europe, themselves drawing on the embellished Persian-Ottoman chronicle tradition. Marlowe's play established Timur's image in European cultural memory as the cruel oriental conqueror, and the cage in particular became one of the signature images of European Orientalism. Modern English-language general readers' knowledge of Timur often runs back through Marlowe rather than directly to the chronicle sources, and the result is an unreliable composite picture of the actual Bayezid captivity.

What does matter, historically, is the political consequences.

---

The political consequences of Ankara ran for half a century.

Bayezid's death triggered the **Ottoman Interregnum** of 1402–1413, during which Bayezid's surviving sons fought a multi-sided civil war for the Ottoman throne. **Süleyman Çelebi**, **İsa Çelebi**, **Musa Çelebi**, and **Mehmed Çelebi** each held parts of the Ottoman territories at various times, with Timurid forces initially backing different claimants in the early years and then withdrawing as Timur died and the Timurid succession crisis (Chapter 5) consumed Timurid attention. **Mehmed Çelebi** finally defeated the last of his brothers in 1413 and was acknowledged as Sultan **Mehmed I**, reconstituting a unified Ottoman state.

The Interregnum cost the Ottomans roughly half a century of recovery time. The institutional structures of the empire had to be rebuilt. The Anatolian Turkish principalities the Ottomans had conquered before 1402 had to be re-conquered (the process was completed only under Murad II in the 1420s and 1430s). The Balkan vassals had to be re-subjected. The blockade of Constantinople was abandoned; the Byzantine capital, which had been on the brink of falling to Bayezid in 1402, survived for another 51 years until **Mehmed II** finally took it in 1453 (Byzantine Empire Ch 12, Ottoman Empire Ch 4).

It is hard to overstate the consequence of this delay. If Bayezid had not been defeated at Ankara, the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople might have happened in the 1400s rather than the 1450s — possibly under Bayezid himself, conceivably with quite different international consequences. The Byzantine refugees who fled west after 1453 carrying Greek manuscripts to Italy, where they fed into the Italian Renaissance's recovery of classical texts (Byzantine Empire Ch 12 details this), would have arrived half a century earlier. The Ottoman wars of the late fifteenth century against Venice, the Mamluks, and various Balkan states would have looked very different. The relationship between the early-modern Mediterranean and the early-modern Islamic world might have developed on a different trajectory altogether.

The Battle of Ankara is therefore one of those tactical events whose strategic consequences ramify far beyond the immediate participants. Timur won a battle in central Anatolia in July 1402 and inadvertently shaped the political geography of the eastern Mediterranean for the next century. The fall of Constantinople in 1453, the survival of the Byzantine remnants until that date, the Ottoman recovery and renewed expansion under Mehmed II, the eventual Safavid-Ottoman confrontation that began at Chaldiran in 1514 (Safavid Persia Ch 3) — all of this was conditioned by what Timur did at Ankara.

A useful comparison: the Ottoman Empire in 1402 was at roughly the same stage of development that the Safavid Empire would reach a century later under **Ismail I** (Safavid Persia Ch 1–2). Both were Sunni-Sufi-derived imperial projects (the Ottomans drawing on the Babai dervish networks of fourteenth-century Anatolia, the Safavids on the Safaviyya order at Ardabil). Both used cavalry-based armies built around tribal-confederation contingents. Both faced the structural problem of building professional standing armies that could replace the founding tribal levies. The Ottomans had gotten further with this — the Janissary corps Bayezid commanded at Ankara was already the most formally institutionalized standing army in the Islamic world, and would become the model that the Safavid Abbas the Great explicitly imitated when building his ghulam corps a century later (Safavid Persia Ch 4). The Ottoman defeat at Ankara was therefore a defeat of one of the most institutionally advanced military systems of the period — by an army that was, in its core, still a Mongol-style cavalry confederation. It was the last great victory of the Mongol military tradition over the early forms of the gunpowder-age standing army.

---

By the end of 1402, Timur had captured the Ottoman sultan, sacked Damascus, ended Tughlaq Delhi, conquered Persia, broken the Golden Horde, and built one of the largest empires in human history. He was sixty-six years old. His next project was Ming China.

Cross-civ check: the years 1398–1402 are extraordinarily dense with major events across Eurasia. The Chinese **Yongle Emperor** seized the Ming throne in a civil war against his nephew (1399–1402, exactly contemporary with Timur's western expedition); the **Aztec Triple Alliance** was completing its conquest of the Valley of Mexico under **Acamapichtli's** successors (Aztec Empire Ch 5); the **Hundred Years' War** in Europe was in a quiet phase between major campaigns, with **Henry IV** consolidating power in England and the French monarchy increasingly dysfunctional under **Charles VI**'s madness; the **Joseon dynasty** in Korea, founded in 1392, was in the early consolidation phase under **Taejong** (Joseon Korea Ch 1); the **Vijayanagara Empire** in southern India was at peak power under **Bukka I** and his successors. These are years that pack four or five major historical inflection points into a single short span, and Timur's campaigns intersect with several of them.

# Chapter 5 — The Death and the Will

In late 1404, Timur was sixty-eight years old. He had spent thirty-five years in continuous warfare. He had built one of the largest empires in human history and conducted some of the most violent campaigns in medieval history. By any reasonable measure he should have settled in Samarkand, enjoyed the wealth he had accumulated, and let his sons and grandsons run the empire. Instead, he assembled the largest army of his career and prepared to invade Ming China.

He did not get there. He died at Otrar in February 1405, before the campaign could properly begin. The succession that followed lasted four years, killed several of his grandsons, fragmented the empire he had assembled, and ended with his fourth son **Shah Rukh** (shah ROOK) emerging as the consolidator from his governorship of Khorasan. The reduced Timurid Empire that Shah Rukh would govern — Khorasan, Transoxiana, parts of Persia — was much smaller than the empire Timur had accumulated. But it was the empire that produced the cultural florescence that defined Timurid civilization and that the next three chapters cover.

This chapter is about the China campaign that did not happen, the death at Otrar, and the succession war that reshaped the Timurid Empire.

---

The China campaign needs some explaining. By 1404, why would Timur — already past sixty-five, with an enormous accumulated wealth and territorial reach — set out on a logistically extreme expedition into the largest and richest state in the world?

The political framing was religious. **Ming China** under the **Yongle Emperor** (yong-LUH, ruled 1402–1424) was, in Timur's framing, a Buddhist-Confucian state ruling a population that included substantial Muslim minorities (particularly in the western frontier provinces of Gansu and Yunnan). Timur claimed his China expedition was a holy war (a **jihad**) to convert China to Islam. The framing is not quite as preposterous as it sounds — there was a real Muslim community in China dating back to Tang dynasty contacts (Tang-Song China Ch 4), the Ming had inherited substantial Muslim populations from the **Yuan dynasty** (Mongol Empire Ch 6), and a Timurid invasion could plausibly have linked up with internal Muslim populations in the western Chinese frontier zones. But the framing was also clearly a public justification rather than the actual motivation. The actual motivations were probably some combination of: continuing the cycle of campaigns that had defined Timur's career and that his army required; the enormous wealth of Ming China, which would dwarf even the Indian loot; the political logic of being seen to outdo Genghis Khan, whose Yuan dynasty descendants had ruled China before being driven out by the Ming founder in 1368; and, possibly, a specific provocation from the Ming court.

The provocation, if there was one, ran back to a 1395 diplomatic incident. The **Hongwu Emperor**, the Ming founder, had sent an embassy to Samarkand that interpreted Timur's response as a tributary submission to Ming overlordship — a misreading that may have been deliberate diplomatic insult, or may have been a misunderstanding of Timur's chancery conventions, but that Timur held against the Chinese state for the next decade. He held the embassy at Samarkand for years (the chronicle accounts say various of the ambassadors died in Timurid custody) and eventually sent counter-letters framing himself as the senior partner. By the early 1400s, in Timurid diplomatic framing, China had insulted Timur and the punitive expedition was overdue.

The military preparations were enormous. Timur assembled an army at Samarkand in late 1404 — the chronicle figures give 200,000 men, though as with all medieval army-size figures this should be treated as an order of magnitude rather than a precise count — supplied with food and equipment for an extended winter march through the Tian Shan and Tarim Basin region. The route was the classic Silk Road northern route: Samarkand to Tashkent, then Otrar on the Syr Darya, then up into the Dzhungarian basin and across to the Ming frontier. The march would take many months. The Ming court, well-informed of the preparations through their own diplomatic network and through merchants traveling the trade routes, was preparing defenses on the Gansu frontier — the **Yongle Emperor**, ruling China from his new capital at Beijing, ordered frontier garrisons reinforced and the relevant sections of the **Great Wall** strengthened through 1404.

Timur left Samarkand in December 1404 — late in the season, against the advice of his commanders, into a Central Asian winter. He reached Otrar (in modern Kazakhstan, on the Syr Darya river) in late January 1405. The army camped at Otrar to wait out the worst of the winter conditions before pushing east into the Tian Shan.

Timur died at Otrar on **February 17 or 18, 1405**. The chronicle accounts describe a sudden onset of fever, several days of illness, and death in his tent surrounded by his commanders. Modern readings of the symptoms (fever, weakness, eventual death within days) are consistent with pneumonia, influenza, or a similar respiratory infection — exactly the kind of illness an elderly man would be vulnerable to in winter conditions in a freezing Central Asian camp. He was sixty-eight or sixty-nine years old.

---

The Yongle Emperor's defensive preparations had been unnecessary. The largest planned military operation of the fifteenth century did not happen.

Timur's body was preserved (the chronicle accounts say it was embalmed by Egyptian techniques learned from craftsmen taken at Damascus) and transported back to Samarkand for burial. He was interred in the **Gur-e-Amir** mausoleum, which his grandson **Muhammad Sultan** had begun building in 1403 — a domed tomb chamber in the Persianate-Timurid architectural tradition, with a high drum, a melon-ribbed turquoise dome on a square chamber, and elaborate banna'i tilework. The completed mausoleum (finished under Shah Rukh by about 1410) became the architectural prototype for the great Persianate domed tombs of the next two centuries — including, most famously, the **Taj Mahal** (1648) at Agra, built by Timur's descendant Shah Jahan more than two centuries later. The Taj Mahal's silhouette is recognizably the Gur-e-Amir's silhouette, scaled up and elaborated. The Mughal mausoleum tradition that produced the Taj started here, in the tomb of the man whose 1398 sack of Delhi the Mughals would later struggle to live down.

The Soviet exhumation of 1941 (Chapter 1) confirmed the identity of the remains and the chronicle descriptions of the lameness and arm injuries. The skeleton showed signs of the active life Timur had led — old wounds, healed fractures, calluses from horseback riding — and confirmed that he had died around the age the chronicles gave. The forensic facial reconstruction Mikhail Gerasimov produced is the standard modern image of Timur, used in textbooks and museum displays from Tashkent to London to Tehran.

---

The succession war began immediately.

Timur's surviving sons in 1405 were:

- **Miran Shah** (mee-RAHN shah, ruled portions of northwestern Iran), Timur's third son, then in his late forties. He had a reputation for instability and possibly mental illness, which had cost him several governorships during Timur's lifetime, but he commanded a significant army and held strategic territory in Azerbaijan.
- **Shah Rukh** (shah ROOK, ruled Khorasan from Herat), Timur's fourth son, then about twenty-eight. He had been a competent governor of Khorasan since the 1390s and had not been involved in any of the more contentious court politics. He was widely respected but not a flashy military leader.

Timur's surviving grandsons in line for parts of the empire included:

- **Khalil Sultan** (kha-LEEL sool-TAHN), son of Miran Shah, then about twenty-one. He happened to be at Samarkand when the news of Timur's death reached the city.
- **Pir Muhammad** (peer moh-HAH-mad), son of Timur's eldest son Jahangir (who had died in 1376), then about thirty. Timur had formally designated him as heir.
- **Muhammad Sultan**, the grandson who had been building the Gur-e-Amir, had died of illness in 1403 — a major blow to the succession because Timur had also favored him for the throne.
- Several other grandsons of varying ages, governing minor provinces.

Timur had nominally designated Pir Muhammad as his successor and made it formal at a kurultai in 1404 before leaving for the China campaign. But the designation was contested. **Khalil Sultan**, taking advantage of being on the spot at Samarkand, simply seized the Timurid capital, the imperial treasury, and Timur's body (when the funeral cortège arrived). He then declared himself sovereign over the central Timurid territories. Pir Muhammad, in southern Persia, refused to recognize Khalil Sultan and began organizing forces to march on Samarkand. Miran Shah, in Azerbaijan, did the same.

Shah Rukh, in Khorasan, did not move immediately. He maintained his governorship at Herat, did not contest the succession openly, and waited.

For the first four years after Timur's death — 1405 to 1409 — the empire fragmented into competing claimant zones. Khalil Sultan held Samarkand and parts of Transoxiana but his rule was profligate and ineffective. He spent the imperial treasury lavishly on his Persian wife **Shad al-Mulk** and her relatives, alienating the senior Timurid emirs whose loyalty he needed. The senior emirs began deserting to other claimants. Pir Muhammad, in southern Persia, was assassinated by his own vizier in 1407 — eliminating one major contender. Miran Shah was killed in battle in 1408 fighting the **Kara Qoyunlu** Turkmen of western Iran (an early sign that Timurid control over Persia was unraveling). The western and southern parts of the empire — Mesopotamia, western Iran, Anatolia, the Caucasus — fell out of Timurid control entirely, absorbed by the Aq Qoyunlu and Kara Qoyunlu Turkmen confederations.

By early 1409, with most of the contenders dead and the empire fragmenting, Shah Rukh finally moved. He marched from Herat with an army of Khorasani troops, advanced on Samarkand, defeated Khalil Sultan's forces, and captured Khalil Sultan himself. (Shah Rukh did not execute his nephew — a notable display of moderation by Timurid standards. Khalil Sultan was sent to Ray as a provincial governor, where he died of illness in 1411.) Shah Rukh consolidated control of Transoxiana, Khorasan, and parts of central Persia by the end of 1409. He installed his son **Ulugh Beg** as governor of Samarkand. He himself returned to Herat, which would remain the Timurid capital for the rest of his reign.

The reduced Timurid Empire that emerged from the four-year succession war was much smaller than the empire Timur had assembled. Mesopotamia, western Persia, the Caucasus, and Anatolia were lost. India was lost. The territories Shah Rukh actually controlled were essentially Khorasan (modern eastern Iran and western Afghanistan), Transoxiana (modern Uzbekistan), and parts of central Persia. This was still a substantial empire — roughly the same area as the original Samanid state of the tenth century, or the eastern half of the old Sasanian Empire (Persian Empire Ch 9) — but it was a fraction of what Timur had ruled at his peak. The empire had been right-sized by the violence of its own succession.

---

The chapter could end here, but it is worth pausing on what Shah Rukh's strategic decision to consolidate rather than expand actually meant.

Timur had spent his career in continuous campaigning. The Timurid army was structured around the expectation of regular campaigns — tribal contingents fighting for shares of loot, senior emirs commanding expeditions in exchange for political loyalty, the entire patronage system depending on a constant flow of conquered wealth. A Timurid ruler who stopped campaigning would have to find a different way to keep the army loyal and the political coalition intact.

Shah Rukh chose to do exactly this. He campaigned occasionally — there were Khorasani campaigns against the Kara Qoyunlu in the 1420s, and he conducted military operations on his eastern frontier — but he made no serious attempt to recover the territories his father had lost. Instead, he reorganized the imperial finances, restored agricultural production in the territories he controlled (some of which had been devastated by Timur's earlier campaigns and were only now recovering), and channeled state resources into administrative reform and cultural patronage rather than into expansion.

This was a fundamental shift in the character of the Timurid project. Under Timur, the empire was a war-making machine that produced extraordinary cultural side effects through deportation of craftsmen. Under Shah Rukh, the empire became a Persianate state in the older medieval Islamic tradition — administered through bureaucratic structures, taxed through agricultural revenues, and oriented toward the patronage of arts, scholarship, and architecture as ends in themselves. The cultural florescence at Herat that Chapter 6 describes was the direct product of this reorientation. It was possible only because Shah Rukh had decided the Timurid Empire was no longer going to be primarily a conquest state.

The decision was politically risky. The senior Timurid emirs — and many of the second-tier military commanders — had been raised in the campaign culture and resisted the shift. There were several rebellions during Shah Rukh's reign, including by some of his own sons (Miran Shah's son **Iskandar Sultan** rebelled in Fars in 1414 and had to be put down by force). But the shift held. By the 1420s the Timurid Empire under Shah Rukh was operating as a settled imperial state in the older Persianate-Islamic tradition, with Herat as a flourishing cultural capital and a stable agricultural-tax economy. The Timurid Renaissance was beginning.

A useful comparison: this is the same kind of generational transition from "founding warrior dynasty" to "settled administrative state" that other post-Mongol successor regimes had to navigate. The **Yuan dynasty** in China (Mongol Empire Ch 6) had to make a similar shift under Kublai Khan and his successors, with mixed results. The **Ilkhanate** in Iran (Mongol Empire Ch 7) made the shift under Ghazan Khan in the 1290s, more successfully. The **Golden Horde** (Mongol Empire Ch 8) made the shift under successive khans through the fourteenth century, less successfully. The Timurid Empire's version of this transition — under Shah Rukh, in 1409–1447 — was probably the most successful of the Mongol successor transitions in cultural terms, even though the political base was the smallest of the four. Shah Rukh's deliberate de-prioritization of military expansion in favor of administrative consolidation produced Herat. It also produced the structural weakness that would let the Uzbeks dismember the empire a century later (Chapter 8).

Cross-civ check: the Timurid succession crisis of 1405–1409 overlaps with **Henry V's** accession in England (1413, just after Mehmed I's reunification of the Ottoman Empire), the **Council of Constance** that ended the Catholic Schism in 1417, the early **Hussite Wars** in Bohemia (after 1419), and the founding of the **Korean Joseon dynasty's** new capital at Hanseong (modern Seoul) in 1392–1395. The early fifteenth century is a moment when several major powers were going through generational transitions — and the Timurid case is one of the more dramatic, with the largest medieval empire of its day right-sizing itself through internal violence.

---

# Chapter 6 — Shah Rukh and the Herat Renaissance

For the next four decades after his consolidation in 1409, **Shah Rukh** (shah ROOK, ruled effectively 1409–1447) governed the reduced Timurid Empire from his capital at **Herat** (HEH-rat) in northwestern Afghanistan. Under his patronage, and the patronage of his remarkable wife **Gawhar Shad** (gow-HAR shahd) and several of his sons, Herat became one of the most important cultural centers of the early-modern Islamic world. The miniature painting tradition that emerged at fifteenth-century Herat is the direct ancestor of all subsequent Persianate book art — Safavid, Mughal, and later. The architectural style developed at Herat is the direct ancestor of Mughal architecture, including the Taj Mahal. The Persian poetic tradition that culminated at Herat in the work of **Jami** (JAH-mee) is the closing chapter of classical Persian poetry. The astronomical and mathematical work conducted at the parallel capital of **Samarkand** under Shah Rukh's son **Ulugh Beg** (oo-LOOG BEG, the subject of Chapter 7) was the most advanced pre-telescopic astronomy in the world.

This chapter is about the Herat Renaissance — what it was, who built it, and what made it possible.

---

The shift from Samarkand to Herat as the political and cultural center of the Timurid Empire is worth pausing on, because it represents the decisive turn from the empire's Mongol-Turkic founding identity toward its Persianate cultural identity.

**Samarkand** had been Timur's capital. It was a Transoxianan city — north of the Amu Darya river, in the heartland of the steppe-adjacent agricultural zone that the Mongol successor states had ruled. Timur had built the great Bibi-Khanym Mosque there, the Gur-e-Amir mausoleum, the Aq Saray palace at his nearby birthplace of Shahrisabz, and the array of formal gardens (the **chahar bagh** quadripartite garden tradition) that surrounded the city. Samarkand was the political center of the empire and the architectural showcase of the dynasty.

**Herat** was different. It was a Khorasani city — south of the Amu Darya, on the western edge of what had been the **Ghaznavid** and **Ghurid** empires of the eleventh and twelfth centuries, and a major center of Persian-language high culture for centuries. The Sufi order networks at Herat ran back through the Naqshbandi (Chapter 8) to the spiritual masters of the eleventh century. The city had been an important regional capital under the **Ilkhanate** (Mongol Empire Ch 7) and the **Karts** (the dynasty Timur had displaced in the 1380s). It had not been Timur's capital but it had been governed by Shah Rukh as a senior provincial center for two decades before the succession crisis. When Shah Rukh emerged as the consolidator after 1409, he kept Herat as his capital and let Samarkand become a secondary center under his son Ulugh Beg.

The choice was deliberate. Herat was further from the steppe — culturally, geographically, and politically. It was deeper in the Persianate world. It was less subject to the tribal pressures from Transoxiana that had produced the political instability of the late fourteenth century. It was also Shah Rukh's own established power base, where he had spent his entire adult life as governor and where his court establishment was based. Moving the capital there was a way of de-prioritizing the Mongol-Turkic dimension of the empire and prioritizing the Persianate dimension.

This was a structural choice with consequences. The Timurid Empire under Timur had been a hybrid — Mongol-Turkic political theater (puppet khans, kurultai assemblies, steppe tribal armies) overlaid on Persianate administration. Under Shah Rukh, the Persianate side won. The puppet khans were quietly dropped (Shah Rukh did not consistently use them). The chancery shifted toward Persian as the primary administrative language. The court ceremony took on Persianate forms drawn from the Ilkhanid and earlier traditions. The patronage of arts, scholarship, and architecture was conducted on the model of older Persian-Islamic dynasties — Samanid, Ghaznavid, Seljuk — rather than in the Turco-Mongol style Timur had favored.

This was the same shift the **Ilkhanate** had made under Ghazan Khan in the 1290s (Mongol Empire Ch 7) — a Mongol successor state Persianizing into a recognizably Iranian dynasty. It was the shift the **Yuan dynasty** in China had partially made under Kublai Khan and his successors (Mongol Empire Ch 6) — Mongols ruling in increasingly Sinified style. The Timurid version of this shift, under Shah Rukh, was particularly successful in cultural terms. It is what made the Herat Renaissance possible.

---

**Gawhar Shad** is the figure who deserves to be introduced first, because she is one of the most important early-modern patrons of architecture in the Islamic world, and because the conventional histories of the period have systematically underplayed her role.

Gawhar Shad ("Joyous Jewel," c. 1379–1457) was Shah Rukh's principal wife — they had married in 1388 when she was about nine and he was about ten, in a politically arranged marriage between two senior Timurid families. As an adult, Gawhar Shad acquired control of substantial financial resources independent of Shah Rukh: she held lands in Khorasan in her own name, governed the city of **Mashhad** (mahsh-HAD, in northeastern Iran — the location of the great shrine of **Imam Reza**, the eighth Shia Imam), commanded her own household troops, and made independent diplomatic and political decisions that the chronicles record. She also dominated court politics in ways that no other Timurid woman had done — installing her sons in key positions, intervening in disputes between Shah Rukh and his other relatives, and conducting her own correspondence with Ming Chinese officials and other foreign rulers.

Her great accomplishment was architectural patronage. The two major projects she funded and personally directed are:

The **Gawhar Shad Mosque** at **Mashhad**, completed in 1418, attached to the great shrine of Imam Reza. The mosque is one of the masterpieces of Timurid architecture — a four-iwan plan (a central courtyard with a vaulted recess on each side, the standard form of monumental Persian mosques developed under the Seljuks), a soaring blue-tiled dome, and elaborate brick mosaic tilework throughout. The inscription frieze around the qibla iwan, in Thuluth script, is attributed to Gawhar Shad's son **Baysunghur** (bye-SOON-ghur), regarded as one of the finest calligraphers of the Timurid era. The mosque survives largely intact and is still in use as a working mosque today, the most important Shia pilgrimage destination in Iran outside the Iraqi shrine cities.

The **Gawhar Shad Musalla** at **Herat**, a much larger religious complex begun in 1417 and completed around 1437. The complex included a mosque, a madrasa (religious school), Gawhar Shad's own intended mausoleum, and a series of monumental minarets. It was, by all surviving descriptions and surviving fragments, the most ambitious single architectural project of the Timurid period — larger than the Bibi-Khanym Mosque at Samarkand, more elaborately decorated, more architecturally innovative. Most of the complex was deliberately demolished in 1885 by British and Russian advisors of the Afghan Amir Abdur Rahman, who decided to clear sight lines for the city's defenses against a feared Russian invasion (the **Panjdeh Incident** of 1885 had nearly produced a war between Britain and Russia in Central Asia, and the Afghan defenses around Herat were being upgraded). The destruction was one of the great architectural losses of the modern period — twentieth-century archaeologists have called it comparable to deliberate destruction at Palmyra. Only Gawhar Shad's own mausoleum and a few minarets survive at the site today.

Gawhar Shad's career did not end well. She survived Shah Rukh by ten years and continued to dominate Timurid court politics through the succession wars after his death. She was finally executed in 1457 by her great-grandson **Sultan Abu Sa'id** (ah-boo sah-EED), then around forty-six, who saw her continued influence as a threat to his own consolidation of power. She was seventy-eight years old at her execution. The chronicle accounts describe her dignity in her final hours; she was buried at her own mausoleum at the Musalla complex in Herat, the surviving fragment of which is now the only place in the city where her memory is publicly preserved.

The treatment of Gawhar Shad in the conventional histories illustrates a recurring pattern in how women's roles in pre-modern dynastic politics are described. She held independent financial resources, governed cities, commanded armies, made foreign policy, executed major architectural projects, and sustained the court patronage system through Shah Rukh's reign and after his death. By any reasonable standard she was one of the most important rulers of fifteenth-century Eurasia. The standard textbook treatment of the period mentions Shah Rukh and Ulugh Beg first, names Gawhar Shad once if at all, and treats the Mashhad mosque as a "Timurid building" rather than as her specific work. This is a familiar historiographical pattern that more recent scholarship has been working to correct. The Timurid Renaissance was at least as much Gawhar Shad's project as Shah Rukh's.

---

The cultural florescence at Herat in the 1410s through 1440s had several distinct components that are worth describing separately.

**Architecture.** The Timurid architectural style — the soaring blue-tiled domes, the four-iwan mosque plans, the elaborate banna'i tilework, the geometric chahar bagh garden plans — reached its mature form at fifteenth-century Herat. The masters were anonymous (the architects of major Timurid buildings are mostly unknown by name, in contrast to the famous Renaissance architects of contemporary Italy), but the style they developed was distinctive and enormously influential. The same style was carried by emigrating craftsmen to the Safavid court at Tabriz after 1500 (Safavid Persia Ch 4), where it produced the great architectural projects of Abbas the Great at Isfahan a century later. It was carried by Babur to India after 1526 (Chapter 8), where it formed the basis of Mughal architecture and produced — eventually — the Taj Mahal in 1648.

The technical innovation that defined Timurid architecture was the **banna'i** (bah-NAH-ee) tilework technique. Earlier Persian-Islamic architecture had used full mosaic tilework, in which small tiles of various colors were assembled into elaborate patterns — labor-intensive, beautiful, but slow to produce. Banna'i used larger glazed and unglazed bricks laid in geometric patterns, allowing whole facades to be covered with ornament at speeds previous traditions could not match. The technique was less subtle than full mosaic but worked well at the monumental scale Timurid patrons preferred, and it enabled the rapid expansion of Timurid building activity through the fifteenth century. Banna'i became the visual signature of late Timurid architecture, was inherited by both Safavid and Mughal architecture, and remains the standard surface treatment for Persianate-style buildings across Central Asia today.

**Miniature painting.** The **Herat school** of Persian miniature painting developed under successive Timurid patrons through the fifteenth century. The early generation worked under Baysunghur in the 1420s and 1430s, producing manuscripts like the **Baysunghur Shahnameh** (1430) — an illustrated copy of Ferdowsi's Persian national epic that contains 21 surviving miniatures of jewel-like quality and is now in the Golestan Palace Library in Tehran. Subsequent generations refined the style further, culminating in the work of **Bihzad** (bee-ZAHD) at Herat in the 1480s and 1490s (Chapter 8).

The Herat school's distinctive features were: flat compositions in elaborate vertical-frame formats; brilliant colors using lapis lazuli blue, malachite green, and gold leaf as primary pigments; extremely precise figure drawing in ink before color was applied; elaborate background landscapes featuring stylized rocks, trees, and architectural elements; and increasingly sophisticated psychological characterization of the figures as the tradition developed. The technical sophistication was extraordinary — these were tiny works (many of the miniatures are no larger than a paperback book page) painted with single-hair brushes under magnifying glasses, requiring years of apprenticeship to master.

The Herat tradition was carried into Safavid Persia and Mughal India by emigrating painters after the Uzbek conquest of 1507 (Chapter 8). Bihzad himself moved to the Safavid court at Tabriz around 1510, where he became director of the royal library and trained the next generation of Safavid masters. Several of his students migrated further east and trained the founding generation of Mughal painters under Akbar in the 1560s. The technical lineage is direct and traceable: every Persianate book painting of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries — Safavid, Mughal, even some of the Ottoman work — runs back through Bihzad to the Herat school under Shah Rukh's patronage.

**Persian poetry.** The classical Persian poetic tradition that had begun under the **Samanids** in the tenth century, peaked in the work of **Rumi** (thirteenth century), **Hafez** (fourteenth century), and **Sa'di** (thirteenth century), produced its closing major figure at Timurid Herat: **Abd al-Rahman Jami** (1414–1492). Jami is conventionally regarded as the last great classical Persian poet — after him, the Persian poetic tradition continued for centuries but is generally considered to have moved into a different phase (the **Sabk-e Hindi** or "Hindi Style" of the Safavid and Mughal periods, Safavid Persia Ch 6). He was a Sufi of the **Naqshbandi** order, a prolific scholar of theology and grammar as well as a poet, and the producer of roughly sixty surviving works including the seven masnavi cycles known collectively as the **Haft Awrang** ("Seven Thrones"). His poetic style combined the lyric elegance of Hafez with explicit Sufi mystical content, and his work was foundational to Persian literary culture both in Safavid Iran and in Mughal India for the next several centuries.

**Calligraphy.** The Timurid era produced the major innovations in Persian calligraphic style that would define the Persianate tradition for the next several centuries. **Mir Ali Tabrizi** (active in the late fourteenth century) is conventionally credited with the development of **nastaliq** (nast-ah-LEEK), the cursive Persian script that combines Arabic naskh and ta'liq elements into a flowing, asymmetric, visually distinctive form. Nastaliq became the standard script for Persian poetry, official chancery documents, and royal correspondence — and remained so until the twentieth century. The Timurid calligraphic establishment produced the master practitioners — Ja'far Tabrizi, Sultan Ali Mashhadi, Mir Ali Heravi — who refined the script through the fifteenth century.

**Theology and scholarship.** The Naqshbandi Sufi order was the dominant religious establishment of Timurid Central Asia. Founded in fourteenth-century Bukhara by **Baha-ud-Din Naqshband** (the order takes its name from him), the Naqshbandis emphasized silent meditation, compatibility with strict Sunni law, and active engagement with political and social life rather than withdrawal into monastic retreat. The order's combination of scholarly orthodoxy and mystical practice fit the Timurid court's needs well — it provided spiritual legitimacy without challenging the dynasty's political authority — and Timurid rulers from Shah Rukh onward extensively patronized leading Naqshbandi sheikhs.

The most politically influential Naqshbandi figure of the late Timurid period was **Khwaja Ubaydullah Ahrar** (KHWA-jah oo-bay-doo-LAH ah-RAR, 1404–1490), based at Samarkand from the 1450s through his death. Ahrar accumulated enormous agricultural estates, arbitrated disputes between Timurid princes, corresponded with rulers from the Ottoman court to the Indian sultanates, and effectively controlled the administration of Samarkand for several decades. His independence from any specific Timurid ruler — combined with his religious authority and his control of land — made him the kind of religious figure that the political class proved unable to constrain. The pattern would be reproduced in later Persianate states: the **Safavid** Shia clerical establishment (Safavid Persia Ch 5) developed a similar institutional independence in the seventeenth century, eventually producing the **marja-i taqlid** system that has dominated Iranian religious politics ever since.

---

A pause to set the cultural achievement in cross-civilizational perspective.

The Herat Renaissance is contemporary with the early Italian Renaissance. The chronological parallel is striking. Shah Rukh's reign (1409–1447) overlaps with the careers of **Filippo Brunelleschi** in Florence (the dome of the Florence Cathedral, completed 1436), **Leon Battista Alberti** (the architectural theorist whose works on perspective and proportion were appearing in the 1430s and 1440s), the early **Donatello** sculpture, and the early generation of **Florentine humanists** rediscovering classical Latin texts. Bihzad's mature career at Herat in the 1480s and 1490s overlaps with the high Renaissance of Botticelli, the early Leonardo, and the founding generation of Venetian painting under Giovanni Bellini.

The two cultural florescences were operating on entirely different aesthetic premises. The Italian Renaissance was rediscovering classical Greek and Roman antiquity through the recovery of Latin texts and (after 1453) Greek manuscripts brought west by Byzantine refugees from Ottoman-conquered Constantinople (Byzantine Empire Ch 12). The Herat Renaissance was synthesizing Persian classical poetry, Islamic religious thought, Mongol political traditions, and Chinese pictorial influences (the chinoiserie elements in Herat painting are real — they reflect direct contact with Yuan and early Ming Chinese art transmitted through the Mongol successor states) into a distinctive Persianate visual and literary culture. The two traditions were aware of each other's existence (Timurid embassies reached the Italian states; Italian travelers reached Samarkand and Herat) but were operating in essentially separate cultural systems with limited mutual influence.

What is striking is the simultaneity. Two of the most ambitious cultural florescences of the fifteenth century happened on opposite ends of Eurasia, in different civilizations, drawing on different traditions, producing different aesthetic results — at the same moment in history. The fifteenth century was an extraordinary period across Eurasia, with Renaissance Italy, Timurid Herat, Ming China under the Yongle Emperor (the Forbidden City was built between 1406 and 1420, exactly contemporary with the Gawhar Shad mosque at Mashhad), and the late **Vijayanagara Empire** in southern India all producing major cultural achievements simultaneously. None of them was the only Renaissance of the fifteenth century. Each was one of several.

The Italian comparison is also useful in another way. Both the Italian Renaissance and the Herat Renaissance depended on patronage from violent political regimes whose wealth was extracted through processes nobody would defend morally. The Medici in Florence funded their patronage through banking and commercial profits accumulated in a brutal Mediterranean trading world. The Borgia popes in Rome funded their patronage through corruption and political assassination. The Sforza dukes in Milan funded their patronage through condottiere warfare. The Timurid patronage at Herat was funded through agricultural taxation in territories whose populations had been pacified by Timurid violence a generation earlier, and the craft labor that produced the great buildings was descended from craftsmen forcibly deported from cities Timur had destroyed. There is no clean way to separate the cultural achievement from the violent extractive systems that funded it. This is true on both sides of Eurasia. It is part of how high cultural production worked across the medieval and early-modern world.

---

Shah Rukh died in March 1447 at Herat, aged about seventy. He had ruled for forty-two years. The empire he left was the reduced Timurid territories — Khorasan, Transoxiana, parts of Persia — but the cultural infrastructure he had built was extraordinarily rich. The succession crisis that followed his death (which Chapter 7 covers from the angle of Ulugh Beg's astronomy) ended much of his political legacy within a decade. The cultural infrastructure largely survived — the architectural style, the painting tradition, the poetry, the scholarship — and was carried by emigrating craftsmen and scholars into the Safavid and Mughal worlds in the next century.

Cross-civ check: Shah Rukh's reign (1409–1447) overlaps with **Henry V** and the early reign of **Henry VI** in England (the long crisis that would produce the Wars of the Roses), the **Hussite Wars** in Bohemia (1419–1434, the prototype of religious-political insurgency in central Europe), the early **Joseon dynasty** in Korea under **Sejong the Great** (whose invention of the Hangul alphabet in 1443 was contemporary with the late Shah Rukh period), the **Yongle Emperor's** building of the Forbidden City and dispatch of Zheng He's voyages from Ming China (1405–1433), and the founding of the **Aztec Triple Alliance**'s peak phase under **Itzcoatl** and **Moctezuma I** (Aztec Empire Ch 5). The first half of the fifteenth century was the high water mark of imperial cultural patronage across Eurasia, and Herat is one of its capitals.

# Chapter 7 — Ulugh Beg the Astronomer-King

In Samarkand, while Shah Rukh ruled the wider Timurid Empire from Herat, Shah Rukh's eldest son **Ulugh Beg** (oo-LOOG BEG, "Great Lord," 1394–1449) governed Transoxiana for forty years. Ulugh Beg's reign at Samarkand is one of the strangest episodes in the history of science: a working political ruler who was also a serious practicing astronomer, who built the largest astronomical observatory in the medieval world, who personally participated in the systematic measurement of the heavens, and who produced the most accurate astronomical handbook of the pre-telescopic era. Then his eldest son murdered him, the observatory was abandoned, and the scientific establishment scattered. The whole enterprise lasted four decades and ended in months.

This chapter is about Ulugh Beg, the Samarkand observatory, the **Zij-i Sultani** ("Imperial Tables") that came out of it, and the immediate cultural collapse that followed his murder.

---

Ulugh Beg was born in 1394, probably at Samarkand, into the Timurid royal family at the height of his grandfather Timur's career. He was on campaign with Timur as a small child — the chronicles describe him being present at Timur's siege of Damascus in 1401 — and was therefore raised inside the operating military culture of his grandfather's empire. After Timur's death and Shah Rukh's consolidation in 1409, Ulugh Beg was installed as governor of Samarkand at age fifteen, with senior advisors managing the actual administration. He held that position until 1447.

Ulugh Beg's training was unusual for a fifteenth-century ruler. The standard education for a Timurid prince consisted of horsemanship, military tactics, Persian and Turkic poetry, religious instruction, and some exposure to Persian historical chronicles — preparation for a life of warfare and provincial administration. Ulugh Beg got all of this, but he was also given systematic instruction in mathematics and astronomy from boyhood. The chronicle accounts say his interest was sparked by a childhood visit (with Timur's army during the western campaigns) to the ruined **Maragha observatory** in northwestern Iran — the great Mongol-era astronomical observatory built by the Ilkhanate scholar **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi** in the 1260s (Mongol Empire Ch 7), which had been the most ambitious astronomical institution of the previous two centuries. Whether the story is literally true or a later embellishment, Ulugh Beg's mathematical and astronomical interests were established by his teenage years and persisted throughout his life.

He combined his astronomical interests with the political duties of governorship in a way that has no real parallel in the history of pre-modern science. Most rulers who patronized scientific work hired specialists — astronomers, mathematicians, physicians — and let them do the work while the ruler attended to politics. Ulugh Beg did this too (Samarkand under his patronage employed dozens of leading mathematicians and astronomers, including Jamshid al-Kashi, Qadi Zada al-Rumi, and Ali Qushji), but he also personally participated in the observational work, made his own astronomical observations using the Samarkand instruments, contributed to the mathematical reductions that produced the Zij tables, and taught astronomy at the madrasa he founded at Samarkand. He was a working astronomer, not just a patron of astronomers.

This is unusual enough that it is worth pausing on the comparison. The standard pattern across world history is that scientific work is conducted by specialists supported by patronage from political or commercial authorities — court astronomers under the Tang and Song Chinese emperors (Tang-Song China Ch 6), Hellenistic mathematicians supported by the Ptolemies in Alexandria (Late Egypt Ch 6), Islamic astronomers supported by the Abbasid court in Baghdad (Islamic Golden Age Ch 3). The patrons might be intellectually engaged, might commission specific projects, might attend astronomical events, but they did not personally take observations or do reductions. Ulugh Beg did. The closest historical parallel might be the Mughal emperor **Akbar** in the late sixteenth century, who personally conducted theological and philosophical debates in his court and commissioned scholarly translations — but even Akbar was a patron rather than a participant in scientific work. Ulugh Beg was both. The phenomenon is rare enough in world history that it deserves to be recognized as such.

---

The **Samarkand observatory** was Ulugh Beg's signature project. Construction began in 1424 and was completed by 1428, on a low hill overlooking the Bibi-Khanym Mosque on the northeastern edge of Samarkand. The site is now one of the major archaeological monuments of Uzbekistan, partially excavated by the Russian archaeologist **Vasily Vyatkin** in 1908 (the observatory's location had been forgotten for several centuries before this rediscovery — one of the ironies of Ulugh Beg's career being that the major institution he built had to be located by archaeological excavation in the twentieth century).

The observatory was a three-story circular building, roughly 165 feet (50 meters) in diameter, containing the largest astronomical instruments yet constructed anywhere in the world. The principal instrument was a **meridian arc** — a quarter-circle stone-and-marble structure with a radius of approximately 130 feet (40 meters), oriented along the local meridian and used to measure the altitude of celestial bodies as they crossed the meridian. The arc's enormous radius was the key technical innovation: a larger arc allowed finer angular measurements, with a precision approaching one minute of arc (1/60 of a degree). This was the highest precision achieved anywhere in the world before the introduction of telescopic measurement in Europe in the early seventeenth century.

The observatory also contained smaller instruments — armillary spheres, astrolabes, sundials, and various specialized devices for measuring planetary positions, equinox dates, and calendar adjustments. The work was conducted by a team of astronomers led by:

- **Jamshid al-Kashi** (jam-SHEED al-KAH-shee, c. 1380–1429), the greatest Islamic mathematician of his generation. Al-Kashi had calculated pi to sixteen decimal places (a precision unsurpassed for nearly two hundred years), produced the **Miftah al-Hisab** ("Key of Arithmetic"), one of the most influential mathematical textbooks of the Islamic world, and developed important techniques in trigonometry, decimal fractions, and root extraction. He was Ulugh Beg's senior mathematical advisor at the observatory until his death in 1429.

- **Qadi Zada al-Rumi** (KAH-dee ZAH-dah al-ROO-mee, c. 1364–1437), an astronomer and mathematician originally from Bursa in Anatolia who had migrated east to Samarkand under Timurid patronage. He was Ulugh Beg's senior astronomer and the head of the observatory team after al-Kashi's death.

- **Ali Qushji** (ah-LEE KOOSH-jee, c. 1403–1474), the youngest member of the team, who survived Ulugh Beg's death and would carry the Samarkand astronomical tradition to Constantinople under Ottoman patronage in the 1470s.

The observatory team operated for roughly two decades, conducting systematic observations of stellar positions, planetary motions, calendar adjustments, and trigonometric calculations. The cumulative results were compiled into the **Zij-i Sultani** ("Imperial Tables"), completed under Ulugh Beg's editorial supervision in 1437.

---

The **Zij-i Sultani** is the most important scientific text produced by the Timurid Empire and one of the great astronomical works of the medieval world. Its principal contents:

- A **star catalog** of 1,018 stars, with positions measured to a precision of one minute of arc. This was the most accurate star catalog produced anywhere in the world between **Ptolemy's** Almagest (second century CE) and **Tycho Brahe's** observations of the 1580s. The Almagest catalog had positions accurate to perhaps ten minutes of arc; Brahe achieved approximately one minute of arc with naked-eye instruments in the 1580s and 1590s; the Zij-i Sultani was already at one minute of arc 150 years earlier. The intervening 1,400 years between Ptolemy and Brahe had not produced a single star catalog of comparable accuracy in any culture — except this one.

- **Planetary tables** for predicting the future positions of the sun, moon, and the five visible planets. The planetary tables were based on the standard Ptolemaic geocentric model with various Islamic-era corrections, but the underlying parameters had been measured fresh at the Samarkand observatory and were significantly more accurate than the previous Islamic tables (the Toledan Tables of the eleventh century, the Maragha-derived Ilkhanic Tables of the late thirteenth century).

- **Trigonometric tables** with extraordinary precision — sine and tangent values calculated to nine decimal places, enabling accurate astronomical calculation that would not be matched in Europe until well into the sixteenth century.

- **Calendar adjustments** establishing the precise length of the solar year and the lunar month, used to calibrate Islamic religious calendars across a multi-century range.

The Zij-i Sultani was used in Islamic astronomy for the next three centuries. Copies were transmitted east to India (where they influenced Mughal astronomers and the work of the eighteenth-century Indian astronomer **Jai Singh II** at Jaipur), west to Ottoman Turkey (carried by Ali Qushji after Ulugh Beg's death and used by Ottoman astronomers through the seventeenth century), and eventually to Europe through Latin translations in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The Polish astronomer **Johannes Hevelius** in the seventeenth century used the Zij-i Sultani's stellar positions in his own work; the English astronomer **Thomas Hyde** produced a Latin edition in 1665. Modern historians of astronomy regard the Zij-i Sultani as the high point of pre-telescopic Islamic astronomy and the most accurate astronomical handbook in the world during the period 1437–1580.

This is worth dwelling on, because it is the kind of fact that gets lost in conventional Western histories of the Renaissance. The standard story has European astronomy stagnating through the Middle Ages and then being revived by the discovery of classical Greek texts in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, eventually culminating in Copernicus, Brahe, Kepler, and Galileo. This is not wrong, but it omits a parallel story. While European astronomy was reorganizing itself from Ptolemaic remnants and rediscovered Greek manuscripts, Islamic astronomy was operating at a higher technical level than the Greek tradition had achieved at its peak, with more accurate instruments, better-trained practitioners, and a continuous research tradition that had been refining itself for several centuries. The Samarkand observatory under Ulugh Beg was the institutional summit of this tradition. The European astronomical revival of the late sixteenth century did not surpass Samarkand levels of precision until Tycho Brahe's work in the 1580s and 1590s. The Greek tradition that European astronomers were rediscovering was technically inferior to the Islamic tradition that was operating contemporaneously — and the Islamic tradition's best work was happening at Timurid Samarkand.

---

The end of the Samarkand observatory came suddenly and is documented in detail.

In 1447, Shah Rukh died. Ulugh Beg, then about fifty-three, became the senior Timurid ruler — inheriting the title of Sultan and effective control of both Samarkand and Herat. He moved part of the court to Herat to consolidate, but the political consolidation went badly. His relations with his eldest son, **Abdal-Latif** (ahb-DAHL-lah-TEEF), had been deteriorating for years — Abdal-Latif felt his father was slighting him in succession arrangements, the chronicle accounts describe several public quarrels, and other Timurid relatives took advantage of the rift to encourage Abdal-Latif's resentment. By 1449, Ulugh Beg and Abdal-Latif were in open conflict.

In October 1449, Abdal-Latif's forces defeated Ulugh Beg's troops in a battle outside Samarkand. Ulugh Beg surrendered and asked permission to make a religious pilgrimage to Mecca — a standard face-saving exit for a deposed ruler in the Islamic world, allowing the deposed ruler to retire from politics under religious cover. Abdal-Latif granted permission. Ulugh Beg set out from Samarkand, accompanied by a small escort.

A few miles outside the city, the escort stopped and Ulugh Beg was forced to dismount. He was put on trial in a hasty proceeding by a religious tribunal Abdal-Latif had assembled, on charges of un-Islamic behavior (the astronomical work was framed as occultism and heresy), and sentenced to death. He was beheaded on **October 27, 1449**. He was fifty-five years old.

The parricide horrified contemporary opinion in the Timurid world. In Islamic political and religious culture, a son's killing of his father was the most grievous sin imaginable, and Abdal-Latif's ordering of the execution destroyed his political legitimacy almost immediately. He was killed by his own troops six months later, in May 1450 — assassinated in his palace by a junior officer who later claimed he had acted to avenge Ulugh Beg. Abdal-Latif was buried in an unmarked grave; the chronicles record his death in tones of moral satisfaction.

The Samarkand observatory was abandoned within months of Ulugh Beg's death. The political instability of the next several years — competing Timurid contenders, Uzbek raids from the north, the lack of any successor with Ulugh Beg's intellectual interests — meant that no new patron took up the work. The observatory's instruments fell into disrepair; the team of astronomers dispersed; the building itself was eventually destroyed by an earthquake in the early sixteenth century. By the time the site was rediscovered by Vyatkin's archaeological work in 1908, only the underground portion of the great meridian arc survived, preserved by being below ground level.

The Samarkand astronomical school continued in attenuated form through the second half of the fifteenth century. Ali Qushji, the youngest of Ulugh Beg's senior astronomers, eventually migrated to **Constantinople** in 1471 under Ottoman patronage. There, at the court of **Mehmed II** (the conqueror of Constantinople in 1453, Byzantine Empire Ch 12 and Ottoman Empire Ch 4), Ali Qushji introduced the latest Timurid astronomical methods to the Ottoman scientific establishment. His proposal that the earth might rotate (in a brief astronomical treatise written shortly before his death in 1474) was debated by the next generation of Ottoman astronomers and prefigured similar discussions in the European Copernican tradition by several decades. The Timurid astronomical legacy therefore survived in fragmentary form, transmitted east to Mughal India and west to Ottoman Constantinople, but the institutional center — the working observatory at Samarkand — was lost.

---

A pause: the Samarkand observatory's collapse is one of the genuinely sad episodes in the history of science. A working research institution at the technical frontier of its field, operating at the highest standards anywhere in the world, was destroyed by a single act of family violence and the political instability that followed. The institutional infrastructure that produced the Zij-i Sultani — the trained personnel, the calibrated instruments, the working procedures, the patronage relationships — could not be reconstructed. A century later, under the Safavid restoration, similar institutional infrastructure had to be rebuilt almost from scratch (Safavid Persia Ch 5 covers the Isfahan school of philosophy and natural science under the Safavids, which represented a partial revival of pre-Timurid intellectual institutions).

The lesson is not that Islamic science was somehow uniquely fragile or vulnerable to political interference. Scientific institutions across world history have been vulnerable to similar failures. The Library of Alexandria was destroyed (probably gradually, over multiple incidents, but the cumulative effect was the same). The Gundishapur Academy was disrupted by the Arab conquest of Persia (Persian Empire Ch 9). The translation movement of Abbasid Baghdad ended with the Mongol sack of 1258 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 8). The Maragha observatory tradition that influenced Ulugh Beg was disrupted by the Ilkhanate's collapse. Scientific institutions need political and economic support to operate, and political crises that disrupt that support can destroy decades of accumulated capacity within months. The Samarkand case is not exceptional. It is just particularly well-documented and particularly tragic.

The astronomical work itself survived through the **Zij-i Sultani**, copies of which were preserved in dozens of manuscript libraries across the Islamic world. The text was not lost. But the institutional capacity to produce more work of comparable quality was lost, and it would be three centuries before any Islamic-world institution achieved comparable astronomical precision again. By that point — the late eighteenth century — European telescopic astronomy had moved far beyond what naked-eye instruments could measure, and the Islamic astronomical tradition that Samarkand had culminated had been overtaken by the Western scientific revolution. The Samarkand observatory was therefore not just the high point of Islamic astronomy but, in retrospect, the last major Islamic astronomical institution before the West took the lead.

---

The political instability after Ulugh Beg's death produced a series of competing Timurid claimants over the following decade. Abdal-Latif's assassination in 1450 was followed by short-lived reigns of various junior Timurid princes (**Abdullah Mirza**, **Sultan Abu Sa'id**, others) and by the gradual loss of Timurid territory to the Uzbeks in the north and the Aq Qoyunlu Turkmen in the west. **Sultan Abu Sa'id Mirza** (a great-grandson of Timur from a junior line, ruled 1451–1469) eventually consolidated most of the central Timurid territories and ruled for two decades — but his reign was less culturally productive than Shah Rukh's, and his death in battle against the Aq Qoyunlu in 1469 inaugurated the late Timurid period that Chapter 8 covers.

Cross-civ check: Ulugh Beg's reign at Samarkand (1409–1449) overlaps with the early Italian Renaissance (Brunelleschi's dome, the early generation of Florentine humanists), the **Joseon dynasty's** scientific patronage in Korea under King **Sejong the Great** (whose own astronomical projects in the 1430s and 1440s were contemporary with Ulugh Beg's — the Joseon court built rain gauges, sundials, and astronomical instruments at the same moment Samarkand was operating its observatory), the **Yongle Emperor's** dispatch of **Zheng He's voyages** from Ming China (1405–1433, partly contemporary with the early Samarkand observatory), and the **Inca expansion** under Pachacuti in the Andes (Andean Kingdoms Ch 6). The first half of the fifteenth century is one of the most scientifically productive periods in pre-modern Eurasian history, with Korea, China, Central Asia, and Italy all running major scientific or technical projects simultaneously. Ulugh Beg's Samarkand was one of these centers. Its abrupt collapse in 1449 was a localized failure that did not stop the broader pattern of fifteenth-century scientific work elsewhere — but it was a serious local loss.

---

# Chapter 8 — The Long Twilight and Babur

The half-century from Ulugh Beg's murder in 1449 to the Uzbek conquest of Herat in 1507 was the long Timurid twilight: a slow political decline punctuated by brief cultural florescence, ending in the extinction of the Timurid Empire as a sovereign state and the flight of the last Timurid prince — **Babur** (BAH-bur, 1483–1530) — to India, where in 1526 he founded the **Mughal Empire** (Persian for "Mongol"), which would rule South Asia for the next three centuries. The Timurid Empire died in 1507 in Khorasan. The Timurid project — the dynastic identity, the architectural style, the literary traditions, the court culture — survived in India for another three hundred years.

This chapter is about the late Timurid Empire and its Mughal afterlife.

---

**Sultan Abu Sa'id Mirza** (1424–1469) had reunified most of the central Timurid territories in the 1450s and ruled them until his death in battle against the **Aq Qoyunlu** Turkmen in 1469. His death is generally treated as the moment when the Timurid Empire ceased to be a single political entity and fragmented permanently into rival Timurid principalities. Three principal centers emerged:

- **Herat** (Khorasan), under various Timurid princes culminating in **Sultan Husayn Bayqara** from 1469.
- **Samarkand** (Transoxiana), under Abu Sa'id's sons, eventually consolidating under **Sultan Ahmad Mirza** and his successors.
- **Ferghana** (the small mountain valley east of Samarkand, in modern Uzbekistan and Kyrgyzstan), under another junior Timurid line, eventually inherited by the young **Babur**.

The political map was further complicated by the rising power of two non-Timurid forces: the **Uzbeks** (a Genghisid Turkic confederation pushing south from the Kipchak steppe, increasingly able to raid and eventually conquer Timurid territories) and, after 1500, the **Safavids** (the new Shia messianic empire of **Ismail I** in northwestern Iran, Safavid Persia Ch 1–2). Both would prove decisive in the eventual extinction of the Timurid state.

But before that extinction, the Herat principality under Husayn Bayqara produced one last cultural florescence that deserves a chapter on its own.

---

**Sultan Husayn Bayqara** (hoo-SAYN bye-KAH-rah, 1438–1506) ruled Herat for thirty-seven years, from 1469 to his death in 1506. He was a great-great-grandson of Timur in the senior line, an experienced military commander, and a cultivated patron of the arts. His reign is the cultural climax of the Timurid period. The political base was thin — Husayn controlled little more than Khorasan, and his territory was constantly under pressure from the Uzbeks in the north and the Safavids in the west — but the cultural production at his court was extraordinary, comparable to (and in some respects exceeding) the earlier achievements at Shah Rukh's Herat in the 1420s and 1430s.

Three figures define late Timurid Herat:

**Abd al-Rahman Jami** (1414–1492), already introduced in Chapter 6, spent his last decades at Husayn Bayqara's court. His later poetic work and his theological writings cemented his reputation as the closing major figure of classical Persian poetry. He died in Herat in 1492 and was buried in a small mausoleum in the city that survives.

**Kamal al-Din Bihzad** (kah-MAHL al-DEEN bee-ZAHD, c. 1465–c. 1535), the greatest of all Persian miniature painters. Bihzad was working at Herat from the 1480s through the 1490s under Husayn Bayqara's patronage, and the surviving works from this period — illustrations for various Persian classical texts including the Bustan of Sa'di (1488), illustrations for Nizami's Khamsa, and dozens of other manuscripts — define the apex of the Herat school. Bihzad's innovations included: naturalistic figure grouping that broke from the more stylized arrangements of earlier Persian painting; psychologically expressive faces with distinct individual characterization; complex spatial compositions using overlapping picture planes; and a richness of decorative detail that exceeded anything in the earlier tradition. After the Uzbek conquest of Herat in 1507, Bihzad migrated to the Safavid court at Tabriz, where he was appointed director of the royal library (the **kitabkhana**) and trained the next generation of Safavid masters. He died in the 1530s, having effectively founded both the Safavid and (through his students) the Mughal painting traditions.

**Mir Ali Sher Nava'i** (meer ah-LEE sheer nah-vah-EE, 1441–1501) was Husayn Bayqara's senior cultural minister and life-long friend (the two had been classmates as boys in Herat). As a poet, Nava'i is conventionally regarded as the founder of the **Chagatai-Turkic** literary tradition. His poems and prose works in Chagatai-Turkic — including the **Khamsa** (a five-part poetic cycle), the **Mahbub al-Qulub** ("Beloved of Hearts," a prose work of moral reflection), and the **Muhakamat al-Lughatayn** ("Judgment of the Two Languages," a critical essay arguing that Chagatai-Turkic was a literary medium equal to Persian) — established Chagatai as a serious literary language for the first time and shaped Turkic literary identity for the next several centuries. As a patron, Nava'i funded Bihzad, Jami, dozens of architects and craftsmen, and most of the late Timurid cultural establishment. His political role at Herat was effectively that of prime minister, conducting administration while Husayn Bayqara handled military affairs and ceremonial duties. The arrangement worked extraordinarily well for thirty years.

The cultural production at late Husayn-era Herat is genuinely astonishing in concentration. Bihzad's painting workshops, Jami's poetic circle, Nava'i's literary patronage, and the architectural projects funded across the city produced one of the most intense cultural moments anywhere in the fifteenth-century world. Visiting European travelers (a few reached Herat in the late fifteenth century, including a Venetian named Iosafa Barbaro) described the city as a marvel — comparable to or superior to the Italian cities they had come from.

---

But the political base was crumbling.

The northern frontier was the immediate threat. **Muhammad Shaybani** (moo-HAH-mad shy-BAH-nee, c. 1451–1510), the leader of the **Uzbek** confederation, had been pushing south from the Kipchak steppe through the 1480s and 1490s, building an Uzbek state that integrated the steppe Turkic-Mongol political tradition with Persianate administrative practice. The Uzbeks were Genghisids in the senior Jochid line — direct descendants of Genghis Khan through his eldest son Jochi — and they used the legitimacy advantage explicitly against the Timurids, whose non-Genghisid status had been the structural weakness of the dynasty since Timur's day (Chapter 1). Shaybani's political program was: replace the illegitimate Timurids with legitimate Genghisid Uzbek rule.

The Uzbek conquest of Timurid territories happened rapidly. **Samarkand** fell in 1500. **Bukhara** fell in 1500 (the same year). Most of Transoxiana came under Uzbek control by 1503. **Herat** fell in 1507. By 1510, Shaybani had taken most of Khorasan as well. The Timurid Empire as a sovereign state had ceased to exist.

Shaybani himself was killed in December 1510 at the **Battle of Merv** (in modern Turkmenistan) by the Safavid Shah **Ismail I**, who had Shaybani's skull encased in gold and made into a drinking cup (Safavid Persia Ch 2). The detail is grotesque and is described in detail in the Safavid chronicles. It is also a deliberate act of political signaling: Ismail was claiming the Mongol political legacy of Timur (skull-cup-from-defeated-enemy is documented in Mongol practice going back to Genghis Khan, Mongol Empire Ch 2) along with the Shia religious legitimacy that defined his own dynasty. The killing of Shaybani temporarily slowed the Uzbek advance, but the Uzbek state survived as the **Shaybanid dynasty** ruling most of Central Asia for the next century.

The Uzbeks pushed many of the surviving Timurid cultural figures into emigration. **Bihzad** fled west to the Safavid court at Tabriz. Other painters, calligraphers, scholars, and architects scattered to wherever they could find patronage — to the new Safavid state, to the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt (which would itself fall to the Ottomans in 1517), to the Indian sultanates, eventually to the Mughal court Babur founded in 1526. The Persianate cultural establishment of fifteenth-century Herat was therefore not destroyed by the Uzbek conquest — it was scattered, and its scattered fragments became the founding generation of Safavid and Mughal court culture in the next century.

This scattering is one of the more remarkable cultural-transmission events in early-modern history. The artistic, literary, and architectural template that the Mughals carried into India and that defined Mughal court culture from Babur to Aurangzeb (1526–1707) was substantially the same template that had been developed at Husayn Bayqara's Herat in the 1490s. The Safavid court culture that took shape at Tabriz under Tahmasp I and reached full development at Isfahan under Abbas the Great (Safavid Persia Ch 3–4) was substantially an inheritance from the same Herat template, transmitted by emigrating Timurid craftsmen. The Persianate world of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries was, in cultural terms, largely a reorganization of Timurid material — distributed across the new political map by the Uzbek conquest of the original Timurid heartland.

---

**Babur** is the Timurid prince through whom the dynasty's afterlife in India happened, and his career deserves its own detailed treatment.

**Zahir-ud-Din Muhammad Babur** (the formal name; "Babur" is a nickname meaning "Tiger") was born in 1483 in **Andijan** in the **Ferghana Valley** — a small mountain-walled valley east of Samarkand in modern eastern Uzbekistan and Kyrgyzstan, with a climate, geography, and political tradition somewhat distinct from the rest of Transoxiana. His father, **Umar Shaykh Mirza**, ruled Ferghana as one of the late Timurid principalities. Babur's mother **Qutlugh Nigar Khanum** was a direct descendant of **Chinggis Khan** through her Moghul royal line — meaning Babur was a Genghisid in the senior line on his mother's side, and a Timurid (fifth-generation descendant of Timur) on his father's side. The combination was exceptionally distinguished by Central Asian political standards. Babur had legitimacy from both the Mongol and the Timurid dynasties. He emphasized this combination throughout his life.

Umar Shaykh Mirza died in 1494 in a freak accident — the chronicle accounts say a pigeon house at the family palace collapsed and he fell from the roof (Babur, who recorded the incident in his autobiography, framed it with a kind of wry detachment that runs throughout his prose). Babur, then twelve years old, inherited the throne of Ferghana. The next decade of his life was a series of disastrous military campaigns against rival Timurid cousins and increasingly powerful Uzbek invaders. He took **Samarkand** briefly in 1497 (at age fifteen), lost it within months, took it again in 1500–01, lost it again to the Uzbeks. He took Andijan and lost it. He spent extended periods as a fugitive in the mountains. By 1504, with the Uzbeks consolidating control of Transoxiana under Shaybani, Babur — now twenty-one — was driven entirely out of his ancestral Central Asian territories.

He turned south. In October 1504, with a small army of personal followers, he crossed the **Hindu Kush** mountains into modern Afghanistan and captured **Kabul**, then a regional center under nominal Timurid suzerainty. Kabul became his base for the next twenty-two years. From Kabul he conducted occasional raids back into Central Asia (he made one more attempt to recover Samarkand in 1511, with Safavid help — Shah Ismail I, having killed Shaybani at Merv the year before, briefly backed Babur against the Uzbeks; Babur held Samarkand for less than a year before being driven out again). But increasingly his attention turned south, toward India.

The reasons for the Indian orientation were practical. Central Asia was lost. The Uzbeks were too strong. The Safavids were potential allies but the alliance was fragile (and required Babur to publicly perform Shia religious observances, which he found uncomfortable as a hereditary Sunni). Kabul could feed itself but was not rich enough to sustain a major dynastic ambition. India, by contrast, was famously wealthy, the Delhi Sultanate (now under the Lodi dynasty since 1451) was politically weak, and Babur's ancestor Timur had set a precedent for the conquest by the 1398 Delhi sack. The southward turn took two decades to develop into action — Babur conducted raiding expeditions into Punjab from the early 1510s onward, gathering intelligence and accumulating the resources for a larger campaign — but the strategic shift was clear from his arrival at Kabul.

The decisive campaign came in 1525–26. The Lodi sultan **Ibrahim Lodi** (ib-rah-HEEM LOH-dee, ruled 1517–1526) had alienated several of his senior provincial governors through arrogant treatment, and in 1525 the governor of Lahore (one **Daulat Khan Lodi**) invited Babur to invade Delhi in alliance with the dissident Lodi nobles. Babur accepted. He marched his army of approximately 12,000 men through the Khyber Pass into Punjab in late 1525, defeated several smaller Lodi forces, and met the main Lodi army at the **First Battle of Panipat** north of Delhi on **April 21, 1526**.

The numerical disparity was stark: Babur's 12,000 against Ibrahim Lodi's force of perhaps 100,000 men plus a thousand war elephants. The disparity was offset by technology. Babur had two field artillery batteries (acquired from Ottoman gunners hired in Anatolia, possibly through Safavid connections) and several hundred matchlock musketeers. Neither weapon was new — gunpowder artillery had been used in European warfare for over a century, matchlocks for several decades — but neither was yet familiar in Indian warfare, where the Lodi army had no equivalent technologies. The musketeers were deployed behind a fortified line of bullock carts (a wagon-fort tactic, similar in concept to the Ottoman tabur cengi formation that would defeat the Safavids at Chaldiran twelve years earlier — Safavid Persia Ch 3). Babur's cavalry maneuvered around the flanks while the artillery and musketeers shot up the dense Lodi center.

The battle was decisive. The Lodi army collapsed in a few hours. Ibrahim Lodi was killed in the fighting. Babur entered Delhi the same week and proclaimed himself emperor, founding the dynasty later called Mughal — a Persian rendering of "Mongol," chosen to emphasize Babur's combined Timurid-Mongol descent. (The dynasty itself called itself **Gurkani** — from the Persian title **Guregen** that Timur had used, "royal son-in-law," referring to his marriage into the Genghisid line. The Persianate world used "Gurkani"; European observers used "Mughal," from the Persian word for Mongol; the English usage that followed was "Mughal" or, sometimes, "Mogul." All three names refer to the same dynasty.)

Panipat was the strategic foundation of the Mughal Empire. Babur conducted further campaigns in 1527–29 to consolidate control of northern India — defeating a Rajput coalition under **Rana Sanga** at the Battle of Khanwa in 1527, defeating an Afghan coalition at the Battle of Ghaghra in 1529 — and ruled from Delhi and Agra until his death in **1530**. He was forty-seven. He had reached the throne late, after twenty-five years of failure, and had ruled India for only four years. But the dynasty he founded would last.

---

The **Baburnama** is one of the great autobiographies in world literature and deserves its own paragraph. Babur kept a personal memoir written intermittently from 1494 (when he was eleven, recording his father's death and his own accession to Ferghana) through his Indian conquests in the late 1520s. He wrote in **Chagatai-Turkic** — his native language, the literary form of Turkic that Mir Ali Sher Nava'i had recently established as a serious literary medium — using a candid, observant, self-critical prose style that has no real parallel in contemporary royal memoirs. The book describes Babur's military campaigns, his political mistakes, his romantic entanglements (he writes about a same-sex attraction in his teenage years with the kind of matter-of-fact description that surprises modern readers expecting sixteenth-century reticence), his depressions, his alcohol consumption (he describes drinking heavily through much of his career, then giving up alcohol in 1527 in a sworn renunciation, then describing the renunciation with a wry awareness that he might break it), his observations of natural history (entire chapters describe the flora, fauna, climate, and geography of every region he passed through, with the precision of a working naturalist), and his reflections on the people he met and the books he read.

The Baburnama's value as a historical source is enormous. It is one of the most detailed primary accounts of Central Asian and northern Indian politics in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, written by a participant rather than by a court chronicler. Its value as a literary work is also enormous — modern scholars have compared it to the autobiographies of Saint Augustine, Benvenuto Cellini, and (occasionally) Marcus Aurelius. The English translation by Annette Beveridge (1922) is the standard reference; more recent translations by Wheeler Thackston (1996) and others have made it more accessible to modern readers.

The Baburnama is also the closing major work of Chagatai-Turkic literature. After Babur, the Chagatai literary tradition continued — particularly at the Mughal court, where Babur's autobiography was treasured and translated multiple times into Persian — but the language was gradually displaced by Persian as the literary medium of the Persianate world and by various successor Turkic literatures (eventually modern Uzbek, modern Uyghur, modern Tatar) for the local Turkic populations of Central Asia. By the early twentieth century, Chagatai had largely ceased to be a working literary language. The Baburnama, written by a man fleeing the destruction of the Timurid Empire and founding a new empire in India, is the most lasting Chagatai literary work.

---

The political continuity from Babur's 1526 victory to the long Mughal afterlife is worth tracing briefly because it is the main way the Timurid project survived.

Babur was succeeded by his son **Humayun** (hoo-MAH-yoon, ruled 1530–1540 and 1555–1556), who lost the empire to the Afghan warlord **Sher Shah Suri** in 1540 and spent fifteen years in exile at the Safavid court of **Tahmasp I** (Safavid Persia Ch 3). With Safavid military help (a 12,000-strong loan force, in exchange for Humayun's nominal Shia conversion and the strategic city of Kandahar), Humayun reconquered northern India in 1555–56 and reestablished Mughal rule. He died shortly afterward, falling down a stone staircase in his library — Babur's posthumous comment in the Baburnama-of-his-mind would presumably have been that the family's relationship with library architecture was permanently unfortunate.

Humayun's son **Akbar** (1542–1605, ruled 1556–1605) consolidated the Mughal Empire as one of the great early-modern Eurasian states. Under Akbar and his successors **Jahangir** (1605–1627), **Shah Jahan** (1628–1658), and **Aurangzeb** (1658–1707), the Mughals ruled most of South Asia for nearly two centuries, during which the Persianate court culture inherited from Timurid Herat reached its full elaboration. The architectural projects of the Mughal period — Akbar's Fatehpur Sikri, Shah Jahan's **Taj Mahal** (1648) at Agra, the **Red Fort** at Delhi (1648), the **Badshahi Mosque** at Lahore (1673) — are all directly descended in style and technique from the Timurid architectural prototypes (the Gur-e-Amir at Samarkand, Gawhar Shad's mosque at Mashhad, Husayn Bayqara's projects at Herat). The Mughal painting tradition, founded under Akbar by the same generation of painters that had migrated from Bihzad's Tabriz workshop, continued the late Timurid Herat tradition for another century. The Mughal court ceremony, the literary patronage, the Persian-language administration, and the religious-political settlements all had Timurid antecedents.

The Mughal Empire as a sovereign political force ended in the early eighteenth century — the death of Aurangzeb in 1707 began a long decline, and the British East India Company gradually displaced Mughal authority over the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. But the dynasty itself persisted as a political fiction in Delhi until 1857, when the failed **Indian Rebellion** against British rule produced the formal abolition of the Mughal title and the exile of the last Mughal emperor **Bahadur Shah Zafar II** to Burma. The Timurid dynasty therefore had a continuous political existence — first in Central Asia, then in India — from Timur's coronation at Samarkand in 1370 to the abolition of the Mughal title in 1858. That is 488 years. By comparison, the **Ottoman dynasty** lasted from 1299 to 1922 (623 years), the **Romanov dynasty** lasted from 1613 to 1917 (304 years), the **Habsburg dynasty** lasted from 1273 to 1918 (645 years). The Timurid dynasty is one of the longest-running dynasties of the early-modern and modern era — measured continuously across its Timurid and Mughal phases as a single political family, which is how its participants understood it.

---

A pause: the comparative point is worth emphasizing because it changes how the Timurid Empire should be assessed.

The conventional treatment of Timur is dominated by the violence — the skull pyramids, the Delhi sack, the Damascus sack, the 1401 Baghdad massacre, the planned China invasion. This is appropriate. The violence was real and is the most distinctive feature of his career. But the conventional treatment also treats the Timurid Empire as essentially a one-generation phenomenon that effectively ended with Timur's death in 1405 and the succession wars that followed. The reduced empire under Shah Rukh, the cultural florescence at Herat, the astronomical work at Samarkand, the late Timurid Renaissance under Husayn Bayqara, the Babur founding of the Mughal Empire, and the long Mughal afterlife are usually treated as separate stories rather than as a single continuous dynastic project.

This is wrong. They are one project. The Timurid identity, the Timurid claim to legitimacy through the Genghisid puppet-khan tradition (eventually transmuted into the Mughal claim to "Gurkani" descent), the Timurid architectural style, the Timurid court culture, the Timurid relationship to Persianate civilization — all of these continued unchanged from Timur's reign through the Mughal afterlife. Babur in 1526 was conscious of being a Timurid recovering territories his ancestor had legitimately conquered in 1398. Akbar in 1556 was conscious of being the senior representative of the Timurid dynasty in continued imperial form. Shah Jahan in 1648, building the Taj Mahal at Agra, was building an architectural descendant of the Gur-e-Amir at Samarkand. The line is continuous and was understood as continuous by the participants.

What this means for assessing Timur: the dynasty he founded did not end with him. It outlasted nearly every other Eurasian dynasty of the period. It produced one of the most successful empires in early-modern world history (Mughal India). It carried the Persianate cultural template that emerged at fifteenth-century Herat into India and made it the dominant high culture of South Asia for three centuries. The cultural legacy ran for five hundred years after Timur's death. He was therefore not just a destructive conqueror; he was the founder of one of the longest-lived imperial dynasties of the early-modern world. The destruction and the dynasty are part of the same story. The cultural achievement and the violence that funded it are part of the same story. There is no honest way to separate the two parts. Both are required for an accurate understanding of what the Timurid Empire actually was.

---

The Timurid heartland — Samarkand, Bukhara, Herat — passed under Uzbek and later Russian control after 1507 and never recovered its earlier centrality in the Persianate world. Samarkand under the Uzbek Shaybanids and their successors continued as a regional capital but never approached its Timurid peak; the great Registan complex (the three monumental madrasas around the central square at Samarkand, completed in 1417, 1636, and 1660 across the Timurid and Uzbek periods) is the most spectacular architectural ensemble in Central Asia today, but its institutional life was a much-attenuated continuation of the Timurid traditions. Bukhara remained a center of Naqshbandi Sufism and Islamic scholarship through the early modern period. Herat declined sharply after the Uzbek conquest, was repeatedly contested between the Safavids and various Afghan dynasties through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, and emerged in the modern period as a regional Afghan city of moderate importance — its great Timurid monuments mostly demolished (the Gawhar Shad Musalla complex's deliberate destruction in 1885 is described in Chapter 6) and its Timurid cultural infrastructure lost.

The recognition of the Timurid achievement in twentieth-century scholarship has therefore depended substantially on the surviving evidence transmitted through the Mughal Indian afterlife — the architectural texts, the painting traditions, the literary works, the dynastic chronicles — which preserved Timurid material long after the Central Asian heartland had ceased to be a center of Persianate culture. The Mughals, in this sense, are why we know what we know about the Timurids.

---

The deepest legacy, however, runs back through the political framework Timur himself constructed.

The structural problem of his career was the legitimacy gap (Chapter 1): a non-Genghisid trying to rule the post-Mongol world had to invent workarounds, and the workarounds — puppet khans, the Guregen title, forged genealogies, the integration of Persianate religious legitimacy through Sunni clerics and Sufi sheikhs — became the political template that the Mughals used. Babur claimed legitimacy through both Timurid and Genghisid descent and through Sunni religious patronage. Akbar elaborated this into one of the most sophisticated multi-religious political settlements in early-modern history (the Mughal court patronized Hindu, Jain, and Muslim religious establishments simultaneously, in a deliberate attempt to construct an Indian-imperial legitimacy that transcended any single religious community). The Mughal political settlement was directly inherited from the Timurid solution to the legitimacy gap, applied in a different geographic context to a different demographic problem.

Even more deeply, the Timurid period established the model that distinguished the early-modern Persianate world from the European national-cultural framework. **Persian** was the literary and administrative language of an enormous geographic region — from Anatolia through Iran into Central Asia and India — that contained multiple competing political states (Safavid, Mughal, Uzbek, Ottoman in the Persian-using parts of its administration). A Persian poet, painter, scholar, or merchant could move between courts as the political winds shifted, finding patronage wherever it was most generous. The cultural unity of the Persianate world was a real fact, sustained for centuries across changing political boundaries. The Timurid Empire was the institutional matrix in which this Persianate cultural unity took its mature form. The Timurid Renaissance at Herat in the 1410s through 1490s was the cultural template that the Safavids and Mughals would then carry forward in their respective geographic spheres.

In this sense, the most lasting Timurid legacy is not the Gur-e-Amir at Samarkand or the Mashhad mosque or even the Mughal Taj Mahal, but the political-cultural framework of the early-modern Persianate world itself. Timur and his successors built the institutional and cultural infrastructure that made it possible for Persian-speaking culture to operate as a transregional civilization for several centuries. That infrastructure outlived the Timurid Empire and the Mughal Empire alike. It survives, attenuated but recognizable, in the contemporary cultural geography of Central Asia, Iran, and South Asia today.

---

The dynasty died — first in Central Asia in 1507, then formally in India in 1858. The civilizational template it built has, in cultural terms, lasted longer than any of the regimes that nominally embodied it.

Cross-civ check: the late Timurid period and the early Mughal founding (1469–1556) overlap with the **High Renaissance** in Italy (Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael), the **Reformation** in Germany (Luther's 95 Theses in 1517 — eight years before Babur's invasion of India), the **Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire** by Cortés in 1519–21 (Aztec Empire Ch 8), the **Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire** by Pizarro in the 1530s, the **early Tudor dynasty** in England (Henry VII, Henry VIII), the **founding of the Safavid Empire** by Ismail I in 1501 (Safavid Persia Ch 1–2), and the **early Ottoman classical age** under Selim I and Suleiman the Magnificent. The early sixteenth century is one of the most densely consequential periods in world history, with imperial expansion, religious revolution, and cultural florescence happening simultaneously across Eurasia and the Americas. The Mughal founding in 1526 is one of these consequential events. The Timurid dynasty's transition from its dying Central Asian phase to its long-lived Indian phase is one of the great dynastic translocations in world history. Babur, riding south through the Khyber Pass in 1525 with twelve thousand men and two batteries of borrowed Ottoman cannons, was the last Timurid prince of Central Asia and the first Mughal emperor of India. The two empires were the same dynasty.

The skull pyramids and the Taj Mahal are part of the same story. The terror of Tamerlane and the cultural splendor of Mughal Agra are part of the same project. The five-hundred-year arc from the capture of Samarkand in 1370 to the abolition of the Mughal title in 1858 is one continuous dynastic history, and it is one of the most consequential in the early-modern world.

That arc is the Timurid Empire. Everything else is commentary.

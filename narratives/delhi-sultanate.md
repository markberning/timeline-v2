# Delhi Sultanate — Three Centuries Between the Ghurids and the Mughals

**Goal:** Narrative for the Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526 CE), the political bridge between the medieval Hindu kingdoms (Medieval India Ch 8) and the Mughal Empire. Across roughly 320 years and five Turkic and Afghan dynasties — Mamluk, Khalji, Tughlaq, Sayyid, Lodi — the Sultanate built the Qutb Minar, repulsed the Mongols, projected armies as far south as Madurai, ran the most ambitious price-control economy of the medieval world, was sacked by Timur, fragmented into provincial sultanates, and finally fell to Babur's matchlocks at Panipat. The Sultanate is why the Mughal Empire that followed it was Persianate, Sunni, and ruled from Delhi — and it is also the reason modern north India has its long Islamic-Hindu-Sikh-Sufi religious palimpsest, its Indo-Islamic architectural tradition, and the Persian-Hindi creole that became Urdu.

A note on names and pronunciation: the Sultanate operated in three language worlds — Turkic among the founding aristocracy, Persian at court and in administration, Hindavi in the streets and bazaars. Names will be given in their most common English form, with pronunciations on first use. Dates are CE unless noted otherwise.

A note on "first" claims: as with every TL in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of," and what survives depends on the documentary luck of which Persian chronicles were copied and recopied versus which burned in city sacks. Sultanate sources are dominated by Persian-language court chronicles — Minhaj-i Siraj, Ziauddin Barani, Shams-i Siraj Afif, Yahya Sirhindi, Nizamuddin Ahmad — written by scholar-bureaucrats who had every reason to glorify their patrons and downplay their failures. The internal Hindu and Jain perspective on the period is documented mainly in regional chronicles and inscriptions, in different languages and from different angles. Read the period through both lenses simultaneously and the picture changes considerably.

# Chapter 1 — The Ghurid Aftermath

The Delhi Sultanate began with a horse accident, an assassination, and a manumitted slave declaring himself king.

The horse accident happened to **Qutb-ud-din Aibak** (kuh-TOB ud-DEEN AY-bak), the Turkic slave-soldier whom Muhammad of Ghor (Medieval India Ch 8) had purchased, trained, and made his commander in north India. After Aibak proclaimed himself **Sultan** at Lahore in 1206 — picking up the Indian provinces of the collapsing Ghurid empire after Muhammad of Ghor's assassination at the Damyak river crossing earlier that year — he ruled for four years before falling off a horse during a polo match in Lahore in 1210 and dying. The horse fell on top of him and the saddle pommel pierced his chest. He was buried in Lahore, where his tomb still stands beside one of the city's modern bazaars.

Four years is not a long reign, but in those four years Aibak did three things that defined the next three centuries.

He installed himself at the head of a Turkic Muslim military aristocracy ruling a Hindu population from a fortified compound in the southern outskirts of an existing Indian city. He began construction of the city's first congregational mosque on the site of demolished Hindu and Jain temples, deliberately recycling their carved columns into the mosque's prayer hall as a visible architectural statement of religious supersession. And he began, beside the mosque, a 240-foot fluted brick-and-sandstone victory tower that would be completed by his successors over the next century and is still the most photographed monument in Delhi.

The mosque was the **Quwwat-ul-Islam** ("Might of Islam"). The tower is the **Qutb Minar**.

Both are still standing.

---

To understand why the Sultanate took the form it did, you have to back up and look at where Aibak came from.

Aibak was born in Turkestan — somewhere in the Turkic-speaking belt of Central Asia north of Khorasan — sometime in the 1150s. He was sold into slavery as a boy, a normal fate for boys captured in the steppe wars between Turkic confederations. He passed through several owners before being purchased as a young man by Muhammad of Ghor's family, brought to the Ghurid capital in central Afghanistan, and trained in royal service. The Ghurids, like every other Islamic dynasty of the eastern Persian world since the ninth century, had built their armies primarily out of trained slave-soldiers — **mamluks** (MAM-looks) — recruited from the Turkic tribes of the Eurasian steppe, converted to Islam, raised from childhood in royal military households, and bound to their owner-king by a tie that combined adoption, conversion, and military training (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6, where the system originates under the Abbasids).

The mamluk system worked because it produced soldiers with no kin ties to anyone except the sultan. They could not run home to a tribal homeland because their tribal homeland was thousands of miles away in a place they had been taken from as children. They had no local family to defect to. Their entire identity — religious, military, social — was the household of the king who had purchased and trained them. In practice, the most talented of these slave-soldiers regularly rose to positions of enormous power. Many became governors of provinces, generals of major armies, and sometimes (when the dynasty above them collapsed) sultans in their own right. The Ghaznavid dynasty (Medieval India Ch 8) had been founded by Sebuktigin, a manumitted Samanid slave-soldier. The **Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt** would be founded by manumitted slave-soldiers in 1250, twenty years after Aibak's Indian sultanate. The Tulunids of Egypt, the Ikhshidids of Egypt, the Ghaznavids, the Khwarazmians — the medieval Islamic east is a long catalogue of slave-founded dynasties.

Aibak fits this pattern precisely. When Muhammad of Ghor was assassinated in 1206 with no son to inherit, Aibak — already the Ghurid commander in India, already running Delhi as a military headquarters — proclaimed himself Sultan at Lahore. The Ghurid central empire fragmented within a few years among rival mamluk commanders. Aibak's rivals **Taj-ud-din Yildiz** in Ghazni and **Nasir-ud-din Qabacha** (kuh-BAH-chah) in Sindh held off claims to his territory for a few years, but the Indian provinces stuck with Aibak. The **Mamluk** or **Slave dynasty** of Delhi was founded.

The technical question of whether Aibak's Lahore proclamation in 1206 or his investiture by the Abbasid Caliph in Baghdad some years later constitutes the formal founding of the Sultanate is a debate medieval and modern historians have continued to have. The conventional date is 1206, and the conventional founder is Aibak. Most modern Indian historiography accepts this, though there are dissenting positions arguing that the dynasty really begins under Iltutmish (Aibak's son-in-law, who actually built the institutional state) in 1211.

---

The city Aibak ruled from was Delhi. Not the modern city of New Delhi, but the older settlement layered onto the same fortified plateau on the southwestern bank of the **Yamuna** river — the great northern Indian river that flows southeast from the Himalayas, joining the Ganges at Allahabad about 600 miles downstream.

Delhi sits at a strategic crux. It controls the route from the Khyber Pass and the northwestern invasion corridor down into the Gangetic plain. It controls the **Doab** (DOH-ahb, "two waters"), the rich agricultural strip between the Yamuna and the Ganges. It is far enough from the coast to be protected from naval attack but close enough to the great rivers to ship grain. The site had been continuously settled since at least the **Mahabharata** epic period (where it appears as **Indraprastha**, the capital of the Pandava kings), through the various Hindu and Buddhist kingdoms of the first millennium, into the period of the **Tomara** Rajputs in the eighth century, and the **Chauhans of Ajmer** who had captured it from the Tomaras around 1150 (Medieval India Ch 8). When Muhammad of Ghor's army defeated Prithviraj Chauhan at the Second Battle of Tarain in 1192 and Aibak occupied Delhi shortly after, the Chauhan citadel passed into Turkic Muslim hands and would not leave them until 1857.

The fortified compound the Mamluks built at Delhi was called **Mehrauli**, on the southern edge of the modern city. The Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque and the Qutb Minar are both at Mehrauli today. The complex would be added to and partially abandoned by successive sultanate dynasties — the Khaljis added a fortified extension at Siri, the Tughlaqs built Tughlaqabad and then Firuzabad and then Jahanpanah, the Lodis built tomb gardens north of all of these, and finally Shah Jahan built the modern Old Delhi at the very north of the cluster in 1648. The "seven cities of Delhi" that tourist guides talk about are the six successive Sultanate capitals plus Shahjahanabad. Each new dynasty preferred to build its own fortified city rather than inherit the moral and architectural baggage of its predecessors.

Aibak's capital at Mehrauli was the first.

---

The Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque deserves some attention because it is the founding statement of Sultanate architecture — and the most direct documentary evidence we have of how the new Turkic regime presented itself to its conquered Hindu subjects.

The mosque was begun in 1192 (immediately after Aibak captured Delhi for Muhammad of Ghor) and substantially completed under Aibak's own sultanate by 1198. It was built on the site of, and using the materials of, twenty-seven demolished Hindu and Jain temples that had stood on the same plateau. The carved sandstone columns of the destroyed temples were dismantled and reassembled into the mosque's prayer hall. You can still see them today: rows of Hindu temple columns, each carved with images of Hindu deities, dancing figures, lotus motifs, and floral patterns, arranged into rows supporting a Muslim prayer hall whose orientation faces Mecca. The figures of the gods were defaced — the sculpted faces chiseled off — but the columns themselves were left visibly Hindu. This was deliberate. The reused columns were a statement: these were temples; they are now a mosque; the new religion has built itself out of the materials of the old.

A Persian inscription on the mosque, from an enlargement built by Iltutmish in the 1220s, makes the political logic explicit. It records that the mosque was built "out of the materials of twenty-seven idol temples" and credits the act to the divine favor that brought Muslim arms to India. The inscription is one of the more uncomfortably honest historical documents of medieval north India: it says exactly what it did and exactly why.

What the mosque does not do, however, is destroy the Hindu artistic vocabulary it built itself out of. The reused columns are still beautiful, still recognizably Indian, still carved with the lotus-and-floral motifs that Indian temple architecture had developed for the previous fifteen centuries. The mosque's iron pillar — which we'll come to in a moment — is still standing in its courtyard, undisturbed, a fourth-century Gupta-era engineering marvel preserved as a kind of curiosity by every Sultanate dynasty that passed through. Sultanate architecture across the next three centuries would proceed in precisely this mixed mode: Persian arch-and-dome construction techniques, Indian craftsmen using Indian techniques and materials, and a constant unresolved tension between the two. The result is a distinct architectural tradition called **Indo-Islamic architecture**, of which the Quwwat-ul-Islam is the founding monument.

The **Iron Pillar of Delhi**, sitting in the Quwwat-ul-Islam courtyard, is its own astonishment. A 24-foot, six-ton pillar of nearly pure wrought iron, originally erected at some other location around 400 CE during the Gupta Empire (Gupta Empire Ch 7) and bearing a Sanskrit inscription dedicating it to a king conventionally identified as **Chandragupta II**. It has stood in the open air for sixteen centuries and has barely rusted. Modern metallurgists have spent decades figuring out why: a combination of high phosphorus content, low sulfur, and the formation of a self-protecting hydrated iron oxide-phosphate film on the surface. The pillar is one of the great surviving artifacts of medieval Indian metallurgy, and one of the strangest things about Sultanate Delhi is that the Mamluks chose to preserve it intact in their flagship mosque, rather than recycling it into iron weapons or pulling it down. They didn't read Sanskrit, and they probably didn't know what it said, but they could see that it was extraordinary, and they left it alone.

---

The **Qutb Minar** — the 240-foot tower beside the mosque — is the more famous monument.

Aibak began the Minar in 1199, intending it as both a victory tower and a minaret from which the call to prayer would be sounded over the new mosque. The first story was completed under his reign. The next three stories were added under his son-in-law Iltutmish in the 1220s. The fifth and final story, with its later marble cladding, was added under Firuz Shah Tughlaq after a lightning strike in 1369 damaged the upper section. The tower thus took the better part of two centuries to reach its final form, with each major Sultanate dynasty adding to it.

It stands 240 feet tall (about a 24-story building, the tallest brick minaret in the world). It is fluted with alternating round and angular ribs in the lower stories, gradually smoothing toward the top. Each story is separated by a projecting balcony resting on **muqarnas** corbels — the honeycomb-like Islamic architectural feature borrowed from Iranian and Central Asian buildings. The exterior is wrapped in carved Arabic and Persian inscriptions in flowing Naskhi calligraphy, including verses from the Quran and praise of the sultans who built each stage.

What the Qutb Minar means is genuinely complicated and has been debated for centuries. The most direct reading — preferred by Sultanate-era court historians and adopted by colonial-era European scholars — is that it is a **victory tower**, celebrating the Islamic conquest of India and broadcasting Muslim power over the conquered Hindu plain. There is a literal historical model: **Mahmud of Ghazni**'s minarets at Ghazni (Medieval India Ch 8) were victory towers in exactly this sense, and Aibak would have known them. The Quwwat-ul-Islam inscription on the mosque it stands beside makes the same Triumphalist note, so the reading is consistent.

But the alternative reading is also not wrong: the Minar is a **functional minaret** for the mosque, oriented to allow the call to prayer to project across the city, in the same architectural tradition as the great Seljuk-era Iranian minarets. And the synthesis reading, increasingly preferred by modern Indian historians, is that the Minar is **both at once** — a deliberately ambiguous monument that combines triumphant Islamic conquest, functional Islamic worship, and (by the time Tughlaq added the marble cladding) a kind of monumental urban civic identity that no longer reads cleanly as either conqueror or conquered. By the time the Minar was finished, Sultanate Delhi was Indo-Islamic, not just Islamic. The monument reflects what it had become.

The Qutb Minar is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site. About three million tourists visit it every year. It is the single most recognized monument of pre-Mughal Islamic India.

---

To get a sense of where this puts the Sultanate in world history, the cross-civ check at the founding moment:

**1206 CE**, the year Aibak proclaimed himself Sultan at Lahore, is also the year **Genghis Khan** was acclaimed Great Khan of the unified Mongol tribes at the **kurultai** (great assembly) on the banks of the Onon river in northern Mongolia (Mongol Empire Ch 2). Two of the most consequential thirteenth-century empires were proclaimed in the same year, on opposite sides of Eurasia, by two figures who never met but whose successors would soon collide on the Indus river. The Mongol world-empire and the Delhi Sultanate are fundamentally contemporary phenomena — in fact, one of the central political achievements of the early Sultanate was being one of the few Eurasian states that successfully resisted Mongol absorption. We'll get to that in Chapters 2 and 3.

In **Europe**, 1206 falls in the long reign of the great medieval-papal Pope **Innocent III**, who was about to launch the Albigensian Crusade against the Cathar heretics in southern France (Byzantine Empire Ch 9 covers the Crusader sack of Constantinople in 1204, two years before our story begins). The Holy Roman Empire was contesting succession after the death of Henry VI. **Genghis Khan and Aibak were declaring empires while medieval Christendom was declaring crusades**.

In **East Asia**, the **Southern Song** dynasty ruled the wealthier southern half of China (Tang-Song China Ch 7), and Genghis Khan would shortly attack its northern Jurchen Jin neighbors. The **Kamakura shogunate** had been founded in Japan in 1192. The **Khmer Empire** at Angkor was rebuilding after the death of Jayavarman VII.

In **Africa**, the **Ayyubid Sultanate** ruled Egypt (the dynasty Saladin had founded in the 1170s after defeating the Crusaders), and the **Mali Empire** had not yet been founded — Sundiata Keita's victory over the Sosso would not come until around 1235. The Islamic world that the Sultanate was joining in 1206 was an Islamic world stretching from Morocco to the Indus, with the Abbasid caliphate technically still in existence at Baghdad but with real power held by various regional Turkic, Kurdish, and Persian dynasties.

The Sultanate, in other words, joined a world that was already mid-transformation. The Mongols would shortly destroy half of it, the Crusader states would shortly collapse, the Song would fall to the Mongols by 1279, and the Sultanate would be the last surviving major Islamic state in the eastern half of this world by the late thirteenth century. The Mongols never managed to take Delhi. That fact is one of the central facts of medieval Indian history. We'll get to how it happened in Chapter 3.

---

Aibak's death in the polo accident of 1210 left a problem. His only son, **Aram Shah**, was politically weak and had alienated the Turkic mamluk nobility at Lahore. The Delhi mamluks rejected him within a few months and invited Aibak's son-in-law, the governor of Badaun, to take the throne. The son-in-law was **Iltutmish** (il-toot-MISH, ruled 1211–1236).

Iltutmish was the real founder of the Sultanate as an institution, and Chapter 2 is mostly about him. Aibak founded the dynasty; Iltutmish built the state. He defeated his rivals (Yildiz of Ghazni, Qabacha of Sindh) and consolidated all the Indian Ghurid territories under Delhi. He secured a **Khilat** (formal robe of investiture) from the Abbasid Caliph al-Mustansir in Baghdad in 1229, giving the Sultanate Islamic legal legitimacy. He built up the **Chihalgani** (chee-hahl-GAH-nee) — the Forty, an inner council of forty senior Turkic slave-officers who served as the dynasty's military and administrative aristocracy. He introduced the silver **tanka** (TANK-uh) and copper **jital** (JEE-tul) as the standard coinage of the realm — units that would survive through the Mughal period. He completed the upper stories of the Qutb Minar that Aibak had begun. He commissioned his own tomb — the **Tomb of Iltutmish** — in the Qutb complex, the earliest surviving Islamic mausoleum in India, with red-sandstone walls covered in interwoven Quranic Arabic and Persian inscriptions in flowing geometric calligraphy.

And he is the figure who makes the central choice that will define his death: nominating his daughter **Razia** to succeed him as Sultan over his surviving sons.

That decision is where Chapter 2 starts.

---

Cross-civ check: while Iltutmish was building the Sultanate at Delhi between 1211 and 1236, **Genghis Khan** was campaigning across Persia and Central Asia (Mongol Empire Ch 3), the **Magna Carta** had been signed by King John in England in 1215, **Frederick II Hohenstaufen** was building his strange Sicilian-Arabic-Latin court in Palermo (Byzantine Empire Ch 10 touches the same period), and the **Cathar Crusade** was nearing its grim end at Montsegur. The intellectual world of the early thirteenth century — **Thomas Aquinas** would be born in 1225, **Ibn al-Arabi** the Sufi metaphysician was teaching in Damascus, **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi** was writing his astronomical works at Maragha (Mongol Empire Ch 7), and **Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi** was synthesizing Sufi theology for the Islamic world (Islamic Golden Age Ch 8) — was producing some of the most important religious thought in any tradition. The Sultanate joined this world as a frontier polity at its eastern edge, with most of its intellectual contributions still ahead of it.

# Chapter 2 — Razia and the Slave Dynasty

Iltutmish died in April 1236 after a long illness. He had spent the last year of his life systematically working through the question of his succession, and he had reached the most controversial conclusion he could possibly have reached.

He had four sons. Two were dead. The eldest surviving was **Rukn-ud-din Firuz**, who was an alcoholic widely regarded as politically incompetent. The other surviving son was a child. The Turkic nobility, by every Islamic dynastic precedent in the eastern world, expected one of these sons to succeed. What they expected especially strongly was that Iltutmish would not nominate his daughter.

Iltutmish nominated his daughter.

**Razia Sultana** (RAH-zee-ah, ruled 1236–1240) was Iltutmish's adult daughter from his chief queen, **Qutub Begum**. According to the chronicler **Minhaj-i Siraj** (whose **Tabaqat-i Nasiri**, written about thirty years later, is the principal Persian source for the early Sultanate), Iltutmish explained his choice publicly: he had observed Razia ruling Delhi as his regent during one of his Bengal campaigns, and she had managed the city better than her brothers ever had. She was educated, militarily literate, politically shrewd, and willing to learn. The sons, by contrast, were a disappointment.

Iltutmish formally proclaimed Razia as his designated successor and had the proclamation read in the mosques of Delhi. Then he died.

The Turkic nobility immediately ignored the will and put Rukn-ud-din Firuz on the throne instead.

---

Rukn-ud-din lasted seven months. His reign was, by all accounts, a disaster. He spent the treasury on entertainment, neglected administrative duties, drank constantly, and allowed his mother (Iltutmish's other queen, who had not been Razia's mother) to assassinate Iltutmish's other surviving sons in a series of palace murders. Within a year, the Delhi populace was in active rebellion against him. Razia, watching from the sidelines, made her move.

The story Minhaj-i Siraj records — and it is impossible to know how much of it is accurate and how much is later legend, but the story itself is the historical fact that mattered for the next thousand years — is that Razia appeared at the Friday congregational prayer at the Delhi mosque in November 1236 wearing red robes (the color of mourning for her murdered brothers), publicly accused Rukn-ud-din of orchestrating the murders, and called on the assembled Muslims of Delhi to depose him in the name of justice. The crowd surged forward. Within hours, Rukn-ud-din had been arrested and Razia was on the throne.

She would rule Delhi for the next three and a half years.

---

What makes Razia historically remarkable is not just that a woman briefly held the throne of a major medieval Islamic empire — though that is remarkable enough on its own. **Shajar al-Durr** would briefly hold the Mamluk throne in Egypt in 1250 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 9 references the same dynasty), so Razia is not absolutely unique. But **Shajar al-Durr** ruled for three months; Razia ruled for nearly four years. **Empress Wu Zetian** had ruled Tang China in the 690s (Tang-Song China Ch 4), and **Boudicca** had led the Iceni revolt against Rome in 60 CE (Celtic Cultures Ch 7), and **Hatshepsut** had ruled Egypt as pharaoh in the fifteenth century BCE (New Kingdom Egypt Ch 3). Female rule in the medieval and ancient world is uncommon but not unheard-of. What makes Razia distinctive is the combination of unambiguous formal succession (she was literally named successor by her father), formal sovereignty (she had coins struck in her own name, as **Sultan Jalalat-ud-din Razia**), and political engagement (she rode into battle, presided over court, and made her own foreign-policy decisions).

She also did several things that scandalized the Turkic nobility.

She abandoned the **purdah** (PUR-dah, the Islamic seclusion of royal women behind veils and curtains), appearing in public and at court without a veil. She wore men's clothing — the standard tunic-and-trousers of a Turkic male warrior — at official functions. She rode horseback in public. She insisted on being called **Sultan** rather than **Sultana**, on the grounds that the masculine title was the legal title and the feminine form was demeaning. (Modern historians use "Sultana Razia" anyway, which is how the name has come down to us, but she herself rejected it.)

And — most consequentially — she promoted a non-Turkic Abyssinian slave-officer named **Yakut** to the position of master of the royal stables, an enormously influential post in a court where horses were a critical military asset and where access to the royal stables meant access to the sultan. The Turkic nobility took this as a personal insult and a sign that Razia was bypassing them in favor of a non-Turkic favorite.

The Forty mobilized.

---

The Chihalgani — the Forty — were the inner aristocracy of the Mamluk dynasty. Iltutmish had assembled them as a corps of senior Turkic slave-officers who served as governors of the empire's major provinces and as the leadership of the central army. Each was a manumitted slave, each had been raised in the royal household, and collectively they considered themselves the kingmakers of the dynasty. They had grudgingly accepted Razia's accession because Rukn-ud-din had been impossible to defend, but they had assumed she would rule as their figurehead and let them run the state.

Razia, instead, was running the state. She made her own appointments, ignored their advice, promoted Yakut over their objections, and was beginning to look like an actual sultan with her own power base.

In 1240, the governor of **Bhatinda** (Bah-TIN-dah), a Turkic noble named **Malik Altunia** (al-TOON-ya), revolted against her. Razia rode out at the head of an army to suppress the revolt. Yakut was killed in the campaign. Razia was captured at Bhatinda. The Forty in Delhi declared her deposed and put one of her brothers, **Bahram Shah**, on the throne.

What happened next is the part of the Razia story that has been most heavily mythologized. The basic facts: Razia, captive at Bhatinda, married Altunia (whether by force, by political calculation, or by genuine attachment, the sources disagree). The two of them then attempted to retake Delhi from Bahram Shah at the head of a combined army. They were defeated outside Delhi in October 1240. Razia and Altunia fled. They were caught and killed, possibly by their own retreating soldiers, possibly by local villagers who robbed them, on the road near **Kaithal** in modern Haryana, on October 14, 1240. Razia was about 35 years old.

She is buried in old Delhi at a small tomb, near the Kalan Masjid mosque, which is now half-buried in residential houses and largely forgotten by the city. The tomb is a simple raised platform with two grave-markers, one for Razia and one (traditionally) for her sister. The site receives almost no visitors. The Qutb Minar that her father commissioned and that was completed under successors who actively suppressed her memory still stands ten miles south, drawing three million tourists a year.

The contrast is the point. Razia's reign was uncomfortable in her own time and has remained politically uncomfortable since, in different ways for different audiences. The Mamluk dynasty's official chronicles, written by Persian scholar-bureaucrats employed by sultans who came after her, treated her reign as an aberration that demonstrated the unsuitability of female rule. Modern Hindu nationalist historiography sometimes (selectively) embraces her as a counter-example to "Islamic patriarchy." Modern Indian feminist historiography has reclaimed her as a proto-feminist hero. Modern Muslim historiography has variously celebrated her competence, lamented her novelty, and ignored her entirely. She remains one of the most contested figures in medieval Indian historiography.

What is uncontested is that she happened. She really was Sultan of Delhi. She really was an effective ruler in a period when most of her competition was less so. And she really was the only woman to hold the throne of any major medieval Indian empire. Her four years are a documented historical fact, however imperfectly remembered.

---

After Razia, the Sultanate spent the next quarter-century in confusion.

Bahram Shah, the brother who had taken her throne, lasted two years before being deposed and killed by his own nobles in 1242. His successor **Alauddin Masud Shah** lasted four years before being deposed and killed in 1246. The Forty cycled through three more nominal sultans — the child **Nasir-ud-din Mahmud** (whose real ruler was the regent **Ghiyas-ud-din Balban**), and brief usurpations between regnal periods — until 1266, when Balban himself took the throne in his own right.

The instability was the problem the Forty had created for themselves. A corps of forty roughly equal slave-officers, each with his own retinue and provincial power base, could not produce a stable monarchy as long as it remained a real political force. Each succession became a faction fight. Each faction fight weakened the central state. By the 1260s, the Sultanate was at risk of disintegration. The Mongol raids on the northwestern frontier (more on which in a moment) were getting worse. Bengal was effectively independent under its own Turkic governor. Most of the Doab was held only loosely.

What the Sultanate needed was a strong central monarch who could break the Forty's power and make the office of Sultan into something more than a symbol manipulated by the nobles.

What it got was **Balban**.

---

**Ghiyas-ud-din Balban** (BAL-ban, ruled 1266–1287) had been one of the Forty himself. He had come into Iltutmish's slave household as a young Turkic captive in the 1230s, risen through the ranks under the chaos of the post-Iltutmish years, served as regent for Nasir-ud-din Mahmud for twenty years, and finally taken the throne in his own right in 1266 as a man already in his sixties. He brought to the throne a clear analysis of what was wrong with the Sultanate and a set of ferocious institutional reforms designed to fix it.

His basic insight was that the Sultanate had become weak because the Sultan had become a peer of his nobles rather than a sacred figure standing above them. The remedy was to elevate the office of Sultan into something near-divine, to break the Forty's institutional power, and to back this up with a credible apparatus of state violence.

The first move was ceremonial. Balban introduced Persian court ceremonial of a deliberately exaggerated kind. Court attendance required full Persian dress; nobles approached the throne by performing **sijdah** (prostration on the ground) and **paibos** (kissing the sultan's feet). Court was conducted in formal Persian; access to the sultan was strictly limited; even close family members had to formally request audiences. Balban explicitly modeled this on the Sasanian Persian court (Persian Empire Ch 8), the most ceremonialized court in the eastern Islamic memory, deliberately importing its hierarchical theology of kingship — the **Zilullah**, "shadow of God on earth" — to elevate his own status.

The second move was political. Balban systematically broke the Forty. He demoted, exiled, or executed the senior surviving members of the Chihalgani over the first several years of his reign. The most senior, his cousin **Sher Khan**, the frontier governor who had effectively held the western frontier against the Mongols for two decades, was poisoned in 1270 (Balban himself ordered the poisoning, by all serious accounts). The other surviving Forty were neutralized through demotion, isolation, or selective execution. By the late 1270s, the Chihalgani as an institution was finished.

The third move was military. Balban built up a standing army of professional cavalry that owed loyalty directly to him rather than to his governors. He created a new intelligence service (the **barid**, Persian for "courier service" — a network of professional informants reporting directly to the sultan from every district). He suppressed banditry across the Doab with calculated terror, hanging the bodies of suspected bandits along the roads to Delhi in numbers that the Persian chronicler Barani records (with some satisfaction) as exceeding the legal proprieties of mainstream Islamic jurisprudence. He campaigned personally against rebels in Bengal, against the **Meo** people of Mewat (the rural population around Delhi who had been raiding the city's outskirts), and against the various smaller principalities that had taken advantage of post-Iltutmish weakness.

It worked. By the time Balban died in 1287 at perhaps eighty years old, the Sultanate was once again a stable, centralized military monarchy. The cost was high — Balban's reign is the most violent and most authoritarian of the Mamluk period — but the institutional state survived. The Sultanate was structurally stronger in 1287 than it had been at any time since Iltutmish.

The question was whether Balban's successors would be able to hold what he had built.

---

They were not. Balban's grandson **Kayqubad** (kay-koo-BAHD, ruled 1287–1290), who took the throne as a teenager after Balban's death, lasted three years before being murdered in his bath. The Mamluk dynasty itself was over.

What replaced it was a new dynasty from a different Turkic lineage — the **Khaljis**, who had been settled in eastern Afghanistan for two centuries, who spoke a Turkic language but had absorbed enough Pashtun culture and intermarriage that the older Turkic mamluks of Delhi considered them slightly impure outsiders. The Khalji takeover in 1290, which Chapter 3 covers, was a kind of revolution within the Sultanate — not a foreign conquest, but a transfer of power from one Turkic ethnic faction to another, with consequential effects on the dynasty's character.

But before we get to the Khaljis, we need to talk about the Mongols.

---

The Mongols were the central external threat to the Sultanate from 1221 to about 1330. The fact that the Sultanate survived this threat is one of the most consequential military facts of the thirteenth century.

The Mongol problem started with **Genghis Khan** himself. After Genghis crushed the Khwarazmian empire of **Sultan Muhammad** in 1219–21 (Mongol Empire Ch 3), the deposed Khwarazmian prince **Jalaluddin Mingburnu** (jah-lahl-ud-DEEN MING-bur-noo) fled south through Afghanistan to the Indus river, attempting to take refuge in north India. Genghis Khan personally pursued him, and in November 1221 Genghis's army caught Jalaluddin at the **Indus** river. Jalaluddin's army was destroyed; Jalaluddin himself escaped by swimming his horse across the Indus and disappearing into the western Himalayan foothills.

Genghis Khan was now camped on the Indus, looking east into India. He sent a small reconnaissance force into the Punjab. The force returned with reports of the climate (oppressive, with monsoon humidity the steppe-bred Mongol horses found difficult), the terrain (forested and broken), and the population (numerous and hostile). Genghis Khan declined to invade. He turned his army around and went back to Mongolia.

Genghis's restraint at the Indus is one of the great might-have-beens of medieval history. Iltutmish, who was sultan at Delhi in 1221, had wisely refused to shelter Jalaluddin or to provoke the Mongols in any way. (His political judgment was excellent; he understood exactly what Mongol revenge for offering shelter would have looked like.) The Sultanate survived its first encounter with the Mongols by being too unimportant to be worth conquering. That would not be true forever.

The Mongol threat returned under Genghis's successors. The **Chagatai khanate** — the western half of the Mongol world-empire, centered in modern Uzbekistan and Afghanistan (Mongol Empire Ch 5) — sent regular raiding parties through the Khyber Pass and into the Punjab from the 1240s through the 1290s. These raids were not full conquest attempts, but they were destructive: they would seize the harvest, burn villages, take captives back across the Indus, and demand tribute from the local Sultanate-appointed governors. The frontier region around Multan and Lahore was repeatedly devastated. The Sultanate's western frontier essentially became a permanent military zone, garrisoned by frontier governors whose entire career consisted of defending against Mongol incursions.

The most prominent of these frontier governors was Balban's cousin **Sher Khan**, who held the Multan-Lahore frontier for twenty years (1240s through 1265) and was one of the few senior Sultanate officials whose name the Mongols genuinely feared. After Balban poisoned Sher Khan in 1270, the frontier was held by a succession of less competent governors, and the Mongol raids accordingly worsened. By the 1280s, Mongol raids were reaching the outskirts of Lahore and beginning to threaten Multan.

Balban's response was to build up a standing frontier army at **Sunam** and **Samana** in the Punjab, garrisoned by professional troops with a dedicated supply chain from Delhi. The system worked, but it was permanently expensive. Balban's military budget was enormous, and the Sultanate was effectively running a war-economy on the western frontier from the 1270s onward.

The full Mongol assault was still to come. It would arrive under the Khalji dynasty, in six separate invasions between 1296 and 1306, and its repulsion would be the central military achievement of the next chapter.

But the institutional foundation for that achievement was Balban's. The standing army he built, the fortified frontier he maintained, the centralized command structure he created — all of these would be inherited by the Khaljis and used to win the wars Balban had prepared for.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Mamluk Sultanate was consolidating in Delhi between 1206 and 1290, the **Mongol Empire** was conquering most of Eurasia (Mongol Empire Ch 3, Ch 4, Ch 5), the **Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt** was being founded in 1250 by another set of slave-soldiers (Islamic Golden Age Ch 9), the **Crusader states** in the Levant were being progressively rolled back, the **Yuan Dynasty** of Kublai Khan was being established in China by 1271 (Tang-Song China Ch 9), and the **Holy Roman Empire** was passing through the long interregnum after the Hohenstaufen collapse. The thirteenth century was the great Mongol century, and one of the few major Eurasian states to survive it without being conquered or restructured by the Mongols was the Sultanate. That fact is the precondition for everything that follows.

# Chapter 3 — The Khalji Imperial Moment

The Khalji dynasty (1290–1320) lasted only thirty years, but it pushed the Sultanate to its first real imperial peak. Under one ruler in particular — **Alauddin Khalji** (al-ah-ud-DEEN KAL-jee, ruled 1296–1316) — the Sultanate defeated six Mongol invasions, conquered the Rajput citadels, projected armies as far south as Madurai, and ran the most ambitious price-control economy of the medieval world. It also ushered in a sustained burst of Indo-Persian literary culture under court poet **Amir Khusrau** (uh-MEER khus-ROW), one of the most prolific writers in any medieval language.

The dynasty began with a murder.

---

**Jalaluddin Khalji** (jah-lahl-ud-DEEN, ruled 1290–1296) was a Khalji general in his seventies who deposed the last Mamluk sultan Kayqubad in 1290. Jalaluddin was, by all the surviving evidence, a genuinely mild and pious man. He pardoned political enemies who would normally have been executed. He refused to plunder cities he conquered. He maintained civil terms even with the relatives of the dynasty he had displaced. He was, in short, the wrong personality for medieval Sultanate politics.

His nephew (and son-in-law), **Alauddin Khalji**, was the right personality. Alauddin had been Jalaluddin's military commander on the southern frontier, and in 1296 he led a successful raid on the wealthy Hindu kingdom of **Devagiri** (in modern Maharashtra), bringing back enormous quantities of treasure. He returned north along the Yamuna and arranged a meeting with his uncle Jalaluddin at the river port of **Kara** (KAH-rah) in eastern Uttar Pradesh, ostensibly to deliver the spoils.

What happened at Kara is recorded in detail by the chronicler **Ziauddin Barani** (zee-ah-ud-DEEN bah-RAH-nee), whose Tarikh-i Firuzshahi is the principal source for the Khalji and early Tughlaq dynasties. Jalaluddin came down to the riverbank to embrace his nephew. Alauddin's men cut Jalaluddin down where he stood. Alauddin had himself proclaimed Sultan on the spot, marched to Delhi at the head of Jalaluddin's own army, distributed the Devagiri loot among the Delhi nobility to buy off opposition, and was firmly on the throne within a few months.

It is one of the most efficiently executed coups in Sultanate history. It also set the political tone of Alauddin's reign: he would not be constrained by traditional considerations of family, piety, or precedent. He would do what worked.

---

What worked, as it turned out, was a great deal.

The first thing Alauddin had to do was deal with the Mongols. Beginning in 1296, the year of his accession, the **Chagatai khanate** sent six separate invasions through the Khyber Pass into north India over the next decade. These were not the small raids of the previous century; they were full-scale armies, sometimes 100,000 strong, intent on actual conquest. The most dangerous of them — under **Targhi** in 1303 — actually reached Delhi and besieged the city. Another, in 1305 under **Ali Beg** and **Tartaq**, penetrated as far as the modern Saharanpur district. A third, in 1306 under **Kebek**, reached the lower Punjab.

Alauddin's response was the most successful sustained anti-Mongol defense achieved by any non-Mongol state in the thirteenth or fourteenth century. He restructured the army on professional lines, paying his cavalry and infantry directly in cash from the treasury rather than through the older **iqta** (IK-tah) land-grant system. He built up new fortified frontier garrisons under generals like **Zafar Khan**, **Ulugh Khan**, and **Ghazi Malik** (the future founder of the Tughlaq dynasty). He moved his own court within the new fortified extension city of **Siri** (SEE-ree), built just north of Mehrauli, after the 1303 Mongol siege of Delhi made the older Mehrauli compound look insufficient. The walls of Siri, according to a tradition recorded by Barani, were partially mortared with the skulls of Mongol prisoners taken in the 1303 campaign — a detail that may be propaganda but that captures the calculated brutality of the project.

The defeats Alauddin's army inflicted on the Mongols were severe. At the **Battle of Kili** in 1299, Zafar Khan broke the army of **Qutlugh Khwaja** outside Delhi. At the battles of 1305 and 1306, Ulugh Khan systematically destroyed the invading forces and sent thousands of Mongol prisoners back to Delhi to be publicly executed. By 1306, the Chagatai khanate had effectively given up. The Mongol invasions of the Sultanate ceased almost entirely after 1306, and would resume only sporadically and at much smaller scale during the subsequent decades.

This is one of the structurally important facts of medieval Eurasian history. The Sultanate was, alongside **Mamluk Egypt** (which defeated the Mongols at the Battle of **Ain Jalut** in 1260, Islamic Golden Age Ch 9), one of only two Islamic states to successfully resist Mongol absorption. **Hungary** was raided in 1241 and survived but was devastated. **Russia** was conquered (Mongol Empire Ch 4). **Persia** was conquered (Mongol Empire Ch 5). The Sultanate's anti-Mongol record is genuinely exceptional and is the central reason the Sultanate's Persianate Indo-Islamic culture was able to develop without being absorbed into a larger Mongol-imposed framework.

---

With the Mongol threat receding, Alauddin turned to internal consolidation and external expansion.

He systematically reduced the Rajput strongholds that had held out since Muhammad of Ghor's time. The **Chauhan** fortress of **Ranthambore** (RAN-thuhm-bohr), one of the most defensively formidable hill-forts in north India, fell in 1301 after a year-long siege; the defending **Hammir Dev** Chauhan died in the final assault. The **Sisodia** fortress of **Chittor** (CHIT-tohr), in modern Rajasthan, fell in 1303 after another protracted siege. The fall of Chittor became the source of the legendary story of **Padmini** (pud-MEE-nee), the Rajput queen whose beauty was the stated cause of Alauddin's siege and who, according to the legend, led the **jauhar** (jow-har, the Rajput practice of mass collective suicide by fire by the women of a defeated fortress to avoid capture) along with thousands of other Chittor noblewomen as the gates fell. The Padmini story comes mainly from a sixteenth-century Sufi poem (**Malik Muhammad Jayasi**'s **Padmavat**) and is almost certainly fictional in its specifics, though the underlying fact of mass jauhar at Chittor is documented in multiple medieval sources. The conquests continued: **Mandu** in Malwa, **Siwana** and **Jalor** in Rajasthan, all reduced over the next decade. By 1311 the major Rajput dynasties had been militarily broken (though most survived as reduced regional states and would reassert themselves in the post-Tughlaq fragmentation).

Then came the southern raids.

---

**Malik Kafur** (KAH-fur) was a Hindu eunuch slave originally from Gujarat, captured by Alauddin's army during a raid on the **Vaghela** kingdom around 1297 and brought back to Delhi to serve in the royal household. He converted to Islam, rose rapidly through the slave hierarchy, and by the early 1300s had become Alauddin's most trusted military commander.

Between 1308 and 1313, Alauddin sent Kafur on three campaigns into south India. The first, in 1308, defeated the **Yadavas of Devagiri** (in modern Maharashtra) again and brought the Yadava king Ramachandra into vassal status. The second, in 1310, attacked the **Kakatiyas of Warangal** (in modern Telangana) and forced their king **Prataparudra** to surrender his treasury, including the Koh-i-Noor diamond — the great Golconda stone that was about to begin one of the most extraordinary biographies of any object in human history. The third, in 1311, swept through the **Hoysalas of Dwarasamudra** (in modern Karnataka) and reached as far south as **Madurai**, the ancient Pandya capital deep in the Tamil country.

Madurai is roughly 1,400 miles south of Delhi (about the distance from New York to Houston). Kafur's army had marched across nearly the entire subcontinent, defeated four major Hindu dynasties in succession, and returned to Delhi with quantities of treasure that the Persian chronicles describe in numbers that read like fantasies — gold by the elephant-load, silver by the tonnage, jewels by the bushel — but that all surviving sources, Hindu and Muslim, agree were genuinely enormous.

What is critical to note is that these were not conquests in the modern administrative sense. Kafur did not annex south India. He did not station Sultanate garrisons at Madurai or Warangal. He raided, extracted treasure and tribute, accepted formal submission, and left. The southern Hindu kingdoms remained nominally independent, paying annual tribute to Delhi but otherwise unchanged. The closest analogy is the **Viking raids** of the eighth-tenth centuries on western Europe (Viking Age Ch 4): great profitable extractions that did not — at least at first — translate into permanent territorial control. The southern raids brought enormous wealth to Delhi but did not produce the southern administrative apparatus that, say, the Cholas had built within the south themselves (Medieval India Ch 5). The Sultanate's actual administrative reach remained the Indo-Gangetic plain plus a few pockets in central India.

But the raids' political consequences were enormous. They demonstrated that the Sultanate's military reach extended across the entire subcontinent. They brought the Koh-i-Noor and a flood of southern gold into the Sultanate treasury, funding decades of subsequent imperial spending. And they established the precedent — confirmed by the Tughlaqs and challenged again only by the Mughals two centuries later — that Delhi's authority was, theoretically, pan-Indian.

The Koh-i-Noor itself is worth pausing on. The stone — at the time perhaps 793 carats in its uncut form, eventually cut and recut to its modern 105.6 carats — passed from the Kakatiyas to the Sultanate treasury in 1310. It would pass to the **Tughlaqs**, then to **Babur** after Panipat, then to the **Mughal** treasury, then to **Nader Shah** of Iran after his 1739 sack of Delhi (Safavid Persia Ch 8 covers the same Nader Shah), then to the **Durranis** of Afghanistan, then to **Maharaja Ranjit Singh** of the Sikh Empire in the 1810s, and finally to the **British Crown** after the 1849 annexation of Punjab. It is now mounted in the crown of Queen Mary's consort and is displayed at the Tower of London, with claims for its repatriation regularly made by the Indian, Pakistani, and Afghan governments. The stone's Sultanate-era arrival in Delhi in 1310 is the start of its eight-century-long career in the treasuries and crowns of every major successor state to the Sultanate.

---

While the Sultanate's armies were marching across south India, Alauddin was conducting an entirely different kind of revolution at home: the **price-control economy**.

The basic problem Alauddin was trying to solve was a logistics-and-fiscal problem. He had built a very large standing army (the chronicles give numbers around 475,000 cavalry at peak, which is almost certainly inflated, but the army was unambiguously huge). Paying them in cash required a stable currency and stable prices. If prices in the Delhi grain markets rose, the army's cash salaries would not buy enough food, and the army would mutiny. The conventional medieval Sultanate solution was to assign each soldier an iqta land-grant and let them collect their own salary from the harvest, but this dispersed the army across the countryside and weakened central control. Alauddin wanted his army concentrated at Delhi, paid in cash, and reliably fed.

The solution he came up with was to set fixed government prices for all major commodities sold in the Delhi markets, and to enforce those prices through an elaborate state apparatus.

The system was administratively breathtaking. The chronicler Barani (whose father and grandfather had served as Alauddin's officials, so he had inside knowledge) describes the apparatus in detail. There were three controlled markets in Delhi: one for grain, one for cloth, and one for slaves and animals. Each had its own state-appointed superintendent, called a **shahna**, who reported directly to a senior court official — **Malik Yaqub**, the chief market controller, the most powerful non-military post in the empire. Each merchant was registered. Each transaction was recorded. Spies — drawn from a network of professional informants reporting to a separate intelligence service — tracked unofficial sales and reported violations.

The prices were set very low. Wheat was fixed at 7.5 jitals per **maund** (a standard weight, about 82 pounds). Cloth, slaves, horses, cattle — all were priced at levels significantly below what an unregulated market would have produced. To keep these prices possible, Alauddin built government granaries, requisitioned grain from the rural cultivators of the Doab at fixed below-market rates (raising the effective land tax to roughly half the harvest, the highest rate in pre-Mughal Indian history), and stockpiled enough grain to crash market prices whenever they threatened to rise.

The penalties for non-compliance were severe. Merchants caught short-weighting or selling above the fixed prices were fined, flogged, or in extreme cases mutilated (the Persian chronicles describe the cutting off of an equivalent weight of flesh from the cheating merchant's body, which is probably hyperbole but captures the deterrent intent). The system was understood by everyone involved as functioning by terror as much as by economics.

It worked while Alauddin was alive. The Delhi army was reliably fed. Prices in the Delhi market remained essentially stable for nearly two decades. The state extracted a much larger share of agricultural surplus than any previous Indian state had managed. The system collapsed within months of Alauddin's death in 1316, when his successors had neither the political will nor the administrative capacity to maintain the apparatus.

The price-control regime is genuinely without parallel in pre-modern Indian history, and it is one of the most ambitious experiments in medieval state economic planning anywhere in the world. Modern economists have looked at it as an ancestor of twentieth-century command economies. Modern historians have looked at it as evidence of how far a determined medieval state could push administrative penetration. What it really was, structurally, was a wartime fiscal-military state that needed to feed its armies cheaply enough to pay for the Mongol wars and the southern raids simultaneously. When the wars ended, the system was no longer necessary. When Alauddin died, the system no longer worked.

---

Alauddin Khalji died in January 1316 at perhaps fifty years old. The succession was contested. His designated heir was the youngest of his sons, but the oldest sons immediately challenged the will. Within months, **Malik Kafur** (still Alauddin's most trusted general, and now also his most trusted political confidant) was effectively running the Sultanate as regent for the infant heir. Kafur was assassinated in his bath about a month later by Alauddin's surviving Turkic mamluks. The infant sultan died (probably murdered) shortly afterward. The throne passed to one of Alauddin's older sons, **Mubarak Shah Khalji**, who lasted four years before being murdered in 1320 by his own favorite, a converted Hindu official named **Khusrau Khan** (khus-ROW), who briefly held the throne for himself before being deposed by **Ghazi Malik**, the Khalji frontier governor.

Ghazi Malik proclaimed himself **Ghiyas-ud-din Tughlaq** at Delhi in 1320. The Khalji dynasty was over.

---

The Khalji moment lasted thirty years and produced more sustained imperial achievement than the previous fifty years of the Mamluk dynasty had managed. The Mongols were defeated decisively. The Rajputs were broken. The south was raided to a depth no previous north Indian dynasty had matched. The price-control economy worked for as long as Alauddin lived. Persian high culture flourished at the court under Amir Khusrau (whose long career we'll cover properly in Chapter 6, with the rest of the Sultanate's literary production). The institutional Sultanate that Iltutmish had built and Balban had stabilized reached, under Alauddin, its first true imperial form.

The cost was high. Alauddin's centralization required brutal enforcement. The southern raids depopulated regions of the Deccan. The price-control economy worked through coercion that the chronicles describe with discomfort even when their authors were Sultanate insiders. The army was vast but expensive. The treasury was full at Alauddin's death but the structural underpinnings were brittle.

When the Tughlaqs took over in 1320, they inherited an empire at peak. What they did with it is the subject of Chapter 4.

---

Cross-civ check: while Alauddin Khalji was running Delhi between 1296 and 1316, the **Mongol Ilkhanate** in Persia was at its peak under **Ghazan Khan** (whose 1295 conversion to Islam ended the Mongol-vs-Islam phase of the Eurasian wars, Mongol Empire Ch 7), the **Mamluk Sultanate** in Egypt under **al-Nasir Muhammad** was the rival Islamic great power of the period, the **Yuan Dynasty** under Kublai Khan's successors ruled China (Tang-Song China Ch 9), the **Mali Empire** of Sundiata Keita and his successors was establishing the great West African gold-empire that would peak under Mansa Musa in the 1320s (Mali Empire Ch 1), and the **Ottoman beylik** had just been founded by **Osman I** in northwestern Anatolia (Ottoman Empire Ch 1) — the Ottoman dynasty that would be the Sultanate's partial mirror-image across the next four centuries was being founded the same decade Alauddin was breaking the Mongols.

Marco Polo, whose travels in Asia included reports from south India that he had visited around 1290, wrote his memoir during this period. The **Hundred Years' War** between England and France was decades away from beginning. The **Black Death** would not strike Eurasia until the 1340s. The world was, in 1316, on the cusp of a series of climatic, demographic, and military shocks that would reshape every major civilization. The Sultanate, having reached imperial peak, was about to experience its own shock under the Tughlaqs.

# Chapter 4 — The Tughlaq Catastrophes

The Tughlaq dynasty (1320–1414) is one of the most paradoxical regimes in medieval Indian history. Under its three principal sultans — **Ghiyas-ud-din**, **Muhammad bin Tughlaq**, and **Firuz Shah** — the dynasty inherited the Sultanate at its peak, expanded it briefly to its largest territorial extent, and then watched it fragment into a collection of provincial sultanates that would never be reunited in its name. The middle of the three, Muhammad bin Tughlaq, was probably the most intelligent ruler the Sultanate ever produced. His reign was a sequence of brilliantly conceived and catastrophically executed policy initiatives that wrecked the empire he had inherited.

The dynasty began with a returning frontier governor, an elaborate welcome ceremony, and a wooden pavilion that fell down on the head of its sultan.

---

**Ghazi Malik** (gah-ZEE), the frontier governor whose defense of the Punjab had repulsed several Mongol invasions during Alauddin Khalji's reign, deposed the converted Hindu usurper **Khusrau Khan** in September 1320 and proclaimed himself **Ghiyas-ud-din Tughlaq** at Delhi. He was perhaps sixty years old. He spent the next five years rebuilding the central administration that the late Khalji chaos had damaged, restoring the price controls in modified form (his approach was looser than Alauddin's), reasserting Sultanate authority over the Doab, and beginning construction of a massive new fortress city, **Tughlaqabad** (toog-LAH-kah-BAHD), on the southern outskirts of Delhi.

Tughlaqabad is one of the most overbuilt fortresses in Indian history. The walls run about six and a half miles around an enormous citadel-and-palace complex on a rocky outcrop. The walls are cyclopean — twenty to thirty feet thick at the base, sloping inward at a defensive batter, built from massive ashlar masonry blocks. The citadel includes Ghiyas-ud-din's own tomb, with its distinctive sloping walls and red-sandstone-and-marble construction (the model that would influence later Tughlaq architecture). Construction took five years and reportedly involved tens of thousands of laborers.

Tughlaqabad was abandoned within a few years of its construction. The official explanation, recorded in later chronicles and traditions, is that a Sufi saint — **Nizamuddin Auliya**, the great Chishti master at Delhi (Chapter 6) — cursed Tughlaqabad after Ghiyas-ud-din offended him, declaring that it would either be inhabited by jackals or by the Gujjar nomads. Nizamuddin's curse, which is documented in Sufi hagiographies but not in court chronicles, became one of the famous folk-explanations for why Tughlaqabad collapsed so quickly. The more prosaic modern explanation is that the site lacked an adequate water supply and was difficult to defend in practice despite its imposing walls.

Ghiyas-ud-din died in 1325 in circumstances that have been debated for seven centuries. He was returning from a campaign in Bengal, and his son **Jauna Khan** (the future Muhammad bin Tughlaq) had built a wooden welcome pavilion at the village of **Afghanpur** outside Delhi to receive him. After the welcome ceremony, Ghiyas-ud-din retired into the pavilion to rest. The pavilion collapsed on him, killing him. The official explanation — that the pavilion was built too quickly and the structural beams gave way — is contradicted by the chronicler Ibn Battuta, who arrived in Delhi nine years later and was told by people who had been present that Jauna Khan had engineered the collapse deliberately, having the pavilion built to fall on his father at a precise moment, in order to seize the throne. Whether the Afghanpur incident was an accident, a murder, or something in between, Jauna Khan was on the throne within days, calling himself **Muhammad bin Tughlaq**.

---

**Muhammad bin Tughlaq** (ruled 1325–1351) is the most documented Sultanate ruler of the fourteenth century, partly because his reign was so eventful and partly because the Moroccan traveler **Ibn Battuta** spent eight years (1334–1342) at his court as a qadi (Islamic judge) and wrote extensive eyewitness descriptions of his rule.

Muhammad was, by every contemporary account, an extraordinary individual. He had been formally educated in Persian, Arabic, mathematics, astronomy, philosophy, and medicine. He could quote Quranic Arabic and write Persian poetry. He had read Greek philosophy in Arabic translation. He corresponded with intellectuals across the Islamic world, including with the Mongol Ilkhanate's surviving scholars and with Mamluk Egyptian theologians. Ibn Battuta describes him as personally generous, intellectually curious, deeply pious, and capable of charming foreign visitors with his learning.

Muhammad was also, by every contemporary account, alarmingly volatile. The same Ibn Battuta who praises his learning describes him spending entire afternoons watching public executions, distributing alms one day and ordering mass beheadings the next. The Persian chronicler Ziauddin Barani, who served in Muhammad's administration for years and clearly admired aspects of his master, called him a man who "habitually mixed mercy with cruelty in proportions no one could predict." Modern historians have variously diagnosed him with everything from manic-depressive disorder to a kind of intellectual hubris that mistook clever ideas for workable policy. None of these diagnoses can be confirmed and most are anachronistic. What is undisputed is that his reign was a sequence of catastrophes, and that he reacted to each catastrophe with another grand initiative whose results were as catastrophic as the last.

Three episodes capture this pattern: the move to **Daulatabad**, the **token currency**, and the **Khurasan expedition**.

---

The first catastrophe was the move of the capital to **Daulatabad**.

Daulatabad (dow-luh-TAH-bahd) — formerly **Devagiri**, the Yadava fortress that Alauddin Khalji had captured and Malik Kafur had used as a base for the southern raids — sits in modern Maharashtra, about 700 miles south of Delhi (a little farther than the distance from New York to Charleston). It is centrally located in the Deccan, on a defensible rocky outcrop that the Yadavas had fortified with concentric walls and a moat carved into living rock. Muhammad bin Tughlaq decided in 1327 that Daulatabad would be the Sultanate's new imperial capital, on the grounds that it was geographically closer to the recently conquered south and would allow tighter administrative control of the empire's expanded territory.

The reasoning was not entirely unreasonable. The southern raids had accumulated nominal authority over a territory the Sultanate could not actually govern from Delhi. Moving the capital south would, in theory, allow Sultanate administration to penetrate the Deccan in a way that the periodic raiding system had not achieved.

What was unreasonable was Muhammad's chosen method. He ordered the entire population of Delhi — not just the administrative elite, not just the army, but all of Delhi's residents, including the artisans, merchants, scholars, religious figures, families — to migrate 700 miles south to Daulatabad. The population was given a year to move. Those who resisted were to be forcibly relocated.

Ibn Battuta, who arrived in Delhi during this period, describes the move as a humanitarian disaster. The migration occurred during the worst phase of a multi-year drought. The road south crossed several hundred miles of arid country with limited water. People died in large numbers along the route. Those who arrived at Daulatabad found a city not yet built to absorb them — there was inadequate housing, inadequate water, no established mosques or markets, no functional bureaucracy. Within a few years, Muhammad reversed the policy and ordered everyone back to Delhi. The return migration killed many of those who had survived the original journey. Delhi, when the survivors returned, was a half-empty ruin that took a generation to repopulate.

The Daulatabad episode is the most often-cited example of Muhammad bin Tughlaq's "brilliant disasters." A reasonable strategic concept (the empire needed a southern administrative center) was implemented in a way that demonstrated complete inability to anticipate the consequences. The empire that had pushed to Madurai under Kafur's raids was not actually capable of relocating itself to govern from the Deccan, and the attempt to do so weakened the central state significantly. The Bahmani revolt of 1347 (more on which shortly) was a direct downstream consequence: the Deccan administrators that Daulatabad had created were the same officials who would later rebel.

---

The second catastrophe was the **token currency** of 1330–1333.

The basic problem was again fiscal. The Sultanate, by 1330, was running a chronic shortage of silver. Alauddin Khalji's southern raids had produced enormous loot, but the loot was now being spent down on Muhammad's military campaigns and grandiose construction projects. The mines that had supplied the silver tanka (the standard Sultanate coin Iltutmish had introduced) were not producing enough new silver to replace what was being spent. Silver was draining out of the economy through purchases of Central Asian horses (the Sultanate had to import its cavalry mounts; India did not produce enough war-quality horses to meet demand).

Muhammad bin Tughlaq's solution was conceptually radical: he introduced a brass and copper token coinage backed by the imperial treasury. The token coins were declared legal tender at the same face value as the silver tanka. Each token, in principle, could be redeemed at the imperial mint for the silver equivalent. The system would, in theory, expand the money supply without requiring new silver and would relieve the fiscal pressure on the treasury.

This was, structurally, exactly the same idea as fiat currency. It was a brilliantly forward-looking concept. **Six hundred years** before the Bretton Woods system formally divorced national currencies from gold backing, Muhammad bin Tughlaq was attempting to create state-backed paper-equivalent money in medieval India. There are no real Eurasian precedents for this innovation; Tang and Song China had tried paper money (Tang-Song China Ch 7) but the Chinese systems were tied to deposits of metal in central treasuries and circulated only for specific commercial purposes. Muhammad's tokens were intended as the actual coin of the realm, used for everything from soldiers' salaries to merchant transactions.

The system collapsed within four years. The reason was forgery. Brass and copper were available everywhere; private foundries across northern India began minting their own tokens within weeks of the official launch. Within a few months, the bazaars were flooded with counterfeit tokens that the imperial mint could not distinguish from its own issues. The face-value-redemption guarantee meant that the imperial treasury was obligated to convert all of these tokens — including the forgeries — into silver tankas on demand. The treasury hemorrhaged silver. Within four years, Muhammad had to abandon the experiment, accept all outstanding tokens (forgeries included) at the mint, and absorb the resulting losses. The treasury was effectively bankrupted. The credibility of the Sultanate currency was severely damaged for the next generation.

The token currency was, in its conception, ahead of its time by six centuries. In its execution, it was a catastrophe that demonstrated that medieval administrative capacity could not police what modern central banks could. The technology of state authentication of currency — the security features that distinguish a real banknote from a forgery — did not exist. Without them, the experiment was doomed.

---

The third catastrophe was the **Khurasan expedition**.

In 1329, Muhammad bin Tughlaq raised an army of 370,000 troops to invade **Khurasan** — the eastern Iranian region that included Herat, Mashhad, and the Central Asian fringes (Safavid Persia Ch 1 covers the same region). His stated goal was to overthrow the Chagatai khanate that had been raiding the Sultanate's western frontier, install a friendly regime in central Asia, and assert Sultanate primacy in the eastern Islamic world.

The army was paid for a year in advance, equipped at vast expense, and assembled outside Delhi. The fiscal cost is recorded by Barani as draining most of what remained of Alauddin Khalji's treasury surplus, supplemented with the new revenues from the (still-functional) token currency.

The army never left Delhi. The political situation in Central Asia changed in 1330 — the Chagatai khan whose territory Muhammad had intended to invade reached an accommodation with his neighbors, and the strategic opening Muhammad had counted on disappeared. Muhammad cancelled the expedition and disbanded the army.

The disbanded soldiers, having been paid for a year and now released without further employment, formed roving armed bands across north India. Some were absorbed into the Sultanate's existing military establishment. Many drifted into banditry. The internal security situation across the Doab and the Punjab deteriorated significantly through the 1330s as a direct result.

This is the third "brilliant disaster": a real strategic opportunity (overthrow the Chagatai khanate while it was internally weak) was prepared for in a way that depended on conditions Muhammad could not control, and when those conditions changed, the prepared army became a liability rather than an asset. The treasury was drained for nothing. The empire was actively destabilized by the disbandment.

---

The cumulative effect of the three catastrophes was to wreck the Sultanate's imperial position by the late 1330s. The treasury was empty. The central army had been disbanded and replaced by a smaller, less reliable force. Famine struck the Doab repeatedly during the 1330s — partly the consequence of fiscal mismanagement, partly the consequence of the Little Ice Age beginning to affect Indian agriculture (the global climate shift that would also push the Norse out of Greenland and trigger crop failures across Europe in the next century).

The political consequences were what we would expect. Provincial governors in the south, the east, and the west began revolting. Bengal had been functionally independent for decades; under Muhammad bin Tughlaq's reign, Bengal formalized its independence by stages, and would proclaim itself the **Bengal Sultanate** in 1352, the year after Muhammad's death.

The most consequential revolt was in the Deccan. In 1347, a discontented Tughlaq officer named **Hasan Gangu** led a mutiny of the Deccan administrators (many of whom had been displaced from Delhi to Daulatabad and back) and proclaimed himself **Alauddin Bahman Shah**, founding the **Bahmani Sultanate** (BAH-mah-nee). The Bahmani capital at **Gulbarga** (later **Bidar**) became the seat of an independent Muslim kingdom in the Deccan that would last two centuries and produce some of the most distinctive Indo-Persian architecture of medieval India. The Bahmanis were the first major Muslim kingdom to establish itself outside the Sultanate's authority, and their existence permanently broke the precedent that Muslim political power in India flowed from Delhi.

A few years earlier, in 1336, two brothers named **Harihara** and **Bukka** — formerly officials of the Hindu **Kakatiya** kingdom who had been captured by the Tughlaq army, converted to Islam, and sent south to govern the Deccan in the 1320s — re-converted to Hinduism and founded the **Vijayanagara** empire (vih-jah-yah-NAH-gah-rah, "City of Victory") on the Tungabhadra river in modern Karnataka. Vijayanagara would become the dominant Hindu power of southern India for the next two centuries — a deliberate Hindu counterweight to the Sultanate north and the Bahmani Sultanate east. Its founders are perhaps the only documented case in medieval Indian history of converted Muslims successfully re-converting to Hinduism (which mainstream Hinduism, in theory, did not allow). The fact that they could do so suggests how much fluidity actually existed between religious identities in the Sultanate's southern frontier.

Muhammad bin Tughlaq died in 1351 while campaigning against rebels in **Sindh**. His exact cause of death is unrecorded, but he had been ill for some time. The reign ended with most of the south permanently lost, the Deccan in revolt, the treasury empty, the Doab depopulated, and Delhi itself a shadow of what it had been at his accession.

---

His successor was his cousin **Firuz Shah Tughlaq** (ruled 1351–1388), a man with diametrically opposite political instincts. Where Muhammad bin Tughlaq had been ambitious, intellectually adventurous, militarily aggressive, and administratively reckless, Firuz Shah was cautious, religiously conservative, militarily defensive, and administratively careful. He was, by personality, exactly what the empire needed after Muhammad: a competent housekeeper who could stabilize what remained.

Firuz Shah explicitly rejected expansion. He made no attempt to retake Bengal, the Bahmani Sultanate, or any of the southern territories that had broken away. He did campaign in Bengal twice (in 1353 and 1359), but both campaigns ended with negotiated settlements that confirmed Bengal's independence. He focused, instead, on internal consolidation: rebuilding the administrative apparatus, restoring the treasury, and undertaking public works.

The public works are the most lasting positive legacy of his reign. He built the **West Yamuna Canal**, the largest irrigation project of medieval north India, diverting water from the Yamuna across hundreds of miles of arid Punjab and Haryana to water previously uncultivated land. The canal was rebuilt by **Akbar** under the Mughals two centuries later (it was one of the engineering inheritances Akbar consolidated) and parts of it are still in use today. Firuz Shah also built or rebuilt several smaller canals from the Sutlej and the Jumna, and dug a network of stepwells and reservoirs across the dry regions of his empire.

He founded several new cities. **Firuzabad** (FEER-ooz-uh-BAHD), in central Delhi (modern Firuz Shah Kotla), became his capital. **Hisar** (in modern Haryana) and **Jaunpur** (in eastern Uttar Pradesh) were founded as administrative centers. The Tughlaq architectural style — austere, fortified, built from rough stone with tapering walls — was developed across these new cities and is one of the more distinctive medieval Indian architectural traditions.

He had a particular fascination with antiquities. He transported two **Ashoka pillars** — twenty-foot polished sandstone columns dating from the third century BCE, originally erected by the Mauryan emperor Ashoka (Maurya Empire Ch 5) and bearing inscriptions in Brahmi script that Firuz Shah could not read — from sites in the Punjab and Meerut to Delhi at enormous engineering effort, mounting one of them at his Firuzabad palace and another at his hunting lodge. The pillars were preserved as imperial trophies and curios; nobody at the Sultanate court could decipher the Brahmi script. The Ashokan inscriptions would not be read for another five hundred years, until **James Prinsep** of the East India Company decoded the Brahmi alphabet in 1837. But Firuz Shah's preservation of the pillars meant that the inscriptions survived to be read.

He was also a religious conservative. His **Futuhat-i Firuzshahi**, his autobiography, records with pride his suppression of various religious and philosophical movements he considered unorthodox — Ismaili Shia communities, Sufi groups he regarded as antinomian, Hindu temples that had encroached on Muslim public life. He systematically applied **jizya** — the Islamic poll-tax on non-Muslim **dhimmi** (DHIM-mee) subjects, which most prior sultans had applied loosely or only in cities — to the Hindu population of his empire as a regular instrument of imperial revenue. This is the period when Sultanate administration becomes most visibly distinct from the more religiously syncretic patterns that prevailed under earlier dynasties. Firuz Shah's reign is therefore something of a hinge point in the religious history of medieval India: he is more orthodox than his predecessors, and the orthodoxy carries downstream consequences for how the late Sultanate is remembered.

He died in 1388 at perhaps eighty-three years old, having held the Sultanate together for thirty-seven years through patient administration and strategic withdrawal. The empire he left was much smaller than the one Muhammad bin Tughlaq had inherited and much smaller than the one Alauddin Khalji had built, but it was still recognizably an empire. It had a treasury, a functional army, an administrative bureaucracy, a network of public works.

His successors lost it within a decade.

---

The post-Firuz Shah period is a textbook succession crisis. Firuz Shah had outlived his eldest son and had to nominate a grandson, **Ghiyas-ud-din Tughlaq Shah II**, who was deposed and killed within a year. The throne passed through a series of cousins and minor relatives — **Abu Bakr Shah**, **Nasir-ud-din Muhammad**, **Mahmud Shah** — none of whom held it for more than a few years. The Tughlaq nobility split into factions backing different claimants, the central administration broke down, and provincial governors increasingly behaved as independent rulers. By the early 1390s, the actual political authority of the Tughlaq sultans had contracted to Delhi and a few surrounding districts, while the rest of the empire — Bihar, eastern Punjab, the Doab, Gujarat, Malwa — was being governed by ambitious provincial officers who paid only nominal tribute to Delhi.

This was the situation that **Timur** (TEE-moor) found when he invaded India in 1398 — and which he would shortly make even worse.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Tughlaqs were running their empire (1320–1388), the **Black Death** was killing perhaps a third of Europe and significant percentages of the Islamic Mediterranean (1346–1353), the **Hundred Years' War** between England and France was getting underway (1337), the **Yuan Dynasty** in China was collapsing into the **Ming** restoration (1368), the **Mali Empire** was at its peak under **Mansa Musa** (whose famous 1324 hajj passed through Cairo, distributing so much gold that prices in the Egyptian markets crashed for a decade — Mali Empire Ch 4), the **Ottomans** were establishing themselves in the Balkans (Battle of Kosovo, 1389; Ottoman Empire Ch 2), and the **Aztecs** were founding **Tenochtitlan** in central Mexico (around 1325, Aztec Empire Ch 1). The fourteenth century was extraordinarily eventful across most of Eurasia and beyond. The Sultanate's decline under the Tughlaqs is one regional manifestation of a much larger pattern of fourteenth-century crisis, climate shift, and political reorganization.

The Black Death itself probably reached parts of north India through the same Central Asian trade routes that brought it to Europe, but the documentation is poor and the demographic impact is debated. Barani and Ibn Battuta both record famines and epidemics in this period without specifically identifying the bubonic plague.

# Chapter 5 — The Timurid Sack and the Sayyid Hangover

In December 1398, **Timur** entered Delhi.

Timur — also called **Tamerlane** in European sources, a corruption of "Timur the Lame" (he had been wounded as a young man and walked with a permanent limp) — was the Turco-Mongol warlord from **Transoxiana** (modern Uzbekistan) who had built one of the most violent empires of the medieval world over the previous three decades. His campaigns through Persia, the Caucasus, the Volga, Central Asia, and Anatolia had produced massacres on a scale not seen since the original Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century: pyramids of skulls outside cities that resisted, populations exterminated, regions depopulated. Timur had defeated the Ilkhanid successor states (Mongol Empire Ch 7), invaded the Golden Horde, sacked **Damascus** and **Baghdad**, defeated the Mamluk Sultanate, and was about to defeat the Ottomans at **Ankara** in 1402 (Ottoman Empire Ch 3, where the Ottoman state nearly collapsed after this catastrophe).

He invaded India in late 1398 with a stated goal of converting the Hindu population by force and removing the lukewarm Tughlaq sultans who, in his theological reading, had been insufficiently rigorous in suppressing non-Islamic practice. (His chronicler, **Sharaf al-Din Ali Yazdi**, whose **Zafarnama** is the principal Persian source for the campaign, makes the religious-justification narrative explicit; modern historians read Timur's stated piety with appropriate skepticism, since his actual targets were almost as often Muslim cities he disagreed with as Hindu kingdoms he was nominally fighting.)

His army crossed the Indus in late September. The frontier garrisons were weak, the Punjab governors mostly fled or capitulated. By October, Timur was outside Multan, which surrendered. By late November, he was approaching Delhi. The Tughlaq sultan, **Mahmud Shah**, fielded an army of perhaps 40,000 outside Delhi at the village of **Loni** on December 17. Timur's veteran cavalry shattered them in a few hours. Mahmud Shah fled south to Gujarat; his vizier Mallu Iqbal Khan died on the field.

Timur entered Delhi unopposed on December 18.

---

The next three days are among the worst-documented atrocities in Indian history.

The basic outline is clear from multiple Persian sources, including Yazdi's Zafarnama and Timur's own account in his **Tuzuk-i Timuri** (which is partly autobiography, partly later compilation, but reflects what the Timurid court wanted recorded). Timur had originally promised the Delhi residents safety in exchange for tribute. His soldiers, once inside the city, broke discipline almost immediately. Looting began on December 19 and accelerated over the next two days. Mass killings began on December 20. By December 22, when Timur formally restored order, perhaps 100,000 Delhi residents — Hindu and Muslim, civilian and military — had been massacred. The Persian sources discuss the killings as an unfortunate breakdown of military discipline; the Hindi-language sources that survive treat them as deliberate atrocity.

Before retreating north on December 26, Timur had also reportedly ordered the mass execution of perhaps 100,000 prisoners — Hindu fighters his army had captured during the campaign south, who had been concentrated in a holding camp outside Delhi. The order was given on the grounds that the prisoners could not be safely taken back across the Indus and could not be released. The execution was carried out over a single day. The detail comes from Yazdi and from Timur's own memoir; modern historians generally accept it as approximately accurate, though the specific number is uncertain.

The total death toll of Timur's occupation of Delhi is impossible to determine precisely, but it was certainly in the hundreds of thousands. The Persian chronicles — sympathetic to Timur — suggest 200,000-plus dead. The conservative modern estimate (Yazdi and the contemporaries cannot be entirely trusted, but neither can they be entirely dismissed) is on the order of 100,000-200,000 killed across the city and the prisoner camp.

Timur left Delhi in late December 1398, marching back to Samarkand with vast caravans of looted treasure, captured artisans (the **Samarkand mosque** at the Timurid capital was built largely by craftsmen taken from Delhi), and the Sultanate's accumulated imperial regalia. He died at Otrar in Central Asia in 1405, on a campaign to invade China that never reached China. His empire fragmented among his descendants — the **Timurids** — who would hold central Asia and eastern Iran for the next century. **Babur**, the Timurid prince who would conquer Delhi in 1526, was Timur's great-great-great-grandson and traced his Indian conquest as a kind of ancestral revisitation of his great-great-great-grandfather's expedition.

---

Delhi after Timur was a city of ruins.

The Persian chronicles describe a population reduced by killing, enslavement, and flight. Most of the city's craftsmen had been deported to Samarkand. Most of the city's bureaucratic class had been killed or fled. The treasury was gone. The physical infrastructure of Firuzabad and Tughlaqabad was substantially damaged. The food economy had collapsed. Famine and plague struck the survivors over the following years. According to Yahya Sirhindi's chronicle (written in the early fifteenth century), the city was substantially depopulated for at least two years and did not return to its pre-Timur population until well into the fifteenth century.

The Tughlaq dynasty technically continued for another sixteen years, but it was a fiction. **Mahmud Shah** returned from Gujarat to a Delhi that no longer recognized his sovereignty in any practical way. The provincial governors — Gujarat, Malwa, Jaunpur, Bengal, the Punjab — declared full independence over the next decade. The Bahmani Sultanate consolidated its hold on the Deccan. Vijayanagara extended its reach across most of the south.

By 1414, when **Khizr Khan** (a Punjab governor who had originally been appointed by Timur as the Timurid representative for north India) captured Delhi from the last Tughlaq sultan, the territory the new "Sultanate" actually controlled consisted of Delhi and a few adjacent districts. Khizr Khan founded the **Sayyid dynasty** as nominal vassals of the Timurids. The Sayyids never controlled more than the territory immediately around Delhi. The dynasty ruled a city, not an empire.

The Sayyid dynasty (1414–1451) is the lowest point in the Sultanate's three-and-a-half centuries. Four Sayyid sultans — Khizr Khan, Mubarak Shah, Muhammad Shah, Alam Shah — ruled in succession, each more nominally than the last. Real power across north India belonged to the breakaway provincial sultanates and the Hindu kingdoms beyond them. The Sayyid sultans, lacking the resources to project military power, lacked the resources to do much of anything. They maintained an attenuated court at Delhi, paid tribute to the Timurids, and watched their notional empire shrink to a circle on the map perhaps fifty miles across.

---

While the Sayyid sultans were ruling Delhi as a city-state, the rest of the subcontinent was being ruled by other people.

The **Bengal Sultanate** under the **Ilyas Shahi** dynasty had been functionally independent since the 1340s and formally so since 1352. By the early fifteenth century, Bengal was one of the wealthiest Islamic states in the world, controlling the lower Ganges delta and the Indian Ocean trade routes through the port of **Chittagong**. The Bengal Sultanate became the major patron of Bengali-language literature (the great Bengali poet **Krittibas Ojha** translated the Ramayana into Bengali under Bengal Sultanate patronage in the fifteenth century), built the distinctive curved-roof mosque architecture of medieval Bengal (the **Adina Mosque** at Pandua, the largest mosque in pre-modern South Asia), and managed a relatively syncretic religious culture in which Hindu and Muslim populations coexisted under a Muslim aristocracy.

The **Jaunpur Sultanate** under the **Sharqi** (SHAR-kee, "eastern") dynasty had broken away in 1394, when Malik Sarwar (formerly a Tughlaq governor) declared independence in the eastern Ganges plain. Jaunpur (jown-POOR), on the Gomti river in modern eastern Uttar Pradesh, became one of the most culturally productive of the breakaway sultanates. The Sharqis built the distinctive **Atala Mosque** at Jaunpur, whose pylon-style central facade has no precedent in earlier Indian or Persian Islamic architecture, and patronized Hindavi-language poetry (the **Padmavat** of **Malik Muhammad Jayasi**, a Sufi-influenced romantic epic about the legendary Padmini of Chittor, was composed in this milieu).

The **Malwa Sultanate**, founded in 1392 by Dilawar Khan Ghuri (originally a Tughlaq officer of central India), built its capital at **Mandu** (MAHN-doo), one of the most spectacular fortified cities of medieval India, perched on a defensible plateau in the Vindhya hills. Mandu's **Jahaz Mahal** ("Ship Palace"), **Hindola Mahal** ("Swing Palace"), and the **Hoshang Shah's Tomb** (a marble-and-sandstone mausoleum that would directly inspire the Taj Mahal a century later) make Mandu one of the great architectural ensembles of the Sultanate-era subcontinent.

The **Gujarat Sultanate**, founded in 1407 by Zafar Khan Muzaffar, controlled the Indian Ocean trade routes through the port of **Cambay** (later **Surat**). Gujarat was the wealthiest of the breakaway sultanates, with revenues from the spice and indigo trade that exceeded those of any other late-Sultanate-era Indian state. Gujarat would survive as an independent Muslim kingdom until the Mughals annexed it in 1573. The **Champaner-Pavagadh** complex (UNESCO World Heritage, modern Gujarat) is one of the most complete late-medieval Islamic urban ensembles surviving in India.

The **Bahmani Sultanate**, founded in 1347, dominated the Deccan from its capitals **Gulbarga** (until 1432) and **Bidar** (after 1432). The Bahmani court was multicultural — Persian-speaking Sayyids and Iranian Shia immigrants competed for influence with the Sunni Turkic and indigenous Indian Muslim **Deccani** factions, and the resulting Sunni-Shia and immigrant-vs-local tensions would eventually fragment the empire. But at its peak under **Mahmud Gawan** (the great Persian-Iranian vizier who ran the empire from 1466 to 1481), Bahmani built the **Madrasa of Mahmud Gawan** at Bidar — among the largest medieval madrasas in the Islamic world, with a three-story facade of intricate Persian-style tile-work — and fielded armies that fought Vijayanagara to a standstill across the Tungabhadra frontier.

In the south, **Vijayanagara**, founded in 1336 by Harihara and Bukka, had grown into the dominant Hindu power of the subcontinent. By the early fifteenth century, Vijayanagara controlled most of southern India, from the Krishna river to Kanyakumari, and ruled a population perhaps three or four times the size of the Sayyid Sultanate's Delhi territory. The Vijayanagara capital — modern **Hampi** in Karnataka, now a vast UNESCO archaeological zone covering 16 square miles — was one of the largest cities in the world in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, with detailed eyewitness descriptions from European visitors (the Portuguese **Domingo Paes** and the Italian **Niccolò de' Conti**) that estimate populations of 500,000 or more. The empire would peak under **Krishnadevaraya** (ruled 1509–1529), the contemporary of Babur, before being catastrophically destroyed at the Battle of Talikota in 1565 by a coalition of the Deccan Sultanates.

The **Rajput** kingdoms of Mewar, Marwar, and Amber (Chapter 7) had reasserted themselves after Alauddin Khalji's reduction. The **Sisodia** rulers of **Mewar** at **Chittor** held off Sultanate and later Mughal pressure for another two centuries; the legendary **Rana Sanga** of Mewar would lead the Hindu coalition that opposed Babur after Panipat at the Battle of **Khanwa** in 1527 (a defeat that consolidated Babur's victory).

---

The geography is the point. By 1450, the political map of the Indian subcontinent was a patchwork of dynastic regional states, of which the Sayyid Sultanate at Delhi was one of the smaller and weaker. The very framework of "the Delhi Sultanate" as a pan-north-Indian imperial state had ceased to exist. What remained at Delhi was an administrative tradition, a Persianate court culture, and a memory of imperial greatness — but not the substance of imperial power.

---

In 1451, the last Sayyid sultan **Alam Shah** abdicated. He had effectively ceded actual authority over the Delhi region to **Bahlul Lodi** (BAH-lool LOH-dee), an Afghan chieftain from the Pashtun **Lodi** tribe who had been serving as the governor of **Sirhind** (SIR-hind, in the Punjab) and had built up a private army of Pashtun cavalry. Alam Shah retired to **Badaun** to live as a private gentleman; Bahlul Lodi entered Delhi in April 1451 and proclaimed himself Sultan.

The Lodi dynasty (1451–1526) was the first non-Turkic dynasty of the Sultanate. The Lodis were ethnic Afghans (Pashtuns), speaking Pashto in their family circles and Persian at court, with a tribal structure quite different from the Turkic mamluk system that had defined the Mamluk, Khalji, and Tughlaq dynasties. They will be the subject of Chapter 7.

But before we get to the Lodis, Chapter 6 covers the most consequential cultural development of the late Sultanate: the religious and cultural synthesis that produced Bhakti devotional poetry, Sufi mysticism, and the founding of Sikhism. That synthesis happened in parallel with everything Chapters 1-5 have covered, and it is what most ordinary inhabitants of Sultanate India actually experienced day-to-day, far more than the dynastic chronologies of the kings.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Sayyid dynasty was holding together a hollowed-out Sultanate (1414–1451), the **Ottomans** were recovering from Timur's defeat at Ankara and rebuilding under **Mehmed I** and **Murad II** (Ottoman Empire Ch 3 and Ch 4); **Mehmed II the Conqueror** would take Constantinople in 1453, ending the Byzantine Empire (Byzantine Empire Ch 12), shortly after the Sayyid period ended. The **Mali Empire** in West Africa was at the end of its peak, soon to be displaced by the **Songhai Empire**. The **Joseon Dynasty** ruled Korea under King **Sejong** (the inventor of Hangul, 1443). The **Ming Dynasty** in China was at the height of its naval-expansion phase under the **Yongle Emperor** and the great voyages of **Admiral Zheng He** (which reached as far as East Africa in 1417). The **Inca Empire** in the Andes was just consolidating under **Pachacuti**. The Sayyid Sultanate, in this fifteenth-century world, was a small kingdom remembered for being the residue of something that had once been great.

# Chapter 6 — The Bhakti and Sufi Revolution

The political chronicle of the Sultanate is a story of dynasties, sieges, and revolts. The cultural chronicle is something quite different.

While the Mamluks were fighting Mongols, the Khaljis were running price controls, and the Tughlaqs were moving their capital to Daulatabad and back, something more interesting was happening across north India at the level of village shrines, urban dargahs, and itinerant poets. A series of religious and literary movements — **Bhakti** devotionalism among the Hindu population, **Sufi** mysticism within the Muslim population, and the founding of **Sikhism** as a deliberately syncretic third tradition — was reshaping the religious and cultural texture of the subcontinent in ways that would outlast the Sultanate by centuries.

This chapter covers those movements. They are not the official chronicle. They were not what the court chroniclers wrote about. But they are the most consequential thing the Sultanate-era Indian population produced, and they shape modern Indian religious life in ways the Sultanate's military campaigns never did.

---

To understand what happened, we have to start with **Sufism** in India.

Sufism (SOO-fizm) is the mystical and devotional tradition within Islam (Safavid Persia Ch 1 covers the same tradition in its Iranian context). Sufi orders, called **tariqas** (tah-REE-kahs, literally "paths"), formed around lineages of spiritual masters and emphasized inner spiritual experience, the cultivation of the soul, and the master-disciple relationship as the route to direct knowledge of God. Each tariqa had its own characteristic practices: meditative chanting, breathing techniques, sometimes ecstatic music or dance.

Sufism arrived in India with the Ghurid armies and spread rapidly through the early Sultanate. The four major orders that took root were the **Chishti** (CHISH-tee), the **Suhrawardi** (soo-rah-WAR-dee), the **Naqshbandi** (NUKSH-bun-dee), and the **Qadiri** (KAH-dee-ree). Each had a different geographic center and a slightly different theological flavor. The Chishti — austere, charitable, deliberately distanced from royal courts, focused on individual devotion and music as a path to the divine — became the most influential, particularly in north India.

The founder of the Chishti order in India was **Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti** (MOY-ee-nud-DEEN), originally from Sistan in eastern Iran, who settled at **Ajmer** (AJ-meer) in Rajasthan around 1192 — within months of Muhammad of Ghor's victory at Tarain. Moinuddin spent the next four decades teaching Sufi devotion at Ajmer, accepting disciples from every social class (including, unusually for the period, both Hindu and Muslim followers), and emphasizing direct service to the poor as the practical content of religious life. He died at Ajmer in 1236, the same year Iltutmish died at Delhi. His tomb, the **Dargah Sharif of Ajmer**, became and remains the most visited Sufi shrine on the subcontinent. Modern pilgrimage statistics put it at roughly four million visitors a year, drawn from every faith tradition. Pakistani Muslim pilgrims who cannot get visas to attend in person send written prayers through the mail. The dargah's annual **Urs** festival, marking the anniversary of Moinuddin's death, has been celebrated continuously for nearly eight hundred years.

The Chishti tradition Moinuddin founded is what makes Indian Sufism distinct from its Arab and Persian counterparts. The Chishtis were unusually open to Hindu participation: their **sama** (devotional music) sessions could include musical forms with Hindu roots, their teaching language was Persian and Hindavi rather than Arabic, their dargahs welcomed pilgrims of any religion, and their practical emphasis on charity and service rather than theological orthodoxy made them accessible to the predominantly Hindu population around them. By the fourteenth century, Chishti dargahs across north India were major institutions of religious, cultural, and economic life, attracting pilgrim donations sufficient to fund extensive charitable infrastructure (free kitchens, lodging for travelers, support for the poor).

---

The most influential of the Chishti masters was **Sheikh Nizamuddin Auliya** (nih-ZAHM-ud-DEEN ow-LEE-ah, 1238–1325).

Nizamuddin spent his entire long career in Delhi. His **khanqah** (KHAN-kah, Sufi monastery and teaching center) at the village of **Ghiyaspur** outside Delhi attracted disciples from every social class — Hindu artisans, Muslim merchants, members of the Sultanate's military aristocracy, immigrant Persian scholars, and ordinary villagers. His disciples included some of the most important figures of fourteenth-century India: the poet Amir Khusrau (more on him shortly), the historian Ziauddin Barani, the chronicler Sayyid Muhammad Mubarak, and a long line of Chishti successors who carried his teachings to other Indian regions.

His teachings emphasized **universal love**, the **equality of all human beings before God**, and the **uselessness of theological dispute compared to direct devotion**. He systematically refused to attend the Sultanate court, even when summoned by the most powerful sultans. He famously remarked, when warned that Sultan Ghiyas-ud-din Tughlaq was approaching Delhi from Bengal and might compel him to court appearance, "**Hanuz Dilli dur ast**" — "Delhi is still far off" — a Persian phrase that became a permanent piece of Indian proverbial wisdom (the sultan died in the wooden pavilion accident before reaching Delhi, validating Nizamuddin's calm). The phrase is now used in Hindi and Urdu to mean any distant, possibly never-arriving fate.

His **dargah** in modern Delhi — Nizamuddin Dargah, in the Nizamuddin neighborhood of central Delhi, one stop on the metro from the **Khan Market** if you visit — is still a working pilgrimage site, with a continuous tradition of qawwali performance every Thursday evening for the past seven hundred years. The dargah's **Sufi qawwali** is the same musical tradition Amir Khusrau is credited with developing in the late thirteenth century. Modern qawwali singers — from **Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan** (the Pakistani master who brought qawwali to global audiences in the 1980s) back through the **Sabri Brothers**, the **Warsi Brothers**, and the unbroken traditional lineages of Delhi-Lahore-Multan — perform in a tradition continuously practiced at this dargah and a handful of others since Nizamuddin Auliya himself was alive.

---

**Amir Khusrau** (uh-MEER khus-ROW, 1253–1325) is the most prolific writer of the Sultanate period and one of the most influential cultural figures in Indian history.

Khusrau was born in **Patiali** in modern Uttar Pradesh, the son of a Turkic Lachin officer in the Sultanate army and a Rajput Hindu mother (a fact he proudly mentioned in his autobiographical writings, presenting himself as the perfect synthesis of the Sultanate's two heritages). He was educated in Persian, Arabic, and Hindavi, and entered court service as a young man under Balban. Over the next fifty years, he served as court poet to seven successive sultans — Balban, Kayqubad, Jalaluddin Khalji, Alauddin Khalji, Mubarak Shah Khalji, Khusrau Khan, and Ghiyas-ud-din Tughlaq — making him the longest-serving court figure of the Sultanate period and the only person to have personally witnessed the entire arc from Balban's iron rule through the Tughlaq founding. He died in 1325, six months after his Sufi master Nizamuddin Auliya, and is buried at the Nizamuddin Dargah in a small tomb adjacent to his master.

Khusrau's literary output is extraordinary. He wrote poetry in Persian (his primary literary language), Arabic, Turkish, and Hindavi (the early form of Hindi-Urdu emerging in the Sultanate's bazaars). His Persian dīwāns total something like half a million verses. His **Khamsa** ("Quintet," five long narrative poems modeled on the Persian **Nizami Ganjavi**'s earlier Khamsa) is one of the great works of Indo-Persian literature. His **Tughluq Nama** chronicles the rise of the Tughlaq dynasty in verse. His **Nuh Sipihr** ("Nine Heavens") is a paean to India and Indian civilization that explicitly argues the superiority of Indian intellectual achievements (mathematics, astronomy, music, language, climate, fauna) to those of the rest of the Islamic world — written by a Muslim Persian-language poet who had never traveled outside India and considered himself fundamentally an Indian writer.

In **Hindavi**, Khusrau is credited with composing some of the earliest surviving examples of what would become Hindi-Urdu poetry. His short verses — quatrains, riddles, work songs, devotional fragments — entered the popular oral tradition and survived in folk memory across north India. Whether Khusrau actually composed all of the verses attributed to him or whether some were added later in his name (the medieval Hindavi tradition was loose about authorship) is debated. What is uncontested is that the basic literary register of the early Hindavi tradition — playful, devotional, vernacular, mixing Persian and Sanskrit-derived vocabulary in approximately equal measure — was established in Khusrau's circle.

In **music**, Khusrau is traditionally credited with developing several **raga** (RAH-gah, the Indian melodic mode) traditions and inventing several musical forms including the **qawwali** (Sufi devotional song with a leader-and-chorus structure, percussion accompaniment, and rising emotional intensity that has been the standard format of Indian and Pakistani Sufi music for seven centuries). The traditional attribution also credits him with inventing the **sitar** (SIT-ar, the long-necked plucked stringed instrument) and the **tabla** (TAH-blah, the paired hand drums). The musicological evidence for these specific instrument-invention attributions is weak — both instruments probably emerged in their modern forms several centuries after Khusrau's death — but the broader claim that Khusrau and his Chishti circle were central to the development of north Indian classical music is well-supported. Modern Hindustani classical music (the north Indian classical tradition) traces much of its lineage through Khusrau and the Chishti khanqahs of Sultanate-era Delhi.

The combination of Khusrau's Persian literary output, his Hindavi vernacular contributions, and his musical innovations made him the central cultural figure of his period and the reason the Sultanate produced a distinct **Indo-Persian** cultural synthesis rather than a simple imposition of Persian forms on an Indian setting. The synthesis was Khusrau's lifework. Modern Indian and Pakistani writers, musicians, and intellectuals across the Hindu-Muslim divide alike claim him as a foundational figure.

---

The **Bhakti** (BHUK-tee) movement was the parallel development on the Hindu side.

Bhakti — the term means "devotion" in Sanskrit — had begun in the Tamil south centuries before the Sultanate (Medieval India Ch 6 covers the original Tamil **Alvar** and **Nayanar** traditions of the seventh through tenth centuries). The Tamil Bhakti tradition emphasized direct personal devotion to a chosen deity (Vishnu, Shiva, or Devi), expressed through vernacular poetry composed in Tamil rather than Sanskrit, accessible to all castes including the very lowest and to women — a deliberate egalitarianism that contrasted sharply with the Brahmin-mediated, Sanskrit-based, ritual-centric mainstream Vedic-Puranic tradition.

What happened in the Sultanate period was the spread of this Tamil-originated Bhakti tradition across northern India, in different vernacular languages — Hindavi-Hindi, Marathi, Bengali, Punjabi — and with new theological and social emphases that responded to the Sultanate's particular religious environment. The northern Bhakti tradition produced some of the most influential poetry in any Indian language and reshaped Hindu religious practice in ways still visible today.

The major figures of the northern Bhakti movement include **Kabir** (kah-BEER, ~1440-1518), **Ravidas** (rah-vee-DAHS, ~1450-1520), **Surdas** (sur-DAHS, ~1478-1583), **Mirabai** (MEE-rah-bye, ~1498-1547), **Tulsidas** (tul-see-DAHS, ~1532-1623), and **Guru Nanak** (NAH-nahk, 1469-1539, the founder of Sikhism). They are not a unified school. They wrote in different languages, lived in different regions, addressed different deities, and developed different theological positions. But they share recognizable features that distinguish the northern Bhakti tradition from earlier Indian devotionalism.

---

**Kabir** is the most striking of them.

Kabir was a weaver — a member of the **Julaha** caste, low-status weavers of Banaras (modern Varanasi, the great Hindu pilgrimage city on the Ganges, Medieval India Ch 6). His parentage is uncertain: most accounts say he was raised by Muslim weavers but was probably born to a Hindu Brahmin widow who left him as an infant. He lived his entire life as a working weaver, never adopted formal religious vows, and produced poetry in plain Hindavi that mocked both Hindu and Muslim religious orthodoxy.

His verses are some of the most ferocious anti-clerical poetry in any tradition. He attacked the Hindu Brahmin priesthood for their caste-based exclusionary rituals, the Hindu temple establishment for confusing image-worship with God-worship, the Muslim ulama for insisting on the superiority of Arabic religious language, the Sufi orders for their veneration of saint-tombs as spiritually identical to idolatry. His positive theology was simple: there is one formless God, accessible directly through inward devotion to the divine name (he called it **Ram**, but explicitly meant the term as a generic name for God rather than as a specific reference to the Hindu deity), beyond all the institutional religions and their boundary-drawing.

The verses themselves are short, blunt, and oral. Kabir spoke (or sang) them to whoever would listen; they were passed on by his disciples and assembled into manuscript collections only generations after his death. The major collections are the **Bijak** (the Kabir Panth canonical text), the **Sant Kabir Granth**, and the verses included in the Sikh **Adi Granth** (Guru Nanak and his successors compiled the first authoritative collection of Kabir's verse alongside their own). The verses were also borrowed extensively into the Hindi-language Bhakti tradition; Kabir's two-line **dohas** (DOH-hahs, a couplet form) became part of the Hindi devotional vernacular and are still recited in modern north India.

His most famous verses include lines that are now Hindi-Urdu folk wisdom:

- "If kahte ho ki Ram hamare, kis ne dekha tum sang dekho?" — "If you say that Ram is yours, who has seen him keeping company with you?"
- "Pothi padh padh jag mua, pandit bhayo na koy / Dhai akhar prem ka, padhe so pandit hoy" — "The whole world has died reading book after book; nobody has become a true scholar / But two-and-a-half letters of love, whoever reads those becomes a scholar."

The two-and-a-half letters are the Devanagari letters that spell **prem** (love). The pun works in Hindi but not in translation. Kabir's verses are full of these multi-layered jokes that work in Hindavi and refuse to work in any language Kabir was not actually writing in. The fact that they have remained in continuous oral tradition for five centuries is part of what makes him so influential.

Kabir's death is also important to mention. The standard Hindu and Muslim accounts both claim him after his death. The legendary story goes: when Kabir died at Maghar (a small village in Uttar Pradesh) in 1518, his Hindu disciples wanted to cremate him according to Hindu rites, and his Muslim disciples wanted to bury him according to Islamic rites. They fought outside the room where his body lay. When they finally entered the room, his body was gone — only flowers remained. They divided the flowers and each community performed its rites accordingly. The Maghar village contains both a Hindu cremation memorial and a Muslim grave for Kabir, side by side, both still active pilgrimage sites today.

The story is almost certainly invented. But it captures something the historical Kabir would have appreciated about the foolishness of religious boundary-drawing.

---

**Ravidas**, contemporary of Kabir and another Banaras weaver (specifically a member of the **Chamar** caste, leather-workers, ranking even lower than the Julaha Kabir came from — Chamars would later be classified as **Dalit** under modern caste-classification systems), wrote Bhakti devotional poetry in plain Hindavi that addressed God directly and explicitly rejected the legitimacy of caste hierarchy. His verses are sung as devotional hymns and are included in the Sikh Adi Granth.

**Mirabai**, the Rajput princess of the Mewar royal family, abandoned her marital obligations as a Sisodia queen to write Krishna-devotional poetry that would become some of the most beloved Hindi devotional verse ever written. Her songs, addressed to Krishna as her divine lover, reframed Bhakti devotion through unmistakably female-voiced longing — explicit in their bodily and emotional vocabulary, refusing to soften the romantic-erotic language that Krishna devotion in the **Bhagavata Purana** tradition had always made central. Mirabai's songs are still sung as devotional standards across north India and have been recorded thousands of times by classical and popular musicians.

**Surdas** and **Tulsidas**, slightly later contemporaries, took the Bhakti tradition in a more textually disciplined direction. Surdas (a blind poet associated with the Krishna-devotional **Pushtimarg** sect of Vallabha) composed thousands of Hindi devotional songs about the childhood of Krishna at Vrindavan that became core liturgical material for the Krishna-worshipping branches of Vaishnavism. Tulsidas's **Ramcharitmanas** ("Lake of the Deeds of Ram"), completed in 1574 in the **Awadhi** dialect of Hindi, retold the Ramayana epic in vernacular Hindi for an audience that did not read Sanskrit. The Ramcharitmanas became the most widely recited religious text in modern Hindi and is the basis of every popular contemporary retelling of the Ramayana, including the famous 1980s television serial that briefly held a global record for largest Indian-language television audience.

The northern Bhakti tradition was, collectively, the largest single transformation of Hindu religious practice between the medieval period and the modern period. It moved devotional life from Sanskrit-mediated Brahmin-controlled temple ritual to vernacular-language individual devotional practice accessible to all castes and both genders. It gave the Hindu tradition a popular literature in every major north Indian language. And it provided much of the cultural material that would later be deployed by Hindu reform movements (the **Brahmo Samaj**, the **Arya Samaj**, the various Vaishnava reform movements) and by the Hindu side of Indian nationalism.

---

In the late Sultanate, in the Punjab, the Bhakti and Sufi traditions met in the person of **Guru Nanak** (NAH-nahk, 1469–1539) and produced something genuinely new: the religion that became **Sikhism**.

Nanak was born in a small Punjabi village called **Talwandi** (now Nankana Sahib in Pakistan) in 1469, into a Hindu Khatri (mercantile-caste) family. He was, by all the accounts assembled in the **Janamsakhis** (the early Sikh hagiographies), an unusual child from the start: spiritually precocious, indifferent to his father's mercantile ambitions, drawn instead to long meditative walks, conversations with both Hindu sadhus and Muslim Sufi pirs, and a kind of inward contemplation that none of the local religious institutions seemed to satisfy.

The pivotal moment of his biography came around age thirty, when (according to the standard Sikh tradition) he disappeared into the river at Sultanpur for three days, was presumed drowned, and re-emerged with a single declaration: "**Na koi Hindu, na Musalman**" — "There is no Hindu, no Muslim." The phrase was a rejection of religious boundary-drawing. What he proposed instead was a single direct devotional path to the one formless God (whom he called **Ek Onkar**, "the One"), accessible through honest work, charitable sharing, and meditative remembrance of the divine name.

Over the next thirty years, Nanak traveled extensively across the subcontinent and beyond — to Mecca, Baghdad, Tibet, Sri Lanka, Bengal, the Deccan — accompanied by his Muslim companion **Mardana** (a rebab-player who provided musical accompaniment for Nanak's devotional songs). The travels accumulated stories, encounters with religious figures of every tradition, and a body of verse that Nanak composed in a Punjabi that combined Sufi-inflected vocabulary with Bhakti devotional themes and his own distinct theological vision.

When he returned to settle at the village of **Kartarpur** (modern Pakistan) in his sixties, Nanak founded the first Sikh **sangat** (congregation) on three principles that would define the religion: **kirat karo** (honest work), **vand chhako** (sharing with the community, manifested in the practice of the **langar** or free communal kitchen open to all regardless of caste or religion), and **naam japo** (meditative remembrance of the divine name). Before his death in 1539, he formally appointed his disciple **Bhai Lehna** (renamed **Guru Angad**) as his successor — the first of nine successor Gurus that would lead the Sikh community through the Mughal period.

The Sikh tradition Nanak founded is genuinely new, drawing on but distinct from both its parents. It rejects caste hierarchy, image-worship, and clerical mediation (all Bhakti positions); it rejects ritual law, prophetic exclusivism, and saint-shrine veneration (all Sufi positions). It accepts (in modified form) the Sufi emphasis on the divine name as the central spiritual practice. It accepts the Bhakti emphasis on vernacular devotion and the equality of devotees. And it adds a distinctive social-ethical dimension — the langar, the egalitarian community structure, the explicit rejection of caste — that became the Sikh contribution to Indian religious history.

The Sikh community Nanak founded would grow steadily through the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, develop a militarized identity under the tenth and final guru **Gobind Singh** (who founded the Khalsa in 1699), become a major political force in the eighteenth-century Punjab under various Sikh **misls** (confederations), establish the **Sikh Empire** of **Maharaja Ranjit Singh** in the early nineteenth century, and emerge as one of the principal religious communities of modern South Asia. Modern Sikhism counts perhaps 30 million adherents worldwide — a small religion by global standards, but the fifth-largest organized religion in the world and one of the very few major religious traditions to have been founded in the late medieval period in a setting where its origins are well documented.

That well-documented origin is the cultural moment of the late Sultanate. Nanak was born in 1469, ten years into the Lodi dynasty (Chapter 7), and died in 1539, thirteen years after Babur's victory at Panipat. He lived the Sultanate's last decades and the Mughal Empire's first. The religion he founded is a direct synthetic product of the Indo-Islamic cultural environment the Sultanate had built.

---

The Sultanate also produced, alongside Sufism and Bhakti, a third literary-cultural development: the emergence of **Hindavi** as a written literary language in its own right.

Hindavi (HIN-duh-vee — the term comes from "Hindi" plus the Persian -vi suffix, meaning "of the Hindi people") was the contact language that developed in the bazaars and barracks of Sultanate-era Delhi. Persian-speaking soldiers, administrators, and merchants from Central Asia interacted daily with Hindi-speaking Indian subjects and had to develop a working language somewhere in between. The language that emerged drew on local Indian (Khariboli, the dialect spoken around Delhi) for its grammatical structure and its core vocabulary, on Persian and Arabic for administrative-political-military-religious vocabulary, and on Sanskrit for philosophical-religious-literary vocabulary. The result was a flexible, accessible street-and-court language in which Persian-Arabic and Sanskrit-Hindi vocabulary could be mixed at will depending on the speaker's audience and purpose.

By the late Sultanate, Hindavi was being written down for literary purposes. Amir Khusrau's Hindavi verses are the earliest substantial surviving examples. Kabir's dohas were composed in Hindavi (or in dialectical variants — Kabir's language has been variously classified as old Awadhi, Bhojpuri, Khariboli, or Sadhukkari, the so-called "saint-language" of the Bhakti tradition). Surdas wrote in Braj Bhasha (the dialect of the Mathura-Vrindavan region, close to but distinct from Khariboli Hindi), Tulsidas wrote in Awadhi (the dialect of Awadh, the central Uttar Pradesh region). All of these are recognizably forms of medieval Hindi.

By the end of the Sultanate, Hindavi had clearly differentiated into the family of related Hindi dialects that would later be standardized as modern Hindi, and was simultaneously absorbing enough Persian and Arabic vocabulary that, by the Mughal period, it would be called **Rekhta** ("mixed") and then eventually **Urdu** (a name from the Turkish word for "army camp," reflecting the bazaar-and-barracks origins). The split between modern Hindi (with more Sanskrit-derived vocabulary) and modern Urdu (with more Persian-Arabic-derived vocabulary) is essentially a nineteenth-century development that came out of colonial and nationalist politics; in the Sultanate period and through most of the Mughal period, Hindavi-Rekhta was a single fluid language with regional and stylistic variations.

The cultural significance of this is hard to overstate. The Sultanate's cosmopolitan Persian-speaking court culture (Khusrau's Persian poetry, Barani's Persian chronicles) coexisted with the rapid emergence of vernacular Hindavi literature (Kabir's dohas, Surdas's bhajans, Mirabai's pads, eventually Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas) at the popular level. The two registers fed into each other. The Sufi qawwali tradition combined Persian and Hindavi verse in the same musical performances. The Bhakti devotional tradition borrowed Sufi vocabulary at the level of its key concepts (the **sant**, the saint-poet figure, is in part a Hindavi-language analogue of the Sufi **pir**). The Indo-Islamic architectural tradition borrowed Hindu craftsmanship at the level of its decorative vocabulary while retaining Persian structural forms.

What was produced, across the Sultanate's three centuries, was a genuinely composite Indo-Islamic culture that was not Persian, not Arab, not classical Sanskrit-Indian, and not simply a sum of any pair of those. It was something new. The Mughal Empire that succeeded the Sultanate at Panipat would inherit this composite culture and develop it further. Modern Indian and Pakistani culture, in their religious, linguistic, musical, architectural, and culinary dimensions, are still substantially shaped by what the Sultanate created.

The official chronicle is the dynasties. The actual chronicle, for most people in late Sultanate India, was the dargah, the bhajan, the qawwali, and the new vernacular literary tradition that was making itself visible in the bazaars. That second chronicle is what made the Sultanate culturally consequential.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Bhakti and Sufi syntheses were unfolding in India, broadly comparable popular religious movements were happening across Eurasia. The **Devotio Moderna** in northern Europe (the **Brethren of the Common Life**, the **Imitation of Christ** of Thomas a Kempis, late fourteenth and fifteenth centuries) emphasized inward personal devotion over institutional religion in ways structurally similar to Kabir and Bhakti. The **Hesychast** movement in Byzantine Eastern Orthodoxy emphasized contemplative inner prayer (the Jesus Prayer) in ways structurally similar to Sufi dhikr (Byzantine Empire Ch 11). The **Lutheran Reformation** (1517) exploded into Europe two years before Kabir's death, with theological and sociological dimensions that paralleled the Bhakti rejection of clerical mediation. The fifteenth century was, across Eurasia, a period when the institutional religious establishments of every tradition were being challenged by popular devotional movements that emphasized direct personal access to the divine. The Indian variants are part of this larger pattern, though they emerged independently rather than under any cross-pollination.

# Chapter 7 — The Lodi Afghans and the Provincial Sultanates

The Lodi dynasty (1451–1526) was the last dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate. Three Lodi sultans — **Bahlul** (BAH-lool), **Sikandar** (sih-KAN-dar), and **Ibrahim** (ih-brah-HEEM) — ruled over a partial recovery of Sultanate authority before the final collapse at Panipat. They were also the first non-Turkic dynasty of the Sultanate: Afghans, Pashto-speakers from the Suleiman mountains of the Pakistan-Afghanistan borderland, with a tribal political structure that operated quite differently from the Turkic mamluk system that had defined the previous dynasties.

The Lodis came in slowly, ruled cautiously, and went out fast.

---

**Bahlul Lodi** had been a Pashtun chief of the **Lodi tribe**, governor of **Sirhind** (in the Punjab) under the late Sayyid sultans, and the head of a private army of perhaps 30,000 Pashtun cavalry that he had built up over years of frontier service. When the last Sayyid sultan **Alam Shah** abdicated in 1451, Bahlul moved into Delhi unopposed, was acclaimed by the Delhi nobility, and began the slow process of rebuilding Sultanate authority over the Doab and the eastern Punjab.

Bahlul ruled differently from the Turkic sultans who had preceded him. His political model was not the centralized autocratic Persianate court of Balban or Alauddin; it was the **Afghan tribal confederation**. He treated his fellow Pashtun nobles as something close to peers — first-among-equals rather than slaves of the throne — sitting them on equivalent platforms at court audiences, sharing decision-making with a council of senior chiefs, and refusing the elaborate prostration ceremonies that earlier sultans had insisted on. The political culture of the Lodi dynasty was, in important respects, more egalitarian than its predecessors had been (within the Pashtun warrior class, that is — outside it, very little changed).

Bahlul's main political project was the reabsorption of the **Sharqi Sultanate of Jaunpur** into the Delhi orbit. Jaunpur had broken away in 1394 under Malik Sarwar and had grown into one of the major fifteenth-century Indian states. The Sharqi sultans had refused to acknowledge Delhi's authority. Bahlul spent most of his thirty-eight-year reign in intermittent warfare with Jaunpur, finally defeating the last Sharqi sultan **Husayn Shah** decisively in 1486 and reincorporating Jaunpur's territory into the Sultanate. The campaign expanded the Sultanate's effective reach back into the eastern Doab and Bihar, recovering territory lost since Timur's invasion almost a century earlier.

He died in 1489 at perhaps eighty years old, leaving the throne to his son **Sikandar Lodi**.

---

**Sikandar Lodi** (ruled 1489–1517) was probably the most capable Lodi sultan and the figure who transformed the Lodi state from a tribal confederation into something more like a centralized monarchy.

Sikandar was the son of Bahlul and a Hindu Khatri mother — the same kind of mixed-religion-and-ethnicity background that Amir Khusrau had had two centuries earlier. He grew up bilingual in Persian and Hindavi, took the throne at perhaps thirty years old, and ruled for twenty-eight years.

His major achievements were administrative and territorial. He continued the reabsorption of breakaway territories: parts of Bihar and the eastern Doab that the Sharqis had held, sections of the central Doab that had been independent under various local strongmen, the lower Ganges plain. He extended Sultanate authority into northern Bihar and made gestures toward influencing Bengal (without being able to actually conquer it). He systematized the Sultanate's revenue administration, restoring something like the iqta system that had operated under earlier dynasties, and rebuilt the central treasury that Timur had destroyed.

His most consequential decision was urban. In 1504, he founded the new city of **Agra** (AH-grah), on the Yamuna about 130 miles south of Delhi, as a forward administrative capital that would allow him to govern the Doab region more closely than was possible from Delhi. The site he chose — on the southern bank of the Yamuna, on a strategic crossing — would become one of the most important cities in subsequent Indian history. **Akbar** would build the Agra Fort there in the 1560s. **Shah Jahan** would build the **Taj Mahal** there in the 1630s. The Mughal capital would oscillate between Delhi and Agra for the next two centuries. Modern Agra, as one of India's major tourist cities, owes its founding to Sikandar Lodi.

Sikandar was also a literary patron and a poet himself. He wrote Persian verse under the pen-name **Gulrukhi** ("Rose-faced") and patronized a circle of Persian poets at his Agra court. He sponsored translations of Sanskrit texts into Persian (a project that would become much more substantial under Akbar). He commissioned the construction of new mosques, madrasas, and libraries across his territory.

He died in 1517 at perhaps fifty-eight years old, having held the Lodi state together for nearly three decades and having significantly expanded its actual administrative reach.

His son and successor was **Ibrahim Lodi**.

---

**Ibrahim Lodi** (ruled 1517–1526) was the last Sultan of Delhi.

Ibrahim was the eldest of Sikandar's sons. He took the throne at perhaps thirty years old and ruled for nine years — long enough to alienate his nobility, lose major battles, and finally lose his life and his throne to a Mughal prince from Kabul.

The basic problem of Ibrahim's reign was political. He inherited from his father a nobility that operated on the older Lodi tribal-confederation model — Pashtun chiefs treated as relative peers, with significant autonomy in their own provinces. Ibrahim attempted to centralize, demanding the kind of formal subordination that the Mamluk and Khalji and Tughlaq sultans had received from their nobles. The Pashtun chiefs took this as an insult and increasingly resisted. Several major nobles were imprisoned or executed for insufficient deference. Others fled to provincial governorships and began behaving as independent rulers. The Lodi state Ibrahim had inherited as a relatively coherent confederation became, under his rule, a sequence of internal conflicts and rebellions.

The most consequential of these conflicts was with his uncle **Daulat Khan Lodi**, the governor of the Punjab. Daulat Khan had supported a different Lodi prince in the 1517 succession dispute and had never reconciled with Ibrahim. By 1523, he was in open rebellion against Delhi from his power base at Lahore. Lacking the military strength to defeat Ibrahim alone, Daulat Khan made the consequential decision to invite an outside power into north India: **Babur**, the Timurid prince ruling from Kabul.

Babur had been raiding the Punjab from Kabul for several years. He had captured Lahore briefly in 1524. Daulat Khan's invitation gave Babur the political cover to mount a full invasion. In late 1525, Babur crossed the Indus with an army of perhaps 12,000 well-equipped troops including Ottoman-trained matchlock musketeers and Turkish field artillery — the first time gunpowder weapons in significant numbers had been deployed on the Indian battlefield. By February 1526, he was approaching Delhi.

What happened next was the **First Battle of Panipat** (April 21, 1526), and what happened after that was the Mughal Empire. Both are the subject of Chapter 8.

But before we leave the Lodis, we should pause on what the Sultanate as a whole had become by the time of its end.

---

The Sultanate that Ibrahim Lodi inherited was geographically much smaller than the one Iltutmish had built. The territory directly under Lodi administration in 1517 consisted of the Doab, parts of eastern Punjab, parts of western Bihar, and a few central Indian districts. Bengal, Gujarat, Malwa, Sindh, the Bahmani successor states (now five of them, after the Bahmani fragmentation), Mewar and the other Rajput states, Vijayanagara, and the various smaller principalities of the south and the Deccan were all independent.

What this means, structurally, is that the political pattern of the late Sultanate was a pattern of **regional sultanates**, of which Delhi was just one. The label "Delhi Sultanate" preserves the name of the founding dynasty but understates how thoroughly the political map had reorganized into multiple roughly equal regional states. Bengal under the late Hussain Shahi sultans was at least as wealthy as Delhi under the Lodis. Gujarat under the Muzaffarids was probably wealthier (because of its Indian Ocean trade revenues). Mandu under the Khaljis of Malwa rivaled Delhi in cultural sophistication. Vijayanagara at its peak under Krishnadevaraya was unambiguously the most populous, wealthiest, and militarily strongest single state in the subcontinent — significantly bigger than the Lodi Sultanate at its peak.

The cultural and architectural legacies of these provincial sultanates are, collectively, more extensive than the Lodi Sultanate's own. The **Adina Mosque** at Pandua (Bengal), the **Atala Mosque** at Jaunpur, the **Jahaz Mahal** and **Hoshang Shah's Tomb** at Mandu, the **Champaner-Pavagadh** complex in Gujarat, the **Jami Mosque** and **Madrasa of Mahmud Gawan** at Bidar (Bahmani), and the temple complex at Hampi (Vijayanagara) collectively produced more lasting architectural monuments than the Lodi sultans built at Delhi. The Lodi Sultanate's own architectural contribution was the **Lodi Tombs and Gardens** in modern Delhi: the octagonal tombs of Sikandar Lodi and Muhammad Shah Sayyid in the modern Lodi Gardens park, with their distinctive double-domed construction and pierced-stone lattice screens. The Mughals would refine this octagonal-tomb pattern into the **Humayun's Tomb** (1565) and ultimately the **Taj Mahal** (1648), so the Lodi tombs are architecturally important as the immediate ancestors of Mughal monumental tomb architecture. But by themselves they are not on the scale of what the provincial sultanates produced.

The literary and religious figures of the late Sultanate were dispersed similarly across the regional states. The Bhakti poets — Kabir at Banaras, Mirabai at Mewar (Rajasthan), Surdas at Mathura — operated in regions outside direct Lodi control. The Sufi dargahs — Ajmer Sharif in Rajasthan, Nizamuddin Dargah in Delhi, Bahauddin Zakariya's khanqah at Multan — were spread across the entire late-Sultanate political map. Guru Nanak founded Sikhism in the western Punjab outside any Lodi administrative reach. Amir Khusrau's literary tradition flowed through generations of Persian-language poets at courts across the entire fragmented landscape.

The point is that the religious and cultural synthesis of Sultanate India was a pan-Indian phenomenon by 1500, not a Delhi-centered one. The Lodis ruled a regional state with significant cultural and political achievements, but not a culturally hegemonic one. When the Mughals arrived in 1526, they were inheriting not a single coherent empire but a constellation of regional states, religious networks, cultural traditions, and economic systems that would take a century to reorganize into the coherent Mughal political-cultural order.

The Mughals would inherit the framework. The provincial sultanates would, mostly, be conquered and absorbed. The Bhakti and Sufi traditions would continue. The Indo-Islamic architectural synthesis would develop further. The Hindavi-Rekhta language would develop further. What was changing in 1526 was the dynastic top of the political pyramid. What was not changing was the underlying religious, cultural, linguistic, and economic fabric that the Sultanate's three centuries had woven.

---

A pause to acknowledge the **Vijayanagara** alternative.

The southern Hindu empire, founded in 1336 in direct opposition to the Sultanate's southern projection, deserves more attention than this chapter has been able to give it. Vijayanagara reached its peak under **Krishnadevaraya** (kreesh-nuh-DEH-vuh-RAH-yuh, ruled 1509–1529) — roughly contemporary with Sikandar Lodi and the early Babur. The empire controlled most of southern India south of the Krishna river, ruled from a capital city (modern **Hampi**) that European visitors estimated at 500,000 inhabitants, fielded armies of perhaps 700,000 troops at full mobilization, and conducted Indian Ocean trade through the ports of the Malabar coast. Vijayanagara was, by every measure of population, wealth, military strength, and territorial extent, the largest single state in the Indian subcontinent during the late Sultanate period.

Krishnadevaraya himself was an extraordinary figure: a Telugu-speaking soldier-king who patronized literature in Telugu, Sanskrit, Kannada, and Tamil simultaneously, built the **Vitthala** and **Krishna** temples at Hampi (the **Stone Chariot** of Vitthala is one of the most photographed monuments of southern India), wrote a Telugu-language treatise on statecraft (the **Amuktamalyada**), and personally commanded armies that defeated the Bahmani successor sultanates at the **Battle of Raichur** in 1520 (which is conventionally dated as Krishnadevaraya's high point of imperial achievement).

The Vijayanagara story is partially the subject of Medieval India Ch 6 and would have been a substantial part of the Vijayanagara Sultanate TL had we written one. For the purposes of the Delhi Sultanate narrative, the important point is that the southern Hindu empire was, throughout the Sultanate's late period, demonstrating that a centralized Hindu state of much larger scale than anything north India had produced was possible. Vijayanagara's existence is one of the central facts of fifteenth- and sixteenth-century Indian history. Its destruction at the Battle of **Talikota** in 1565 (by a coalition of the Deccan Sultanates, decades after the Lodi Sultanate had ended) is one of the great "what-if" inflection points of Indian history — what the subcontinent's political map would have looked like if Vijayanagara had survived and consolidated as a competing imperial center is one of the major counterfactuals of Indian historiography.

For the Sultanate at Delhi, Vijayanagara was simultaneously a competitor (the Bahmani-Vijayanagara wars across the Deccan frontier consumed enormous resources on both sides), a model of what Hindu political centralization could look like, and a constant reminder that the Sultanate's pan-Indian pretensions were never actually achieved in practice. The Lodi Sultanate at its peak controlled less than a quarter of the subcontinent's population and territory.

---

In April 1526, **Babur** crossed into India for what would be his last and decisive campaign.

Daulat Khan Lodi had originally invited him, but had then, predictably, attempted to redirect him: Daulat Khan wanted Babur to defeat Ibrahim Lodi but then withdraw to Kabul, leaving Daulat Khan in possession of Delhi. Babur had no intention of doing this. By the time the campaign began, Daulat Khan had switched sides again and was attempting to fight Babur from the Punjab. Babur defeated him outside Lahore in early 1526 and continued east toward Delhi.

Ibrahim Lodi mobilized the imperial Sultanate army to meet him. The Lodi force was substantial — Persian and Mughal sources agree on something between 100,000 and 130,000 men, including the major war elephants of the imperial cavalry, supported by tens of thousands of camp followers and supply trains. By comparison, Babur's army at Panipat was perhaps 12,000 to 25,000 (the sources disagree, but everyone agrees the Lodi force was several times larger).

What Babur had that Ibrahim did not was **gunpowder weapons**, used in a coordinated way that the Sultanate had no experience defending against. We'll cover the battle in detail in Chapter 8. But the structural fact at the end of Chapter 7 is that the Sultanate's military system — built around heavy cavalry, war elephants, and infantry archers, refined across three centuries of experience against pre-gunpowder armies — was about to encounter a small, mobile, gunpowder-equipped army using tactics borrowed from the Ottoman Janissary system, and was about to lose decisively.

The Lodi dynasty would not survive April 21, 1526. Neither would the Sultanate.

The early sixteenth century was the inflection point at which gunpowder armies, Atlantic seafaring, and centralized fiscal-military states collectively transformed every major Eurasian civilization. The end of the Sultanate is one regional manifestation of this larger global transformation.

# Chapter 8 — Panipat 1526 and the End

The First Battle of **Panipat** (PAH-nee-put) was fought on April 21, 1526, on a flat plain about 55 miles north of Delhi. It lasted from sunrise to about midday — perhaps six hours of actual fighting. By the end, **Ibrahim Lodi** was dead on the field, the imperial Sultanate army of perhaps 100,000 had been broken, and **Babur** was the new master of north India.

The battle is one of the textbook engagements in the global history of military technology. It is the moment when the gunpowder army, used in coordinated combination with traditional cavalry tactics, definitively replaced the pre-gunpowder cavalry charge as the decisive military technology of the Indian subcontinent. The same technological transition had happened at **Chaldiran** in 1514 (Ottoman gunpowder defeating Safavid cavalry, Safavid Persia Ch 3), and would happen at the **Battle of Mohacs** in 1526 (the same year as Panipat, Ottoman gunpowder defeating Hungarian cavalry, Ottoman Empire Ch 5). India's version came at Panipat. After Panipat, no Indian state could afford to fight wars without artillery and matchlock infantry. The Sultanate's military system, perfected over three centuries against pre-gunpowder enemies, was suddenly obsolete.

Babur's army was small but technologically state-of-the-art. He had perhaps 12,000 to 25,000 troops (sources disagree). He had a few hundred matchlock musketeers under his Turkish gun-master **Ustad Ali Quli**, and a smaller number of field cannons (probably 10-20, some of them captured at recent campaigns, some manufactured at Kabul). He had perhaps 3,000-4,000 mounted archers, primarily Mongol and Uzbek mercenaries. He had a general staff that included **Humayun** (his son and eventual successor) and several experienced Timurid commanders.

Most importantly, he had read the Ottoman tactical manuals on what was called **arabakorpus** or **wagenburg** — the deployment of gunpowder infantry behind a defensive perimeter of chained-together baggage carts, with the artillery mounted on or between the carts to provide supporting fire while the infantry musketeers fired through gaps in the cart line. The Ottomans had developed this tactic against Persian and European cavalry over the previous century. Babur's gun-master Ustad Ali Quli had served in the Ottoman army before taking employment with the Timurids, and brought the tactical knowledge with him.

Babur deployed his force in this configuration on the morning of April 21. He had his roughly 700 baggage carts chained together along the front of his line, gaps left at intervals for cavalry sallies. His matchlock musketeers were positioned behind the carts. His field cannons were positioned at intervals along the cart line. His cavalry was held in reserve, divided into a center force and two wings, with mounted archers further forward to harass the approaching Lodi army.

Ibrahim Lodi's army arrived on the field with the standard Sultanate military doctrine of the period: massed heavy cavalry, supported by infantry archers, with war elephants on the flanks. The Lodi general staff, lacking experience with gunpowder warfare, made the standard pre-gunpowder decision: charge.

The Lodi charge broke against Babur's defensive perimeter. The matchlock fire from behind the carts decimated the leading ranks of cavalry. The cannon fire shattered the war elephants (which spooked at the explosions and caused havoc in the Lodi ranks behind them). The mounted archers harassed the Lodi flanks while the heavy cavalry was pinned at the cart line. By midday, the Lodi army was disintegrating. Babur's mounted reserves swept around the flanks of the broken line and completed the destruction.

Ibrahim Lodi fell somewhere on the field, along with perhaps 15,000 of his soldiers. His body was identified after the battle and beheaded; the head was reportedly carried back to Babur as proof of victory. Babur entered Delhi unopposed three days later, on April 27, 1526, and proclaimed himself **Padshah** (literally "master-king," the Persian-Turkic imperial title that the Mughal dynasty would use for the next three centuries).

The Delhi Sultanate, founded by Aibak in 1206, had ended.

---

What came next was the Mughal Empire. We will not cover the Mughal story here — it is the subject of a different TL — but we should note what Babur inherited and what he had to do to consolidate his victory.

Babur did not, in fact, immediately control all of the Sultanate's former territory. The Hindu Rajput coalition under **Rana Sanga** of Mewar mobilized to oppose him almost immediately, and Babur had to fight a second major battle — the **Battle of Khanwa** in March 1527 — to secure his northern Indian conquest. The Afghan Lodi nobility scattered across the eastern Sultanate territories and would resist Mughal authority for the next thirty years (eventually rallying under **Sher Shah Suri**, the great Pashtun general who would briefly displace Babur's son **Humayun** from the Mughal throne and rule north India as the Sur Sultanate from 1540 to 1555). The provincial sultanates — Bengal, Gujarat, Malwa, the Deccan Sultanates, Vijayanagara, the Sindh — remained independent for decades after Panipat. The full consolidation of north India under Mughal rule would not be completed until Akbar's reign in the 1560s and 1570s.

But the Sultanate as a Delhi-based political institution was over. The dynasty was extinguished. The political continuity from Aibak to Ibrahim Lodi was broken by Babur's conquest. The institutional and cultural continuities, however, were preserved. The Mughals adopted, modified, and refined nearly everything the Sultanate had built.

---

What the Sultanate left behind, after Panipat, deserves a final tally.

The **administrative framework**. The iqta land-grant system, modified into the more elaborate Mughal **mansabdari** system, would continue to organize Indian land revenue and military finance for the next three centuries. The basic structure of provincial governorship (the **suba** under the Mughals), district administration (the **sarkar**), and rural revenue extraction (the **pargana**) all derive from Sultanate precedents. The Persian language of imperial administration would continue as the official language of north Indian governance through the Mughal period and into the early British period — until 1837, when the East India Company finally replaced Persian with English in court and administrative use, ending a 631-year run that Aibak had inadvertently begun in 1206.

The **architectural tradition**. The Indo-Islamic synthesis of Persian arch-and-dome construction with Indian craftsmanship would continue as a continuous tradition through the Mughal period and beyond. The Qutb Minar and Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque are the founding monuments of this tradition; the Tughlaq fortresses of Tughlaqabad and Firuzabad are major Sultanate-era expressions; the Lodi tombs are the immediate ancestors of Humayun's Tomb and the Taj Mahal. Modern Indian visual culture's sense of "Islamic architecture in India" is essentially a Sultanate-era invention that the Mughals refined to its most famous forms.

The **religious-cultural synthesis**. The Bhakti devotional tradition would continue to grow through the Mughal period, eventually becoming the dominant form of popular Hindu religious practice across north India. The Sufi dargah network would continue and expand under Mughal patronage (Akbar in particular was a major patron of the Chishti dargahs at Ajmer and Fatehpur Sikri). The Sikh community Nanak founded would grow through the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, develop its distinctive militarized identity under Guru Gobind Singh, and emerge as a major religious community in modern South Asia. The Hindavi-Rekhta language would develop into modern Hindi and modern Urdu. The Indo-Islamic musical traditions — qawwali, the north Indian classical raga system — would become the foundation of all subsequent north Indian musical practice.

The **demographic-religious geography**. The Sultanate's three centuries of Turkic and Afghan rule, plus the conversion of substantial portions of the rural population to Islam (especially in the eastern Bengal delta, where the Sultanate-era expansion of rice cultivation was associated with conversion to Islam, and in the western Punjab and Sindh), permanently altered the religious geography of the subcontinent. By 1526, north India had a Muslim population of perhaps 15-20% in the cities and 5-10% in the countryside, varying enormously by region. Bengal had Muslim majorities in many districts. The Punjab had substantial Muslim populations. Sindh was largely Muslim. The Deccan and the south remained overwhelmingly Hindu. The geographic distribution of Muslims and Hindus that would later produce the 1947 Partition of India and Pakistan is recognizably traceable to the demographic patterns the Sultanate had established.

The **political precedent**. The Sultanate established that a centralized military monarchy could rule a Hindu-majority subcontinent through a Muslim military aristocracy, governed by Persianate administrative tradition, with Islamic religious law applied to the Muslim minority and customary Hindu law applied to the Hindu majority, financed by land revenue and trade taxes. Every subsequent Indian state — the Mughals, the Marathas (Hindu but governing through similar institutional structures), the British, even in some respects modern India — has built on this institutional template. The Sultanate is the matrix of pre-modern Indian state-building.

---

A few longer-term things the Sultanate gave India that are worth noting separately:

**Persian as the cultural common language** of north Indian elites. Persian poetry, Persian historical writing, Persian administrative documents — all of this would continue to be the central register of north Indian elite culture from 1206 through the early nineteenth century. **Mirza Ghalib** (the great early-nineteenth-century Persian and Urdu poet of late-Mughal Delhi) was writing in a tradition that ran continuously back to Amir Khusrau six centuries earlier.

**The Indian Ocean trading network**. The Sultanate maintained and extended the trading connections that the Cholas had built (Medieval India Ch 5), particularly through the Bengal and Gujarat ports. The horse trade with Arabia and Iran (which the Sultanate's perpetual cavalry shortage made critical) flowed through the same Indian Ocean routes that brought Chinese porcelain, African gold, Indonesian spices, and European trade goods. By 1500, the Indian subcontinent was more deeply integrated into the Indian Ocean trading system than it had been before, partly because of Sultanate-era developments in port infrastructure and trade administration.

**The Sufi-Bhakti devotional vocabulary**. Modern Indian devotional practice, both Hindu and Muslim, draws on a shared vocabulary of devotional intensity, divine love, the inadequacy of formal religious institution, the importance of vernacular expression, and the equality of devotees before God — vocabulary that was substantially worked out in the Sultanate-era dialogue between Sufi tariqas and Bhakti sant-traditions. The fact that modern Hindi and Urdu devotional songs (whether religious bhajans, Sufi qawwalis, or even popular film songs that mimic devotional registers) sound recognizably similar to a non-specialist listener — both languages, both traditions, both registers — is partly the product of this Sultanate-era synthesis.

**The Indo-Islamic culinary tradition**. The cuisines of north India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh — biryanis, kebabs, naan, samosas, jalebi, kheer, and the entire vocabulary of urban north Indian-Pakistani cooking — are substantially Sultanate-era developments, drawing on Persian and Central Asian techniques applied to Indian ingredients. The first reliable mentions of biryani as a distinct dish appear in late-Sultanate sources. The samosa appears in Amir Khusrau's writings. Modern north Indian street food is, gastronomically, a Sultanate-era invention that the Mughals codified.

---

The historical reputation of the Delhi Sultanate is genuinely difficult to summarize, because what you see depends on where you stand.

From the perspective of the Sultanate's Persian court chroniclers — Minhaj-i Siraj, Ziauddin Barani, Shams-i Siraj Afif, Yahya Sirhindi — the Sultanate was the legitimate political project of bringing Islamic rule and high Persianate culture to a previously chaotic Hindu subcontinent. From the perspective of nineteenth- and twentieth-century British colonial historians — who built on the Persian chronicles plus their own ideological framework — the Sultanate was a foreign Muslim despotism that interrupted the natural development of Hindu civilization and prepared the way for the British "civilizing mission." From the perspective of twentieth-century Indian nationalist historians — particularly the secular-Marxist tradition associated with **Mohammad Habib**, **Irfan Habib**, and **Romila Thapar** — the Sultanate was a complex multi-ethnic state that integrated Indian and Central Asian institutions, produced significant administrative and cultural achievements, and prepared the institutional ground for the Mughal Empire and ultimately for modern Indian state-building. From the perspective of twentieth-century Hindu nationalist historiography — the **Hindutva** tradition associated with figures like **Veer Savarkar** — the Sultanate was an extended period of foreign Muslim oppression that destroyed Hindu temples, persecuted Hindu populations, and represented an interruption of Hindu civilizational continuity that needed to be reversed in modern Indian politics.

All four perspectives have something to draw on in the historical record. The Sultanate did, demonstrably, build a Persianate Islamic state ruling a Hindu majority. It did, demonstrably, destroy Hindu temples in conquest campaigns and on a sustained basis under certain sultans. It did, demonstrably, develop a sophisticated multi-ethnic administrative apparatus that absorbed Hindu officials and functioned for three centuries. It did, demonstrably, generate cross-religious cultural syntheses (Sufism-Bhakti, Indo-Persian architecture, Hindavi-Rekhta language, north Indian classical music) that reshaped popular religious and cultural life across the subcontinent.

The Sultanate's record cannot be honestly reduced to any one of these descriptions. Modern historians have spent decades trying to develop frameworks that hold all of them simultaneously — the Sultanate as a complex pre-modern state with both repressive and integrative dimensions, both destructive and creative cultural impacts, both foreign-imposed and locally-rooted institutional features. The honest answer to "what was the Sultanate?" is that it was several things at once, and the same set of facts can be interpreted very differently depending on what political-religious-cultural framework you bring to the interpretation.

---

The actual end of the Sultanate at Panipat in 1526 is a moment of military-technological transition (gunpowder defeats heavy cavalry) much more than it is a moment of dynastic-cultural rupture. The Lodi Afghans were defeated by the Timurid Babur, but the Indo-Islamic-Persianate cultural-administrative-religious world the Sultanate had built continued largely uninterrupted under the Mughals. The cooks kept cooking biryanis. The qawwalis kept being sung. The Persian poets kept writing ghazals. The Bhakti sants kept composing devotional songs. The Hindu temples kept being built (the great Vijayanagara temples, the Rajput palace-temples of Mewar, the Bengal terracotta temples of the Kantanagar tradition) outside Mughal reach. The Sufi dargahs kept attracting pilgrims. The Indian Ocean trade kept moving Indian textiles and indigo to the east and west.

What changed at Panipat was the particular dynasty at the top of the political pyramid in Delhi. What did not change was the underlying social, economic, religious, and cultural fabric of the subcontinent. That fabric had been substantially woven by the Sultanate's three centuries. The Mughals would alter it but not unmake it. The British would alter it more but not unmake it either. Modern India and Pakistan are still living in the long downstream consequences of the world the Sultanate built.

---

In the modern city of Delhi — which is to say, modern New Delhi plus modern Old Delhi (Shahjahanabad) plus the surrounding districts that have absorbed all the older Sultanate cities into their suburban growth — the Sultanate-era monuments are all still there.

The **Qutb Minar** at Mehrauli is the most visited tourist site in southern Delhi. The **Quwwat-ul-Islam** mosque ruins, with their reused Hindu temple columns and the still-standing Iron Pillar of Delhi, sit beside it in the same UNESCO complex. The **Tughlaqabad** fortress walls run for miles across south Delhi, partially inhabited by squatter villages, partially preserved as archaeological zone, mostly visible from passing cars on the Mehrauli-Badarpur road. The **Firuzabad** complex (Firuz Shah Kotla) sits beside the Yamuna in central Delhi, a small archaeological park where pilgrims still come on Thursday evenings to leave letters at the ruins of the old palace, asking the **jinn** (Islamic spirits) believed to inhabit the site for help with various problems. The **Lodi Tombs** sit in the modern Lodi Gardens in central New Delhi, an immaculate landscaped park that joggers and picnickers use daily without necessarily knowing whose tombs they are sitting beside. The **Nizamuddin Dargah** is in active use as a working pilgrimage site in central Delhi, with daily qawwali performances, weekly Thursday-evening special programs, and a continuous flow of pilgrims of every faith.

Out beyond Delhi, the Sultanate-era monuments are even more numerous. The **Adina Mosque** at Pandua (now Bangladesh), the **Atala Mosque** at Jaunpur, the **Jahaz Mahal** at Mandu, the **Champaner-Pavagadh** complex in Gujarat, the **Madrasa of Mahmud Gawan** at Bidar, the **Chittor Fort** complex in Rajasthan, the **Hampi** temple and palace ruins, the various provincial-sultanate mosques and tombs scattered across Bengal, the Doab, the Deccan, and Gujarat — all are still standing, mostly preserved, many on the UNESCO World Heritage list.

The Sultanate is not gone. It is still there in the architecture, still there in the language people speak in the bazaars of Delhi and Lahore, still there in the qawwalis sung at the Nizamuddin and Ajmer dargahs, still there in the Bhakti songs and the Sikh hymns recorded from Sultanate-era sources, still there in the cuisines of north India and Pakistan, still there in the Persian-derived vocabulary of legal and administrative Hindi-Urdu, still there in the institutional template of modern Indian governance. Three centuries of dynastic history, several hundred thousand victims of conquest, several million immigrants and converts, and a cultural-religious-architectural synthesis that has shaped the subcontinent for the nine hundred years since are all part of what the Delhi Sultanate left for the modern world to inherit.

The story that began with Aibak and his polo accident in Lahore in 1210 is still going on.

The mid-1520s are the global pivot point at which gunpowder weapons, Atlantic trade, religious reformation, and centralized fiscal-military states transformed every major civilization simultaneously. The end of the Delhi Sultanate is one regional manifestation of this larger global transformation. Babur's victory at Panipat is the same kind of event as Cortes's victory at Tenochtitlan five years earlier (Aztec Empire Ch 8) and Pizarro's victory at Cajamarca seven years later: the moment when a small, well-equipped, disciplined invading army defeated a large pre-gunpowder defending army using imported military technology that the defenders could not match.

The Sultanate joined a world in 1206 that included the Mongols, the Crusader states, and the Song. The Sultanate left a world in 1526 that included the Ottomans, the Habsburgs, the Safavids, the Reformation, the Aztec collapse, and the global maritime trading networks. Three hundred and twenty years is a long time. The world had changed beyond recognition. The Sultanate's three centuries had run their course.

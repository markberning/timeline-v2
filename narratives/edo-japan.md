# Edo Japan — The Closed Country

**Goal:** Narrative for the Tokugawa shogunate (1603–1868 CE), the strangest political experiment in early-modern history. For 265 years, an aristocracy of unemployed warriors governed a sealed island country in which Christianity was a capital crime, foreign travel was punishable by death, and the emperor was a ceremonial recluse in Kyoto with no political power. During those same 265 years, that supposedly stagnant society produced Hokusai's wave, Bashō's haiku, kabuki, the world's first commodity futures market, the largest city on earth (Edo), and a literacy rate higher than Britain's. Then, in 1853, four American steamships sailed into Edo Bay and the whole structure unraveled in fifteen years. The Edo period is the answer to the question: what does a complex society do with itself when it has been told to stop?

# Chapter 1 — The Road to Sekigahara

The Edo period begins on October 21, 1600, on a foggy plain about 250 miles (400 km) west of modern Tokyo, where two armies of roughly 80,000 men each slaughtered each other for six hours in the rain to decide which of two factions would govern Japan. The battle was called **Sekigahara** (seh-kee-gah-HAH-rah). The winner was a sixty-year-old daimyo named **Tokugawa Ieyasu** (toh-koo-GAH-wah ee-eh-YAH-soo). The loser was a forty-year-old bureaucrat-general named **Ishida Mitsunari** (ee-SHEE-dah mit-soo-NAH-ree). The dynasty that came out of Ieyasu's victory ruled Japan for the next 265 years and shaped everything in this book.

To understand why two enormous Japanese armies were trying to kill each other on a rainy October day in 1600, you need to understand what had happened in Japan over the previous half century — the period the Japanese call the **Sengoku Jidai** (sen-GOH-koo jee-DYE), the "Age of Warring States," covered in the closing chapters of the Ancient Japan TL.

---

The short version: from roughly 1467 to 1600, Japan was a failed state. The emperor in **Kyoto** (KYOH-toh) was a ceremonial figurehead with no real power. The **shogun** (SHOH-gun, the military dictator who had governed Japan in the emperor's name since 1192) had become equally powerless, and the **Ashikaga shogunate** (ah-shee-KAH-gah, the dynasty that had held the office since 1336) had collapsed into civil war. In place of central authority, the country was divided among roughly 250 regional military lords called **daimyo** (DIME-yo, "great names"), who ran their territories as effectively independent kingdoms, fought each other constantly, and built the vast hilltop fortresses that still dot the Japanese landscape — castles like **Himeji** (HEE-meh-jee) and **Matsumoto** (mat-soo-MOH-toh).

Three figures unified the country in stages, in a process Japanese historians call the **Sengoku Unification**.

The first was **Oda Nobunaga** (OH-dah noh-boo-NAH-gah), a brutal and brilliant daimyo from central Honshu who began his career with a tiny domain and by the time of his assassination in 1582 controlled roughly half of Japan. Nobunaga was the first Japanese commander to use European-imported **arquebuses** (matchlock firearms, introduced by Portuguese traders in 1543) on a mass scale. At the **Battle of Nagashino** in 1575, he stationed three thousand arquebusiers in rotating volley fire behind wooden palisades and shot the famous **Takeda cavalry** to pieces — the same kind of gunpowder-versus-cavalry mismatch the Ottomans inflicted on the Safavids at Chaldiran in 1514 (Safavid Persia Ch 3), and the Spanish on Aztec warriors at Tenochtitlan in 1521 (Aztec Empire Ch 8). The Sengoku Unification was a gunpowder revolution as much as a political one.

Nobunaga was killed at the **Honnō-ji Incident** in June 1582, betrayed and forced into ritual suicide by one of his own generals. His succession passed not to his sons but to his most capable lieutenant, a man of peasant origin named **Toyotomi Hideyoshi** (toh-yoh-TOH-mee hee-deh-YOH-shee).

Hideyoshi's career is one of the most extraordinary social ascents in pre-modern history. Born into a peasant family with no surname, he rose through the ranks of Nobunaga's army to become a senior general; after Nobunaga's death he outmaneuvered the rival generals, was granted the surname Toyotomi by the emperor, and by 1590 had completed the unification of Japan. Hideyoshi could not become shogun (the title was reserved by tradition for descendants of a specific noble lineage to which his peasant background obviously did not belong), so he took the older imperial-court title of **kampaku** (KAM-pah-koo, "regent") instead. He confiscated swords from the peasantry in the **Sword Hunt** of 1588, fixed the four-class social order that the Tokugawa would inherit, and set in motion the conditions that would produce the Edo settlement.

He also did one disastrous thing: in 1592, he invaded **Korea**.

---

Hideyoshi's **Korean invasions** (Imjin War, 1592–98) are covered in detail in Ancient Korea Ch 8 and the Joseon Korea TL. From the Japanese side, what matters is that the war was a catastrophe. Hideyoshi mobilized roughly 158,000 Japanese troops for the first invasion in 1592 and another 141,000 for the second in 1597. Korean and Chinese Ming forces, supported by the brilliant Korean admiral **Yi Sun-sin** and his armored "turtle ships," fought the Japanese to a standstill on land and destroyed Japanese supply lines at sea. The campaigns dragged on for six years, devastated the Korean Peninsula, killed perhaps a million Koreans, drained the Toyotomi treasury, and produced no territorial gains whatsoever. They ended only because Hideyoshi died in September 1598 and his successors withdrew the troops.

Hideyoshi's death is where the Sekigahara story properly starts.

He left a five-year-old son and heir, **Toyotomi Hideyori** (hee-deh-YOH-ree), and a council of five senior daimyo regents (the **Go-Tairō**, GOH TAI-roh) plus five administrators charged with governing in the boy's name until he came of age. The senior regent was Tokugawa Ieyasu, lord of the **Kantō** (KAHN-toh) plain — the rich agricultural region around modern Tokyo where Hideyoshi had relocated him in 1590, partly as reward, partly to get him out of central Japan where he could cause trouble.

Ieyasu was, by 1598, the richest and most experienced daimyo in Japan. He had survived since the 1540s, fought under Nobunaga, kept his independence under Hideyoshi, refused to participate in the Korean invasions (citing the need to defend his eastern domains), and used the war years to build up his treasury while everyone else burned theirs in Korea. He was old, patient, and completely without scruples about using legal pretexts to seize what he wanted. The Toyotomi council was the kind of arrangement that practically begged for someone like Ieyasu to break.

The break came in 1599 and 1600. Ieyasu began arranging unauthorized political marriages between his family and other daimyo houses — a violation of Hideyoshi's last orders — and the rest of the council split over how to respond. The bureaucrat **Ishida Mitsunari**, a Toyotomi loyalist who had served as Hideyoshi's chief administrator in Korea (and who was hated by many of the field commanders for his perceived favoritism toward Hideyoshi's home retainers), began organizing a coalition of western daimyo to check Ieyasu before he could consolidate power. The other regents either died, declared neutrality, or joined Ieyasu.

By summer 1600, Japan had divided into two armed camps. Ieyasu commanded the **Eastern Army** — primarily daimyo from the Kantō and northeastern Honshu, supported by several powerful western lords who had personal grievances against Mitsunari. Mitsunari commanded the **Western Army** — daimyo from western Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu, nominally loyal to the seven-year-old Hideyori.

---

The Sekigahara campaign of summer and autumn 1600 was an extraordinarily fluid affair, with sieges and skirmishes scattered across the country. The decisive action happened on a single day, October 21, on the Sekigahara plain in modern Gifu Prefecture, where Mitsunari deployed his army in the morning fog to block Ieyasu's advance toward the imperial capital at Kyoto.

Numbers are estimated at roughly 80,000 on each side, though the Western Army may have been somewhat larger. The Western Army held strong defensive positions in the surrounding hills — on paper, the position favored Mitsunari. The battle began around 8 in the morning and was fought ferociously through the early afternoon, with neither side able to break the other.

What decided it was treachery. Two western daimyo, **Kobayakawa Hideaki** (koh-bah-yah-KAH-wah hee-deh-AH-kee) and **Wakizaka Yasuharu**, had entered into secret negotiations with Ieyasu before the battle. Around noon, Ieyasu fired arquebus volleys at Kobayakawa's stationary force on the western flank as a prearranged signal. Kobayakawa charged — not into the Tokugawa line but into the Western Army's right wing, the troops of **Ōtani Yoshitsugu** (OH-tah-nee yoh-shee-TSOO-goo), a leper-warlord and Mitsunari's most loyal ally. Other secretly compromised western commanders followed Kobayakawa's lead. Within an hour, the Western Army's flank had collapsed. By mid-afternoon the battle was over. Ōtani committed ritual suicide. Mitsunari fled the field, was captured three days later, and was beheaded in Kyoto along with two of his senior allies.

The casualty figures are debated — chronicle accounts give numbers ranging from 8,000 to 30,000 dead, mostly on the Western side — but the political result was unambiguous. Ieyasu now controlled all of Japan in fact, even though he was technically still acting in the name of the eight-year-old Toyotomi heir.

It took him three more years to formalize the result. In 1603, the emperor appointed Ieyasu **shogun** — reviving the office that had been dormant for half a century, and granting it (this time) to a man who actually had the military force to enforce its claims. Ieyasu was sixty-one years old. He chose as his administrative capital not Kyoto (the imperial capital, where the powerless emperor resided) and not Osaka (Hideyoshi's stronghold, where the Toyotomi heir still held court) but **Edo** (EH-doh, modern Tokyo) — at the time a small fishing village on a swampy bay, which he had been developing since Hideyoshi assigned him the Kantō in 1590. Within a century, Edo would be the largest city in the world.

The Tokugawa shogunate had begun.

---

One loose end remained: the Toyotomi heir.

Hideyori was, by the early 1610s, a young adult living in **Osaka Castle** (the immense fortress Hideyoshi had built as his capital). He had no political power, but he was the focal point for any daimyo who might object to Tokugawa rule, and his mother **Yodo-dono** (yoh-DOH DOH-noh) maintained an alternative court that worried Ieyasu. Ieyasu manufactured a pretext in 1614 — an inscription on a temple bell at Osaka was interpreted as a curse against him — and besieged Osaka Castle in two campaigns over the winter of 1614–15.

The **Sieges of Osaka** were the last major military operations of the Sengoku era. The first siege ended in a negotiated peace under which Hideyori agreed to fill in the castle's outer moats. As soon as the negotiation was complete, Ieyasu's troops also filled in the inner moats — without authorization but with the kind of legalistic excuse Ieyasu specialized in — leaving the castle defensible only from the keep itself. The summer 1615 siege then breached the keep within weeks. Hideyori committed ritual suicide. His mother committed ritual suicide. His seven-year-old illegitimate son (Hideyoshi's grandson) was beheaded at Kyoto's Sanjō bridge as a precaution. The Toyotomi line was extinguished.

That same year, Ieyasu issued the **Buke Shohatto** ("Laws for the Military Houses"), the basic legal code regulating daimyo conduct, and the **Ikkoku Ichijō Rei** ("One Castle Per Province" edict), requiring daimyo to demolish all but one castle per province and break the military infrastructure of the warring-states era. He died the next year, in 1616, having lived just long enough to see the Tokugawa system safely established.

He was buried at **Nikkō** (NIK-koh) in the mountains north of Edo, and shortly afterward enshrined as the deity **Tōshō Daigongen** ("Great Avatar Illuminating the East") at the **Nikkō Tōshō-gū** shrine, one of the most ornately decorated religious complexes in Japan and the cult center of the dynasty.

---

A pause to set up what comes next: the Edo period is structurally weird in a way that takes some getting used to.

It was a feudal warrior society in which there were no wars. The samurai class — roughly 7% of the population, whose entire social identity was built around martial skill — spent the next two and a half centuries doing accounting, collecting taxes, attending Confucian academies, and writing nostalgic essays about the warrior ethos of their ancestors. Many never drew a sword in anger. Some never wore real swords at all by the late Edo period (wooden practice swords were cheaper, and there was nobody to fight).

It was a hereditary social hierarchy of unprecedented rigidity. The four-class order put samurai on top, peasants second (because they fed the country), artisans third, and merchants at the bottom (because they produced nothing — they only moved goods around). Within a century the merchants had most of the money; the samurai were chronically broke; and the social hierarchy had inverted economically while remaining intact legally and culturally.

It was an isolated country in which foreigners were forbidden to enter and Japanese were forbidden to leave. The death penalty applied in both directions. Yet the small Dutch trading post at Nagasaki transmitted European medicine, astronomy, and physics, in dribs and drabs, to a small but growing community of Japanese scholars who knew that everything they were learning would be useful when (not if) the closure ended.

It was an authoritarian state in which the central government deliberately limited its own ambition. The shogunate did not collect a national tax. It did not recruit a national army. It governed roughly a quarter of Japan directly and left the remaining three-quarters to the daimyo, with controls (sankin-kotai, marriage approval, castle limits) that prevented daimyo coordination but otherwise let them run their domains as they pleased.

It was, in short, a society that had decided to stop. The next chapter explains how the stopping was institutionalized.

Cross-civ check: Sekigahara in 1600 was almost exactly contemporary with the founding of the **English East India Company** (1600), which would two and a half centuries later play a major role in Japan's forced reopening — and which had already, by 1600, established the trading post system on the Indian coast that would form the core of the **British Empire** in India. **Akbar the Great** of the Mughal Empire (Mughal Ch 3) died five years after Sekigahara, having ruled India through almost the same span as Hideyoshi. **Shah Abbas the Great** of Safavid Persia (Safavid Persia Ch 4) was at the height of his power in the 1610s, transforming Isfahan into a city Europeans called "half the world" while Ieyasu was building Edo into what would become the largest city anywhere. The **Ming Dynasty** in China was in its terminal decline — the Manchu invasion that would replace it with the Qing was thirty-five years away. The early seventeenth century was a moment of imperial consolidation across most of Eurasia, and Japan was joining the pattern at the eastern end.

# Chapter 2 — The Tokugawa System

Tokugawa Ieyasu died in 1616 without writing a constitution. There was no founding document, no Magna Carta, no formal statement of how the new regime was supposed to work. What there was instead, accumulated over the first half-century of Tokugawa rule, was a dense web of edicts, customs, hierarchies, and physical constraints that together produced the most stable political order in Japanese history. By the time the third shogun **Tokugawa Iemitsu** (toh-koo-GAH-wah ee-eh-MIT-soo) died in 1651, the system was complete. It would last with only marginal modification until Perry's ships arrived in 1853.

This chapter walks through the components.

---

The first component was the **bakuhan system** — the dual structure that distinguished the Edo regime from anything Japan had seen before.

**Bakufu** (BAH-koo-foo, "tent government") was the original Japanese term for a shogunate, dating to the twelfth century when the first shogun's headquarters were a literal field tent. Under the Tokugawa, the bakufu was the central government in Edo: the shogun, his council of senior ministers (**Rōjū**, ROH-joo), his junior councillors (**Wakadoshiyori**, wah-kah-doh-shee-YOH-ree), and the various magistracies that handled finance, religion, foreign affairs, and the territories the shogunate governed directly.

**Han** (HAHN) was the term for the daimyo domains — roughly 270 of them by the mid-seventeenth century, ranging in size from the **Kaga han** of the Maeda family (one million **koku** of rice, the largest non-Tokugawa domain) down to small holdings of just 10,000 koku, the technical minimum to qualify as a daimyo. A **koku** (KOH-koo) was the amount of rice considered sufficient to feed one person for one year — roughly 5 bushels or 180 liters — and it was the standard unit of value, taxation, and military rank for the entire Edo period. A daimyo's wealth was measured in koku. A samurai's stipend was paid in koku. The shogunate's own holdings were measured in koku (around 4 million, plus the personal estates of the shogun and the Tokugawa branch families, totaling roughly a quarter of Japan's productive land).

The genius of the bakuhan system was that it was simultaneously highly centralized and highly decentralized. The shogunate held overwhelming military power (the Tokugawa and their direct retainers fielded the largest army in Japan by an enormous margin), exclusive authority over foreign relations, the right to confiscate or relocate any daimyo, control of the major cities and mines, and the authority to legislate for the whole country on certain matters. But within their domains, daimyo were essentially autonomous — they ran their own administrations, levied their own taxes (paid in koku of rice, just like everyone else), maintained their own armies, and answered to the shogunate only on a narrow set of matters defined by the **Buke Shohatto** ("Laws for the Military Houses"), the basic legal code Ieyasu issued in 1615.

The Buke Shohatto was itself a remarkably restrained document. It mostly told daimyo what they could not do — could not repair castles without permission, could not arrange marriages with other daimyo families without permission, could not harbor criminals from other domains, could not build large ships, could not introduce Christianity. It did not tell them how to govern. The shogunate's interest was in preventing inter-daimyo coordination that might threaten Tokugawa supremacy, not in dictating local policy.

---

Daimyo were not all equal. The system divided them into three categories that determined their distance from the shogun, the kinds of offices they could hold, and the kinds of trouble they could cause.

**Shinpan** (SHIN-pan, "related daimyo") were branches of the Tokugawa family itself — the great cadet branches at **Owari**, **Kii**, and **Mito** known collectively as the **Gosanke** ("Three Houses"), and several smaller branches. They held important domains, were ineligible to serve in shogunate ministerial offices (to prevent any dilution of their dynastic prestige), and provided the pool from which a successor would be drawn if the main Tokugawa line failed (which it did several times).

**Fudai** (FOO-die, "hereditary vassals") were daimyo whose ancestors had been Tokugawa retainers since before 1600. They were generally given small but strategically located domains, and they monopolized the major shogunate offices — the senior ministers, the junior ministers, the magistracies, the inspectorates. They were the bureaucratic operating system of the Tokugawa state.

**Tozama** (toh-ZAH-mah, "outside lords") were the daimyo whose families had been independent of the Tokugawa before 1600 — including many who had fought against Ieyasu at Sekigahara and had submitted only after the battle. They held the largest domains (the great tozama houses of **Maeda** in Kaga, **Shimazu** in **Satsuma** at the southern tip of Kyushu, **Mōri** in **Chōshū** at the western end of Honshu, and **Date** in northeastern Honshu, were among the wealthiest), but they were excluded from the shogunate's central government. Their domains were typically remote — Satsuma in the far southwest, Chōshū at the western end of Honshu, Date in the far northeast — placed deliberately far from Edo.

This three-tier system contained a long-term geological flaw. The fudai who actually ran the shogunate were tied to relatively small, fragmented domains and depended on the bakufu for status. The tozama, excluded from central power, ran their own large rich domains for two and a half centuries with minimal interference, accumulated grievances they could neither express nor act on, and developed independent administrative traditions, military experiments, and (in some cases) covert foreign contacts. When the system finally cracked in the 1860s, the great tozama domains of Satsuma and Chōshū — kept out of central power for 265 years — would be the ones that overthrew the Tokugawa (Chapter 8).

---

The second component was the **sankin-kōtai** (SAHN-kin KOH-tai, "alternate attendance") system, formalized by Iemitsu in 1635, which is probably the single most consequential institution of the Edo period.

Under sankin-kōtai, every daimyo in Japan was required to spend alternate years in residence at Edo and to leave their wife, children, and immediate heir there permanently as hostages. The standard schedule was one year in Edo, one year back in the home domain, on rotation. There were variations (some smaller daimyo spent half-years; some Kantō daimyo had different schedules), but the basic principle applied to everyone.

The mechanism is worth thinking through, because it solved several political problems at once.

First, the **hostage function**. With every daimyo's heir living permanently in Edo, no daimyo could rebel without sacrificing their family. The hostages were not held in cells — they lived in lavish daimyo mansions in designated districts of Edo, were educated by tutors, married off to other daimyo families with shogunate approval — but they were hostages nonetheless. This single arrangement made daimyo rebellion, the standard political problem of the Sengoku era, structurally impossible.

Second, the **fiscal function**. Maintaining a residence in Edo, traveling to and from the home domain every other year with a full retinue of samurai retainers, and supporting a permanent household of family members and servants in the capital was ruinously expensive. Estimates suggest sankin-kōtai consumed somewhere between 50% and 75% of the average daimyo's annual income. This expense drained the daimyo treasuries that might otherwise have funded military buildup, and it transferred enormous wealth into Edo's economy.

Third, the **integrative function**. The biennial daimyo processions to and from Edo — typically several hundred armed retainers walking in formation along specific routes — bound the country together physically. The five great highways radiating from Edo (the **Gokaidō**, GOH-kai-doh: the **Tōkaidō** along the Pacific coast to Kyoto, the **Nakasendō** through the central mountains to Kyoto, the **Kōshū Kaidō** to the central highlands, the **Ōshū Kaidō** to the northeast, and the **Nikkō Kaidō** to the Tōshō-gū shrine) became the busiest road network in pre-railway Asia. Inn towns, post stations, and carrier services flourished along the routes. Travel guides, regional cuisine specialties, and souvenir industries developed to serve the annual flood of daimyo retinues. The sankin-kōtai roads were the circulatory system of Tokugawa Japan.

Fourth, and perhaps most important in the long run, the **cultural-economic function**. Concentrating the daimyo aristocracy in Edo for half of every year produced an enormous urban consumer market for luxury goods, entertainment, education, and services. Edo grew explosively as a result. Estimates of the city's population by 1700 range from 800,000 to 1.2 million people, making it almost certainly the largest city in the world at that point — bigger than London, bigger than Paris, bigger than Beijing. This urban scale produced the merchant culture, the entertainment districts, the bookstores and theaters and brothels and teahouses that would define Genroku-era Japan (Chapter 4).

The cost-benefit calculation from the shogunate's perspective was unambiguous. Sankin-kōtai prevented rebellion, drained daimyo finances, and built the largest city on earth at the heart of the Tokugawa state. From the daimyo's perspective, it was a slow-motion impoverishment they could not refuse.

---

The third component was the **shi-nō-kō-shō** (shee-noh-koh-SHOH) system — the four-class social order that fixed Japanese society in legal categories for the entire Edo period.

The four classes, in descending Confucian rank:

**Shi** — **samurai** (SAH-moo-rye), the warrior class. Roughly 6–8% of the population. Hereditary status. The only class permitted to wear the long-and-short sword combination (the **daishō**, DIE-shoh). The only class permitted to use family surnames in formal documents (commoners had given names but generally no surnames before the Meiji period). The only class from which government officials could be drawn. Samurai were paid in fixed rice stipends from their domain or from the shogunate, and these stipends did not adjust for inflation — which over time produced the chronic samurai indebtedness that became one of the central social problems of the Edo period.

**Nō** — **peasants** (NOH, hyakushō, hyah-KOO-shoh), the farming class. Roughly 80–85% of the population. They produced the rice that fed the country, paid the taxes that funded the state, and were ranked second under the Confucian principle that those who produce food are more valuable than those who only move it around. In practice, peasant life ranged from prosperous landowners running multi-generation village holdings to landless tenant laborers ground down by famine, taxes, and natural disaster. Farmers were forbidden to leave their villages, change occupations, or wear silk. The shogunate's view of peasants was famously summarized in a quotation attributed to Tokugawa-era officials: peasants should be "neither alive nor dead" — kept just affluent enough to keep paying taxes, never affluent enough to demand anything else. (Whether anyone actually said this is debated; the sentiment is documented.)

**Kō** — **artisans** (KOH, shokunin, shoh-KOO-nin), the producing class. Roughly 5% of the population. Craftsmen, builders, blacksmiths, lacquerers, papermakers, swordsmiths, carpenters, dyers. They lived in the cities, organized in guilds, and produced the material culture of Edo Japan. Their position above merchants reflected the Confucian principle that those who make things are more valuable than those who only trade them.

**Shō** — **merchants** (SHOH, chōnin, CHOH-nin), the trading class. Roughly 5–7% of the population. Bottom of the Confucian hierarchy because, in the official theory, they produced nothing — they only moved goods, lent money, and skimmed off the productive labor of others. In practice, they were the people who ran the commercial economy, financed the daimyo, and (by the eighteenth century) controlled most of the country's liquid wealth.

Below the four classes were two excluded categories: the **eta** (EH-tah) and **hinin** (HEE-nin), the "pollution" castes who handled leather, butchering, executioner duties, undertaking, and similar work considered ritually impure under Buddhist and Shinto traditions. They lived in segregated communities, were excluded from the temple registration system that defined Japanese identity, and faced legal and social discrimination that persisted (in attenuated form) long after the Meiji-era abolition of the formal class system. The descendants of the eta-hinin communities, now generally called **burakumin** (boo-rah-KOO-min), still face residual discrimination in modern Japan.

The four-class system was meant to be hereditary and immutable. In practice, mobility happened — wealthy merchants could buy samurai status by marrying into impoverished samurai families and adopting samurai surnames; samurai who could not pay their debts sometimes fell out of the class entirely; some peasants in commercial regions effectively functioned as merchants. But the formal hierarchy held until 1869, when the new Meiji government abolished the four classes by decree.

---

The fourth component was the controlled apparatus of religion.

Buddhism became, in effect, a state-sponsored institution. The **terauke** (teh-rah-OO-keh) system, formalized in the 1640s, required every Japanese household to register with a designated Buddhist temple and obtain certificates of religious affiliation that functioned as identity documents. Originally the system was created to identify and screen for crypto-Christians (Chapter 3), but it quickly became a comprehensive population register — the first such system in any major state, predating European census-keeping by two centuries. Temple priests issued the certificates; the shogunate used the registers to track migration, marriage, and inheritance; and the temples drew steady income from registration fees and required services.

Shinto, the indigenous Japanese spiritual tradition (Ancient Japan Ch 3), was incorporated into the system at the level of the **Ise Grand Shrine** (Mie Prefecture, the central Shinto shrine, dedicated to the sun goddess Amaterasu) and the various shrine networks across the country. Mass pilgrimages to Ise — the **okage-mairi** (oh-KAH-geh MY-ree) movements — periodically swept through the country, with several million commoners temporarily abandoning their villages to walk to Ise in waves that the shogunate could neither prevent nor control. The Ise Grand Shrine continued its ancient practice of ritual rebuilding every twenty years (still observed in the modern era), with the wooden shrine buildings reconstructed identically on adjacent plots in alternation. The Tokugawa state subsidized this practice.

Christianity, by contrast, was a capital crime, and we'll spend Chapter 3 on how that came to be.

Confucianism — particularly the **Neo-Confucianism** developed by the Song Dynasty Chinese philosopher **Zhu Xi** in the twelfth century — became the official intellectual framework of the samurai class and the curriculum of samurai education. The Tokugawa state founded the **Shōheikō** academy in Edo in 1690 to train officials in Zhu Xi orthodoxy, and similar academies opened in domain capitals across the country. The Neo-Confucian emphasis on hierarchy, loyalty, filial piety, and the rectification of names provided ideological cover for the entire Tokugawa social order.

The fifth component was the controlled apparatus of foreign relations — but that requires its own chapter.

Cross-civ check: the Tokugawa system Ieyasu and his successors built was structurally similar to the **Ming Dynasty** Chinese system in several respects (Confucian orthodoxy, suspicion of merchants, controlled foreign trade through designated ports, tributary diplomacy) but very different in others (no civil service exam, no centralized national army, no uniform tax code). The closest contemporary parallel is, surprisingly, the **Habsburg** system in central Europe — a central monarchy presiding over a federation of semi-autonomous noble territories, balancing rival aristocratic factions, controlling commerce through chartered monopolies, restricting religious heterodoxy. The Habsburgs were no more successful than the Tokugawa at preventing the long-term erosion of their structural foundations, and both systems collapsed in the same nineteenth-century wave of modernization. The Tokugawa held on longer than they would have any right to expect, given the structural strain.

# Chapter 3 — Sakoku and the Christian Purge

The Tokugawa shogunate's most distinctive policy — the one that defines the Edo period in popular imagination — was its near-total isolation from the rest of the world for over two centuries. The Japanese term for this policy is **sakoku** (SAH-koh-koo, "closed country"), though the term itself was not in use until the nineteenth century — Edo-era Japanese simply referred to it as the system of foreign relations.

The decision to close Japan was not made all at once. It accumulated through a series of edicts between 1612 and 1639, driven by an escalating fear of foreign influence — specifically, the Catholic Christianity that Portuguese and Spanish missionaries had been spreading in Japan since the 1540s. To understand the closure, you have to understand the Christian century that preceded it.

---

Christianity arrived in Japan in 1549, when the Spanish Jesuit missionary **Francis Xavier** (FRAN-sis ZAY-vee-er) landed at Kagoshima on the southern tip of Kyushu. Xavier was one of the founding members of the **Society of Jesus** (the Jesuits, the Catholic missionary order founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540, specifically organized for global missionary work in the wake of the Reformation). The Jesuits had a strategy: target the elites first, accommodate local customs as much as Catholic doctrine permitted, and rely on prestige rather than force to convert populations. In Japan they applied this strategy effectively. They learned Japanese, dressed in Japanese clothing, adopted Japanese manners, and built personal relationships with daimyo and senior samurai.

The conversion campaign succeeded spectacularly. By the 1580s, several daimyo on Kyushu had converted to Christianity and brought their domains with them — most notably **Ōmura Sumitada** (the first Christian daimyo, baptized 1563) and **Ōtomo Sōrin**, who at his peak controlled most of central Kyushu and treated Christianity as the official religion of his domain. The southern port city of **Nagasaki** (nah-gah-SAH-kee) was effectively a Christian city by the 1580s, with thirty churches, a Jesuit seminary, and a cathedral. Estimates of the Christian population in Japan at the 1614 expulsion edict range from 300,000 to 500,000 — perhaps 2% of the country's roughly 25 million people, concentrated heavily in Kyushu.

The Spanish and Portuguese behind the missionaries also brought trade. The Portuguese **Black Ship** (kurofune, koo-roh-FOO-neh — the same word later applied to Perry's vessels in 1853, and a useful label for "alarming foreign ship" in general) arrived at Nagasaki annually, carrying Chinese silk that the Ming dynasty had banned from being sold directly to Japan, plus European weapons, clocks, and curiosities. The trade was lucrative for the Portuguese, the Christian daimyo, and the central government — when there was one to participate.

This was the situation Hideyoshi inherited in 1582. Initially he tolerated the missionaries; in 1587, abruptly and without warning, he issued the **Bateren Tsuihōrei** ("Edict of Expulsion of Padres") ordering all Jesuits out of Japan. The edict was not enforced — Hideyoshi needed Portuguese trade for his Korean campaigns, and the Jesuits stayed — but it signaled that the political weather was changing.

The change accelerated under the Tokugawa.

---

Ieyasu's reasons for ultimately suppressing Christianity were several, and worth distinguishing from the simplified version that emphasizes only religious intolerance.

First, **theological**. Christianity made exclusive claims that conflicted with the Buddhist–Shinto–Confucian synthesis underpinning the Tokugawa social order. Christian doctrine held that Japanese ancestors were in hell because they had not been baptized, that the emperor's sun-goddess descent was false, and that loyalty to God superseded loyalty to the shogun. This was not a religious tradition the shogunate could simply incorporate the way it incorporated Buddhist sects.

Second, **political**. The Christian daimyo in Kyushu were a coordinated subgroup with foreign backers. Their loyalty to the Tokugawa was conditional on the Tokugawa tolerating their faith. If push came to shove, where would they ultimately stand? The shogunate could not afford to find out.

Third, **geopolitical**. By the early seventeenth century, news had filtered back to Japan about what European powers had done in the **Philippines** (Spanish conquest beginning 1565) and parts of Asia. The Spanish friar **Bernardino de Avila Girón** wrote candidly in his Japanese-language sermons that the missionaries were the advance guard of conquest. Several Spanish friars were said to have boasted to Japanese audiences that the king of Spain would invade Japan if the Japanese resisted Christianity. Whether or not these threats were real, the shogunate took them seriously.

Fourth, **commercial**. As long as Portuguese trade was indispensable, the shogunate had to tolerate Portuguese missionaries. By the early seventeenth century, the **Dutch** had arrived (the Dutch ship **Liefde** wrecked on the Kyushu coast in 1600, with the English navigator **William Adams** aboard — Adams became Ieyasu's adviser on Western affairs and lived out his life in Japan). The Dutch were Protestants, had no missionary interest in Japan, and were happy to trade silk and silver without trying to convert anyone. They offered an alternative to the Portuguese arrangement, and the shogunate eventually took it.

In 1614, Ieyasu issued the **Christian Expulsion Edict**, banning the practice of Christianity, expelling all foreign missionaries, and confiscating Christian properties. The 1614 edict was followed by a series of intensifications under Hidetada and Iemitsu. By the 1620s, the persecution had reached the level of systematic atrocity. Christians who refused to renounce their faith were tortured by methods specifically chosen to maximize prolonged agony — suspension upside-down over a pit of excrement, scalding with boiling water from the **Unzen** sulfur springs, slow crucifixion. Estimates vary widely, but somewhere between 5,000 and 6,000 Japanese Christians were executed for their faith between 1614 and the 1640s, in addition to perhaps tens of thousands killed in associated military operations. The Catholic Church has formally recognized 188 Japanese martyrs from this period, with hundreds more under consideration.

The shogunate also developed the **fumi-e** (FOO-mee EH, "trampling picture") test — bronze plaques bearing images of Christ or the Virgin Mary, which suspected Christians were forced to step on. Refusal meant arrest, torture, and likely execution. The fumi-e test was administered annually at Nagasaki to the entire population well into the eighteenth century, and continued in attenuated form until the 1850s.

---

The ultimate purge was triggered by the **Shimabara Rebellion** of 1637–38, the largest armed revolt of the Edo period.

The setting was the **Shimabara Peninsula** (shee-mah-BAH-rah) and the **Amakusa Islands** (ah-mah-KOO-sah) on western Kyushu — historically Christian regions where forced apostasy and crushing taxation by local daimyo had produced a desperate population. In late 1637, peasants and rōnin (masterless samurai) led by a charismatic seventeen-year-old named **Amakusa Shirō** (ah-mah-KOO-sah SHEE-roh) rose in rebellion. The rebels were heavily Christian — many bore Christian banners, called themselves Christian names, and reportedly believed that Shirō was a returning savior who would lead them to victory. They captured the abandoned **Hara Castle** on the southeastern Shimabara coast and fortified it.

The shogunate dispatched an army of over 100,000 troops drawn from neighboring daimyo, reinforced by Dutch warship cannons (the Dutch at Hirado bombarded Hara from the sea in support of the besieging army — buying the Dutch credit with the shogunate that would prove valuable when Catholic-Protestant distinctions were being made). The siege of Hara Castle lasted from December 1637 to April 1638. When the castle finally fell on April 15, 1638, the besiegers killed almost the entire population — an estimated 37,000 rebels, plus their families. Shirō was beheaded and his head displayed at Nagasaki as a warning. The Shimabara region was depopulated and resettled with reliable Buddhist immigrants.

The Shimabara Rebellion convinced the shogunate that residual Christianity was an existential threat, and that the Portuguese in Japan were a permanent vector for it. Iemitsu acted decisively.

In 1639, the **Portuguese were expelled from Japan**, completing the closure that had been building for a generation. To make the point unmistakable, when a Portuguese embassy from Macau arrived at Nagasaki in 1640 to negotiate reopening trade, sixty-one of the seventy delegation members were beheaded on arrival; the surviving thirteen were sent back to Macau with their colleagues' heads to deliver the message that there would be no more discussion. The shogunate also issued the comprehensive sakoku edicts of 1635 (banning Japanese travel abroad) and 1639 (banning Portuguese entry), formalizing the closure as a permanent policy.

For the next 214 years, Japan was effectively sealed.

---

"Effectively sealed" is doing some work in that sentence, because sakoku was never absolute.

There were four authorized channels of foreign contact, sometimes called the **four mouths** (yotsu no kuchi, YOH-tsoo NOH KOO-chee):

The **Nagasaki mouth** — the most famous, controlled directly by the shogunate. After 1641, all European trade was confined to a single Dutch trading station on the artificial fan-shaped island of **Dejima** (DEH-jee-mah, literally "exit island") in Nagasaki harbor. Dejima was a tiny artificial island connected to the mainland by a single guarded bridge. About twenty Dutch employees lived there at any given time, in a compound roughly the size of a city block. They were forbidden to enter Nagasaki without permission, prohibited from practicing Christianity in any visible form (including burials — Dutch employees who died on Dejima were dropped at sea), and required to participate in elaborate annual rituals demonstrating subordination to the shogun. They were allowed to import Chinese silk, European clocks and instruments, books on certain subjects, and they were allowed to export Japanese silver and copper. Dejima operated continuously from 1641 to 1858, the longest-running European trading post in Asia. Chinese merchants also had a designated quarter at Nagasaki, somewhat less constrained than the Dutch, and the volume of Chinese trade was actually considerably larger than Dutch trade through most of the period.

The **Tsushima mouth** — the Tsushima domain in the Korea Strait managed all Japanese-Korean diplomacy and trade through a Japanese trading post at **Busan** on the Korean coast. After Hideyoshi's catastrophic Korean invasions (Chapter 1), the Tokugawa restored relations with **Joseon Korea** and maintained periodic exchanges of envoys. Twelve Korean diplomatic missions to Edo took place between 1607 and 1811, each one a major political and cultural event in Japan, with the Korean delegations of several hundred people processing along the Tōkaidō to Edo and being received by the shogun in elaborate ceremony. Trade through Tsushima was substantial — Korean ginseng, Chinese silk routed through Korea, and various luxury goods flowed in; Japanese silver, copper, and lacquerware flowed out.

The **Satsuma mouth** — the Satsuma domain in southern Kyushu had invaded the **Ryukyu Kingdom** (modern Okinawa) in 1609 and thereafter controlled an indirect channel of trade with China through Ryukyuan tributary missions to Beijing. The Ryukyuan king was technically a Chinese tributary and technically a Satsuma vassal — a useful ambiguity that allowed Japanese trade with Ming and then Qing China to continue under Ryukyuan cover throughout the sakoku period.

The **Matsumae mouth** — the Matsumae domain at the southern tip of **Hokkaido** (then called **Ezo**, EH-zoh) managed trade with the indigenous **Ainu** (EYE-noo, the Jōmon-descended people of Hokkaido — Ancient Japan Ch 1 and Ch 8) and indirectly with the Russians who began appearing in Hokkaido waters in the eighteenth century. The Ainu trade was small in volume but politically sensitive, especially after Russian expansion brought European powers to Japan's northern frontier.

The four mouths together represented less than 1% of the volume of foreign trade Japan had conducted in the early seventeenth century. The closure was not a fiction. Foreign goods became expensive luxuries, foreign news arrived in fragments, foreign techniques (especially in medicine and military technology) fell behind. By the early nineteenth century, the gap between Japanese and Western military technology had grown so large that it could no longer be denied — but that crisis is for Chapter 8.

---

Cross-civ check: sakoku is sometimes presented as a uniquely Japanese eccentricity, but it was actually a recognizable variant of a broader early-modern East Asian pattern. **Ming China** had operated under similar restrictions through much of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries — the famous **haijin** ("sea ban") prohibited private foreign trade, restricted maritime movement, and channeled all foreign relations through the tributary system. **Joseon Korea** maintained an even stricter closure, conducting nearly all foreign relations through the Tsushima channel and maintaining only token contact with the Qing Dynasty — earning Korea the European nickname "the Hermit Kingdom." The Tokugawa system was actually more open than the Korean equivalent. Within the broader pattern, what made Japan distinctive was the duration (over two centuries), the systematic enforcement, and the particular fixation on Christianity as the existential threat. The closest religious-suppression parallel is the **Spanish Inquisition's** persecution of crypto-Jews and crypto-Muslims — same period, comparable methodology of forced apostasy and ritual humiliation tests, but conducted in the opposite direction (Christian state suppressing non-Christian heresies, vs. non-Christian state suppressing Christianity).

The other thing to note about sakoku is that it was not a passive choice. It was an active policy that required continuous administrative effort to maintain — coastal patrols, the fumi-e test, the temple registration system that screened for crypto-Christians (Chapter 2), the Dejima isolation, the periodic interrogations of Dutch ship arrivals about world events. The shogunate spent considerable resources to keep Japan closed. They thought it was worth it.

The chapter that follows shows what the closed country produced.

# Chapter 4 — Genroku Culture

If you had to pick one fifteen-year stretch as the moment when Edo Japan's culture became something distinctively Japanese and distinctively new, it would be the **Genroku** era (gen-ROH-koo), the imperial era name covering 1688 to 1704.

This is the period of **Matsuo Bashō**'s haiku, **Ihara Saikaku**'s novels, **Chikamatsu Monzaemon**'s puppet plays, the early masterpieces of **kabuki**, the explosion of single-sheet **ukiyo-e** woodblock prints, and the founding of the **Osaka Rice Exchange** — the world's first organized commodity futures market, eighty years before the **Chicago Board of Trade** would be founded in the United States. The shogunate during these fifteen years was the eccentric **Tokugawa Tsunayoshi** (toh-koo-GAH-wah tsoo-nah-YOH-shee), the so-called Dog Shogun, whose reputation has fluctuated wildly over the centuries (mocked by his contemporaries, recovered slightly by modern revisionists). What is not in doubt is that during his reign, Japanese popular culture took on the form it would hold until Perry's arrival.

This chapter is about the culture, not the politics — though the politics produced the conditions that made the culture possible.

---

By 1688, Edo had been the de facto capital for eighty-five years. The sankin-kōtai system (Chapter 2) had drawn the daimyo aristocracy into permanent residence, and the city had grown to roughly a million people. The peace had held; merchants had accumulated wealth; the technologies of urban life (water supply, fire-control, paved streets, postal services, lending libraries) had been built out. The two other great cities — **Kyoto**, the ancient imperial capital and the center of refined high culture, and **Osaka**, the merchant capital and commercial heart of the country — had grown to several hundred thousand each. By 1700, Japan was probably the most urbanized country in the world: roughly 10–12% of the population lived in cities of 10,000 or more, a higher proportion than any European country at the time except the Dutch Republic.

This urbanization produced a new kind of audience. The merchant and artisan classes (the **chōnin**, choh-NIN, "townspeople," covering the bottom two classes of the official four-tier order — Chapter 2) had money, leisure, literacy, and an appetite for cultural products that the older Buddhist–aristocratic culture did not satisfy. They wanted entertainment, romance, spectacle, satire, gossip, and beauty. The Genroku culture is what emerged when a generation of Japanese artists, writers, and entrepreneurs figured out how to give them what they wanted at scale.

The aesthetic that organized this culture had a name: **ukiyo** (oo-kee-YOH), the **floating world**.

The term itself has a Buddhist origin — *ukiyo* in older usage meant "the sad world" of human suffering, the world the Buddhist seeker tried to transcend. By the late seventeenth century, the homophone *ukiyo* (written with different characters) had been repurposed to mean "the floating world" — the world of pleasure, transient beauty, fashion, sensual delight, and aesthetic refinement. The shift from "sad world" to "floating world" through pure pun is the kind of thing the period did constantly, and it captured the new sensibility perfectly. Life is short, the merchant-poet **Asai Ryōi** wrote in his 1666 *Tales of the Floating World*, so we should give ourselves to enjoying every moment of it: living for the moment, gazing at the moon, the snow, the cherry blossoms, the maple leaves, drinking sake, singing songs, drifting along the river of pleasure like a gourd carried by the current — and not letting the misery of life trouble us. This was the antithesis of the samurai bushido ethos. It was also, very deliberately, the cultural alternative the chōnin built for themselves.

The floating world had physical addresses. The most famous was the **Yoshiwara** (yoh-shee-WAH-rah) pleasure quarter, north of central Edo. After the Great Meireki Fire of 1657 destroyed most of the city (the worst urban disaster of the entire Edo period — somewhere around 100,000 people killed in three days, with roughly two-thirds of Edo burned to the ground), the shogunate took the opportunity to relocate Yoshiwara from its central location to a more peripheral site, where it grew into a walled and moated entertainment district housing several thousand licensed prostitutes, hundreds of teahouses, restaurants, theaters, and an entire support industry. Yoshiwara had its own ranks of courtesans (the highest-ranked, the **tayū**, were closer to celebrity entertainers than sex workers — they were trained in poetry, calligraphy, music, dance, the tea ceremony, and could refuse clients they did not like). It had its own dialect, its own etiquette, its own ceremonial calendar of seasonal events. It functioned as the cultural laboratory of Edo, the place where new fashions, new music, new slang, and new sexual practices first appeared and from which they spread to the rest of the city. Similar quarters existed in Osaka (**Shinmachi**) and Kyoto (**Shimabara** — confusingly, the same name as the rebellion location, but referring to the licensed quarter in Kyoto).

The floating world also had its theaters.

---

**Kabuki** (kah-BOO-kee, "leaning, off-center, avant-garde") had begun in the early seventeenth century as performances by all-female troupes (and occasionally female-and-male mixed troupes) at riverbank stages in Kyoto, mixing dance, song, and short skits with offstage prostitution. The shogunate considered the combination disruptive of public order and banned women from the stage in 1629, then banned young male performers (who had filled the same role with the same complications) in 1652. From then on, kabuki was performed exclusively by adult male actors, with male specialists called **onnagata** (on-nah-GAH-tah) playing all female roles in highly stylized costume, makeup, and movement that was understood to represent a kind of distilled essence of femininity rather than a literal impersonation.

The bans and restrictions, paradoxically, made kabuki into a serious art form. The forced focus on craft, training, and aesthetic discipline produced acting families whose techniques were passed down across generations — the **Ichikawa Danjūrō** lineage (ee-chee-KAH-wah dan-JOO-roh, founded by the first Danjūrō in 1660, still active today as the twelfth and thirteenth in the line) became the most prestigious of the Edo families. By the Genroku period, kabuki had developed its full repertoire of stylized acting techniques (the dramatic frozen pose called **mie**, the stylized walk, the slow dramatic tempo punctuated by sudden violent movement), elaborate costumes and stage machinery, and a body of standard plays drawn from history and contemporary sensational events. The most famous Genroku play, **Sukeroku** (premiered 1713 — slightly post-Genroku but quintessential of the period), set in Yoshiwara and featuring a flamboyant samurai hero who turns out to be a fugitive in disguise, became one of the foundational plays of the kabuki tradition.

**Bunraku** (boon-RAH-koo) — the puppet theater — developed in parallel and arguably reached even higher artistic peaks during the Genroku era. Bunraku puppets are roughly three feet tall, manipulated by three black-clad puppeteers each (one for the head and right arm, one for the left arm, one for the legs), accompanied by a single chanter who voices all the characters and a single shamisen player who provides musical accompaniment. The technical complexity is extraordinary; a master puppeteer trains for thirty years before being permitted to manipulate a head. Done well, bunraku produces an emotional intensity that often eclipses the kabuki versions of the same plays, and the great Genroku-era playwright **Chikamatsu Monzaemon** (chee-kah-MAT-soo mon-zah-EH-mon) wrote primarily for the puppets.

Chikamatsu's masterpiece, **The Love Suicides at Sonezaki** (premiered 1703), dramatized a real-life double suicide between a paper merchant and a Yoshiwara courtesan that had taken place in Osaka the previous month. The play was an immediate sensation, and it set a pattern that Chikamatsu would repeat in several subsequent works: a sympathetic merchant-class hero, a sympathetic courtesan he genuinely loves, a ruthless social-economic pressure that makes their love impossible, and a final act in which the lovers travel through a poetic landscape to the suicide site (the famous **michiyuki** or "going-along-the-road" scene that became one of the great set-piece conventions of bunraku and kabuki). The merchant-class audience saw their own world reflected with unprecedented seriousness and depth. The plays were so successful that they triggered an actual epidemic of double suicides in imitation, prompting the shogunate to ban dramatizations of contemporary suicides — a ban routinely circumvented by setting the plays in the recent past.

---

The other great Genroku innovation was **ukiyo-e** (oo-kee-YOH-eh, "pictures of the floating world") — single-sheet woodblock prints sold cheaply to the urban market, depicting kabuki actors, Yoshiwara courtesans, sumo wrestlers, scenes from popular plays, landscapes, and (later) erotic illustrations.

The technology was old; the application was new. Woodblock printing had existed in Japan since the eighth century (imported from China, originally for Buddhist sutras), but ukiyo-e took the same technology and applied it to mass-produced popular images at low cost. A single ukiyo-e print sold for roughly the price of a bowl of noodles — affordable to almost anyone with a regular income, immediately throwaway, designed to be consumed and replaced as fashions shifted. The technical sophistication of the prints would increase dramatically over the next century and a half, eventually producing the masterpieces of **Hokusai**, **Hiroshige**, and **Utamaro** (Chapter 7), but the basic Genroku-era prints by **Hishikawa Moronobu** (hee-shee-KAH-wah moh-roh-NOH-boo, 1618–1694) and his students established the format and the market.

What is striking about the Genroku culture in retrospect is how little it owed to either the samurai aristocracy or the imperial-court tradition. The chōnin had built their own parallel high culture from the ground up. The poetry was different — the **haiku** (HIGH-koo, "starting verse"), seventeen syllables in a five-seven-five pattern, often presenting a juxtaposition of two contrasting images, had been refined by **Matsuo Bashō** (1644–1694) into a serious literary form that drew on Chinese poetic traditions while being distinctively Japanese. Bashō's most famous work, **Oku no Hosomichi** ("The Narrow Road to the Deep North," published posthumously in 1702), recounted a 1,500-mile (2,400 km) walking journey through northern Honshu in 1689, interspersing prose travel narrative with haiku written along the way. The book's blend of physical journey, spiritual journey, literary allusion, and observation of ordinary life remains the single most influential work in Japanese literature.

The novels were different — **Ihara Saikaku** (ee-HAH-rah sye-KAH-koo, 1642–1693), an Osaka merchant-turned-poet, wrote a series of **ukiyo-zōshi** ("books of the floating world") novels in the 1680s that essentially invented Japanese realist popular fiction. *The Life of an Amorous Man* (1682), *The Life of an Amorous Woman* (1686), *Five Women Who Loved Love* (1686), and *The Eternal Storehouse of Japan* (1688) presented merchant-class characters dealing with money, sex, family obligation, and social embarrassment in a tone that was simultaneously cynical, sympathetic, sexually frank, and moralistic. They sold in thousands of copies. They drew the merchant world for the merchant world.

The visual arts were different. The performing arts were different. The architecture of the entertainment districts was different. By 1700, Edo had a distinctively chōnin culture that the samurai class observed with disapproval, the imperial court ignored, and the merchants themselves embraced as their own — the first major popular-cultural formation in Japanese history that did not derive its prestige from elite patronage.

---

The economic foundation of all this was a commercial economy that was, by 1700, the most sophisticated in the world.

The shogunate's fiscal system was based on rice. Daimyo were ranked by **koku** assessment, samurai were paid in koku stipends, and tax was collected in koku of rice from the peasantry. But the economy beyond government was monetized — Edo Japan used gold coins (the **koban**, KOH-ban — the iconic oval gold piece with the Tokugawa crest), silver coins (measured by weight, not face value), and copper coins for daily transactions. The exchange rates between gold, silver, and copper fluctuated; entire merchant fortunes were made by **ryōgaeshō** (ree-YOH-gah-eh-shoh) money-changers arbitraging between the three currencies. By the late seventeenth century, the country had national-scale wholesale markets, futures contracts, bills of exchange, joint-stock arrangements, insurance schemes, and a banking infrastructure that paralleled the contemporary developments in Amsterdam and London.

The most famous institution was the **Dōjima Rice Exchange** (DOH-jee-mah, in Osaka), formally licensed by the shogunate in 1697 after operating informally for several decades. The exchange was where daimyo, who needed to convert their tax-rice into cash to pay their sankin-kōtai expenses (Chapter 2), sold rice futures contracts. The contracts specified a certain quantity of rice at a certain quality grade to be delivered at a specified future date, and they could be bought, sold, and resold before the delivery date. The Dōjima Exchange had standardized contract sizes, formal trading hours, organized clearing of contracts at the close of each session, and a system of margin deposits to enforce performance. It was, by every reasonable definition, the world's first organized commodity futures market — predating the Chicago Board of Trade by 151 years and the formalized Amsterdam grain market by similar margins.

The rest of the commercial infrastructure was equally innovative. The **House of Mitsui**, founded in Kyoto in 1673 by **Mitsui Takatoshi** (MIT-soo-ee tah-kah-TOH-shee), opened a dry-goods store in Edo with the radical innovation of fixed prices and cash-only sales (rather than the traditional Japanese practice of bargaining and seasonal credit). The model spread, the Mitsui family expanded into money-changing and commodity trading, and by the early eighteenth century Mitsui was one of the great financial houses of Japan — the direct ancestor of the modern Mitsui *zaibatsu* (financial conglomerate) that became one of Meiji-era Japan's industrial pillars.

This was the infrastructure that supported the Genroku culture. Bashō could afford to spend years walking through northern Honshu writing haiku because there was an audience of literate urban consumers to buy the books that resulted. Chikamatsu could write puppet plays for a living because the bunraku theater drew audiences large enough to sustain a professional playwright. Saikaku could publish satirical novels because there were printers and bookstores willing to invest in the production. The cultural flowering rode on the back of an economic infrastructure the political-philosophical framework of the Tokugawa state never quite managed to acknowledge.

---

The shogun who presided over all this was **Tokugawa Tsunayoshi** (1646–1709, fifth shogun from 1680), and his reputation deserves a moment.

Tsunayoshi was a serious Confucian scholar — he lectured personally on the Confucian classics, founded the Shōheikō academy in 1690 (Chapter 2), and cultivated literary patronage. He was also responsible for the most ridiculed series of edicts in Edo history: the **Shōrui Awaremi no Rei** ("Edicts of Compassion for Living Beings"), issued from 1687 onward, which prohibited cruelty to animals on a scale that exceeded any rational interpretation of the Buddhist precepts they invoked. Killing a dog was a capital crime. Abandoning a sick horse could lead to exile. Mosquitos and flies were technically protected. The shogunate built **Nakano Dog Kennels** northwest of Edo to house an estimated 50,000 stray dogs at public expense, with the dogs fed rice, fish, and miso paid for from the bakufu treasury. Edo residents took to calling Tsunayoshi the **Inu Kubō** (EE-noo KOO-boh, "Dog Shogun"). The edicts were repealed immediately after his death in 1709 by his successor, who killed the dogs.

The dogs aside, Tsunayoshi was an effective patron of high culture, an accomplished Confucian scholar, and a sponsor of the institutions that made Genroku possible. He was also fiscally disastrous — the bakufu's first major budget crisis, requiring debasement of the gold and silver currency in 1695, happened on his watch. The pattern of cultural florescence riding on top of a fiscal crisis would repeat throughout the Edo period.

Cross-civ check: the Genroku era roughly corresponds to the European **Baroque** period — Vivaldi, Bach, Newton, Locke, the founding of the Royal Society, the late phase of the Dutch Golden Age. **Louis XIV** of France was at the height of his reign at Versailles. **Aurangzeb's** Mughal Empire (Mughal Ch 7) was reaching its largest territorial extent in India. The **Qing Dynasty** under the **Kangxi Emperor** was consolidating Chinese rule in the early phase of what would become the **High Qing** golden age. **Peter the Great** was beginning the modernization of Russia. **Sir Christopher Wren** was building the new St. Paul's Cathedral in London after the 1666 Great Fire. The Genroku era was a moment of unusual cultural and economic dynamism across Eurasia, and Edo Japan was a full participant — even if the country was technically closed.

# Chapter 5 — The 47 Rōnin

This chapter departs from the chronological flow to tell a single story.

The story is the **Akō incident** (AH-koh) of 1701–1703, also known as the **47 Rōnin Incident**. It is, by some considerable margin, the most famous individual story of the Edo period — the subject of dozens of plays, hundreds of films, countless novels, woodblock prints by every major ukiyo-e artist, and the subject of an annual commemoration ceremony in Tokyo that still draws tens of thousands of visitors. It is the closest thing Japan has to a national legend on the scale of Robin Hood or El Cid. It is also, structurally, the moment when Japan publicly confronted what was happening to its samurai class — and what the four-class hierarchy actually meant in a society without wars.

This requires some setup, because the meaning of the story depends on context the modern reader has to be told.

---

By 1700, the samurai class had a problem that everyone in Japan recognized but nobody could openly discuss. The samurai were a hereditary warrior aristocracy in a country that had been at peace for nearly a century. Most samurai did no fighting and would do no fighting for the rest of their lives. Their fixed rice stipends had been losing value to inflation since the Genroku currency debasements (Chapter 4), and many samurai were in chronic debt to the very merchants whom the four-class hierarchy placed officially below them. The romantic ethic of bushido — service unto death, loyalty to the lord above all other considerations, the warrior's readiness to die at any moment — had become, for many, a private nostalgia for a way of life their ancestors had practiced and they themselves never would.

Most samurai, as a result, lived ordinary administrative lives. They worked as domain officials, accountants, magistrates, schoolteachers, scholars. They paid their bills, raised their children, attended Confucian lectures, and (privately) chafed at the gap between what they had been raised to believe they were and what they actually did with their days.

The Akō incident is the story of what happened when one group of samurai chose, in a single dramatic moment, to act out the bushido ideal completely.

---

The setting was **Edo Castle** in 1701. The ceremonial occasion was a formal reception in the spring for envoys from the imperial court at Kyoto, the kind of high-ceremony shogunal ritual that happened periodically and was managed by specialist court officials trained in the elaborate etiquette involved.

One of those specialist officials was **Kira Yoshinaka** (KEE-rah yoh-shee-NAH-kah), a high-ranking shogunate **kōke** ("master of court ceremony") in his early sixties. The kōke were not powerful daimyo — they held relatively small stipends — but they had immense influence over the protocol of state ceremonies, and they expected, and routinely received, generous gifts from the daimyo who had to perform under their guidance. The customary "presents" to the kōke were essentially a system of fees paid by daimyo for ceremonial coaching.

The two daimyo charged with hosting the imperial envoys at the 1701 ceremony were **Asano Naganori** (ah-SAH-noh nah-gah-NOH-ree), the lord of Akō (a small domain in modern Hyōgo Prefecture), and a colleague. Asano, by some accounts, refused to pay the customary fees to Kira (or paid them at a level Kira found inadequate); by other accounts, the dispute was over Asano's behavior or competence at the rehearsal. The exact provocation is unrecoverable from the historical sources. What is recoverable is what happened on March 14, 1701, in a corridor of Edo Castle: Asano drew his short sword and attacked Kira, slashing him across the forehead and shoulder before being restrained by other officials. Kira survived. Asano was arrested.

Drawing a sword inside Edo Castle was a capital offense regardless of provocation. It was also, under the dispute-resolution principle called **kenka ryōseibai** (KEN-kah ree-OH-say-bye, "both parties are equally guilty in a fight"), supposed to result in equal punishment for both sides of any quarrel — Asano for drawing his sword, Kira for whatever provocation he had offered. The shogunate, however, applied an asymmetric verdict: Asano was ordered that same day to commit ritual suicide (**seppuku**, sep-POO-koo) and his Akō domain was confiscated, his retainers dispossessed, his family extinguished. Kira received no punishment at all.

Asano committed seppuku that evening in the garden of the daimyo Tamura Tateaki, where he had been temporarily detained. He was thirty-four years old. He left a death poem expressing only that his last regret was not having achieved what he set out to do.

The verdict left roughly 320 of his samurai retainers as **rōnin** (ROH-nin, "wave-people," masterless samurai with no domain to serve and no income).

---

The rōnin had two options.

The conventional option was to accept the dispossession, find another lord to serve (most rōnin did, eventually), and let the matter drop. This was what the shogunate clearly expected, and what the surviving Asano family members urged. Pursuing revenge against Kira was forbidden by the shogunate's law on private vendettas, would result in execution or worse for everyone involved, and would not undo Asano's death.

The bushido option was vendetta. The traditional samurai code held that a retainer's loyalty did not end with the lord's death — and that the dishonor of an unjust lord's death was a stain that only blood revenge could erase. The Akō rōnin's chief retainer, the senior chamberlain **Ōishi Kuranosuke** (OH-ee-shee koo-rah-NOH-soo-keh), was forty-three years old and a competent administrator with no previous reputation as a particularly bushido-fixated figure. He had to choose.

He chose vendetta. But he also chose patience.

The plan, conceived over the months following Asano's death, was to wait. Kira would be on guard immediately after the incident — surrounded by additional retainers, his Edo mansion fortified, alert for any sign of Akō revenge. If the rōnin attacked too soon, they would be killed in the assault and accomplish nothing. If they waited long enough — months, perhaps years — Kira would relax, his security would slacken, and an opportunity might come.

So the rōnin dispersed. Ōishi himself moved to Kyoto, where he conspicuously took up residence in a brothel district, drank heavily, lost himself in dissipation, and (the chronicle accounts say) ignored his wife and children to the point that his wife divorced him and took the children to her family. The story spread that the Akō chamberlain had become a drunken degenerate, that the Akō samurai were broken and scattered, that there would be no revenge. Kira's spies confirmed the assessment. Kira's security relaxed.

In the autumn of 1702, the rōnin began secretly reassembling in Edo. They had identified the layout of Kira's mansion, the routine of his retainers, the ceremonial events that would draw down his guard. They acquired armor, weapons, and uniforms. They prepared rope, lanterns, ladders. Forty-six men committed to the operation; one had died of illness in the interim.

On the night of December 14, 1702 (in the Western calendar; the date in the Japanese calendar was the night of the fourteenth day of the twelfth month, year 15 of Genroku), in a heavy snowfall, the forty-six raiders attacked Kira's Edo mansion. They divided into two parties, breached the front and back gates simultaneously, and overwhelmed the surprised garrison in a brief but ferocious fight. Ōishi went to the room where Kira had been sleeping and found it empty. The raiders searched the mansion methodically — and found Kira hiding in a charcoal storage shed behind the building, a small old man in his nightclothes who had escaped through a back door during the initial confusion.

Ōishi offered Kira the chance to commit ritual suicide as a samurai. Kira refused. Ōishi beheaded him with the same short sword Asano had used to attempt his life on the day of the incident. The rōnin wrapped Kira's head in a cloth and carried it across Edo to the **Sengakuji** (sen-GAH-koo-jee) temple in southern Edo, where Asano was buried, washed the head ceremonially, and laid it on Asano's grave. Then they surrendered to the shogunate authorities and waited for the verdict.

---

The verdict took two months. The shogunate was confronted with a problem the legal system had no clean answer for.

On the one hand, the rōnin had committed a clear crime: armed assembly, breaking and entering, murder, violation of the ban on private vendettas. The legal penalty was unambiguous — execution as common criminals.

On the other hand, the rōnin had behaved exactly as the bushido ethic the shogunate officially endorsed required them to behave. They had served their lord even after his death, accepted the consequences without flinching, surrendered themselves rather than fleeing, and asked only for the privilege of dying as samurai rather than as criminals. To execute them as common criminals would have implicitly repudiated the bushido ethic and the entire warrior identity the four-class system was supposed to honor.

The shogunate's compromise verdict, issued in February 1703 after an extraordinarily long deliberation, was that the forty-six rōnin would be permitted to commit seppuku rather than be executed. They were divided among four daimyo households for the ceremony. They committed ritual suicide on March 20, 1703. They were buried at Sengakuji, beside Asano. The Akō family was eventually allowed to reestablish itself with a smaller domain.

The story was already a national sensation by the time the seppuku verdict was carried out. Within weeks of the verdict, the first dramatic versions began appearing on the Edo and Osaka stages — initially as kabuki and bunraku productions transposed to medieval Japan to evade the shogunate's ban on dramatizing contemporary political events. The most famous version, **Kanadehon Chūshingura** ("The Treasury of Loyal Retainers"), was written in 1748 by the playwright **Takeda Izumo II** and his collaborators, and remains the standard dramatic treatment to this day. Versions for kabuki, bunraku, the cinema (over fifty film adaptations), television, anime, and novels have continued to appear in every generation since 1703.

---

What the story meant, and means, is contested.

The conventional Japanese reading of the story is that the rōnin embodied the highest expression of bushido virtue: loyalty to a lord who could no longer reward them, willingness to wait years to fulfill an obligation, acceptance of certain death as the inevitable consequence of doing what honor required. Under this reading, they are heroes, and the Sengakuji graves are a shrine to a moral ideal that survived the disappearance of the social conditions that originally produced it.

A more critical reading notes several uncomfortable facts. The vendetta was a private quarrel, not a defense of any larger principle. Kira's actual offense was unclear — Asano may have lost his temper for reasons that did not justify what followed. The shogunate had explicit laws against exactly this kind of vigilante revenge, and the rōnin's act was, on one reasonable reading, less a defense of bushido than a defiance of legitimate authority. The romantic version that idealizes the rōnin's loyalty obscures the fact that what they actually did was murder a sleeping old man in his nightshirt — a fact the dramatic versions tend to elide.

There is also a structural reading that may be the most useful. The Akō incident happened at a moment when the samurai class was confronting, collectively and unconsciously, the question of what they were for. The bushido ethic had been developed for warriors who actually fought; the Edo samurai mostly did not fight. Forty-seven men, by acting out the bushido ethic literally, demonstrated that the ethic was still operative — that it could still produce dramatic action in extremis. The reception of the story across the eighteenth century, with its intensifying romantic glow, suggests that the samurai class found in the Akō rōnin a reassurance about its own meaning that the rest of its existence increasingly failed to provide. The rōnin became, retrospectively, the proof that bushido was not yet a hollow form.

The most influential codification of bushido as a coherent doctrine — **Yamamoto Tsunetomo**'s *Hagakure* ("In the Shadow of Leaves"), recorded in conversations between 1709 and 1716 — was produced in exactly the same generation as the Akō incident. *Hagakure* opens with the famous line: "The Way of the Samurai is found in death." The book's intensification of the death-readiness theme to almost suicidal levels is generally read by historians as a reaction to the very development that had made bushido decorative rather than practical: that the warrior class no longer had a war.

Cross-civ check: the Akō incident has structural analogues in many warrior-aristocracy traditions. The European chivalric tradition produced its own crisis of meaning when full-plate armor and cavalry charges became militarily obsolete after the gunpowder revolution; **Cervantes**'s *Don Quixote* (published 1605, six years after Sekigahara) is essentially the same problem worked out as comedy rather than tragedy — an aging knight commits himself to literal chivalric performance in a society that has moved on, with absurd results. The Akō rōnin and Don Quixote are, in a sense, the same character: people whose social role has lost its substantive function and who respond by performing the form even more intensely. Where Cervantes laughs at the result, the Japanese tradition canonized it. The difference says a lot about the two cultures' divergent handling of the warrior-aristocracy problem.

# Chapter 6 — Rangaku and the Knowledge Trickle

In 1771, three Japanese physicians attended an autopsy in Edo. The body was that of a fifty-year-old woman who had been executed for a crime — execution-grounds dissections were technically legal in some circumstances, though socially controversial — and the three physicians, **Sugita Genpaku** (soo-GHEE-tah gen-PAH-koo), **Maeno Ryōtaku** (mah-EH-noh ree-OH-tah-koo), and **Nakagawa Jun'an**, brought with them a borrowed copy of a Dutch anatomy textbook called **Tafel Anatomia** (originally published in German by Johann Adam Kulmus in 1722, translated into Dutch in 1734). The three physicians had been trained in **kanpō** (KAN-poh, traditional Chinese medicine, the official medical orthodoxy in Tokugawa Japan), but they had heard rumors that the Dutch books described human anatomy differently from the Chinese tradition, and they wanted to see for themselves.

What they saw at the autopsy was a complete refutation of the Chinese tradition.

The Dutch textbook's diagrams of the lungs, heart, intestines, and skeletal structure matched what they observed in the cadaver almost exactly. The classical Chinese medical description of internal anatomy — based on philosophical principles rather than dissection — did not match at all. Liver and stomach were in different positions than Chinese medicine described. Lung structure was different. The bones did not match. Working organ-by-organ down through the body, the three physicians confirmed that the Western book was correct on every specific the Chinese tradition was wrong on.

Sugita Genpaku later described in his memoir what the three of them did walking home that evening. They were stunned. They had spent their entire professional lives practicing a medical tradition that had core anatomical claims they could now see were factually incorrect. And the only window they had onto the correct knowledge — the only access they had to the Western scientific tradition that had produced the Tafel Anatomia — was a tiny Dutch trading post on a fan-shaped artificial island in Nagasaki harbor, several hundred miles away, that they could not visit and whose books they could only obtain through chains of intermediaries operating in legal grey zones.

So they decided to translate the book.

---

The translation took three years. None of the three physicians could read Dutch fluently. Maeno Ryōtaku had perhaps the strongest Dutch — he had spent some years studying with the small group of officially licensed Nagasaki Dutch interpreters — but even he was working far above his fluency level. The three of them reportedly held weekly translation sessions, sitting around the Tafel Anatomia for hours, debating individual sentences, working from a Japanese-Dutch phrase book that Maeno had compiled, sometimes spending entire afternoons trying to decipher a single technical term. The completed translation, **Kaitai Shinsho** ("New Book of Anatomy"), was published in 1774 — the first Western scientific work translated directly from a European language into Japanese.

Kaitai Shinsho was not just a translation. It was a manifesto. It demonstrated, with unanswerable clarity, that there was a body of European technical knowledge that was demonstrably superior to the Chinese tradition Japan had been working with for a millennium. It also demonstrated that this knowledge could, with sufficient effort, be acquired and used by Japanese scholars working entirely within the constraints of sakoku. The book launched the field that would come to be known as **Rangaku** (RAN-gah-koo, "Dutch learning").

This chapter is about Rangaku — what it was, how it worked, what it produced, and why it mattered.

---

The institutional infrastructure of Rangaku was tiny, fragile, and built almost by accident.

It started with **Tokugawa Yoshimune** (toh-koo-GAH-wah yoh-shee-MOO-neh), the eighth shogun (ruled 1716–1745), who is generally considered the most capable Tokugawa ruler after Ieyasu. Yoshimune inherited a bakufu in fiscal crisis, instituted the **Kyōhō Reforms** (austerity measures, expansion of rice cultivation, encouragement of sweet potato planting against famine), and made a small but consequential decision in 1720: he partially lifted the ban on the importation of Western books at Nagasaki.

The book ban itself was a holdover from the anti-Christian purges of the seventeenth century (Chapter 3). Western books in Chinese translation had been banned because they often contained Christian content — but they also contained, by 1720, an enormous body of European mathematical, astronomical, geographical, and medical knowledge that the Chinese had been studying since the Jesuit missions to Beijing in the early seventeenth century. Yoshimune, who had personal interests in calendar reform, astronomy, and Western horse breeds for the shogun's stables, decided that the practical value of the technical content outweighed the residual Christian risk. After 1720, books that were not directly religious in subject matter could be imported through Nagasaki and circulated in Japan.

The decision was not framed as a major reform — Yoshimune did not announce a new policy, did not invite Western scholars, did not relax the ban on Christianity itself. The 1720 lifting was a small administrative change that opened a narrow channel. But the channel was enough.

The Dutch ships arriving at Dejima each year (Chapter 3) began carrying small quantities of European books in addition to their commercial cargoes. The handful of officially licensed **Nagasaki Dutch interpreters** (oranda tsūji, oh-RAN-dah TSOO-jee) — a hereditary guild of about a hundred individuals who managed all communication between the Dutch and the Japanese authorities at Nagasaki — became the seed institution from which Dutch-language competence radiated outward. Some of the interpreters' sons learned the language thoroughly enough to read scientific texts. Some of them began transmitting that knowledge to interested scholars from across Japan who traveled to Nagasaki to study. By the 1740s, when Yoshimune appointed **Aoki Kon'yō** (ah-OH-kee kon-YOH) as his official Dutch-learning specialist (charged primarily with sweet-potato cultivation and Dutch language study), the network of Japanese rangaku scholars had begun to consolidate.

By the 1770s, when Sugita Genpaku and his colleagues began the Kaitai Shinsho translation, there were perhaps a few dozen serious rangaku scholars in Japan. By 1820, several hundred. By 1850, perhaps several thousand, with private rangaku schools operating in Edo, Osaka, Kyoto, and several provincial cities. The total community remained small relative to the population (Japan in 1850 had perhaps 30 million people), but it was the only segment of Japanese intellectual life with sustained access to European scientific developments — and when the country opened in the 1850s, it was these scholars who became the indispensable interface between Japan and the modernizing West.

---

What did rangaku scholars actually study?

Medicine first. The Kaitai Shinsho launched a generation of Japanese physicians who reformed kanpō practice along Western anatomical lines. **Ōtsuki Gentaku** (1757–1827), Sugita's pupil, founded the **Shirandō** academy in Edo in 1786 — the first formal rangaku school — and trained students who would go on to dominate medical reform in the early nineteenth century. **Philipp Franz von Siebold**, a German physician serving as the Dutch factor's medical officer at Dejima from 1823 to 1829, taught Western medicine to over fifty Japanese students at his Nagasaki school, several of whom became major figures in Bakumatsu-era reform (until Siebold was expelled in 1829 for the supposed offense of acquiring Japanese maps and other materials).

Astronomy and calendrics next. The Tokugawa calendar was a lunisolar system inherited from Tang China; it had become inaccurate by Yoshimune's time, requiring reform. **Shibukawa Shunkai** had reformed the calendar earlier (1685), but further reforms in 1755, 1797, and 1844 drew on Western astronomical knowledge increasingly available through the Dutch books. The shogunate maintained an official astronomy bureau in Edo that staffed itself with rangaku-trained scholars.

Geography and cartography. The most extraordinary product of rangaku-era science was **Inō Tadataka**'s coastal survey of Japan (1800–1816). Inō, a wealthy retired sake brewer, took up the formal study of Western surveying methods at the age of fifty, then proposed to the shogunate that he undertake a comprehensive survey of the Japanese coastline. The shogunate gave permission, partial funding, and the authority to require local cooperation. Inō walked the coastline of Japan over seventeen years, measuring distances by his own pace count, taking sun-shot latitudes, and triangulating coastal features against inland landmarks. He died in 1818 with the work nearly complete; his students finished the maps in 1821. The resulting **Dai Nihon Enkai Yochi Zenzu** ("Comprehensive Maps of the Coastal Regions of Greater Japan") was so accurate that the British Royal Navy used the maps in the 1860s for hydrographic charts of Japan, and the maps were not significantly improved by Western surveyors until the early twentieth century.

Physics and mechanics. **Hiraga Gennai** (HEE-rah-gah GEN-nye, 1728–1779) is the rangaku world's most colorful figure — a polymath who produced experimental Western-influenced oil paintings, wrote satirical novels and kabuki plays, conducted mineralogical surveys, founded a glass workshop, and built Japan's first electrostatic generator (the **elekiteru**, eh-leh-kee-TEH-roo) by reverse-engineering it from Dutch books and broken examples obtained from the Dutch. The elekiteru produced static electric sparks for entertainment and what Hiraga claimed were medical purposes; he toured it around Edo in demonstrations that drew curious crowds. Hiraga's career ended badly — he killed a colleague in a fit of rage in 1779 and died in prison the following year — but his work demonstrated the breadth of what one motivated rangaku amateur could accomplish working from Dutch books.

Military technology. By the early nineteenth century, the gap between Japanese and Western military capability had become impossible to ignore. Cannon design, naval architecture, infantry drill, fortifications — all had advanced in Europe in ways the Tokugawa military had not absorbed. Domains with foresight (notably **Satsuma**, **Chōshū**, **Saga**, and the shogunate itself in the 1850s) began to use rangaku scholars to study Western military manuals and adapt the techniques. By the 1860s, several southwestern domains had armed substantial portions of their samurai forces with European-style rifles drilled in European-style infantry tactics — innovations that would prove decisive in the Boshin War (Chapter 8).

---

The peculiar thing about rangaku is how much it accomplished on how little.

The Japanese knowledge of Europe was severely limited. The Nagasaki Dutch interpreters were trained in the language as it was spoken in the seventeenth century, when the Dutch trading post was established; they were never updated as Dutch evolved. The books arriving at Dejima were the books the Dutch factor happened to bring (often selected on the basis of popularity rather than importance) plus those Japanese scholars had specifically requested. There were no Western teachers in Japan after Siebold's expulsion. There were no Japanese students sent to Europe (Japanese citizens were forbidden to travel abroad, and the death penalty applied to violators well into the 1850s). The annual visit of the Dutch factor to Edo to pay respects to the shogun included an audience at which curious shogunal officials could question the Dutch about world events, but the audience was brief, the questions were mostly trivial, and the Dutch had no incentive to volunteer information that might disturb the trading arrangement.

And yet, working through this absurdly narrow channel, Japan accumulated a Western scientific knowledge base that was, by 1850, sufficient to launch the most rapid modernization any country has ever undertaken. The Meiji-era engineers who built Japan's first railway (1872), telegraph network (1869), Western-style army (1873), and constitutional government (1889) were largely the rangaku scholars and their students from the late Edo period. The shogunate's rangaku scholars built Japan's first Western-style steam ironclad warship at the **Yokosuka** shipyards, contracted with French naval engineers. The Satsuma rangaku scholars built the first Western-style ship in Japan, the **Shōhei Maru**, in 1854 from drawings in Dutch books.

The rangaku scholars knew throughout the nineteenth century that they were operating under conditions that could not last. The world outside Japan was changing rapidly in ways their fragmentary information could only hint at. The Western powers were industrializing, building railways, deploying steam warships, projecting power across the globe. Sooner or later, the closure would end — by Western action if not by Japanese choice — and when it ended, the country would need every rangaku-trained scholar it had produced. They prepared, quietly, for the moment they expected.

That moment, as it turned out, was the morning of July 8, 1853, when four black ships steamed into Edo Bay (Chapter 8).

Cross-civ check: the rangaku world was, in a real sense, the global periphery of the **European Enlightenment**. The scientific tradition Sugita Genpaku translated had developed in the universities of the Netherlands, France, Germany, and Britain across the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — Newton, Linnaeus, Lavoisier, Boerhaave, Cuvier, Volta, the founders of modern biology, chemistry, physics, and medicine. By the time Kaitai Shinsho was published in 1774, the European knowledge it represented was already several decades old by European standards. The lag between European discovery and Japanese transmission was always 30 to 80 years through the rangaku channel, sometimes longer. But the lag was consistent — meaning that once the rangaku channel was operating, Japan was systematically receiving Western technical knowledge on a delay rather than on a one-time basis. When the channel widened dramatically after 1853, the latency collapsed. This is one of the structural reasons Japan's modernization was so much faster than China's: China had no pre-existing infrastructure for absorbing Western technical knowledge in 1840 (the Jesuit channel had collapsed in the eighteenth century), while Japan had been quietly preparing for the moment of opening for a century and a half.

# Chapter 7 — The Long Decline

The Edo period had two distinct halves. The first half, roughly 1603 to 1700, was a period of state consolidation and economic growth: the Tokugawa system (Chapter 2) was built, the country was sealed (Chapter 3), the Genroku culture flowered (Chapter 4), and the population grew from perhaps 18 million to perhaps 30 million. The second half, roughly 1700 to 1853, was a period of structural strain that the shogunate spent the entire century and a half failing to resolve.

The strains were of three kinds: fiscal, social, and geopolitical. Each had its own logic, its own escalation pattern, and its own repeated reform attempts that mostly failed. Together they produced the conditions in which Perry's arrival in 1853 (Chapter 8) could trigger such rapid collapse.

This chapter walks through the strains.

---

The fiscal strain came first and never went away.

The bakufu's fundamental fiscal problem was structural. The shogunate's tax base was the rice produced on the territories it directly governed — about 4 million koku per year, plus various non-agricultural revenues from the shogunate-controlled cities and mines. The shogunate's expenses included its own administration, its garrison forces, payments to the imperial court at Kyoto, infrastructure maintenance, and (intermittently but expensively) reconstruction after earthquakes, fires, and famines. The expenses grew over time. The income, denominated in fixed quantities of rice from a fixed land base, did not.

This is the same problem that broke many pre-modern states — the gap between expanding monetary expenses and a static agricultural tax base — and the Tokugawa response was the same response other states tried: currency debasement.

The first major debasement happened under **Tsunayoshi** (Chapter 4) in 1695, when the gold and silver content of the standard coins was reduced by roughly a third. This produced a one-time fiscal windfall (the shogunate could mint more coins from the same precious metal stock) but also produced inflation, eroded the purchasing power of fixed samurai stipends, and contributed to the Genroku-era cultural ferment by increasing nominal money supply. The pattern repeated periodically over the next century and a half. **Yoshimune** in the 1730s reformed the currency back toward higher precious-metal content as part of his Kyōhō Reforms, then debased it again later in his reign when fiscal pressures returned. Each subsequent shogun faced the same equation and made some version of the same choice.

The shogunate's repeated reform programs — the **Kyōhō Reforms** (1716–1736 under Yoshimune), the **Kansei Reforms** (1787–1793 under **Matsudaira Sadanobu**), the **Tenpō Reforms** (1841–1843 under **Mizuno Tadakuni**) — all attempted variations on the same theme. Cut samurai expenses through enforced sumptuary laws. Improve agricultural yields through irrigation projects, sweet potato planting, and exhortations to peasant industriousness. Curb merchant influence through forced loans and price controls. Restore Confucian moral discipline through censorship of frivolous literature and entertainment. Each reform program produced some short-term fiscal improvement and was then partially or fully reversed when its costs became unbearable.

The reforms produced their own pathologies. The **Tanuma Okitsugu** ministry (1772–1786) had attempted the opposite approach — encouraging mercantile activity, licensing monopoly cartels, planning Hokkaido development, considering trade with the Russians — but was undermined by corruption scandals and a series of natural disasters that included the **Tenmei famine** (Chapter 7's central catastrophe, see below) and was replaced by the puritanical Kansei Reforms under Matsudaira Sadanobu, which reversed almost everything Tanuma had done. The whiplash between mercantile experimentation and Confucian retrenchment was characteristic of the period. The shogunate did not have a coherent answer to its fiscal problem; it had successive incoherent answers that were periodically substituted for each other.

The chronic budget deficit produced chronic borrowing from merchant creditors, who were technically forbidden by Confucian theory from being creditors of the warrior class but were unmistakably so in practice. By the early nineteenth century, individual daimyo houses were carrying debt loads equivalent to multiple years of their annual income. The bakufu itself was deeply in debt to the great Osaka merchant houses. The samurai class as a whole was, in aggregate, the financial debtor of the merchant class — an inversion of the official social hierarchy that everyone could see and nobody could legally acknowledge.

---

The social strain came from below, and intermittently exploded.

The peasant majority bore the bulk of the tax burden, and as fiscal pressures intensified through the eighteenth century, the daimyo and shogunate squeezed harder. Tax rates rose. Demands for corvée labor — the requirement that peasants contribute uncompensated labor to public works, road maintenance, and (especially) the periodic processions of sankin-kōtai daimyo — increased. Rice quality grading became stricter. Side-payments and irregular levies multiplied. The official ideology that peasants should be "kept neither alive nor dead" (Chapter 2) was something the shogunate was, by the eighteenth century, increasingly bad at maintaining; squeezing peasants past the survival threshold became chronic in many domains.

The peasantry responded through a tradition of organized uprising called **ikki** (EE-kee, "league" or "uprising"). A typical ikki began with a village or group of villages presenting a formal petition to the daimyo or the shogunate for tax relief or redress of grievances. If the petition was ignored or denied, the petitioners might escalate to mass demonstrations — peasants from multiple villages assembling outside a daimyo's castle, sometimes in the tens of thousands, to make their grievances impossible to ignore. If demonstrations failed, the next escalation was rioting — burning the houses and storehouses of officials, money-lenders, and rich peasants identified as collaborators or hoarders. The shogunate generally responded to ikki with a combination of token concessions on the immediate grievances and exemplary executions of identified leaders, and the cycle would reset.

The frequency of ikki tracks the fiscal strain almost exactly. There were perhaps 30 to 50 documented uprisings per decade in the first half of the Edo period, rising to over 100 per decade by the late eighteenth century, and over 200 per decade in the 1830s. The uprisings during the **Tenpō famine** of 1833–1837 reached counts that some historians have placed in the thousands across the country. The shogunate could suppress any individual uprising; it could not stop the underlying pattern.

The most catastrophic single event of the period was the **Tenmei famine** of 1782–1788, which killed approximately 920,000 people across northeastern Japan (about 3% of the country's total population). The famine was triggered by a combination of cold summers (caused, in part, by the global atmospheric effects of the 1783 **Laki volcanic eruption** in Iceland, which depressed temperatures across the Northern Hemisphere for years), the **Mount Asama** volcanic eruption of August 1783 (which killed roughly 1,400 people directly and destroyed agricultural land in central Honshu), crop failures from rice blast disease and locust plagues, and the structural inability of the regional governance system to redistribute food across affected areas. Some northeastern domains lost 20 to 30 percent of their populations. Survivors reportedly resorted to eating bark, leather, and (in the chronicle accounts) human flesh. The famine triggered hundreds of peasant uprisings, contributed directly to the fall of the Tanuma ministry, and shocked the country into the puritanical retrenchment of the Kansei Reforms.

A second major famine, the **Tenpō famine** of 1833–1837, killed perhaps 200,000 to 300,000 people and triggered, in February 1837, an event that shocked the political system: a former shogunate official named **Ōshio Heihachirō** (OH-shee-oh hay-hah-CHEE-roh) led a one-day armed uprising in Osaka against grain hoarders and corrupt officials. The uprising was suppressed, Ōshio committed suicide, but his action demonstrated that the bakufu's authority was no longer secure even within its own administrative class. A senior Confucian scholar, formerly employed by the shogunate, had publicly turned against the system. The implications were ominous, and the Tenpō Reforms that followed in 1841–1843 attempted (and failed) to address them.

---

The geopolitical strain came from outside.

For most of the sakoku period, the world beyond Japan had been distant enough that Japanese isolation had been a defensible policy. By the early nineteenth century, this was no longer true. European and American maritime power had reached the western Pacific, and incidents of foreign ships approaching Japanese coasts had become regular.

Russian expansion in the late eighteenth century brought the Russian navigator **Adam Laxman** to Hokkaido in 1792, requesting trade. The shogunate refused but did not yet treat the encounter as a crisis. **Nikolai Rezanov** arrived at Nagasaki in 1804 with the same request and was kept waiting for six months before being refused; in retaliation, Russian raiders attacked Japanese settlements on Sakhalin and the Kurile Islands in 1806–1807. British whalers and trading ships began appearing off Japanese coasts in increasing numbers from the 1810s onward. The British naval frigate **HMS Phaeton** sailed into Nagasaki harbor in 1808, demanded supplies, and threatened to bombard the city if refused — the **Phaeton Incident** that drove the local Nagasaki magistrate to suicide and shocked the bakufu into building coastal defenses. American whalers approached Edo Bay multiple times in the 1820s and 1830s.

The shogunate's response to the foreign-ship problem evolved through three phases. The 1825 **Edict to Repel Foreign Ships** ordered coastal batteries to fire without warning on any approaching foreign vessel — a hardline policy reflecting the shogunate's (correct) sense that the foreign pressure was escalating. The implementation of this policy led to the **Morrison Incident** of 1837, when an unarmed American merchant ship attempting to return shipwrecked Japanese castaways to Japan was fired upon by Edo Bay coastal batteries and turned back. The incident became a public scandal when Japanese intellectuals publicly criticized the policy as inhumane and irrational; the shogunate responded with the **Bansha no Goku** purge of 1839, arresting prominent rangaku scholars who had criticized the Morrison response and forcing several to commit suicide.

After news of China's defeat in the **First Opium War** (1839–42) reached Japan, the shogunate quietly modified its policy. The 1842 **Edict to Provide Firewood and Water** replaced the 1825 hardline approach with a more accommodating policy of providing supplies to foreign ships that requested them, while still refusing trade and preventing landings. The Opium War had demonstrated that Western military superiority over East Asian states was now too large to challenge, and the shogunate was preparing to manage the inevitable encounter rather than provoke it.

The encounter, when it came, took the form of a sixty-year-old American naval commodore with four steam warships. His name was **Matthew Calbraith Perry**, and Chapter 8 covers what he did.

---

A note on what was happening outside Japan during the long decline period that the Japanese themselves were largely unaware of.

The **Industrial Revolution** in Britain transformed European economies between 1760 and 1840. Steam power, mechanized textile production, railway construction, mass-produced steel, and the new factory system of organizing labor produced an order-of-magnitude increase in European productivity that no Asian economy could match. The military implications followed: industrial economies could produce vastly more weapons, vastly more shipping, vastly more men under arms than any pre-industrial system. The European projection of military power across the globe in the nineteenth century was the operational consequence.

The **Napoleonic Wars** (1799–1815) reshaped European politics; the **Vienna Settlement** of 1815 produced an unstable peace that periodic European wars would disrupt over the following century. The **American Revolution** had created the United States in 1783; the **War of 1812** had confirmed it; westward expansion across the North American continent had reached the Pacific coast by the 1840s, producing the strategic interest in Pacific ports that would directly motivate Perry's mission. The **First Opium War** (1839–1842) had forced the **Qing Dynasty** of China to open five ports to British trade, cede Hong Kong, and accept extraterritoriality for British subjects; the **Second Opium War** (1856–1860) was about to extract still more concessions. The European (and American) imposition of "unequal treaties" on East Asian states had become the default pattern, and Japan was the next target.

Cross-civ check: the structural decline of late-Edo Japan paralleled almost exactly the structural decline of late-Qing China and the late Ottoman Empire in the same period. All three were once-formidable states whose internal arrangements (closed economies, hereditary aristocracies, rigid orthodoxies) had become incapable of responding to European industrial-military pressure. All three faced a series of foreign-imposed humiliations in the nineteenth century. All three eventually collapsed and were replaced by very different political systems: Japan's Meiji Restoration in 1868, China's revolutions of 1911 and 1949, the Ottoman dissolution in 1918–22 (Ottoman Empire Ch 11). The Japanese case was the fastest, the most complete, and the most successful in transforming from the old system to a viable modern state. Why Japan succeeded where the others struggled is one of the most argued-over questions in nineteenth-century world history. Part of the answer is the rangaku infrastructure (Chapter 6); part is the structure of the daimyo system, which produced powerful provincial actors (Satsuma, Chōshū) capable of organizing reform from below; part is luck. The Japanese themselves were, throughout the long decline, mostly unaware of how lucky they had been to develop the structural preconditions for what was about to happen.

# Chapter 8 — The Black Ships and the End

Edo's last morning began on July 8, 1853.

A coastal lookout posted at **Uraga** (oo-RAH-gah), a small port town at the mouth of Edo Bay roughly forty miles south of the city, sighted four ships approaching from the south. Two of them were unlike anything the lookout had ever seen. They were huge — over 200 feet long. They were painted black. They had no sails set, but they were moving against the wind at a steady pace, with thick black smoke pouring from tall metal funnels amidships. They were warships: two side-wheel **steam frigates**, the **USS Susquehanna** and **USS Mississippi**, accompanied by two sail sloops in tow. The runner the lookout dispatched to the local magistrate's office reached Uraga within the hour. By that evening, the news was racing along the Tōkaidō toward Edo. The next morning, the entire Edo political class was confronting the problem that the long decline (Chapter 7) had been making inevitable.

The man on the lead steam frigate was **Matthew Calbraith Perry** (PEHR-ee), a sixty-year-old commodore of the United States Navy, son and brother of distinguished naval officers, veteran of the **War of 1812** and the **Mexican-American War** (1846–48). He had been dispatched the previous year by President **Millard Fillmore** with a specific mission: open Japan to American trade and obtain American access to Japanese ports as coaling stations for the Pacific steamship trade. He carried letters from Fillmore to "the Emperor of Japan" (the Americans, not yet briefed on the political situation, did not realize that the actual political authority was the shogun, not the emperor in Kyoto) and instructions to use whatever level of force was required short of starting a war.

This chapter is about what happened over the next fifteen years.

---

Perry's tactical approach at Uraga was simple. He refused to deal with anyone except the highest available Japanese officials. He refused to move his ships to the conventional foreign-affairs port at Nagasaki. He sailed his ships closer to Edo than any Western ship had been allowed in two centuries. He fired blank salutes from his cannon (loud enough to be heard in the city). He sent surveying boats into the bay with an armed escort. And he announced that he would deliver Fillmore's letters to representatives of the Japanese government and would return in spring 1854 with a much larger fleet to receive the Japanese response.

The shogunate's response was confused. The senior **rōjū** (council of senior ministers, Chapter 2) under the chief minister **Abe Masahiro** (AH-beh mah-sah-HEE-roh) had no authority to make foreign policy decisions of this magnitude. The shogun, **Tokugawa Ieyoshi**, was sixty years old, ill, and would die within weeks. The bakufu had no military force capable of defeating the American steamships — the Edo coastal defenses were undergunned, the shogunal navy was non-existent, and the daimyo armies (such as remained in fighting condition) were equipped with weapons two centuries out of date. The country was being threatened with overwhelming force by a power it had no means to resist.

Abe did something the shogunate had never done in 250 years: he asked for help. He circulated Perry's letters to all the daimyo in the country, asking for their advice on how to respond. The daimyo responded with predictable inconsistency — some advocating military resistance, some advocating immediate accommodation, most advocating delay — but the consultation itself was a constitutional revolution. The bakufu had implicitly admitted that it could not handle the problem alone, and it had implicitly invited the daimyo into the foreign-policy decision. The daimyo would not soon forget that they had been consulted. The principle that the shogunate's decisions on the most important questions might require daimyo input had been established. It would be a major mechanism in the events that followed.

Perry returned in February 1854, on schedule, with a fleet of nine ships including three steam frigates. The shogunate, having spent six months without finding any way to mount a credible refusal, agreed to negotiate. The **Treaty of Kanagawa** was signed on March 31, 1854: the United States obtained the right to call at the ports of **Shimoda** (on the southern Izu Peninsula) and **Hakodate** (in southern Hokkaido) for supplies, the right to station an American consul at Shimoda, and a most-favored-nation clause that automatically extended any future privileges granted to other foreign powers. The treaty did not yet open Japan to commercial trade — it was a treaty of friendship and limited access, not of commerce — but it ended sakoku as a legal regime. After 214 years, foreign vessels had a treaty-recognized right to enter Japanese waters.

---

The commercial treaty came four years later.

The American consul who arrived at Shimoda in 1856 was **Townsend Harris**, a New York merchant turned diplomat. Harris's instructions were to negotiate a comprehensive commercial treaty on the terms the British had imposed on China in the **Treaty of Nanking** (1842) — extraterritoriality for foreign nationals, fixed low tariffs, multiple open ports, freedom of trade. Harris spent over a year negotiating with the shogunate, pressuring the chief negotiator **Hotta Masayoshi** with the threat that if Japan refused American terms, the British would soon arrive after the **Second Opium War** then concluding in China and impose worse terms.

The result was the **Harris Treaty** (Treaty of Amity and Commerce) of July 29, 1858. The treaty granted American extraterritoriality in Japan (Americans accused of crimes would be tried by their own consular courts, not by Japanese courts), limited Japanese tariffs on imports to roughly 5%, opened five additional ports (Yokohama, Nagasaki, Niigata, Hyōgo, and the Edo and Osaka treaty zones), permitted free residence for Americans in the open ports, and established a fixed American currency-to-Japanese currency exchange rate that would have the unintended consequence of triggering massive gold-for-silver arbitrage that would drain Japan's gold stocks within months. Within weeks, Britain, France, the Netherlands, and Russia had signed near-identical treaties — the **Ansei Treaties**, named for the imperial era then in effect. The unequal treaty regime that would humiliate Japan for the next four decades was now in place.

The signing of the Harris Treaty had to be authorized by someone, and the shogunate's request for imperial sanction had been denied. **Emperor Kōmei** (KOH-mei), the reactionary nationalist on the Kyoto throne, refused to sign off on the treaties. The bakufu's chief minister, **Ii Naosuke** (EE nah-oh-SOO-keh), authorized the signing without imperial approval — a decision that triggered a constitutional crisis. The imperial court had been ceremonially powerless for centuries. Now, suddenly, its refusal to ratify a treaty became a political issue, because the rapidly growing **sonnō jōi** (son-NOH JOH-ee, "Revere the Emperor, Expel the Barbarians") movement had made imperial loyalty into a banner that opposition daimyo could rally around.

Ii Naosuke responded to the crisis with the **Ansei Purge** of 1858–59: arrests, exiles, and forced suicides imposed on over a hundred opponents of the treaty regime. The most notable casualty was **Yoshida Shōin** (yoh-SHEE-dah SHOH-in), a thirty-year-old Mito-school activist, scholar, and teacher who had attempted (illegally) to stow away on Perry's flagship in 1854 to study the West, had been imprisoned in his Chōshū domain for the offense, and had used his confinement to teach students who would become the leaders of the Meiji Restoration. Yoshida was arrested, brought to Edo, and beheaded in October 1859. He became a martyr of the imperial-loyalist movement; his students inherited his cause.

In March 1860, **Ii Naosuke** himself was assassinated outside the **Sakurada Gate** of Edo Castle on a snowy morning by a band of Mito loyalists who had crossed Edo to deliver vengeance for the Ansei Purge. The **Sakuradamon Incident** demonstrated that the bakufu could no longer protect even its highest officials from political violence. The shogunate's authority began visibly cracking.

---

The next eight years were the most chaotic in late-Edo history. A short summary will have to suffice.

The opposition to the bakufu coalesced around three southwestern domains: **Satsuma** (the Shimazu domain in southern Kyushu), **Chōshū** (the Mōri domain at the western end of Honshu), and **Tosa** (the Yamauchi domain on Shikoku). All three were tozama domains (Chapter 2), excluded from central power for two and a half centuries. All three had spent the late Edo period quietly building modernized military forces and rangaku-trained reform networks. All three had reasons to hate the bakufu specifically (Satsuma had been forced to pay reparations to Britain after the **Namamugi Incident** of 1862, in which a British merchant was killed by Satsuma samurai; Chōshū had been bombarded by a four-power Western fleet at **Shimonoseki** in 1864 after firing on Western ships, and had been sanctioned by the bakufu for the underlying joi xenophobia).

The first **Chōshū Expedition** of 1864 was a bakufu punitive campaign against Chōshū after the Shimonoseki bombardment — the bakufu mobilized an enormous coalition of daimyo forces and forced Chōshū to back down without fighting. The result was a bakufu apparent victory that further radicalized Chōshū's reform faction, who now controlled the domain government. The **second Chōshū Expedition** of 1866 was the same operation attempted again, this time after Chōshū had thoroughly modernized its military with French and British weapons and trained its samurai in Western infantry tactics. The bakufu campaign was a humiliating failure: the Chōshū riflemen outclassed every bakufu force sent against them, and the campaign collapsed in July 1866 when the fourteenth shogun **Tokugawa Iemochi** died of beriberi at age twenty in Osaka and the operation was suspended. The bakufu's military credibility was destroyed.

The decisive political move had taken place earlier, in March 1866, when the **Tosa rōnin Sakamoto Ryōma** (sah-kah-MOH-toh ree-OH-mah), one of the great political brokers of the period, had personally arranged a secret alliance between Satsuma and Chōshū. The two domains had been bitter enemies for years; Sakamoto's alliance brought them into coordinated opposition to the bakufu. The **Satsuma-Chōshū Alliance** (Satchō Dōmei) was the coalition that would, within two years, overthrow the Tokugawa shogunate.

The fifteenth and last Tokugawa shogun, **Tokugawa Yoshinobu** (toh-koo-GAH-wah yoh-shee-NOH-boo), came to office in late 1866. Yoshinobu was perhaps the most capable Tokugawa ruler since Yoshimune — energetic, intelligent, well-versed in Western affairs, with a clear understanding of how serious the situation was. His response to the situation was, in a sense, the most graceful surrender any shogun could have managed. In November 1867, he formally returned political authority to the emperor in the **Taisei Hōkan** ("Restoration of Imperial Rule"), proposing that he continue to head the new government as a constitutional minister under imperial sovereignty. It was a calculated gamble — Yoshinobu was betting that no other faction had the administrative experience to govern, and that the new imperial government would have to retain him.

The bet did not pay off. Sakamoto Ryōma was assassinated in Kyoto in December 1867, removing the most influential mediator. The imperial faction, dominated by Satsuma and Chōshū, refused to include Yoshinobu in the new government. The shogunate's military forces in and around Kyoto, refusing to accept the political settlement, marched on the imperial capital in January 1868. They were intercepted at **Toba-Fushimi**, just south of Kyoto, by a smaller but better-armed Satsuma-Chōshū force flying the imperial banner. The bakufu forces broke and fled.

The **Boshin War** (Year of the Yang Earth Dragon War, January 1868 to June 1869) is the civil war that completed the overthrow. Imperial forces marched east, took Edo without serious resistance in May 1868 (Yoshinobu having ordered no defense), pursued the shogunate diehards north into the Tōhoku and Hokkaido. The last shogunate force, a naval contingent that had set up an independent **Republic of Ezo** at Hakodate under Admiral **Enomoto Takeaki**, surrendered in June 1869 after a six-month siege. The Boshin War's casualties — perhaps 10,000 dead total — were modest compared to the Korean invasions or even the Sengoku wars, but the political consequence was total. The Tokugawa shogunate, after 265 years of rule, was over.

---

The new government was nominally headed by the sixteen-year-old **Emperor Meiji** (MEH-jee), grandson of Kōmei (who had died of smallpox in early 1867). The actual administration was run by a coalition of senior Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa, and Hizen officials — most of them under forty years old, most of them former samurai of relatively modest stipend rank, most of them rangaku-trained. In April 1868, they issued the **Charter Oath**, a five-article document promising public assembly, abolition of class restrictions, and the seeking of knowledge throughout the world. The new government's program was the diametric opposite of the late Tokugawa program: open the country, modernize the military, abolish the four-class hierarchy, build a centralized state on the European model.

Within two decades, they would do all of it. Japan in 1888 had a constitution, a parliament, a national army on the Prussian model, a national navy on the British model, a national railway network, a national education system, a Western-style cabinet government, and embassies in every major Western capital. By 1895 it had defeated China in a war. By 1905 it had defeated Russia. By 1910 it had annexed Korea (Joseon Korea TL). The transformation of Japan from a feudal isolated agrarian state to a modern industrial imperial power took roughly forty years — the most rapid such transformation any country has ever undertaken.

The transformation was carried out, mostly, by men who had grown up in the Tokugawa system, who had read Bashō and Saikaku as children, who had attended Confucian academies, who had observed the rangaku tradition's quiet preservation of Western knowledge against the day it would be needed. The Edo period's apparent stagnation had been preparation. The rangaku scholars had built the human infrastructure (Chapter 6). The merchant economy had built the financial infrastructure (Chapter 4). The sankin-kōtai system had built the road network and the urban concentration that would form the basis for industrial development. The temple registration system had built the population registers that would underpin Meiji conscription and taxation. The samurai class, displaced from its functions by the Meiji reforms, would supply the soldiers, bureaucrats, and entrepreneurs of the new Japan, including most of the founders of the Mitsubishi, Mitsui (whose Tokugawa-era house had survived), and Sumitomo industrial conglomerates.

The Edo period had ended, but its products kept compounding.

---

One last thing.

In 1860, a Japanese embassy traveled to the United States to ratify the Harris Treaty. They sailed across the Pacific on the American steam frigate **USS Powhatan** and the Japanese-built sail steamship **Kanrin Maru**, which had been designed in Japan based on Dutch books and constructed at the shogunate's Nagasaki shipyard with limited Dutch technical assistance. The Kanrin Maru was the first Japanese-built ship to cross the Pacific. Its captain was **Katsu Kaishū**, a rangaku-trained naval officer from a low-ranking samurai family in Edo, who would later negotiate the bloodless surrender of Edo to the imperial forces in 1868.

On board the Kanrin Maru as a junior officer was a young scholar named **Fukuzawa Yukichi** (foo-koo-ZAH-wah yoo-KEE-chee), the son of a low-ranking Nakatsu samurai who had taught himself Dutch and then English over the previous decade, who had attended Ogata Kōan's Tekijuku rangaku academy in Osaka, and who would, after returning from America, become Japan's most influential public intellectual of the Meiji era — founder of Keio University, advocate of Western-style education, author of *An Encouragement of Learning* (1872, a million copies sold) and *The Outline of Civilization* (1875). His face would later appear on the 10,000-yen note, the highest denomination of Japanese currency.

Fukuzawa was thirty years old in 1868 when the shogunate fell. He had been raised entirely under the Tokugawa system. He had absorbed Western knowledge through the rangaku channel that the shogunate had created and barely tolerated. He had crossed the Pacific on a ship the shogunate had built using rangaku techniques. He spent the rest of his life as one of the architects of the Japan that replaced the Tokugawa world.

That is the Edo period in one biography. A man trained in a closed country to use the leaked knowledge of the open world, who lived to help build a Japan that was both. The transformation he and his generation accomplished was the most successful industrial-political modernization any country has carried out, and it was carried out by people whose intellectual formation had taken place inside the system they were replacing.

That, in the end, may be what the Edo period was for.

Cross-civ check: the Meiji Restoration (1868) was almost exactly contemporary with two other transformative political events. The **American Civil War** (1861–65) had just concluded in the United States, ending slavery and producing the constitutional amendments that would underwrite American industrial expansion. The **Italian unification** (Risorgimento, 1859–71) had just consolidated the Italian peninsula into a single state. The **Austro-Prussian War** of 1866 and the **Franco-Prussian War** of 1870–71 produced the unification of Germany under Prussian leadership, with the **Meiji Constitution** of 1889 explicitly modeled on the Prussian constitution. The 1860s and 1870s were a moment of nation-state consolidation across the Western world, and Japan's Meiji project was both a participant in and a successful imitator of that broader pattern. Within East Asia, the Japanese transformation accelerated past China and Korea so dramatically that within a generation Japan would invade both — the Sino-Japanese War of 1894–95, the Russo-Japanese War of 1904–05, the annexation of Korea in 1910, and ultimately the Pacific War of 1937–45. The Edo period, in its last act, set in motion the chain of events that would carry Japan to the brink of total destruction in 1945. The post-1945 reconstruction is the next story; the Edo story ends in 1868 with the abdication of Yoshinobu and the proclamation of the Meiji era.

Edo is over. What it built is not.

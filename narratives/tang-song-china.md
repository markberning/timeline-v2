# Sui, Tang & Song China — The Cosmopolitan Centuries

**Goal:** Narrative for the seventh TL in the Chinese Dynasties chain. Covers 581–1279 CE, the seven hundred years between the Six Dynasties era of fragmentation (Six Dynasties Ch 8) and the Mongol Yuan conquest (Mongol Empire Ch 8). The Sui reunifies China and burns out in 37 years. The Tang builds the most cosmopolitan empire of its time around Chang'an, gets gutted by the An Lushan Rebellion, and limps another 144 years. After fifty years of Five Dynasties chaos, the Song rebuilds on entirely different lines — civilian over military, commerce over conquest — and turns out to be the most innovative society on Earth: paper money, movable type, the navigation compass, gunpowder weaponry. The Jurchen catastrophe of 1127 cuts the Song in half. The Mongols finish the job at Yamen in 1279.

**Status:** First draft, 9 chapters.

A note on names: Chinese personal names are rendered in pinyin (the standard modern romanization), with pronunciation guides on first use. Reign names (Taizong, Xuanzong, Huizong) and personal names (Li Shimin, Li Longji, Zhao Ji) are both given for major emperors, since the historical literature uses both. Dates are CE unless noted otherwise.

A note on "first" claims: this era is full of them — first paper money, first movable type, first navigation compass, first gunpowder weapons. Each is qualified in context. As with every TL in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of," and absence of evidence elsewhere is not evidence of absence. Archaeological luck shapes what we can claim. China's bureaucratic record-keeping survives much better than most ancient civilizations', which is part of why the Tang-Song "firsts" are so well-documented; that does not necessarily mean nobody else got there sooner without leaving a trace.

# Chapter 1 — The Sui Reunification

China had been broken for 369 years. From the moment the Han dynasty officially ended in 220 CE (Han Dynasty Ch 8) until the Sui armies crossed the Yangtze in 589, no single government had ruled all of China. The Three Kingdoms split it three ways. The brief Western Jin reunified it for a generation and then collapsed into the Disaster of Yongjia (Six Dynasties Ch 2). The north fell to a long sequence of nomadic and Sino-nomadic dynasties; the south held out under a series of refugee Chinese courts (Six Dynasties Ch 4 and Ch 7). For almost four centuries, "China" was a cultural memory rather than a political fact.

The reunifier was a northern aristocrat named **Yang Jian** (yahng JEE-en), and the dynasty he founded — the **Sui** (SWAY) — lasted just 37 years. That sounds short, and it is. But those 37 years did three things that shaped the next 1,400. The Sui reassembled the country into a single state. The Sui built the **Grand Canal**, the world's longest artificial waterway, which is still in use today. And the Sui burned itself out so spectacularly that the Tang dynasty, which inherited the wreckage in 618, drew explicit lessons from the collapse and used those lessons to govern for nearly three centuries.

The pattern is one Chinese historians noticed early: a short, harsh, transformative dynasty reunifies the country through massive state projects, then bankrupts itself through overreach. Then a long, successful golden-age dynasty inherits the infrastructure and refines the institutions. The **Qin** did this in 221–206 BCE and the Han inherited it (Qin Dynasty Ch 7). The Sui did it in 581–618 and the Tang inherited it (Chapter 2). The pattern is so neat that some Chinese historians treated it as a moral lesson — harsh reunifiers are historically necessary but morally doomed — and as a governance principle: the next dynasty's job is to learn what not to do.

---

Yang Jian's path to the throne is one of the strangest in the dynasty-founding genre.

He was born in 541 CE into the **Northern Zhou** ruling class, one of several short-lived northern dynasties of the late Six Dynasties period. The Yang family was Chinese in language and culture but had served the **Xianbei** (shyen-BAY) — the Inner Asian people whose **Northern Wei** dynasty had ruled north China from 386 to 534 (Six Dynasties Ch 6). Like most of the northern aristocracy, the Yangs were ethnically and culturally hybrid: they held both a Chinese surname (Yang) and a Xianbei surname (Puliuru), spoke Chinese at home but Xianbei at court, and intermarried freely with both groups. This is the world the Sui came out of. It is also the reason the Tang founders, who came out of the same milieu, could project Chinese imperial authority into Central Asia later — they were already partially Inner Asian themselves.

Yang Jian rose through the Northern Zhou military and bureaucracy, married his daughter to the Northern Zhou emperor, and when his son-in-law died in 580 leaving a six-year-old grandson on the throne, Yang Jian became regent. Within months he had eliminated rival princes, consolidated military command, and arranged for the child emperor to "abdicate" in his favor. He proclaimed the Sui dynasty in 581 and took the reign name **Wen** (WEN — meaning "civil" or "cultured"), so he is known in the historical literature as **Emperor Wen of Sui**. He was forty years old.

Eight years later, in 589, the Sui invasion force of 518,000 troops crossed the Yangtze in a coordinated multi-pronged amphibious assault, defeated the southern **Chen** (CHEN) dynasty in weeks, and reunified China. The captured Chen emperor, **Chen Shubao** (chen shoo-BOW), was reportedly hiding in a palace well with two concubines when Sui soldiers found him. The reunification was anticlimactic precisely because it was so overwhelming — the Sui had spent eight years consolidating the north and building the Yangtze fleet, and the Chen had spent the same period in court intrigue and luxury.

---

What Emperor Wen built in his 23-year reign (581–604) is the institutional foundation of everything that followed.

He commissioned a **new capital** in 582. The old Han Chang'an site was cramped, polluted, and superstitiously inauspicious after centuries of dynastic failure on the location. Wen ordered his architect **Yuwen Kai** (yoo-WEN KIGH) to lay out an entirely new city southeast of the old one, on a strict north-south grid plan. The new capital was called **Daxing** (dah-SHING — "Great Prosperity"); the Tang would inherit it and rename it **Chang'an** (chahng-AHN — "Perpetual Peace"). Daxing covered approximately 32 square miles (84 km²), with the imperial palace at the north, government offices in the center, and 108 walled residential wards plus two enormous markets to the south. It was the largest planned city in the world at the time of its construction and would become the largest city in the world during the Tang. Its grid was copied by Japan for **Nara** in 710 and **Heian-kyō** (modern Kyoto) in 794, and by Korean capitals as well. The architectural template of a Chinese imperial capital — central avenue, walled wards, north-south orientation, ceremonial palace at the head — was effectively codified at Daxing in the 580s.

Wen also promulgated the **Kaihuang Code** (kigh-HWAHNG) in 583, a unified legal code that drew on the best elements of the various northern and southern legal traditions that had developed during the Age of Division. It reduced the number of capital offenses, distinguished intentional from accidental crimes, and ran to 500 articles in 12 chapters. The Tang inherited it almost wholesale and revised it into the **Tang Code** of 624 (revised again in 653 with full commentary), which is the oldest complete Chinese legal code to survive. The Tang Code in turn became the model for Japanese (the **Taihō Code** of 701), Korean, and Vietnamese law. The legal infrastructure of all of East Asia traces back to Wen's Kaihuang reforms.

He began the **civil service examination system** that would define Chinese governance for the next 1,300 years. Earlier dynasties had recruited officials through aristocratic recommendation and hereditary privilege. The Sui introduced standardized written examinations in Confucian classics and policy as a parallel route into the bureaucracy, originally on a small scale. The Tang would expand it dramatically (Chapter 2), and by the Song the **jinshi** exam would be the dominant path to high office (Chapter 6). Emperor Wen did not invent meritocracy — but he opened the door.

He standardized weights, measures, and coinage. He built a network of imperial granaries to stabilize the food supply across the reunified empire. He restored Buddhist institutions that had been suppressed during a brief Northern Zhou persecution and used Buddhist relics as a propaganda instrument — in 601, he had relics distributed to thirty temples across the empire on a single day, a coordinated demonstration of dynastic reach and religious legitimacy. He fought off the **Eastern Turkic Khaganate** (the dominant steppe power, occupying the same Mongolian and Inner Asian territories that the Xiongnu had held seven centuries earlier — Xiongnu-Huns Ch 8), playing different Turkic factions against each other to keep the northern frontier quiet.

Wen was not a glamorous ruler. The historical record describes him as personally austere, suspicious of his own court, frugal to the point of stinginess, and increasingly paranoid in his later years. But he was extraordinarily effective. Modern historians rank him alongside **Qin Shi Huang** (Qin Dynasty Ch 4) as one of the two most consequential reunifiers in Chinese history — the man who reassembled what had been fragmented for centuries.

He was succeeded in 604 by his second son **Yang Guang** (yahng GWAHNG), who reigned as **Emperor Yang of Sui** and destroyed the dynasty in fourteen years.

---

The succession itself is one of the dynasty's mysteries.

Wen had originally designated his eldest son Yang Yong as heir. Yang Guang, the second son, conducted a multi-year campaign of court intrigue to discredit his brother — feigning frugality and Confucian propriety while accusing Yang Yong of debauchery. He succeeded in getting Yang Yong demoted from the position of Crown Prince in 600. When Wen died in 604, Yang Guang inherited the throne. Within hours, Wen's old chief minister was reporting that Yang Guang had murdered his father. Modern historians remain divided on whether the parricide actually happened or whether the charge was a later Tang-era smear designed to delegitimize the Sui. What is clear is that Yang Yong was killed within days of Wen's death, on Yang Guang's orders, removing the only viable rival.

Whatever the truth of the murder, **Emperor Yang** was now Sui. He was thirty-five years old, intellectually brilliant, ferociously ambitious, and apparently unable to comprehend the concept of overreach. He launched everything at once.

---

The signature project was the **Grand Canal**.

China's two great agricultural regions are separated by hundreds of miles of intervening terrain. The **Yellow River** north grows wheat and millet on its loess plain (Ancient China Ch 1). The **Yangtze** valley south grows rice in flooded paddies and supports enormously denser populations per square mile. By the 6th century, the south had quietly become more populous and more economically productive than the north, partly because of agricultural advantages and partly because waves of refugees fleeing northern barbarian conquests over four centuries had pushed Chinese culture and labor south. But moving rice from the south to the imperial capitals in the north was prohibitively expensive — overland transport of bulk grain in pre-modern conditions consumes most of what is being transported just feeding the porters and oxen along the way.

Emperor Yang's solution was to connect the existing river systems and shorter local canals into a single artificial waterway running north-south through the populated heart of China. The **Grand Canal** stretched over 1,000 miles (1,700 km) in its full Sui-era form, from **Hangzhou** (hahng-JOH) on the south coast through the Yangtze, the **Huai** (HWIGH) River, the Yellow River, and on to the vicinity of modern **Beijing** in the north. Sections were up to 130 feet wide, lined with stone where the soil was unstable, with willows planted along the banks for shade and to stabilize the embankments.

The canal was built between 605 and 611 — six years for over a thousand miles of waterway, much of it dug by hand. The cost in human lives was staggering. Contemporary chronicle accounts speak of more than five million laborers conscripted under **corvée** (kor-VAY — unpaid labor required by the state), with one adult male per every five households mandatorily drafted. Death rates from overwork, exposure, and disease appear to have been very high; specific death tolls in the chronicles (one source claims roughly half the workers died) are probably exaggerated, but the underlying reality of mass mortality is not in serious dispute. Whole villages were depopulated of their working men. Communities fell behind schedule and were collectively punished. Yang's Grand Canal is a piece of infrastructure on the same engineering scale as the **Great Wall** built four centuries earlier (Qin Dynasty Ch 4 — though the iconic stone Great Wall most tourists visit today is a Ming-era reconstruction, not Qin), and it was built by the same kind of mass-mobilized corvée system.

The political point of the canal was practical: it bound the wheat-and-millet north to the rice-growing south economically and allowed surplus southern rice to feed northern capitals and armies. The strategic effect was permanent — for the next thousand years, control of the Grand Canal was control of China's economic backbone, and every subsequent dynasty rebuilt and maintained it. It is still in operation today as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, the longest artificial river system in the world, with Sui-era sections still carrying barge traffic in 2026.

The political effect on the Sui itself was catastrophic. The corvée burden fed peasant resentment exactly when the state needed peasant loyalty. Yang made the situation worse by traveling the new canal in person on dragon-shaped pleasure barges so enormous that they reportedly required tens of thousands of laborers walking along the banks to pull them. The image of imperial luxury floating on the bones of dead corvée workers became the defining symbol of Sui excess in later Chinese moralizing. The Tang historians had every motive to exaggerate the picture; modern scholarship has questioned the most colorful details. What is not in question is that the canal worked, that millions worked on it, and that many died.

---

The Grand Canal alone might have been survivable.

Then Yang invaded **Korea**.

The target was **Goguryeo** (go-GUR-yo, sometimes Romanized **Koguryo**), the powerful Korean kingdom whose territory ran across **Manchuria** and northern Korea (Ancient Korea Ch 6). Goguryeo had refused to formally submit to Sui suzerainty, and Yang seems to have decided that the conquest of Goguryeo was the demonstration of Sui supremacy that would crown his reign. He launched three invasions in three years, and all three failed catastrophically.

The **612 invasion** is the campaign the historical record remembers. Yang mobilized an army that contemporary chronicles place at over a million troops — a number that is probably inflated but that was certainly enormous by any standard. The supply train was so vast that the rear of the marching column was reportedly leaving the staging ground when the front was already approaching the Yalu River. The Goguryeo general **Eulji Mundeok** (UL-jee MUN-duck) used a classic feint-and-trap tactic — he allowed the Sui forces to advance deep into Korean territory, even pretending to surrender at one point, drawing them past their supply lines and into terrain favorable for ambush. At the **Salsu River**, he had Korean engineers dam the river upstream while Sui troops were crossing, then released the dam during the crossing. Of the 305,000 Sui troops that had crossed the Yalu, only about 2,700 returned. Eulji Mundeok is still a national hero in Korea today.

The 613 and 614 invasions did no better. Yang would not stop. By 614, the empire was openly rebelling — peasant uprisings in multiple provinces, deserter bands forming armies of their own, regional governors raising private militias. The Sui economy could not finance another mobilization, and the population could not be mobilized again without total revolt. Yang withdrew to his southern pleasure capital at **Jiangdu** (jyahng-DOO, near modern **Yangzhou**) on the Yangtze and reportedly spent his last months in a haze of palace luxury, apparently unable to confront the disaster he had created.

In the spring of 618, Yang's own palace guards mutinied. The story preserved in the chronicles has him on a balcony as the rebels closed in, asking what crime he had committed. They strangled him with a silk sash. The Sui dynasty had ruled all of China for 29 years (since the conquest of Chen in 589) and had been founded only 37 years earlier. It was finished.

---

A Sui general named **Li Yuan** (lee yoo-EN), military governor of **Taiyuan** in Shanxi, had marched on Chang'an in late 617 with an army backed by Turkic cavalry. He had taken the city in November and installed a puppet Sui emperor; when news of Yang's death arrived in 618, Li Yuan had the puppet abdicate and proclaimed himself emperor of a new dynasty: the **Tang** (TAHNG). He was a Sui aristocrat, his mother had been a sister of Emperor Yang's mother, and his family — like Yang Jian's — was Sino-Xianbei in origin and Turkic in connections. The dynasty he founded would last 289 years and become, in many later evaluations, China's greatest.

The Tang founders drew explicit lessons from the Sui collapse. Don't overtax the peasantry. Don't launch vanity wars before the economy can support them. Don't build infrastructure faster than the population can endure. Studying the Sui's mistakes became a centerpiece of Tang governance philosophy. Wen had been the harsh reunifier; Yang had been the catastrophic overreacher; the Tang would inherit the canal, the new capital, the legal code, the granary system, the examinations, and the reunified state — and would govern with an institutional memory of what could go wrong.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Sui were reunifying China and building the Grand Canal, the Mediterranean world was watching the **Byzantine Empire** under **Justinian I** attempt his own reconquest of the Western Roman Empire (Byzantine Empire Ch 3). Justinian rebuilt the **Hagia Sophia** in Constantinople (532–537), nearly contemporary with Yuwen Kai's design of Daxing. Both projects involved massive imperial budgets and skilled engineering corps. Justinian's empire reached its territorial peak in the 550s and immediately overstretched, much as Sui Yang would. In **Persia**, the **Sassanid Empire** under **Khosrow I** and his successors was approaching its final century before the Arab Muslim conquest (Persian Empire Ch 10) — Khosrow died in 579, two years before Yang Jian became emperor. In **India**, the post-**Gupta Empire** kingdoms (Gupta Empire Ch 8) were fragmenting into the regional states that would dominate medieval India. The early **Maya** city-states in Mesoamerica were entering their Classic Period peak (Maya Civilization Ch 5). And in northern Europe, the various successor kingdoms to the Western Roman Empire were still consolidating. The 7th and 8th centuries — when the Tang would peak — became one of the most globally connected moments before the early-modern world, with the Tang at the eastern end of Eurasia trading along the Silk Road with the Sassanids, the Byzantines, and shortly the new Islamic Caliphate.

# Chapter 2 — The Tang Founding & High Tang

The Tang dynasty was founded in 618 by **Li Yuan** (Chapter 1) — the Sui aristocrat-turned-rebel who took Chang'an and proclaimed himself **Emperor Gaozu** (GOW-zoo — "High Ancestor"). He was 51 years old, a competent but cautious general, with three adult sons who immediately began competing for the succession.

The middle son, **Li Shimin** (lee shih-MIN), was the family's military genius. He had led the campaign that took Chang'an in 617, fought the unification wars that consolidated Tang control over a still-fragmented China through the early 620s, and accumulated the loyalty of the senior military commanders. The eldest son, **Li Jiancheng** (lee jyen-CHENG), was the designated Crown Prince and held the political faction at court. The younger son **Li Yuanji** (lee yoo-en-JEE) was allied with Jiancheng. The brothers' rivalry was open and increasingly dangerous through the early 620s.

On July 2, 626, Li Shimin resolved the rivalry the way most Chinese succession disputes were resolved — by killing his brothers. The episode is called the **Xuanwu Gate Incident** (shoo-en-WOO GAH-tuh), after the northern gate of the Chang'an palace where it took place. Shimin and a small group of loyal officers ambushed Jiancheng and Yuanji as they rode toward the palace. Shimin personally shot the Crown Prince dead with an arrow. His ally **Yuchi Gong** (yoo-CHIH GAWNG), one of the most famous warriors of the era, killed Yuanji. The brothers' families — including their adult and infant sons — were systematically hunted down and killed in the days that followed, eliminating any future rivals from the lines. (Shimin spared the wives and small daughters and, in a gesture of imperial magnanimity that may have been genuine or political, formally adopted some of his murdered brothers' surviving sons as his own.) Two months later, his shocked father Emperor Gaozu abdicated.

Li Shimin, twenty-eight years old, took the throne. His reign name was **Taizong** (TIE-tsong — "Grand Ancestor"), and he is the figure known to history as **Emperor Taizong of Tang**. He is, in most assessments, one of the two or three greatest emperors in Chinese history.

The contradiction between the bloody seizure of power and the model reign that followed is one of the things Tang historians struggled with for centuries. Confucian historiography teaches that legitimate rule comes from virtue, and Shimin had begun his reign with fratricide. The reconciliation the historians eventually settled on was that the brothers had themselves been plotting against him, that the Xuanwu Gate ambush was a defensive preemption, and that the subsequent quality of governance demonstrated retroactive virtue. Modern historians are less certain — the "they were going to kill him first" version is largely supported by Tang-era sources written under Taizong's own court — but what is not in doubt is the quality of the reign.

---

Taizong's twenty-three year reign (626–649) is called the **Zhenguan era** (jen-GWAN — after the reign-name slogan "True View"), and it became the gold standard against which all subsequent Chinese reigns would be measured.

The Zhenguan reign got several big things right at once.

Taizong refined the **equal-field system** (juntian — joon-TYEN). The system itself originated under the Northern Wei in the late 5th century (Six Dynasties Ch 6) as a way to distribute reclaimed farmland to peasant households after the chaos of nomadic wars. Under Tang law (codified in 624), each adult male received an allotment of about 80 mu (roughly 12 acres) of farmland. In return, he owed the state a fixed grain tax, a fixed cloth tax, and 20 days of corvée labor per year. The land reverted to the state on his death and was redistributed to other households. The system kept the peasantry tied to the land, kept the tax base broad, and kept the army supplied with men through a parallel **fubing** system in which militia soldiers held farm allotments in exchange for military service. The equal-field model would underpin Tang prosperity for over a century before aristocratic estates and Buddhist monasteries quietly bought, encroached on, and inherited their way around it (Chapter 4).

Taizong expanded the **civil service examination system**. Where Wen had introduced exams as a parallel track, Taizong made them the primary route to the most prestigious positions. The most important degree was the **jinshi** (JIN-shr — "presented scholar"), which tested literary composition (including poetry — yes, the Tang government required prospective bureaucrats to compose poetry as part of the entry exam), Confucian classics, and policy argument. The number of jinshi degrees granted per year was small — typically only a few dozen — but a jinshi pass became the dream of every ambitious family, and the resulting flood of literary effort is one major reason Tang poetry exploded into the form we know it as. (More on the poetry in Chapter 3.)

He expanded the **Tang military** beyond Chinese borders. In 630, the general **Li Jing** led a winter strike that captured the **khagan** (the supreme title of the Turkic ruler) of the **Eastern Turkic Khaganate**, Illig Qaghan. The khaganate collapsed. Taizong resettled the surrendered Turks south of the Gobi, near Chang'an, where they continued to serve in Tang armies as cavalry. The Central Asian Turkic peoples acclaimed Taizong as **Tian Kehan** (TYEN kuh-HAHN — "Heavenly Khan"), an unprecedented title that explicitly combined the Chinese imperial role with supreme rulership over the steppe. No previous Chinese emperor had claimed both. The dual identity was real, not just rhetorical: Taizong's court included Turkic generals, Sogdian translators, and Inner Asian advisors as a matter of routine, and Tang armies continued to push west into the **Tarim Basin** (the great oasis-strung desert that connects China to Central Asia, today's Xinjiang). By 657, under Taizong's successor, the **Western Turkic Khaganate** would also collapse before Tang general **Su Dingfang**, and Tang military protectorates would extend to the borders of Persia.

Taizong cultivated the image of a philosopher-king who welcomed criticism. His most famous advisor was **Wei Zheng** (way JENG), a former servant of his murdered brother who had bluntly opposed Taizong's faction during the succession struggle. Taizong, instead of executing him, made him a senior official and tolerated decades of public criticism. The collected dialogues of their court discussions, compiled later as the **Zhenguan Zhengyao** ("Essentials of Government from the Zhenguan Era"), became required reading for Chinese rulers for centuries. The famous Taizong line, addressed to his court after Wei Zheng's death in 643, runs roughly: "With a mirror of brass, you can adjust your hat. With a mirror of history, you can understand the rise and fall of states. With a mirror of men, you can see whether your actions are right or wrong. I have lost a mirror." Whether or not the sentiment is exactly as recorded — the chronicles polish such moments — the Wei Zheng relationship was real and was the deliberate model Taizong wanted projected.

This combination — institutional reform, military expansion, examination meritocracy, dual Chinese-steppe legitimacy, and the philosopher-king image — defined what people meant by "the Zhenguan era" and the Tang at its early peak. The Sui had reunified China; the Tang under Taizong made the reunification work.

---

Taizong died in 649 and was succeeded by his ninth son, who reigned as **Emperor Gaozong** (GOW-tsong, not to be confused with the founder Gaozu — Chinese emperors had complicated reign-name conventions that produced these collisions). Gaozong's reign continued Taizong's policies, including the conquest of the Western Turks in 657. But Gaozong's most consequential decision was personal rather than political: he married, and then politically empowered, a former low-ranking concubine of his father's named **Wu Zhao** (woo JOW), better known to history as **Wu Zetian** (woo zuh-TYEN).

She would become the only woman in Chinese history to formally hold the title of **Huangdi** ("Emperor") in her own name.

---

Wu Zetian's career is a chronicle of how an exceptionally capable woman could navigate one of the most patriarchal political systems ever constructed.

She entered the imperial palace around age fourteen as a fifth-rank concubine of Emperor Taizong. After his death in 649, all the concubines without children were sent to a Buddhist convent — the standard fate. Gaozong, who had apparently noticed her during his father's lifetime, brought her back to court within a few years. She bore him sons, eliminated rival consorts (the chronicles accuse her of strangling her own newborn daughter and blaming it on the Empress, which would have been a horrifying piece of political maneuvering if true; modern historians regard the accusation as plausible but unproven), and was elevated to Empress in 655.

When Gaozong suffered a debilitating stroke in the early 660s, Wu Zetian effectively governed in his name for years. After Gaozong's death in 683, she ruled through two of her own sons — installing one, deposing him when he proved insufficiently pliable, installing another. In 690, judging that the political conditions were finally right, she deposed her own son, declared the Tang dynasty suspended, and proclaimed herself founder of a new dynasty called the **Zhou** (JOH — chosen for its association with the legendary virtuous **Zhou** dynasty of the early first millennium BCE, Zhou Dynasty Ch 1, not to be confused with the Northern Zhou of Yang Jian's rise). She took the title **Huangdi** — Emperor — the same title used by every previous male ruler. She was sixty-six years old.

This was, by the standards of the Confucian political order, cosmically wrong. Confucian thought taught that the universe operated on a hierarchy of yin (female, passive, receiving) and yang (male, active, governing), and that political rule was structurally yang. A woman ruling as Emperor was not merely improper; it was a violation of the natural order. Wu Zetian's response to this objection was largely to ignore it — she promoted Buddhism, which offered more flexible roles for women than Confucianism, lavishly patronized Buddhist monasteries (commissioning the colossal Buddha at the **Longmen Caves** near Luoyang, with features said to be modeled on her own), and used Buddhist apocalyptic literature suggesting she might be the **Maitreya** (the future Buddha) reborn as a female ruler.

She governed effectively. She expanded the examination system to bring in officials from outside the established northwestern aristocracy, creating a new political class of men who owed their careers to her rather than to family. She moved the capital to **Luoyang** (LWAW-yang), the eastern capital first built under the Sui and Tang as a secondary administrative center. She maintained military pressure on the Turkic peoples on the northern frontier. And she invented around twelve new Chinese characters — most famously a new character for her own name, **Zhao** (照, or in her invented form 曌), composed of "sun" and "moon" above "sky." The new characters mostly fell out of use after her death; her name-character is still occasionally seen.

She was also ruthless. She maintained a network of secret police and informers. She was not above ordering the execution of opposition figures, including members of her own family (two of her own sons died under suspicious circumstances). The Tang historiographical tradition that consolidated after her death vilified her with the standard misogynistic vocabulary — usurper, witch, harlot, depraved — and the picture of her in traditional Chinese histories is uniformly hostile. Modern scholarship has substantially revised the judgment, noting that her administration was genuinely competent, that the empire was stable, that her economic and military policies were sound, and that her usurpation was no bloodier than any number of male-led successions in the same period (the Xuanwu Gate Incident, after all, was a male-led massacre that produced one of China's greatest emperors).

She abdicated in 705 under pressure from a court coup — she was eighty-one years old by then and clearly failing — and the Tang dynasty was formally restored under her son. She died a few months later. Her tombstone at her tomb west of Xi'an was deliberately left blank. This is the **Wordless Stele** (无字碑, *wu zi bei*), which still stands today. Whether the blankness was Wu Zetian's own instruction (her legacy was for history to judge) or her successors' decision (they could not agree on what to write about her) remains debated. Either reading captures something true: she was a ruler whose actual achievements could not easily be reconciled with the moral framework that her own successors used to evaluate rulers, and the easiest solution was silence.

---

The dynasty that Wu Zetian had restored stabilized under a few short reigns and then settled into the long reign of her grandson, **Li Longji** (lee LONG-jee), who ruled from 712 to 756 as **Emperor Xuanzong** (shoo-en-TSONG — yet another Chinese reign name that English speakers must keep distinct from Gaozong, Gaozu, and Taizong; the Tang naming conventions were not built for foreign convenience).

Xuanzong's reign produced the **Kaiyuan era** (713–741), the absolute peak of the Tang. The economy was prosperous, the equal-field system was still functioning, the examination system was producing a steady stream of capable officials, the military protected expansive Central Asian frontiers, and Chang'an was the most cosmopolitan city in the world. The cultural flowering of the Kaiyuan era produced China's greatest poetry, most celebrated court music, and most brilliant Buddhist philosophy. This is the **High Tang** that Chinese historical and literary tradition celebrates above all other moments. Chapter 3 covers it.

The Kaiyuan era was also the setup for the catastrophe. By the 740s, Xuanzong was in his sixties, increasingly disengaged from governance, and infatuated with a consort named **Yang Guifei** (yahng GWAY-fay) whose family was acquiring uncomfortable political power. The frontier military commands had been delegated to a handful of generals who controlled standing armies far larger than the central court could match. The most powerful of those generals was a man of mixed Sogdian-Turkic origin named **An Lushan** (AHN loo-SHAHN), and in December 755 he rebelled. Chapter 4 covers what happened next.

But before the rebellion, before the catastrophe, there is the High Tang itself — the cosmopolitan capital, the poetry, the Silk Road at its peak, the absorption of foreign religions and foreign cultures into Chinese life on a scale that would not be seen again. That is Chapter 3.

Cross-civ check: Wu Zetian's reign (effectively 660s–705) overlaps with the rise of the **Umayyad Caliphate** in the Middle East — the first Islamic empire, which by 700 controlled territory from Spain to Central Asia (Islamic Golden Age Ch 2). The Umayyad-Tang frontier in Central Asia was already shaping up; the Battle of Talas in 751 (Chapter 4) would be its first major collision. The **Byzantine Empire** under the Heraclian dynasty was simultaneously losing Egypt, Syria, and North Africa to the Arab conquests (Byzantine Empire Ch 5). In Japan, the **Taika Reform** of 645 had remodeled the Japanese government on Tang lines, copying Tang law, examinations, and capital design (Ancient Japan Ch 7) — Nara, founded in 710 on the Daxing/Chang'an grid, is one of the clearest examples of Tang cultural projection. In Korea, the **Silla** kingdom unified the peninsula in 668 with Tang military assistance (Ancient Korea Ch 6), then promptly fought Tang armies to assert independence. The Tang influence on East Asia in this period was so deep that historians sometimes treat the entire region as a Tang-centered cultural sphere — the original "Sinosphere."

---

# Chapter 3 — The Cosmopolitan Capital

There are not many cities in human history that you can say with confidence were the largest, most populous, and most culturally important on Earth at a specific moment. Tang **Chang'an** in the early 8th century is one of them.

The numbers matter, so it's worth pausing on them. Chang'an covered approximately 32 square miles (84 km²) inside its walls — the inner walled city alone was about seven times the size of contemporary **Constantinople**, the largest city in Europe at the time, and roughly five times the area of Baghdad after the Abbasids built it in the 760s. The walls themselves were about 18 miles (29 km) in circumference. The central north-south avenue, **Zhuque Boulevard**, was 150 meters wide — nearly 500 feet, broader than any street in any modern city. The population at peak is estimated at over a million inside the walls, with another million in the suburbs and surrounding districts. By comparison, contemporary Constantinople probably held 300,000–500,000; Baghdad after its 762 founding would briefly approach the same range; Rome at its imperial peak had reached around a million but had collapsed to perhaps 50,000 by the 8th century. Tang Chang'an in 700–750 was the largest and most populous city on Earth, and was probably the largest city the world had ever seen up to that point.

It was also, by a significant margin, the most cosmopolitan.

---

The grid-plan structure of Chang'an, inherited from Yuwen Kai's Sui-era Daxing (Chapter 1), was a perfect machine for managing a multicultural population. The 108 walled wards — each a city block surrounded by its own internal walls with gates that closed at curfew — could be assigned to specific communities. Foreign quarters, religious enclaves, and residential districts for different social classes all had their own ward or cluster of wards. This was social engineering by urban planning.

The foreign communities were extensive and visible. **Sogdian** merchants from Central Asia (Iranian-speaking traders, the dominant intermediaries of Silk Road commerce) had their own quarters — Sogdian-named families show up routinely in Tang tax records, court documents, and tomb inscriptions. **Persian** refugees and merchants formed substantial communities, particularly after the Sassanid Empire fell to the Arab Muslim conquest in the 650s (Persian Empire Ch 10) and the last Sassanid prince **Peroz III** fled east to seek Tang protection. **Arab** merchants arrived after the establishment of the Umayyad and then Abbasid caliphates, traveling the Silk Road and the southern sea route. **Indian** Buddhist monks and scholars came overland through the **Tarim Basin** or by sea through the southern ports. **Korean** students came in numbers — the Tang court ran what was essentially an international university for sons of Korean and Japanese aristocrats — and **Japanese** delegations came on the periodic embassies known as the **kentōshi** (Ancient Japan Ch 7), spending years studying Tang government, law, art, and Buddhism before returning home.

The religions present in Chang'an reflected this. **Buddhism** was by far the most influential foreign religion, having arrived from India centuries earlier (Six Dynasties Ch 5) and now fully integrated into Chinese life. But the city also hosted **Zoroastrian** fire temples, **Nestorian Christian** churches (the Christian community even put up a stele in 781, the **Nestorian Stele**, recording the arrival of Christianity in 635 under Taizong — an extraordinary document that astonished European missionaries when it was rediscovered in 1625, since they had assumed they were bringing Christianity to China for the first time), **Manichaean** shrines (Manichaeism was a Persian religion that survived in Central Asia long after dying out in Persia itself), and Islamic **mosques** following the establishment of regular Muslim merchant communities in the 7th and 8th centuries.

This was not religious tolerance in the modern liberal sense. The Tang state regulated foreign religions, restricted certain kinds of conversion, and would in 845 launch a major persecution of Buddhism (Chapter 4). What the Tang state did do, especially in the High Tang period, was permit foreign communities to maintain their own religious institutions in their own neighborhoods on the assumption that this kept them stable and their merchants productive. The framework was pragmatic, not principled. It produced something like the most religiously diverse single city in the early-medieval world.

---

The economic engine driving all this was the **Silk Road** at its historical peak.

The "Silk Road" is a name coined by the 19th-century German geographer **Ferdinand von Richthofen** (a useful one-line intro: he was a Berlin geographer who traveled extensively in China in the 1860s–70s and named the trade network *Seidenstrasse* in an 1877 publication). It was not a single road but a network of overland and maritime trade routes connecting China to Central Asia, Persia, and the Mediterranean. The network had operated since the **Han dynasty** (Han Dynasty Ch 5), when Chinese diplomatic missions had first opened direct contact with the Central Asian kingdoms. It had been disrupted during the Six Dynasties chaos. The Tang military expansion into the Tarim Basin in the 630s–650s, and the establishment of the **Anxi Protectorate** (the Tang military command zone covering the four great oasis cities of **Kucha**, **Khotan**, **Kashgar**, and **Karashahr**) gave Chinese armies direct control over the Silk Road oases for the first time since the Han.

What flowed through the network is worth pausing on. From China westward: silk (China was the only producer of silk for centuries, and silk remained an enormous luxury throughout the Mediterranean world), porcelain, paper, lacquerware, and silver. Eastward from Central Asia, Persia, and beyond: horses (Tang armies depended on imported horses for cavalry, and the Tang state ran an enormous bureaucracy just to manage the horse trade), gemstones, glassware (Tang Chinese glassblowing was minimal; almost all glass was imported from Persia or further west), spices, aromatic woods, exotic foods, musical instruments, and slaves. The trade was conducted primarily by **Sogdian merchants** speaking the Sogdian language, which served as the **lingua franca** of Silk Road commerce. Sogdian-language documents have been found from China to the Black Sea. (Sogdian is now extinct as a spoken language; its closest modern relative is **Yaghnobi**, spoken by a few thousand people in the mountains of Tajikistan.)

The cultural traffic was as significant as the goods. Central Asian music, dance, food, fashion, and art flooded into Tang Chang'an. The **pipa** (a four-string lute) is a Central Asian instrument that Chinese musicians adopted and that became a defining sound of Chinese classical music. The **whirling dances** of the Sogdian and Central Asian performers (one famous example: An Lushan, of whom more shortly, was reportedly an enormously fat man — over 300 pounds — whose performance of Sogdian whirling dances delighted Emperor Xuanzong's court) became standard entertainment at imperial banquets. Tang ceramic figurines from this period preserve a remarkable visual record of the era: Bactrian camels loaded with goods accompanied by bearded Sogdian merchants, foreign musicians playing Central Asian instruments, dancers in foreign dress, foreign ambassadors in their distinctive costumes. These figurines are the single best surviving record of how visibly multicultural Chang'an was — the **sancai** (san-TSIGH, "three-color") glazed Tang figurines in amber, green, and cream are among the most recognizable Tang artifacts.

The maritime trade route — through the South China Sea, the Strait of Malacca, the Indian Ocean, and on to East Africa — was also active by the High Tang, particularly through the southern ports of **Guangzhou** (gwang-JOH) and **Quanzhou** (chwen-JOH). Arab merchants established communities in Guangzhou by the late 7th century; by 879 the Arab community in Guangzhou would number in the tens of thousands (with disastrous consequences in that year — Chapter 4). The maritime route would become dominant after the An Lushan Rebellion disrupted the overland Silk Road, and would peak under the Song (Chapter 8).

---

The cultural product the Tang is most remembered for is **poetry**.

Tang poetry occupies a position in Chinese culture roughly comparable to what Shakespeare occupies in English — except that the Tang produced about a dozen poets of comparable stature rather than one, and the result is that contemporary Chinese schoolchildren memorize hundreds of Tang poems through their education. Two figures dominate the canon, and they have been paired in Chinese literary criticism for over a thousand years: **Li Bai** (lee BIGH) and **Du Fu** (doo FOO).

**Li Bai** (701–762), called the **Immortal Poet** (Shixian — SHEE-shyen), was the wild one. He was probably born in Central Asia to a Chinese family — there are theories that his family had Sogdian or Turkic connections, though the evidence is thin — and grew up in what is now **Sichuan**. He never sat the civil service examinations and never had a stable government career; instead he wandered China as a Daoist-influenced poet, drinking, writing, occasionally appearing at court (he had a brief stint as a court poet under Xuanzong before being dismissed for, depending on the source, drunkenness or arrogance or political missteps), and dying in obscurity. Legend says he drowned trying to embrace the moon's reflection in a river while drunk; the reality was probably more prosaic.

His poetry is what survives him. Li Bai wrote with an apparent effortlessness on subjects like wine, moonlight, friendship, the natural world, and the sense of cosmic isolation that comes from being a human in a vast universe. His best-known poem, "Quiet Night Thought" (床前明月光, *Chuangqian mingyue guang*), runs only twenty characters in Chinese — about a paragraph in English translation. It is probably the most widely memorized Chinese poem ever written; virtually every Chinese schoolchild can recite it. Its surface is minimal. Its emotional reach is enormous. The combination of formal compression and emotional vastness is the Li Bai signature.

**Du Fu** (712–770), called the **Poet Sage** (Shisheng — SHEE-shung), was the disciplined one. He came from a family of scholars, sat the examinations and failed (multiple times — Du Fu's examination failures became part of his legend), spent much of his adult life in poverty, and lived through the An Lushan Rebellion as a refugee. The catastrophe of those years shaped his work permanently. Where Li Bai writes about cosmic isolation, Du Fu writes about national tragedy: families torn apart by war, peasants conscripted to die in border campaigns, the spring view from his windowsill turning into a meditation on dynastic collapse. His poem "Spring View" (春望, *Chun wang*) — written in 757, after the An Lushan rebels had captured Chang'an and Du Fu was trapped in the occupied city — opens with the characters 国破山河在 (the country is broken; the mountains and rivers remain). It is one of the most quoted lines in all of Chinese literature.

Du Fu also perfected the formal technical structure of Chinese poetry. The form called **lüshi** (LOO-shr — "regulated verse") is a strict eight-line poem with rigid rules about tonal patterns and parallelism between paired lines; Du Fu was its supreme practitioner, and "Du Fu lüshi" became the benchmark against which all later regulated verse was measured.

The pairing of Li Bai and Du Fu — the wild Daoist transcendentalist and the disciplined Confucian witness, the sky and the earth, the wine cup and the conscience — became the structuring frame for how Chinese literary tradition organized its understanding of poetry. They knew each other (briefly) in person; Du Fu wrote multiple poems for and about Li Bai, in tones of deep affection and deeper anxiety about Li Bai's drinking and instability. The friendship is one of the most touching footnotes in Chinese literary history, two of the greatest poets in any language each writing about the other.

Tang poetry is enormous — the standard *Complete Tang Poetry* compiled in 1705 contains over 48,000 poems by more than 2,200 authors — and Li Bai and Du Fu are only the two most famous of dozens of major figures. **Wang Wei** (701–761) was a court official who wrote spare, Chan-Buddhist-influenced landscape poetry that paired with his work as a painter (Wang Wei is sometimes considered the founder of Chinese literati landscape painting, though most of his actual paintings have been lost). **Bai Juyi** (BIGH joo-EE, 772–846) wrote in a deliberately accessible style — legend has it he read his drafts aloud to an illiterate elderly servant and revised anything she could not understand, which is probably an exaggeration but captures something true about his voice. His 120-line narrative poem "Song of Everlasting Sorrow" (长恨歌, *Changhen ge*), about Emperor Xuanzong's love affair with Yang Guifei and her death during the An Lushan Rebellion, is one of the most famous poems in the language. Bai Juyi was also enormously influential abroad — his collected works arrived in Japan within his own lifetime and shaped the **Tale of Genji** (the great Heian-period Japanese novel of around 1010) and centuries of Japanese poetry.

The structural reason Tang poetry exploded in this period was partly the examination system: composing regulated verse on assigned topics was a required component of the jinshi exam, which meant that every educated man in the empire — every aspiring official, every gentleman-scholar — was practicing poetry as a professional skill from his teenage years onward. This produced a vast amateur poetic culture out of which the great geniuses emerged. The structural reason it eventually mattered globally is that the Chinese examination model was eventually adopted by Korea (Gwageo), Vietnam, and Japan (the Heian court adopted Chinese-style exams in modified form), spreading Tang literary forms and conventions throughout East Asia.

---

Beyond poetry, the High Tang produced equally important developments in religion, calligraphy, and visual art.

**Chan Buddhism** (CHAHN — pronounced "chan" in Chinese, transmitted to Japan as **Zen**, to Korea as **Seon**, to Vietnam as **Thiền**) crystallized as a distinctly Chinese school during the Tang. Its tradition traced its lineage back to **Bodhidharma** (a monk said to have arrived from India in the early 6th century, though his historicity is debated; Six Dynasties Ch 5), but its mature form developed over the 7th and 8th centuries. Chan emphasized meditation (*chan* is the Chinese pronunciation of the Sanskrit *dhyana*, "meditation") and the possibility of sudden enlightenment through direct experience rather than through textual study or accumulated merit. The **Sixth Patriarch Huineng** (HWAY-nung, c. 638–713) is the foundational Chan figure; his **Platform Sutra**, supposedly recorded by his disciples after his death, is the only Chinese-composed text accorded the status of "sutra" (a word normally reserved for the discourses of the historical Buddha himself). Chan would survive the Tang's anti-Buddhist persecution (Chapter 4) better than most Buddhist schools precisely because it required minimal institutional infrastructure — no large monasteries, no expensive liturgical equipment, just teacher-student transmission. Chan would shape East Asian aesthetics for the next thousand years through its influence on tea ceremony, garden design, ink-painting, and martial arts.

**Calligraphy** reached one of its peaks in the Tang. **Yan Zhenqing** (YEN jen-CHING, 709–785) developed a bold, structured style of regular script (kaishu) that broke from the elegant lyrical tradition of the great Six Dynasties calligrapher **Wang Xizhi** (Wang Xee-JEH, 303–361, often called the "Sage of Calligraphy" — Six Dynasties Ch 4). Yan's "muscular" style emphasized structural integrity over delicacy. He was also a model Confucian scholar-official: he organized military resistance to the An Lushan rebels in the northeast, served the Tang faithfully through decades of crises, and was finally executed at age seventy-six by a regional warlord rebel for refusing to defect. The combination of artistic mastery and personal integrity made Yan Zhenqing the Confucian ideal of a scholar-official in posterity, and his calligraphic style became one of the four standard models taught in Chinese schools for over a millennium.

**Painting** developed under the Tang in directions that the Song would extend (Chapter 8). Tang painters worked in colored mineral pigments on silk, with a specialty in horse paintings (the Tang court's Central Asian horses were prestige objects and frequent subjects), figure paintings, and decorative court scenes. The shift to monochrome ink and to the dominant Chinese genre of monumental **shanshui** (mountains-and-water) landscape painting would happen under the Song.

**Pottery** is one of the era's most globally recognizable products. The **sancai** (three-color) glazed ceramics of the Tang — usually camels, horses, court ladies, foreign musicians, and tomb guardians, glazed in amber, green, and cream — are among the most distinctive Tang artifacts. They have been found across Eurasia. The **Belitung shipwreck**, an Arab dhow that sank around 830 in the Java Sea while carrying Tang export ceramics back to the Middle East, contained over 60,000 pieces, including bowls, dishes, ewers, and the earliest known Chinese **blue-and-white** porcelain (which would later become the global signature of Chinese ceramics under the Yuan and Ming). The English word "china" for porcelain comes from this trade. Tang ceramics established the global demand for Chinese pottery that would define international trade for the next thousand years, and that would eventually drive the **Ming** to mass-produce blue-and-white for European markets seven centuries later.

---

A pause, before the catastrophe.

The High Tang of Xuanzong's Kaiyuan era (713–741) is one of those moments in human history that produces more than its fair share of cultural heritage. China had been reunified for 150 years by Xuanzong's reign and was at peace internally. The economy was prosperous. The military had pushed Chinese influence further west than at any point in history. The capital was the largest city in the world and the most multicultural city in pre-modern Eurasia. The poetry, calligraphy, ceramics, music, and Buddhism of the era are still actively quoted, performed, and practiced in modern China. There is a reason the Kaiyuan reign is the moment Chinese cultural memory returns to most often.

It is also the case that this moment was created by structural conditions that would not last. The equal-field land system was already breaking down — the chronicles of the 740s record growing problems with private estates, falling tax revenues, and the corruption of local administration. The frontier military system had drifted from the Sui-era model of fubing militia farmers to professional standing armies under regional military governors called **jiedushi** (jyeh-DOO-shr — "military commissioners"), each commanding tens of thousands of professional soldiers. The court was dominated by Yang Guifei's family faction (her cousin **Yang Guozhong** rose to become chancellor) in growing conflict with the most powerful jiedushi, **An Lushan**, whose three frontier commands in the northeast controlled over 160,000 troops. The fiscal cost of the standing armies was enormous, and the Tang had no good way to control commanders who controlled their own soldiers, payrolls, and supply networks.

Xuanzong, by the 740s and 750s, was in his sixties and increasingly disengaged. He had been a vigorous reformist emperor in his early decades; in his later years, he delegated. The combination — failing land system, restive professional armies, weak central control, factional war at court — was exactly the recipe for the catastrophe that arrived in December 755.

---

Cross-civ check: the High Tang Chang'an of the early 8th century is contemporary with the **Umayyad Caliphate** at its peak, with **Damascus** as its capital and territory from Spain to Central Asia. The Umayyads would be overthrown by the **Abbasid Caliphate** in 750 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1), shifting the Islamic capital east to **Baghdad** (founded 762). The Abbasid Baghdad of the 8th–9th century would briefly approach Chang'an in size and would inherit the cosmopolitan-trade-network position the Tang was about to lose. The **Byzantine Empire** under the **Isaurian** dynasty was wracked by the **Iconoclast** controversy over religious images (Byzantine Empire Ch 5). The **Carolingian** dynasty in western Europe was consolidating under **Charles Martel** (defeated the Arabs at Tours in 732) and his grandson **Charlemagne** (crowned Holy Roman Emperor in 800). In Mesoamerica, the great **Maya** city of **Tikal** was at its peak (Maya Civilization Ch 6). In the Andes, the **Wari** and **Tiwanaku** empires were dominating their respective regions. The 8th century Eurasian world was unusually densely interconnected — the silk that left Tang Chang'an reached Constantinople and Baghdad and was traded onward to Carolingian Europe, while Buddhist and Islamic ideas circulated along the same routes — and Tang Chang'an was the eastern anchor of the system.

The system was about to break.

---

# Chapter 4 — The An Lushan Rebellion and the Late Tang

In December 755, **An Lushan** rebelled against the Tang dynasty, and it took eight years of warfare to suppress him. The fighting killed something on the order of 13 to 36 million people, by the most cited modern estimate. That number deserves some context.

The estimate comes from a comparison of Tang census records before and after the rebellion. The 754 census recorded a registered population of about 53 million. The 764 census, after the rebellion's end, recorded about 17 million. A 36-million decline in registered population in a decade would imply, if taken literally, the deadliest war in human history before the 20th century — comparable in raw scale to World War II. Modern historians treat the figure as imperfect but suggestive. Some of the apparent decline reflects people who simply stopped being registered for taxation as the central state collapsed (many regions slid out of central control, with their populations vanishing from the imperial books while continuing to live and pay taxes to local warlords). Some reflects refugee dispersal, with people fleeing south or to remote areas where census-takers could not reach them. Some reflects deliberate evasion as families dropped off the registers to avoid corvée and military conscription. The conservative range — even adjusting downward for these factors — still puts the death toll in the millions, possibly the low tens of millions, making the An Lushan Rebellion one of the deadliest single conflicts in pre-modern human history. The Mongol conquests (Mongol Empire Ch 5) and the Ming-Qing transition (Qing Dynasty's founding) are the only competitors.

Whatever the exact number, the demographic and political impact was catastrophic. The Tang dynasty technically lasted another 144 years after the rebellion ended in 763, but it never recovered its central authority. The rest of the Tang story is the slow, often beautiful decay of an empire that had lost its core.

---

**An Lushan** himself is one of the strangest figures in Chinese history.

He was born around 703 in the borderlands northeast of China, of mixed **Sogdian** and **Turkic** ancestry (his father was probably Sogdian; his mother was a Turkic shamaness). His name in Sogdian was probably **Rokhshan**, with **An** being the Sinified version of his clan name and **Lushan** the Sinified version of his given name. He grew up multilingual — he reportedly spoke six languages — and entered the Tang military as a frontier officer in his twenties, rising rapidly through ability and through the patronage of the powerful Tang general **Zhang Shougui**.

He was also enormously fat. The chronicles describe him as weighing over 330 pounds, with a belly that hung to his knees, and report that Emperor Xuanzong's court found his obesity comically endearing. An Lushan exploited this — he played the role of the loyal foreign clown at court, performing Sogdian whirling dances despite his bulk, addressing Yang Guifei (despite being older than her) as his "mother," and presenting himself to Xuanzong as a barbarian buffoon whose loyalty was beyond question. In private, he was acquiring three of the Tang's most important frontier military commands. By the early 750s, he was simultaneously **jiedushi** of three of the seven major frontier zones (Pinglu, Fanyang, and Hedong) — controlling the northeast frontier from the area of modern Beijing west to Shanxi — with over 160,000 professional troops under his direct command. Almost half the Tang's frontier military strength reported to a single man.

The political crisis that triggered the rebellion was the rivalry between An Lushan and **Yang Guozhong** (yahng gwaw-CHONG), Yang Guifei's cousin who had risen to become chief chancellor at court. Yang Guozhong, watching An Lushan accumulate military power, repeatedly warned Xuanzong that An Lushan was about to rebel. An Lushan, hearing of Yang Guozhong's accusations, became convinced that he had to rebel before being purged. Xuanzong refused to credit the warnings — he liked An Lushan, considered him a personal favorite, and reportedly said his trust was so complete that he would believe An Lushan if he said the sun and moon had collided.

In December 755, An Lushan marched south from his base at **Fanyang** (modern Beijing) at the head of his frontier armies, claiming he had been sent by secret imperial decree to remove Yang Guozhong from court. Loyalty to "Emperor Xuanzong against treacherous officials" was the conventional cover story for any Chinese coup. Within weeks, An Lushan had crossed the Yellow River and captured **Luoyang**, the eastern capital. In January 756, he formally proclaimed himself emperor of a new **Yan** dynasty.

---

The Tang response was incompetent at the highest level and brave at the operational level.

The strategic disaster was at the **Tongguan Pass** (TONG-gwan), the fortified mountain pass that controlled the approach to Chang'an from the east. Tang general **Geshu Han** held the pass with a Tang army that, while inferior in quality to An Lushan's professionals, occupied a position so strong that the rebels could not force it. Yang Guozhong, fearing Geshu Han's growing political importance and possibly suspecting his loyalty, pressured Xuanzong to order Geshu Han to abandon the pass and attack the rebel army in the open. Geshu Han knew this was suicidal but obeyed. The Tang army was destroyed. The road to Chang'an was open.

Xuanzong fled west toward Sichuan in June 756, with Yang Guifei, Yang Guozhong, and the imperial entourage. At a place called **Mawei Station** (mah-WAY), about 60 miles west of Chang'an, the imperial guards mutinied. They killed Yang Guozhong and his sons, then demanded that Yang Guifei be killed as well. Xuanzong, aged seventy-one, refused for a moment and then conceded. Yang Guifei was strangled with a silk cord by the chief eunuch **Gao Lishi**. The episode became one of the great tragic love stories in Chinese literature, mourned by Bai Juyi in his "Song of Everlasting Sorrow" (Chapter 3) and retold in Chinese poetry, opera, and film for over a thousand years afterward.

Xuanzong continued into Sichuan. His son **Li Heng**, who had been left behind with another army at the city of **Lingwu** in the northwest, was acclaimed emperor by his troops without his father's approval. He took the throne as **Emperor Suzong** (SOO-tsong), and Xuanzong, now in Sichuan, accepted the fait accompli and abdicated. The dynasty had two emperors briefly, then one again. Chang'an itself fell to An Lushan's forces in late 756. The rebels looted the capital and conducted what amounted to ethnic-cleansing raids on the city's Persian, Sogdian, and other foreign communities — many of which were unfairly associated with An Lushan because of his Sogdian ethnicity, even though most foreigners in Chang'an had no political connection to him.

---

The recovery took eight years.

The Tang court, operating from refuge in the northwest, called on multiple sources of military aid. The most important was the **Uyghur Khaganate** (OO-ee-gur — a Turkic-speaking confederation that had risen on the Mongolian steppe after the collapse of the Eastern Turkic Khaganate, displacing the Tang's earlier Turkic vassals). The Uyghurs sent cavalry south in exchange for trade privileges, formal recognition, and substantial bribes. The Uyghur cavalry was militarily decisive — the recovery of Chang'an in 757 and Luoyang shortly afterward depended on Uyghur shock cavalry assaulting positions Tang infantry could not take alone. The price was that the Uyghurs were allowed to sack Luoyang as their reward in 762, and that the Tang court formally treated the Uyghur khagan as a peer rather than as a vassal — a major status shift away from the Tang's earlier dual-monarchy posture in Central Asia.

The rebellion itself fragmented and turned on itself. An Lushan was murdered in January 757 by his own son **An Qingxu**, who had been alarmed by his father's growing erratic behavior — An Lushan was going blind, possibly diabetic, possibly mentally ill, and had reportedly been beating his entourage with sticks. An Qingxu then ruled the rebel state briefly before being murdered in turn by another rebel general, **Shi Siming**, who in turn was murdered by his son **Shi Chaoyi** in 761. The rebel dynasty consumed itself through patricide and assassination over four years before finally collapsing in 763. The pattern is too neat to be entirely accidental — once the founding charisma of An Lushan was gone, the rebel army fragmented along the same lines that the late Tang frontier military had fragmented, with each commander fighting for personal power.

By the official end of the rebellion in 763, the Tang dynasty had survived but was structurally transformed. Northern China was depopulated. The economy was wrecked. The equal-field land system had collapsed beyond repair — too many records had been destroyed, too many peasants had fled, too many estates had absorbed too much abandoned land. The Tang military system based on **fubing** militia-farmers was gone, replaced by professional standing armies under regional military governors. Most consequentially, the central government had largely lost control over those regional governors. The **jiedushi** (military commissioners) of the old frontier zones — and a growing number of jiedushi in the interior, set up during the rebellion to fight the rebels and never demobilized after — became effectively autonomous regional warlords. They collected their own taxes, raised their own armies, controlled their own succession (often passing the office father-to-son), and paid only nominal loyalty to the Tang court.

The Tang dynasty after 763 was no longer the centralized empire of the Zhenguan and Kaiyuan eras. It was an emperor in Chang'an surrounded by a patchwork of semi-independent military regions, with central control most effective in the southern and central regions and weakest in the northeast. This "fragmented Tang" lasted from 763 to 907 — 144 years, longer than the dynasty had existed before the rebellion — and produced its own art, poetry, and political innovations even as it slowly disintegrated.

---

The cultural life of the Late Tang was, against expectations, almost as rich as the High Tang.

**Du Fu** (Chapter 3) wrote his greatest work in the years after the rebellion, as a refugee. His poems on the war's effects on ordinary people — "Song of the War Chariots" on the conscription system, "Three Officials" and "Three Partings" on the recruitment of new troops from devastated villages — are among the most powerful war poetry in any language. He died in 770, in poverty, on a small boat on the Xiang River in Hunan. His reputation grew slowly over the next two centuries; by the Song dynasty, he was generally accepted as the greatest poet in the Chinese tradition.

**Bai Juyi** (Chapter 3) wrote his "Song of Everlasting Sorrow" in 806, retelling the Yang Guifei tragedy. He also wrote the **New Yuefu** poems, which used verse to critique social injustice — the corvée system, the conscription burden, the corruption of the military commissions. His commitment to clarity over obscurity made him one of the most widely read poets in East Asian history; his collected works arrived in Japan within his lifetime.

**Han Yu** (HAHN YOO, 768–824) is a different sort of figure. He was a senior official, a successful examination candidate, and a leader of a literary movement called the **guwen** (GOO-wen — "ancient prose") movement, which rejected the highly ornate parallel-prose style that had dominated Chinese writing since the Six Dynasties (Six Dynasties Ch 4) in favor of a return to the more direct, classical style of pre-Qin and Han writers. Han Yu wrote a famously controversial **memorial** to Emperor Xianzong in 819, opposing the imperial veneration of a Buddhist relic (a finger bone of the Buddha that had been brought to Chang'an for ceremonial display). Han Yu argued that Buddhism was a foreign corruption of Chinese values, that the cult of relics was vulgar superstition, and that the emperor was demeaning himself by participating. The emperor was furious; Han Yu was nearly executed and was instead exiled to the southern frontier. The memorial became one of the most famous prose pieces in Chinese literature, and Han Yu is generally counted as one of the **Eight Great Prose Masters** of the Tang and Song. His anti-Buddhist Confucian revival also planted the seeds that would grow into **Neo-Confucianism** under the Song three centuries later (Chapter 8).

The Late Tang also produced the **Diamond Sutra** of 868 — a Buddhist text printed by woodblock on a 17-foot scroll, commissioned by a man named **Wang Jie** "for universal free distribution," and recovered from the **Mogao Caves** at **Dunhuang** (DUN-hwahng — the great oasis-city Buddhist library on the Silk Road, in the western Gansu corridor). The Diamond Sutra is the world's oldest dated complete printed book, predating Gutenberg's first European movable-type printing by nearly six hundred years. It was sealed in a hidden cave at Dunhuang around 1000 CE, rediscovered by a Daoist caretaker named **Wang Yuanlu** in 1900, and acquired by the Hungarian-British archaeologist **Aurel Stein** in 1907 for the British Museum. It is now in the British Library. Its survival is one of the great accidents of textual history — the Mogao Caves' dry desert climate preserved tens of thousands of Buddhist manuscripts, paintings, and printed texts that would have been destroyed almost anywhere else in China.

---

Two events bracket the dynasty's actual end.

The first is the **Great Anti-Buddhist Persecution** of 845, under **Emperor Wuzong** (WOO-tsong, ruled 840–846). Wuzong was a devoted **Daoist** (in the religious sense — Daoism in this period had developed into a full religious tradition with its own clergy, monasteries, and rituals, parallel to Buddhism). His advisors, particularly the Confucian official **Li Deyu**, argued that Buddhism was a foreign religion that had accumulated vast tax-exempt monastic lands and was draining the empire's economic productivity. The economic argument was sound — by some estimates, Buddhist monasteries owned 10–15% of the productive land in China by the mid-9th century, and the monastic population was a substantial demographic loss to the tax base.

In 845, Wuzong ordered the destruction of 4,600 Buddhist monasteries and 40,000 minor shrines, the forced laicization of 260,000 monks and nuns, and the confiscation of monastic landholdings. Bronze Buddhist statues were melted down and recoined as currency (a one-way conversion of religious art into money that historians have lamented ever since). The persecution was carried out systematically over several years, and despite being formally rolled back after Wuzong's death in 846, the institutional damage to Chinese Buddhism was permanent. The schools that survived best were the ones that needed least infrastructure: **Chan**, with its emphasis on personal teacher-student transmission rather than monastic learning, came through almost intact, which is part of why Chan dominates East Asian Buddhism today. The scholastic schools — **Tiantai**, **Huayan**, **Yogacara** — that depended on large monasteries with libraries and trained scholarly clergy never recovered to their former scale.

The second is the **Huang Chao Rebellion** (HWAHNG CHOW), 875–884. **Huang Chao** was a salt trader from **Caozhou** (TSAW-joh, in modern Shandong) who had repeatedly failed the civil service examinations. When peasant uprisings broke out in the late 870s in response to floods, famine, heavy taxation, and government corruption, Huang Chao rose to lead them. His army moved across China for nearly a decade. In 879, the rebels sacked **Guangzhou** (the great southern port that hosted China's largest foreign merchant community); contemporary accounts claim 120,000 foreign merchants were massacred — a figure that is probably exaggerated but that captures the scale of the catastrophe and that effectively ended Guangzhou's role as a major international port for over a century. In 880, Huang Chao captured Chang'an and declared himself emperor of a new "Great Qi" dynasty. Tang loyalist forces, supported by the **Shatuo** (SHAH-twaw — a Turkic-speaking people of the northern frontier) cavalry under their leader **Li Keyong**, drove him out by 884. Huang Chao died (suicide, or possibly killed by his nephew, depending on the source).

But Chang'an was finished. The capital had been sacked, depopulated, looted, and partly burned. The Tang court returned briefly but the city never recovered as a functioning capital. The warlords who had fought the rebellion were now effectively independent. The most powerful of them was **Zhu Wen** (JOO WEN), originally a Huang Chao rebel who had defected to the Tang side in 882 and been rewarded with major commands. By 900, Zhu Wen controlled the central court, and the last Tang emperor was a hostage to his power.

In 907, Zhu Wen forced the last Tang emperor to abdicate and proclaimed his own dynasty, the **Later Liang**. The Tang dynasty was over after 289 years. Chinese historians would later count Zhu Wen's 907 coup as the beginning of the **Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms** period (Chapter 5) — fifty years of fragmentation that would only end with the Song reunification in 960.

---

Cross-civ check: the An Lushan Rebellion (755–763) was contemporary with the **Abbasid Revolution** that overthrew the Umayyad Caliphate in 750 and established the Abbasid capital at **Baghdad** in 762 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). The two events together — the Tang's structural collapse and the Abbasid consolidation — fundamentally redrew the Eurasian power map. The **Battle of Talas** in 751 had already established that the Tang and the Abbasids would share the Central Asian frontier; the An Lushan Rebellion forced the Tang to withdraw its westernmost garrisons, leaving the Tarim Basin oases under nominal Tang control but with no actual Tang military presence, and effectively ended Chinese westward expansion for nearly a millennium. The cultural exchange continued — Abbasid astronomers, mathematicians, and physicians were absorbing Chinese papermaking technology (transmitted by the prisoners taken at Talas) and would shortly be transmitting Indian numerals and Greek philosophy to a Tang-Song China at the eastern end of the network. In Europe, **Charlemagne** was crowned **Holy Roman Emperor** on Christmas Day 800, a few decades after An Lushan's rebellion. In Japan, the **Heian** period began in 794 with the founding of **Heian-kyō** (modern Kyoto) — modeled on Chang'an — and produced the great Heian court culture (Ancient Japan Ch 8). In Mesoamerica, the **Maya** Classic Period was beginning to collapse around 800 (Maya Civilization Ch 7), an unrelated catastrophe that took down the Maya city-states for reasons still debated. The 8th and 9th centuries were a moment of widespread structural strain across the Eurasian and Mesoamerican civilizations; the Tang's collapse was one of the most consequential examples.

---

# Chapter 5 — The Five Dynasties & Song Founding

The fifty years between the fall of the Tang in 907 and the founding of the Song in 960 are called the **Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms** period. It is the shortest interval of significant fragmentation in Chinese history after the Han collapse, and it has the unusual characteristic that almost nothing of cultural lasting importance was created during it. Five short-lived dynasties succeeded each other in the north, each lasting on average about thirteen years before being overthrown by its own military commanders. Ten more stable but smaller kingdoms divided the south. The northern dynasties — **Later Liang** (907–923), **Later Tang** (923–936), **Later Jin** (936–946), **Later Han** (947–950), and **Later Zhou** (951–960) — all imitated the Tang's institutional structures while controlling far less territory and far less population. They were ruled, with one or two exceptions, by Shatuo Turkic generals who had risen to military prominence in the late Tang anti-rebel campaigns.

The single most consequential event of the Five Dynasties period is also one of the saddest: the **Loss of the Sixteen Prefectures** in 938.

The story is short and sordid. **Shi Jingtang** (shih jing-TAHNG), a Shatuo Turkic general serving the Later Tang dynasty, rebelled against his own emperor in 936. To win the support he needed to take the throne, he allied with the **Khitan Liao** dynasty — a steppe empire that had risen in **Manchuria** and **Inner Mongolia** in the early 10th century under the leader **Abaoji**. Shi Jingtang offered the Liao a price for their military support: the **Sixteen Prefectures** of northern China, including the area around modern **Beijing**, the city of **Datong**, and the mountain passes through the **Yan Mountains** and the **Taihang Mountains** that formed the natural defensive barrier between the northern Chinese plain and the steppe. The Khitan accepted, helped Shi Jingtang win the throne (he founded the Later Jin dynasty in 936), and took possession of the prefectures.

The strategic significance of the cession is hard to overstate. The Sixteen Prefectures included the major mountain passes through which any cavalry-based steppe army would have to enter the Chinese heartland. Without them, the rich agricultural northern Chinese plain — including the future Beijing region, the Yellow River corridor, and the productive farmland of modern Hebei and Henan — lay open to direct cavalry attack. The Song dynasty would spend its entire 167-year northern existence trying to recover these prefectures, and would never succeed. The Jurchen conquest of 1127 (Chapter 7) and the Mongol conquest of the 13th century (Chapter 9) both poured through these passes; without them, both invasions might have been delayed or blunted.

Shi Jingtang's deal is one of those moments where you can name the specific decision that produced centuries of consequence. Modern Chinese historiography treats him about the way English-language historiography treats the figures who let the Goths into the Roman Empire: a short-term opportunist who created a permanent strategic disaster. The Later Jin dynasty itself lasted only ten years before being overthrown by the Khitan Liao, who briefly invaded and looted central China in the late 940s before withdrawing because their tribal organization could not sustain occupation of densely populated Chinese cities.

---

The reunifier who emerged from Five Dynasties chaos was **Zhao Kuangyin** (JOW kwahng-YIN).

Zhao Kuangyin was a general of the **Later Zhou** dynasty, the last of the five northern dynasties. The Later Zhou's founding emperor **Guo Wei** (951–954) and his successor **Chai Rong** (954–959) had been unusually capable rulers — Chai Rong in particular had launched a vigorous reform program and reconquered some lost territory in the south. When Chai Rong died in 959, leaving a six-year-old son on the throne, the Later Zhou court was vulnerable to the same kind of military coup that had ended every previous Five Dynasties regime.

In early 960, Zhao Kuangyin was the senior military commander of the Later Zhou, with control of the imperial guard and most of the field armies. The court sent him north on campaign against a reported (possibly fabricated) Khitan threat. At a place called **Chenqiao Station** (chen-CHYOW), a few days' march out of the capital at **Kaifeng** (KIGH-fung), Zhao Kuangyin's troops "spontaneously" placed an imperial yellow robe on his shoulders and acclaimed him emperor. The gesture was a Chinese political convention dating back centuries — soldiers acclaiming their general as emperor was the standard mechanism for legitimizing a military coup, with the implication that the general had been forced to accept the throne by his loyal followers rather than seizing it on his own initiative. Whether the Chenqiao acclamation was actually spontaneous or was carefully staged by Zhao Kuangyin's circle is one of the standard debates of the early Song historical literature; the consensus view is that it was probably staged but that we cannot rule out a genuine soldier initiative.

Zhao Kuangyin marched on Kaifeng. The Later Zhou court surrendered without a fight — the imperial guard was already on Zhao's side, and the regents had no military force to resist. Zhao Kuangyin proclaimed the **Song dynasty** in February 960, took the reign name **Taizu** ("Grand Progenitor"), and began the long campaign of reunifying China.

The reunification took twenty years. The southern kingdoms — most of them long-stable, well-governed, prosperous regions that had been quietly independent for half a century — submitted one by one through a combination of military pressure and negotiated capitulation. By 979 (under Taizu's brother and successor, **Emperor Taizong of Song** — yet another Taizong, distinct from the Tang one), most of China south of the lost Sixteen Prefectures had been reunified under Song rule. The Sixteen Prefectures themselves remained Khitan; multiple Song military campaigns to recover them in the 980s and afterward all failed.

---

What made the Song founding different from the Tang founding was the conscious philosophical decision Taizu made about the role of the military.

The Tang had been founded by aristocratic military families and had governed through a fusion of aristocratic and military power. The Sui had been the same. The Five Dynasties had been entirely a military affair, with each dynasty founded by a general who had then been overthrown by the next general. Zhao Kuangyin had himself come to the throne through a military acclamation — and he understood, better than almost anyone, how unstable a system that produced was. The fundamental problem of Chinese governance, as he saw it, was that powerful generals always tried to seize the throne, and there was no structural mechanism to prevent them.

His solution was the famous **"exchanging military power for wine cups"** episode of 961. Within a year of taking the throne, Taizu invited his most senior generals — the men who had supported him at Chenqiao and through the early consolidation — to a banquet at the imperial palace. Over wine, he openly told them that their military power made him uneasy, as it had made every emperor since the Tang collapse. He offered them an alternative: surrender their commands voluntarily, accept generous lifetime pensions and large estates, marry into the imperial family, and retire to comfortable lives in the capital. The implication was clear: refuse, and the offer would not stand. They accepted. The senior military command of the Song was reorganized over the following years, with field armies divided into smaller units commanded by a rotating roster of officers who held no permanent regional commands and who served at the pleasure of the central court.

Taizu went further. He systematically subordinated the military to civilian bureaucratic control. The most prestigious position in the Song state was no longer the military commissioner; it was the **chief grand councilor** (the senior civilian official). The civil service examinations became the dominant route to power, with the Song increasing the annual jinshi quota dramatically — by the mid-Song, several hundred jinshi were being awarded each year, far more than the Tang had ever produced. The result was a bureaucracy of unprecedented size, sophistication, and prestige. Generals reported to civilian officials. Provincial military commanders had no permanent regional bases. Frontier defense was organized through a complex system of multiple overlapping commands, designed to make any single general's coup impossible.

The trade-off was real and obvious from the start. The Song had the most stable internal governance of any Chinese dynasty and the longest peaceful reigns; it also had the weakest military relative to its size and wealth. Song armies were large but were organized to prevent internal coups rather than to win battles. Song frontier defense depended on bribes, diplomacy, and elaborate fortification rather than on offensive cavalry capability. The Song would lose the Sixteen Prefectures forever, lose half the country to the Jurchen in 1127, and ultimately lose everything to the Mongols in 1279. But the bureaucratic state Taizu built was so effective that Mongol Yuan and Ming Chinese governments after them would all be variations on the Song model, and a version of it would persist all the way to the end of imperial China in 1912. Taizu had, in effect, traded military power for governance technology, and the technology proved to be more durable than the military.

---

The Song dynasty Taizu founded (the Northern Song, 960–1127) is the subject of Chapter 6. But it is worth pausing here on what the founding represented in long-term Chinese history.

The Sui-Tang reunification of 581–589 had restored a single Chinese state after centuries of fragmentation but had governed through aristocratic military power. The collapse of that model in the 9th and 10th centuries had produced fifty years of warlord chaos. The Song founding restored unity again — but this time through a deliberate constitutional shift toward civilian bureaucratic governance. The shift would shape every subsequent Chinese dynasty.

It is also one of those rare dynastic transitions that did not require a major war. The Song reunification of the south was conducted largely through diplomacy and negotiated submission. The casualty count from the 960–979 reunification campaigns was tiny by the standards of the An Lushan Rebellion, the Mongol conquest, or the Ming-Qing transition. The Song emperors were able to consolidate their bureaucracy, expand the examinations, and begin the cultural and economic flowering of the Northern Song precisely because the founding war had not destroyed the productive base of the country. This is a structural advantage that historians sometimes overlook in evaluating dynasties: the Song got to start from a relatively undamaged starting position.

Cross-civ check: the founding of the Song in 960 was contemporary with the height of the **Abbasid Caliphate's** cultural golden age (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4) and with the rise of the **Holy Roman Empire** under **Otto I** in central Europe (crowned 962). The **Byzantine Empire** under the **Macedonian dynasty** was experiencing its own military and cultural revival under emperors **Nikephoros II Phokas** and **Basil II** (Byzantine Empire Ch 7). In southern India, the **Chola** empire was beginning its great maritime expansion. In Mesoamerica, the **Toltec** civilization was at its peak. The Song founding marks the beginning of what historians sometimes call the "medieval optimum" — a relatively stable, prosperous, intellectually creative period across multiple Eurasian civilizations that lasted until the climate cooling and Mongol disruptions of the 13th century.

---

# Chapter 6 — The Northern Song

The Northern Song dynasty (960–1127) was the most internally peaceful, economically prosperous, technologically innovative, and intellectually productive period in pre-modern Chinese history. It is also the period that most contemporary historians of world economic and technological history return to, repeatedly, when looking for the closest pre-modern analog to a modern industrial society. Some of the technologies the Northern Song produced — paper money, movable type, the navigation compass, weaponized gunpowder, mass-produced cast iron at industrial scale — would not be matched in Europe for between 400 and 700 years.

The dynasty was founded on the Five Dynasties' ruins (Chapter 5) by **Zhao Kuangyin** in 960 and inherited his constitutional commitment to civilian over military rule. This had two large consequences. The first was that the Song could never recover the **Sixteen Prefectures** in the north and would forever be vulnerable to nomadic and semi-nomadic invasion from that direction. The second was that the resources the state did not spend on offensive military conquest were available for everything else — bureaucratic infrastructure, public works, education, scientific research, commerce, the arts. The Song traded conquest for prosperity, and the result was a society visibly more sophisticated than anything else in the world at the time.

---

The Song capital was **Kaifeng** (kigh-FUNG), on the Yellow River in **Henan**.

Kaifeng was a different kind of city from Tang Chang'an (Chapter 3). Chang'an had been laid out on a strict ward grid with internal walls and night curfews — a closed-block city designed for controlled administration. Kaifeng broke from that template entirely. Its streets were open. Shops, restaurants, and entertainment quarters lined the public thoroughfares without internal walls separating them. **Night markets** operated past midnight — a revolutionary departure from the Tang curfew system, and a sign of how thoroughly the Song's commercial economy had outgrown the older imperial-administrative urban model. By 1000 CE, Kaifeng was the most populous city in the world, with over a million residents inside the walls. The Iron Pagoda built in 1049 — a 180-foot, 13-story tower faced in 28 types of brown glazed brick that still stands today — is the most visible Northern Song monument to survive in the city.

We have a remarkable visual record of Kaifeng from a single painting. **Zhang Zeduan** (jahng zeh-DWAHN), a Northern Song court painter, completed a 17-foot handscroll around 1120 called **Along the River During the Qingming Festival** (清明上河图, *Qingming shanghe tu*). The scroll depicts the city's commercial life on the Qingming spring festival: over 800 human figures from every social class, 28 boats of various sizes navigating the canal, 60 animals (donkeys, oxen, horses, camels), 170 trees, the famous Rainbow Bridge (a single-span arched bridge of timber, with details that have allowed modern engineers to reconstruct the Song-era bridge-building technique), shops, teahouses, restaurants, brothels, fortune-tellers, and street performers. It is the single most valuable visual record of Song urban life and is now considered a national treasure of China, held in the Palace Museum in Beijing. Multiple Ming and Qing reinterpretations exist, but the original is the historical document.

---

The political life of the Northern Song was dominated by a half-century of bitter intellectual conflict between two factions — the **reformers** led by **Wang Anshi** and the **conservatives** led by **Sima Guang**. The conflict shaped Song governance from the 1060s to the 1090s and exhausted the state at the worst possible moment.

**Wang Anshi** (wahng AHN-shih, 1021–1086) became chancellor in 1069 under the young **Emperor Shenzong**. Wang's diagnosis of the Song's problems was that the state was financially weak, militarily inadequate, and structurally incapable of meeting the threats from the Khitan Liao to the north and the **Tangut Western Xia** to the northwest. (The Western Xia was a Tibetan-related kingdom that had emerged in the 1030s in modern Ningxia and Gansu, and that would harass the Song's western frontier for two centuries before the Mongols destroyed it in 1227.) His solution was a sweeping reform program known as the **New Policies** (新法, *xin fa*).

The New Policies included: government loans to peasant farmers at below-market rates (the **Green Sprouts** program, intended to break the power of rural moneylenders who charged extortionate harvest-time rates); state trading bureaus to stabilize commodity prices; a **baojia** (BOW-jya) militia system in which households were organized into groups of ten for collective security and military training; reforms to the civil service examinations to test practical policy and administration rather than literary elegance; a state-monopoly tea trade; and government direct production of military equipment. The reforms were aggressive, comprehensive, and explicitly designed to expand state capacity at the expense of established economic interests.

**Sima Guang** (sih-MAH GWAHNG, 1019–1086) led the opposition. He argued that the reforms gave too much power to government bureaucracy, undermined the traditional gentry's social role, generated corruption through the loans-and-monopolies system, and undermined the moral foundations of Confucian governance. Sima Guang and his allies were repeatedly purged, recalled, and purged again as Shenzong wavered in his support for the reforms. The factional warfare reached a peak in the 1080s after Shenzong's death, when an anti-reform regency reversed most of the New Policies, then was reversed again by a pro-reform faction in the 1090s, then partially reversed again in the 1100s. The continuous flip-flopping consumed the dynasty's political energy and produced a political class that hated each other more than it feared the foreign threats on the borders. Su Shi (Chapter 8), one of the era's great writers, was repeatedly exiled by both factions for being too independent.

Sima Guang spent his years out of power on a different project. He compiled the **Zizhi Tongjian** (zih-jih tong-JYEN — "Comprehensive Mirror to Aid in Government"), a 294-volume chronological history of China from 403 BCE to 959 CE — covering 1,362 years of Chinese history. Emperor Shenzong, despite their political differences, personally titled the finished work, framing it as a teaching device: history as a mirror in which rulers could see how their decisions had played out in past dynasties. The Zizhi Tongjian became the second most influential work of Chinese historiography, after Sima Qian's **Records of the Grand Historian** of the Han period (Han Dynasty Ch 4). Every subsequent Chinese emperor was expected to study it. Modern Chinese historians still treat it as a primary reference for the periods it covers.

---

The Northern Song's lasting legacy, however, is technological rather than political. Four innovations in particular reshaped the world.

**Gunpowder** had been recorded in Daoist alchemical texts since the late Tang (Chapter 4), but it took the Song to weaponize it. The **Wujing Zongyao** (woo-JING zong-YOW — "Complete Essentials of Military Classics"), compiled by **Zeng Gongliang** under imperial commission in 1044, contains the earliest known weaponized gunpowder formulas, with specific proportions of saltpeter, sulfur, and charcoal alongside recipes for incendiary bombs, fire arrows, **fire lances** (*huo qiang* — bamboo or paper tubes packed with gunpowder that shot flames and shrapnel at close range, a proto-flamethrower that doubled as a proto-firearm), thunderclap bombs, and signal rockets. By the early 12th century, Song armies were deploying gunpowder weapons on a substantial scale. The Song would never have the offensive military strength to use gunpowder in conquest — that was inherited by the Jurchen and Mongols, who absorbed Song gunpowder technology and used it more aggressively — but the technology that ended the age of castles, transformed European warfare, and reshaped global politics over the next eight centuries originated as a Song military innovation.

**Printing** had emerged in the Tang as woodblock printing (Chapter 4), but the Song took it to industrial scale. Around 1040, a commoner artisan named **Bi Sheng** (bee SHENG) invented **movable type** — small reusable individual character pieces fired in clay, set in a wax-and-resin bed inside an iron frame, used for printing and then redistributed. **Shen Kuo** (shen KWAW), the great Song polymath whose **Dream Pool Essays** of 1088 we will return to in a moment, described the process in detail; without his account, Bi Sheng's invention might be lost to history. Movable type was less practical for Chinese (which has thousands of distinct characters and therefore requires thousands of distinct types) than for alphabetic writing systems with twenty-six letters; for Chinese, woodblock remained the dominant printing technology through the Ming. But the principle was independently rediscovered by Korean metalworkers in the 13th century (using metal type, an improvement on Bi Sheng's clay) and then by **Gutenberg** in Mainz around 1450, three to four centuries later. The combination of printing and the Song commercial economy produced the first true mass-publishing culture in human history: Confucian classics, examination-prep texts, encyclopedias, medical manuals, agricultural treatises, poetry collections, Buddhist sutras, and government documents in print runs of thousands.

**Paper money** is the most consequential single Song innovation by some measures. The story begins in **Sichuan**, where the local economy ran on **iron coins** rather than copper because copper was scarce in the region. Iron coins were so heavy that large transactions required cartloads of money — it reportedly took 1.5 kilograms of iron coins to purchase a pound of salt. Private merchants began issuing **paper certificates of deposit** in the late 10th century to avoid carrying iron coinage. By the 1020s, the system was so widespread that the Song government took it over. In 1024, the government began issuing the **jiaozi** (jyow-ZIH), the world's first state-backed paper currency, with multi-color printing to fight counterfeiting and three-year circulation periods to control the money supply. The jiaozi system was eventually expanded to other regions and to higher-denomination notes called **huizi**. The Song discovered, and the Mongol Yuan dynasty would catastrophically rediscover (Yuan Dynasty), the dangers of overprinting — Song-era inflation from excess currency was a serious recurring problem. But the basic concept of fiat paper money, redeemable in principle for metal coin, is a Song invention that would not appear in Europe for seven centuries (Stockholm's Banco issued the first European paper money in 1661).

The **magnetic compass** was the fourth great innovation. Chinese geomancers had been using magnetic lodestone for **feng shui** divination since the Han period (Han Dynasty Ch 6) — aligning buildings, tombs, and other structures with the Earth's magnetic field. By the Song, the magnetic properties of magnetized iron needles were well understood. **Shen Kuo** in his **Dream Pool Essays** (1088) described floating-needle compasses in detail and noted **magnetic declination** — the slight offset between magnetic north and true geographic north — about four centuries before European scientists made the same observation. (Christopher Columbus rediscovered magnetic declination during his 1492 transatlantic voyage and treated it as a novel observation; Shen Kuo had documented it in 1088.) By the early 12th century, Chinese maritime navigators were using magnetic compasses for ocean voyages, enabling the Song maritime trade revolution that would reach East Africa (Chapter 8). The compass reached Arab navigators by the 12th century and European navigators by the 13th, and combined with improvements in shipbuilding and the astrolabe, made the European Age of Exploration possible.

Shen Kuo himself deserves a longer mention than he usually gets. He was a Song official, military commander, diplomat, hydraulic engineer, and astronomer who, after his political career ended in the factional warfare of the 1080s, retired to a garden retreat called the **Dream Pool** near modern Zhenjiang and compiled his observations into the **Dream Pool Essays** (*Mengxi bitan*). The Essays cover geology (Shen Kuo recognized fossilized seashells in mountain rocks as evidence that mountains had been undersea, four centuries before any European naturalist made the same inference), astronomy (he reformed the imperial calendar and improved the gnomon-and-armillary observatory at Kaifeng), magnetism, optics (camera-obscura demonstrations, observations on the inverted-image projection through a pinhole), pharmacology, military technology (he commanded armies on the Western Xia frontier), and the technological innovations of his contemporaries — including Bi Sheng's movable type and the magnetic compass. His empirical method, his willingness to test claims by direct observation, and his refusal to defer to traditional authorities anticipated the European scientific method by several centuries. He is one of those figures the broader world should know about and largely does not.

---

The Northern Song's economic backbone was something less glamorous than printing and gunpowder but at least as important: **iron and steel**.

By 1078, Song iron production reached approximately 125,000 tons annually. To put that in context: this exceeded England's iron production at the start of the Industrial Revolution in the 18th century by a substantial margin. The Song produced more iron in 1078 than the entire continent of Europe would produce in 1700. Song iron was used for weapons, agricultural tools, the iron coins of the Sichuan economy, construction, and water-control infrastructure. The technology behind the production was equally striking. China had been producing **cast iron** since the 5th century BCE — Europe would not master the technique until the late medieval period — but Song-era production reached genuinely industrial scale. Deforestation in north China had reduced the supply of wood for charcoal-fired blast furnaces, so Song ironworkers switched to **coke** (a refined coal product). Europe would not adopt coke-fired blast furnaces until **Abraham Darby** developed the technique in 1709 — a difference of over six hundred years.

The other major Northern Song economic development was the **Champa rice** introduction. In 1012, **Emperor Zhenzong** ordered the importation of **fast-ripening, drought-resistant rice strains** from **Champa** (the Cham kingdom in central Vietnam). The new strains matured in 60 days rather than 120, and could grow on previously marginal hillside land. Adoption across south China within a few decades enabled **double-cropping** (two harvests per year instead of one) and roughly doubled rice production. The result was a population boom: China's population grew from approximately 60 million in 960 to over 100 million by 1100 — the first society in human history to surpass the 100-million-person threshold. The agricultural surplus enabled urbanization (Kaifeng, Hangzhou, Quanzhou, and other cities all grew to populations the Tang capitals could never have supported), commerce (the surplus rural population moved into commercial production of silk, ceramics, tea, and metal goods), and the cultural and intellectual flowering of the Song period.

The combined picture — paper currency, mass printing, gunpowder weapons, the navigation compass, industrial-scale iron production, double-cropping rice agriculture, the largest urban population in the world — has led many world economic historians to ask why the Song did not spark a full industrial revolution six or seven centuries before Britain. The standard answers all involve some combination of: incomplete labor markets (the Song had abundant cheap labor, reducing the incentive to mechanize), missing institutional structures (no European-style legal framework for joint-stock corporations or modern banking), the catastrophic disruption of the Jurchen and then Mongol conquests (which interrupted the technological-economic trajectory before it could fully consolidate), and the cultural priorities of the scholar-gentry class (which valued classical learning over technical innovation). The debate is alive in current economic history. What is not debated is that the Song achieved what looks, in retrospect, like a proto-industrial economic structure five hundred years before Europe matched it.

---

The Northern Song also had a final emperor whose career encapsulates the dynasty's contradictions: brilliant in art and culture, catastrophic in politics.

**Emperor Huizong** (HWAY-tsong) — born **Zhao Ji** (jow JEE) — became emperor in 1100 and ruled until his abdication in 1126. He was, by general agreement, one of China's greatest artists. He invented a distinctive calligraphic style called **"Slender Gold"** (瘦金体, *shoujin ti*), characterized by extremely thin sharp strokes that look almost like twisted gold wire — still practiced and admired a thousand years later. He painted bird-and-flower studies of remarkable quality, including the famous "**Five-Colored Parakeet**" handscroll now at the Boston Museum of Fine Arts. He compiled the imperial art collection catalogue, which listed over 6,000 paintings, providing one of the most important pre-modern records of Chinese painting. He was a passionate patron of court music, ceramics (the imperial kilns under his patronage produced the most refined Ru ware and Guan ware ceramics in Song history), and Daoist religion.

He was simultaneously a calamity as a ruler. He delegated governance to corrupt favorites — most notoriously **Cai Jing** (TSIGH JING), who used the chancellor position to enrich himself and his network at the state's expense. He poured money into Daoist building projects and an enormous imperial garden complex (the **Genyue**, "Heightened Mountain") in Kaifeng that drained the treasury. He neglected the military at a moment when the dynasty needed military strength most. And in 1115, he made the strategic decision that destroyed the dynasty: he allied with the rising **Jurchen** people against the Khitan Liao.

The Jurchen were a Tungusic-speaking forest people from **Manchuria** (the same region the **Manchu** would later emerge from to found the Qing dynasty in the 17th century). Under their leader **Aguda**, they had revolted against Khitan Liao overlordship in 1114 and established their own **Jin** dynasty in 1115. Huizong's calculation was that joining the Jurchen against the Liao would allow the Song to recover the Sixteen Prefectures lost in 938 (Chapter 5). The strategic logic was sound; the execution was not. The Song military failed to deliver on its promised contributions to the joint campaign. The Jurchen conquered the Liao essentially on their own (the last Khitan emperor was captured in 1125), and in the process they took an accurate measure of Song military weakness. They did not stop. In 1125, Jurchen armies turned south against their Song allies. In 1126 they besieged Kaifeng. In early 1127 they captured the city, deposed Huizong (who had abdicated to his son Emperor Qinzong in panicked hopes of avoiding personal capture), and took both former emperors, the imperial court, the imperial library, the imperial treasures, and roughly 3,000 members of the imperial family and entourage north into captivity.

This is the **Jingkang Incident** of 1127. It is the subject of Chapter 7.

Cross-civ check: the Northern Song was contemporary with the **Crusader** era in the eastern Mediterranean (the First Crusade in 1099, the establishment of the Crusader states), the rise of the **Seljuk Turkic** empires across the Middle East, the consolidation of the **Holy Roman Empire** under the Salian and Hohenstaufen dynasties in central Europe, and the early flowering of medieval European intellectual life around the universities at Bologna, Paris, and Oxford. The **Khmer Empire** at **Angkor** in Southeast Asia was building **Angkor Wat** in the early 12th century, contemporary with the construction of the Song Iron Pagoda and the Northern Song's most active building period. In Japan, the **Heian** court culture was at its late peak, with **Murasaki Shikibu** writing the **Tale of Genji** around 1010 (Ancient Japan Ch 8). The Song traded with all of these regions through its maritime network and its silk-road connections; the world the Song operated in was already significantly more interconnected than any earlier period.

# Chapter 7 — The Jurchen Catastrophe and the Southern Song

The **Jingkang Incident** of 1127 — named after the reign-period during which it happened — is the single most traumatic event in pre-modern Chinese national consciousness, comparable in cultural weight to the sack of Constantinople in 1204 in the Byzantine memory or the Norman conquest of 1066 in the English. It was the moment when the world's most sophisticated civilization was decapitated and humiliated by a forest people from Manchuria that the Song had considered uncouth allies eighteen months earlier.

The Jurchen siege of **Kaifeng** began in late 1126 and lasted into early 1127. Emperor **Qinzong** (CHIN-tsong) — Huizong's son, who had been hastily elevated to the throne in late 1125 when his father abdicated to escape personal blame — attempted the only defensive strategy still available to a militarily collapsing state: he tried to buy the Jurchen off. The Jurchen demands escalated week by week — first an indemnity, then territorial concessions, then the surrender of imperial princesses, then the personal appearance of the emperor at the Jurchen camp. Qinzong eventually had to comply with all of it. In early 1127, the Jurchen entered Kaifeng, looted the imperial palace and treasury, and rounded up the imperial family, court officials, palace women, scholars, and skilled artisans. Both former emperors — Huizong (still living) and Qinzong — along with nearly 3,000 members of the imperial entourage were marched north into captivity.

Most of them never came back. Huizong died in northern captivity in 1135, in a cold border garrison far from the Yangtze valley he had spent his life painting and patronizing. His son Qinzong survived in captivity until 1161, treated with the calculated humiliation that the Jurchen reserved for high-status hostages — he was at one point made to dress as a Khitan slave and parade publicly in front of the Jurchen court as a demonstration of conquest. The captured imperial princesses, palace women, and noblewomen suffered a fate the Chinese sources record with the uncomfortable mix of moralizing horror and prurient interest characteristic of male chroniclers writing about female suffering: many were given to Jurchen officers as concubines, slaves, or lower-ranking palace servants. The contemporary chronicle accounts of these abductions are some of the most painful documents in Chinese historical literature.

What survived was a single Song prince, **Zhao Gou** (jow GO), Huizong's ninth son, who had been outside Kaifeng on a diplomatic mission when the city fell. He fled south. In May 1127, in the city of **Yingtian** (modern Shangqiu in Henan), the surviving Song officials acclaimed him emperor. He took the reign name **Gaozong** (GOW-tsong, yet another Gaozong distinct from the Tang one) and is known in the historical literature as **Emperor Gaozong of Song**. He continued retreating south, harassed by Jurchen pursuit. By 1138, his itinerant court had settled at **Hangzhou** on the **Qiantang River** estuary, which would serve as the **Southern Song** capital for the next 142 years. The official designation was always **Lin'an** (lin-AHN — "Temporarily at Peace"), the Song court's polite fiction that the move south was provisional and the recovery of Kaifeng was always just a few campaigns away. It never was.

---

The Southern Song of Hangzhou was a strange political entity. It controlled only the wealthier southern half of China — roughly the Yangtze valley and points south — but this half was the half that had been growing fastest in population, agricultural production, and commercial vigor for two centuries. The Southern Song's per-capita income was probably higher than the Northern Song's had been; its tax revenues, despite the territorial loss, were comparable to the Northern Song's at peak; its maritime trade was larger; its urban culture was as sophisticated as anything Kaifeng had produced.

**Hangzhou** itself became, by some estimates, the largest city in the world during the Southern Song. It sat at the southern end of the Grand Canal, on the **West Lake**, with the bay of the Qiantang River producing one of the world's most spectacular tidal bores (a wall of incoming seawater up to thirty feet high that surges up the river estuary at certain phases of the moon — a tourist attraction even in the Song period, when poems and paintings recorded it). The city's population at peak in the 13th century is estimated at over a million, with another million in surrounding districts. **Marco Polo**, visiting Hangzhou in the 1270s under Mongol rule (a few decades after the Southern Song fell), called it "the finest and noblest city in the world" and described twelve thousand bridges, ten massive markets, public bathhouses on every block, and crowds of foreign merchants from Persia, India, and the Arab world. The figure of twelve thousand bridges is probably exaggerated; the rest is roughly accurate. Hangzhou's West Lake became one of the most painted, poemed, and culturally celebrated landscapes in Chinese tradition; the **Su Causeway** crossing it was built by **Su Shi** during one of his governorships (Su Shi appears properly in Chapter 8).

The Southern Song court at Hangzhou was politically traumatized. The collective memory of the Jingkang Incident sat at the center of every political debate — to fight the Jurchen and try to recover the north, or to negotiate peace and consolidate the south. The two positions divided the court for generations. The most famous casualty of the debate was the general **Yue Fei**.

---

**Yue Fei** (yueh FAY, 1103–1142) is the most revered patriotic hero in Chinese history. His story is almost too neat for history, and almost certainly has been polished by centuries of patriotic retelling, but the bones of it are well-attested.

He was born to a peasant family in **Tangyin** in modern Henan in 1103, just before the Jurchen catastrophe. His mother famously tattooed four characters on his back: **精忠报国** (*jing zhong bao guo* — "Serve the country with utmost loyalty"). The episode is probably apocryphal in detail but captures the texture of Yue Fei's eventual reputation. He entered the Song military in his teens and rose through the chaotic warfare of the 1120s and 1130s by sheer ability — he was a genuinely brilliant cavalry commander whose troops were known for both tactical effectiveness and unusual discipline (Yue Fei's army was famous for refusing to loot the Chinese villages they passed through, which made it dramatically more popular with the Chinese peasantry than most Southern Song armies).

By the late 1130s, Yue Fei was leading the most successful Southern Song offensives against the Jurchen Jin. His 1140 campaign recovered substantial territory in central China and was preparing to recapture **Kaifeng** itself. He famously addressed his officers with the line that has been quoted ever since: "Restore our rivers and mountains" (huan wo heshan — *huan wo hé shān*, "give us back our land"). The Jin armies were retreating before him.

This is where the politics destroyed everything.

**Qin Hui** (CHIN HWAY) was the Southern Song chancellor, a man of complicated personal history (he had been captured by the Jurchen during the Jingkang Incident and had been released to return south, leading to suspicions for the rest of his life that he was secretly working for the Jin). Qin Hui's faction at court — backed by Emperor Gaozong, who had his own reasons to fear a successful general who might recover the north and produce inconvenient questions about whose throne the recovered emperor ought to occupy — was committed to a negotiated peace with the Jin. A successful Yue Fei was a political problem.

In late 1141, Qin Hui issued twelve gold-embossed imperial decrees in a single day recalling Yue Fei from the front. Yue Fei, after some hesitation, obeyed. On returning to the capital, he was arrested on fabricated charges of plotting rebellion. He was tortured for a confession and refused to confess. His chief subordinate **Zhang Xian** was tortured to death with him. The legal proceedings were transparently corrupt — even contemporary officials objected — but Qin Hui pressed forward. The official **Han Shizhong**, another senior general, asked Qin Hui what evidence existed for the charges. Qin Hui's reply, recorded in the chronicles, became one of the most quoted phrases in Chinese political vocabulary: **"莫须有"** (*mo xu you*, "perhaps it exists" or "it's something maybe"). Yue Fei was executed in early 1142, aged 39. The peace treaty with the Jin (the **Treaty of Shaoxing**) was signed shortly afterward, ceding all the territory north of the **Huai River** and obligating the Song to pay annual tribute in silk and silver.

Yue Fei was posthumously rehabilitated in 1162, two decades after his execution, when Gaozong abdicated and his successor needed a heroic symbol for a renewed anti-Jin policy. Yue Fei's tomb was rebuilt at the foot of **Yuelai Mountain** beside West Lake in Hangzhou. Iron statues of Qin Hui and his wife **Lady Wang** — and two other officials who had collaborated in the execution — were placed in front of the tomb in the kneeling posture of contrition, where they have remained for nearly nine hundred years. Visitors to the tomb traditionally spit on the iron statues and beat them with sticks; the statues have been replaced multiple times because visitors damaged them. Modern signs ask visitors to express their contempt verbally rather than physically, but the ritual continues. Yue Fei's name is the standard Chinese metaphor for patriotic loyalty betrayed by domestic political corruption, and Qin Hui's name is the standard Chinese metaphor for the corrupt minister who sells out a hero. The closest English-language analog might be **Benedict Arnold** in American memory, but Yue Fei's place in Chinese culture is much more central than Arnold's in American culture — Yue Fei is taught in Chinese schools, dramatized in operas, and invoked in Chinese political rhetoric whenever a public figure wants to accuse an opponent of betraying the national interest.

---

The Southern Song that had executed Yue Fei was militarily limited but technologically and economically inventive. Its defining military innovation was the **Song navy**.

Pre-modern Chinese dynasties had typically fielded river fleets when needed but had never maintained a permanent standing navy. The Southern Song needed one. The Yangtze River was the primary defensive barrier between the Jurchen north and the Song south, and Jurchen cavalry could not effectively cross it without a fleet. The Song's strategic priority was therefore controlling the Yangtze and its tributaries.

The **Song navy** that emerged from this strategic imperative was unlike anything that had existed before in Chinese history. Its warships included **paddle-wheel vessels** powered by foot-operated treadmills inside the hull — sailors walking treadmill drums that turned external paddle wheels, providing maneuverable propulsion against river currents that sail-powered ships could not match. (Paddle-wheel propulsion would not be reinvented in Europe until the early 19th century, when steam-powered paddle wheelers transformed Mississippi and trans-Atlantic shipping; the Song versions were human-powered, but the engineering principle was identical.) Larger Song warships could carry over 1,000 soldiers and deploy gunpowder weapons including **thunderclap bombs**, **fire arrows**, and the early **proto-cannons** that the Song military engineers were experimenting with throughout the 12th and 13th centuries. The Song navy held the Yangtze line for over a century against repeated Jin attempts at large-scale crossing. The Mongol conquest of the Southern Song in the 1270s would require, among other things, the construction of a Mongol naval force capable of matching the Song fleet on its own water — a process that took years.

The Southern Song's sustained survival despite its territorial limits depended on a combination of factors: the natural defensive line of the Yangtze, the technological advantages of gunpowder weaponry and naval engineering, the agricultural and commercial wealth of the south, and the sheer demographic weight of a population that probably exceeded the Jurchen Jin's by a factor of three or four to one. The dynasty would last until 1279, longer than the Northern Song had survived, and would produce some of the most sophisticated cultural achievements in Chinese history — the subject of Chapter 8.

Cross-civ check: the Jurchen catastrophe of 1127 was contemporary with the **First Crusade**'s consolidation of the Crusader States in the Levant, the Norman expansion in Sicily and southern Italy, and the rise of the **Almoravid** and **Almohad** Muslim empires in North Africa and Spain. In northern Europe, the **Holy Roman Empire** was wracked by the **Investiture Controversy** between popes and emperors. The **Khmer Empire** at Angkor was approaching its peak. In Japan, the **Heian** court was in its long decline; the **Genpei War** of 1180–1185 would shortly establish the **Kamakura Shogunate** (Ancient Japan transitions to the shogunate-era TLs after this point). The Jin-Song conflict continued for over a century in parallel with these developments, with the Song effectively ceding the role of Eurasia's most powerful state and the Jin filling part of that role in northern Asia.

# Chapter 8 — Song Society

This chapter steps out of chronological narrative to look at four developments that crystallized during the Song and that defined Chinese society for centuries afterward: **Neo-Confucianism**, the printing-and-examination culture, the **Jingdezhen porcelain** trade, and the maritime trade revolution that connected Song China to the Indian Ocean world. Some of these emerged in the Northern Song; some emerged or matured in the Southern Song; all of them were Song innovations that outlived the dynasty and shaped what came after.

---

**Neo-Confucianism** is the most important of them, and the hardest to summarize.

The basic problem the movement set out to address was that Confucianism — the philosophical tradition founded by **Confucius** in the 5th century BCE (Zhou Dynasty Ch 7), elaborated by **Mencius** and **Xunzi** in the centuries afterward, and made the official state ideology under the **Han** in the 2nd century BCE (Han Dynasty Ch 4) — had lost intellectual ground to **Buddhism** during the Six Dynasties and Tang centuries. Buddhism offered things that classical Confucianism did not: a sophisticated metaphysical framework explaining the nature of reality, a rigorous theory of consciousness and mind, a coherent account of suffering and salvation, monastic institutions for serious philosophical study, and a path to personal spiritual transformation that did not depend on political conditions. Confucian scholars who wanted to take metaphysics or epistemology or psychology seriously had been gravitating toward Buddhist categories for centuries. The Tang anti-Buddhist persecution of 845 (Chapter 4) had reduced Buddhism's institutional power but had not really refuted its intellectual claims.

The Northern Song saw a series of philosophers begin to address the problem by building a Confucian metaphysics that could compete with Buddhism on its own intellectual ground. **Zhou Dunyi** (joh DUN-ee, 1017–1073) wrote a short but enormously influential treatise called **"Explanation of the Diagram of the Supreme Polarity"** (Taijitu shuo), arguing that the universe emerged through a series of cosmic transformations from an original undifferentiated principle. The brothers **Cheng Hao** (CHENG HOW, 1032–1085) and **Cheng Yi** (CHENG EE, 1033–1107) developed the categories of **li** (LEE, "principle" — the underlying rational pattern of reality) and **qi** (CHEE, "material force" — the dynamic substance through which principle is realized). **Zhang Zai** (jahng TSIGH, 1020–1077) elaborated the qi side of this metaphysics with the famous "Western Inscription" arguing that all humans share a common cosmic substance and therefore stand in a relationship of metaphysical kinship.

The synthesizer who pulled all of this into a coherent system was **Zhu Xi** (joo SHEE, 1130–1200), the most influential Chinese philosopher after Confucius himself. Zhu Xi's career spanned the Southern Song. He was educated in the Cheng-brothers tradition, served as a regional official, retired periodically to teach at private academies, and produced a vast body of philosophical commentary, education theory, and ritual prescription. His central philosophical position is that all things — every rock, every plant, every animal, every human, every emotion, every relationship — contain both **li** (principle: what makes a thing what it is, what its proper nature and function are) and **qi** (material force: the substance and energy through which the principle is actually realized). Moral cultivation, in Zhu Xi's account, consists in **gewu** (geh-WOO, "investigating things") — examining the principles inherent in particular cases until one comes to understand the universal principles underlying them. The good life is the life lived in accordance with the principles that one has, through patient investigation and reflection, come to understand.

Zhu Xi also designated a specific curriculum as the canonical Confucian core. The **Four Books** (Sishu) — the **Analects** of Confucius, the **Mencius**, and two short chapters extracted from the **Book of Rites** called the **Great Learning** and the **Doctrine of the Mean** — became the foundational texts of Neo-Confucian education, prioritized over the older **Five Classics** (which Han-era Confucians had treated as the canonical body). Zhu Xi wrote definitive commentaries on the Four Books that became required reading. He also developed an account of personal moral cultivation that emphasized **xiu shen** (cultivating the self) through careful study, ritual practice, and reflective examination of one's own motives.

The institutional consequences of all this were enormous. Starting in 1313 (under the Mongol Yuan dynasty), Zhu Xi's commentaries on the Four Books were designated as the official curriculum for the Chinese **civil service examinations**. They held that position until the abolition of the examination system in 1905 — a continuous run of 592 years during which every Chinese government official was selected on the basis of his mastery of Zhu Xi's interpretation of Confucianism. The system was adopted by **Korea** (in modified form, with the Joseon dynasty's exams being explicitly Neo-Confucian after 1392), **Japan** (where Tokugawa-era Neo-Confucianism became the official ideology of the shogunate), and **Vietnam**. From the 14th century to the 20th, Zhu Xi's account of the relationship between principle, material force, and moral cultivation was the dominant intellectual framework across East Asia. Arguments about whether Zhu Xi was right or wrong continued, of course — particularly the **Wang Yangming** school in Ming China developed an alternative Neo-Confucianism that emphasized intuition over investigation — but the basic Neo-Confucian framework was the assumed background of all subsequent East Asian intellectual life.

The contemporary Islamic Republic of Iran, which has structured its religious-political authority around clerical hierarchies that the Safavid dynasty established (Safavid Persia Ch 5), is a good comparison case for what happens when a particular religious-philosophical synthesis becomes the state ideology and institutional foundation of an enduring civilization. Neo-Confucianism shaped East Asian governance, family structure, education, and gender norms with comparable depth, for comparable durations.

The most consequential gender-related Neo-Confucian innovation was the increasing rigidification of women's roles. The earlier Tang period had been notably less restrictive of elite women than later Chinese dynasties — Tang noblewomen rode horses, played polo, wore Central Asian fashions, and operated more visibly in public life than would later be the case. Wu Zetian's reign (Chapter 2), however controversial, was at least possible. The Song shift toward Neo-Confucian moral philosophy went along with — though not necessarily as a straightforward cause of — a tightening of the cultural norms around female seclusion, chastity, and economic dependence. The most visible and historically painful expression of this shift was **foot-binding**, which appears to have begun as a fashion among Song court dancers in the 10th or 11th century and spread gradually through elite and then non-elite female populations over the following centuries. Foot-binding involved tightly wrapping young girls' feet (typically starting at ages 4–7) so that the toes folded under the sole and the arch was forced upward, producing a permanently deformed foot a few inches long. The practice became progressively more widespread over the Song, Yuan, and Ming periods and was nearly universal among Han Chinese women by the late Qing. It would not be effectively suppressed until the early 20th century. Foot-binding's exact origins, social meanings, and reasons for spread are still debated by historians; the textual record is much less specific than later moralizing makes it sound. What is clear is that the practice originated in the Song, that it spread alongside the Neo-Confucian tightening of female roles, and that it became one of the most physically destructive cultural practices ever institutionalized at scale — affecting tens of millions of women over nearly a thousand years.

---

The **printing-and-examination culture** of the Song was the second great structural innovation that shaped what came after.

The Tang had introduced the examination system (Chapter 2); the Song massively expanded it. By the mid-Northern Song, several hundred jinshi degrees were being awarded each year, against the Tang's typical few dozen. Examination-prep schools, study societies, lineage academies, and private tutors proliferated across China to serve the demand. Woodblock printing (Chapter 4) made standardized examination texts cheap and widely available — Confucian classics, anthologies of model essays, study guides, the works of admired scholars — so that ambitious families in any prefecture could prepare their sons for the exams without the resources that earlier aristocratic families had needed.

The result was an unprecedented expansion of the literate scholar class in China. Estimates of male literacy in Northern Song urban areas run as high as 30–40% — a figure that would not be matched in Europe until the late 17th century and that, in some interpretations, was not matched anywhere else in the pre-modern world. The Song produced a culture in which books were cheap, examinations were a real path to power for talented men of moderate means, classical literature was widely read, and intellectual debate was conducted in print in something like the volume that European intellectual debate would reach during the Enlightenment. This is one of the major reasons world economic and cultural historians see the Song as a near-modern society.

It is worth pausing on the gender dimension here as well. The expansion of literacy was overwhelmingly male — the examination system was closed to women, and the cultural infrastructure that supported male literary culture did not have a female equivalent. There were exceptions: the Southern Song poet **Li Qingzhao** (lee ching-JOW, 1084–c. 1155) is generally considered the greatest female poet in Chinese history. Born to a literary family and married to the antiquarian scholar **Zhao Mingcheng**, she shared in his collecting of inscriptions, paintings, and rare books. Their comfortable life was destroyed by the Jurchen catastrophe of 1127 — Zhao Mingcheng died in 1129, the collection was lost in the southern flight, and Li Qingzhao spent the rest of her life as a refugee, occasionally writing poems on her displacement that are among the most directly devastating works in Chinese literature. Her theory of the **ci** (TSIH) verse form — a kind of lyric poetry set to musical tunes with irregular line lengths — shaped the genre for centuries afterward. Li Qingzhao is the major exception that proves the Song rule: she was an extraordinary female poet in a literary culture that had effectively no infrastructure for producing female poets, and her achievement says more about her individual talent than about any gender-inclusive Song literary system.

The other great Song literary figure of the period was **Su Shi** (SOO SHIH, 1037–1101), also known by his pen-name **Su Dongpo** (soo dong-PWAW). He passed the jinshi exam at age twenty with distinction, served in various official positions throughout the Northern Song, and was repeatedly exiled by both the reform and conservative factions for being too independent. Su Shi excelled simultaneously in **ci poetry**, **prose essays**, **calligraphy** (his handwritten manuscript of the prose poem **"Cold Food Observance"** is considered one of the greatest works of Chinese calligraphy ever produced), **painting** (he is one of the founders of the **literati painting** tradition that emphasized personal expression over technical skill), and **gastronomy** (Su Shi is credited with inventing **Dongpo pork** — a slow-braised pork-belly dish still on Chinese restaurant menus today). His **"Red Cliff Rhapsodies"** — two short prose-poems written during one of his exiles, meditating on the historical battle of Red Cliffs (Six Dynasties Ch 1) and on the smallness of human life against the eternity of the river and the moon — are among the most celebrated pieces of Chinese literature. Su Shi embodied the Song ideal of the cultivated gentleman-official: poetry, prose, calligraphy, painting, and political administration all integrated into a single life. He also built the **Su Causeway** across **West Lake** during one of his governorships of Hangzhou, which still bears his name and is still walked by tourists nine centuries later.

---

The third defining Song development was the **porcelain trade** centered on **Jingdezhen** (jing-deh-JEN).

Chinese ceramics had been advancing for millennia, but the Song combination of refined kilns, high-quality kaolin clay, and large-scale commercial demand produced the first true global-scale Chinese porcelain industry. **Jingdezhen**, in northeastern Jiangxi province, sat on enormous deposits of the white kaolin clay that produces the most refined porcelain when fired to high temperatures. In 1004, the Song court designated Jingdezhen as the official imperial porcelain supplier — the beginning of a thousand-year run during which Jingdezhen would be the global capital of porcelain production, lasting through the Ming and Qing dynasties and continuing as a porcelain town today.

Song porcelain came in many varieties. The most famous categories were the **Five Great Kilns** (wu da yao): **Ru ware** (small-scale imperial production with a distinctive sky-blue celadon glaze, prized above almost all other Song ceramics), **Guan ware** ("official ware," produced for the imperial court), **Ge ware** (with deliberate cracking patterns in the glaze), **Ding ware** (white porcelain with delicate molded or carved decoration), and **Jun ware** (with bold copper-red and blue color patches achieved through controlled firing variation). Jingdezhen specialized in **qingbai** ("blue-white") ware, a transparent, slightly bluish glaze on white porcelain. **Longquan** kilns produced spectacular jade-green **celadon** that became one of the most valued export ceramics across Eurasia.

The export trade was enormous. Song porcelain has been excavated from sites across Southeast Asia, India, the Persian Gulf, the Red Sea, and the East African coast — the same archaeological pattern that the Tang **Belitung shipwreck** of c. 830 had foreshadowed (Chapter 3). The **Quanzhou** shipwreck of c. 1271 (a Southern Song ocean junk that sank near the southern coastline) carried tens of thousands of pieces of porcelain among its other cargo. Major shipwrecks recovered in modern decades from waters off Sinan in Korea and the Philippines have all carried Song porcelain in commercial quantities. Chinese porcelain was so dominant in international trade by the late Song that European, Arabic, and Persian languages all eventually adopted Chinese names for the major ceramic categories, and the English word "china" for the material itself was the standard term in European usage by the 17th century.

---

The fourth Song innovation was the **maritime trade revolution**.

The overland Silk Road that had peaked under the Tang (Chapter 3) had been increasingly closed to Song China by the Tangut Western Xia in the northwest and by the Khitan Liao and then Jurchen Jin in the north. The Song response was to pivot to the sea. By the 12th and 13th centuries, Song maritime trade in the South China Sea, Indian Ocean, and beyond was operating at a scale that had never existed before in Eurasian history.

The technical infrastructure was impressive. Song **junks** — multi-masted ocean-going ships with watertight bulkhead compartments (an innovation that would not appear in European shipbuilding until the 19th century), stern-mounted rudders, and the magnetic compass for navigation — could carry hundreds of tons of cargo across thousands of miles of open water. The largest Song junks reportedly approached 100 feet in length, with crews of several hundred. The compass made oceanic navigation reliable in conditions where coastal-only navigation had previously been required.

The trade routes ran from Song ports — primarily **Quanzhou** (chwan-JOH), **Guangzhou**, and **Mingzhou** (modern Ningbo) — through the **South China Sea** to **Java** and **Sumatra**, then through the **Strait of Malacca** to the Indian Ocean. From there the routes split: northwest to **India** and the **Persian Gulf**; west to the **Red Sea** and indirectly to **Egypt**; further west across the Arabian Sea to **East Africa**, where Song porcelain has been excavated at coastal sites in modern Kenya, Tanzania, and Mozambique. Song silver coins have been found as far west as the **Swahili coast** trading cities. The Indian Ocean trade network of the 12th and 13th centuries was already the most globally integrated maritime economy ever to have existed up to that point, with Song porcelain, silk, and tea exchanged for Southeast Asian spices, Indian cottons, Persian glass, Arab pearls and aromatics, and African ivory and gold.

**Quanzhou**, the southeastern port that Arab merchants knew as **Zayton**, was the largest of the Song's international ports. By the late 13th century, it was arguably the busiest port in the world. Its harbor hosted ships from across the Indian Ocean. Its foreign communities had their own quarters — Arab, Persian, Indian, Tamil, Southeast Asian — with their own mosques, Hindu temples (one Tamil-style Hindu temple's column fragments have been excavated at Quanzhou), and Nestorian Christian churches. **Marco Polo**, visiting in the 1290s, called it one of the world's two greatest ports, alongside Alexandria. UNESCO designated Quanzhou a World Heritage Site in 2021 in recognition of its importance as a node in the medieval Indian Ocean trade network.

The Song maritime tax revenues alone — the customs duties charged at the major ports — exceeded the entire annual income of some European medieval kingdoms. The economic gravity of the Indian Ocean trading world, in the 12th and 13th centuries, sat squarely on the Song coast. This is the trade network that the Mongols would inherit when they conquered the Song; the Mongol Yuan dynasty that followed would maintain and extend the Song maritime infrastructure and would briefly extend Chinese influence to **Cambodia**, **Java**, and as far as **Madagascar** in their attempted naval invasions of the late 13th century.

Cross-civ check: the Song maritime trade revolution was contemporary with the rise of the Italian maritime republics (**Venice**, **Genoa**, **Pisa**, **Amalfi**) in the western Mediterranean, the **Hanseatic League** in northern Europe, and the great **Indian Ocean** trading networks operated by Persian, Arab, and Gujarati merchants. The 12th and 13th centuries were a moment of unusually intense long-distance trade across the Eurasian world, and the Song was the eastern anchor of the system. Goods moving from Song Quanzhou to **Alexandria** could be relayed onward to Venice and Genoa, which in turn traded with the Hanseatic ports of northern Europe. The medieval Eurasian commercial world was already significantly globalized before the Mongol conquests reorganized it (Mongol Empire Ch 6) and well before the European Age of Exploration extended it across the Atlantic.

# Chapter 9 — The Mongol Conquest of the Song

The **Mongol Empire** (Mongol Empire Ch 1) that **Genghis Khan** had founded in 1206 had absorbed China's northern neighbors over the course of the 13th century with terrifying efficiency. The **Tangut Western Xia** was destroyed in 1227, in the campaign during which Genghis Khan himself died. The **Jurchen Jin** dynasty was destroyed in 1234, after a generation of Mongol-Jin warfare that had left northern China devastated (the Jurchen had themselves devastated northern China after the Jingkang Incident; the Mongol conquest then completed what had been a multi-generational catastrophe for the population of the north Chinese plain). The **Goryeo** kingdom in Korea (Ancient Korea Ch 7) submitted as a Mongol vassal in 1259. The **Dali** kingdom in Yunnan was conquered in 1253. The Mongol territorial sweep across Eurasia was, by the mid-13th century, unprecedented — from Korea to Hungary, the Mongol khanates ruled or dominated the largest contiguous land empire that had ever existed.

The Southern Song was the holdout. The dynasty had survived in its Yangtze-valley retreat for over a century after the Jurchen catastrophe, defended by the natural barrier of the Yangtze, the technological advantages of gunpowder weapons and the Song navy, and the sheer demographic-economic weight of the wealthier southern half of China. The Mongols had been conducting limited campaigns against the Song since the 1230s, but the systematic conquest required focused attention from a Mongol Khan who saw it as his life's project.

That Khan was **Kublai** (KOO-buh-lie, in the standard English transliteration), a grandson of Genghis Khan who had been the senior Mongol commander in north China for two decades. After his elder brother **Möngke** died in 1259 during a campaign against the Song in Sichuan, Kublai fought a four-year civil war against his younger brother **Ariq Böke** for the position of **Great Khan** of the Mongol Empire. Kublai won by 1264 but the cost was permanent — the Mongol Empire effectively split into four constituent khanates after this succession struggle, with Kublai's own domain centered on China and Mongolia, and with the western khanates (the **Golden Horde**, the **Chagatai Khanate**, and the **Ilkhanate** in Persia) operating as effectively independent powers. Kublai responded to the political fragmentation of the larger Mongol Empire by doubling down on the project that mattered most to his personal legitimacy: the conquest of southern China and the establishment of a unified Mongol-ruled Chinese empire.

In 1271, Kublai formally proclaimed the **Yuan dynasty** (元, *yuan* — meaning "primordial" or "origin"). The proclamation was a deliberate Sinification of the Mongol position — Kublai was claiming the Chinese imperial mandate, presenting himself as a legitimate Chinese emperor in the established dynastic succession rather than as a Mongol khan governing Chinese territory. The launching of the final Song campaigns followed almost immediately.

---

The decisive campaign of the Mongol-Song war was the six-year **siege of Xiangyang** (shyahng-YANG), 1267–1273.

**Xiangyang** was a heavily fortified Song city on the **Han River** (a major tributary of the Yangtze), commanding the corridor through which any Mongol force coming south would have to pass to reach the Yangtze valley. The Song garrison commander **Lü Wenhuan** (LOO wen-HWAHN) defended Xiangyang for six years against repeated Mongol assault. The Mongols had perfected most of the conventional siege techniques — encirclement, starvation, mining, conventional bombardment — but Xiangyang's walls were exceptional, and Mongol assaults on the walls had repeatedly failed.

The Mongol solution was to import **counterweight trebuchets** from the Middle East. Two Muslim engineers — **Ismail** and **Ala al-Din**, sent by the **Ilkhanate** of Persia (the Mongol successor state in Iran, ruled by Kublai's relative Hulagu Khan) — built large trebuchets capable of hurling stones of approximately 220 pounds (100 kg) at the city walls from the besiegers' positions. These were the **counterweight trebuchet** designs that had been developed in the Mediterranean Crusader-states context in the 12th and early 13th centuries — a Eurasian technology transfer running from the Crusader-Islamic interface through the Mongol communications network all the way to the Yangtze. The Chinese sources called them **huihui pao** ("Muslim machines"). The trebuchets broke through Xiangyang's walls in early 1273. The garrison surrendered. The Yangtze defensive line was broken.

After Xiangyang's fall, the Mongol advance accelerated. Kublai sent his Mongol-Chinese commander **Bayan** (BIGH-yahn), one of the most capable generals of the era, with a large army down the Yangtze valley. Song defenses crumbled. The capital **Hangzhou** surrendered in early 1276 under the regent dowager empress, who handed over the imperial seals along with the six-year-old Emperor **Gong**. The boy emperor and most of the Hangzhou court were sent to Beijing as captives — the Hangzhou capitulation paralleled, in dynastic-trauma terms, the Jingkang Incident a century and a half earlier, except that this time the conquerors were the Mongols rather than the Jurchen.

The Song dynasty was not yet quite finished, however. Loyalist officials had escaped from Hangzhou with two of the boy emperor's younger half-brothers and with the remnants of the Song navy. They proclaimed one of the brothers as Emperor **Duanzong** in southern China and continued resisting. Duanzong died in 1278, possibly of injuries from an accident at sea or possibly of disease. The loyalists then proclaimed his even younger half-brother — a six-year-old named **Zhao Bing** — as Emperor **Bing** of Song. They were now operating as a coastal-fortress and naval-resistance force in modern Guangdong, with the Mongol fleet relentlessly closing in.

---

The end came on March 19, 1279, at **Yamen** (yah-MEN), in the bay of the **Pearl River** estuary in modern Guangdong.

The Song fleet — by now reduced to about a thousand ships, with the boy emperor and the loyalist court aboard the largest of them — had taken refuge in the bay at Yamen. The Mongol fleet, commanded by **Zhang Hongfan** (a Chinese commander in Mongol service), trapped the Song fleet in the bay. The battle that followed lasted hours and was not really a contest — the Song ships were tactically pinned, the Mongol ships were tactically free, and the Song's gunpowder weapons could not change the outcome.

When defeat became certain, the Song minister **Lu Xiufu** (LOO shyoo-FOO) carried the eight-year-old Emperor Bing to the deck of the imperial flagship. He told the boy that they could not allow themselves to be captured. He bound the child to his back with a sash. They jumped together into the South China Sea. Both drowned.

Other loyalists followed. The chronicle accounts say more than 100,000 Song officials, soldiers, palace women, and civilian followers either jumped into the sea after Lu Xiufu and Emperor Bing or refused capture and fought to the death on their burning ships. The bodies were said to wash ashore for days afterward. Local fishermen reportedly recognized the imperial robes on a small body that washed ashore some days later, identified it as Emperor Bing, and secretly buried him. A small temple was eventually built at the burial site near modern Hong Kong; it still stands.

The Song dynasty was over after 319 years (counting from 960). For the first time in Chinese history, all of China — every region, every population, every city, every political institution — was under foreign rule. The Mongol Yuan dynasty inherited the most sophisticated economy, the most advanced bureaucracy, the largest urban population, the most innovative technology base, and the most globally connected commercial network on Earth. The tension between Mongol military traditions and Chinese civilian governance — and the question of whether the Mongols would govern China as outsiders or be absorbed by it — would define the Yuan period that followed (Mongol Empire Ch 8 and the forthcoming Yuan Dynasty TL).

---

A pause, on what was lost and what was inherited.

The Sui-Tang-Song arc covered nearly seven hundred years — from Yang Jian's reunification in 589 to the drowning of Emperor Bing in 1279. Across that span, Chinese civilization had moved through reunification, golden age, catastrophic civil war, partial recovery, second collapse, second reunification under different constitutional principles, technological revolution, Jurchen catastrophe, Southern recovery, and finally Mongol conquest. Some of the cultural and technological achievements of the period — Tang poetry, Song philosophy, Song landscape painting, Jingdezhen porcelain, paper money, movable type, the navigation compass, gunpowder weapons, examination meritocracy, Neo-Confucianism — would shape East Asia and, indirectly, the rest of the world for the next thousand years and counting. Some of the catastrophes — the An Lushan Rebellion's death toll, the Jingkang Incident's national trauma, the systematic Mongol destruction of north China's cities and population in the 13th century — would shape Chinese cultural memory for just as long.

The Mongol Yuan dynasty that followed in 1279 would be the first foreign dynasty to rule all of China. The Ming dynasty that overthrew it in 1368 would self-consciously model itself on the Tang and Song, treating those dynasties as the standard for what proper Chinese civilization looked like. The Qing dynasty that overthrew the Ming in 1644 would do the same, even though the Qing rulers were themselves Manchus (descendants of the Jurchen) ruling as foreigners. The constitutional model of civilian bureaucratic rule with examination-selected officials, which the Song had pioneered in deliberate reaction against the failures of the Tang military system, would persist as the basic structure of Chinese governance through the entire imperial period — for nearly a thousand years after the Song's founding, until the abolition of the imperial examinations in 1905 and the fall of the Qing in 1912 finally ended the system.

The cosmopolitan Chang'an of the High Tang and the maritime-commercial Hangzhou of the Southern Song are still, in 2026, the cities that contemporary Chinese cultural memory returns to most often when imagining what Chinese civilization at its peak could look like. The Tang and Song are not just dynasties; they are the standard against which all subsequent Chinese history has measured itself.

That standard was set during the seven hundred years this book covers.

Cross-civ check: the Mongol conquest of the Song in 1279 was contemporary with the height of the **Crusader** era's collapse (the fall of **Acre**, the last Crusader stronghold in the Levant, in 1291), the end of the **Abbasid Caliphate** in Baghdad (the Mongol sack of Baghdad under Hulagu in 1258, which the Mongol-Song war was effectively the eastern parallel of — Islamic Golden Age Ch 10), the rise of the early **Renaissance** in Italy, and the development of the **Hanseatic League** in northern Europe. The 13th century was the moment when the Mongol Empire briefly unified most of Eurasia under a single political order, enabling the long-distance travels of merchants like **Marco Polo** (who crossed Asia in the 1270s and returned to Venice in 1295 with stories that shaped European understanding of the East for centuries) and missionaries like the Franciscans **John of Plano Carpini** and **William of Rubruck**. The Mongol conquest of Song China was the eastern hinge of this Eurasian moment. The world that emerged afterward — with the Yuan dynasty governing China, the Ilkhanate governing Persia, the Golden Horde governing the Russian steppes, and the Chagatai Khanate governing Central Asia, all linked by Mongol-protected trade routes — was the most globally interconnected pre-modern world that had ever existed. The Tang-Song China that the Mongols conquered was the eastern cornerstone of that world. Its fall in 1279 ended the dynasty but inaugurated the era of Eurasian exchange that would, four centuries later, produce the European voyages of discovery that would, in turn, produce the modern world.

The Sui-Tang-Song story ends here. The Yuan story begins next.

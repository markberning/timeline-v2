# Srivijaya — The Empire That Got Lost

**Goal:** Narrative for Srivijaya (650–1377 CE), the maritime Buddhist empire based in Sumatra that controlled the Strait of Malacca for roughly seven centuries. Srivijaya is the lost civilization of Southeast Asia. Almost nothing of it survives in the soil where it stood — no royal palace, no monumental temple at Palembang, no statuary of its kings, only a handful of stone inscriptions and the foundations of a few outlying shrines. The French scholar George Coedes only proposed its identity in 1918, reconstructing the empire from references in Old Malay, Sanskrit, Tamil, Arabic, and Chinese sources that had been lying around for centuries without anyone connecting them. What follows is the story of an empire that was, in its time, one of the wealthiest and most cosmopolitan in Asia — a Buddhist scholarly center where more than 1,000 monks lived at the capital, a customs regime that taxed every ship sailing between China and India, and a thalassocracy whose reach ran from East Africa to southern China — and that left so little physical evidence behind that for centuries no one knew it had ever existed.

# Chapter 1 — Maritime Southeast Asia Before Srivijaya

To understand Srivijaya, you have to first picture the world it grew out of: a vast island archipelago whose people had been sailors for thousands of years, sitting at the natural pivot point of a long-distance trading system that already ran from the Roman Mediterranean to Han China. Srivijaya did not invent maritime Southeast Asia. It inherited it.

The geography matters. **Maritime Southeast Asia** is the chain of islands and peninsulas stretching from modern Myanmar in the west to New Guinea in the east — a region the size of the continental United States, divided by hundreds of straits and seas, dominated by tropical rainforest and volcanic mountains, and almost impossible to traverse by land. To get from one place to another in this world, you took a boat. The geography forced everyone living here into a maritime culture, and over thousands of years it produced one of the most formidable seafaring traditions in human history.

The people who built this tradition were the **Austronesians** (AW-stroh-NEE-zhuns), one of the great linguistic dispersals in human history. The Austronesian language family originated somewhere in what is now Taiwan, probably around 3000 BCE, and spread southward through the Philippines, across the Indonesian archipelago, and eventually outward in two directions: westward all the way to Madagascar off the east coast of Africa, and eastward across the entire Pacific to Easter Island. By the time of European contact, Austronesian languages were spoken across more than 13,000 miles (21,000 km) of ocean — the widest geographical spread of any pre-modern language family on Earth, and they did it almost entirely by boat. (For comparison, the Indo-European family that produced English, Spanish, Hindi, and Russian spreads roughly 5,000 miles from Ireland to India. The Austronesians more than doubled that range.) The Polynesians who voyaged into the central Pacific (Polynesian Voyagers Ch 1) were the easternmost branch of this same family. The Malay-speakers of Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula were a central Asian branch. They were linguistic cousins — distant ones by the time the Polynesians reached Hawaii, but cousins nonetheless.

The Austronesians who settled the Indonesian archipelago brought with them the technology that defined regional culture: the **outrigger canoe**, a hull with one or two lateral floats connected by crossbeams that prevent the vessel from capsizing in rough seas; **rice agriculture** adapted to wet tropical conditions; pigs, chickens, and dogs as domesticated animals; and a maritime worldview in which the village's relationship to the ocean was as defining as a Mesopotamian village's relationship to its irrigation canal (Mesopotamia Ch 2). By the early centuries CE, Austronesian-speaking communities along the coasts of Sumatra, Java, Borneo, and the Malay Peninsula had several thousand years of coastal and inter-island sailing experience. The infrastructure for a major maritime trading empire was there long before any state existed to organize it.

---

What changed in the first few centuries CE was the arrival of long-distance trade with India and China.

By around 100 CE, Chinese imperial records were already describing ships from "the southern seas" calling at Guangzhou with rhinoceros horn, ivory, pearls, tortoiseshell, kingfisher feathers, and exotic woods — the first written references to ports in modern Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula. Han-dynasty merchants were reaching south for these goods (Han Dynasty Ch 5), and the southerners were sending tribute missions to the Han court in return. At roughly the same time, Indian merchants were sailing east, drawn by the spice islands and by the gold their texts called **Suvarnabhumi** (soo-var-nah-BHOO-mee, "the land of gold") and **Suvarnadvipa** ("the gold island") — terms that referred most often to Sumatra. Pliny the Elder, writing from Rome in the first century CE, complained that the Roman Empire was hemorrhaging silver to India to pay for spices that originally came from Indonesian islands he had no name for.

The economics of long-distance trade in the early common era was governed by a single physical fact: the **monsoon** wind cycle. Half the year, from roughly May to September, prevailing winds blew from the southwest, carrying ships eastward from India and the Middle East across the Bay of Bengal and the South China Sea. The other half, from November to March, the winds reversed and blew from the northeast, carrying ships home. A merchant who set out from a port in Tamil Nadu in early summer could expect to reach Sumatra in a few weeks, and would then need to wait several months in port for the wind to flip before sailing back. The Strait of Malacca and the surrounding ports were the natural waiting room. A Chinese merchant heading west operated on the same cycle in mirror image.

The result is that long before any centralized political authority existed in maritime Southeast Asia, the region had become a layover zone for international commerce. Ports along the Strait of Malacca and on the eastern Sumatran coast filled up with traders waiting for the monsoon to change. The traders needed food, water, fresh ship repairs, lodging, and sometimes companionship. They needed protection from local pirates. They needed reliable currency exchange. They needed warehousing for goods that had to be transferred from Indian Ocean vessels to South China Sea vessels (different ship designs, different crews, different sailing techniques). The communities that figured out how to provide these services took a cut of every transaction. The community that figured out how to monopolize these services became fabulously wealthy.

That community would eventually be Srivijaya. But before it could exist, the regional template had to be invented.

---

The first major Southeast Asian state attested in outside sources is **Funan** (foo-NAHN), a kingdom in the lower Mekong delta in modern southern Vietnam and Cambodia, which flourished from roughly the first century CE to the mid-sixth. Funan's capital was probably at a site called **Vyadhapura**, and its principal port at **Oc Eo** has yielded archaeological finds that document the cosmopolitan trade culture of the era: Roman gold coins from the second century, Chinese mirrors from the third, Sanskrit inscriptions, a Hindu-Buddhist religious establishment, and warehouses full of glass, gemstones, and Indian goods. Funan was not on the Strait of Malacca route — it sat further east, on the South China Sea side of the Malay Peninsula — but it was the first regional power to demonstrate what an Indianized Southeast Asian state looked like.

What does "Indianized" mean? Beginning around the early common era, Indian cultural elements began spreading across maritime Southeast Asia: Sanskrit, the **Brahmi**-derived scripts of India, Hindu and Buddhist religion, Indian temple architecture, Indian-style royal titles and coronation rites, Indian legal concepts, and the Indian cosmological framework in which a king served as the earthly mirror of a divine universal order. Local Southeast Asian rulers adopted Sanskrit names ending in *-varman* ("protected by"), patronized Brahmin priests and Buddhist monks, built Hindu temples on the Indian model, and wrote royal inscriptions in Sanskrit. The transfer was not military conquest. India never invaded. The mechanism was trade, prestige, and the practical utility of an existing literate religious culture for legitimizing emerging states.

This process is sometimes called the **Indianization of Southeast Asia**, and it is one of the most consequential cultural transfers in pre-modern history. The closest comparison is the Hellenization of the eastern Mediterranean after Alexander, or the Sinicization of Korea (Ancient Korea Ch 4) and Japan (Ancient Japan Ch 3) under the long Chinese influence: a great civilization radiating cultural prestige across a wide region without ever conquering it. The structural parallel to **Romanization** in the western Mediterranean is closer in scale but less precise, because Romanization required Roman troops and Roman administrators on the ground, while Indianization spread without a single Indian soldier setting foot in Sumatra. Indian texts, ideas, and religious specialists traveled by ship and were welcomed by local elites who found them useful. The result was that, by the fifth or sixth century, every emerging state in maritime Southeast Asia was building itself in Indian dress.

A few of these early Indianized states deserve names. **Champa** (CHAHM-pah), on the central coast of modern Vietnam, was a Hindu-Buddhist kingdom that controlled the Vietnamese coast through medieval times and produced extraordinary brick temple complexes at My Son. **Kedah** (KEH-dah), on the western coast of the Malay Peninsula, was an early Indianized port state that would later become a major Srivijayan tributary. The **Pyu** city-states in upper Burma were a third. Each of these polities adopted Indian writing, Indian religion, and Indian-style royal symbolism while remaining ethnically and linguistically Southeast Asian. They were the regional template Srivijaya would inherit and surpass.

---

Funan collapsed in the mid-sixth century, conquered or absorbed by an upstart neighbor called **Chenla** (CHEN-lah, the predecessor state to the later Khmer empire). The collapse opened a regional vacuum. The trade routes did not stop — international demand for Indonesian spices and Sumatran gold was as strong as ever — but no single state was now positioned to dominate them. For roughly a century, between 550 and 650 CE, the maritime trade of Southeast Asia was distributed among many small port-states, none of them powerful enough to extract serious tolls.

In this vacuum, a small Sumatran community at the mouth of the Musi River began to grow.

The site was **Palembang** (PAH-lem-bahng), a settlement set inland on the Musi about forty miles upstream from the open sea but accessible to ocean-going ships through a wide navigable channel. Palembang's geography was almost ideal for the role it would shortly play. The inland location made the city safe from naval attack — any hostile fleet would have to fight its way upriver through a narrow channel that local defenders could easily block. The river itself was deep enough to take large trading vessels at high tide. The surrounding hinterland was rich in forest products (camphor, eaglewood, dammar resin, and gold panned from the upland streams) that could be carried down to the port for export. And, most importantly, Palembang sat just south of the Strait of Malacca — close enough to dominate strait traffic if the city ever gained the means.

Around 650 CE, by the conventional date, that means appeared. Out of the small Palembang polity emerged a ruler named **Dapunta Hyang Sri Jayanasa** (dah-POON-tah HYAHNG sree JAH-yah-NAH-sah), who within a generation had conquered the surrounding territories, brought the Strait of Malacca under his control, and proclaimed a new kingdom called Srivijaya — Sanskrit for "shining victory." Within two decades, Chinese sources were writing about him. Within fifty years, his successors were sending tribute missions to the Tang court at Chang'an, taxing every merchant ship passing through the Strait, and presiding over a Buddhist scholarly establishment that pilgrims from across Asia would soon describe.

That is where the next chapter starts.

---

A pause, before the chronology takes over: maritime Southeast Asia in 650 CE was a far more sophisticated and connected world than European or American readers usually imagine. The region had several thousand years of continuous maritime tradition behind it, an active commercial network linking it to India, China, the Roman Mediterranean (via India), and the Persian world, a literate Indianized political culture across most of its emerging states, and a population of skilled seafarers with the deep-water ship technology to make long voyages routine. What it lacked was a single state powerful enough to capture the wealth that flowed through its waters. Srivijaya filled that gap. The empire's growth was less an invention from scratch than the consolidation of a pre-existing system under unified management.

Cross-civ check: the world that Srivijaya was about to grow into was the seventh-century Eurasian world of the late antique transition. The **Sassanid Empire** in Persia (Persian Empire Ch 9) had just been destroyed by the Arab Muslim conquests of the 630s and 640s. The **Byzantine Empire** had lost Egypt, Syria, and North Africa to the same Arab armies (Byzantine Empire Ch 4). In China, the **Tang Dynasty** had been founded in 618 and was about to enter its great age under Empress Wu and Emperor Xuanzong. In India, the Pushyabhuti emperor **Harsha** had just died in 647 (Medieval India Ch 1), ending the brief reunification of the north. In Mesoamerica, the **Maya** were entering their Late Classic peak (Maya Civilization Ch 5). Srivijaya was a contemporary of all of these — a smaller and more invisible state than any of them, but plugged into the same Eurasian system through trade.

The story of how a small Sumatran river port became the gatekeeper to Asia's wealthiest trade route is the story of one ruling family, one strategic geographic accident, and one Chinese pilgrim who happened to write everything down.

# Chapter 2 — The Founding

In June of the year 671, a Chinese Buddhist monk named **Yijing** (yee-JING, also spelled I-Tsing) boarded a Persian merchant ship at Guangzhou and sailed south for Sumatra. He was thirty-six years old and on his way to study Buddhism in India. The ship took twenty days to reach his destination, a port he calls in Chinese sources **Foshi** (FOH-shee) and which all modern scholars identify as Palembang. He stayed there for six months. He came back several times over the next two and a half decades. And what he wrote about the kingdom he found is, to this day, the longest and most detailed surviving description of Srivijaya from any contemporary observer.

Yijing's account is the foundation of everything we know about early Srivijaya, and it deserves a moment of explanation. He was a serious scholar — a member of the great wave of Chinese Buddhist pilgrims who, in the seventh century, undertook the dangerous journey to India to study the Sanskrit originals of Buddhist scriptures. The most famous of these pilgrims was **Xuanzang** (shwen-DZAHNG, 602–664), who had crossed Central Asia overland to India in the 630s and returned to China seventeen years later with hundreds of Sanskrit texts and a memoir of his travels that was so detailed it later helped Indian archaeologists rediscover lost Buddhist sites. Yijing was a younger contemporary who took the southern sea route instead, and his memoir of his Indian and Southeast Asian travels — usually translated as *A Record of Buddhist Practices Sent Home from the Southern Seas* — is one of the great travel books of the medieval world. It is also, by sheer historical luck, the principal evidence for Srivijaya's existence in its first century. Without Yijing, we would have only a few stone inscriptions and a great deal of uncertainty.

What did Yijing find at Palembang in 671? He found a thriving Buddhist establishment with more than 1,000 monks studying Sanskrit, debating doctrine, and copying scriptures — a community he considered comparable in scale and seriousness to the great Indian monasteries he was on his way to visit. He found a king who patronized Buddhism actively and whose realm was apparently expanding. He found a port full of foreign merchants, with traders from China, India, the Persian world, and parts of the Indonesian archipelago all transacting business under what was clearly a regulated and orderly customs regime. And he found enough Sanskrit instruction at Palembang that he recommended Chinese pilgrims spend a year or two there before continuing on to India, both to learn Sanskrit grammar and to acclimate to the rigorous Indian-style monastic life. Palembang, he wrote, was effectively a Sanskrit prep school for Chinese monks heading to Nalanda.

That is a strikingly developed institutional complex for a kingdom we have no other contemporary description of. It is also strikingly Buddhist for a region that had been, until recently, a patchwork of Hindu and animist port-states with smaller Buddhist communities scattered in. Something had happened at Palembang in the recent past. Yijing's account does not really explain what.

---

The Srivijayan side of the story comes from a small handful of Old Malay stone inscriptions, all dating to the 680s, that surface across southern Sumatra and the offshore islands.

The most important is the **Kedukan Bukit Inscription** (keh-DOO-kahn BOO-kit), discovered in 1920 on a hillside near Palembang. The stone is small — about eighteen inches across — and the text is short, but its content is dramatic. It records that in 682 CE, a king called **Dapunta Hyang Sri Jayanasa** set out from a place called Minanga with a fleet of perhaps twenty thousand troops, made a journey, and the next year, in April or May 683, founded **Srivijaya** ("shining victory" in Sanskrit). The inscription is fragmentary and parts of the translation are debated — exactly where Minanga was, exactly what the journey accomplished, and exactly what the founding of Srivijaya consisted of are all matters scholars argue about. But the basic outline is solid: somewhere in the early 680s, a Sumatran ruler named Dapunta Hyang launched a major military expedition that resulted in the establishment of a new kingdom.

The second inscription is the **Talang Tuwo Inscription** (TAH-lahng TOO-woh), dated 684 CE and also found near Palembang. This one records the founding of a Buddhist sacred garden by the same Dapunta Hyang. The text is longer than the Kedukan Bukit and includes elaborate Mahayana Buddhist devotional language — wishes that the merit generated by the garden's foundation should benefit all sentient beings, a list of physical comforts the king hopes will be available to those who use the garden, and praise to various Mahayana figures. It is the earliest Buddhist text yet found in maritime Southeast Asia. It also confirms that Dapunta Hyang's regime was already deeply Buddhist by the time it consolidated political power, which fits Yijing's contemporary description of the 1,000-monk monastic establishment. The Buddhist character of the new Srivijayan state was there from the start.

The third and fourth inscriptions are the **Telaga Batu Inscription** (teh-LAH-gah BAH-too, found at Palembang) and the **Kota Kapur Inscription** (KOH-tah KAH-poor, found on Bangka Island), both dated to 686 CE. The Telaga Batu is an elaborate oath text, the kind that vassals would swear to a king. It threatens supernatural punishment — death, sickness, the destruction of one's family — on anyone who rebels against Srivijayan authority, and the punishments are described with vivid precision. The Kota Kapur is similar in form and includes a specific reference to a planned military campaign against Java. Together, the two inscriptions document the political mechanism of early Srivijaya: not a centralized administered state on the Roman or Tang Chinese model, but a network of vassal port-states bound to the king by personal oaths backed by religious sanctions.

This matters. Srivijaya was, structurally, less like a territorial empire and more like a federation of harbor towns owing allegiance to a central royal court. The king did not station governors in vassal cities or collect taxes through a salaried bureaucracy. He extracted tribute and military service from local rulers who had personally sworn loyalty to him. When those rulers failed to maintain their oaths, the king used naval force to discipline them. When they failed to do even that, the empire weakened. Most pre-modern empires worked this way to some degree, but Srivijaya's dependence on personal loyalty and naval punishment was unusually pure. The empire had no land army of consequence and no provincial bureaucracy. It had a fleet, a treasury, and a network of sworn vassals. That was the system.

---

A pause on what we don't know about Dapunta Hyang. The inscriptions tell us his name and his role as founder. They tell us he commanded a substantial military expedition in 682–683. They tell us he was Buddhist, that he founded a sacred garden, and that he claimed sovereignty over a wide swath of southern Sumatra and the offshore islands. They do not tell us where he came from, who his parents were, what he looked like, how he claimed legitimacy beyond military success and Buddhist patronage, when he died, or what his successor did. We know almost nothing about him as a person. The Srivijayan king who founded one of the most consequential maritime empires in pre-modern history is, as a historical individual, almost invisible.

This is a recurring pattern with Srivijaya, and it is worth flagging early because it affects how the rest of the chapters read. The empire's internal records have not survived. There is no Srivijayan equivalent of the Tang dynastic chronicles, the Pala copperplate grants, the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, or the Sasanian inscription tradition. There is no king-list. There are no preserved royal annals. Most Srivijayan kings are named only in passing in foreign sources — Chinese tribute records, Tamil military inscriptions, Tibetan Buddhist hagiographies — and many of them are not named at all. We know the empire existed, we know what it controlled, and we know what it accomplished. We know almost nothing about the specific human beings who ran it. The closest analogue is probably the Indus Valley civilization (Indus Valley Ch 7), which has a comparable archaeological footprint but an even more profound silence on names. Srivijaya at least has names — just very few of them.

---

Conquest of the surrounding territory came in waves through the late seventh century. Bangka Island, just off the southeastern Sumatran coast, was annexed by 686 — the location where the Kota Kapur inscription was found. The **Jambi** (JAHM-bee) region of central Sumatra, north of Palembang along the Batang Hari River, came under Srivijayan control by the end of the century. The **Bangka Strait** between Sumatra and Bangka, and the broader Sunda Strait between Sumatra and Java, fell under Srivijayan naval patrol. By around 690 CE, the kingdom controlled all the southern entrances to the Strait of Malacca — the great waterway between Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula through which all shipping between China and India had to pass.

That control of the Strait of Malacca is the single fact that made Srivijaya an empire rather than another minor port-state. The Strait is roughly 600 miles long and varies in width from about 40 miles to about 250. It is shallow in places and threaded with small islands that would shelter pirates well into the nineteenth century. A merchant ship sailing from China to India in the seventh or eighth century had a choice between using the Strait of Malacca or taking the much longer southern route around Sumatra through the Sunda Strait between Sumatra and Java. The Malacca route was shorter, faster, and had more safe harbors, but it was also more dangerous unless someone was actively suppressing piracy and providing harbor services. Srivijaya provided both. The price was a customs payment at the kingdom's principal ports. The arrangement worked because everyone understood the alternative — pirates and unfamiliar harbors — was worse than the toll.

The conquest of the Strait was therefore not really conquest in the sense of taking new territory. It was the establishment of a regulated commercial regime. The empire's "rule" over the Strait of Malacca was not occupation by Srivijayan troops; it was the suppression of pirates and the collection of fees from merchants who chose to use the safer route. Modern readers should think of it less as Roman provincial occupation and more as something like an early-modern port authority backed by a navy. Srivijaya did not own the Strait. It taxed it. The wealth this generated funded the next several centuries of the empire's existence.

---

By the 690s, the new kingdom was sending tribute missions to **Tang Dynasty China**. The Chinese imperial court system distinguished sharply between recognized tributary states (which had formal diplomatic and commercial standing at Chang'an) and other foreign powers (which did not). Srivijayan tribute missions in 695, 702, 716, 724, and 728 — the dates are recorded in Tang dynastic histories — established the kingdom as a formal Chinese commercial partner with secure trading rights at the Tang ports of Guangzhou and Quanzhou. Tribute missions were not, in practice, charity to the emperor. They were trade delegations carrying high-value goods that the emperor would reciprocate with gifts of equal or greater value, plus the diplomatic recognition that gave the visiting merchants commercial access. Srivijaya played this game well for the next four centuries.

By 700 CE, Srivijaya had a Buddhist scholarly establishment that Yijing had personally vouched for, a Sumatran territorial base extending from Palembang up the Musi River and across to Jambi, a naval force capable of patrolling the Strait of Malacca, a commercial relationship with the Tang court, and the beginnings of a tributary network that would eventually stretch across the Malay Peninsula and into central Java. The empire had been founded. The next step was to make it last.

Cross-civ check: while Dapunta Hyang was founding Srivijaya in the 680s, the world looked like this. In the **Islamic Caliphate**, the Umayyad dynasty had just consolidated control after the Second Fitna and was about to begin the massive expansion across North Africa toward Spain (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). In China, the **Empress Wu Zetian** had effectively seized the Tang throne and would shortly proclaim herself emperor — the only woman ever to rule China in her own right. In India, the **Pushyabhuti dynasty** had collapsed forty years earlier with Harsha's death (Medieval India Ch 1), and the regional kingdoms of north India were settling into the long Tripartite Struggle. In the Mediterranean, the **Byzantine Empire** under Justinian II was shrinking under sustained Arab pressure (Byzantine Empire Ch 5). In Mesoamerica, the **Maya** were at the height of their Late Classic peak. The world was politically connected in ways that are sometimes hard to recover. Srivijaya was a small new piece on a big global board.

# Chapter 3 — The Buddhist Center

What kind of Buddhism did Srivijaya practice, and why did it matter beyond the empire's borders?

The short answer is that Srivijaya was a major center of **Mahayana Buddhism** and emerging **Vajrayana Buddhism**, and that for several centuries the empire functioned as a translation, scholarly, and pilgrimage hub linking the Buddhist worlds of India, China, Tibet, and Java. The longer answer requires some setup, because Mahayana Buddhism is the part of Buddhist history most American readers know least about.

Buddhism began in northern India in the fifth century BCE with the teachings of **Siddhartha Gautama** (sid-DHAR-tha GOW-tah-mah, the historical Buddha — see Maurya Empire Ch 5 for the Mauryan-era spread). Over the following five centuries, Buddhist thought split into multiple schools. The conservative branch, known to its rivals (and now in scholarly usage) as **Theravada** (THAIR-ah-VAH-dah, "the doctrine of the elders"), held that the Buddha had been a human teacher whose recorded teachings preserved a precise path to individual enlightenment. The reformist branch, which called itself **Mahayana** (mah-hah-YAH-nah, "the great vehicle"), developed in the early centuries CE and added several major innovations: a vastly expanded cosmology with multiple Buddhas across multiple universes, a class of compassionate enlightened beings called **bodhisattvas** (boh-dee-SAHT-vahs) who postpone their own final liberation to help others reach enlightenment, and an emphasis on universal salvation rather than individual monastic discipline. Mahayana also developed an enormous additional scriptural literature — the great Mahayana sutras like the *Lotus Sutra*, the *Heart Sutra*, the *Diamond Sutra*, and the *Avatamsaka Sutra*.

By Srivijaya's era, Mahayana was the dominant form of Buddhism in India north of the Vindhya Mountains, in central Asia, in China, in Korea, and in Japan. Theravada survived in Sri Lanka and in parts of mainland Southeast Asia (Burma, Thailand, Cambodia after the Khmer empire's later conversion). The cultural lineage Srivijaya plugged into was Mahayana. **Vajrayana** ("the diamond vehicle") was a still-newer development, emerging in northern India in the seventh and eighth centuries, that overlaid Mahayana metaphysics with a tantric ritual technology — mantras, visualizations, mandalas, and elaborate guru-disciple lineages designed to accelerate the path to enlightenment. Vajrayana would become the dominant form of Buddhism in Tibet, and Srivijaya was one of the key transmission stations through which it reached the Tibetan world.

This is the doctrinal context in which Yijing's 1,000 monks at Palembang make sense. They were a Mahayana monastic community studying Sanskrit Buddhist texts at a level of seriousness that compared favorably with the great Indian monasteries. The scholarship was real. The texts were real. The translation work was real. Srivijaya was not a peripheral outpost imitating Indian Buddhism — it was an active node in the late-antique Buddhist intellectual world, producing original commentaries, hosting visiting scholars from across Asia, and copying and transmitting texts that have survived only because they were translated and preserved at places like Palembang.

---

Yijing's writings give the texture of how this worked.

He describes the daily rhythm of monastic life at Palembang: the morning study sessions in Sanskrit grammar, the recitation of mantras and sutras, the formal disputations between scholars from different lineages, the elaborate purification rites before meals, the contemplative practices in the afternoons. He describes the Buddhist lay community supporting the monks: merchants and artisans who provided food and donations, royal patronage that funded monastery construction and maintained the libraries, pilgrim hostels for travelers in transit. He describes the textual practice — Chinese pilgrims arriving with shopping lists of sutras to acquire, Srivijayan monks helping them locate manuscripts and arrange copies, the slow patient labor of comparing variant readings and producing reliable Sanskrit editions for transmission back to China.

He also describes things that are mundane but historically priceless: the kind of food the monks ate (vegetarian, with rice and fruit), the cloth they wore (Indian-style cotton robes, dyed in the regulation colors of Mahayana monasticism), the way they arranged their bedding and washed their alms bowls, the climate and the diseases (the wet tropical environment caused chronic fevers that several Chinese pilgrims, including some of Yijing's companions, did not survive). His memoir is the closest thing we have to a documentary film of a seventh-century Buddhist monastery, and it happens to be set at Palembang.

Yijing eventually returned to China in 695, carrying back hundreds of Sanskrit manuscripts that he translated into Chinese over the following decades. He spent a total of perhaps seven years at Srivijaya across his various stays, which is more time than he spent at any single Indian monastery. The Chinese Buddhist canon, as it eventually stabilized, contains his translations — including many texts whose Sanskrit originals are now lost and survive only in his Chinese versions. A significant fraction of the medieval Chinese Buddhist intellectual tradition runs through Palembang.

A useful comparison: Yijing's near-contemporary **Xuanzang** (the great seventh-century Chinese pilgrim mentioned earlier) had taken the overland Silk Road route to India and back — the route that gives us the legendary *Journey to the West* and the Monkey King. Xuanzang's name is famous. Yijing's is not, in the West. But Yijing's southern sea route was, by his time, becoming the more important of the two — faster, cheaper, and capable of carrying more bulk material. The shift from overland to maritime as the principal Buddhist transmission corridor between India and China is one of the major structural changes of seventh-century Eurasian religious history, and Srivijaya was the key middle station.

---

The empire's Buddhist culture also reached across the Java Sea to **central Java**, where a closely allied dynasty produced one of the wonders of the world.

The dynasty was the **Sailendras** (sye-LEN-drahs), who ruled central Java from roughly 750 to 850 CE. The exact relationship between the Sailendras and the Srivijayan ruling family is one of the great puzzles of early Southeast Asian history. The two dynasties shared royal titulature, religious orientation (both were patrons of Mahayana Buddhism), and political alliance for over a century. Some scholars argue they were branches of the same family; some argue they were separate dynasties bound by marriage and religious affinity; some argue the Sailendras were originally Srivijayan vassals who eventually went their own way; some argue they were originally Javanese rulers who absorbed Srivijayan political culture. The evidence is fragmentary and the debate is unresolved. What is not in dispute is that the two dynasties were closely connected for the better part of a century, that their religious patronage flowed in the same channels, and that the Sailendra-funded monumental architecture of central Java has no Srivijayan parallel because Srivijaya invested its religious patronage in scholarship, manuscripts, and monastery operations rather than in stone monuments.

What the Sailendras built was **Borobudur** (BOR-oh-boo-door).

Borobudur is the largest Buddhist monument in the world. It is a massive stepped pyramid carved as a three-dimensional **mandala** (a sacred geometric diagram representing the structure of the cosmos), built in central Java between roughly 780 and 825 CE. The structure has nine stacked terraces — six square at the bottom, three circular above — topped by a great central stupa. The terraces carry more than 500 Buddha statues sitting in meditation niches, and the walls are covered with 2,672 relief panels carved in low relief, depicting stories from the Buddha's previous lives, scenes from key Mahayana sutras, and the cosmology of the path to enlightenment. The whole structure is built from approximately two million blocks of dark volcanic stone, fitted together without mortar.

The monument is meant to be walked, not just looked at. The pilgrim begins at the base and circumambulates the structure clockwise, climbing the terraces in order, reading the relief panels in sequence as a visual encyclopedia of Buddhist teaching. The lower square terraces depict the realm of desire and the consequences of unenlightened action. The middle terraces depict the early career of the Buddha and the bodhisattva path. The upper circular terraces depict the formless realms of advanced meditation, with the seventy-two Buddha statues in the upper levels each enclosed in a perforated stone bell, half-hidden but visible to the eye that knows where to look. The central stupa at the summit is empty — the symbol of ultimate emptiness that is the goal of the Mahayana path.

This is an extraordinary monument, and the size statistic is not the only one that matters. Borobudur is contemporary with the great **Carolingian** building campaigns of Charlemagne in Western Europe (Early Medieval Europe), with the early **Heian** period in Japan (Ancient Japan Ch 7), and with the late **Tang** monumental Buddhist building in China. None of those building programs produced a monument on Borobudur's scale. The closest contemporary comparisons are probably the great Hindu temple complexes that the **Pallava** and early **Chola** dynasties were building in southern India during the same century (Medieval India Ch 4–5). Borobudur is, by any measure, one of the great architectural achievements of the medieval world.

The fact that it was built in central Java, not at Palembang, tells us something important about Srivijaya. The empire's Sumatran heartland never produced monumental religious architecture on this scale. Palembang itself has yielded almost nothing — a few foundations at the Bukit Seguntang sacred hill, a few brick stupas at the rural Muara Takus complex in central Sumatra, a handful of bronze Buddha statues. The grandiose religious building programs of Srivijayan-era Buddhism happened in Java, under Sailendra patronage. Why? Probably because Java had more readily available stone, a denser agricultural population to mobilize as labor, and a cultural tradition more inclined toward monumental construction. Sumatra had wealth from trade but was thinly populated and lacked the rural labor force to sustain massive construction. The empire's patronage flowed where the conditions favored it.

The result is that Borobudur is a Sailendra monument that is in many ways the most visible surviving expression of Srivijayan-era Buddhist culture, even though Srivijaya itself did not build it. This is a paradox the modern visitor needs to keep straight. Visiting Borobudur today gives you a sense of the religious depth of the civilization Srivijaya was at the center of — but you are not visiting a Srivijayan monument. You are visiting its Javanese counterpart.

---

The Buddhist culture of Srivijaya reached its widest external influence in the early eleventh century with the Tibetan story of **Atisha**.

**Atisha** (ah-TEE-shah, also called Atiśa, full name Atiśa Dīpaṃkara Śrījñāna, born around 982 in Bengal) was a Bengali Buddhist scholar from a noble family in the Pala-dynasty heartland of north India. He was one of the great intellects of his generation, with thorough training in Mahayana philosophy, Vajrayana ritual, and the full range of late Indian Buddhist scholarship. As a young man, drawn to deepen his understanding of the bodhisattva path, he sailed from Bengal to Sumatra around 1012 CE and spent the next twelve years at Srivijaya studying under a Srivijayan teacher named **Dharmakirti** (dhar-mah-KEER-tee — not to be confused with the famous seventh-century Indian Buddhist logician of the same name; the two are different people separated by several centuries).

The Srivijayan Dharmakirti's instruction to Atisha focused on the *Bodhicaryavatara* ("Guide to the Bodhisattva's Way of Life") of Shantideva, an eighth-century Indian Mahayana text on the cultivation of compassion, and on a set of practical mind-training techniques (Tibetan **lojong**) for transforming everyday emotional reactions into instances of bodhisattva practice. These twelve years of training shaped Atisha's mature thought decisively. He returned to India around 1024 and taught at the great monastery of **Vikramashila** in Bengal for several years before, in 1042, accepting an invitation to travel to Tibet to lead a Buddhist revival there.

In Tibet, Atisha's teaching established what later Tibetan tradition called the "second diffusion" of Buddhism — the revival period after several centuries of decline that produced the foundational Tibetan Buddhist schools. The mind-training techniques he taught became, through his Tibetan disciples, the *Lamrim* tradition that organizes the Tibetan Buddhist curriculum to this day. The dharma transmission line of every modern Tibetan Buddhist school — Kagyu, Sakya, Gelug, and to some extent Nyingma — runs back through Atisha and therefore through his twelve years of study at Palembang. The Buddhism that the current Dalai Lama practices is descended, by an unbroken teacher-student lineage, from a Sumatran Buddhist abbot named Dharmakirti who lived a thousand years ago and whose name is otherwise barely preserved.

This is one of the great unexpected ripple effects in religious history. A Bengali pilgrim sails to a Sumatran trading port to study with a teacher who would otherwise be entirely forgotten, then carries that teacher's instruction to a Himalayan kingdom whose religious tradition would in turn become one of the most globally visible forms of Buddhism in the modern world. The chain of historical contingency runs through the Strait of Malacca.

---

A pause on what we can and cannot say about Srivijayan Buddhism. The textual evidence is real but limited. Yijing's account, the few Sanskrit and Old Malay inscriptions that mention religious foundations, the Tibetan hagiographies of Atisha, and a handful of references in Chinese Buddhist sources are essentially all we have. We do not have the libraries the monasteries presumably contained. We do not have the daily liturgical texts. We do not have the sermons. We do not have the local Buddhist art at the scale that would let us reconstruct popular practice. What we have is documentation that the institutions existed and operated at a high level. The lived religion of seventh- to eleventh-century Srivijayan Buddhism is, like so much else about the empire, a shape we can outline but not fill in.

---

# Chapter 4 — The Strait Empire

It is time to address a word that has been doing a lot of quiet work in this narrative: **empire**.

Srivijaya is conventionally called an empire, and it has been so called ever since Coedes reconstructed its identity in 1918. But "empire" is a tricky word for what Srivijaya actually was, and using it without examination misleads modern readers who carry around a default image of empire shaped by Rome, China, the Mughals, the British, or the Spanish. Srivijaya was none of those things. It was a **thalassocracy** (thah-lah-SOK-rah-see), and the structural difference matters.

A thalassocracy — from the Greek *thalassa*, "sea," and *kratos*, "power" — is a state whose power is exercised across water rather than across territory. Its sovereignty is over ports, sea lanes, and shipping rather than over fixed inland borders. Its capital is typically a major harbor, its army is primarily a navy, and its tax base is primarily customs duties from passing trade rather than agricultural rents from a settled population. Its provincial structure is a network of allied or tributary harbor cities rather than a graduated hierarchy of provinces, districts, and counties on the Tang Chinese model. Its frontiers are weather-dependent (where the monsoon ends, the empire ends) and shifting (a port that pays customs this year may not pay them next year if a rival fleet appears). Its political legitimacy depends on its continued ability to suppress piracy and provide harbor services rather than on a pact between ruler and population.

The classic ancient examples of thalassocracy are **Phoenicia** (Phoenicia Ch 4–5), the **Athenian** maritime league of the fifth century BCE, and certain phases of **Carthage** (Carthage Ch 3–4). The classic medieval examples — concurrent with later Srivijaya — are the **Venetian Republic** and the **Genoese Republic**, the Italian merchant states that built fortunes by controlling Mediterranean shipping during exactly the same centuries Srivijaya was controlling the Strait of Malacca. The structural parallel between Srivijaya and Venice is uncannily precise. Both states were built on a single chokepoint of long-distance trade. Both extracted wealth primarily from customs and merchant services. Both operated through alliance and toll-extraction rather than territorial occupation. Both produced glittering wealth out of all proportion to their territorial size or agricultural base. Both eventually lost out to rival powers (Venice to the Ottoman Empire, Srivijaya to the Cholas and then to Java) once the strategic value of their chokepoint was successfully challenged. The two thalassocracies were doing more or less the same thing on opposite ends of Eurasia.

What distinguishes Srivijaya from a typical European-style empire is the relative thinness of its territorial control. The empire's directly ruled core was always small — the Palembang region, the Musi River basin, parts of the southern Sumatran interior, the offshore islands of Bangka and Belitung. Beyond that core was a wide ring of tributary port-states up the Sumatran coast (Jambi to the north), across the Strait into the Malay Peninsula (Kedah, Tambralinga, and others), and intermittently into central Java (during the Sailendra period). The tributaries paid an annual tribute, hosted Srivijayan envoys and customs officers, and accepted Srivijayan naval supervision of their harbors. They were not occupied by Srivijayan troops. Their internal politics were mostly their own. Their loyalty depended on the empire's continued ability to enforce the tribute relationship — which is to say, on the strength of the Srivijayan fleet.

When the fleet was strong, the system worked beautifully. When the fleet was weakened — as it would be after the Chola raid of 1025 (Chapter 6) — tributaries broke away with relative ease. The empire's institutional structure was not built to survive serious military reversal.

---

Geographically, Srivijaya's reach defines itself by the two great straits.

The **Strait of Malacca**, between Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula, was the empire's economic core. Roughly 600 miles long and varying in width from about 40 to about 250 miles, the Strait carried virtually all sea trade between China and India. To the north it opens into the Andaman Sea and from there to the Bay of Bengal and the Indian Ocean. To the south it narrows into the Singapore Strait and from there into the South China Sea. Every major port along the Strait — Kedah on the Malayan side, Lamuri and later Pasai on the Sumatran side, Tambralinga at the peninsular bottleneck — was within Srivijaya's tributary system at the empire's height.

The **Sunda Strait**, between Sumatra and Java, was the alternative. A merchant who wanted to avoid the toll regime in the Malacca Strait could in theory sail the long way around — south down the Indian Ocean coast of Sumatra, through the Sunda Strait between Sumatra and Java, and then north up the Java Sea and through the Karimata Strait. This was substantially longer and substantially more dangerous (rougher seas, fewer safe harbors, less hydrographic knowledge among ship captains) but it bypassed Srivijaya's customs collectors. Srivijaya responded to this threat by extending its naval patrols down the Sumatran coast to the Sunda Strait and by maintaining tributary relations with the Javanese states that controlled the Strait's eastern side. The chokepoint logic worked both ways: anyone who controlled both Malacca and Sunda controlled essentially all the long-distance trade traffic of maritime Southeast Asia, and Srivijaya, for several centuries, did.

The empire's territorial reach in any given decade was always somewhat fluid. At its peak in the eighth and ninth centuries, Srivijaya could extract tribute from ports as far north as southern Thailand (Tambralinga and the Isthmus of Kra portage routes) and as far east as central Java (during periods of close Sailendra alliance). Its sphere of influence touched parts of Cambodia, the Borneo coast, and possibly the Philippines, though the evidence for those further reaches is thinner. The empire's "borders" on a modern map are essentially the coastlines of regions where it had treaty relations with local rulers; everywhere else was someone else's problem.

---

The wealth this all generated came from one place: the tax that ships paid to use Srivijayan ports.

The mechanics of the customs regime are partially reconstructable from Chinese, Arab, and Tamil sources. A merchant ship sailing through the Strait of Malacca in, say, 850 CE would normally make at least one stop at a major Srivijayan port — Palembang itself, or Kedah, or Lamuri, or one of the smaller waystations. At the port, the ship's cargo would be inspected by Srivijayan customs officials. A percentage of the cargo's value — the percentage varied by category of goods and by the diplomatic relationship between Srivijaya and the merchant's state of origin — was charged as duty, payable in goods, in silver, or in services. The ship would then take on supplies (water, food, ship repairs), trade some of its cargo for local goods (Sumatran spices, gold, camphor, eaglewood) at the port markets, and continue its voyage. If the merchant was waiting for the monsoon to change, the layover might be months long, during which time the port collected food and lodging fees as well.

The volume of trade was enormous by pre-modern standards. We do not have specific Srivijayan customs records, but we can estimate from Chinese, Arab, and Tamil sources that hundreds of major ships passed through the Strait every year, carrying total cargoes worth what would be, in modern terms, hundreds of millions of dollars. The Srivijayan court took its cut from every single ship. The empire was, by the standards of the eighth and ninth centuries, fabulously wealthy.

The wealth flowed primarily through the royal court rather than into a broad merchant class. Srivijaya did not develop an internal commercial culture comparable to the merchant guilds of Tamil Nadu (Medieval India Ch 5) or the Italian cities of Venice and Genoa. Most of the merchants doing the actual trading were foreign — Chinese, Tamil, Arab, Persian, Javanese — and the local Srivijayan population was relatively small. The royal monopoly on customs collection, combined with the royal monopoly on patronage of the Buddhist establishment, made the king the dominant economic actor in his own state. This was a structural weakness that would matter later. When the Cholas captured the king in 1025, the entire commercial regime was disrupted in a way that would not have happened in a more institutionally distributed economy.

---

The cosmopolitan composition of the merchant communities at Srivijayan ports deserves a closer look, because it is one of the most interesting features of the empire and a useful corrective to any image of Srivijaya as a self-contained ethnic state.

The merchants visible in our sources include:

**Chinese** merchants from the Tang and later Song ports, particularly Guangzhou and Quanzhou, carrying silks, ceramics, copper coins, and worked metals south, and returning with spices, aromatic woods, and the products of the Indonesian archipelago. The Chinese trade was the largest single component of Srivijayan commerce.

**Tamil** merchants, particularly the merchant guild called the **Ainurruvar** (eye-noo-ROO-var, "the Five Hundred"), which was a powerful corporate entity based in south India that operated trading houses across the Bay of Bengal (Medieval India Ch 5). Tamil inscriptions found at **Lobu Tua** in northern Sumatra document a self-governing Tamil community at that port operating under Srivijayan protection in the late eleventh century. The Tamil presence brought Indian textiles, gold and silver coins, and Indian-made luxury goods into Srivijayan markets.

**Arab and Persian** merchants from the Abbasid Caliphate (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4), who reached the Strait via the Indian Ocean monsoon route and brought West Asian glass, silver, ceramics, and the products of Mediterranean and Mesopotamian agriculture. The Arab geographers wrote about a fabulously wealthy Maharaja of **Zabag** (ZAH-bag) — almost certainly Srivijaya — whose realm they described in mythical terms. The unnamed compiler of the *Akhbar al-Sin wa-l-Hind* ("Account of China and India," ninth century) wrote that the king of Zabag was so rich he threw a gold ingot into the harbor every morning at dawn, accumulating an underwater treasure that would be his on the day of judgment. This is obviously a folktale, but it captures the Arab perception of Srivijaya as a place of extraordinary wealth.

**Javanese** merchants, both from the Sailendra-era central Javanese kingdoms during periods of alliance with Srivijaya and from the later eastern Javanese states (Kediri, Singhasari) during periods of competition.

**Bugis, Malay, Orang Laut, and other Southeast Asian** seafarers, who provided much of the local shipping that moved goods between Srivijayan ports and into the wider archipelago. The Orang Laut (literally "sea people") were a particularly important constituency — boat-dwelling communities of Malay-speakers who patrolled the empire's waters and supplied much of the actual naval manpower of the Srivijayan fleet.

This was, by any standard, a cosmopolitan port complex. A walk through the Palembang waterfront in 850 CE would have meant hearing Old Malay, Sanskrit, Tamil, Mandarin, Arabic, Persian, and Javanese spoken on the same pier. The merchants would have prayed at Buddhist monasteries, Hindu shrines, Muslim mosques (after the Arab presence began in the eighth and ninth centuries), and to local Austronesian sea spirits, sometimes within blocks of each other. The cuisine, the music, the costume, and the social life of the port would have reflected all these contributing populations. Srivijaya was a small Sumatran-ruled core surrounded by a vast cosmopolitan trading suburb. The empire's capacity to manage this cosmopolitan complex without ethnic violence was one of its quieter accomplishments and one of the things that made it durable.

---

A pause to compare. Two structural parallels are useful for getting the sense of Srivijaya as a thalassocracy.

The first is **Venice** in roughly the same centuries. Venice in 1000 CE was a small lagoon city with no agricultural hinterland and no serious land army, controlling the eastern Mediterranean trade through customs duties at its docks, alliance with Byzantine emperors and crusader kings, and a fleet of merchant galleys that doubled as warships when needed. Venice's wealth came from Asian luxury goods — spices, silks, gemstones — that arrived through its port from the Levant. Venice's political system was an oligarchic merchant republic that managed the customs regime collectively rather than monarchically; that is one major structural difference from Srivijaya, which was a Buddhist monarchy. But the underlying economic logic — control a chokepoint, tax the trade, protect your fleet, maintain alliances with the powers on either end of the trade route — is essentially identical. Two parallel thalassocracies, on opposite ends of Eurasia, doing more or less the same thing during the same centuries.

The second is the **Phoenician** city-states two thousand years earlier (Phoenicia Ch 4). Phoenicia in 800 BCE was a chain of small coastal ports on the Levantine coast — Tyre, Sidon, Byblos — that controlled Mediterranean long-distance trade through technical superiority in shipbuilding and navigation. Phoenician trade reached from the Levant to Spain, North Africa, and probably (via the Cassiterides legend) the British Isles. Phoenicia produced fabulous wealth, alphabetic writing (Phoenicia Ch 5), and the colonial daughter-state of Carthage (Carthage Ch 1) without ever consolidating into a single territorial empire. Srivijaya consolidated more politically than Phoenicia did, but the underlying maritime logic was the same: small physical bases, vast commercial reach, wealth from customs and trade rather than from agriculture, vulnerability to any large land power that decided to project force across water.

These parallels are the right mental model for Srivijaya. Forget the Roman or Tang Chinese or Mughal images of empire. Think Venice. Think Phoenicia. The empire was real and consequential and in its time fabulously wealthy, but its reach was thin and its institutional foundations were always more fragile than its glittering reputation suggested.

---

# Chapter 5 — Trade Networks

The trade routes that flowed through Srivijaya are worth tracing in some detail, because the empire's prosperity is otherwise hard to make tangible. Numbers and abstractions about "Indian Ocean commerce" do not carry the texture of what was actually moving across those waters.

The China-side trade ran from the great southern Chinese ports — primarily **Guangzhou** (gwong-JOH, in modern Guangdong), **Quanzhou** (chwan-JOH, in modern Fujian), and later **Mingzhou** (modern Ningbo) — through the South China Sea. Tang and later Song Chinese merchants loaded their ships with **silks** (the great export, valued across Eurasia for both the fabric itself and the dyed luxury versions); **porcelain** and stoneware ceramics (Chinese kilns produced the highest-quality fired ceramics in the world by the Tang dynasty, and demand in foreign markets was insatiable); **iron and copper goods**, particularly cooking vessels and tools; **lacquerware**; **books and writing supplies**; and small quantities of high-value **gold and silver coins** for transactions where barter was impractical. Tang and Song China was, in commercial terms, an export superpower, producing manufactured goods at quality and scale that no other contemporary economy could match. The trade ran fundamentally in China's favor.

These ships sailed south on the northeastern monsoon, typically in late autumn and winter, calling first at Champa on the Vietnamese coast, then at major Srivijayan ports in the Strait of Malacca region. At Palembang or Kedah or Lamuri, they paid customs duties, restocked supplies, and traded a portion of their cargo for local Southeast Asian goods. They then continued west across the Bay of Bengal to the **Tamil coast** of southern India, to **Sri Lanka**, or further west to the **Persian Gulf** and the Arab world. The full one-way voyage from Quanzhou to Basra took perhaps six months at favorable winds, with several extended port layovers along the way.

The India-side trade flowed in mirror image. **Gujarati** and **Tamil** merchants from western and southern India loaded Indian cottons, gemstones, pearls, ivory, and a variety of exotic agricultural products onto their ships and sailed east on the southwestern monsoon, typically in late spring and summer. They called at Srivijayan ports in the Malacca region, traded for Southeast Asian goods, and either continued north to Chinese markets or turned around to return on the next monsoon. Indian merchants were particularly important traders in **textiles** — Indian cotton fabrics, dyed in the brilliant colors that Indian dyers had perfected over centuries, were a major export across all of Asia and Africa, comparable in commercial significance to Chinese silk. The Indian textile trade is one of the great unsung commercial stories of the medieval world.

The local Southeast Asian goods that Srivijayan ports specialized in deserve a closer look, because they explain why merchants kept coming back.

---

**Spices** were the foundation of the long-distance trade. The most valuable were **cloves** and **nutmeg**, both of which grew only in the **Moluccas** (mol-LOO-kahs, the spice islands of eastern Indonesia, particularly Ternate, Tidore, and the Banda Islands). Cloves were the dried unopened flower buds of an evergreen tree native to the northern Moluccas; nutmeg was the seed of a tree native to the Banda group. Both were used in cooking, in medicine, and as preservatives in an era before refrigeration, and both fetched extraordinarily high prices in the great markets of Cairo, Constantinople, Baghdad, Chang'an, and (eventually) medieval European cities. The distance from the Moluccas to European markets, combined with the multiple middlemen at each transfer point, drove the prices up by factors of fifty or a hundred between source and final consumer. Srivijaya did not produce cloves or nutmeg itself, but it controlled the trans-shipment routes through which they reached Indian, Arab, and Chinese buyers. The empire took a cut at every transfer.

**Black pepper** came from the southern Indian coast (the Malabar) and was traded in vast quantities, with Srivijayan ports sometimes serving as intermediate trans-shipment points. **Sandalwood** — an aromatic wood used for incense, religious ritual, and luxury furniture — came from Timor and the eastern Lesser Sunda Islands, transiting Srivijayan ports for export. **Frankincense** and **myrrh** came from the Arabian and East African coasts and moved east through the same routes. The international spice trade was the medieval world's version of a globally integrated luxury market, and Srivijaya sat at one of its most profitable junctions.

Beyond spices, Srivijaya itself produced and exported several distinctive Sumatran goods. **Camphor** from Sumatran trees was the most prestigious — a small bag of crystalline camphor could be worth its weight in silver in Persian or Egyptian markets, where it was valued as a perfume base, a medicinal ingredient, and a fixative for other scents. Sumatran camphor in particular fetched higher prices than Chinese camphor; Arab geographers wrote about it in admiring terms. **Eaglewood** (or aloeswood, or **gaharu** in Malay) was the resinous heartwood of certain Southeast Asian trees, prized for incense and as raw material for decorative carving; it too commanded extraordinary prices abroad. **Dammar resin** had multiple industrial applications. **Benzoin**, another aromatic resin, was used in perfumery and incense. And **gold**, panned from the rivers of central and western Sumatra, gave the region its old Sanskrit name of **Suvarnabhumi** ("the land of gold") and provided the empire with a steady supply of high-value currency for use in foreign trade.

---

The Tamil merchant guilds deserve special attention because they were the most institutionally elaborate component of the foreign trading communities at Srivijayan ports.

The **Ainurruvar** ("the Five Hundred") was a corporate body of South Indian merchants, almost certainly originating in Tamil Nadu around the eighth or ninth century, that operated trading houses across south and Southeast Asia under its own self-governance. The Five Hundred had standardized procedures for capital pooling, dispute resolution, religious patronage, and protection of merchant interests, and they operated under the political protection of the Chola kings (Medieval India Ch 5) when those kings could be persuaded to provide it. A second Tamil guild, the **Manigramam**, operated in similar fashion in different regions. By the tenth and eleventh centuries, both guilds had self-governing communities at Srivijayan ports, including the documented community at **Lobu Tua** in north-central Sumatra mentioned earlier.

The Tamil merchant communities were, in effect, partly autonomous trade enclaves embedded in Srivijayan territory under treaty terms. They paid their customs duties to the Srivijayan king, but they ran their internal affairs according to their own Tamil legal customs, maintained their own Hindu temples, practiced their own religious rituals, and had recognized juridical authority over their own members. This was not unusual in the medieval Indian Ocean world — similar enclaves existed for Arab merchants in Quanzhou, for Persian merchants on the Indian coast, for Indian merchants in East Africa — but the Tamil presence at Srivijayan ports was unusually organized and well-documented.

The institutional sophistication of the Tamil guilds is one reason the Chola raid of 1025 was such a dramatic event. The Cholas had a powerful merchant constituency operating in Srivijayan territory, and when relations between the Chola court and the Srivijayan court soured (Chapter 6), the Chola king had organized commercial interests pushing him toward action.

---

Two Srivijayan-funded religious foundations on the Indian mainland deserve mention, because they are direct evidence of the depth of the Bay of Bengal cultural traffic.

In 860 CE, King **Balaputradeva** (bah-lah-poo-trah-DEH-vah) of Srivijaya — a Sailendra prince who had taken the Sumatran throne after Sailendra power collapsed in central Java — endowed a Buddhist monastery at the great university of **Nalanda** in northeastern India. The endowment was confirmed by the Pala emperor Devapala in a copperplate grant that survives. The grant assigns the income from five villages to the upkeep of the Srivijayan-funded monastery and provides for its operation as a pilgrim hostel and study center for monks traveling between Sumatra and Bihar. This is direct epigraphic evidence of formal cross-Bay-of-Bengal religious patronage at the highest level of state. Srivijayan kings considered Nalanda part of their own religious and cultural orbit and were willing to invest substantial resources in its support.

A second foundation, recorded in later sources, was the **Manjusri Vihara** (mahn-JOO-shree vee-HAH-rah), a monastery dedicated to the bodhisattva of wisdom that operated under the Pala kings of Bengal as a hostel for monks and merchants traveling between Sumatra and northern India. The vihara is referenced in several Tibetan Buddhist sources discussing the late Indian Buddhist establishment, and its existence confirms a sustained Srivijayan presence in the Pala-controlled Buddhist heartland.

These foundations are striking. A Sumatran king sent representatives across more than 1,500 miles of ocean to negotiate with the Pala emperor of Bengal for the establishment of a permanent Buddhist endowment at India's most prestigious monastic center. The relationship was reciprocal — Indian Buddhist scholars came to Srivijaya, Srivijayan Buddhist patronage flowed to India, and the Bay of Bengal functioned as an internal sea of a single Buddhist cultural region. This is the mature shape of the Buddhist trans-oceanic culture that Yijing's seventh-century pilgrimage had begun.

---

A modern discovery brings the trade tangibly into the historical record. In 1998, salvage divers off **Belitung Island**, just off the southeastern Sumatran coast, located the wreck of a small Arab dhow that had sunk in shallow water around 826 CE. The cargo was extraordinary: more than 60,000 Tang Chinese ceramics, dozens of gold cups and silver bowls, ingots of copper and other metals, and assorted personal effects of the crew. The ceramics included the largest single cache of Tang ceramic exports ever discovered, with examples from kilns across China and pieces clearly produced specifically for the export market — including ceramics with Arabic-style decorative motifs that the Chinese potters had adopted to please their foreign buyers.

The Belitung shipwreck is direct physical evidence of the trade triangle — Tang China, Srivijaya, Abbasid Arabia — that the written sources had described. An Arab vessel, almost certainly returning from Guangzhou or another Tang port to the Persian Gulf, sank in Srivijayan waters with a cargo whose composition reveals exactly how the trade worked: massive volume of Chinese ceramics moving west, with smaller-quantity but higher-value gold and silver moving east. The wreck was salvaged commercially in the late 1990s and most of the cargo is now held by the **Asian Civilizations Museum in Singapore**, with disputed legal status. As a piece of evidence about ninth-century Indian Ocean trade, the Belitung wreck is unmatched. It is also, in its own way, a Srivijayan artifact — a ship that died in Srivijayan waters carrying Srivijayan trade.

---

Cross-civ check: by 900 CE, the Indian Ocean trade network in which Srivijaya was a central pivot was the most economically integrated maritime economy in the world up to that point. The system linked Tang China at one end to the Abbasid Caliphate at the other, with Indian merchants operating along the entire route and East African coastal cities (Mogadishu, Mombasa, Kilwa) serving as the western terminus of the spice trade. **Song silver coins** have been found as far west as the Swahili coast (Kingdom of Aksum and the later Swahili networks). Tang ceramics turn up in Sudanese excavations. Persian glass appears in Sumatran graves. Sumatran spices end up in Carolingian aristocratic kitchens by the late ninth century. The integration was real, and Srivijaya was at the geographic and commercial pivot of it.

The closest contemporary parallel is, again, the Mediterranean trade system of the same centuries, in which Byzantine, Italian, Arab, and Iberian traders moved goods across a similarly integrated network. The Indian Ocean system was larger in geographic scope, more diverse in participating cultures, and probably larger in total commercial volume — but it was also more decentralized politically, with no single state ever dominating it the way Rome had once dominated the Mediterranean. Srivijaya was the single greatest political player in the system, and the empire's wealth was a measure of how much of the system's traffic it could tax. But Srivijaya never dominated the Indian Ocean as a whole. It dominated a chokepoint within it.

---

# Chapter 6 — The Chola Raid 1025

In the spring of 1025 CE, a fleet of war vessels from the **Chola** dynasty of southern India crossed approximately 1,200 nautical miles of the Bay of Bengal, fell on the Srivijayan port of Palembang without warning, sacked the city, captured the king, looted the royal treasury, and then proceeded to attack a series of additional Srivijayan tributary ports along the Malay Peninsula and the Sumatran coast. By the time the fleet returned home several months later, fourteen ports had been hit, the Srivijayan king was a Chola prisoner, and the political shape of maritime Southeast Asia had been permanently altered.

The Chola raid is the most famous single event in Srivijayan history and deserves a careful telling, because it is also one of the most extraordinary single military operations of the medieval world. To understand it, you need to know who the Cholas were, why they attacked, and what changed afterward.

The **Chola** dynasty (CHOH-lah) was a south Indian Tamil power based at **Thanjavur** (TAN-jah-voor) in the Cauvery delta of modern Tamil Nadu. The medieval Cholas peaked in the early eleventh century under two extraordinary kings, **Rajaraja Chola I** (rah-jah-RAH-jah, ruled 985–1014) and his son **Rajendra Chola I** (rah-JEN-drah, ruled 1014–1044). What they built in fifty-nine years was a south Indian empire that extended over most of the Tamil and Kannada-speaking regions, ruled the island of Sri Lanka, projected naval power across the Bay of Bengal, and built monumental Hindu temples on a scale that has barely been equaled since (Medieval India Ch 5). Rajaraja's **Brihadeeswara Temple** at Thanjavur, completed in 1010, was the tallest structure in India when it was consecrated. His son Rajendra extended Chola power further north (campaigning all the way to the Ganges in the 1010s) and further out to sea. Both kings were Shaivite Hindus — devotees of the god Shiva — whose royal ideology fused political authority with cosmic and religious symbolism on the Indian model.

The relationship between Srivijaya and the Cholas before 1025 was, in commercial terms, awkwardly symbiotic. Tamil merchant guilds — the Ainurruvar and related corporations — operated trading houses at Srivijayan ports under treaty terms (Chapter 5). Chola diplomatic missions had visited Srivijaya in the late tenth and early eleventh centuries. The Srivijayan king had even, around 1005, sponsored the construction of a Buddhist temple at **Nagapattinam** (a Chola port on the Tamil coast) for the use of Buddhist pilgrims and merchants traveling between Sumatra and the south Indian Buddhist sites. The construction was confirmed by a copperplate grant from Rajaraja Chola — the **Larger Leiden Grant**, named for the Dutch museum that now holds the original copper plates — which records the formal Chola acceptance of the Srivijayan-funded foundation. Two decades before the raid, the two courts were exchanging gifts and respecting each other's religious foundations. Something went badly wrong in the intervening years.

---

What turned this commercial partnership into war is unclear. The Chola inscriptions are propagandistic and not specific about causes; the Srivijayan side of the story is, as usual, almost entirely missing. The most plausible reconstruction, drawing on the pattern of Tamil merchant grievances visible in the inscriptions and on the political situation of the early eleventh century, is that Srivijaya had begun extracting unfavorable tolls or trade restrictions from the Tamil merchant guilds operating through the Strait of Malacca. The Tamil merchant constituency in turn pressed the Chola court for intervention. There may also have been a religious dimension — Srivijaya was a Mahayana Buddhist state and Rajendra's reign was a high-water mark of Shaivite Hindu triumphalism, and the religious-political language of the period emphasized the ruler's duty to discipline foreign powers that mistreated his subjects. There was almost certainly also a sheer demonstration-of-power dimension. Rajendra had already crossed north India to the Ganges to demonstrate Chola power against the Pala kingdom; sending a fleet across the Bay of Bengal to sack a wealthy maritime rival was the next escalation.

Whatever the precipitating cause, around 1025 CE, Rajendra dispatched a Chola war fleet across the Bay of Bengal. The fleet's specific size and composition are not recorded in detail, but the operation must have involved at least dozens of large vessels and several thousand fighting men, plus the logistical support needed to sustain them for several months at sea. The expedition crossed the Bay using monsoon wind patterns — sailing east on the southwestern monsoon in late spring, returning west on the northeastern monsoon in autumn, with operations on the Sumatran-Malayan coast during the intervening months.

The fleet's first objective was Palembang. The attack appears to have caught Srivijaya unprepared. The empire's naval forces were dispersed, the Strait of Malacca was undefended against a force coming from the west across the Bay of Bengal rather than from any expected direction, and Palembang itself was a riverine port with limited fortification against a serious naval assault. The Chola fleet sailed up the Musi, sacked the city, and took the Srivijayan king prisoner. The king's name, recorded in Chola inscriptions in elaborate Sanskrit, was **Sangrama Vijayottungavarman** (sahn-GRAH-mah vee-jah-yoht-toon-gah-VAR-mahn — the long Sanskrit phrase translates roughly as "the heroically victorious warrior in battle"). What happened to him after his capture is not recorded; no Srivijayan source mentions his fate.

The fleet then proceeded along the coast attacking additional Srivijayan tributary ports. Chola inscriptions list fourteen captured cities, including **Kedah** (the major Malayan-side Srivijayan port), **Lamuri** (in northern Sumatra), **Tambralinga** (on the Malay Peninsula's eastern coast), and other smaller ports. The raid was systematically destructive — the inscriptions list the wealth carried off from each captured city, including treasures, gemstones, fine textiles, and (in one specific reference) a "war elephant" gate-tower that the Cholas removed from one of the captured cities and carried back to Tamil Nadu as a trophy.

After several months of operations, the Chola fleet returned home. The Cholas did not attempt to permanently occupy Srivijayan territory — that would have required sustaining an army across an ocean indefinitely, which was logistically impossible. What they accomplished was the demonstration of Chola naval power, the temporary disruption of Srivijayan trade dominance, and the establishment of a clear Chola sphere of commercial influence across the eastern Indian Ocean. Tamil merchant guilds operated unimpeded in Southeast Asia for the next century. Srivijaya recovered its political existence — successor kings appear in later sources — but its dominance of the Strait of Malacca was permanently weakened, and the empire's monopoly position would never be restored.

---

The Chola raid is, by some distance, the most ambitious single naval operation of any pre-modern Indian state. It is the only confirmed pre-modern Indian transoceanic military expedition. The Mauryan, Gupta, and Mughal states all had powerful armies but never used them across open oceans on this scale. Rajendra's expedition was, in practical military terms, more akin to the great early-modern European naval expeditions than to anything else in Indian history.

The cross-civ comparisons help put the raid in context. The closest contemporary parallel in Eurasian terms is the **Norman Conquest of England** in **1066**, which involved crossing the much shorter English Channel — about 22 miles at the narrowest point — with a fleet of perhaps several hundred vessels carrying 10,000 men. The Chola expedition crossed roughly 1,200 nautical miles of open ocean, several orders of magnitude more demanding logistically than the Norman crossing of the Channel, and involved attacks on fourteen separate fortified ports rather than a single decisive land battle. In terms of pure naval logistical accomplishment, the Chola raid was the more remarkable operation. (The Normans, however, won permanent control of England, while the Cholas chose not to occupy Srivijaya — a difference in strategic objectives that shaped the long-term consequences.)

Two earlier comparisons are also useful. The **Athenian Sicilian Expedition** of 415 BCE (Ancient Greece) was comparable in scale, comparable in the specific challenge of projecting force across open water, and comparable in ambition — except that the Athenians lost catastrophically and the Cholas won. The early-fifteenth-century **voyages of Zheng He** under the Ming dynasty were larger in personnel and ship count, but they were primarily diplomatic missions, not military expeditions; the Ming treasure fleets carried impressive force but rarely used it. Rajendra's was a war fleet. It went to a foreign capital, sacked the capital, captured the king, looted the treasury, and went home. There is essentially nothing else like it in pre-modern Asian military history.

A pause on what we can say honestly. Modern accounts of the raid sometimes overstate its decisiveness. Srivijaya did not collapse in 1025. The empire continued to exist, send tribute missions to China (the next Srivijayan tribute mission to Song China was recorded just a few years after the raid), and operate as a regional commercial power for at least two more centuries. What ended in 1025 was not Srivijaya as a state — it was Srivijaya as an unchallenged maritime hegemon. After the raid, the empire was one regional player among several rather than the dominant force in the Strait. The Tamil merchant guilds operated more freely. The Javanese kingdoms began their long rise. The political shape of the region was permanently more pluralist and less centered on Palembang than it had been before.

---

A second Chola expedition followed in **1068**, under Rajendra's grandson **Virarajendra Chola** (vee-rah-RAH-jen-drah). This second raid focused on Kedah and reasserted Tamil commercial primacy in the Strait. Like the first, it did not result in territorial occupation. After 1068, Tamil merchant communities operated essentially unimpeded across the eastern Indian Ocean for the rest of the eleventh century. The institutional inheritance of the two raids was the establishment of Tamil — rather than Srivijayan — commercial dominance over the Bay of Bengal trade for several generations.

Cross-civ check: the Chola raid happened in the same decade as the **Battle of Manzikert** (1071, Byzantine Empire Ch 6), in which the Seljuk Turks defeated the Byzantine emperor and changed the religious geography of Anatolia for the next thousand years. It happened a generation before the **First Crusade** (1095–99, Byzantine Empire Ch 7 and Islamic Golden Age Ch 9). It overlapped with the long peak of the **Khmer Empire** under Suryavarman I in mainland Southeast Asia. It overlapped with the consolidation of the **Northern Song dynasty** in China and the construction of the great Song commercial cities. The eleventh century was one of the densest moments of medieval Eurasian history — naval expeditions across the Bay of Bengal, Norman cathedrals going up in England, Romanesque churches in northern Europe, the Brihadeeswara Temple in Tamil Nadu, Khmer temples beginning their move toward what would become Angkor Wat, and the Hagia Sophia mosaics being installed in Constantinople were all happening in the same generations. The Srivijayan world was not isolated. It was part of a connected eleventh-century Eurasia.

# Chapter 7 — The Long Decline

After the Chola raids, Srivijaya did not collapse but it never recovered its monopoly position. The next two and a half centuries — roughly 1025 to 1293 — saw a slow drainage of power, with the empire fragmenting into competing port-states, the Javanese kingdoms steadily rising, the religious composition of the region beginning a profound shift, and the empire's traditional commercial and political role gradually transferred to other powers. The story of the long decline is complex and somewhat episodic — there is no single dramatic event after 1025 — and some chapters of it run through Java, Tibet, Bengal, and Mongol China rather than through Sumatra itself.

The first phase of the decline was the gradual shift of political center from Palembang to **Jambi** (JAHM-bee), the upriver port further north along the Sumatran coast. By around 1088 CE, the Srivijayan ruling family appears to have relocated, and the kingdom of **Melayu** (MEH-lah-yoo) — based at Jambi-Dharmasraya along the Batang Hari River — becomes the dominant successor entity in the sources. The shift reflected several pressures: the disruption of Palembang itself by the Chola raid, the growing importance of Jambi as a source of upriver Sumatran gold and forest products, and probably some internal political dynamics we cannot recover from the surviving record. Whatever the cause, Jambi became the new center, and Palembang dwindled to a secondary port.

The Tamil presence in the Strait, meanwhile, continued to grow. The **Lobu Tua Tamil inscription** of 1088 records a self-governing Tamil merchant guild operating in north-central Sumatra under what was nominally Srivijayan authority. The inscription is direct evidence that the empire's capacity to control its own territory had eroded to the point where Tamil merchants were running their own internal affairs without much Srivijayan oversight. The Tamil-Srivijayan trade continued, but on terms increasingly favorable to the Tamils.

---

Through the twelfth century, the empire fragmented further. Different Sumatran ports — Jambi, Lamuri, Pasai, and others — pursued their own commercial relationships with China, India, and the Arab world. The Chinese Song dynasty, by then dealing with multiple Sumatran tribute missions from different cities rather than a single Srivijayan delegation, recognized this fragmentation in its diplomatic correspondence. The Song customs official **Zhao Rugua** (jow roo-GWAH), supervisor of the maritime trade office at Quanzhou, compiled the *Zhu Fan Zhi* (Description of Foreign Peoples) around 1225. His work describes Srivijaya's still-substantial trade networks but also documents the fragmented political situation — multiple Sumatran ports with separate diplomatic identities, the empire's traditional centralized authority a memory more than a fact.

Zhao Rugua's account is the last detailed external description of Srivijaya as a coherent power. After his time, the references in Chinese, Tamil, and Arab sources thin out, and the empire becomes increasingly invisible to outside observers — not because it had vanished (it had not) but because it was no longer a single unified actor that foreign observers could meaningfully describe.

---

The second phase of the decline involved the rise of Java.

The Javanese kingdoms had always been a complicating factor in Srivijayan politics. During the eighth and ninth centuries, the close Sailendra-Srivijaya alliance had effectively unified Sumatra and central Java under a common political and religious orientation (Chapter 3). After the Sailendras' loss of central Java to the rival Sanjaya dynasty around 850, that alliance broke down. Through the tenth century, the central Javanese **Mataram** kingdom (in the modern Yogyakarta region) was a regional power competitive with Srivijaya. In 1016, Srivijaya raided Mataram and sacked its capital, killing the king Dharmavamsa — a campaign that briefly reasserted Sumatran dominance over central Java but that opened the door for the rival eastern Javanese kingdom of **Kahuripan** (kah-hoo-REE-pahn) under **Airlangga** (eye-er-LAHNG-gah) to consolidate eastern Javanese power.

Through the eleventh and twelfth centuries, Javanese power steadily shifted east, away from the Sailendra-era central Javanese heartland of Borobudur and toward the new political centers of eastern Java. The kingdom of **Kediri** (keh-DEE-ree, 1042–1222) emerged as the dominant eastern Javanese power, with extensive maritime trade interests of its own. After Kediri came **Singhasari** (sing-hah-SAH-ree, 1222–1292), an aggressive eastern Javanese state under the kings Vishnuvardhana and **Kertanagara** (ker-tah-nah-GAH-rah). The pattern of these centuries was Javanese expansion at Srivijayan expense — Javanese fleets becoming more powerful, Javanese ports drawing more of the regional commerce, Javanese religious and cultural institutions becoming more prominent.

In **1275**, the Singhasari king Kertanagara launched the **Pamalayu Expedition**, a naval campaign against the Jambi-based Melayu kingdom in central Sumatra. The expedition occupied the Melayu territories and held them under Singhasari control until 1292. This was, in effect, the Javanese conquest of what remained of the Srivijayan core. The Melayu kingdom continued to exist as a vassal of Singhasari rather than as an independent state. The Sumatran heartland of the old empire was now, for the first time in its history, ruled from outside.

The Pamalayu Expedition was a Javanese demonstration that the political flow of maritime Southeast Asia had reversed. For six centuries, Sumatra had been the center and Java the periphery. After 1275, the situation was inverted. It would remain inverted for the rest of the medieval period.

---

The third phase of the decline involved the arrival of Islam.

Through the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, Muslim merchants from India, the Arab world, and Persia became increasingly important in the Indian Ocean trade. This was partly a function of the broader Islamic expansion of the centuries — by 1200, Muslim communities and Muslim political authority had spread across most of the major Indian Ocean trading zones, with the Delhi Sultanate established in northern India after 1206 (Delhi Sultanate Ch 1), the Mali Empire emerging in West Africa, the Swahili coast Islamizing across East Africa, and Muslim traders dominating the major ports from Mogadishu to Quanzhou. It was also partly a function of the gradual conversion of populations along the trade routes — a process driven by the practical advantages of shared religious-legal infrastructure, the prestige of Arabic literacy, and the active missionary work of Sufi orders.

In maritime Southeast Asia, the Islamization began in northern Sumatra. The Muslim **Pasai Sultanate** (PAH-sye, also spelled Samudra-Pasai), founded around 1267 in the Aceh region of northern Sumatra, was the first formal Islamic state in the archipelago. Pasai became, within a few decades, a thriving Muslim port-city with active commercial relations with the Indian Muslim states, Yemen, and Egypt. The Moroccan traveler **Ibn Battuta** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 8) stopped at Pasai during his Asian travels in 1345 and recorded a Muslim court of considerable elegance, with Arabic-language scholars and active trade with Bengal and southern India.

After Pasai, Islam spread south along the Sumatran coast, then across to the Malay Peninsula, then into Java, then eastward through the archipelago. The mechanism was gradual — merchant communities converting first, local rulers converting next, populations converting over centuries through a combination of conviction, intermarriage, and economic advantage. By 1500, much of coastal maritime Southeast Asia was Muslim. By 1700, much of the interior was as well. The Islamization of the archipelago is one of the great religious transformations of the medieval and early-modern world.

For Srivijaya, the spread of Islam was partly cause and partly symptom of decline. Islam offered Sumatran ports an alternative religious-cultural alignment that did not require subordination to a Buddhist Srivijayan center. Muslim merchants were more interested in trading with Muslim ports than with Srivijayan Buddhist ones. The empire's traditional Buddhist religious infrastructure — its monasteries, its pilgrim circuits, its doctrinal authority — was less and less commercially useful as the surrounding maritime world Islamized. By the end of the thirteenth century, Buddhism in Sumatra was a fading minority religion, gradually displaced by Islam in the coastal communities and increasingly confined to the upriver hinterland.

---

The fourth phase of the decline involved the Mongols.

In **1289**, the Yuan dynasty emperor **Kublai Khan** (Mongol Empire Ch 6) sent a diplomatic mission to the Singhasari king Kertanagara demanding tribute and submission. Kertanagara, who was apparently a man of strong character and limited diplomatic finesse, responded by mutilating the Mongol envoy — branding the man's face and sending him home with a message that any future demands would be answered the same way. This was one of those decisions that looks worse in retrospect than it did at the time.

In **1293**, a Yuan punitive expedition of perhaps 1,000 ships and 20,000 troops arrived in Java to deliver Kublai's response. The Mongol fleet was the largest naval force ever to enter Indonesian waters, before or arguably for several centuries afterward. By the time the Mongols arrived, however, Kertanagara had already been killed in a coup by a rival Javanese ruler named **Jayakatwang** (jah-yah-KAHT-wahng) of Kediri. The new ruler, **Raden Wijaya** (RAH-den wee-JAH-yah), had taken refuge in eastern Java and was looking for allies. He cleverly maneuvered the arriving Yuan fleet into supporting his bid for power, used Mongol troops to crush Jayakatwang, and then turned on the Mongols and drove them out of Java in a sequence of ambushes that killed several thousand Yuan soldiers.

The Yuan fleet returned to China in disgrace. Kublai Khan reportedly fired several officials in connection with the failed expedition. Raden Wijaya consolidated power and founded the **Majapahit** (MAH-jah-pah-hit) empire, which would dominate the archipelago for the next two centuries.

The Mongol expedition's impact on Srivijaya was indirect but significant. The collapse of Singhasari and the rise of Majapahit transferred Javanese political power into hands that would prove more aggressive than Singhasari had been. Majapahit's expansion plans included finishing the conquest of the surviving Srivijayan port-states. The 1293 Mongol expedition is, in this indirect way, one of the precipitating events of Srivijaya's final end.

---

A pause to reflect on what the long decline reveals about Srivijaya as a state.

The empire was extraordinarily durable as a commercial and cultural network. It survived the Chola raids of 1025 and 1068 with substantial damage but no collapse. It survived the gradual rise of Javanese power for two centuries. It survived the slow shift of religious orientation across the surrounding region. It even survived the Pamalayu Expedition's takeover of its Sumatran heartland in 1275. What it did not survive was the cumulative effect of all these pressures combined with its own structural weaknesses — the dependence on personal royal authority, the absence of a deep institutional bureaucracy, the vulnerability of its trade-based wealth to commercial diversion, and the difficulty of defending a thalassocratic empire against any combination of internal fragmentation and external rivals.

By 1300, Srivijaya was a memory more than a state. Some Srivijayan princely lineage continued to rule at Palembang under nominal Javanese suzerainty. Various surviving Sumatran port-states continued their commercial activity. The Buddhist establishment was fading. The Islamic alternative was rising. The Javanese were the dominant regional power, the Chinese Song dynasty had been replaced by the Mongol Yuan, and the world that had built Srivijaya in the seventh century was, eight centuries later, a world that no longer needed a Sumatran-Buddhist thalassocracy to coordinate its trade.

What remained was the formal end, which would come in 1377.

Cross-civ check: the long Srivijayan decline (1025–1293) overlapped with some of the most consequential developments in medieval Eurasian history. The **Chola** raid was contemporary with the **Norman Conquest** of England (1066). The **Pamalayu Expedition** of 1275 was contemporary with **Marco Polo**'s travels in China (1271–95) and with the consolidation of the **Mamluk** Sultanate in Egypt (Islamic Golden Age Ch 9). The **Mongol attack on Java** of 1293 happened the same decade the Mongols sacked Baghdad and ended the Abbasid Caliphate (Islamic Golden Age Ch 10). The Srivijayan world was deeply embedded in a connected medieval Eurasia even as its own political existence was unraveling.

---

# Chapter 8 — The End and the Inheritance

The formal end of Srivijaya came in **1377 CE** with the Majapahit conquest of Palembang. The dynasty that had ruled — in increasingly attenuated form — from the Musi River basin since the 680s was extinguished. What survived was different: a Malay-Muslim political and commercial culture that inherited Srivijaya's geography, its trading relationships, and its language, but rejected its religion and its dynastic identity. The story of Srivijaya's inheritance is the story of the early-modern Malay world.

The conquest itself was carried out under the great Majapahit prime minister **Gajah Mada** (GAH-jah MAH-dah, served roughly 1336–1364) and his successor, during the long reign of king **Hayam Wuruk** (HAH-yahm WOO-rook, ruled 1350–1389). Gajah Mada is famous in Indonesian historical memory for his **Palapa Oath**, sworn around 1336, in which he reportedly vowed to abstain from worldly pleasures until he had brought the entire Indonesian archipelago under Majapahit rule. The vow was largely symbolic, but the program of expansion it represented was real — Majapahit campaigns over the following decades extended Javanese authority across Sumatra, Borneo, the Lesser Sundas, and into the spice islands of the eastern Moluccas.

The 1377 conquest of Palembang was the formal endpoint of this expansion as it applied to Srivijaya. The Majapahit chronicle the *Nagarakretagama* (a long Old Javanese poem composed in 1365 by the court poet Mpu Prapanca) lists Palembang as one of Majapahit's recognized vassals, with the conquest itself recorded in later sources. After 1377, no independent Srivijayan polity existed. The Sumatran ports operated as Majapahit tributaries or as independent Muslim port-states; the old Buddhist royal lineage was effectively extinguished.

---

The fall of Palembang in 1377 was, however, not the actual end of the Srivijayan story. A more interesting end came twenty-three years later, in 1400, when a fugitive Palembang prince named **Parameswara** (PAH-rah-MEH-shvah-rah) — possibly the last surviving member of the Srivijayan royal family, possibly a related noble — fled the Majapahit-controlled city and, after a decade of wandering through Malay and Singaporean territory, established a new port-city at **Malacca** on the western coast of the Malay Peninsula.

Parameswara's new state was Srivijaya in everything but name. It sat at the same chokepoint — the Strait of Malacca — that the original empire had built its wealth around. It controlled the same strategic harbor functions that Srivijayan ports had specialized in. It managed the same merchant communities (Tamil, Chinese, Arab, Persian) that had thronged the old Srivijayan ports. And under Parameswara's son **Megat Iskandar Shah** (the title "Iskandar Shah" being a Persian-Islamic adaptation of Alexander the Great), the new state converted to Islam — a deliberate political choice that aligned the new sultanate with the rising Muslim trading powers of the Indian Ocean world.

The **Malacca Sultanate** (1400–1511) became the dominant trading and political power of fifteenth-century maritime Southeast Asia. Its diplomatic relationship with Ming China gave it international standing — including visits from the great **Zheng He** treasure fleets in the 1410s and 1420s, which formally recognized Malacca's sultan as a vassal of the Ming emperor. The sultanate's commercial wealth rivaled what Srivijaya had achieved at peak. Its Muslim religious infrastructure provided exactly the kind of cultural-legal framework that the broader Indonesian Islamization required.

The Malacca Sultanate was, in effect, Srivijaya 2.0 — same geography, same chokepoint logic, same cosmopolitan merchant culture, but with Islam in place of Buddhism and a different ruling lineage. The continuity is striking. From the seventh century to the sixteenth, the Strait of Malacca was the most important commercial chokepoint in maritime Southeast Asia, and from the 680s to the 1500s, with only brief interruptions, a single great trading state — Srivijaya, then later Malacca — controlled the customs regime that taxed it. The dynasties changed. The religion changed. The capital city changed. The function did not.

The Malacca Sultanate fell in 1511 to the Portuguese, in one of the early military exchanges of the European entry into the Indian Ocean trade. After 1511, the regional commercial and political pattern would shift again, with European empires (Portuguese, Dutch, English) gradually displacing the Asian trading states. But the geography of the Strait remained what it had always been, and the modern city of Singapore — founded by the British in 1819 at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula — would inherit, in turn, the same chokepoint role. Today, the Strait of Malacca is one of the busiest shipping lanes on Earth, with about a quarter of all globally traded goods passing through it. The geographic logic that made Srivijaya wealthy in the seventh century continues to make ships pay tolls in the twenty-first.

---

The Islamization of the archipelago continued through and beyond the end of Srivijaya, and it deserves a fuller treatment because it is the most consequential cultural transformation of the post-Srivijayan period.

Islam arrived in northern Sumatra at Pasai and Aceh in the late thirteenth century (Chapter 7). It spread south along the Sumatran coast over the following century. By 1400, much of the Sumatran coast was Muslim. The conversion of the Malacca sultans was one of the watershed moments — once the dominant trading state of the region became Muslim, Islam acquired the institutional infrastructure and political prestige needed for rapid further expansion. Islam reached central and eastern Java through the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, displacing the older Hindu-Buddhist religious establishment that had built Borobudur and Prambanan. The conversion of Java was particularly consequential because it brought Islam into the most populous and agriculturally rich part of the archipelago, transforming what had been a coastal-trading-elite religion into a mass faith.

The mechanism of conversion was largely peaceful and gradual. Sufi orders — the same kind of mystical brotherhood that would later produce the Safavid dynasty in Persia (Safavid Persia Ch 1) — were particularly important, providing accessible spiritual practice and pastoral authority that competed effectively with the older Hindu-Buddhist establishment. Royal conversions were typically followed by elite conversions, then by a slow century or two of conversion among the general population. There were occasional episodes of violence — particularly when local rulers resisted the political-religious shift — but the broad pattern was steady gradual displacement rather than dramatic forced conversion. The Indonesian Islamization is sometimes contrasted with the Safavid Iranian conversion of the early sixteenth century (Safavid Persia Ch 2), where the state used systematic violence to impose Twelver Shia Islam on an overwhelmingly Sunni population. The Indonesian process was less coercive and slower, but its eventual success was equally complete.

By 1700, the great majority of the Indonesian archipelago was Muslim, with significant Hindu remainders only on Bali (where the old Hindu-Buddhist culture survived in concentrated form) and in scattered upland communities. Modern Indonesia is the world's largest Muslim-majority country, with about 230 million Muslims — and the religious geography that produces that number is the long downstream consequence of the Islamization that began in the dying decades of Srivijaya.

---

The cultural inheritance of Srivijaya is more diffuse than the political one but in some ways more profound.

The first inheritance is **language**. Old Malay, the language of Srivijayan inscriptions and the trading lingua franca that the empire's ports had spread along the strait, evolved through Classical Malay into the modern Malay and Indonesian languages. Modern Malay is the official language of Malaysia, Brunei, and Singapore; modern Indonesian (called Bahasa Indonesia) is the national language of Indonesia. Together they have more than 300 million speakers. The success of Malay as the lingua franca of maritime Southeast Asia — adopted as the national language of Indonesia in 1928 partly because it was already the regional trading language and was not the native language of any single major ethnic group — is one of the most demographically successful linguistic legacies of any pre-modern empire. The trading language of Srivijayan ports became, a thousand years later, the unifying language of two large modern nation-states.

The second inheritance is the **maritime trade tradition**. The Malacca Sultanate, and after it the Aceh Sultanate, the various Muslim sultanates of Java and the Spice Islands, and eventually the modern Indonesian and Malaysian states, all inherited the basic commercial geography that Srivijaya had defined. The Strait of Malacca remained the central artery; the islands remained the producers of spices and forest products; the cosmopolitan merchant culture of the ports remained the institutional matrix in which long-distance trade happened. Modern Singapore's role as the Strait's dominant port is the latest iteration of a function that Palembang first defined in the seventh century.

The third inheritance is the **Buddhist heritage**, now mostly preserved in Java rather than in Sumatra. Borobudur and Prambanan, the great Sailendra-era monuments, survived the Islamization of Java because they were located in the rural interior rather than in major urban centers, and because by the time Islam arrived they had already been partially abandoned and overgrown. Borobudur was rediscovered by Western archaeologists in 1814 (under the brief British administration of Java during the Napoleonic Wars) and gradually restored over the following two centuries. It is now a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most-visited cultural monuments in Indonesia — even though modern Indonesia is overwhelmingly Muslim. The Sailendra-era Buddhist culture that produced Borobudur was an offshoot or close cousin of Srivijayan Buddhism, and the surviving Javanese monuments are the most visible physical inheritance of that culture. To visit Borobudur is, in a real sense, to encounter the Srivijayan religious world — even though Srivijaya itself produced no monument to compare.

---

A pause to address the strangeness of Srivijaya's modern status as a "lost civilization."

Until 1918, no scholar outside Asia had a coherent picture of what Srivijaya had been. Chinese sources mentioned a kingdom called Sanfoqi or Foshi. Tamil inscriptions mentioned a kingdom whose king Rajendra Chola had captured. Arab geographers wrote about a fabulously wealthy Maharaja of Zabag. Old Malay inscriptions mentioned a kingdom of Srivijaya. Tibetan Buddhist hagiographies mentioned a teacher named Dharmakirti who had taught Atisha at a place called Suvarnadvipa. Each of these traditions referred to the same empire, but no one had connected the references. The relevant texts were in different languages, scattered across different scholarly disciplines, and not generally read by the same people.

In 1918, the French Indologist **George Coedes** (zhorzh KOH-dez, 1886–1969), then working at the École française d'Extrême-Orient in Hanoi, published a paper in the *Bulletin* of the school that pulled the threads together. The Srivijaya of the Old Malay inscriptions, the Sanfoqi of the Chinese sources, the kingdom Rajendra had attacked, the Zabag of the Arabs, and the Suvarnadvipa where Atisha had studied were all the same place. Coedes had effectively reconstructed an entire major medieval empire from scattered references. His paper opened a half-century of follow-up scholarship that gradually filled in the empire's political, religious, and commercial outlines. By the 1960s, when Coedes published his synthetic *Indianized States of Southeast Asia*, Srivijaya was a recognized major civilization with its own scholarly literature.

But the empire's modern recovery did not produce the kind of physical reconstruction that, say, the recovery of Babylon or Pompeii produced. There was no buried capital to dig up. Palembang had been built almost entirely of perishable timber and bamboo, and the wet tropical environment had reduced almost everything to organic mud. Excavations at Palembang have produced some artifacts — pottery sherds, gold ornaments, a few Buddhist sculptures — but no temples, no royal palaces, no foundations of monumental architecture comparable to what survives at almost every other major medieval imperial capital. Srivijaya remains a textual and inscriptional reconstruction. The empire's physical traces are sparse, scattered, and modest. Most modern visitors to Palembang see a busy modern Indonesian city with very little visible connection to its ancient role. The Srivijayan past is, even after a century of scholarly recovery, mostly invisible on the ground.

This is what makes Srivijaya genuinely one of the world's "lost civilizations" — not lost in the sense of being unknown (modern scholarship knows it exists and broadly what it did) but lost in the sense of being almost entirely absent from its own former territory. The empire's fingerprints are clearer in foreign sources than in domestic remains. To learn about Srivijaya, you go to Chinese imperial archives, Tamil temple inscriptions, Tibetan monastic hagiographies, and Arabic geographical treatises — not to surviving Srivijayan ruins. The empire that ruled the most important trading chokepoint in medieval Asia for seven centuries left almost nothing material behind. The Buddhist scholarship that connected the religious worlds of India, China, and Tibet survives in the Tibetan and Chinese transmission lineages but not in any local Srivijayan textual tradition. The cosmopolitan commercial culture of the ports survives in the modern multilingual maritime trade of Southeast Asia but not in any direct documentary record. Srivijaya is a ghost empire — present in its consequences, almost absent in its remains.

---

A final cross-civ comparison. The pattern of Srivijaya — a great medieval trading state that left limited physical remains and was forgotten until modern scholarship reconstructed it — has a striking parallel in the West African empire of **Mali** (Mali Empire), which similarly grew rich on long-distance trade (the trans-Saharan gold trade), produced a great medieval Muslim political and intellectual culture, and then declined in ways that left modern observers puzzling over fragmentary inscriptions and references in foreign sources. Mali's capital at Niani has been excavated with results almost as modest as Palembang's. Mali, like Srivijaya, is best known through outside accounts — particularly the Arab traveler Ibn Battuta's visit in 1352 — rather than through its own internal records. The two empires did not interact directly (they were on opposite sides of the Indian Ocean trade network, and most of their trade with each other ran through multiple intermediaries) but they were structurally analogous: medieval trading states whose modern reputation depends more on what foreigners wrote about them than on what they wrote about themselves. The pattern is a useful reminder that the "lost civilization" phenomenon is not unique to Southeast Asia. It is what happens to medieval states that built their wealth on trade and their cultural infrastructure on perishable materials, and that did not leave behind the kind of stone-and-text record that European medieval kingdoms (or, for that matter, Borobudur-builders in Java) typically did.

The Indian Ocean world Srivijaya helped to weave together is, in its current iteration, larger than ever — bigger ships, more containers, more globally distributed cargoes. The chokepoint geography of the Strait of Malacca still functions exactly as it did in the seventh century. The Malay language still ties together the maritime communities of insular Southeast Asia. The Buddhist intellectual lineage still runs from Atisha's Sumatran teacher through Tibet to the present-day Dalai Lama's monastery. The world Srivijaya built is, in many of its consequences, still here. The empire itself is gone — left as little more than a name in a French scholar's 1918 paper, a few stone inscriptions on Sumatran hillsides, a great Buddhist temple in central Java built by allies it never quite controlled, and the ghost of a port at Palembang where almost nothing of the original city survives. For an empire that controlled Asia's wealthiest trade route for seven centuries, that is a small physical inheritance. The institutional, linguistic, and religious inheritance is much larger.

That, in the end, is the point of telling the story. Srivijaya matters not because its monuments dazzle the modern visitor — they don't — but because the world it shaped is still the world. The trading chokepoint, the multilingual maritime culture, the Buddhist transmission line, the commercial wealth flowing from Asia to Europe along sea routes that pre-modern empires controlled by taxing the chokepoints — all of this began with the Sumatran river port that Dapunta Hyang Sri Jayanasa founded in the 680s. The empire is a ghost. The world it made is alive.

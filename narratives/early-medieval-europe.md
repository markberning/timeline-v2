# Early Medieval Europe — From the Fall to the Year 1000

**Goal:** Narrative for the early medieval west (476–1000 CE), the long crucible in which a Roman world dissolved into Germanic kingdoms, a Frankish chieftain named Charlemagne briefly reassembled a Roman-shaped empire on Christmas Day 800, and the 9th-century triple shock of Vikings, Magyars, and Saracens forced the continent to invent the castle, the mounted knight, and the proto-feudal contract. This is the period nineteenth-century writers tarred as the "Dark Ages." It was not dark. It was formative — the half-millennium in which almost every modern European nation was set on its trajectory.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

The early medieval west was a documentary thin spot, not as thin as Sumerian prehistory but thinner than what came on either side. Every claim about a "first castle" or a "first Romance text" is shadowed by what may not have survived.

A note on dispelling a few popular myths up front: the Catholic Church never said the earth was flat. Bede in 8th-century Northumbria taught that it was a sphere, and so did every educated medieval European thereafter. The "Dark Ages" label was invented by the Italian humanist Petrarch in the fourteenth century to insult the centuries between him and classical Rome; it tells you about Petrarch, not about the period. Average lifespan was not 30 — that was the average across all births, dragged down by terrible infant mortality; an early medieval European who survived to age 20 had a fair chance of reaching 60. # Chapter 1 — The Fall and the Migration Period

The Western Roman Empire ended on a specific day. The day was September 4, 476. The Germanic general **Odoacer** (OH-doh-AY-ser, of probable Scirian or Hunnic background) walked into the imperial palace at Ravenna, deposed a teenage emperor named **Romulus Augustulus** (ROM-yoo-lus aw-GUS-tyoo-lus, whose name contained both Rome's legendary founder and its first emperor — an irony that even contemporaries noticed), exiled him to a country villa in Campania on a generous pension, and sent the imperial regalia to Constantinople. Odoacer informed the eastern emperor **Zeno** that the western half of the empire no longer needed an emperor of its own. He, Odoacer, would govern Italy as king on Constantinople's behalf.

It is the most anticlimactic ending in world history. There was no battle. There was no last stand. There was no burning of the city. There was a man with too many soldiers walking into a palace full of paperwork and informing a teenager that his services were no longer required. Romulus Augustulus survived for years in genteel retirement and we don't know exactly when he died.

This is the conventional end of antiquity and the conventional start of the Middle Ages. (Ancient Rome Ch 10 covers the same event from the Roman side and explains the eighty-year cascade of catastrophes that preceded it.) The actual texture of the moment, though, was not ending. It was continuing-under-new-management. The Roman Senate continued to meet. The Roman bureaucracy continued to function. The bishops of the Catholic Church continued to administer their dioceses. Latin continued to be the language of literacy across the former Western provinces. The aqueducts continued to bring water into cities that continued to be called by their Roman names. The change was real and it was profound, but it was not the kind of change that announces itself with trumpets. It was the kind that you only see clearly two or three centuries later, when you look back and realize that the imperial postal service has stopped running, the senatorial families have intermarried with Gothic dukes, the cities have shrunk to a third of their former population, and somebody is plowing a field across what used to be the forum.

This chapter is about the long century after 476 — the **Migration Period** (German *Völkerwanderung*, "wandering of peoples") in which the Germanic confederations that had been pushing across the Roman frontier for two centuries finally settled down, subdivided the western provinces among themselves, and started building the kingdoms that would eventually become the medieval European states.

---

The Germanic peoples who took over the West were not a single group. They were a collection of tribal confederations speaking related but distinct Germanic languages (the same language family that would eventually produce English, German, Dutch, the Scandinavian languages, and Yiddish), arriving on the western Roman frontier from different directions over roughly two centuries.

A short roster, because the names matter:

The **Visigoths** (VIH-zee-goths, "Western Goths") had crossed the Danube into Roman territory as refugees in 376, defeated the eastern emperor Valens at Adrianople in 378, sacked Rome in 410, and eventually settled in southern Gaul and Spain, where they founded a Visigothic kingdom that lasted until the Arab conquest of 711.

The **Ostrogoths** (OS-troh-goths, "Eastern Goths") followed somewhat later and ended up in Italy, where their king Theodoric ruled from 493 to 526.

The **Vandals** (the same Germanic word that survives in our "Vandalism" — though the connection is a 17th-century coinage, not a fair description of the actual Vandals) crossed the Rhine in 406, marched through Gaul, swept down through Spain, crossed to North Africa in 429, captured Carthage in 439, and built a Vandal kingdom in the richest province of the former Western Empire that controlled the western Mediterranean grain supply for nearly a century.

The **Franks** (the people who would eventually give France its name) settled in northern Gaul. They were less mobile than the others — they expanded gradually from a homeland on the lower Rhine rather than migrating across the entire continent — and they would prove to be the most important of all.

The **Burgundians** settled in the upper Rhône valley and gave their name to the region of Burgundy.

The **Anglo-Saxons** — a confederation of Angles, Saxons, and Jutes from the North Sea coast of modern Germany and Denmark — crossed to Britain in the fifth century, displaced the Romano-British inhabitants, and founded the small kingdoms that would eventually become England.

The **Lombards** (Latin *Langobardi*, "Long-Beards") arrived last, crossing the Alps into Italy in 568 — almost a century after Romulus Augustulus's deposition — and undoing most of what Justinian's reconquest had achieved (Chapter 2).

These are not, despite what older textbooks sometimes implied, separate races physically distinguishable from Romans. The Germanic peoples had been in contact with Rome for centuries, often serving in the Roman army, intermarrying with the provincial population, adopting Roman material culture, and learning Latin alongside their own languages. By the late fifth century the visible difference between a "barbarian" and a "Roman" was sometimes a haircut, sometimes a religion (most Germanic peoples were Arian Christians at this point, while the Roman provincial population was largely Catholic), sometimes a name, sometimes nothing visible at all. The people who replaced the Roman state were thoroughly Romanized. They had been the Roman state's army for a generation before they took it over.

---

The single most consequential decision of the entire migration period was made in the small Frankish town of Reims around 496. The **Frankish** king **Clovis I** (KLO-vis, the Latinized form of *Chlodovech* — the same name eventually softened in modern French to *Louis*, which is why French kings would carry that name into the eighteenth century) accepted Catholic baptism from the bishop of Reims, **Remigius**.

This was a choice. The other major Germanic peoples — the Visigoths, the Ostrogoths, the Vandals, the Burgundians — were already Christian, but they were **Arian** Christians. Arianism was a fourth-century theological position, named after the Alexandrian priest Arius, that held Christ to be a created being, subordinate to God the Father, not co-eternal with Him. The Council of Nicaea in 325 had condemned Arianism as a heresy and adopted the **Nicene Creed**, which declares Christ "of one substance with the Father." But Arianism had survived and prospered among the Goths in particular, who had been converted to Christianity in the fourth century by an Arian missionary bishop named **Ulfilas** before the Nicene victory had become decisive on the frontier. When the Gothic peoples migrated west, they brought their Arian Christianity with them.

Clovis was a pagan when he came to the Frankish throne in 481. The Franks had not yet been Christianized at all; they still worshipped the old Germanic gods (the same gods we will meet again in Chapter 7 as the Norse pantheon — Wodan, Donar, Frigg, the lot). The story preserved by the Frankish chronicler **Gregory of Tours** has Clovis vowing to accept the Christian God if a particular battle went his way. The battle went his way. He was baptized along with three thousand of his warriors at Reims, on Christmas Day 496 (or 508 — the chronology is contested).

The choice that mattered, though, was not Christianity vs. paganism. It was Catholicism vs. Arianism. By choosing Nicene Catholicism — the religion of the Gallo-Roman bishops, the Roman provincial population, and the Eastern Roman Empire that still claimed nominal sovereignty in the West — Clovis aligned the Frankish monarchy with the entire surviving Roman religious establishment. Every Catholic bishop in Gaul became, in effect, a partisan of the Frankish king. The Arian Visigoths and Ostrogoths were heretics in the eyes of their Catholic Roman subjects, which would always be a structural weakness. The Frankish kings were not. When Clovis defeated the Visigothic king Alaric II at the Battle of **Vouillé** (voo-YAY) in 507 and pushed the Visigoths back across the Pyrenees, the Catholic Gallo-Roman population of southern Gaul welcomed him as a liberator from heretical rule.

The decision to be Catholic rather than Arian is one of the great hinges of European history. Without it, Francia would have been a heretic kingdom — a hostile religious enclave inside Catholic Christendom — rather than the eventual political backbone of Latin Europe. The Carolingian-papal alliance of the eighth century (Chapter 4), Charlemagne's coronation as Roman emperor (Chapter 5), the entire medieval entanglement of French royal power with the institutional church — all of it traces back to a Frankish warlord's calculated decision to accept the religion of his Roman subjects in 496.

Clovis ruled until 511 and founded the **Merovingian** (mer-oh-VIN-jee-an, named after a legendary ancestor Merovech) dynasty that would govern Francia for the next 250 years. The Merovingians were not an impressive lot of rulers, on the whole. They had a chronic tendency to die early (palace politics, dynastic struggle, occasional violence at the dinner table), and the kingdom was repeatedly subdivided among multiple sons, leading to civil wars among brother-kings. By the seventh century the Merovingians had become **rois fainéants** — French for "do-nothing kings" — figureheads over a state actually run by hereditary chief stewards called **mayors of the palace**. By the early eighth century the mayors were the de facto kings of Francia (Chapter 4).

But Clovis's choice held. The Frankish kingdom remained Catholic. And it remained the kingdom in the West with the closest religious-political alignment to surviving Roman institutions.

---

While the Franks were settling into northern Gaul, **Italy** went through its own version of the same process under a king who deserves more attention than he usually gets.

**Theodoric the Great** (THEE-oh-dor-ik, ruled 493–526) was an Ostrogothic king who had grown up as a hostage at the imperial court in Constantinople. He spoke Greek, had been educated in Roman literature, and understood the eastern Roman political world from the inside. In 488, the eastern emperor Zeno — the same Zeno who had received Romulus Augustulus's regalia twelve years earlier — sent Theodoric and his Ostrogoths west with a commission: dispose of Odoacer, take over Italy, and rule it as the empire's deputy. This solved two problems at once: it removed the troublesome Ostrogoths from Constantinople's borders, and it brought Italy back under nominal imperial authority.

Theodoric did the job. In 493 he murdered Odoacer at a banquet in Ravenna (the chronicle says Theodoric himself swung the sword and split Odoacer in half, though the detail is suspect) and assumed the kingship of Italy. He then proceeded to govern the country for thirty-three years with a sophistication and lightness of touch that no later medieval ruler would match for centuries.

Theodoric's Italy was a curious hybrid. He kept the entire Roman senatorial bureaucracy in operation — the same officials who had administered the late western empire continued to administer it under Theodoric. He kept the Roman Senate, gave it ceremonial honors, and consulted it (more or less) on important questions. He kept the Roman law for his Roman subjects and used Gothic law for his Gothic ones — a "personal law" system in which your legal code depended on your ethnicity rather than your geography. He minted coins with his portrait on the reverse and the eastern emperor's portrait on the obverse, advertising the constitutional fiction that he ruled as Constantinople's appointee. He sponsored the rebuilding of Roman public buildings — the famous Mausoleum of Theodoric in Ravenna, with its enormous monolithic dome carved from a single block of Istrian limestone, still stands.

He employed two of the great late-antique intellectuals as his ministers. **Boethius** (boh-EE-thee-us, c. 480–524), a Roman aristocrat who had translated Aristotle into Latin and written treatises on arithmetic and music, served as Master of Offices at Theodoric's court. **Cassiodorus** (kass-ee-oh-DOR-us, c. 485–585), another Roman senator, served as Theodoric's secretary, wrote the official correspondence (which survives — the *Variae* — and is one of the great primary sources for Ostrogothic Italy), and was eventually placed in charge of Roman law in the kingdom.

But Theodoric's Italy had a structural problem. He was an Arian Christian; his Roman subjects were Catholic. As long as relations between the kingdom and Constantinople were good, the religious gap could be papered over. When relations broke down — Constantinople began to suppress Arianism inside the eastern empire in the early 520s — Theodoric's situation became precarious. He grew paranoid. He convinced himself that the Roman senatorial class was conspiring with Constantinople against him. In 523 he had Boethius arrested on charges of treason and corresponding with the eastern emperor. He had Boethius's father-in-law, the senator Symmachus, arrested with him.

Boethius was executed in 524 after months in prison. While awaiting execution he wrote **The Consolation of Philosophy** — a dialogue in mixed prose and verse between himself and the personification of Philosophy, who consoles him by walking him through the classical doctrines about fortune, providence, the nature of evil, and the relationship of human freedom to divine foreknowledge. The book is the deepest statement of late-antique philosophical synthesis between Plato, Aristotle, the Stoics, and Christian theology. It would become the most widely read philosophical work of the entire Middle Ages, translated into every European vernacular — including Old English by King Alfred himself in the late ninth century (Chapter 7), and Middle English by Geoffrey Chaucer in the late fourteenth.

Theodoric died in 526, two years after Boethius's execution. His Ostrogothic kingdom would survive him by less than three decades before Justinian's eastern Roman armies destroyed it (Chapter 2). But the Italy he had governed — Roman bureaucracy, Roman law, Roman cities, Catholic bishops, classical learning still alive at the level of high culture — was the closest thing to a successful late-antique synthesis between the old empire and the new Germanic rulers. It was also a preview of what the early medieval west might have looked like if the eastern Roman reconquest and the Lombard counter-invasion had not turned Italy back into a war zone for two centuries.

---

The Roman senator and minister **Cassiodorus**, who had served Theodoric and lived through the catastrophe of Justinian's wars in Italy, retired around 540 to his family estate in southern Italy and founded a monastery there called **Vivarium** (vih-VAH-ree-um, "fish-pond," after the artificial fishponds on the property). What he did at Vivarium matters out of all proportion to its size or its survival.

Cassiodorus had watched the secular Roman intellectual establishment collapse — the senatorial families dying out or fleeing, the public schools closing, the government chancery shrinking, the supply of new educated men drying up. He realized that if classical Latin literature was going to survive at all, it would have to survive inside the church, in the only institution that still had a continuous reason to keep teaching young men to read Latin. So at Vivarium he set up a deliberately learned monastic program: monks would copy not just biblical and patristic Christian texts but also pagan classical works — Cicero, Virgil, Horace, the technical writers on astronomy and grammar — on the principle that these were necessary for a properly educated reader of scripture.

He wrote a handbook for the program called the *Institutions of Divine and Secular Learning*, which became the model for medieval monastic education. The principle that monasteries should preserve the classical literary heritage — not just Christian texts — entered the medieval educational tradition through Cassiodorus's example, and it would become the institutional reason why most surviving Latin classical literature exists at all.

Vivarium itself did not survive; we don't know exactly when it closed. But the model survived. Two centuries later the Carolingian scholars at Aachen would adopt exactly Cassiodorus's principle and apply it on an empire-wide scale (Chapter 5). And nine hundred years later, when Italian Renaissance humanists like Petrarch went hunting for lost classical manuscripts, the books they found in monastic libraries had survived because Cassiodorus and his successors had put the right idea into the right institution at the right moment. The thread is that direct.

---

The other essential figure of the Migration Period in the West is the bishop. **Bishops** are the social load-bearing structure of late antiquity in a way that is hard to overstate.

The Roman empire had organized itself, by the fourth century, around a network of city-based bishoprics. Each major city had a bishop; the bishop had a permanent cathedral, a permanent staff, permanent property, and direct authority over the Christian community in his diocese. When the western imperial government dissolved in the fifth century, the bishops did not. They had landed property that nobody could easily take from them. They had the loyalty of the urban population. They had Latin literacy and administrative experience. And they had the moral standing to negotiate with the new Germanic kings, who needed someone with social authority to talk to.

Across western Europe in the fifth and sixth centuries, the bishop becomes the principal figure of urban government. In Gaul, bishops ransom captives from raiders, organize famine relief, build churches and hospitals, and manage relations between the surviving Gallo-Roman population and the new Frankish overlords. In Spain, bishops convoke councils that issue both religious and civil legislation. In Italy, the bishop of Rome — increasingly called the **pope** (from Greek *pappas*, "father") — emerges as the de facto civil authority of the city after the imperial administration leaves. The institutional form of the medieval west is taking shape: a continent of small kingdoms, each with a king at the top and a network of bishops alongside him, with the bishops forming the connective tissue between the kingdoms because they all answer (more or less) to Rome.

We will pick up the popes properly in Chapter 3, with Gregory the Great. The point for now is that when historians say "the Catholic Church inherited the Roman Empire," they don't mean the church became a state. They mean that the Roman administrative system — the dioceses, the bureaucracy, the literate Latin record-keeping, the city-based civil authority — kept running inside the church after it stopped running outside it.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Western Roman Empire was dissolving into Germanic kingdoms in the fifth and early sixth centuries, the **Eastern Roman Empire** (Byzantine Empire Ch 1) was thriving — Justinian was about to come to the throne, the Hagia Sophia was about to be built, and the Roman state was about to attempt the western reconquest that Chapter 2 covers. In **Persia**, the **Sasanian Empire** under the Sasanid kings was at the height of its rivalry with Byzantium, with the long Roman-Persian wars devouring resources on both sides (Persian Empire Ch 9). In **India**, the **Gupta Empire**'s classical period was tapering off into the regional kingdoms that would dominate the subcontinent for centuries (Gupta Empire). In **China**, the **Six Dynasties** period had divided the country between rival northern and southern regimes — China would not be reunified until the Sui in 581 (Six Dynasties). In **Mesoamerica**, the great pyramid-city of **Teotihuacan** was at or near its peak (Teotihuacan), and the Classic **Maya** city-states of the Yucatán were entering their golden age (Maya Civilization Ch 4–6). The world that the early medieval west was joining was one in which it was, for the first time in five hundred years, a peripheral civilization rather than a dominant one. It would take six centuries to climb back.

# Chapter 2 — Justinian's Reconquest and the Plague

The story you usually get about the early Middle Ages skips Justinian. This is a mistake. The eastern emperor **Justinian I** (jus-TIN-ee-un, ruled 527–565) is the principal reason the early medieval west looks the way it does — not because of what he built, but because of what his attempted reconquest broke and what the plague that arrived during his reign did to everything else.

Byzantine Empire Ch 2 covers Justinian properly from the Constantinopolitan side. This chapter covers what his reign meant for the western half of the former Roman world.

---

Justinian came to the eastern throne in 527 with an enormous ambition and a remarkable team. The ambition was the **renovatio imperii**, the "renovation of empire" — the reunification of the eastern and western Roman worlds under a single emperor at Constantinople, recovering the lost provinces of the West that had been overrun by Germanic kingdoms in the previous century. The team consisted of his wife **Theodora** (a former actress and possibly former prostitute who had become his closest political partner and the most formidable empress in Roman history), his commander **Belisarius** (bel-ih-SAH-ree-us, the great general who would conduct the western reconquest), his jurist **Tribonian** (who would codify Roman law), and his architects **Anthemius of Tralles** and **Isidore of Miletus** (who would build Hagia Sophia).

Two of these projects were spectacular successes. The third was a disaster.

The legal codification was the spectacular success that mattered most for the long term. Tribonian's commission compiled the entire surviving body of Roman law — fifteen centuries of imperial decrees, juristic opinions, and case-law — into a single coherent corpus between 529 and 534. The result was the **Corpus Juris Civilis** ("Body of Civil Law"), in four parts: the Code (imperial legislation), the Digest (juristic writings), the Institutes (a textbook for first-year law students), and the Novels (Justinian's own new laws). The Corpus codified Roman jurisprudence at a level of sophistication that would not be matched anywhere in Europe for a thousand years. It was promptly forgotten in the West (the Germanic kingdoms had their own Germanic law codes), survived intact in the East, and was rediscovered at Bologna around 1070 — at which point it triggered the rebirth of European legal scholarship and became the foundational text of every continental European legal system. The legal code of modern France, Germany, Italy, Spain, and Latin America descends, by an unbroken chain of commentary, from the work Tribonian did in the 530s.

The architectural success was Hagia Sophia. Anthemius and Isidore built the **Church of the Holy Wisdom** in Constantinople in just five years, dedicating it on December 27, 537. Its 105-foot dome on pendentives (a structural device that lets a circular dome rest on a square base) was the largest enclosed space in the world for nearly a thousand years. Justinian is supposed to have walked into the finished building on its dedication day and exclaimed "Solomon, I have surpassed thee" — referring to the Temple of Jerusalem. The line is almost certainly invented, but it captures what he was doing. Hagia Sophia is the architectural statement that the Roman Empire is not over, that its capital is now Constantinople, and that imperial Christianity can still build at a scale nothing in the West can rival. The building still stands.

The third project — the western reconquest — was the disaster.

---

Justinian's reconquest started brilliantly. **Belisarius** sailed for North Africa in 533 with an expeditionary force of perhaps 18,000 men and destroyed the Vandal Kingdom in a single campaigning season. Carthage fell almost without resistance. The Vandal king Gelimer surrendered. The richest grain-producing province of the former western empire was back under Roman rule. Belisarius returned to Constantinople in 534 to celebrate a triumph — the first formal Roman triumph since the early empire — with the Vandal royal treasury dragged through the streets of Constantinople behind him.

Then he was sent to Italy. The Italian campaign opened equally promisingly: in 535 Belisarius captured Sicily, in 536 he took Naples, in 536 he occupied Rome unopposed, and in 540 he accepted the surrender of Ravenna. The Ostrogothic kingdom of Italy seemed to have collapsed.

It hadn't. The Goths regrouped under a new king, **Totila**, who recovered most of Italy in the 540s. Belisarius was recalled to Constantinople and Justinian's resources were stretched thin. A second eastern Roman commander, the eunuch **Narses**, was eventually sent west with a larger army and finally crushed the Goths at the Battle of **Taginae** (552) and again at **Mons Lactarius** (553). Italy, after twenty years of intermittent warfare, was nominally back under Roman rule.

But the campaigns had wrecked the country. Italy in 535 had been the seat of Theodoric's sophisticated Ostrogothic kingdom — Roman cities still functioning, classical buildings still standing, Latin literacy still alive. Italy in 555 was a war zone. The cities had been besieged repeatedly, sometimes by both sides in succession. Rome itself had been besieged five times during the Gothic War, with the population dropping from perhaps 800,000 in 400 to perhaps 30,000 in 600. The aqueducts had been cut by besieging armies and never properly repaired. The senatorial families had been killed, dispersed, or impoverished. The agricultural countryside had been devastated. The recovery Justinian's armies had achieved was a recovery only in the technical sense that the territory was now in imperial hands — there was very little left of what the territory had previously been.

A third campaign in the western Mediterranean recovered a strip of southern Spain from the Visigoths in 552. This was Justinian's high-water mark. The Mediterranean was once again, just barely, a Roman sea.

It would not stay one for long.

---

In 541, while Belisarius was being recalled from Italy and Justinian was negotiating with the Goths, the bubonic plague arrived in **Pelusium** in Egypt, almost certainly carried by ship from East Africa or India along the Red Sea trade routes. By 542 it had reached Constantinople. Within a few years it had spread across the entire Mediterranean basin and as far north as Britain. The first wave killed an estimated quarter to a third of the eastern Mediterranean's population — perhaps 25 to 50 million people — over the course of a few terrible years. Subsequent waves returned at roughly fifteen-year intervals through the rest of the sixth century and into the seventh, killing more.

This was the **Plague of Justinian**, the first of the great Eurasian bubonic plague pandemics. The same disease (*Yersinia pestis*) and the same vector (rat fleas spreading from infected rodents to humans) would return as the **Black Death** in 1347 and kill another third of Europe (high-medieval-europe, late-medieval-europe). But the Plague of Justinian came first, hit a Mediterranean world already destabilized by Justinian's wars, and inflicted demographic damage that took centuries to repair. The contemporary historian **Procopius** (proh-KOH-pee-us, who served as Belisarius's secretary on the western campaigns and is the principal source for the period) wrote a horrified description of Constantinople during the plague's first wave: 5,000 to 10,000 people dying per day at the peak, bodies stacked in the streets because there was no one left to bury them, the imperial guards detailed to fling corpses into mass graves, the city emptied of normal commerce and worship.

The plague did things no other event of the period did. It crashed the eastern empire's tax base by killing the taxpayers. It crippled the army by killing the soldiers and the men of military age. It hollowed out the cities by killing the urban populations who paid for and lived in them. It destroyed Justinian's ability to consolidate his western conquests — there were no longer the men or the money to hold what Belisarius and Narses had taken. And it set up the eastern empire for the catastrophes of the seventh century: the Persian invasion of 602, the Arab conquests of 632–650 (Byzantine Empire Ch 3), the loss of Syria, Egypt, and most of North Africa to the new Islamic caliphate, the reduction of the Eastern Empire from a Mediterranean superpower to a beleaguered Anatolian-Balkan state.

For the western Mediterranean, the plague meant that whatever Justinian's armies had done to recover would not be sustained. The men were not there to garrison Italy. The money was not there to rebuild it. The administrative infrastructure was not there to absorb it. When the next wave of Germanic invaders arrived in 568 — the **Lombards** crossing the Alps into northern Italy — there was almost no one to stop them.

The Plague of Justinian is one of the structural reasons the early medieval west looks the way it does. The classical world had survived the fall of the Western Empire in 476 with most of its institutional substance intact under new management. It did not survive the plague. The half-century from 540 to 590 is the moment when late antiquity ends and the early Middle Ages begin, and the trigger is bubonic.

---

The **Lombards** (Latin *Langobardi*, "Long-Beards," from a tribal legend about the god Wotan playing favorites in a battle when the women of the tribe combed their hair forward over their faces and the god mistook them for additional warriors) were the last Germanic people to invade the former Western Empire. They had been moving for centuries from somewhere in southern Scandinavia, through the Hungarian plain, down toward Italy. In April 568 their king **Alboin** crossed the Alps with an army that included Lombards, Saxons, and a substantial number of Pannonian followers, and within four years they held everything in northern Italy north of the Po valley plus most of the central Italian highlands.

The Lombards left the eastern Romans (the **Byzantines** — for clarity I will start using the modern term from here onward, recognizing it is anachronistic; the people themselves continued to call themselves Romans) in possession of Ravenna, the corridor running across central Italy through Rome, and the southern coastal strip and Sicily. The political map of medieval Italy that resulted — Lombard duchies in the north, papal Rome in the center, Byzantine pockets along the coasts — would persist for two centuries and shape Italian politics into the present.

The Lombards eventually converted from Arianism to Catholicism (the process took until the seventh century) and produced the most extensive Germanic law code of the period: the **Edictum Rothari** in 643, codifying Lombard customary law in 388 numbered chapters in Latin. They built a kingdom centered on the city of **Pavia** (PAH-vee-ah). They gave their name to **Lombardy**. They were the people Charlemagne would conquer in 774 (Chapter 5).

For the moment, what matters is that the Lombard invasion completed the unraveling of Justinian's reconquest. The Vandals had been destroyed and the African province kept (it would be lost to the Arabs in the 690s instead). The Visigoths had been pushed back into Spain. The Ostrogoths had been crushed. But Italy — the ostensible prize of the western reconquest — was now in the hands of yet another Germanic kingdom, this time one that the eastern empire had no resources to dislodge.

By 600, the Mediterranean was no longer a Roman sea. It would not be again. Justinian's reconquest was the last serious attempt to restore the political unity of the late-antique world. The next emperor to even attempt something on a comparable scale — Charlemagne in 800 — would attempt it from the wrong end of the Mediterranean, on a much smaller scale, and explicitly as an outsider claiming the inheritance.

---

What happened to the cities is worth pausing on, because the urban collapse of the sixth and seventh centuries is one of the structural facts of the early medieval west, and most modern readers underestimate it.

A late-Roman city of 50,000–100,000 people was a substantial place: forum, basilica, theater, baths, aqueducts, paved streets, multi-story masonry buildings, public food distribution, regular trade. By 700 most of these cities had shrunk to villages of 1,000–5,000 people, often relocated from the original Roman site to a more defensible hilltop nearby, with the old urban infrastructure crumbling or actively dismantled for building stone. The aqueducts stopped functioning because there was no one with the engineering knowledge to maintain them and no one with the political authority to fund them. The baths closed. The theaters closed. The public food distribution stopped. The economy ruralized. Long-distance trade survived in luxury goods (silk, spices, slaves, swords) but the volume bulk-trade in olive oil, wine, and grain that had moved across the Mediterranean for centuries dropped to a fraction of late-Roman levels — with measurable consequences in the archaeological record (fewer amphorae, fewer shipwrecks, fewer coins struck).

This is the classic "Pirenne Thesis" image of the early medieval west — the Belgian historian Henri Pirenne argued in the 1930s that it was the Arab conquests of the seventh century that broke Mediterranean trade and produced the deep ruralization of the West. Modern archaeology suggests Pirenne was partly right and partly overdramatic: the trade collapse was real, but it had begun in the sixth century with the plague and the Italian wars, not the seventh with the Arabs. Either way, the urban infrastructure of the late Roman west did not survive.

What survived instead were two things: the **church**, in the persons of bishops who continued to occupy the old Roman cities even after the cities had shrunk to a fraction of their former size; and the **monastery**, a new institution we will meet properly in Chapter 3 that was just becoming a major social presence in the West. The monastery especially would carry the late-antique intellectual and administrative tradition into the early medieval centuries. Without it, the early Middle Ages would look much darker than they actually do.

---

One more thing happened during Justinian's reign that turned out to matter, though nobody at the time saw it.

In 552 — the same year Narses crushed the Goths at Taginae — a pair of Christian missionary monks slipped out of China with silkworm eggs hidden in hollow bamboo canes and brought them west to Justinian's court. **Sericulture** — the cultivation of silkworms and the production of silk — had been a Chinese monopoly for two thousand years, with all the profits of the long Silk Road trade flowing through Chinese and central-Asian intermediaries to reach Constantinople. The smuggled eggs broke the Chinese monopoly. Within a generation Byzantine workshops were producing their own silk. The Byzantine silk industry — concentrated at Constantinople, Thebes in Greece, and a few other centers — became one of the great state-controlled luxury industries of the early Middle Ages, its products reaching western European courts as diplomatic gifts that established Byzantine prestige across Christendom.

The episode is a small reminder that the early medieval west was never sealed off from the rest of Eurasia. The Mediterranean trade networks contracted but they did not disappear. The wider world kept supplying western Europe with religious ideas (from the Christian East), with goods (silk, spices, slaves, silver coinage), and occasionally with technologies (the stirrup, the horse collar, eventually the lateen sail). The west was peripheral in this period in a way it had not been before, but peripheral is not isolated.

---

Cross-civ check: while Justinian was attempting his western reconquest and the plague was eviscerating Constantinople, **China** was about to be reunified by the **Sui dynasty** in 581 after three centuries of division (Six Dynasties, then Tang-Song China). The **Gupta Empire** in India had collapsed around 550 under Hunnic pressure (Gupta Empire Ch 8). The **Sasanian Persians** were locked in their long final war with Byzantium that would exhaust both empires and leave them defenseless against the Arab onslaught (Persian Empire Ch 9). In **Mesoamerica**, **Teotihuacan** was burning around 550 (Teotihuacan), the **Maya** Classic period was at its height (Maya Civilization Ch 5–6), and **Tiwanaku** in the Andes was beginning the long ascent that would make it one of the great Andean civilizations (andean-kingdoms). The Plague of Justinian, by some estimates, may have reached as far as Britain by 549 and southern China by the seventh century — a genuinely intercontinental disaster that prefigured the Black Death by 800 years.

# Chapter 3 — Christianizing the West

The early medieval west was Christianized by monks. This is not the romantic version of Christianization — there were no soaring sermons in great cathedrals, no popular conversions en masse. It was conducted, slowly, between roughly 500 and 800, by small groups of celibate religious men setting up communal religious houses on the frontier of pagan Europe and waiting for the locals to come to them. The model was invented in late-antique Egypt and Syria. It was adapted for the West by a single man — **Benedict of Nursia** — and then propagated by Irish missionaries from a marginal Atlantic island that the Roman Empire had never even tried to conquer. By 800 the monastic system Benedict had outlined was the principal religious-cultural-economic infrastructure of Latin Europe.

This chapter is about how that happened.

---

**Monasticism** — the practice of religious withdrawal from ordinary society into communities devoted to prayer, manual labor, and ascetic discipline — was a Christian invention of the late third century. It started in the deserts of Egypt with hermits like **Antony the Great** (the original "desert father," who lived alone in the eastern desert from roughly 270 to 356, attracting disciples who imitated his life). It quickly evolved from solitary anchorite life into communal **cenobitic** monasticism — monks living together under a common rule and an abbot — pioneered by figures like **Pachomius** in fourth-century Egypt. By 500 there were monastic communities scattered across the Christian East: in Egypt, in Palestine, in Syria, in Cappadocia.

The model spread to the West, but slowly. There were monastic experiments in fifth-century Gaul and Italy, but they were small, scattered, and operated under varying rules borrowed haphazardly from the East. What the West did not have was a single, coherent, practical monastic rule that could be adopted by any community anywhere and produce a functional monastery.

That is what **Benedict of Nursia** (BEN-eh-dikt, c. 480–547, "Nursia" being a small town in central Italy) provided around 530.

Benedict's biography is sketchy — almost everything we know comes from the *Dialogues* of Pope Gregory the Great, written half a century after Benedict's death and thick with edifying miracle stories that may or may not be reliable. The basic outline: he was born to a moderately well-off Roman family, sent to Rome to study, dropped out in disgust at the dissolute student life of late-antique Rome, became a hermit in a cave at Subiaco for several years, attracted disciples, eventually founded the monastery of **Monte Cassino** on a hilltop in central Italy around 529, and spent the rest of his life there as abbot writing and refining his monastic rule.

The **Rule of Saint Benedict** is one of those documents whose practical impact is enormous and whose actual contents are unspectacular. It is a short book — about 30,000 words, the length of a modern paperback — divided into 73 short chapters covering everything from how to run an election for an abbot to what kind of bread to serve in Lent. It prescribes a balanced day for each monk: about eight hours of prayer (the eight canonical "hours" of the divine office, from Matins before dawn through Compline at nightfall), six hours of manual labor, four hours of private reading. It requires three vows from each monk: **obedience** (to the abbot and the rule), **stability** (to the specific monastic community he has joined, for life), and **conversion of life** (a continual moral and spiritual reform). It establishes the abbot's authority but constrains him by requiring that he consult the senior monks on important decisions and the entire community on the most important. It prescribes hospitality to all strangers, "for every guest is to be received as Christ." It legislates the diet, the clothing, the punishments, the night office, the care of the sick, the admission of children, and a hundred other practical matters of running a household of religious men in a self-sufficient agricultural community.

The Rule's defining quality, in the words of the great twentieth-century Benedictine historian David Knowles, is **moderation**. It is harder than the lives of the desert fathers (Egyptian monks who fasted on bread and water and slept on stone) but easier than the lives of the most ascetic Eastern monks. It assumed monks who were ordinary men trying to live an extraordinary religious life, not spiritual athletes attempting heroic feats. It worked. It worked everywhere it was tried. By the seventh century it had spread across most of western monastic communities. By the ninth century it was, by Carolingian decree (Chapter 5), the universal monastic rule of the Latin west, binding on every monastery from Ireland to Italy.

What Benedict had built was an institutional template — a portable model for a self-sufficient religious community of about 30 to 100 men that could be set up almost anywhere, would feed itself, would educate its own next generation, would maintain a library and copy books, would provide hospitality to travelers, and would serve as a stable cultural center in a region that had little else. Multiplied across thousands of monasteries over four centuries, this template became the basic infrastructure of medieval European culture. Almost every Latin classical text that survives — Cicero, Virgil, Tacitus, Livy, the technical writers, the entire surviving corpus — survived because Benedictine monks copied it onto fresh parchment when the original was wearing out. Without Benedict, almost nothing of Latin literature would exist today. He was not trying to save Latin literature. He was trying to save souls. The literature came along for the ride because his Rule required monks to read, and to read you have to have books, and to have books you have to have someone copying them.

Monte Cassino itself, where he wrote the Rule, has had a complicated afterlife. The Lombards destroyed it in 577. It was rebuilt. Saracen pirates sacked it in 883. It was rebuilt. An earthquake leveled it in 1349. It was rebuilt. The Allies bombed it flat on 15 February 1944 during the Italian campaign of World War II, on the (mistaken) assumption that German troops were using it as an observation post. It was rebuilt. The original sixth-century building does not survive in any meaningful sense; the current monastery is essentially a postwar reconstruction. But the Rule survives, and it is the same rule Benedict wrote.

---

While Benedict was building Monte Cassino, an entirely separate monastic tradition was developing on the absolute opposite end of the western world.

**Ireland** had never been part of the Roman Empire. The Romans had looked at it from the Welsh coast, said no thank you, and gone back to Britain. Christianity had reached the island in the fifth century, traditionally by way of **St. Patrick** — a Romano-British Christian who had been kidnapped as a teenager by Irish slavers, escaped, became a priest, and returned around 432 as a missionary. Patrick is mostly legendary in detail (he probably did not actually drive the snakes out of Ireland — there had never been any snakes in Ireland, the climate is wrong) but he is solidly historical in outline; the Christianization of Ireland really did happen in the fifth century, and it really was largely accomplished by missionaries from Roman Britain.

What happened next was unusual. Ireland adopted Christianity but it adopted Christianity into a society that had no cities, no Roman bishops, no late-antique civic infrastructure. The result was a distinctively Irish form of Christianity in which the basic religious unit was not the urban diocese (as in continental Christianity) but the **monastery** — a religious-agricultural community usually founded on land donated by a local king, often with the king's family providing the abbot. The abbots of these Irish monasteries became the principal religious authorities in the country, often outranking the bishops. Monastic life in Ireland tended toward the ascetic — Irish monks were famous for fasting all night standing in cold rivers, for elaborate self-imposed penances, and for **peregrinatio pro Christo**, "pilgrimage for Christ," which meant leaving Ireland forever to go preach Christianity in foreign lands as an act of religious self-exile.

The masterpiece of Irish monasticism was **Iona** (eye-OH-nuh), founded by the monk **Columba** (kuh-LUM-buh, also called Colmcille, c. 521–597) on a small Hebridean island off western Scotland in 563. Iona became the mother house of an entire missionary network that Christianized Pictish Scotland and then northern England. The monk **Aidan** crossed from Iona in 635 at the invitation of the Northumbrian king Oswald and founded **Lindisfarne** on a tidal island off the Northumbrian coast — the cradle of northern English Christianity. The Lindisfarne community produced, around 715, the **Lindisfarne Gospels**: an illuminated manuscript whose dense interlaced page borders, intricate carpet pages, and zoomorphic capital letters are one of the masterpieces of Insular ("island," meaning Irish-British) art.

Other Irish monks fanned out across Europe. **Columbanus** (different person from Columba) crossed to Gaul in 590 and founded a network of monasteries that included **Luxeuil** in Burgundy and **Bobbio** in northern Italy. Irish missionaries reached Switzerland (founding **St. Gall**), Germany (Würzburg, Salzburg), and even Iceland, where Irish monks were probably living before the Norse arrival in the ninth century. The **Book of Kells**, made around 800 either at Iona or at Kells in Ireland (probably both — work began at Iona and finished at Kells after Vikings forced the community to evacuate), is the most spectacular surviving example of the Insular illumination style.

So the early medieval west had two monastic traditions — the Benedictine tradition rooted in Italy and gradually spreading north, and the Insular tradition rooted in Ireland and spreading south. They eventually merged, with the Benedictine model winning out across most of the Continent. But the Irish contribution was structural: it was Irish monks who carried Christianity to the pagan margins of northern Europe — Scotland, Northumbria, Frisia, parts of Germany — that the Roman missions had never reached, and Irish scriptoria that preserved a startling amount of Greek and Latin learning that had been lost on the Continent.

---

The point at which the Roman and Insular traditions had to be reconciled was northern England. **Northumbria** in the seventh century was a kingdom with two competing Christianities: a Roman tradition descending from Pope Gregory's Augustine of Canterbury mission to Kent in 597, and an Irish tradition descending from Aidan's Lindisfarne foundation in 635. The two had different ways of calculating the date of Easter (the most important feast of the Christian year), different styles of monastic tonsure (the haircut monks wore as a marker of their religious profession), and different relationships to broader Christendom.

King **Oswiu** of Northumbria convened the **Synod of Whitby** in 664 to settle the question. The Roman side argued that the practices of the Roman tradition came directly from Saint Peter, the rock on whom Christ had founded his church. The Irish side argued that their practices came from Saint Columba and the apostolic tradition transmitted through the Irish church. Oswiu, asked to choose between Peter and Columba, made the political choice: he chose Peter. The English church aligned itself with Rome. The Irish-style monks at Lindisfarne who would not accept the Roman calendar withdrew to Iona; the rest of the English church adopted Roman practices.

The Synod of Whitby is a small event with large consequences. It tied the English church into the broader continental Latin tradition rather than the more isolated Insular one. It made possible the long English contribution to continental Christianity — the eighth-century English mission to convert Frisia and Germany, led by **Boniface** (a Wessex monk who became "the apostle of the Germans" before being killed by pagan Frisians in 754); the Northumbrian scholar **Alcuin of York**, who would join Charlemagne's court in 781 and lead the Carolingian Renaissance (Chapter 5); and the broader cross-Channel ecclesiastical traffic that knit the early medieval west into a single Latin Christian intellectual community.

It also helped produce the **Venerable Bede** (the most learned man in early-eighth-century Europe). Bede (c. 673–735) entered the Northumbrian monastery of Wearmouth as a child of seven and never left it for the rest of his life — for the next sixty years he lived, prayed, studied, taught, and wrote at the twin monastery of Wearmouth-Jarrow. His **Ecclesiastical History of the English People**, completed in 731, is the foundational source for early Anglo-Saxon history. It is also the first major work to use **Anno Domini** dating systematically (counting years from the supposed birth of Christ), the convention that became universal in the Latin west and is still in worldwide use today as "AD" (or, in the modern secularized version, "CE"). Bede's textbook on the calendar — *De Temporum Ratione*, "On the Reckoning of Time" — taught generations of monks how to compute the date of Easter, including the standard medieval discussion of why the earth has to be a sphere (it's the only shape that produces the observed shadow of the earth on the moon during a lunar eclipse). The flat-earth myth has Bede teaching the opposite. Bede taught the opposite of the flat-earth myth.

---

While Bede was writing in Northumbria, the principal organizer of the western church was the bishop of Rome.

**Pope Gregory I** (the Great, ruled 590–604) is the single most important pope of the early medieval period. Like Benedict before him and Cassiodorus before that, he was a Roman senatorial-class figure (he had served briefly as prefect of Rome before retiring to a monastic life around 575) who tried to use surviving Roman institutional skills to organize the post-Roman Christian world. He became pope reluctantly in 590, served for fourteen years, and in that time set the model of medieval papal authority that every later pope would inherit.

Gregory's papacy did roughly four big things.

First, he **organized famine relief and refugee management** in a Rome that was a fraction of its former size, repeatedly besieged by Lombards, devoid of effective imperial protection. He used the substantial papal estates (the *patrimonium Petri*) — agricultural land scattered across Italy, Sicily, and as far as Gaul, donated to the Roman church over centuries — to feed the urban poor and ransom captives from Lombard raids. The papal administrative system that managed these estates became, in effect, the surviving administrative system of central Italy.

Second, he **asserted papal primacy** over the Western patriarchates with new clarity. The pope had always had a special status in the Christian world as the bishop of the imperial capital and the supposed successor of Saint Peter, but the relationship between Rome and the other major patriarchates (Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem) had been one of theoretical equality. Gregory asserted that the bishop of Rome had a special apostolic authority over the entire Latin west — over Gaul, Spain, Italy, Britain — that no other bishop possessed. The assertion was not new but the systematic insistence was, and it set the long medieval pattern of papal centralization.

Third, he **dispatched the Augustine mission** to convert the Anglo-Saxons. In 596 Gregory sent a Roman monk named Augustine (not the same as Augustine of Hippo, the great fourth-century theologian — a different Augustine entirely) and forty companions to Britain to attempt a missionary effort. Augustine landed in Kent in 597, was politely received by King **Æthelberht** (whose Frankish wife was already Christian), and within a few years had baptized the king. The Christianization of England would take another century to be substantially complete, but the see of **Canterbury** founded by Augustine remains the senior English archbishopric to this day. The Augustine mission is also the structural reason the eventual English church aligned with Rome rather than with the Celtic tradition — a fact that mattered when the Synod of Whitby came to choose between them in 664.

Fourth, he **codified the liturgical chant** — a body of plainsong used in the Roman liturgy that became known as **Gregorian chant** and forms the foundational repertoire of western liturgical music. Modern scholars are divided on how much Gregory personally contributed and how much was developed in the following two centuries by Carolingian cantors who attached his name to it for prestige; the standard current view is that "Gregorian chant" is mostly post-Gregory but that he probably did contribute to liturgical reform, and the attribution stuck for very good reasons of medieval branding. The music is still sung today.

Gregory's papacy is the moment at which the medieval papacy comes into focus. Earlier popes had been important figures, but Gregory was the first to combine the moral authority of the papal office with the administrative competence of the late-Roman senatorial class and to turn the combination into a coherent program of Western church organization. He also wrote prolifically (commentaries on Job, the *Pastoral Rule* on the duties of bishops, the *Dialogues* including the life of Benedict) and was canonized as one of the four traditional **Doctors of the Latin Church** alongside Augustine, Jerome, and Ambrose. By 700 the papal model Gregory had built was the organizing framework of western Christianity.

---

A note on what Christianization actually did, because popular accounts often conflate it with what it didn't do.

Christianization in the early medieval west did **not** convert the entire population overnight to a deeply held personal religious commitment of the kind that nineteenth-century Protestant readers were trained to expect. Mass conversion in this period was a top-down phenomenon: a king accepted baptism, his warriors followed, and over the next several generations the population gradually adopted the new religion, with old practices either banned, syncretized, or quietly tolerated. The peasant who burned a candle to a saint in 800 was often the descendant of a peasant who had burned a similar candle to a local spirit in 600 — and the saint and the spirit often had suspiciously similar attributes. The line between "Christian practice with pagan survivals" and "pagan practice with Christian veneer" was fuzzy and stayed fuzzy for centuries. The institutional church understood this perfectly and largely tolerated it.

What Christianization did do was put a bishop and (eventually) a parish priest in every settlement, give every settlement a calendar of religious feasts that organized the agricultural year, give every individual a baptismal record that established legal personhood, give every dispute a moral framework drawn from Christian ethics, and give the political ruler a religious authority that anointed his power. It built the institutional infrastructure within which medieval European life would be lived. The personal piety stuff — the deep individual conversion experience, the literate engagement with theological doctrine — would mostly come later, in fits and starts, with the high-medieval revival of urban preaching and (much later) with the Reformation.

In 700, the deep work of building that institutional infrastructure was about half done. Kingdoms had Christian kings. Monasteries dotted the landscape. Bishops ran the cities. Missionaries were still working at the pagan northern and eastern frontier — Frisia, Saxony, Bavaria, the Slavic lands beyond the Elbe. Pope Gregory's organizational model was being applied almost everywhere from Spain to England. The Catholic version of Christianity was on the way to being the universal religion of western Europe.

That uniformity was about to be tested by an outside force nobody had seen coming.

---

Cross-civ check: while the western European church was organizing itself under Gregory the Great and Bede, the **Byzantine Empire** was about to enter the catastrophic seventh century — the Persian invasion, the loss of Syria and Egypt, the Arab conquests, the Iconoclast controversy (Byzantine Empire Ch 3, Ch 4). The **Tang Dynasty** in China had just begun its long classical period (Tang-Song China). **Buddhism** was at its medieval peak in East Asia. **Aksum** in highland Ethiopia was the southernmost outpost of Christian late antiquity, having converted to Christianity in the fourth century and now functioning as the only African Christian state outside the soon-to-be-conquered Egyptian and Nubian territories (Kingdom of Aksum). The Islamic-led Arab armies were about to break out of the Arabian peninsula and rewrite the Mediterranean political map.

# Chapter 4 — Mohammed's Wave and the Iberian Loss

In 732 — exactly a century after the death of the Prophet Muhammad in Medina — a Frankish army under **Charles Martel** met an Umayyad Arab raiding force on a road between **Tours** and **Poitiers** in central Gaul, and the Frankish line held. The battle is one of the most famous events in early medieval history and the story is told in roughly the same shape in every textbook: the Arab tide that had swept across North Africa, Spain, and southern Gaul reached its high-water mark on a cold October day, the Franks held, the tide receded, and Christian Europe was saved.

That story is half right. The Frankish line did hold and the Arab raiding force was repulsed. But Tours was not the climax of a continental conquest project; it was a frontier raid, one of many, conducted by a regional commander rather than the central Caliphal government. The "tide" had been receding for some time before Tours because the Umayyad center of gravity was busy with civil war. And the long-term consequences of what had happened in the previous twenty years were so much larger than Tours that the battle is, by the standards of what it allegedly settled, almost a footnote.

The actual story of how Islam reached western Europe in the early eighth century is the story of **Spain**, **Tariq ibn Ziyad**, the **Visigothic kingdom**, and a permanent civilizational fault line that would not be erased until 1492.

---

The Islamic explosion out of Arabia between 632 (the year Muhammad died) and roughly 750 is one of the most rapid expansions in world history. Islamic Golden Age Ch 1 covers the religious and political background from inside the new caliphate. From the perspective of the western Mediterranean, the relevant chronology is:

- 632: Muhammad dies in Medina; his successors (the **caliphs**, "deputies") inherit a unified Arab state stretching across the Arabian peninsula
- 636: Arab armies destroy the Byzantine field army at the Battle of **Yarmouk** in Syria
- 637: Sasanian Persian capital of Ctesiphon falls
- 642: Egypt falls; Alexandria is captured; the western Roman grain breadbasket since the time of Augustus is now Muslim
- 670s: Arab armies push west across North Africa, founding the city of **Kairouan** in modern Tunisia as their base
- 698: Carthage falls; Roman/Byzantine Africa is gone
- 711: Arab armies cross from Morocco into Visigothic Spain

In one human lifetime — eighty years from Muhammad's death to the conquest of Spain — Islam went from a regional Arabian movement to the dominant religious-political force of the southern Mediterranean rim, controlling territory from the Atlantic to the Indus, killing or absorbing the Sasanian Persian Empire, amputating two-thirds of the eastern Roman Empire, and destroying every Christian state along the southern Mediterranean coast.

By the time the Arabs reached Spain in 711, the eastern Mediterranean was already Muslim — Syria, Egypt, North Africa, all of it. The southern Mediterranean was not just disputed territory; it was lost, and lost permanently. Western Europe was being squeezed into the northwestern corner of the former Roman world.

---

The conquest of Spain itself was extraordinarily quick. Spain in 711 was ruled by the **Visigothic kingdom** that had survived in the peninsula since being pushed out of Gaul by Clovis at Vouillé (Chapter 1). The Visigothic kingdom was Catholic (since Reccared's conversion in 589) and reasonably sophisticated (it produced the most extensive law code of the western successor kingdoms and the great encyclopedist Isidore of Seville). It was also riven by succession disputes and unable to mobilize effective military resistance.

In April 711, an Arab-Berber army of perhaps 7,000 men under the Berber commander **Tariq ibn Ziyad** crossed from Morocco at the strait that the Romans had called the Pillars of Hercules. The strait took its medieval Arabic name from Tariq's first landing point on the European side: *Jabal Tariq*, "Mount Tariq" — softened in modern English to **Gibraltar**. Tariq destroyed the Visigothic king Roderic at the Battle of **Guadalete** in July 711, killing the king and most of the Visigothic nobility in a single engagement. Within seven years a series of follow-up campaigns had swept across nearly the entire Iberian peninsula.

Most of Spain became **al-Andalus** — the Arabic name for the Muslim portion of Iberia, eventually applied to Spain as a whole and the source of the modern Spanish region "Andalusia." Only a thin strip of Christian kingdoms survived in the northern mountains: the **Kingdom of Asturias** in the Cantabrian uplands (founded by the Visigothic noble Pelayo, who won a small skirmish at **Covadonga** around 722 that the later Christian Reconquista would memorialize as its founding moment), and a few Pyrenean valleys that would eventually become Navarre and the Basque country. From these tiny mountain refuges, the Christian counter-conquest — the **Reconquista** — would creep south at glacial pace over the next 781 years, finally completing in 1492 with the fall of Granada.

But for the early medieval west, the immediate fact was: Spain was gone. The largest single piece of the former western Roman Empire outside Italy, the most Romanized of the Germanic kingdoms, the home of Isidore of Seville and the most sophisticated Latin legal tradition in the West, had been absorbed in seven years into the new Islamic civilization. The western Mediterranean was now bounded on three sides by Muslim power: the Maghreb, al-Andalus, and the sea between them dominated by Muslim raiders.

Islamic Golden Age Ch 7 covers the brilliant Andalusian civilization that grew up in conquered Spain — Cordoba, the great mosque, the philosophical and scientific work that would feed back into Latin Europe centuries later. From the early-medieval Latin perspective, though, Spain was not yet a source of culture; it was a source of pirates, raids, and the permanent reminder that Christendom was no longer secure in its own western Mediterranean.

---

After Spain, the Arabs probed northward into Gaul. They were not attempting a continental conquest in the way they had attempted Spain; the Pyrenees and the distance from Damascus made that impractical. What they were doing was raiding — large armed forces crossing the mountains on summer expeditions, looting the wealthy monasteries and cities of southern Gaul, retreating with plunder before winter.

The Frankish kingdom these raids were probing was, by the early eighth century, a strange political entity. Officially the kingdom was still ruled by the Merovingian dynasty Clovis had founded — but the actual Merovingian kings had become the *rois fainéants*, the do-nothing kings, and real power had drifted to the hereditary chief stewards of the royal household, the **mayors of the palace**.

The dominant family in this office was descended from **Pippin of Herstal** and his son **Charles Martel** (KHAR-uhl mar-TEL, ruled as mayor of the palace 718–741, "Martel" being a nickname meaning "the Hammer"). Charles Martel was, by every functional measure, the king of the Franks. He commanded the army, conducted foreign policy, distributed offices, and had united most of Francia under his control by force in the 720s. He just wasn't called "king." His grandson Charlemagne would later be called the model of the Carolingian dynasty; the dynasty itself takes its name from Charles Martel's grandfather, who was also called Charles, and the family is properly called **Carolingian** (kair-oh-LIN-jee-an, from Latin *Carolus*, "Charles").

When the Arab raiders began pushing across the Pyrenees in the 720s, Charles Martel was the man with the army to stop them. The largest of these raids came in 732. The Umayyad governor of al-Andalus, **Abd al-Rahman al-Ghafiqi**, led a major expedition north that sacked **Bordeaux** and was advancing toward the wealthy abbey of **Saint-Martin** at **Tours** when Charles Martel's Frankish army intercepted him on a road south of the city. The exact site is debated; the engagement is variously called the Battle of Tours or the Battle of Poitiers depending on which nearby town you prefer.

The battle itself, by the surviving Arab and Frankish chronicle accounts, was conventional. The Franks deployed in a tight infantry formation on a defensive position in wooded terrain that limited the use of Arab cavalry. The Arabs attacked repeatedly; the Frankish line held. Abd al-Rahman was killed; the Arab army retreated under cover of darkness; Charles Martel did not pursue. The Franks took the field.

The medieval significance of the battle was enormously inflated by Frankish chroniclers (and later by Edward Gibbon and even later by nineteenth-century textbook writers) into the salvation of Christian Europe. The actual significance was somewhat smaller. Tours was one of many checks the expanding Caliphate received in the 730s — the **Khazars** in the Caucasus, the eastern Roman defense of Anatolia, internal Berber revolts in North Africa, and eventually the Abbasid Revolution of 750 — and the cumulative effect of all these checks was that the Umayyad expansion phase ended around mid-century. The Caliphate did not retreat from Spain after Tours — al-Andalus was Muslim for another seven and a half centuries — but it stopped trying seriously to push north of the Pyrenees.

What Tours did do, even if the salvation-of-Christendom rhetoric oversells it, was establish Charles Martel's military reputation across western Europe and confirm the Carolingian family as the dominant force in Francia. Within a generation his son Pippin would feel confident enough to depose the last Merovingian and put on the crown himself.

---

The deposition happened in 751, and it required the help of the pope.

**Pippin the Short** (PIP-in, also called Pepin III, son of Charles Martel) had inherited the office of mayor of the palace from his father in 741. By 750 he was the most powerful man in the Frankish kingdom, but he was not the king. The last Merovingian, **Childeric III**, sat on the throne — long-haired, ceremonial, useless. Pippin wanted the title to match the power. He needed religious cover for the deposition.

He turned to the **pope** because the pope needed him. **Pope Zachary** (ruled 741–752) was facing the Lombards, who were once again pressing Rome from the north and threatening to absorb the Papal patrimonium and the city itself. The Byzantine emperor was busy with Iconoclasm and had effectively given up on protecting Rome. The pope needed a powerful military ally. Pippin needed religious legitimation for a coup. The two requirements lined up.

In 750, Pippin sent ambassadors to Pope Zachary with a single carefully framed question: "Is it good or not that someone with no royal power should be called king?" Zachary returned the answer: "Whoever holds the power should be called king" — so that there should not be a discrepancy between the title and the reality.

That was the green light. In November 751, an assembly of Frankish nobles deposed Childeric III, sent him to the monastery of Saint-Bertin (where his hair was shaved off, ending the Merovingian dynastic claim, since the long royal hair had been a symbol of Merovingian sacral kingship), and elected Pippin king of the Franks. Pippin was anointed with holy oil — a new ritual for Frankish royal accession, deliberately echoing the Old Testament anointing of King David by the prophet Samuel, and laying the foundation for the medieval theory that Christian kings ruled by sacred consecration. The Carolingian dynasty had its throne.

Three years later, in 754, Pope **Stephen II** crossed the Alps in winter to come and personally re-anoint Pippin and his sons in Saint-Denis outside Paris — the first time a pope had ever traveled to Francia, and a public ceremony that sealed the Frankish-papal alliance. In return, in 754 and 756, Pippin marched his army into Italy, defeated the Lombard king Aistulf, and donated the conquered Lombard territory in central Italy to the pope. This was the **Donation of Pippin**: a strip of central Italy, including Ravenna and the corridor west to Rome, transferred from the Lombards to direct papal rule, becoming the **Papal States** that would survive as a temporal sovereign entity until Italian unification absorbed them in 1870.

The Donation of Pippin matters out of all proportion to its territorial extent. It gave the papacy its own kingdom — a temporal political base that would let popes operate as independent international political actors for the next 1,114 years. It permanently aligned the papacy with the Franks rather than with Constantinople — a re-orientation that would become decisive in the Iconoclasm controversy and the eventual East-West religious split (Byzantine Empire Ch 4, the Great Schism of 1054 in high-medieval-europe). And it set the institutional model of the medieval Western political order: a partnership between the Frankish-derived emperor and the bishop of Rome, each legitimating the other, both claiming to rule over the rest of Christendom on different but complementary grounds.

The arrangement Pippin made in 754 is the political DNA of the Holy Roman Empire that would survive in some form until 1806 and of the medieval papacy that would survive in altered form to the present day. Pippin's son **Charlemagne** inherited the framework. The next chapter is what he did with it.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Arabs were conquering Spain and the Carolingians were inventing themselves in Francia, the **Abbasid Revolution** in 750 was overthrowing the Umayyad caliphs in the East and shifting the center of the Islamic world from Damascus to the new city of **Baghdad** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). The Tang Dynasty in China was at its golden classical peak (Tang-Song China). In **Mesoamerica**, the great Maya cities of the southern lowlands (Tikal, Palenque, Copán) were entering their late-Classic Florescence (Maya Civilization). In the Andes, the **Wari** and **Tiwanaku** empires were the dominant powers (middle-horizon-empires). In **Polynesia**, the great voyaging expansion across the Pacific was reaching New Zealand and Hawaii within the next two centuries (Polynesian Voyagers). The early medieval west was, for the first time since Roman Britain, a small player in a much larger Eurasian system.

# Chapter 5 — Charlemagne and the Carolingian Renaissance

Pippin the Short died in 768 and divided his kingdom (in the standard Frankish fashion) between his two sons, **Charles** and **Carloman**. Carloman conveniently died three years later and Charles took the entire kingdom. Charles ruled the Franks for the next 43 years, conducted 53 separate military campaigns, conquered or defeated the Lombards, the Saxons, the Bavarians, the Avars, and the Bretons, was crowned Emperor of the Romans by the pope on Christmas Day 800, sponsored a copying program that preserved most of surviving classical Latin literature, and is, by any reasonable standard, the most consequential figure of the early Middle Ages.

He is known to history as **Charlemagne** (SHAR-luh-mayn, French for *Carolus Magnus*, "Charles the Great"). His older Frankish-language nickname was *Karl der Grosse* in German and *Karol Wielki* in Polish — the same epithet in three different languages, all meaning the same thing. His Latin name *Carolus* is the source of the modern name "Charles" and gives the Carolingian dynasty its name.

This chapter is about him, about what his empire did, and — perhaps most importantly — about what his copying program accomplished that nothing else in early medieval Europe came close to matching.

---

The military reputation comes first because the Carolingian Empire was founded on military success. A short list of major Charlemagne campaigns:

- **The Saxon Wars** (772–804): a brutal 32-year project to conquer and forcibly convert the pagan Saxons of northern Germany. Includes the infamous **Massacre of Verden** in 782, in which Charlemagne ordered the execution of 4,500 Saxon prisoners taken in a single battle, and the **Capitulary on the Saxons** of 785, which made it a capital offense to refuse Christian baptism, to eat meat in Lent, or to cremate the dead in pagan fashion. The Saxon Wars are the moment at which the Carolingian project of Christian universalism shows its sharpest edge: conversion was offered, but conversion-or-death was the actual policy.

- **The Lombard Conquest** (773–774): Charlemagne destroyed the Lombard kingdom of Italy in a single campaign, taking the Lombard capital of Pavia in June 774 and adding "King of the Lombards" to his Frankish title. He took the **Iron Crown of Lombardy** (a Lombard royal regalia incorporating, by tradition, a nail from the True Cross) and the kingdom became part of the Frankish realm. The Donation of Pippin was confirmed and extended; the popes now had a Frankish king as their permanent military patron in Italy.

- **The Bavarian Annexation** (788): Charlemagne deposed the Bavarian duke Tassilo III, absorbed Bavaria into the Frankish kingdom, and pushed the eastern frontier to the Enns river.

- **The Avar War** (791–803): the Avars were a steppe-nomad people who had occupied the Hungarian plain for two centuries and accumulated, through tribute extracted from Constantinople, an enormous treasure. Charlemagne's armies destroyed the **Avar Khaganate** between 791 and 803. The Frankish chronicler reports that the captured Avar treasure required fifteen ox-carts to haul back to Aachen.

- **The Spanish March** (778 onwards): Charlemagne intervened in Muslim Spain in 778 in support of one Andalusian faction against another. The expedition itself was inconclusive (and produced the rear-guard ambush at the **Pass of Roncesvalles** in which Roland died, eventually mythologized into the great vernacular epic *La Chanson de Roland* in the late eleventh century — the foundational text of medieval French literature). But subsequent campaigns established a Frankish-controlled buffer zone south of the Pyrenees, the **Spanish March**, that included Barcelona by 801 and would eventually become Catalonia.

By 800, Charlemagne ruled an empire stretching from the Atlantic at the Pyrenees to the Elbe in the east, from the Italian peninsula in the south to the North Sea coast. It was the largest political entity in western Europe since the western Roman Empire's high-imperial period. It included all of modern France, almost all of modern Germany west of the Elbe, the Low Countries, Switzerland, northern Italy down to Rome, and a strip of northeastern Spain. The fragmented post-Roman west had been (briefly) reassembled under a single ruler.

---

The fixed capital of this empire was **Aachen** (AH-ken, German; *Aix-la-Chapelle* in French — both names refer to the city's hot springs). Earlier Frankish kings had been **itinerant** — they had moved constantly between royal estates, eating up the produce of each estate for a few weeks before moving on, because the agricultural surpluses of any single estate were not large enough to support a permanent court. Charlemagne fixed his court at Aachen in the 790s, building a permanent palace complex that included the famous **Palatine Chapel** — an octagonal church modeled directly on Justinian's San Vitale in Ravenna, completed and consecrated around 805. The chapel still stands; it is one of the few essentially intact early medieval buildings in northern Europe, and Charlemagne is buried in it.

Aachen was Charlemagne's deliberate statement that the Frankish kingdom was a permanent institution with a permanent capital, modeled on the imperial capitals of late Rome. It also gathered around itself a court of the most learned men Charlemagne could recruit from anywhere in Latin Christendom — and from a few places outside it.

The most important of these was the Northumbrian scholar **Alcuin of York** (AL-kwin, c. 735–804). Alcuin had been trained at the cathedral school of York under the heirs of Bede; by 781 he was the leading scholar in northern England. Charlemagne met him in Italy in 781, recruited him on the spot, and put him in charge of the **palace school** at Aachen. For the next twenty years Alcuin was the principal intellectual of the Frankish court, the architect of what later historians would call the **Carolingian Renaissance**.

The Carolingian Renaissance was not a "rebirth" in the sense the later Italian Renaissance was — there was no rediscovery of lost classical learning, no overturning of medieval orthodoxy. What it was was much more practical: a deliberate, top-down, empire-wide program to standardize Latin grammar, copy and preserve the surviving classical and Christian Latin literature, train a generation of administrators and clergy capable of reading and writing that Latin to a high standard, and reform the liturgy and the law on the basis of carefully copied authoritative texts. It was, essentially, an empire-scale intervention to fix the manuscript and educational infrastructure of Latin Europe before it deteriorated any further.

The mechanism was the monastery. Charlemagne issued **capitularies** (royal decrees on specific subjects, the basic legislative form of the Carolingian state) requiring every monastery and cathedral school in his empire to maintain a school for boys, to teach Latin grammar and the basic disciplines, to copy specified texts, and to send their best scholars to court. The surviving capitularies on this subject — most famously the **Admonitio Generalis** of 789 — read like a modern Department of Education curriculum reform memo, full of practical instructions on what to teach and how. The monasteries complied because Charlemagne could replace any abbot who didn't.

The result was the most significant book-copying program in European history. Across the empire, in monasteries from Tours to Reichenau, scriptoria turned out fresh copies of every classical Latin text that the previous centuries' monks had managed to preserve, plus the entire surviving Christian Latin patristic corpus, plus the standard reference works (Isidore's Etymologies, the encyclopedias, the legal texts, the technical grammars). A single Carolingian book historian estimates that around 7,000 manuscripts survive from the ninth century alone — more than from the previous five centuries combined. Almost everything we have of Cicero, Tacitus, Livy, Suetonius, Lucretius, the Augustan poets, the technical writers — exists today because Carolingian monks copied them in the eighth or ninth century from earlier manuscripts that have not survived. Without the Carolingian copying program, the great majority of Latin classical literature would simply not exist.

The script those monks used for the copies is the second consequential Carolingian invention: **Carolingian minuscule**.

---

The script needs a paragraph because it is one of those small technical things that turn out to have outsize cultural consequences.

Pre-Carolingian Latin scripts had a problem. Late-Roman cursive had fragmented after the western collapse into a half-dozen regional **national hands** — Insular minuscule in Britain and Ireland, Visigothic minuscule in Spain, Beneventan minuscule in southern Italy, Merovingian cursive in Gaul, and so on. Each was difficult to read for anyone trained in a different national hand. The letters were cramped, often joined together (so that you couldn't tell where one letter ended and the next began), abbreviated heavily (saving parchment but requiring expert decipherment), and lacked any consistent spacing or punctuation system. A Frankish monk reading a Visigothic manuscript was reading something close to a foreign alphabet.

Carolingian scribes — first at Corbie in northern France, then at Tours under Alcuin, then across the empire — developed a new script that solved all of these problems. **Carolingian minuscule** was a clean, upright lowercase letterform, with separated letters of uniform height, generous spacing between words (a genuine Carolingian innovation — earlier Latin had often been written without spaces), consistent abbreviation conventions, and clear punctuation marks (the modern question mark and exclamation point are descended from Carolingian invention). It was easy to read. It was easy to teach. It could be read by any literate person anywhere in the empire.

The script became the standard book hand of the Carolingian world by 800 and remained the dominant Latin script of Europe until it was replaced (very gradually) by Gothic textura in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries.

It was rediscovered in the fifteenth century by Italian Renaissance humanists hunting for ancient classical manuscripts. They found Carolingian copies — clean, easy to read — and mistook them for the original ancient Roman hand. They copied the script as their model for "ancient Roman" handwriting, called it *littera antiqua* ("ancient script"), and Italian Renaissance typesetters then turned it into the **roman type** of the new printing press. The lowercase letters of every Western book printed in roman type since around 1470 — including this one — descend in unbroken visual lineage from a script developed in northern Frankish monasteries around 780. The "humanist" lowercase letters of Italian printing are Carolingian. The medieval contribution to the modern visual world is right there on every page.

---

Charlemagne's reign culminated, in symbolic terms, on Christmas Day 800.

He had spent the autumn of that year in Rome, helping Pope **Leo III** (a recent appointee whose election had been disputed and who had been physically attacked by Roman aristocratic opponents) reconsolidate his position. On Christmas morning Charlemagne attended Mass at St. Peter's basilica. As he rose from prayer, Pope Leo placed a crown on his head and the assembled congregation acclaimed him: *Carolo Augusto a Deo coronato magno et pacifico imperatori Romanorum, vita et victoria!* — "Long life and victory to Charles, August, crowned by God, the great and peace-giving Emperor of the Romans!"

It was the first time a Roman imperial title had been conferred in the West since 476 — three hundred and twenty-four years.

The political theology of the moment was complicated. The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantine Empire Ch 4) considered the Roman emperorship its own monopoly. There was a Roman emperor at Constantinople in 800 — a woman, the **Empress Irene**, ruling as senior empress after blinding her son Constantine VI to take the throne — and the Byzantine position was that there could not be another. Pope Leo, by crowning Charlemagne, was implicitly arguing that the Byzantine throne was vacant (because of Irene's gender or her usurpation, depending on which Latin commentary you read) and that the Roman imperial title had reverted to the West. Charlemagne, by accepting the crown, was claiming to be the legitimate continuation of Augustus, Constantine, and Theodosius.

The Byzantines were furious and refused to recognize the title for over a decade. (They eventually accepted the formula that Charlemagne could be called *basileus* — "emperor" — but only of the Franks, not "of the Romans," which remained Constantinople's exclusive claim.) The Carolingian chronicler **Einhard**, in his later biography of Charlemagne, claimed that Charlemagne would not have entered the church that day if he had known what Leo was going to do — a face-saving claim probably written to manage the diplomatic awkwardness, since the entire ceremony was so clearly stage-managed in advance that nobody could have been surprised by it.

Whether Charlemagne was surprised or not, the coronation founded a political idea that would dominate medieval European politics for the next thousand years: the **Holy Roman Empire** as a Christian-Roman monarchy in the West, distinct from but structurally rivalrous with the Byzantine East, ruled by the Frankish-German emperor in partnership (and recurrent conflict) with the Roman pope. The institutional realities would change repeatedly over the next millennium; the basic idea — that there is a properly Roman, properly Christian, properly imperial sovereignty in the West, not the same as either the local kingdoms or the universal church — survived all the changes.

The Holy Roman Empire itself, as a continuous institution, dates more accurately from Otto I's coronation in 962 (Chapter 8) than from Charlemagne's in 800. But the model is Carolingian.

---

Charlemagne died at Aachen on January 28, 814, at the age of about 71. He was buried, sitting upright on a marble throne wearing his imperial regalia, in the Palatine Chapel he had built. The empire he had assembled passed intact to his only surviving legitimate son, **Louis the Pious** — a pious, scholarly, well-intentioned monarch whose reign would be consumed by civil wars among his own sons over the planned succession. The empire would not survive Louis's death. By 843 it would be permanently divided (Chapter 6).

But the cultural infrastructure Charlemagne had built — the Latin standardization, the copying program, the Carolingian minuscule script, the network of cathedral and monastic schools, the administrative apparatus of capitularies and *missi dominici* (royal inspectors who toured the empire enforcing decrees), the alliance between the Frankish crown and the Roman papacy — survived. It survived the breakup of the empire. It survived the ninth-century crises. It survived the century of Viking and Magyar raids. By the time the Ottonian dynasty rebuilt the eastern half into the Holy Roman Empire in the tenth century (Chapter 8), the Carolingian Renaissance's institutional residues were what they had to work with, and they were enough to anchor the recovery.

That is why Charlemagne is not remembered primarily for the territorial empire — which fragmented within thirty years of his death — but for the cultural project, which lasted. The empire was a moment. The script, the curriculum, and the saved books were a permanent inheritance.

---

Cross-civ check: while Charlemagne was building his empire and his palace school, the **Abbasid Caliphate** was at the height of the Islamic Golden Age under **Harun al-Rashid** (Islamic Golden Age Ch 2 — al-Rashid and Charlemagne were diplomatic correspondents, exchanging gifts including, by tradition, an elephant named Abul-Abbas). The **Tang Dynasty** in China was at its classical peak, with the great poets Li Bai and Du Fu writing during exactly this period (Tang-Song China). In the Andes, the **Wari Empire** was the dominant power on the Peruvian highlands (middle-horizon-empires). In Mesoamerica, the great Classic Maya cities were entering their terminal collapse over the next century (Maya Civilization Ch 7). In **Polynesia**, voyagers were settling Hawaii and pushing toward New Zealand (Polynesian Voyagers). Charlemagne's empire was, by Eurasian standards of the early ninth century, a respectable but not exceptional medium-sized state. The Tang and the Abbasids were both substantially larger and substantially more sophisticated. What made Charlemagne consequential was not scale; it was that his cultural project happened to provide the institutional framework on which Latin Europe would build the much larger civilization of the High Middle Ages.

# Chapter 6 — The Treaty of Verdun and the Carolingian Inheritance

Charlemagne's empire fell apart almost as soon as he was buried. The reason was not external pressure (the Vikings were just starting to bite, but they had not yet reached the catastrophic phase that Chapter 7 covers) and not internal incompetence (Charlemagne's son and grandsons were perfectly competent monarchs by the standards of the period). The reason was **Frankish inheritance custom**, which required that a king's lands be divided among his sons.

This had not been a problem under Charlemagne himself, because his only surviving legitimate son when he died was Louis the Pious, who therefore got the entire empire. Louis's three surviving sons all reached adulthood, all had their own ambitions, and all expected to inherit. The Treaty of Verdun in 843 — twenty-nine years after Charlemagne's death — divided the empire into three pieces, drew the borders that would shape the political map of western Europe for the next millennium, and is the deep structural origin of the modern Franco-German divide.

This chapter is the story of that breakup and what it produced.

---

**Louis the Pious** (Latin *Ludovicus Pius*, ruled 814–840) was Charlemagne's only surviving legitimate son and inherited the entire empire intact. He was a serious, scholarly, deeply religious monarch — the epithet "the Pious" was earned, not ironic — who took his duties as Christian emperor more solemnly than his father had. He sponsored monastic reform under the great Benedictine reformer **Benedict of Aniane** (a different Benedict than the founder of the order, c. 747–821, whose program of strict observance to the original Rule of Benedict was made empire-wide policy at the **Synod of Aachen** in 817). He pushed for stricter Christian behavior at court, banished some of the more theatrical late-Carolingian customs, and tried to govern as a proper Christian sovereign rather than a pagan-warlord-with-a-church-veneer.

The trouble was the succession.

In 817 — three years into his reign, and well in advance of any actual succession crisis — Louis issued a planning document called the **Ordinatio Imperii** ("Ordering of the Empire") that attempted to do something Frankish kings had never tried before: it set up a permanent inheritance scheme that would keep the empire intact across generations. Louis's eldest son **Lothair** would inherit the imperial title and the bulk of the empire. The two younger sons, **Pippin** and **Louis the German**, would receive subordinate kingdoms (Aquitaine and Bavaria) but would acknowledge Lothair as senior emperor.

This made dynastic sense (it preserved the empire as a single unit) but it was a radical departure from Frankish tradition (which would have given roughly equal shares to all three sons). The plan was contested almost immediately and never really stabilized.

Things got worse in 823 when Louis's second wife, **Judith of Bavaria**, gave birth to a fourth son, **Charles the Bald**. Now there was a child son who under the old custom should have received an inheritance share, and the question of how to fit him into the Ordinatio Imperii consumed the rest of Louis's reign. Three civil wars broke out between Louis and his elder sons over the allocation. Louis was deposed at the Field of Lies in 833, then restored, then subjected to public penance, then deposed again, then restored. By the time he died in 840, the Frankish nobility had been picking sides for nearly twenty years and any consensus on the succession had collapsed.

---

The actual division came at **Verdun** (vehr-DUN, an old Roman town on the Meuse river in northeastern France) in August 843, after three years of further civil war among the three surviving brothers (Pippin had died in 838).

The eldest, **Lothair I**, had inherited the imperial title and the central position. The middle brother, **Louis the German**, controlled the eastern Frankish lands. The youngest, **Charles the Bald**, controlled the western Frankish lands. The decisive moment had come in February 842, when Louis the German and Charles the Bald — recognizing that they would each be eaten by Lothair separately if they did not combine — met at **Strasbourg** and exchanged sworn oaths of mutual support against their elder brother. Each swore in the other's vernacular language so that both armies could understand the oath being taken.

The text of those oaths survives, and it is a small linguistic miracle. Louis the German swore in **Old High German** (the ancestor of modern German); Charles the Bald swore in what the document calls **lingua romana rustica** ("rustic Roman language") — and the surviving text is the earliest substantial document in any language that is identifiably **French** rather than Latin. The Strasbourg Oaths of 842 are the document with which the French language enters the historical record. (Old High German has earlier surviving documents.) The oaths also demonstrate something the chroniclers of the period otherwise mostly leave implicit: by 842 the population of West Francia and East Francia were already speaking different languages and could not understand each other in the vernacular. The cultural division between what would become France and what would become Germany was already emerging in the ninth century.

After Strasbourg, the war continued through 842 and into 843, until the three brothers finally agreed at Verdun to formal partition. The settlement gave each brother a share roughly proportional to his current military strength:

- **Charles the Bald** (the youngest) received **West Francia** — the western Frankish lands stretching from the Atlantic east to roughly the Scheldt, Meuse, Saône, and Rhône rivers. This is, very roughly, the territory of medieval and modern France west of the Rhône and east of the Atlantic.

- **Louis the German** received **East Francia** — the eastern Frankish lands east of the Rhine, plus Bavaria, Saxony, and the Frankish territories in modern Germany. This is, very roughly, the territory of medieval and modern Germany.

- **Lothair I** kept the imperial title and received **Middle Francia** — a long, awkward strip running from the Low Countries (modern Belgium and the Netherlands) south through Lorraine and Burgundy to most of Italy, including Rome and the imperial capital of Aachen. This was the wealthiest of the three shares (it included most of the major late-Roman cities of the empire and the imperial regalia) but it was also the most strategically incoherent — a thin north-south corridor with no natural defensive frontier and bordered by both his brothers.

Verdun is the deep structural origin of the modern political map of western Europe. West Francia became France. East Francia became Germany. The unstable Middle Frankish strip in between would be fought over by France and Germany for the next 1,100 years — Lorraine, Alsace, Burgundy, the Low Countries, northern Italy. The Franco-German contestation of these lands runs through the wars of Louis XIV in the seventeenth century, the Napoleonic wars, the Franco-Prussian War of 1870, both World Wars, and ends only with the European Coal and Steel Community in 1951 and the European Union that grew out of it. The EU itself can be read as the long-deferred political resolution of the Verdun partition: a way of governing the Middle Frankish territories without either France or Germany having to dominate them militarily.

---

Lothair's Middle Frankish strip did not stay a single kingdom for long. When Lothair died in 855 it was divided again among his three sons: **Louis II** received Italy and the imperial title; **Lothair II** received the strip from the Low Countries to Burgundy (which became known as **Lotharingia**, the source of the modern "Lorraine"); **Charles** received Provence. Lothair II died without legitimate heirs in 869 and his uncles Louis the German and Charles the Bald promptly carved up Lotharingia between themselves at the **Treaty of Meersen** (870), confirming the basic east-west division.

The imperial title itself bounced around among Carolingian relatives for the next several decades, mostly held by figures who controlled only fragments of the original empire. **Charles the Fat** — the youngest son of Louis the German — briefly reunited almost the entire Carolingian inheritance under a single sovereign in 884–887, but he was an unimpressive monarch whose military failures (he paid the Vikings 700 pounds of silver to leave the Siege of Paris in 886, see Chapter 7) had so destroyed his authority that his nephew **Arnulf of Carinthia** deposed him in 887. With Charles the Fat's death the following January, the unified Carolingian Empire as a single political entity was definitively dead.

What replaced it was a patchwork of regional successor kingdoms: **West Francia** (the future France), **East Francia** (the future Germany), the **Kingdom of Italy**, the **Kingdom of Burgundy**, the **Kingdom of Provence**. Each was nominally Carolingian (most of the dynasties were descended from Charlemagne by some line) but each operated independently. The imperial title was claimed by the Italian kings sporadically through the rest of the ninth century but had no real political content.

The Carolingian dynasty itself did not end immediately — the West Frankish kings continued to be Carolingian until 987, when **Hugh Capet**, the count of Paris and head of a powerful aristocratic family, was elected king after the death of the last Carolingian Louis V. Hugh founded the **Capetian** dynasty that would rule France for the next 300 years (and through cadet branches — the Valois, the Bourbons — into the nineteenth century). The East Frankish Carolingian line ended in 911 with the death of Louis the Child; the German nobility elected a non-Carolingian, **Conrad I**, and after his death in 918 elected the Saxon duke **Henry the Fowler** (Chapter 8), founding the Ottonian dynasty.

By 911–987 the original Carolingian project had completely fragmented. The three western European political units that emerged from it — France, Germany, and Italy — were on the trajectories that would shape their entire medieval and modern histories. France was on its way to becoming the most centralized of the major European monarchies. Germany was on its way to becoming the loosest of them. Italy was on its way to becoming the most fragmented, with the popes, the German emperors, the Lombard cities, and the Norman south all contesting the peninsula until Italian unification in 1861.

The Verdun partition is the deepest single fact about the political geography of western Europe.

---

The other Carolingian inheritance worth noting is institutional. The Carolingian Empire never functioned as a centralized state in the modern sense — it had no permanent capital after Aachen was lost to the Lothair line, no professional bureaucracy, no standing army, no regular taxation system. What it had was a network of **counts** (the local royal officials who governed individual administrative districts called **counties**, plural Latin *comitatus*), **dukes** (military commanders of frontier or composite regions), **bishops** (who exercised both ecclesiastical and quasi-secular authority over their dioceses), and **abbots** (heads of the great monasteries, often with substantial landed property and political influence). The whole apparatus was held together by the personal authority of the king, the periodic visits of the **missi dominici** ("envoys of the lord," royal inspectors sent in pairs — usually one bishop and one count — to tour the districts and enforce royal decrees), and the network of capitularies (royal legislation on specific matters).

Under a strong monarch like Charlemagne, this worked. Under weak monarchs in the mid- and late-ninth century facing mounting Viking, Magyar, and Saracen pressure (Chapter 7), it didn't. The counts began converting their public offices into private hereditary fiefs. The bishops did the same for their dioceses. Local landowners who could afford to build fortified residences — the early **castles** — began controlling their immediate territory more or less independently of any royal authority. By 900 the counts of West Francia and East Francia were no longer royal officials; they were independent regional dynasts who happened to acknowledge the king as a feudal superior. The political fragmentation that defines the central Middle Ages was beginning.

This is the **proto-feudal** transition that will dominate Chapter 7. The structural cause was the failure of Carolingian centralized authority to defend against the ninth-century triple shock from outside. The institutional consequence was that public power got fragmented downward into private hands. The new model — local magnates exercising public authority through privately controlled castles and warriors — would be the basic political architecture of western Europe until the centralized monarchies began re-asserting royal authority in the late twelfth and thirteenth centuries.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Carolingian Empire was breaking up in the mid-ninth century, the **Tang Dynasty** in China was at its mature golden classical period and beginning to face its own imperial overstretch problems with the An Lushan Rebellion (Tang-Song China). The **Abbasid Caliphate** was at the height of its cultural and political power under al-Mu'tasim and al-Wathiq, with Baghdad as the largest city in the world (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4). The **Byzantine Empire** was just beginning the long Macedonian Renaissance recovery that would restore much of its former power (Byzantine Empire Ch 5). In the **Andes**, the **Tiwanaku** and **Wari** empires were beginning to weaken (middle-horizon-empires). In **Mesoamerica**, the great Classic Maya cities had collapsed within the previous half-century (Maya Civilization Ch 7), and the Postclassic Toltec and Aztec successor cultures were emerging. The world that the post-Carolingian west was joining was, in absolute terms, smaller and poorer than the worlds of the Tang and the Abbasids — but it was on a steeper upward trajectory than either, and the gap would close fast in the coming three centuries.

# Chapter 7 — The Triple Shock: Vikings, Magyars, Saracens

The hundred-and-fifty years from roughly 793 to 955 are the closest thing the early medieval west has to a sustained external crisis, and they are often the thing modern readers know best about the period because the raiders involved have outsize cultural footprints. From the North Sea came the **Vikings** — Scandinavian raiders in clinker-built longships who sacked monasteries, conquered chunks of England and Ireland, founded Russia, and reached Newfoundland. From the Hungarian plain came the **Magyars** — Finno-Ugric horse archers who galloped deep into Italy, Germany, and even northern France in lightning summer raids. And from Sicily and Spain came the **Saracens** — Muslim pirate fleets who sacked Rome, kidnapped the abbot of Cluny, and made the western Mediterranean a Muslim lake.

Three different enemies, attacking from three different directions, all hitting western Europe at roughly the same time. The Carolingian state could not stop any of them. The response — the proto-feudal transformation, the spread of the castle, the rise of the mounted knight, the conversion of public authority into private hands — defined European political structure for the next four centuries.

This chapter is about the shock and the response.

---

We start with the Vikings, because they came first and they are the most famous. The Viking Age proper is treated at chapter length in viking-age — eight chapters covering the Norse world from the longship to Stamford Bridge in 1066. This chapter covers the same material from the perspective of the European victims rather than the Norse raiders.

The conventional opening date is **June 8, 793**, when Northumbrian monks at the monastery of **Lindisfarne** on a tidal island off the northeastern English coast looked up from their morning duties to see dragon-prowed wooden boats running up the beach. The raiders killed some of the monks, dragged others into the sea and drowned them, looted the church treasures, and were gone before any local force could react. The chronicler **Symeon of Durham**, writing centuries later, called it the moment when "the heathens miserably ravaged God's church on Lindisfarne with plunder and slaughter." The Northumbrian scholar **Alcuin of York**, then at Charlemagne's court, wrote a horrified letter to King Æthelred: "Never before has such a terror appeared in Britain as we have now suffered from a pagan race. Behold the Church of St. Cuthbert spattered with the blood of the priests of God, despoiled of its ornaments."

It was a small event in absolute terms. A handful of monks dead, perhaps a dozen captured, modest plunder. But the symbolic meaning — that the sea, which had always been a barrier protecting the Christian west, was now a highway being used by somebody on the other side — propagated through the Latin world and was the first sign of what was coming. (Viking Age Ch 2 covers Lindisfarne and the early western raids in detail.)

For the next two decades the raids stayed at the small-fleet level — a handful of longships hitting an unprotected coastal monastery, looting it, leaving. Iona was sacked in 795 and again in 802 and 806. The Frankish coast was raided. The Frisian coast (the Low Countries) was raided. Charlemagne organized coastal defenses but the Frankish state had no navy in any serious sense, and a Carolingian land army could never assemble fast enough to catch a Viking raid.

Then, in the 830s and 840s — coincident with the civil wars among Louis the Pious's sons — the Vikings shifted gears. The fleets got larger, the raiders started staying ashore over winter rather than retreating in autumn, and the targets started including not just monasteries but cities. **Dublin** was founded as a Norse fortified base in 841 (and would become the largest slave market in northern Europe within a century — Viking Age Ch 7). **Paris** was sacked in 845 and the Frankish king Charles the Bald paid the Vikings 7,000 pounds of silver to leave. The Vikings came back. They sacked Paris again in 856. They sacked it again in 861. By 865 a coalition of Danish armies — the **Great Heathen Army**, an enormous combined force probably numbering several thousand warriors — landed in East Anglia and within a decade had destroyed three of the four major Anglo-Saxon kingdoms: Northumbria fell in 867 (with King Ælla famously killed by being subjected to the "blood eagle," though the historicity of this Norse execution method is contested), East Anglia fell in 869 (with King Edmund killed), and Mercia fell in 874.

By 878 only **Wessex** under King **Alfred** survived as an independent Anglo-Saxon kingdom.

---

**Alfred the Great** (ruled 871–899) is the only Anglo-Saxon king to be called "the Great" and one of the few medieval English kings to be remembered for his administrative work rather than just his battles. He was the youngest son of King Æthelwulf of Wessex; his elder brothers had all reigned briefly and died, mostly young, in the wars against the Vikings. By the time he became king in 871 at the age of 22, Wessex was the last surviving Anglo-Saxon kingdom and the Danish Great Heathen Army was preparing to finish it off.

The decisive moment came in the winter of 878. A Viking force under the warlord **Guthrum** launched a surprise winter attack on Alfred's headquarters at Chippenham in early January, catching the Wessex government off guard during the Christmas season when armies were not supposed to campaign. Alfred fled with a small bodyguard into the marshes of Athelney in Somerset and spent the late winter as a hunted refugee. (The famous story of King Alfred letting a peasant woman's cakes burn while hiding in her hut is from a 12th-century life of Saint Neot and probably invented, but the underlying historical fact — that the king of Wessex was reduced for several months in 878 to hiding in the swamps with a handful of loyalists — is real.)

Alfred recovered. He summoned a small army, marched it across Wiltshire, and confronted Guthrum's main force at the **Battle of Edington** in May 878. Alfred won. He pursued Guthrum to his fortified base, besieged it, and forced Guthrum's surrender. The terms of the **Peace of Wedmore** that followed were dramatic: Guthrum and thirty of his senior commanders accepted Christian baptism (Alfred standing as Guthrum's godfather), and the Danish army accepted partition of England into a Wessex-controlled south and west and a Danish-controlled north and east — the **Danelaw**.

The Danelaw is a fact every English speaker still encounters daily. The English place names ending in **-by** (Whitby, Derby, Grimsby), **-thorpe** (Scunthorpe, Mablethorpe), and **-toft** are all Norse in origin, marking villages founded or held by Danish settlers in the ninth and tenth centuries. The English vocabulary loaned from Norse includes some of the most basic words in the language: **sky**, **leg**, **knife**, **ill**, **call**, **happy**, the pronouns **they**, **them**, **their** (which replaced the Old English equivalents *hi*, *him*, *hira* over the late-Saxon centuries), and the verb **to be** in its plural form ("are" is a Norse loan replacing the Old English *sind*). The word **law** itself is a Norse loan; the older English word was *æ*. English is, in a real linguistic sense, an Anglo-Norse hybrid language, with the Anglo half winning the framework but the Norse half contributing a remarkable amount of the high-frequency vocabulary.

After Edington, Alfred consolidated. He codified West Saxon law in his own law-code. He built a defensive system of fortified towns called **burhs** (BURGS, the source of modern English "borough" and German *Burg*) — about 30 of them across Wessex, each within a day's march of any other so that no Viking force could move very far without being intercepted. The burh system was financed by a tax called the **burhbot** assessed on landholders proportional to the length of wall they were responsible for maintaining. He founded the English navy, building a fleet of ships specifically designed to outclass Viking longships. And he sponsored a translation program — Latin classics rendered into Old English — that included Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, Gregory the Great's *Pastoral Care*, parts of Augustine, and several others. He claimed to have done some of the translations himself, which is at least plausible — the prefaces survive and are written in his name.

Alfred died in 899, having stabilized Wessex and laid the foundation for the gradual reconquest of the Danelaw under his son Edward the Elder and grandson Athelstan. By 927, Athelstan had absorbed all of the Danelaw and was calling himself "King of the English" — the first ruler to use that title meaningfully. The unification of England as a single kingdom is, in this sense, a Viking-era achievement: the Danish destruction of all the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms except Wessex made political consolidation possible in a way that the heptarchic patchwork of seventh-century England never had been.

---

The Frankish response to the Vikings was less successful than the Wessex response, mostly because the post-Carolingian state was too fragmented to mount the kind of coordinated defense Wessex could.

The pattern that emerged in West Francia in the late ninth century: a Viking fleet would sail up the Seine or the Loire, raid the cities and monasteries along the river, demand a payment of silver to leave, leave, come back the following year. The king's army (when there was one) usually arrived too late. Local counts and bishops increasingly took matters into their own hands, building castles and raising private armies to defend their districts.

The episode that crystallized this dynamic was the **Siege of Paris** in 885–886. A massive Viking fleet of perhaps 700 ships and 30,000 men under the warlord Sigfred sailed up the Seine in November 885 and besieged Paris — at this time a small fortified city on the Île de la Cité in the middle of the Seine, plus two fortified bridges connecting it to the riverbanks. The defense was led by **Count Odo** of Paris (later known as Odo I, king of the Franks), who held the city through a winter and spring of intermittent assault. Emperor Charles the Fat — supposedly the senior Frankish monarch — eventually arrived in October 886 with an enormous army that should have crushed the Vikings outright. Instead, he negotiated. He paid the Vikings 700 pounds of silver and gave them permission to ravage Burgundy (a region currently held by his rebellious nephew). The Vikings happily took both the silver and the permission and went off to plunder Burgundy.

Charles the Fat's settlement was so humiliating that it shattered his political authority. Within a year his nephew Arnulf of Carinthia had deposed him; he died in disgrace in 888. The unified Carolingian Empire died with him.

The pattern of paying off Viking raiders to leave — institutionalized in England as the **Danegeld** ("Dane-payment") — was the most expensive way possible to handle the problem. The Vikings always came back for more. The actual long-term solution was the one Alfred had pursued: build defensive fortifications, raise local forces, and when possible negotiate not just payments but settlements.

The ultimate Frankish version of the Alfred solution came in 911. The West Frankish king **Charles the Simple** (a grandson of Charles the Bald, ruled 898–922) faced a Norse warlord named **Rollo** (REL-loh, from Norse *Hrólfr*) who had been raiding the lower Seine for fifteen years and was effectively beyond the kingdom's military reach. By the **Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte** in 911, Charles ceded the lower Seine valley to Rollo as a hereditary fief on condition that Rollo accept baptism, hold the territory in vassalage to the Frankish king, and stop the raids. Rollo accepted.

The ceded territory became **Normandy** — "Land of the Northmen" — and Rollo and his successors became the **Dukes of Normandy**, technically vassals of the French crown but in practice independent. Within four generations the Normans had Frenchified completely (their language, their religion, their political customs were all Frankish by 1000) but retained the Viking aggressive military tradition. The Normans would conquer England in 1066 (a story for high-medieval-europe Ch 1), conquer southern Italy and Sicily in the 1060s and 1070s, and become one of the most expansionist political-military groups in the high medieval world. The chain: a Viking warlord makes a settlement deal in 911, his great-great-great-grandson conquers England in 1066. Rollo's bones are still buried in Rouen Cathedral.

The Norse east — the **Rus'** — also founded states during this period. Scandinavian traders and warriors sailing down the great Russian rivers established a fortified base at **Novgorod** under the legendary chieftain **Rurik** in 862, then moved south to take control of the strategic crossroads at **Kyiv** by 882. The **Kievan Rus'** state that grew from this Norse-Slavic synthesis became the ancestor of Russia, Belarus, and Ukraine. Rus' grand prince **Vladimir the Great** accepted Eastern Orthodox Christianity in 988, married the Byzantine emperor's sister Anna, and brought the entire Rus' state into the Byzantine cultural and religious sphere — choosing the Greek tradition over the Latin, with consequences that have shaped Russian-European relations to the present day. (The Cyrillic alphabet, the Eastern Orthodox liturgy, the political ideology of caesaropapism — all came in with the Vladimir conversion. Viking Age Ch 4 covers the Rus' eastward thrust in detail.)

---

While the Vikings were attacking from the north, the **Magyars** were attacking from the east.

The Magyars (sometimes confusingly called "Hungarians" — they are the same people, the modern Hungarian state takes its English name from a different etymology, but the people call themselves *Magyar*) were a nomadic confederation speaking a **Finno-Ugric** language. Finno-Ugric is the language family that also includes Finnish and Estonian (and the Sami languages of the Arctic) — completely unrelated to the Indo-European languages spoken by every other people in early medieval Europe. The Magyars had spent centuries on the Eurasian steppe before being pushed west by the **Pechenegs** (another Turkic steppe people) in the 890s; they crossed the Carpathians under their leader **Árpád** in 895 and settled in the Hungarian plain, the broad flat fertile basin where they have lived ever since. Their conquest of the Hungarian plain probably extinguished the surviving Avar population and pushed back the Slavic peoples already there.

Almost as soon as they arrived, the Magyars began raiding deep into western Europe. Their style of warfare was the standard steppe-nomad combination of mounted archers and lightning maneuver — armies of perhaps 5,000–20,000 horsemen, capable of covering 30–50 miles a day, hitting western European armies on terms that the western armies (built around heavy infantry and slower cavalry) could not effectively counter. Between 899 and 955 the Magyars launched an estimated 33 major raiding expeditions into northern Italy, Bavaria, Saxony, Burgundy, and even northern France. In 924 they reached the Atlantic coast at Provence. In 937 a Magyar raid penetrated as far as the Loire valley in France. They besieged Pavia (in northern Italy) repeatedly, sacked Brescia and Verona, and burned dozens of monasteries. Frankish chronicles describe the Magyar raids in language reminiscent of the Mongol terror that would arrive 300 years later: lightning attacks out of nowhere, slaughter of populations, abduction of women and children for the slave market, withdrawal before any defense could be organized.

The Magyar raids were the eastern half of the ninth-century triple shock. They were not stopped until the German king Otto I crushed them at the Battle of Lechfeld in 955 (Chapter 8). Until then, the eastern German lands (Bavaria, Saxony, Franconia) and northern Italy lived under permanent Magyar threat for two generations.

---

And from the south came the **Saracens**.

"Saracen" (SAIR-uh-suhn) was the Latin medieval term for any Muslim raider — derived obscurely from a Greek word for an Arabian tribe, but used in Western chronicles to mean essentially "the Muslim pirates raiding our coasts." The Saracen problem in the early medieval west had two main sources.

The first was **Sicily**. Byzantine Sicily had been a wealthy province in the early medieval Mediterranean — large grain production, important sea-lane control. The **Aghlabid** emirs of Tunisia (the Muslim dynasty that controlled the central North African coast in the ninth century) began the conquest of Sicily in 827 and completed it over the next 75 years: Palermo fell in 831, Syracuse in 878, Taormina in 902. Muslim Sicily would become one of the wealthiest and most culturally productive provinces of the medieval Mediterranean — a sophisticated Arabic-Greek-Latin trilingual society at the same time it was a base for raiding the Italian mainland. The Saracen raids on the Italian peninsula throughout the ninth and tenth centuries were largely launched from Sicily.

The most famous of these raids was the **Sack of Rome in 846**. An Aghlabid Muslim raiding fleet sailed up the Tiber in August 846, looted the basilicas of St. Peter and St. Paul outside the Aurelian Walls (which were outside the city's main defensive perimeter), and stripped the tombs of the apostles. They left without entering the inner city, but the symbolic shock was enormous: the burial place of Peter, the spiritual center of Latin Christendom, had been desecrated by Muslim raiders. Pope Leo IV responded by enclosing the Vatican Hill in a new defensive perimeter called the **Leonine Wall**, completed in 852. It is the first time St. Peter's was given fortified protection in its eight centuries of existence; previously the Aurelian Walls of imperial Rome had been considered sufficient.

The second main source was a small Muslim base on the **Provençal coast** at a place called **Fraxinetum** (modern La Garde-Freinet near Saint-Tropez), established around 887 by Muslim raiders from al-Andalus. Fraxinetum was tiny — perhaps a few hundred fighters — but it sat in a strategic position from which raiders could strike at any monastery, town, or trade route in southern France, Provence, Liguria, or the western Alps. Fraxinetum raiders kidnapped the abbot of **Cluny** (Maiolus) in 972, demanding a ransom of 1,000 pounds of silver — the kidnapping that finally galvanized the Provençal lords to act. A coalition under William of Provence destroyed Fraxinetum in 973.

The cumulative effect of the Saracen raids was that the western Mediterranean became an unsafe sea throughout the ninth and tenth centuries. The Italian coastal cities (other than the few like Venice that could maintain their own naval forces) saw repeated raids. Sardinia and Corsica were repeatedly looted. The southern French coast was lightly defended. Maritime trade contracted. The line between "Christian Mediterranean" and "Muslim Mediterranean" hardened, with the Italian peninsula and the islands forming a contested zone in between.

Islamic Golden Age covers the same Saracens from the Muslim side: the Sicilian Aghlabids, the Andalusian Umayyads, and the broader Mediterranean dynamics from a Caliphate-centric perspective. From the Latin-Christian early-medieval perspective, the Saracens were one part of the three-front siege.

---

The response to the triple shock was the **proto-feudal** transformation of European political structure.

The basic mechanics: the central state could not protect against the raiders. Local strongmen — counts who had inherited their offices from Carolingian appointees, regional bishops, ambitious smaller landholders — could, on a small scale, by building fortifications and raising local fighters. The arrangement that emerged was a contract: a man who could afford a horse and armor would swear personal **loyalty** (Latin *fidelitas*, the source of "fealty") to a more powerful man — a **lord** — in exchange for a grant of land (a **fief**, Latin *feodum*) sufficient to support him. The vassal owed military service — usually 40 days a year, fully equipped, including the cost of his horse, armor, and weapons — to his lord. The lord owed protection to his vassal. The arrangement was personal, sworn before God, and (in the early phase) lifetime rather than hereditary.

This is what historians eventually called **feudalism**, though the word itself is a 17th-century invention and modern medieval scholars are wary of it — the actual late-Carolingian arrangements varied enormously across regions and never formed the clean pyramidal "feudal system" of the textbooks. The cleaner formulation is that in the late-ninth and tenth centuries, public authority in much of western Europe was **privatized**: the functions previously exercised by the king's officials (justice, taxation, military service, defense) were converted into the private property of local landed magnates, who exercised them on the lands they controlled and held them by hereditary right.

The architectural signature of this transformation was the **castle**. The first true castles — privately owned fortified residences serving as both home and instrument of local political control — appeared in Frankish territory in the late ninth and tenth centuries. They were typically **motte-and-bailey** structures: a wooden tower (**motte**, French for "mound") on top of an artificial earthen mound, with a fortified courtyard (**bailey**) below containing stables, storerooms, and quarters for the garrison. They were quick to build (a few weeks for a small castle, requiring only timber and labor), defensible against the kind of raiding force that terrorized the late Carolingian world, and small enough that even a modest local lord could afford one. Within a century the European countryside was sprinkled with thousands of them. The landscape that the high medieval period inherits — the lord's castle on the hill, the village clustered below, the cathedral in the valley — was made by the proto-feudal response to the ninth-century triple shock.

The military companion piece was the **mounted knight**. A heavily armored cavalryman, mounted on a strong warhorse, equipped with a long lance for the couched charge, capable of breaking infantry formations and outrunning raiders, was the kind of military asset that could actually contest the Vikings (on land), the Magyars (with proper deployment), and the Saracens. But mounted knights were expensive — the horse, the armor, the weapons, and the years of training added up to a level of investment that only the landed nobility could afford. The proto-feudal arrangement of fiefs in exchange for military service was, in effect, the financing system that paid for the new mounted aristocracy. The lord granted his vassal enough land to support a knight's worth of equipment; the vassal showed up for military service when required.

The historian **Lynn White Jr.** argued in 1962 that the **stirrup** — introduced into western Europe via the steppe nomads in the seventh and eighth centuries — was the technological enabler that made the heavy cavalry charge possible and therefore the trigger for feudalism. Modern scholars treat the connection as much more diffuse than White's clean causal chain — the social transformation had many causes, and the rise of heavy cavalry was gradual rather than sudden — but the technological enabler is real. Without the stirrup, the couched-lance charge that defined medieval cavalry warfare cannot work; the rider has nothing to brace against and is unhorsed by his own weapon's impact.

---

By 950, then, western Europe had survived the triple shock, mostly through institutional improvisation. The Vikings had been settled (Normandy 911, Danelaw 878 onward), the Magyars were about to be defeated decisively (Lechfeld 955, Chapter 8), the Saracen bases were under pressure (Fraxinetum destroyed 973). The kingdoms had not been replaced by anything new; the Carolingian fragments were still the political framework. But the social and military structure inside those kingdoms had changed permanently. The mounted knight, the castle, the proto-feudal contract, the privatization of public authority — all of it would define European political life from 950 to roughly 1300, when the centralized monarchies began re-asserting royal power.

The next chapter is the start of the recovery.

Cross-civ check: the ninth-century triple shock that hammered the Latin West coincided with the **An Lushan Rebellion** (755–763) and its long aftermath that crippled Tang China (Tang-Song China), the **Abbasid civil wars** of the mid-ninth century that fragmented the Caliphate (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6), and the long crisis of the Maya Classic collapse in **Mesoamerica** (Maya Civilization Ch 7). The mid-ninth century is, by some recent climate-history arguments, a period of unusually difficult Northern Hemisphere weather and possibly volcanic-driven cooling — with social-political consequences from China to the Yucatán. The triple shock in Europe may have been one regional manifestation of a broader Eurasian-American crisis. Or it may have been bad luck. Both readings have respectable defenders in the recent literature.

# Chapter 8 — Ottonian Recovery and the Year 1000

The eastern Frankish kingdom that had been Louis the German's share of Verdun — the kingdom that would eventually become Germany — found itself by 900 in particularly bad shape. The Magyars were raiding annually. The Slavic peoples beyond the Elbe were pushing back against earlier Frankish-Saxon expansion. The royal authority had collapsed completely; the Carolingian East Frankish line ended in 911 with the death of the eight-year-old Louis the Child, and the German nobility had to elect new kings from their own ranks. The first elected king, Conrad I, was an unimpressive Franconian who could not control the dukes and died in 918 having achieved nothing. 

What followed was the **Ottonian** dynasty — the Saxon dukes who took the East Frankish throne in 919 and reinvented it. Within four generations they had defeated the Magyars decisively, revived the Roman imperial title in the West, sponsored a deep monastic reform, overseen the conversion of Hungary, Poland, and Russia to Christianity, and produced what historians sometimes call the **Ottonian Renaissance**. By the year 1000 the western European world had stabilized politically, religiously, and culturally. The threshold conditions for the High Middle Ages — the urban revival, the cathedrals, the universities, the Crusades — were all in place.

This chapter is the story of the recovery.

---

**Henry I the Fowler** (HEN-ree, ruled 919–936) was Duke of Saxony and the most powerful regional magnate in the East Frankish kingdom when Conrad I died. His nickname comes from a probably-apocryphal story about messengers finding him hunting birds when they arrived to inform him of his election as king. He was elected by an assembly of the German dukes — the first time a non-Frankish lineage had taken the throne — and his reign establishes the Ottonian dynasty.

Henry's accomplishments were structural rather than spectacular. He negotiated a nine-year truce with the Magyars in 924 by paying tribute, then used the pause to build a network of fortified frontier strongholds (German *Burgen*, related to Old English *burhs* — these are the fortified villages from which words like "Hamburg," "Magdeburg," and "Bamberg" derive their names) and to train his nobility in mounted warfare. When the truce expired in 933, Henry crushed a Magyar invasion at the **Battle of Riade**, in central Germany — the first significant Frankish military victory over the Magyars in nearly forty years. He brought Bohemia into nominal vassalage to the German crown. He established the precedent that the East Frankish (German) crown would be elective among the major dukes rather than hereditary in any one family.

Henry died in 936 and was succeeded by his son **Otto I**.

---

**Otto I** (ruled 936–973), called **the Great** — the first medieval ruler since Charlemagne to earn that epithet by general consensus — is the founder of the medieval German monarchy and arguably the second-most-important political figure in the early medieval west. He inherited his father's kingdom, faced down a series of internal revolts among his own family and the German dukes in the 940s and early 950s, defeated the Magyars decisively, conquered northern Italy, and was crowned Roman Emperor in 962. The institutional Holy Roman Empire that would survive (in various forms) until 1806 dates from Otto I's coronation.

The decisive military moment was the **Battle of Lechfeld** in 955.

The Magyars had launched their greatest single invasion of western Europe in summer 955 — perhaps 50,000 horsemen sweeping across Bavaria toward Augsburg. Otto assembled a coalition army of about 8,000 heavy cavalry from across the German duchies and met them on the plain of the **Lech River** (LEKH) outside Augsburg on August 10, 955. The battle was the decisive demonstration of what the new heavy-cavalry mounted-knight tactics could do against steppe horse archers. The Magyar archery was negated by the German heavy armor; the German charges shattered the Magyar formations; the Magyar leadership was killed or captured. The pursuit lasted for days and accounted for most of the surviving Magyar warriors.

Lechfeld ended the Magyar raids on western Europe. It was also a personal political triumph for Otto — he was acclaimed *Imperator* by his soldiers on the battlefield, the first time the title had been popularly conferred since the late Roman Empire. More important in long-term political effect, it consolidated Otto's authority over the German dukes (who had not always been reliable) and made possible the eastern Christianization of Hungary itself: the Magyars who had survived Lechfeld returned to the Hungarian plain, abandoned the lifestyle of seasonal nomadic raiding, and within a generation began the process of conversion to Latin Christianity that culminated in the coronation of Stephen I as Christian king of Hungary on Christmas Day 1000.

After Lechfeld, Otto turned south. The Italian peninsula was politically chaotic — the Lombard kingdom of Italy had effectively disintegrated, regional warlords were fighting over the imperial title, and the popes in Rome were being manipulated by the local Roman nobility. In 951 and 961–962 Otto led major expeditions into Italy. The 961 expedition was decisive: Otto entered Rome in early 962, was crowned Emperor by Pope John XII on February 2, and took the imperial title that had been effectively dormant since the death of Berengar I in 924. Pope John promptly tried to play Otto off against other Italian factions and was deposed by Otto in 963 — establishing the precedent that the German emperor could appoint and depose popes when the popes proved disloyal.

The 962 coronation is the conventional founding date of the **Holy Roman Empire** as a continuous institution. (Charlemagne's 800 coronation founded the idea but the institution did not survive in continuous form between 924 and 962.) The empire Otto created was a complex constitutional artifact: an elected German monarchy whose ruler was also king of Italy, claimed the Roman imperial inheritance, exercised theoretical authority over the universal Catholic church, and balanced uneasily between papal claims and German ducal autonomy. It would continue under the Ottonians through Otto II (973–983), Otto III (983–1002), and Henry II (1002–1024) before passing to the Salian dynasty in the eleventh century. Otto's institutional architecture would shape it through the next eight centuries.

---

The other Ottonian innovation worth noting is the rise of an imperial church — the **Reichskirche**, "imperial church," in German historiography. Otto's strategy for managing the German nobility was to lean heavily on the bishops and the major monastic abbots as instruments of royal authority. He appointed loyal clerics to bishoprics and abbacies; gave them substantial landed property and political authority within their dioceses; required them to maintain knights at royal service; and used them as administrators, ambassadors, and military commanders. The system worked because bishops were celibate and could not pass their offices and lands to legitimate sons — which meant that, unlike the secular nobility, they could not convert their fiefs into hereditary dynasties.

The Reichskirche made the Ottonian and Salian emperors substantially more powerful (and substantially more involved in church appointments) than the West Frankish kings of the same period. It also set up the long medieval struggle between emperor and pope over who had the authority to appoint bishops — the **Investiture Controversy** that would consume European politics in the eleventh and twelfth centuries (covered in high-medieval-europe).

---

Religious life in the tenth century was being transformed by a quieter revolution — the **Cluniac monastic reform**.

The abbey of **Cluny** (KLOO-nee, in Burgundy) was founded in 910 by Duke William the Pious of Aquitaine with an unusual charter: the abbey would be subject directly to the pope, free from all secular and even episcopal control. This independence — formally guaranteed by papal privilege — was the institutional innovation that made the Cluniac reform possible. Cluny did not have to compromise its monastic discipline to satisfy any local lord or bishop; it answered only to Rome, which was usually willing to leave it alone.

Under a series of remarkably long-lived abbots — **Odo** (927–942), **Maiolus** (954–994, the one kidnapped by Saracens at Fraxinetum), **Odilo** (994–1049), **Hugh** (1049–1109) — Cluny pushed for stricter observance of the Rule of Benedict, longer liturgical hours (the great Cluniac liturgy eventually consumed most of the monks' day), elaborate church architecture (the great church of Cluny III, built between 1088 and 1130, was the largest church in Christendom for several centuries until the new St. Peter's was built in Rome), and freedom of monasteries from secular interference. By 1100 Cluny had over a thousand daughter monasteries spread across western Europe, all under the direct authority of the abbot of Cluny — a network of religious houses larger than any ecclesiastical structure had ever been before.

The Cluniac reformers also pushed for changes in the broader church: an end to **simony** (the buying and selling of ecclesiastical offices), enforcement of **clerical celibacy** (parish priests through much of the early Middle Ages had been informally married), and a clearer separation between religious and secular authority. By the mid-eleventh century these arguments would produce the **Gregorian Reform** that fully established papal supremacy and triggered the Investiture Controversy with the Salian emperors. The Cluniac groundwork laid in the tenth century is what made the eleventh-century papal revolution possible.

---

The other great religious-political development of the late tenth century was the **Christianization of Northern and Eastern Europe**.

Through the eighth and ninth centuries, the Latin Christian world had ended somewhere east of the Elbe and somewhere north of Saxony. The peoples beyond — the West Slavs (Bohemians, Poles, Wends), the East Slavs (Russians, Ukrainians, Belarusians), the Magyars, the Norse, the Baltic peoples — were largely pagan. By 1050 most of them would be Christian. The conversion happened in a remarkable fifty-year burst between roughly 950 and 1050.

The Polish prince **Mieszko I** accepted baptism in 966 from Latin missionaries in Bohemia, marrying the Bohemian Christian princess Doubrava as part of the package, and brought the new Polish state into Latin Christendom. **Bohemia** itself had begun converting earlier under Boleslav I but was definitively Christianized by Mieszko's generation. **Hungary** was Christianized through the conversion of its grand prince Géza in the 970s and the formal coronation of his son **Stephen I** as Christian king on Christmas Day 1000, with a crown sent by Pope Sylvester II. **Norway** and **Denmark** became Christian under Olav Tryggvason and Sweyn Forkbeard respectively in the 990s and 1010s. **Sweden** followed in the eleventh century. **Iceland** voluntarily adopted Christianity by parliamentary vote at the **Althing** of 999 — possibly the only such case in history.

The exception was **Russia**, which converted to **Eastern Orthodox** Christianity rather than Latin. **Vladimir** (volo-DEE-mir, ruled c. 980–1015), grand prince of Kyiv, accepted baptism in 988 and married the Byzantine emperor's sister Anna. The Rus' Christianization brought the alphabet, the church architecture, and the political ideology of Byzantium rather than of Rome. The Latin-Cyrillic line bisecting modern Europe — running roughly through the Baltic, central Poland, the Carpathians, and down to the Adriatic — was drawn in the late tenth and early eleventh centuries by which missionaries got there first.

By 1050 western Christendom extended from the Atlantic to the Russian rivers, from Sicily to Iceland. It was, for the first time, a coherent religious-political-cultural unit at continental scale. Almost everything that the High Middle Ages would build — the Crusades, the cathedral schools turning into universities, the rise of the towns, the scholastic synthesis — assumed this Christianized continental unit as the platform.

---

The agricultural backdrop to all of this was an **agricultural revolution** that gathered pace through the early Middle Ages and reached its takeoff phase around the year 1000. The four key innovations:

- The **heavy mouldboard plow** — drawn by oxen and later horses, capable of turning the heavy clay soils of the north European plain that the older Mediterranean scratch-plow could not work. The north European agricultural heartland that had been marginal under the Romans became the breadbasket of medieval Europe.

- The **three-field crop rotation** — winter grain, spring grain, fallow — replacing the older two-field system. Increased the area planted each year from one-half to two-thirds of the total, and the spring crop (oats, legumes) added nitrogen to the soil and provided fodder for horses.

- The **rigid horse collar**, transmitted from China and Central Asia, allowed horses to pull heavy loads with their shoulders rather than choking against an ox-yoke around their throats. Combined with iron horseshoes and better breeds, it tripled the draft power of a working horse.

- **Watermills**, increasingly numerous and increasingly used for purposes beyond grinding grain — fulling cloth, sawing wood, hammering iron. The Domesday Book of 1086 would record over 6,000 mills in eleventh-century England alone. Europe was developing a deep mechanical-power infrastructure that no other contemporary civilization quite matched.

These four innovations spread across northern Europe between roughly 800 and 1100, with most of the spread happening in the tenth and eleventh centuries. Their cumulative effect was that European food production probably more than doubled between 800 and 1200, with corresponding effects on population (which roughly doubled in the same period from perhaps 30 million to perhaps 70 million), urban revival (the cities began growing again after their post-Roman collapse), and economic complexity. The High Middle Ages will run on the agricultural surplus that the early medieval centuries created.

---

The final image of the early medieval west should probably be the most famous: the **year 1000**.

The approach of the year 1000 — counting from the supposed birth of Christ — produced, in some western Christian communities, a wave of apocalyptic anxiety. Chroniclers reported popular fears that the millennium of Christ's birth or, more often, the millennium of his Resurrection (so 1033 rather than 1000) would bring the end of the world: the return of Christ, the Last Judgment, the end of human history. The French chronicler **Rodulfus Glaber** (ROD-ulf-us GLAB-er, c. 985–1047), writing fifty years later, described enthusiastic rebuilding of churches and pilgrimages to Jerusalem in the years before 1000 as evidence of millennial expectation. Other chronicles describe panicked expectations of the apocalypse.

How widespread the panic actually was remains hotly contested. The French historian **Jules Michelet** (jools mish-LAY) popularized "the terrors of the year 1000" in his nineteenth-century *History of France* as a kind of medieval doom-spectacle. Twentieth-century revisionist scholars, especially in mid-century French scholarship, dismissed Michelet's picture as overdrawn — most chronicles do not mention millennial fears, and the institutional church does not seem to have officially expected the apocalypse on any specific date. Recent scholarship since the 1990s has partly rehabilitated the millennial-anxiety reading, arguing that the popular religious-cultural mood of the late tenth century did include a genuine apocalyptic strain even if the institutional church played it down.

What is not contested is that the year 1000 was, by any reasonable historical metric, a turning point. The triple shock had ended. The Carolingian institutional inheritance had been recovered and extended by the Ottonian dynasty. The agricultural revolution was producing a sustained increase in population and surplus. The Christianization of northern and eastern Europe had created a continent-scale Latin-Christian civilizational unit. The proto-feudal order had stabilized into the recognizable medieval social structure of lords, knights, monasteries, and villages. The pope and the emperor were both established as universal authorities. The Cluniac reform was preparing the ground for the more radical religious transformations of the eleventh century. The cathedral schools at Reims, Chartres, Liège, and Magdeburg were becoming the institutional ancestors of the universities. The Italian maritime cities (Venice, Pisa, Genoa, Amalfi) were beginning to rebuild Mediterranean trade. The Jewish merchant communities of the Rhine valley, southern France, and Spain were building the long-distance commercial networks that would feed the High Medieval economic revival.

All of these threads are about to converge.

The year 1000 is the threshold of the High Middle Ages — the period of the urban revival, the Crusades, the Gothic cathedrals, the universities, Magna Carta, and the institutional foundations of modern Europe (high-medieval-europe Ch 1). The continent that crossed that threshold was, in 1000, far from the largest or richest civilization of Eurasia (the Tang-Song China and the Abbasid Caliphate were both demonstrably bigger and more sophisticated). But it was the one with the steepest forward trajectory. Within three centuries it would be building Notre Dame, founding the universities of Bologna and Paris, and writing Magna Carta. Within four centuries it would be sending Columbus across the Atlantic. Within seven, it would be running an industrial revolution that would absorb the entire planet.

The institutional materials of all that were assembled between 476 and 1000. That is what the early Middle Ages were for.

# Songhai Empire — Gao, Timbuktu, and the Matchlock at Tondibi

**Goal:** Narrative for the Songhai Empire (~1430–1591 CE), the third and largest of the great medieval West African gold empires, centered on the Niger Bend city of Gao. Covers the Songhai pre-history under the Za and Sonni dynasties, the breakaway from Mali in the 1430s, Sonni Ali Ber's brutal conquest of Timbuktu and Djenné, the 1493 coup that brought Askia Muhammad to the throne, the 1497 hajj that branded Songhai onto the Islamic world, the bureaucratic empire and Timbuktu's late peak, the long succession decay after Askia Dawud, and the 1591 Moroccan strike that destroyed the empire with matchlock muskets at Tondibi.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

A note on names and pronunciation: Songhai sat at the meeting point of several language families. Songhai itself is a Nilo-Saharan language unrelated to anything else in the western Sahel — as different from Mandinka or Wolof or Hausa as English is from Hungarian. Arabic was the language of religion, scholarship, and inter-imperial diplomacy. Mande-speaking Soninke and Mandinka scholars and merchants were a major part of the urban population. Berber and Tamasheq (Tuareg) were the languages of the Sahara fringe. Names are given in their most common English form, with pronunciations on first use. Spellings vary widely across sources — Songhai is also Songhay or Sonrai, Sonni Ali is also Sunni Ali Ber, Askia Muhammad is also Askiya Muhammad I. Dates are CE.

A note on "first" claims: as with every TL in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of." The medieval West African documentary record is thin — most of what we know runs through Arabic-speaking outsiders or through chronicles written a century after the events they describe. The principal narrative sources for Songhai are two Arabic-language chronicles composed in Timbuktu in the seventeenth century, the *Tarikh al-Sudan* of Abd al-Sadi (completed around 1655) and the *Tarikh al-Fattash* attributed to Mahmud Kati (begun around 1519, heavily revised later). Both were written by Timbuktu scholars from a perspective sympathetic to the Askia dynasty and hostile to Sonni Ali — a bias that shapes everything the chronicles say about the Sonni period. External sources are sparser: Leo Africanus visited Timbuktu in 1510, the Moroccan invaders left some accounts of the 1591 campaign, and a few Maghrebi geographies mention Songhai in passing. The reconstruction below tries to keep these source layers visible.

A note on a stubborn myth: Songhai is often presented as proof of "what West African civilization could have been if not for European colonialism" — a framing that gets the history backwards. Songhai was destroyed in 1591, more than three centuries before the European Scramble for Africa. The agent that ended Songhai was not European colonialism but a Moroccan Saadian army equipped with matchlock muskets — and the matchlock revolution itself was a global early-modern phenomenon that linked the Saadians, the Ottomans, the Safavids (Safavid Persia), the Mughals, and the gunpowder transformation that ran from Tang-Song China to the Habsburg armies of central Europe. Songhai's defeat at Tondibi was the local expression of a worldwide military revolution, not a consequence of European intervention. What European trade did do, in the long run, was reorient the West African gold flow away from the Sahara and toward the Atlantic coast, hollowing out the economic basis for any successor Sahelian empire. But that was a slow shadow, not the killing blow.

# Chapter 1 — The Songhai Before Songhai

The Songhai Empire was the third and largest of the three great medieval West African gold empires. Ghana came first, Mali came second, Songhai came third — and Songhai inherited the institutional architecture of both predecessors while building a much more centralized state on top of it. This chapter is about the foundation: the Niger Bend geography, the Songhai people themselves, the long-running Za and Sonni dynasties at the city of Gao, and the moment in the early fifteenth century when the Songhai stopped paying tribute to a fading Mali Empire and started running their own affairs.

A word on what is not in this chapter. The dramatic conquests, the brilliant generals, the gold-laden hajj, the matchlock catastrophe — all of that is coming in Chapters 2 through 8. This chapter is the slow build, the geography and the institutions and the dynastic backstory that made the rest possible. Skim it if you want; come back to it when later chapters reference something that needs grounding.

---

The geography first.

The **Sahel** (sah-HEL, from the Arabic word for "shore" or "coast" — the shore of the Sahara, the way you might say the coast of the sea) is a band of semi-arid grassland that runs east-to-west across Africa, separating the Sahara desert to the north from the wetter savanna and forest to the south. It is roughly 600 to 1,000 miles (1,000 to 1,600 km) wide, depending on where you draw the line, and it stretches for around 3,400 miles (5,500 km) from the Atlantic coast of Senegal to the Red Sea coast of Sudan. Songhai's heartland sat in the central part of this band, in what is now northeastern Mali and western Niger.

Cutting across the Sahel like a peculiarly shaped highway runs the **Niger River** (NYE-jer — not to be confused with the modern country called Niger, which takes its name from the river). The Niger is the third-longest river in Africa, after the Nile and the Congo. It has a famously strange course: it rises in the highlands of modern Guinea, only about 150 miles from the Atlantic coast, and then, instead of running west to the sea, it bends north into the Sahara. It runs north for nearly a thousand miles, brushing the southern edge of the desert at Timbuktu, before turning sharply southeast at the **Niger Bend**, then curving south through modern Niger and Nigeria, and finally reaching the Atlantic Ocean at the Niger Delta after a 2,600-mile (4,200 km) journey.

The shape of that arc is the single most important geographic fact in the history of the western Sudan. The Niger pushes north into the Sahel and then comes back south, depositing silt along an inland flood plain called the **Inner Niger Delta** (a strange name for a feature that is nowhere near the actual sea-coast delta — but the Inner Delta has the same braided-channel, seasonal-flood structure as a coastal river mouth, except that it sits 1,500 miles inland in the middle of the Sahel). The annual flood is enormous — millions of acres of seasonally flooded grassland — and it makes possible an unusually dense population in what would otherwise be marginal land. **Djenné** (jen-NAY), **Timbuktu** (tim-BUCK-too), **Gao** (GOW, rhymes with "now"), and the slightly more inland city of **Niani** (NYAH-nee, the Mali capital) all sit on or near this Niger arc. Every great empire of the western Sahel — Ghana, Mali, Songhai — built its political center on the Niger.

Songhai's specific niche on this geography was the eastern leg of the arc. Where Mali had been a Mandinka kingdom of the upper Niger and the Inner Delta, Songhai was a Songhai-speaking kingdom of the **Niger Bend** — the long stretch where the river runs east past Timbuktu before turning southeast at Gao and heading for modern Nigeria. This eastern position had two consequences. First, Songhai sat closer to the Hausa city-states of modern northern Nigeria and to the trans-Saharan caravan routes coming south from modern Algeria and Libya, which gave Songhai a different commercial orientation than Mali's western Bambuk-and-Bure gold focus. Second, the river was navigable and predictable along this stretch, which is why Sonni Ali's war fleet (Chapter 2) could become a strategic instrument in a way that no Mali ruler had ever quite managed.

The Niger also runs through the structural geography of the gold-and-salt trade, which is the economic bedrock of every Sahelian empire. Gold came from south of the Sahel, from alluvial deposits in the headwaters of the Senegal and Niger rivers — the **Bambuk** (BAM-buck) and **Bure** (boo-REH) gold fields, both on small upper tributaries of the Niger and Senegal river systems. Salt came from north of the Sahel, from dry lakebeds in the central Sahara — the great mining site of **Taghaza** (tag-HAH-zah), about 500 miles north of Timbuktu, was the most important. Camel caravans linked the two economies. The Sahelian empires were the middlemen, and the middleman position was extraordinarily lucrative.

Songhai, like Mali before it, did not control the gold mines themselves. The Bambuk and Bure miners were independent populations who jealously guarded their locations from any imperial center — a pattern that persisted from Ghana through Mali to Songhai. The empires got rich by taxing the gold as it passed through their markets on its way north, and by controlling the import of the salt and trade goods coming the other direction. This middleman model is essential to understanding both Songhai's wealth and its eventual vulnerability: the empire's revenues depended on the trans-Saharan trade flow, and anything that disrupted that flow — the Atlantic shift after 1471, the Moroccan strike at Taghaza in 1585, the post-Tondibi commercial collapse — threatened the foundation of state finance.

---

Now the people.

The **Songhai** (SONG-high, sometimes written Songhay or Sonrai) are a Nilo-Saharan-speaking people of the eastern Niger Bend. Their language is part of the **Nilo-Saharan** language family — an ancient and somewhat controversial grouping that links Songhai to Nubian (the language of medieval Christian Nubia, Kingdom of Aksum cousin), to Maasai in East Africa, and to several scattered languages across the Sahel and southern Sahara. Whether Nilo-Saharan is a single coherent family or a residual category for languages that don't fit elsewhere is still debated by linguists. What matters for our purposes is that Songhai is **not** a Mande language — it is unrelated to Mandinka, Soninke, Bambara, and the other Mande tongues of Mali — and it is **not** a Berber language. As different from Mandinka as English is from Hungarian, and from Berber as English is from Arabic. The Songhai were a genuinely distinct people from their Mali neighbors, even though they had lived alongside the Mandinka for centuries and had borrowed many institutional patterns from them.

The Songhai homeland was a fishing, farming, and trading society along the eastern Niger long before any state apparatus had emerged. The river supported a specialized fishing population — the **Sorko**, river-dwelling Songhai who lived in stilt villages and made their living from catfish, perch, and tilapia smoked and traded inland. Other Songhai groups farmed millet, sorghum, and rice along the floodplain, herded cattle on the dry-season pastures, and traded along the river with Soninke and Wangara merchants from the west. The political organization was loose — village heads, regional war-chiefs, traditional ritual specialists — and unified state structure did not appear until the Za dynasty established a kingship at Gao.

A note on names. "Songhai" refers to the people, the language, and the empire. "Songhay" is an alternative English spelling, and the spelling "Songhay" is preferred in some recent scholarly works. "Sonrai" is an older French rendering, occasionally used in older texts. The political entity was called by multiple names in different periods — the Gao Kingdom in the early period, the Songhai Empire after the breakaway from Mali, the Askia state in some chronicle usage. Use whichever you like; they all refer to the same broad civilizational tradition.

---

The kingship at Gao goes back to perhaps the eighth century.

**Gao** sits on the eastern Niger Bend, just upstream from the point where the river turns south for its long run toward modern Nigeria. The location was a natural commercial center: south-facing for Sahel trade, west-facing for the river network running through Timbuktu and the Inner Delta, north-facing for trans-Saharan caravan routes coming down from modern Algeria and Libya through the central Sahara. Camel caravans had been crossing the Sahara since at least the third century CE — the systematic introduction of the dromedary camel to the Sahara from the Roman-Arabian world had made regular trans-Saharan trade possible for the first time, opening the gold-salt circuit that would underwrite every Sahelian empire from the eighth century forward. Gao was one of the southern terminuses of this circuit.

Excavations at Gao-Saney, just northwest of the modern city, have revealed monumental stone-and-mud-brick tombs of Muslim rulers from the eleventh century onward — including stelae with inscriptions in Arabic, attesting royal Islamic burial practice as early as the 1080s. The archaeology suggests a substantial commercial city by the eleventh and twelfth centuries, with stone-built royal residences, a large Friday mosque, and active trading quarters housing North African and Andalusian merchants. By any reasonable measure, Gao was a working city well before any of the dramatic political events of this story occurred.

The traditional dynastic history of Gao starts with the **Za dynasty** (also spelled Dia or Dya). The Za kings ruled Gao from perhaps the eighth century. Their traditional founder, **Za Alayaman**, is a semi-legendary figure said to have come from Yemen — a typical post-conversion Islamicizing genealogical motif (after kings convert to Islam, their dynastic histories often retroactively acquire Yemeni or Arabian origins, claiming a connection to the Prophet's homeland) rather than verifiable history. What is verifiable is that a royal lineage at Gao, which the chroniclers called Za, controlled the kingdom through the high middle ages.

The Gao kings converted to Islam around 1010 CE. The conversion was elite and commercial — useful for trans-Saharan trade and diplomatic recognition by Maghrebi states — and coexisted for centuries with traditional Songhai religious practices among the rural population. This is the same layered religious pattern we see in Mali (Mali Empire Ch 5): a Muslim royal court and merchant class sitting on top of a peasant population that practiced traditional religion, with intermarriage and ritual borrowing in both directions. Gao's Islamization predates Sundiata's founding of Mali by more than two centuries.

Around 1325, Gao came under the control of Mali. Mansa Musa's eastern campaigns (Mali Empire Ch 4) brought the Niger Bend cities into the Mali Empire as tributaries. For roughly a century thereafter, the Songhai paid tribute to the mansas at Niani and supplied military contingents to Mali's wars, while preserving their own ruling dynasty, language, and local administration. Gao's tributary period under Mali is poorly documented — neither Mandinka oral tradition nor Songhai oral memory had much reason to dwell on it — but it was real, and it was the political experience the Sonni dynasty would eventually overthrow.

---

The **Sonni dynasty** (also spelled Sunni — no relation to the Sunni branch of Islam) replaced the Za as rulers of Gao around the late thirteenth century. The chronicle tradition credits **Sonni Sulayman Dama** as the dynasty's founder. The Sonni held Gao through the fourteenth century as a tributary of Mali and through the early fifteenth century as something closer to a satellite kingdom — paying nominal tribute, maintaining their own administration, and watching for the moment when Mali's central authority would weaken enough that the tribute could be quietly stopped.

That moment came in the 1430s. Mali was simultaneously losing Timbuktu to Tuareg raiders (1433), losing the western provinces to the rising Wolof and Tukulor kingdoms, and consumed by a long succession dispute among the Keita royal lineage. The Sonni under **Sonni Sulayman Dandi** simply stopped paying tribute around 1430, and Mali did not have the military capacity to compel resumption. There was no dramatic moment of rebellion; Songhai independence was a quiet fact on the ground that took several decades for both sides to fully recognize.

The Tuareg occupation of Timbuktu from 1433 to 1468 is worth a paragraph because it sets up Sonni Ali's signature military achievement. The **Tuareg** (TWAH-reg, sometimes Touareg) are a Berber-speaking nomadic people of the central and western Sahara. They speak **Tamasheq**, a Berber language closely related to the languages of modern Morocco and Algeria and unrelated to either Songhai or the Mande languages — the Berbers had spread south into the Sahara from the Maghreb over the previous two millennia and were the dominant pastoralist population of the desert by the medieval period. Tuareg society was structured around camel-herding, caravan-running, and intermittent raiding of settled Sahelian populations whenever central authority weakened. When Mali's grip on Timbuktu loosened in the 1420s, Tuareg raiders under **Akil ag Malwal** seized the city around 1433 and held it for thirty-five years, taxing the trade and broadly leaving the urban population alone but providing no central authority capable of restoring imperial order. Timbuktu's Sanhaja Berber clerical families — the scholarly lineages that had run the city under Mali — adapted to Tuareg taxation while keeping the trade flowing and the manuscripts copying. This is the situation Sonni Ali would inherit when he took the throne in 1464.

---

Before the curtain rises on Sonni Ali, a pause for cross-civilizational context, because the western Sudan in the 1430s sits inside a much larger network than is usually appreciated.

When the Songhai were quietly stopping tribute payments to Mali, the **Ottoman Empire** (Ottoman Empire Ch 2) had recently taken Constantinople — the city would fall in 1453, fundamentally restructuring the eastern Mediterranean. **Ming China** was at the height of its early-fifteenth-century power, with **Zheng He's treasure fleets** having just completed their last voyage in 1433 (the same year the Tuareg took Timbuktu, by coincidence). The **Aztec Triple Alliance** had just been founded in 1428 (Aztec Empire Ch 3), the same decade Sonni Sulayman Dandi was breaking from Mali. The **Inca Empire** was beginning its great expansion under Pachacuti, who would come to the throne around 1438 and transform Cuzco into a continental capital. **Renaissance Florence** was producing Brunelleschi's dome, Donatello's statues, and the first cracks of European humanism. The **Mamluk Sultanate** in Egypt was at its institutional peak, the **Timurid Empire** was collapsing into successor states across central Asia, and the **Joseon dynasty** in Korea was inventing the hangul alphabet under King Sejong.

Songhai did not know about most of these civilizations, and most of them did not know about Songhai. The Mediterranean and Islamic worlds knew that gold flowed north across the Sahara from somewhere in the western Sudan, but the political map of the Sahel was hazy in Cairo and almost entirely unknown in Florence. Mali's reputation from the Mansa Musa era was still alive in European cartography (Mali Empire Ch 4), but Mali itself had been reduced to a regional power. The world that would briefly turn its attention to the Niger Bend two and a half centuries later — the Saadian Morocco of Ahmad al-Mansur — did not yet exist as an organized state.

# Chapter 2 — Sonni Ali the Great

In 1464, the Sonni throne at Gao passed to a young prince in his early twenties named **Sonni Ali Ber** (SOH-nee AH-lee BEHR — "Ber" means "the Great" in Songhai, an epithet earned later, not part of the original name). For the next twenty-eight years until his death in 1492, Sonni Ali waged nearly continuous war across the western Sudan. He captured Timbuktu in 1468. He took Djenné after a seven-year siege in 1473. He campaigned against the Tuareg, the Mossi, the Dogon, and remnants of Mali. He built a war fleet on the Niger River. He attempted ambitious hydraulic projects. He ran a religious court that mixed traditional Songhai kingship rituals with public Islamic observance. And he became, in the later Timbuktu chronicles, the great villain of West African Islamic history — a brutal pagan tyrant who slaughtered scholars and trampled the law.

The reality is more complicated, as we will see. But the chronicle tradition is the source we have to work with, and the chronicle tradition is unanimous: Sonni Ali was the warrior-king who made Songhai an empire, and he was a monster.

---

The capture of Timbuktu in 1468 is the founding act.

By 1468, the Tuareg occupation of Timbuktu had lasted thirty-five years. The city was paying tribute to Akil ag Malwal's confederation, the Tuareg garrison was rotating through the city without occupying it permanently, and the local scholarly establishment — the Sanhaja Berber clerical families who had run Timbuktu's religious and educational life since the Mansa Musa era — had developed a workable accommodation with the Tuareg overlords. The arrangement was less satisfactory to the city's Songhai population, who were taxed without political voice, and to Sonni Ali, who saw the Tuareg garrison as a foreign occupation of what he considered Songhai territory.

The chief qadi of Timbuktu at this point was **Muhammad Naddi**, head of the Aqit family, the most prominent Sanhaja Berber clerical lineage. According to the *Tarikh al-Sudan*, Muhammad Naddi or one of his colleagues invited Sonni Ali to enter the city peacefully and remove the Tuareg garrison, expecting that the Sonni regime would be a more comfortable overlord than the Tuareg had been. Sonni Ali agreed and brought his army to Timbuktu in 1468. The Tuareg garrison withdrew without a fight. The city was Songhai's.

What happened next is the founding act of the Sonni Ali villain narrative. According to al-Sadi's chronicle, Sonni Ali's forces sacked the scholarly quarter at **Sankoré** (san-koh-RAY), killed many of the Sanhaja Berber clerics including members of the Aqit lineage, confiscated property from the Timbuktu scholarly families, and forced others into exile. The chronicle describes the violence in detail — specific scholars killed, specific property confiscated, specific exiles documented. Al-Sadi was working from oral memory preserved by the surviving scholarly families and from documentary fragments, two centuries after the events; the broad outlines of the violence are probably accurate, though the specific numbers may be inflated.

Why did Sonni Ali turn on the scholars who had invited him in? The likely answer is a combination of political and religious motives. Politically, the Sanhaja scholars had been the administrative class under the Tuareg occupation; they were the institutional infrastructure of a regime Sonni Ali was now displacing, and their continued power would have constrained his authority. Religiously, the Sankoré clerics represented an orthodox Maliki Sunnism that was hostile to Sonni Ali's syncretic blend of Islam and traditional Songhai religion. And personally, Sonni Ali seems to have despised the Sanhaja Berbers as a cultural matter — as desert-dwelling foreigners who had been running his river city without his permission. The combination produced atrocity.

The Timbuktu chroniclers, two centuries later, would frame the Sankoré massacre as the defining act of Sonni Ali's reign. **Abd al-Sadi** in the *Tarikh al-Sudan* calls Sonni Ali "the celebrated villain" and accumulates page after page of detailed crimes — scholars killed, women violated, property confiscated, mosques desecrated. Al-Sadi was the descendant of one of the surviving scholarly families, writing from inside the institutional memory that Sonni Ali had tried to destroy, and his hostility is total. The *Tarikh al-Fattash* takes a similar line. Both chronicles need to be read as the partisan testimony of a survivor community, not as neutral description. But the basic event — a violent takedown of Timbuktu's scholarly class in 1468–69 — is well-attested and historical.

---

The capture of Djenné in 1473 was the second great conquest, and it took much longer.

Djenné sits on a small island in the Bani River, a tributary of the Niger, in the southwestern corner of the Inner Niger Delta. The island position made it almost uniquely defensible by Sahelian standards: the seasonal flood surrounded the city most of the year, and the only land approach was a narrow causeway that could be defended with a small garrison. Djenné had been a major Inner Delta commercial center since well before Mali's expansion (the Djenné-Djenno archaeological site, just south of the medieval city, has urban occupation going back to roughly 250 BCE — older than Ghana itself), and it controlled the river network that ran from the gold fields up through Timbuktu and on to Gao. Whoever held Djenné held the Inner Delta economy.

The chronicle tradition holds that Sonni Ali's siege of Djenné lasted **seven years and seven months and seven days** — a suspiciously folkloric set of numbers that almost certainly stylize what was actually a long but more conventional military investment. The reality was probably a multi-year campaign, with intermittent active siege punctuated by periods of withdrawal and reinforcement, against a city protected by its island position from any conventional Sahelian army. What allowed Sonni Ali to eventually take Djenné was something no previous Sahelian ruler had managed: a war fleet on the Niger.

The **Niger River war fleet** — large dugout canoes carrying archers, lancers, and provisions, capable of moving troops along the river system far faster than any overland march — was Sonni Ali's signature military innovation. The Songhai had been a river people for centuries, and the Sorko fishing population provided the boat-building expertise and the river-handling crews. Sonni Ali militarized this expertise, building a fleet of war canoes that could move several thousand troops along the Niger and into the Bani in a single coordinated movement. The fleet allowed the Songhai to project military power through the Inner Niger Delta in a way that no pure cavalry army could; it also allowed Sonni Ali to maintain the long siege of Djenné by keeping his besieging force supplied and reinforced through years that would otherwise have starved out a conventional army.

When Djenné finally surrendered in 1473, Sonni Ali handled the city very differently than he had Timbuktu. He married the local queen — a Mali-era survival of female political authority in the Inner Delta — confirmed the city's commercial autonomy, and treated the merchant class with general respect. The Djenné scholars survived the conquest substantially intact. The chronicle tradition explains the difference as Sonni Ali respecting the seven-year resistance, but the more likely explanation is structural: Djenné's wealth came from its commerce, the commerce required the merchants and the scholars who staffed the qadi courts, and even Sonni Ali could see that destroying that infrastructure would destroy the city's value to him.

---

The wars against the **Mossi** were the southern frontier of Sonni Ali's empire and the campaign he never quite finished.

The **Mossi kingdoms** (MOH-see) were a cluster of cavalry-based states in the upper Volta basin, in what is now Burkina Faso, just south of the Niger Bend. Mossi cavalry were excellent — better, by many accounts, than the Sahelian cavalry of the imperial centers — and the Mossi made a regular practice of raiding Sahelian markets for slaves and livestock. Through the late fifteenth century, Sonni Ali campaigned repeatedly against the Mossi, attempting to push the southern Songhai frontier into Mossi territory and to permanently subjugate the kingdoms. He never succeeded. The Mossi cavalry could fade into the bush when outnumbered and emerge to raid when the Songhai forces withdrew. The Mossi remained an independent southern frontier throughout Songhai's history — and, in the long run, the Mossi tradition would survive the destruction of Songhai by another two centuries before being absorbed into the French colonial empire.

A failed conquest is still informative. The Sonni-vs-Mossi standoff demonstrates that Songhai's military system had limits: it was excellent for capturing fortified cities and controlling river networks, but it was not configured for the chase-and-pursuit warfare needed to subjugate a mobile cavalry-based opponent in densely vegetated country. This same limitation would matter at Tondibi in 1591, where Songhai cavalry would prove unable to close with a smaller Moroccan musketeer formation that fought from a defensive position.

---

The **Lake Faguibine canal project** is one of those tantalizing fragments of Songhai history that the chronicles preserve only in passing.

According to the *Tarikh al-Fattash*, Sonni Ali planned an ambitious canal connecting the Niger River with **Lake Faguibine** (fah-gwee-BEEN), the largest lake in the western Inner Delta system. The intended canal would have been roughly 90 miles (150 km) long, would have expanded the lake's flood-fed agricultural zone, and would have provided a new water route for the war fleet to bypass natural obstacles in the Niger. The chronicle says that work began in earnest, that thousands of laborers were assembled, and that the project was eventually abandoned when Sonni Ali's military commitments drew his attention elsewhere.

How much of this is real? The chronicle is the only source, and the *Tarikh al-Fattash* is generally less reliable than the *Tarikh al-Sudan*. No archaeological evidence of canal construction on the planned route has been published. The project may have been a planning study that never broke ground, or it may have been a small-scale local irrigation effort that the chronicle inflated into an imperial enterprise. But if even part of the chronicle's account is accurate, Sonni Ali was attempting hydraulic engineering on a scale that would have been unusual for any medieval West African state — and the attempt itself, even unfinished, suggests an imperial ambition that the destruction-of-scholars narrative does not entirely capture.

---

The **religious heterodoxy debate** is the question that drives almost every modern reading of Sonni Ali.

The chronicle tradition is unanimous that Sonni Ali was a "bad Muslim" — that he performed the obligatory prayers in public while consulting traditional Songhai ritual specialists in private, that he observed pre-Islamic kingship rituals (the practice of the imperial regalia, the seasonal ceremonies, the sacrifices to river spirits), and that his court included pagan diviners alongside Muslim qadis. Al-Sadi's *Tarikh al-Sudan* calls him "an unjust tyrant, a notorious miscreant" and accumulates specific accusations of impiety. The chronicle's framing has shaped the standard reading of Sonni Ali for four centuries: a syncretic king who was nominally Muslim but actually a traditional African sacred-king.

Modern historians have pushed back. The chronicle tradition is the partisan testimony of the Timbuktu scholarly class, who hated Sonni Ali for the 1468–69 massacres and had every reason to portray him as a religious enemy. Sonni Ali's actual religious practice probably did include traditional Songhai elements — the kingship-ritual apparatus of pre-Islamic Sahelian monarchy was deeply embedded in Songhai political culture, and it would have been politically impossible for any Songhai king to govern without performing those rituals. But the chronicle's framing of Sonni Ali as essentially a pagan ruler with Islamic veneer is probably overstated. The honest answer is that Sonni Ali practiced a syncretic blend of Islam and traditional Songhai religion that was probably typical of fifteenth-century Sahelian rulers — including, in muted form, his predecessors and his successor Askia Muhammad — and that the chronicle tradition exaggerated his particular impiety because his particular violence had hurt the chroniclers' families.

This matters because the religious framing was the political framing. When Askia Muhammad overthrew Sonni Ali's son in 1493 (Chapter 3), he did so explicitly in the name of orthodox Islam against the syncretic Sonni regime. The framing was politically necessary — it gave Askia Muhammad theological cover for what was technically a usurpation — and it was almost certainly partly true. But it was also a piece of propaganda, and the modern reader should not take the chronicle's pagan-Sonni-Ali framing entirely at face value.

---

Sonni Ali's death in 1492 is one of the more anti-climactic moments in the chronicle tradition.

According to the *Tarikh al-Sudan*, Sonni Ali drowned in the Niger River while returning from a campaign against the Gurma people in the southeast. The exact circumstances are not recorded — whether it was an accident, an assassination, or a flood-related military disaster is unclear. The chronicle describes his body being carried back to Gao by his soldiers and buried with traditional Songhai royal honors. The successor was his son **Sonni Baru** (SOH-nee BAH-roo), who took the throne under the title Abu Bakr Dao.

Sonni Baru lasted less than a year. His open commitment to traditional Songhai religion — he refused the public performances of Islamic prayer that even his father had observed — provoked the Muslim military commanders to revolt. In April 1493, the Soninke general **Muhammad Ture** (moo-HAH-mahd too-RAY), one of the senior commanders of Sonni Ali's standing army, defeated Sonni Baru at the **Battle of Anfao** near Gao. Sonni Baru fled south and disappears from the historical record. Muhammad Ture seized the throne, ended the Sonni dynasty, and took the title that would define his line: **Askia**.

---

A pause for cross-civ context. Sonni Ali's career runs from 1464 to 1492 — almost exactly the same years as several momentous events elsewhere in the world.

In 1453, eleven years before Sonni Ali took the throne, Mehmed II's Ottomans had captured **Constantinople** (Ottoman Empire Ch 2), ending the Byzantine Empire (Byzantine Empire Ch 12) and demonstrating that gunpowder cannons could break centuries-old fortifications. In 1492, the year Sonni Ali drowned, **Granada** fell to Ferdinand and Isabella, ending the last Muslim political presence in Spain — and **Christopher Columbus** crossed the Atlantic for the first time on his way to the Caribbean. The same year. While Sonni Ali was concluding twenty-eight years of West African empire-building, the Atlantic was being opened by the first European voyage to the Americas, and the structural reorientation of African trade away from the trans-Saharan routes and toward the Atlantic seaboard was about to begin in earnest.

In China, the **Ming Dynasty** under Hongzhi was at the start of a relatively stable middle period. In Persia, the **Safavid dynasty** was still a militant Sufi order at Ardabil — Shah Ismail would not declare himself in Tabriz until 1501 (Safavid Persia Ch 2). In India, the **Lodi sultanate** was the dominant power in the north, but Babur's Mughal expansion was still thirty years away. In the Americas, the **Aztec Triple Alliance** under Ahuitzotl was at its expansive peak, and the **Inca Empire** under Huayna Capac was reaching its greatest extent. Almost none of these civilizations had heard of Songhai. Within a century, several of them would be entangled with it through the gunpowder revolution.

---

# Chapter 3 — Askia Muhammad the Pilgrim-King

Muhammad Ture, the Soninke general who deposed Sonni Baru in April 1493, was not a member of the Sonni royal lineage and had no traditional claim to the Songhai throne. He was a senior military commander — one of the *fari-mondzo* (chief of slaves and royal estates) under Sonni Ali — and his takeover was a military coup dressed up as a religious restoration. To make the coup stick, he needed two things: a theological justification that satisfied Timbuktu's scholarly class, and an institutional reorganization that prevented any future general from doing to him what he had just done to Sonni Baru. He spent the next thirty-five years building both.

The reign of Askia Muhammad I (1493–1528) is the empire's institutional peak. He made Songhai genuinely Islamic in its ruling class. He built a bureaucratic administration of a sophistication that no previous West African state had matched. He undertook the spectacular hajj of 1497 that put Songhai on the diplomatic map of the Islamic world. He expanded the empire's territory to its greatest extent. And he was eventually deposed, in 1528, by his own son — a foreshadowing of the succession problems that would eventually destroy the dynasty.

This chapter is about how Askia Muhammad took the throne, what he did with the hajj, and the years between when he came back from Mecca and when his son blinded him on the throne.

---

Muhammad Ture's coup needed theological legitimation, and the natural source was Timbuktu.

The Sankoré clerical families who had been butchered by Sonni Ali in 1468–69 had spent the next twenty-five years rebuilding what they could under Sonni Ali's reluctant tolerance. They were not natural allies of any Sonni successor, and the news that a Muslim general had overthrown the openly pagan Sonni Baru was received in Timbuktu as a long-overdue restoration of orthodoxy. The Timbuktu chief qadi, **Mahmud Aqit** (the rebuilt Aqit family had survived Sonni Ali and was again the dominant scholarly lineage), publicly endorsed Muhammad Ture's coup and issued a fatwa declaring that the Sonni regime had been religiously illegitimate. The endorsement gave Muhammad Ture theological cover. He took the title **Askia** (AHS-kee-ah, originally a Songhai term meaning roughly "forceful one" or "general," though the chronicle tradition gives multiple folk etymologies) and proclaimed himself **Askia al-Hajj Muhammad I**.

Askia Muhammad's first major act was the **1497 hajj** — the pilgrimage to Mecca that had been a tradition among Sahelian Muslim rulers since Mansa Uli of Mali (Mali Empire Ch 3). The Askia hajj was deliberately modeled on **Mansa Musa's 1324 procession** (Mali Empire Ch 4) but built around a different political purpose. Where Mansa Musa had gone to Mecca to display Mali's wealth, Askia Muhammad went to Mecca to acquire institutional Islamic legitimacy that he could carry back to Songhai and use against any future challenger.

The caravan departed Gao in 1496 with **1,500 soldiers** as escort and **300,000 gold pieces** for distribution and ceremonial gifts. The numbers are smaller than Mansa Musa's figures (60,000 attendants, 12,000 slaves, tons of gold), and they read as more credible — the round 300,000 gold pieces is suspicious in itself, but not implausibly large. The route was the standard trans-Saharan caravan path: northeast across the Sahara through Taghaza and Tuat, east along the North African coast through Cairo, south across the Red Sea to the Hejaz. The journey took roughly two years total, with Askia Muhammad spending most of the second year in the Hejaz performing the pilgrimage rituals and conducting his diplomatic business in Cairo and Mecca.

---

The **Cairo stay** was the diplomatic centerpiece.

Askia Muhammad spent several months in Cairo in 1497 as the guest of the **Mamluk Sultan Qansuh al-Ghuri** — the second-to-last of the Mamluk sultans, whose defeat by the Ottomans at Marj Dabiq in 1516 would end Mamluk rule in Egypt twenty years later. The Mamluks were the dominant Sunni power in the Mediterranean Islamic world at this point, and Cairo was the cultural and economic capital of the eastern Mediterranean. Askia Muhammad's reception by Qansuh was warm; the Mamluk court treated him as a peer ruler, not as a tributary, and the Sahelian visitors moved through the institutions of Cairo as honored guests.

The most consequential meeting was with the great Egyptian scholar **Jalal al-Din al-Suyuti** (jah-LAHL ad-DEEN as-soo-YOO-tee). Al-Suyuti was the most prolific writer of late-medieval Sunni Islam — author of more than 500 works on jurisprudence, hadith, theology, Quranic exegesis, history, and Arabic grammar — and one of the most respected senior scholars in the Mamluk world. Askia Muhammad consulted him on questions of Islamic governance: what was the proper relationship between a Sunni ruler and a partly pagan population, how should a ruler treat Muslim subjects who had converted under coercion, what were the legal obligations of an Islamic state in a non-Muslim majority territory. Al-Suyuti's responses were preserved in a treatise that traveled back to Songhai with the returning caravan and shaped Askia legal thinking for decades.

Al-Suyuti also provided the theological framework for the title that mattered most: **Caliph of the Sudan**. The Abbasid caliph in Cairo at this point — a powerless religious figurehead maintained by the Mamluks for ceremonial purposes, descended from the Abbasid line that the Mongols had driven out of Baghdad in 1258 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 10) — formally invested Askia Muhammad with the title **Caliph of the Sudan** (caliph of the Bilad al-Sudan, "the land of the blacks"). The investiture was symbolic; the Cairo Abbasid had no actual political authority. But the title gave Askia Muhammad theological cover for asserting religious authority over local clerical factions back in Songhai. He was now formally recognized by the senior caliphal institution of Sunni Islam as the legitimate Muslim sovereign of West Africa. The Timbuktu qadis could no longer claim independent religious authority against him.

The hajj also produced a more personal political legacy: Askia Muhammad acquired in Mecca a **green turban and a sword from the Prophet's family** (the historicity of the relics is doubtful, but the political symbolism was potent), which he wore at official ceremonies for the rest of his life as visible markers of his Mecca-confirmed status. The ceremonial regalia of the Askia dynasty would include these symbolic objects for the next century.

---

When Askia Muhammad returned to Gao in 1498, he had what he needed: theological legitimacy, an Islamic-world reputation, and the institutional confidence to begin the long project of restructuring the Songhai state.

He began with **provincial reorganization**. The empire was divided into roughly a dozen provinces — the chronicles give somewhat different numbers in different reigns — each governed by an appointed *koi* (governor) drawn from the royal family or from trusted military commanders. The major provinces included **Kurmina** (the western frontier toward Mali, governed by the *Kurmina-fari*), **Bara** (south of Lake Faguibine), **Dirma** (around Djenné), **Bandiagara** (the Dogon escarpment), **Hombori** (the central south), and **Dendi** (the eastern Niger frontier toward modern Nigeria). The governors were rotated periodically to prevent local power bases from forming — a structural innovation rare among medieval African states and modeled loosely on Mamluk Egyptian administration. The principle was old (the Persian and Roman empires had used similar rotation systems), but Songhai's implementation of it was the most systematic seen in West Africa to that point.

He continued with the **ministerial system**. The Askia court included a named set of officers each responsible for a specific functional area: the **hi-koi** (HEE-koi, commander of the war fleet), the **fari-mondzo** (FAH-ree MOND-zoh, chief of slaves and royal estates), the **kalisa-fari** (kah-LEE-sah FAH-ree, treasurer), the **korei-farma** (koh-REH-ee FAR-mah, in charge of relations with Berber tributaries), the **balama** (commander of the standing cavalry), and the chief qadi of each major city responsible for religious-legal administration. The system gave the empire something closer to a functional bureaucracy than any previous West African state had achieved. It was not a Chinese-style scholar-bureaucracy or a Byzantine civil service — there was no examination system, no bureau of records, no archived state documentation that has survived — but it was a more elaborated administrative apparatus than Mali had ever managed, and it allowed Askia Muhammad to govern a much larger territory more effectively than his predecessors.

He restructured the **standing army**. Sonni Ali had built the army around tribal levies and a war fleet, with cavalry drawn from the Songhai aristocracy and from allied confederations. Askia Muhammad separated the imperial standing army from these tribal contingents, building a permanent corps of slave-soldier infantry and cavalry recruited from war captives and royal estates. The professional cavalry wore mail armor (some imported from North Africa, some made by Songhai blacksmiths), wielded lances and curved swords, and rode horses imported from the Maghreb. The infantry carried shields, javelins, and short swords. The Niger River fleet under the *hi-koi* remained a separate command. The system gave Askia Muhammad a military force loyal directly to him rather than to the cavalry aristocracy — though the loyalty arrangement would never become as institutionally robust as the Janissary corps in the Ottoman Empire (Ottoman Empire Ch 3) or the ghulam army in Safavid Persia (Safavid Persia Ch 4), and it would not be enough to prevent his eventual overthrow.

---

He also expanded the empire's territory.

The eastern campaigns of the early sixteenth century brought the **Hausa city-states** of modern northern Nigeria into intermittent Songhai suzerainty. **Kano** and **Katsina** — the two largest Hausa walled commercial cities — paid tribute to Gao after Askia Muhammad's eastern expedition of around 1513–14, though the tribute relationship was loose and the Hausa kingdoms continued their own commercial and political life. The northern frontier expanded to include the salt-mining oases of the central Sahara: Askia Muhammad briefly brought **Taghaza** under direct administration around 1500, taxing the salt trade at the source rather than at the Sahelian markets, before military pressure from the Sanhaja Berbers forced him to cede effective control back to local Mesufa Berber families. The southern frontier remained the Mossi line, which Askia Muhammad could not break any more than Sonni Ali had.

By around 1515, the Songhai Empire stretched from the Atlantic coast of modern Senegal to the Hausa city-states of northern Nigeria, north into the southern Sahara, south to the Mossi frontier — an area roughly the size of Western Europe and the largest continuous territory ever ruled by a sub-Saharan African state. The peak was brief; structural overstretch and succession crises would begin eroding it within a generation. But for a moment in the second decade of the sixteenth century, Askia Muhammad presided over an empire that was, by territorial measure, larger than any of its contemporaries except the Ming, the Ottomans, and the Safavids in their respective heartlands.

---

The **Tomb of Askia** at Gao is the surviving monument of this era.

Askia Muhammad commissioned a monumental pyramidal mud-brick tomb at Gao around 1495 — interestingly, before the hajj rather than after, which suggests he had imperial-tomb plans well established before he left for Mecca. The tomb is roughly 56 feet (17 meters) high, built of beaten earth (a mud-and-straw mixture compressed in layers, then plastered) with timber-beam reinforcement in the standard Sudano-Sahelian style. Projecting palm-wood **toron** beams stick out from the four sides at regular intervals; the toron function as built-in scaffolding for the regular re-plastering that mud-brick walls require after each rainy season. A small mosque sits next to the tomb.

The Tomb of Askia is the most distinctive surviving monument of the Songhai Empire. It survived the Moroccan invasion of 1591 — Judar Pasha's forces pillaged Gao but did not destroy the tomb — and it has been continuously maintained by local communities for five centuries. UNESCO inscribed it as a World Heritage Site in 2004. The maintenance challenge is real: the mud-brick structure requires re-plastering every few years, and the encroaching Sahara and intermittent political instability in northern Mali both threaten the long-term preservation of the site.

---

Askia Muhammad's reign ended badly. By the 1520s he was elderly (probably in his sixties or seventies — birth dates for medieval Sahelian rulers are not generally recorded), partially blind, and increasingly dependent on his sons to actually run the empire. His son **Askia Musa** — not to be confused with Mansa Musa of Mali a century earlier — engineered a coup in 1528, deposing his father and exiling him to a small island in the Niger called **Gungu**. The deposed Askia Muhammad lived for another decade in exile, allegedly visited by occasional members of the court but politically powerless. He died in 1538, near a hundred years old by the chronicle's accounting.

The son-overthrowing-father pattern that Askia Musa established would haunt the dynasty for the rest of its existence. Almost every subsequent Askia faced challenges from his sons or brothers, and the resulting succession crises consumed enormous political energy. The empire's institutions could survive a single coup; they could not survive what would shortly become a near-continuous succession civil war.

---

A pause for cross-civilizational comparison. Askia Muhammad's reign (1493–1528) sits inside one of the most concentrated thirty-year stretches in world history.

While Askia Muhammad was building his Mecca-blessed Sunni empire on the Niger Bend, **Shah Ismail** was forcibly converting Iran from Sunni to Twelver Shia Islam at the other end of the Islamic world (Safavid Persia Ch 2). The two projects were almost exactly contemporary — Ismail took Tabriz in 1501, three years after Askia Muhammad returned from his hajj — and they represent two opposite responses to the same problem of building Islamic legitimacy in a recently-conquered territory. Askia Muhammad imported orthodoxy from Cairo; Ismail imported Lebanese Shia clerics and converted his population by force. Both worked.

The **Ottoman Empire** under Selim I and then Suleiman the Magnificent was completing its transformation into a Mediterranean superpower — Selim conquered Mamluk Egypt in 1517, ending the dynasty that had hosted Askia Muhammad twenty years earlier and putting the Cairo Abbasid caliph (the same one who had invested Askia Muhammad as Caliph of the Sudan) under Ottoman control. **Suleiman** would take Belgrade in 1521 and Rhodes in 1522 (Ottoman Empire Ch 3). The Mamluk world that Askia Muhammad had visited as a peer in 1497 had effectively ceased to exist by 1517, twenty years later.

In India, **Babur** founded the Mughal Empire at the Battle of Panipat in 1526 — two years before Askia Muhammad was deposed. The Mughal-Songhai timing is striking: two of the great Islamic gunpowder empires of the early-modern world reaching foundational moments in the same decade. In China, the **Ming Zhengde Emperor** was running an unstable middle-Ming court. In the Americas, **Hernán Cortés** destroyed the Aztec Empire in 1521 (Aztec Empire Ch 8) and **Francisco Pizarro** would destroy the Inca Empire a decade later. The European voyages of discovery and conquest were transforming the Atlantic world while Askia Muhammad was building a Sahelian state that would, in the fullness of time, be reshaped by the very same trade reorientation those voyages had begun.

The single most consequential development from Songhai's perspective, though, was the one that received the least notice in Gao at the time: the steady accumulation of European trading forts on the West African Atlantic coast through the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries. The Portuguese built **Elmina** in 1482, on the Gold Coast of modern Ghana, and from Elmina they began intercepting some of the gold flow that had previously moved north across the Sahara to Songhai's markets. The diversion was small at first, but it was structural and growing. The trans-Saharan gold trade — the economic backbone of every Sahelian empire from Ghana through Songhai — was beginning to be shadowed by an Atlantic alternative that would, over the next two centuries, hollow it out completely.

# Chapter 4 — The Empire's Administration

This chapter is the institutional infrastructure: the bureaucracy, the army, the demographic mosaic, the way Songhai governed itself between Askia Muhammad's accession in 1493 and Askia Dawud's death in 1582. Most of the institutional structure was either created or refined by Askia Muhammad and persisted (with intermittent crises) through the long peace under Askia Dawud. The chapter is short because the structure has already been sketched in Chapter 3 — but the details matter, because the institutional weaknesses that brought Songhai down at Tondibi in 1591 were not last-minute developments. They were built in.

---

Start with the demographic mix. Songhai was unusually cosmopolitan even by Sahelian standards.

The **Songhai-speaking population** itself — fishermen, farmers, river traders, the demographic core of the imperial heartland around Gao — probably numbered somewhere between half a million and a million. This was the language of the army's tribal levies, of court ceremony, and of everyday administration in the eastern Niger Bend.

Around the Songhai core sat several substantial non-Songhai populations:

The **Wangara** (also called Soninke or Mande-Dyula) were the great commercial network of the western Sudan. Mande-speaking Soninke merchants had been running the trans-Saharan trade since Ghana, and their commercial diaspora extended from the Mauritanian Saharan towns through Timbuktu and Djenné to the Hausa city-states and the gold-trading market towns south of the Niger. Most of Songhai's commercial scholars, qadis, and Friday-mosque imams were Wangara. The relationship between the Songhai-speaking ruling class and the Wangara commercial class was structurally similar to the relationship between an early-modern European court and its merchant cities: mutual dependence, periodic friction, no possibility of the court dispensing with the merchants or the merchants dispensing with the court.

The **Sanhaja Berbers** — the desert pastoralist confederations that ran the western Sahara salt routes and provided most of the Berber clerical lineages of Timbuktu — were a smaller but politically important group. The Aqit family (Mahmud Aqit, who had legitimated Askia Muhammad's coup) was the most prominent Sanhaja lineage. The **Tuareg** (Tamasheq-speaking pastoralists of the central Sahara) were a parallel desert population further east, often hostile to Songhai administration and intermittently raiding Niger Bend caravans.

The **Mandinka and Songhai-Tuareg-mixed populations** of the western provinces — the regions that had been Mali under the mansas and were now Songhai under the askias — retained their Mandinka language and Mandinka social institutions while paying tribute and providing military contingents to Gao.

The **Hausa** of the eastern frontier (Kano, Katsina, and the smaller Hausa walled cities) were Songhai tributaries from the early sixteenth century but maintained their own substantial commercial and clerical institutions and operated in many ways as semi-autonomous client states.

The **slave population** — drawn from raids against the Mossi, the forest peoples of modern Burkina Faso and northern Côte d'Ivoire, and to a lesser extent from the Saharan slave trade out of the Maghreb — was substantial. Slavery was central to Songhai's economy; royal slave estates produced grain in the Niger floodplain, slave-soldiers staffed the standing army, slave caravans were a major export commodity north across the Sahara, and household slaves staffed elite domestic establishments. The slave population of Songhai itself was probably on the order of a quarter of the empire's total population, though the figure is not directly attested in any source and depends heavily on estimation.

The result was a multi-ethnic, multi-lingual, multi-religious imperial core that no single group dominated. This was a strength under Askia Muhammad — it gave the dynasty room to balance factions against each other — and a weakness under his weaker successors, who could not prevent the factions from organizing against the throne.

---

The **provincial administration** divided the empire into roughly twelve named provinces, each governed by an appointed *koi* or *fari*. The largest and most important provinces were:

**Kurmina** — the western frontier toward the contracting Mali Empire, governed from a regional capital at Tindirma. The Kurmina-fari was the second-most-powerful position in the empire after the Askia, often held by a younger brother or son of the reigning monarch. **Bara** — south of Lake Faguibine, in the western Inner Delta. **Dirma** — around Djenné, controlling the Inner Delta's commercial heartland. **Bandiagara** — the Dogon escarpment region, an ancient cliff-and-cave settlement zone south of the Niger where the **Dogon** (DOH-gohn, a non-Mande, non-Songhai-speaking forest-edge population with elaborate religious traditions) lived in autonomous villages under nominal Songhai suzerainty. **Hombori** — the south-central frontier, containing some of the empire's most fertile farmland. **Dendi** — the eastern Niger frontier toward modern Nigeria, the province where the surviving Askia royal family would eventually flee after 1591 and establish the Dendi successor kingdom (Chapter 8).

The provincial governors were rotated periodically, though the rotation system was applied unevenly across the dynasty and tended to break down when central authority weakened. The provincial governors paid annual tribute to Gao in gold dust, slaves, livestock, and grain, and they were expected to provide military contingents on demand. The governors themselves were drawn from the royal family, from senior military commanders, and occasionally from the Wangara commercial class — a different mix than the pure royal-appointment systems of contemporary Islamic states like Mamluk Egypt or Safavid Persia.

---

The **ministerial system** at Gao was the central administrative apparatus. The named ministers each headed a functional department:

- The **hi-koi** commanded the Niger River war fleet, which remained a separate military structure from the cavalry army. The fleet was the empire's strategic transport asset and had its own dockyards at Gao, Timbuktu, and Djenné.
- The **fari-mondzo** managed the royal slave estates and the forced-labor workforce that produced grain and livestock for the imperial treasury.
- The **kalisa-fari** was the treasurer, responsible for tribute collection, gold-dust assays, and the imperial currency reserves.
- The **korei-farma** handled relations with the Sanhaja Berber and Tuareg confederations of the Saharan frontier — a delicate position that mixed taxation, diplomacy, and intermittent military pressure.
- The **balama** commanded the standing cavalry army.
- The **dyina-koi** was the chief administrator of foodstuffs, including the salt trade.
- The chief **qadi** of Timbuktu was the senior religious-legal authority, responsible for sharia courts and for issuing fatwas on legal questions referred by the central court.

A few of these positions appear in only some chronicle accounts, and their exact responsibilities shifted across reigns. But the broad structure — a named set of ministers each with a defined functional brief, reporting to the Askia — is what made Songhai's administration distinctive among medieval West African states. It looked more like a Mamluk or Ottoman provincial government than like the looser tributary-network structure of Mali.

---

The **standing army** had three main components:

**Cavalry**, drawn from the Songhai aristocracy and from the slave-soldier units, equipped with mail armor (some imported from the Maghreb, some made by Songhai blacksmiths), lances, and curved swords. The cavalry was the strike arm — fast, well-trained, expensive to maintain. A trained warhorse cost the equivalent of several enslaved people in trade, and horses had to be imported from North Africa across the Sahara because the tsetse fly belt south of the Sahel made it impossible to breed horses in the southern provinces. Cavalry was, structurally, an aristocratic monopoly; only the central state and its top nobility could afford to maintain it.

**Infantry**, drawn from the slave-soldier units and from the empire's free peasant levies, equipped with shields, javelins, short swords, and bows. The infantry was the empire's mass military force — large numbers, lower individual quality, used for sieges, garrison duty, and in coordination with the cavalry charge. Some infantry units carried imported chain mail and swords; most were lightly equipped.

**The Niger River war fleet**, built around large dugout canoes and specialized boat-handling crews drawn from the Sorko fishing population. The fleet was the strategic transport asset that allowed Songhai to project military power along the Niger faster than any cavalry march could manage. The fleet's military value was demonstrated in Sonni Ali's siege of Djenné and its strategic value in moving troops along the empire's commercial spine.

What the army did not have was **firearms**. Cannons and matchlock muskets had been spreading through the Mediterranean Islamic world from the late fifteenth century — the Ottomans had used them at Constantinople in 1453 (Byzantine Empire Ch 12), Selim used them at Marj Dabiq against the Mamluks in 1516, the Saadians had them in Morocco from the early sixteenth century. The trans-Saharan trade was perfectly capable of moving gunpowder weapons south to the Sahel, and a few personal arquebuses and small cannons did reach the Songhai court as diplomatic gifts. But Songhai never developed an indigenous gunpowder manufacturing capacity, never built large gunpowder-armed infantry units, and never restructured its army around firearms as the Ottomans, Safavids, Mughals, and Saadians all did during the same decades.

The reasons are partly geographic — gunpowder requires sulfur, saltpeter, and a substantial metallurgical infrastructure for producing barrels, none of which Songhai possessed in commercial quantities — and partly institutional. The cavalry aristocracy had every reason to resist a military restructuring that would devalue their status, and the Askias never had the central authority to override aristocratic resistance the way the Ottoman sultans and the Safavid shahs did during the same period. The result was a military that was, by 1591, generations behind the global gunpowder revolution. This was the gap that Tondibi exposed.

---

The **qadi court system** ran in parallel with the imperial administration.

Askia Muhammad established **Maliki-school sharia courts** in the major cities — Gao, Timbuktu, Djenné, Walata, and the larger provincial centers — applying Islamic law to commerce, inheritance, marriage, and criminal justice. The chief qadi of each city was drawn from the local clerical lineages: in Timbuktu, from the Sanhaja Berber families like the Aqits; in Djenné, from the Wangara commercial scholars; in Gao, from the court ulama close to the Askia.

The **Maliki school** of Sunni jurisprudence — one of the four main legal schools of orthodox Sunni Islam, founded by **Malik ibn Anas** in eighth-century Medina, dominant across North Africa and the western Sahel — was the legal framework. Maliki jurisprudence emphasized the customary law of Medina and was relatively conservative on commercial and family questions; it had been the standard West African legal school since the Almoravid period and was deeply embedded in the Sahelian scholarly tradition.

The chief qadi of Timbuktu was a particularly powerful position. Timbuktu's commercial importance and its scholarly density gave the chief qadi institutional weight that no other provincial qadi could match, and on several occasions the chief qadi successfully overruled imperial rulings on religious grounds. The institutional independence of the Timbuktu judiciary from the Gao court is a recurring motif in the chronicles, and it was a structural feature of Songhai government that no Askia ever managed to fully control.

This independent judicial institution mattered for the legitimacy of the regime: it gave the Timbuktu scholarly class a stake in the system that prevented them from becoming an oppositional force, while also constraining the Askias from arbitrary acts that might trigger judicial resistance. It was a delicate equilibrium, and one that broke down in the late sixteenth century when the succession crises destroyed the central authority that had held it together.

# Chapter 5 — Timbuktu's Renaissance

For most of the world, "Timbuktu" is a name for the impossibly remote — a city of legendary gold and learning that nobody has actually been to. The name has detached from the actual geographic place and floated free as a synonym for the edge of nowhere. Even people who know perfectly well where Timbuktu sits on a map use the name in this metaphorical way: the back of beyond, the end of the line, the place a postcard would take a year to reach.

This is a strange afterlife for a real city, and most of it is a European invention — the late-eighteenth-century European myth of Timbuktu as an unreachable Wonder, an inversion of what Timbuktu had actually been at its peak. For now, the question this chapter is going to answer is: what was Timbuktu under Songhai rule, when it actually mattered? The short answer is: a working trans-Saharan trade hub on the southern edge of the desert, with three notable mosques, a thriving manuscript-copying economy, an Islamic scholarly community of perhaps several hundred senior teachers and several thousand students, and a population that probably never exceeded 50,000 even at the peak. It was a real city, with a real intellectual culture, and the intellectual culture was substantially more impressive than the city itself.

The chapter is also about the gold-and-salt economy at high tide — the trans-Saharan commerce that paid for the manuscript tradition and the mosques and the scholarly community. The economy and the scholarship were structurally inseparable: the scholars staffed the qadi courts that adjudicated the commercial disputes, the merchants funded the libraries and the madrasas, and the prestige of Timbuktu's intellectual culture attracted the talent that kept the qadi courts functioning. Pull either side out and the whole apparatus collapses, which is essentially what happened after 1591.

---

Timbuktu sits about 8 miles (13 km) north of the Niger River in modern Mali, just south of the Sahara, at the point where the Niger makes its great bend east before turning south. The location is unromantic — a flat sandy plain with a few wells, with the Niger's seasonal floodplain to the south and the desert immediately to the north. The city was founded sometime in the eleventh or twelfth century, probably initially as a seasonal Tuareg encampment. The name "Timbuktu" is generally believed to derive from a Tuareg word meaning "place of Tin Buktu" — Tin Buktu being a reference to a Tuareg woman, possibly a slave, who was said to have minded the wells at the original encampment site. The etymology is folk-etymological and may not be reliable.

By the time Mansa Musa absorbed it under Mali in the early fourteenth century, Timbuktu was already a functioning trade settlement. Mansa Musa transformed it from a regional trading post into an imperial showcase city, commissioning the **Djinguereber Mosque** on the western edge of the town in 1327, installing a community of Islamic scholars recruited from the Maghreb during his hajj, and providing capital for additional religious and educational infrastructure. Under Sonni Ali's brutal occupation from 1468 to 1492, the scholarly community was severely damaged but not destroyed; under the Askia restoration from 1493 onward, it recovered and reached its greatest density.

The three great mosques of Timbuktu are the **Djinguereber** (jin-GAH-reh-bar — a Songhai word meaning "great mosque," the largest, on the western side, founded c. 1327 under Mali), the **Sankoré** (the northern mosque, founded a generation later in the mid-fourteenth century), and the **Sidi Yahya** (named for its first imam, founded in the early fifteenth century). All three are mud-brick buildings in the Sudano-Sahelian style, with characteristic projecting palm-wood toron beams that double as built-in scaffolding for the regular re-plastering that mud-brick walls require after each rainy season. The roofs are flat, supported on internal palm-trunk columns. The minarets are conical, tapered, and buttressed.

None of the three is architecturally spectacular by the standards of contemporary Cairo or Istanbul or Isfahan. They are substantially smaller than the Hagia Sophia (Byzantine Empire Ch 4) or the Shah Mosque at Isfahan (Safavid Persia Ch 4), and they use a mud-brick construction technique that requires constant maintenance. What makes them remarkable is institutional continuity: they have been maintained as functioning mosques for nearly seven centuries, in a building material that requires constant care to survive. The Djinguereber Mosque has been re-plastered probably more than two hundred times since al-Sahili built it. That is the achievement.

---

The **Sankoré scholarly community** was the city's intellectual heart.

Sankoré was the northern mosque and madrasa complex, and the scholarly community that grew up around it was Timbuktu's distinctive contribution to the Islamic world. The popular figure of "25,000 students at Sankoré" is almost certainly inflated — Timbuktu's total population probably never exceeded 50,000, and a student body of half the city is not credible — but the scholarly community of perhaps 200 to 400 senior teachers and several thousand students at any given moment made it one of the major Islamic learning centers of the post-Mongol Sunni world. Comparable in scale to the smaller madrasas of Cairo, smaller than al-Azhar at its peak, larger than anything else in the western Sahel.

What did students at Sankoré study? The curriculum was the standard Maliki-Sunni curriculum of the late medieval Islamic world: **Quranic memorization** in the lower grades; **Arabic grammar and rhetoric** for the intermediate students; **Maliki jurisprudence** (the school's specialty); **hadith** (the collected sayings and practices of the Prophet); **theology** (kalam); and a smaller offering of advanced subjects including **astronomy**, **mathematics**, **medicine**, **logic**, and **history**. The teaching was conducted in Arabic, and an advanced student was expected to read, write, and converse in Arabic at the level of a contemporary Cairo or Damascus scholar.

The teaching method was the standard Islamic-world model: students studied with individual master scholars rather than with institutional faculties, accumulating *ijazas* (formal teaching licenses certifying that a student had mastered a specific text under a specific teacher) over years of study. A senior scholar might hold a dozen ijazas across different subjects from different teachers. The certification system was the medieval Islamic world's accreditation infrastructure, and it linked Timbuktu's scholarly community directly to the broader networks of Islamic learning across the Mediterranean and the Middle East. A Sankoré-trained scholar could take his ijazas to Cairo or Damascus and be recognized as a peer, and Cairo or Damascus scholars who came south to teach in Timbuktu were similarly recognized.

This integration into the broader Islamic intellectual world is the point that the popular characterization of "African universities centuries before Europe" tends to miss. Sankoré was not unique because it was African; it was significant because it was a fully participating node in the post-classical Islamic scholarly network, on the same institutional template as the madrasas of Cairo, Fez, and Bukhara. The achievement was integration into a global scholarly tradition, not isolation as an indigenous African institution.

---

**Ahmed Baba al-Timbukti** (AH-mad BAH-bah at-tim-BUK-tee) is the era's symbolic representative.

Ahmed Baba was born in Timbuktu in 1556, into the Aqit clerical lineage — the same Sanhaja Berber family that had been butchered by Sonni Ali in 1469 and rebuilt under the Askias. He studied at Sankoré under the leading scholars of the late sixteenth century, including the great jurist **Muhammad Baghayogho** (who had himself trained at Sankoré and was one of the most-cited Maliki jurists of the period). By his early thirties, Ahmed Baba was teaching at Sankoré and had begun the prodigious literary output that would make him the most-cited Maliki jurist of the post-classical period.

He wrote more than forty books on Islamic law, biography, and theology. The most influential are:

- **Kifayat al-Muhtaj** — a biographical dictionary of Maliki jurists, building on and extending the earlier biographical dictionary of Ibn Farhun. The work is one of the standard reference texts for the history of the Maliki school and is still cited in modern Islamic scholarship.
- **Mi'raj al-Su'ud** (the "Ladder of Ascent") — a treatise on the Islamic law of slavery, arguing that enslavement of free-born Muslims is forbidden regardless of the slave's geographic origin or skin color. The text is one of the great early-modern African legal arguments against the racial slavery that had begun to develop in the trans-Saharan and Atlantic trades, and it remains an important reference in modern Islamic legal-historical scholarship.
- A series of legal commentaries and judicial opinions (fatwas) on practical questions of commerce, marriage, inheritance, and criminal justice in the West African Islamic context.

Ahmed Baba was deported to Marrakesh after the 1591 invasion (Chapter 8), spent fifteen years in exile teaching Moroccan students, and was eventually allowed to return to Timbuktu in 1608. He died there in 1627. The Ahmed Baba Institute in modern Timbuktu, established in the 1970s and devastated by the 2012 jihadist occupation, preserves his name and houses (or housed, before the 2012 evacuations) the largest single collection of Timbuktu manuscripts.

The Ahmed Baba career is the late Songhai scholarly tradition in microcosm. A scholar of Berber-Sanhaja descent, trained in the Sankoré tradition that the Askia restoration had rebuilt after Sonni Ali's destruction, producing canonical Maliki jurisprudence that was read and cited across the Sunni Islamic world, eventually deported by the Saadian conquerors and finishing his career in exile in Marrakesh — the trajectory captures both the institutional vitality of late Songhai scholarship and the catastrophe of 1591.

---

The **manuscript tradition** is the great surviving achievement.

Timbuktu's manuscripts were not housed in any single state library. The Songhai state had no equivalent of the great Mamluk or Ottoman state libraries — no Bayt al-Hikma, no royal archive, no centralized academy. Instead, the manuscripts lived in **private libraries owned by the city's scholarly families**, passed down across generations as inheritance. The largest collections — the **Mamma Haidara**, the **Kati**, the **Wangara**, the **Aqit**, and several others — held thousands of volumes each by the late sixteenth century. Smaller private collections of a few hundred volumes were common across the city's clerical class.

The decentralized structure was, in retrospect, a great strength. No single sack could destroy the tradition. When Judar Pasha's forces occupied Timbuktu in 1591 and began systematically looting libraries, the Moroccans got only a fraction of what was actually in the city — the rest was hidden in family compounds, buried in the courtyards, walled into storage rooms, or carried out into the desert with fleeing scholars. The same protective decentralization saved the manuscripts again in 2012, when jihadist forces occupied the city: the librarian **Abdel Kader Haidara** organized a network of volunteers to smuggle tens of thousands of medieval manuscripts out in metal trunks and rice sacks, hiding them in private homes in Bamako. The 2012 rescue worked because the manuscripts had always lived in private hands; they could be moved one family at a time without anyone noticing.

What did the libraries contain? The bulk was Maliki jurisprudence — legal commentaries, fatwa collections, biographical dictionaries of judges. But Timbuktu manuscripts also covered theology, hadith, Quranic exegesis, Arabic grammar and lexicography, Sufi devotional texts, poetry (in Arabic and occasionally in local languages transcribed in Arabic script), astronomy (especially the calculation of prayer times and the qibla direction toward Mecca), mathematics (including arithmetic, algebra, and geometric applications to inheritance law), medicine (combining the Avicenna tradition with West African herbal practice), history (the Tarikh chronicles and earlier Sahelian historical works), and the occasional treatise on more unusual subjects — divination, music theory, falconry, the local ecology of the Inner Niger Delta.

The estimated total surviving manuscript count from the Timbuktu tradition runs into the **hundreds of thousands** — by far the largest body of pre-colonial sub-Saharan African writing. The number is uncertain because no comprehensive catalog has ever been completed, the manuscripts are scattered across multiple institutional and family collections in Timbuktu, Bamako, and abroad, and the work of digitizing and translating the collection is still ongoing decades after it began. What can be said with confidence is that the manuscript tradition is one of the great surviving intellectual archives of the medieval Islamic world, and the work of understanding what it actually contains is one of the great unfinished projects of African historical scholarship.

---

The **gold-and-salt economy at high tide** paid for everything.

Songhai inherited and consolidated the trans-Saharan gold trade that had underwritten Ghana and Mali. Camel caravans of several hundred animals carried gold dust north from the Bambuk and Bure goldfields through Timbuktu and across the Sahara to Sijilmasa (in southeastern Morocco), Tlemcen (in modern Algeria), and ultimately Cairo and the Mediterranean ports. The caravans returned with **salt** from Taghaza, **copper** from the Saharan oasis mines, **books** from Cairo and Damascus, **horses** from the Maghreb, **fine cloth** (silks, brocades, woven textiles) from Egypt and the Mediterranean, **dates and dried fruits** from the Saharan oases, and **slaves** from the Mediterranean trade. At peak, several major caravans crossed the Sahara each year in each direction, with the round trip taking six to nine months.

The economic mechanism is worth pausing on. **Salt** — apparently the less glamorous of the two principal commodities — was actually as important as gold in the Sahelian economy, and at certain stages the two reportedly traded weight-for-weight. The reason is that the West African savanna does not produce salt naturally in sufficient quantity for human and animal nutrition. Without imported salt, large agricultural populations cannot maintain themselves; the human body needs sodium, the cattle need salt licks, and the only sources within reach were either coastal evaporation (limited supply, expensive transport) or Saharan rock salt (better supply, but a thousand-mile camel transport away). Taghaza salt was the lifeblood of the Sahelian agricultural economy. Songhai's control of the Niger Bend caravan terminus gave it the ability to tax that lifeblood at every market on the route.

**Cowrie shells** — small white shells from the Maldives in the Indian Ocean — served as everyday small-change currency throughout the Songhai economy, while gold dust handled larger transactions. The cowries traveled overland through Egypt and across the Sahara, one of the longer commodity chains of the early-modern world economy: harvested by Maldivians in the Indian Ocean, carried north through the Red Sea ports, distributed across Cairo's markets, loaded onto camel caravans for the Saharan crossing, and finally circulating in Niger Bend markets as small change. The Songhai economy was, in this sense, integrated into a trading network that ran from the Maldives to the Maghreb. Few medieval economies were more thoroughly globalized.

The **kola nut trade** (KOH-lah) was a smaller but distinctive commerce. Kola nuts harvested from the forest belt of modern Guinea and Ivory Coast — prized as caffeinated stimulants and used in religious and social ceremonies across the Sahel — moved north from the forest to Songhai's southern provincial markets, were taxed and consolidated by Wangara middlemen, and traveled across the Sahara to North African markets where they served as both a stimulant for caravan crews and a luxury commodity. Kola became one of Songhai's most distinctive non-gold exports.

---

A pause for the broader picture.

The economic-and-scholarly system that Songhai had built by the early sixteenth century was, on its own terms, one of the most sophisticated in the contemporary world. Compare it to the contemporary kingdoms of England or France or Castile: those states had similar populations, comparable cities (Paris around 200,000 in 1500, London around 50,000, Lisbon around 50,000 — Timbuktu's 50,000 was in roughly the European middle range), and broadly similar levels of administrative complexity. They had no equivalent of Sankoré's manuscript tradition; they had no equivalent of Songhai's trans-Saharan caravan economy; they had no equivalent of the Maliki sharia court system that integrated commercial law across an empire-spanning network. They had things Songhai did not have — ships capable of crossing oceans, gunpowder weapons being rapidly adopted across the European military system, printing presses just emerging — but the comparison was much closer than the popular narrative of "European modernity vs. African backwardness" usually suggests.

What Songhai did not have, and what would matter, was the **gunpowder revolution**. The Ottomans had it. The Safavids were acquiring it (Safavid Persia Ch 3). The Mughals would adopt it (medieval India context). The Saadians had it. The Habsburg armies of central Europe had it. Songhai did not. The gap would prove decisive.

# Chapter 6 — Islam, Animism, and Daily Life

The Songhai Empire was officially a Sunni Muslim state. Its rulers were Muslim. Its commerce ran through Maliki sharia courts. Its scholarly elite was integrated into the broader Sunni Islamic intellectual world. Its monumental architecture — the Tomb of Askia, the great mosques of Timbuktu — was Islamic.

But the empire's actual religious life was more complicated. Most of its population — peasants, fishermen, herders, the vast rural majority outside the cities — practiced traditional Songhai or other African religions. Islam was the religion of the court, the cities, and the merchant networks. Traditional religion was the religion of the village, the river, and the field. The two systems coexisted and intermingled rather than competing for exclusive allegiance. This is the same layered religious pattern that characterizes much of West Africa to the present day — a Sahel-wide phenomenon that Mali had displayed in milder form (Mali Empire Ch 5) and that Songhai displayed in more developed form.

This chapter is about how the layered religious system actually worked in daily life: the court Islam at Gao and Timbuktu, the traditional Songhai religious practice in the villages, the Sufi brotherhoods that mediated between them, the legal system that applied Islamic law to commercial cases and traditional law to most others, and the slavery economy that linked all of it to the wider trans-Saharan and emerging Atlantic trades.

---

**Court Islam** at Gao and Timbuktu was orthodox Sunni Maliki, modeled on the Mediterranean Islamic norms of contemporary Cairo and Fez.

The Askia performed the obligatory five daily prayers, observed Ramadan, sponsored religious scholarship, maintained the Friday mosque at the imperial capital, supported the qadi courts, and on at least one famous occasion (Askia Muhammad in 1497) made the hajj. The court included senior ulama — religious scholars who provided theological advice, issued fatwas on disputed legal questions, and supervised the religious education of the royal princes. The chief qadi of Timbuktu held a position of sufficient prestige and institutional independence that he could, on occasion, overrule imperial decisions on religious grounds.

Timbuktu's scholarly density made it the religious center of the empire. The Friday sermons at the Djinguereber Mosque drew worshippers from across the city, and the religious schools at Sankoré attracted students from as far away as Cairo, Fez, and the Hijaz. Senior teachers in the city held *ijazas* from the great Cairo and Damascus madrasas, and corresponded with their peers across the Mediterranean Islamic world. The intellectual culture was integrated into the broader Sunni scholarly network on essentially equal terms.

But court Islam was a city religion. Outside the imperial capital and the major commercial centers, Islam shaded into the much older traditional religious practices of the rural Songhai population.

---

**Traditional Songhai religion** was structured around three main features.

First, **ancestor veneration** — the dead were not gone but continued to interact with the living, requiring regular offerings and remembrance and providing protection and guidance to their descendants. Family compounds maintained shrines for recent ancestors; village shrines honored more distant founding figures; royal shrines preserved the lineage of the Sonni and the Za dynasties.

Second, the **holey** (HOH-lay) — a class of spirit-beings who interacted with the human world through possession of human mediums. The holey were associated with specific natural phenomena (the river, particular trees, certain hills), with specific places, and with specific human professions (the fisherman's spirits were different from the smith's spirits). Possession ceremonies — long ritual events featuring music, dance, and trance states — served as the principal religious technology for communicating with the holey, diagnosing problems, and resolving community conflicts. The ceremonies were regulated by trained ritual specialists, the **zima**, who held a position of social authority parallel to but separate from the qadi or imam.

Third, the **river spirits** — particularly the spirits associated with the Niger River itself, which was personified as a sentient being with moods, demands, and the power to give or withhold the annual flood. Riverside communities maintained shrines and made periodic offerings to the river. Some of the more elaborate ceremonies involved animal sacrifice, ritual immersion, and the recitation of traditional formulas in older Songhai liturgical language.

The royal cult of the Sonni and Za dynasties had been built around exactly this religious apparatus — the king as a sacred figure linked to the river spirits, ancestor veneration of the dynastic founders, ritual specialists at court who maintained the spiritual infrastructure of kingship. Sonni Ali's "religious heterodoxy" (Chapter 2) was largely his continued patronage of this traditional royal cult alongside his public Islamic observance. Askia Muhammad's coup attempted to suppress the most prominent traditional royal rituals — the public ones that the chronicle tradition could observe and condemn — but did not eliminate the underlying religious system, which continued to operate in modified form throughout the Askia period.

---

The interaction of court Islam and traditional religion produced a layered system rather than a clean replacement.

Most rural Songhai practiced traditional religion as their primary religious framework, while observing some Islamic forms — fasting during Ramadan if practical, occasional prayer, attendance at the mosque on major Islamic festivals. The integration was reciprocal: the holey ceremonies sometimes incorporated Quranic verses or Islamic prayer formulas, and Sufi practitioners (next paragraph) borrowed elements from the traditional religious vocabulary.

In the cities, the balance tilted the other way: Islamic practice was the dominant framework, with traditional religious elements present in the background — the urban smith might still observe the rituals appropriate to his trade, the urban herbalist might still consult the holey through a zima, but the dominant public religion was Islamic. The merchant class was the most consistently Muslim part of the population; commerce required participation in the qadi court system, and full participation required at least nominal Islamic identity.

The court occupied an intermediate position. The Askia was officially a Muslim ruler, performed the public rituals of Islamic kingship, and patronized the Islamic scholarly establishment. But the court also maintained — or at least tolerated — many of the older royal rituals associated with traditional Songhai sacred kingship, and the line between the two systems was deliberately fuzzy in court ceremony. The ambiguity was politically functional: it gave the Askia legitimacy in both cultural systems simultaneously.

---

**Sufi brotherhoods** were the connective tissue.

The principal Sufi order in West Africa was the **Qadiriyya** (kah-dee-REE-yah), founded by **Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani** in twelfth-century Baghdad. The Qadiriyya spread south across the Sahara through trans-Saharan scholarly contacts and was well-established in Timbuktu and the western Sudan by the fifteenth century. Qadiri sheikhs — Sufi-trained scholars — served as teachers, spiritual guides, mediators, judges, healers, and occasionally amulet-makers. Their position was neither quite that of the orthodox ulama (the Sankoré qadis) nor that of the traditional zima ritual specialists; they occupied a middle ground that allowed them to interact with both systems.

A Qadiri marabout (the Berber-influenced West African term for Sufi-influenced scholar-clerics) might travel from village to village teaching basic Quran, mediating local disputes, recommending healing practices that combined Islamic prayer with herbal remedies, and providing protective amulets that combined Quranic verses with traditional protective formulas. The amulet-making in particular was a contested practice — orthodox Sankoré scholars were often hostile to it, viewing it as superstition with a thin Islamic veneer — but it was widely practiced and culturally important.

The Sufi orders' role in Songhai religious life cannot be overstated. They provided the mechanism by which Islamic practice penetrated the rural population, in a form that did not require complete abandonment of traditional religious frameworks. They produced the bulk of the empire's village-level religious leadership. And they preserved the institutional connection between rural religious practice and the broader Islamic intellectual tradition, even when the traditional religious elements predominated. Without the Sufi orders, the layered religious system would not have held together.

---

The **legal system** mirrored the religious layering.

The qadi courts in the major cities applied **Maliki sharia** to commercial cases, inheritance disputes among Muslims, marriage and divorce among Muslims, and criminal cases involving Muslims. The system was integrated into the broader Maliki legal world — Songhai qadis cited the same legal authorities as Cairo and Fez qadis, applied the same procedural rules, and produced fatwas that circulated within the broader Maliki scholarly network. Ahmed Baba (Chapter 5) was the late-Songhai legal tradition's representative figure.

Outside the cities, most disputes were resolved by traditional Songhai legal mechanisms — the village headman's court, the customary law of the clan, the mediation of Sufi marabouts, the consensus-building of community elders. These traditional institutions did not generally claim to be sharia courts and did not apply Islamic legal categories. They handled the vast majority of routine legal business in the rural empire.

The boundary between the two systems was negotiated case by case. Commercial cases involving Muslim merchants typically went to the qadi court regardless of the parties' rural or urban origin. Inheritance cases involving Muslim heirs followed Maliki rules even in rural settings, often with a marabout substituting for an absent qadi. Cases involving land tenure or pastoral rights — areas where traditional Songhai law had deep roots and Maliki law had limited applicability — were handled by traditional institutions. Cases involving non-Muslims (the majority of the rural population) were handled by traditional institutions across the board.

The Askia rulers periodically attempted to extend Maliki legal authority more deeply into rural areas — Askia Muhammad's qadi court system was the most ambitious of these attempts — but they could not realistically force every village to accept a foreign legal framework, and the practical result was the negotiated coexistence described above. This was not bad governance; it was a working accommodation of plural legal traditions in a single empire, and one that worked tolerably well for most of the sixteenth century.

---

**Slavery** was central to the economy and to Songhai's place in the early-modern global commercial system.

Songhai slavery had multiple sources. The largest was **war captives** — slaves taken during military campaigns against the Mossi, the Dogon, the forest peoples south of the Sahel, and during raids on smaller polities at the empire's frontiers. The slave-taking was systematic; military campaigns were planned partly with slave-acquisition as an explicit objective, and the slave caravans returning from successful campaigns were one of the regular features of imperial military life.

The slaves had several destinations. Many remained inside the empire, working royal estates that produced grain and livestock for the imperial treasury, staffing elite households as domestic servants, serving in the standing army's slave-soldier units (the *sofa*), or working in artisanal production. Others were sold north across the Sahara to the Maghreb and Egypt, where they entered the broader Islamic-world slave system that supplied military slaves to the Mamluks and Ottomans, domestic slaves to the wealthy households of Cairo and Tunis, and laborers to the salt mines of the central Sahara. A smaller flow went west to the coast as the Atlantic trade developed.

The total slave population of Songhai itself was substantial — probably on the order of a quarter of the empire's total population, though the figure is not directly attested in any source and depends heavily on estimation. The institution was deeply embedded in the social and economic system in a way that made it structurally inseparable from imperial governance: the standing army depended on slave-soldiers, the royal estates depended on slave labor, the imperial treasury depended on the slave trade as a revenue source.

The **Maliki legal tradition** that the Songhai courts applied accepted slavery as a legitimate institution under specified conditions. War captives could be enslaved; debt slaves could be acquired through judicial process; the children of slaves were slaves. But Maliki law also imposed significant restrictions: free-born Muslims could not be enslaved regardless of circumstance; slaves had certain rights that masters were obligated to respect; manumission was meritorious. Ahmed Baba's *Mi'raj al-Su'ud* — the treatise arguing against the enslavement of free-born Muslims regardless of skin color — was a serious application of Maliki legal principles to the practical question of who could and could not be enslaved in the trans-Saharan trade. Ahmed Baba was not arguing against slavery itself; he was arguing against its racialization, and against the practice of enslaving Muslims of African origin who were free-born under Maliki law.

The argument matters historically because the Atlantic slave trade that was about to emerge as the dominant West African commercial flow had no such legal restrictions. European slavers operating from the Atlantic coast did not care about Maliki legal categories, did not distinguish between Muslims and non-Muslims, and did not respect the Islamic legal protections for free-born status that the Songhai system had incorporated. The Atlantic system was, on its own terms, more brutally racialized and less legally constrained than the trans-Saharan Islamic system. The shift from one to the other over the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries was not the introduction of slavery to West Africa — slavery was already endemic — but the introduction of a fundamentally different and more destructive form of it.

---

The **Tuareg and Mossi raids** were the persistent security problem.

The Tuareg confederations of the central Sahara — the Iwellemmedan, the Kel Ahaggar, and several smaller groups — were intermittently hostile to Songhai's caravan trade and made periodic raids on Niger Bend markets when central authority weakened. The relationship was complicated by the fact that some Tuareg groups were trading partners and tributary allies of the empire, while others were raiders and rivals. The *korei-farma* minister at the Gao court managed this complex relationship, alternating taxation, diplomatic gifts, military pressure, and selective alliance-making across the various Tuareg groups.

The Mossi cavalry from the upper Volta basin — south of the Songhai heartland — were a recurring problem on the southern frontier. Mossi raids targeted Sahelian markets for slaves and livestock, and the Mossi warriors were skilled enough at fast cavalry warfare to evade Songhai pursuit columns and disappear back into their territory before reinforcements could arrive. Sonni Ali had campaigned against the Mossi without subjugating them; Askia Muhammad and his successors continued the campaigns with similar lack of decisive success. The Mossi remained an autonomous southern frontier through the entire Songhai period.

The persistent inability to handle either threat was a structural feature of Songhai's military system — designed for conquest of fortified cities and control of river networks, less effective at the kind of fast-pursuit cavalry warfare the Tuareg and Mossi forced. The same structural limitation would matter at Tondibi.

# Chapter 7 — The Long Decay

Askia Muhammad's deposition by his son in 1528 was the first sign that the dynastic succession system Askia Muhammad had built was structurally unsound. Over the sixty-three years between Askia Muhammad's overthrow and the Moroccan invasion of 1591, the empire saw a rapid succession of contested reigns, palace coups, civil wars, and intermittent restorations of stability. The institutions Askia Muhammad had built were resilient enough to absorb individual succession crises; they were not resilient enough to absorb a continuous succession crisis that ran across multiple generations.

This chapter is about the long decay: the post-Askia-Muhammad chaos, the long stable reign of Askia Dawud as the empire's last great ruler, the post-Dawud collapse, and the structural gap between Songhai's military system and the gunpowder revolution that was about to arrive at the gates.

The chapter is shorter than the rest because the decay is unpleasant to read about and because the structural pattern is the same throughout: a short reign by a contested Askia, a coup by a brother or son, a brief moment of restored stability, another coup. Distinct events do not stand out so much as a cumulative pattern of institutional decay. By the time Tondibi came around in 1591, Songhai had spent two generations weakening itself.

---

The **immediate post-Askia-Muhammad crisis** ran from 1528 to roughly 1549.

**Askia Musa** (1528–1531), the son who had deposed his father, was deposed and killed by his own brothers within three years. **Askia Muhammad II Bonkana** (1531–1537) was deposed by another brother. **Askia Ismail** (1537–1539) reigned briefly before dying — possibly of natural causes, possibly poisoned, the chronicles disagree. **Askia Ishaq I** (1539–1549) finally provided a decade of relative stability before being challenged by his brother, the future Askia Dawud. The pattern was: a coup brought a new Askia to power, the new Askia faced rival claims from his brothers, palace conspiracies and outright civil war intermittent through each reign. The empire's institutions absorbed the chaos at significant cost — the cavalry aristocracy fragmented into competing factions, the provincial governors became increasingly independent, the tribute flows from the eastern provinces became erratic.

At the same time, the **Saadian dynasty** in Morocco was rising to power. The Saadians — an Arab-descended family from the Sous Valley in southern Morocco — had displaced the Wattasid Berber dynasty in 1549 (the same year Askia Dawud took the Songhai throne) and were beginning the territorial consolidation that would eventually produce Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur. The Saadians had inherited the Wattasid commercial relationship with Songhai across the Sahara, and they were beginning to think about whether trans-Saharan commerce could be supplemented by direct trans-Saharan military projection.

---

**Askia Dawud** (1549–1582) was the empire's last great ruler.

Dawud's thirty-three-year reign restored stability after the post-1528 chaos. He suppressed the most disruptive factional conflicts in the cavalry aristocracy, normalized the tribute flows from the provincial governors, maintained the trans-Saharan trade at peak volume, and continued the patronage of Timbuktu's scholarly community. He repelled the first serious Moroccan raid on Taghaza (around 1556, when Sultan Muhammad al-Shaykh of the Saadians sent a small expedition to seize the salt mines, which the Songhai forces eventually drove back). He added a personal library to the imperial court and encouraged the manuscript trade. The chronicles treat Dawud as the last competent Askia and one of the great Sahelian rulers — comparable to Askia Muhammad in his administrative competence, though without the spectacular hajj and the founding-figure status.

Two features of Dawud's reign deserve attention.

First, Dawud's **handling of the cavalry aristocracy** was the most successful in the dynasty's history. He used a combination of careful patronage, strategic marriage alliances among the noble lineages, periodic rotation of provincial governors, and occasional punitive campaigns against particularly disruptive factions to keep the aristocracy in line. The system worked because Dawud was personally adroit at it; it did not transfer to his successors, who lacked his political skill.

Second, Dawud **avoided** the gunpowder restructuring that the Ottomans, Safavids, Mughals, and Saadians were all undertaking during the same decades. The Songhai military remained a cavalry-and-fleet army, with at most a handful of personal arquebuses at the imperial court and no organized gunpowder infantry units. The decision was probably driven by the cavalry aristocracy's resistance to a restructuring that would have devalued their status, by the geographic difficulty of producing gunpowder in commercial quantities in West Africa, and by the absence of any acute external pressure that would have forced the issue. The Saadian raid on Taghaza in 1556 should have been a warning sign — a small Moroccan force armed with firearms had reached deep into Songhai's northern frontier — but the raid had been driven off, the threat had not materialized at the imperial center, and the cavalry aristocracy had every reason to interpret the episode as proof that the existing military system was adequate.

The structural mistake was made during Dawud's reign, not during his successors'. By the time the Saadians came back in force in 1591, the gap between Songhai's cavalry army and the gunpowder forces of the Mediterranean Islamic world was wide enough that no quick adjustment could close it. The institutional decision against gunpowder restructuring was a generation-long commitment that locked Songhai into a military trajectory it could not easily reverse.

---

The **post-Dawud civil wars** (1582–1588) destroyed the empire's central authority faster than anyone expected.

Dawud's death in 1582 was followed by a sequence of contested successions. **Askia al-Hajj** (1582–1586), one of Dawud's sons, was deposed by his brother **Askia Muhammad Bani** (1586–1588) in a brief civil war that involved at least one major battle and substantial casualties among the cavalry aristocracy. Bani was deposed in turn by another brother, **Askia Ishaq II** (1588–1591), in another brief civil war. Each succession involved palace coups, the killing or exiling of competing princes, the reshuffling of provincial governorships, and the breakdown of the careful factional balance that Dawud had maintained.

The cumulative effect was that by 1588, when **Askia Ishaq II** took the throne, the cavalry aristocracy was fractured into competing factions, several of the largest provincial governorships were effectively independent of central control, and the standing army's morale was visibly poor. The empire's institutions still existed on paper; in practice, they were operating at a small fraction of their capacity. Tribute flows were down, military readiness was down, the trans-Saharan trade was still functioning but increasingly dependent on local arrangements rather than imperial authority.

This was the situation that Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur of Morocco was watching from Marrakesh.

---

**Ahmad al-Mansur** had become Saadian sultan in 1578, after the Battle of the Three Kings.

The **Battle of the Three Kings** (also called the Battle of Wadi al-Makhazin) at Ksar el-Kebir in northern Morocco on August 4, 1578, was one of the great Mediterranean military catastrophes of the sixteenth century. King **Sebastian I of Portugal**, a young and inexperienced ruler obsessed with the idea of a crusade against Muslim Morocco, had landed an army of around 17,000 Portuguese troops and Moroccan allies (under the Saadian claimant Abu Abdallah Mohammed II, who was trying to recover the throne from his uncle) on the Moroccan coast. The Saadian sultan **Abd al-Malik** met them with a larger army of perhaps 50,000 troops including a substantial gunpowder infantry contingent. The battle was a catastrophic Portuguese defeat: the Portuguese army was annihilated, King Sebastian was killed (his body was identified the next day), Abu Abdallah Mohammed II was killed, and Sultan Abd al-Malik died of natural causes during the battle itself. Three kings dead on a single battlefield.

Abd al-Malik's brother **Ahmad** — soon to be al-Mansur, "the Victorious" — succeeded immediately. The ransom money for the surviving Portuguese nobles, the captured arms and equipment, and the political prestige of the victory gave Ahmad al-Mansur both a treasury surplus and the international standing of a major Islamic ruler. He spent the next thirteen years building Morocco into a regional power, modernizing the Saadian army with European-trained gunpowder specialists (some of them captured Portuguese converts to Islam), and quietly developing the intelligence and logistical preparation for a project that no Moroccan ruler had ever attempted: a trans-Saharan invasion of Songhai aimed at seizing the Sahelian gold supply at its source.

Why did al-Mansur undertake this? The standard explanation is the gold motive: Saadian Morocco was running structural budget deficits, the Mediterranean European demand for gold was high, and the trans-Saharan trade was perceived as inadequately profitable for Morocco when the Songhai were the middlemen taking most of the profit. If Morocco controlled the source — Taghaza for the salt, the Bambuk-Bure goldfields for the gold — Saadian revenues would expand dramatically. The calculation was probably partly correct (Taghaza was a real and sizable revenue source) and partly wrong (the Bambuk-Bure goldfields were not actually located where al-Mansur thought they were, and the Moroccan occupation never managed to control the gold supply at the source).

But there were also non-economic motives. Al-Mansur wanted the prestige of expanding the Islamic world's southern frontier. He wanted to demonstrate the Saadian dynasty as the dominant power of the western Mediterranean Islamic world. He wanted to secure a southern strategic depth against Ottoman pressure on Morocco's eastern flank. The gold was the public justification, but the broader project was political and prestige-driven.

---

Al-Mansur sent a series of diplomatic feelers to Songhai during the late 1580s, demanding tribute payments and presenting various claims to the Taghaza salt mines and to broader Sahelian territory. **Askia Ishaq II**, on the throne from 1588, replied with the gestures of contempt that Sahelian rulers traditionally extended to upstart Maghrebi sultans: he sent al-Mansur a gift of two hundred sticks of iron and a token offering of gold dust, with a message that suggested the Saadian sultan's claims were as ridiculous as the gift was small. The diplomatic exchange was, in effect, a declaration that Songhai did not take al-Mansur seriously.

This was a miscalculation. Al-Mansur was already preparing the invasion. The Moroccan expeditionary force was being assembled, the trans-Saharan logistics were being planned, the European-converted gunpowder specialists were being trained. Ishaq's contemptuous reply removed any remaining diplomatic constraints on the invasion plan. Within two years the Moroccan army would be marching south.

---

A pause for cross-civilizational context. The 1580s were a remarkable decade across the early-modern world.

While Songhai was tearing itself apart in succession civil wars, the **Spanish Armada** was being built and then destroyed (1588) by the English navy and the Atlantic weather. **Philip II of Spain** was at the peak of his global power, ruling a Habsburg empire that included Iberia, the Low Countries, much of Italy, and the entire Spanish New World. **Elizabeth I of England** was consolidating Protestant rule and patronizing Shakespeare and Marlowe at the start of their careers.

In the Islamic world, the **Ottomans** under Sultan Murad III were at the territorial peak the empire would ever reach (Ottoman Empire Ch 4). The **Safavids** under Shah Abbas I (taking the throne in 1588, the same year as Askia Ishaq II) were beginning the ghulam revolution that would restructure the Iranian state (Safavid Persia Ch 4). The **Mughals** under Akbar were consolidating their grip on northern India, having defeated the last major Rajput resistance and integrated the Hindu nobility into the imperial administrative system. The **Saadians** under Ahmad al-Mansur were preparing the invasion that would shortly destroy Songhai.

All four of these Islamic empires — Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal, Saadian — had completed their gunpowder transitions by 1588. All four were running professional standing armies built around firearms infantry and artillery. Songhai had not made the transition, and was about to discover what that meant.

---

# Chapter 8 — Tondibi 1591

In late 1590, a Moroccan expeditionary force of around 4,000 men set out from Marrakesh on a march that no organized military had ever attempted. They were heading south across the Sahara to invade the Songhai Empire — a thousand-mile march across some of the harshest desert on the planet, against an enemy fielding a much larger cavalry army on terrain the Moroccans had never seen, with logistics that depended on a chain of oases and salt-mining outposts that might or might not provision them adequately. The expedition was, by any sober military assessment, extremely high-risk. It was also a complete success.

This chapter is about the 1591 campaign: al-Mansur's calculation that produced the project, the trans-Saharan crossing of Judar Pasha's force, the matchlock-vs-cavalry showdown at Tondibi, and the long aftermath — the Pashalik of Timbuktu, Ahmed Baba's exile, the fragmentation of the western Sudan, the Dendi successor kingdom, and the structural reorientation of West African commerce away from the Sahara and toward the Atlantic that would prevent any Sahelian successor empire from emerging.

---

**Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur's calculation** was the strategic foundation.

Al-Mansur had been preparing the trans-Saharan invasion for at least five years before the 1590 expedition departed. The preparations included intelligence gathering on Songhai's military and political condition (probably through the Taghaza salt traders and through Sahelian merchants visiting Marrakesh), logistical planning for the Saharan crossing (identifying water sources, calculating provisioning requirements, training pack-animal handlers), recruitment of European converts and Andalusian Moriscos with gunpowder expertise, and the assembly of an expeditionary force whose composition was specifically designed for the conditions it would face.

The force was small by Sahelian battlefield standards — al-Mansur knew that any larger force would be impossible to provision across the Sahara — but heavily armed. The composition included around 2,500 musketeers (mostly Andalusian Moriscos and European converts trained in matchlock infantry tactics), 1,500 cavalry (mounted lancers serving as a screening force and pursuit element), a small artillery train of perhaps 6 to 10 light cannons, and a substantial logistics tail of camel handlers, water-carriers, and support personnel. Total expedition size was around 4,000 fighting men and a roughly equal number of support personnel.

The commander was **Judar Pasha** (joo-DAR PAH-shah), a Spanish-born eunuch who had been captured as a child in Almería, taken to Marrakesh as a slave, and risen through the Saadian military hierarchy. The choice of commander is interesting: Judar was a competent professional officer with no significant political connections in the Saadian court, which made him both a reliable executive of al-Mansur's plan and a politically expendable scapegoat if the expedition failed. The Pasha rank was a Saadian institutional borrowing from the Ottomans — a senior military and provincial-administrative title.

---

The **trans-Saharan crossing** lasted from October 1590 to February 1591 — roughly four months across some of the most lethal terrain on the planet.

The route went south from Marrakesh through the **Draa Valley**, then southwest into the western Sahara, then across the central Sahara via the salt-mining oasis of Taghaza, then on south to the Niger Bend. Total distance was around 1,500 miles (2,400 km). The expedition had pre-positioned water and provisions at intermediate stopping points where possible, but several stretches between water sources ran 50 to 100 miles with nothing — fatal for any group that lost its way or ran short on water.

The crossing was catastrophic for the expedition. Heat, thirst, exhaustion, disease, and accident killed roughly **half** of the original force before the survivors reached the Niger. The casualty rate was particularly heavy among the camels and pack animals; equipment had to be abandoned along the route to keep the survivors moving. Some of the artillery was lost. By the time the column reached the Niger Bend in February 1591, Judar's effective fighting force was probably around 2,500 to 3,000 men, with the rest dead or too debilitated to fight.

That this was still enough to win the war is the great structural fact of Tondibi.

---

The **Battle of Tondibi** was fought on March 13, 1591, at the village of **Tondibi** (TON-dee-bee) on the Niger River about 35 miles north of Gao.

**Askia Ishaq II** had assembled the Songhai army at Tondibi to block the Moroccan advance. The chronicle figures for the Songhai force vary widely — from 12,000 to 50,000 — but the most plausible estimate is around 30,000 cavalry plus a much larger but less mobile infantry contingent. The cavalry was the strike arm; the infantry was the mass formation expected to follow up the cavalry's initial breakthrough. Ishaq personally commanded the army, accompanied by the senior cavalry aristocracy and the imperial standing-army units.

Judar's force, by contrast, was probably around 2,500 effectives. The Moroccans were a tiny fraction of the size of the Songhai army. They had two structural advantages: the matchlock muskets, and the artillery.

The battle began with the standard Sahelian opening: a Songhai cavalry charge, intended to break the Moroccan formation and open the way for the infantry to roll up the broken units. The Songhai cavalry probably numbered several thousand at the leading edge, with thousands more in supporting waves. The charge crossed open ground toward the Moroccan position.

The Moroccans responded with **organized matchlock volley fire**. The musketeers were arranged in disciplined lines, trained to fire in rotation so that one rank fired while the others reloaded. Maintained continuous fire — perhaps two volleys per minute from the front line, sustained over the few minutes it took the cavalry to cover the open ground. The artillery fired solid shot through the leading cavalry ranks. The combined firepower destroyed the cavalry charge before it reached the Moroccan formation. Songhai cavalry casualties were heavy. The supporting cavalry waves saw the front rank shatter and either pulled up short or attempted to maneuver around the flanks of the Moroccan formation. The flanking attempts ran into the Moroccan cavalry screen and were repulsed.

What turned the engagement into a rout was a tactical error by **a herd of cattle** that the Songhai had positioned on the flank to break the Moroccan formation. The cattle, panicked by the gunfire, stampeded **back into** the Songhai infantry rather than forward into the Moroccans. The Songhai infantry formation broke. The cavalry, seeing the infantry collapse, began to withdraw. Within a few hours the Songhai army had disintegrated entirely. Ishaq fled south with a small bodyguard. The Moroccans, still numbering several thousand, occupied the battlefield.

Tondibi was, in retrospect, one of the foundational early-modern demonstrations that gunpowder infantry could destroy a much larger pre-gunpowder cavalry force in open battle. The Ottomans had demonstrated something similar at Chaldiran in 1514 (Safavid Persia Ch 3) against the Safavid Qizilbash. The Mughals would demonstrate it again in their later sixteenth-century campaigns. The Habsburgs and the Swedes would demonstrate it across the Thirty Years' War. The same structural pattern was running across the entire early-modern Islamic and European military world. Tondibi was the West African instance of a global military revolution.

---

The **Moroccan occupation** of Gao came within days. Judar's force entered the Songhai capital without resistance. Ishaq's bodyguard had withdrawn south down the Niger toward the Dendi region, and the city's remaining population had no military capacity for resistance. The Moroccans found Gao less wealthy than al-Mansur's intelligence had suggested — much of the imperial treasury had been removed by the fleeing Askia — and the looting was extensive but commercially disappointing. Gao would never recover its imperial role.

Judar then moved on **Timbuktu**, occupying the city in April 1591. The conquest of Timbuktu was the strategic centerpiece of the campaign — it was the city al-Mansur had really wanted, both for its commercial centrality and for its symbolic status as the capital of West African Islamic learning. The occupation was followed by systematic looting of the city's libraries, the deportation of leading scholars including Ahmed Baba (next section), and the imposition of direct Moroccan military rule.

Within a few months of Tondibi, the structural occupation of the Niger Bend was complete. The Moroccans held Gao, Timbuktu, and Djenné under direct military government. The provincial governors of the more distant provinces accommodated as best they could to the new reality — some by submitting to Moroccan suzerainty, some by quietly going independent in their own provinces, some by attempting and failing to organize resistance. The Songhai imperial administrative structure had simply ceased to function. The empire was over.

---

**Ahmed Baba's exile** is the single act that captures the cultural cost of the conquest.

Ahmed Baba was forty-five years old in 1591, in his prime as a Maliki jurist and scholar, with a major library, an active teaching practice at Sankoré, and a position as one of the most respected figures in the Timbuktu scholarly community. The Moroccans arrested him in Timbuktu in 1593, charged him (probably correctly) with leading the Sankoré scholars' resistance to the occupation, and transported him in chains across the Sahara to Marrakesh. Sultan al-Mansur intended to display him as a trophy of the conquest. Ahmed Baba's library, possibly the largest single collection in Timbuktu at that point, was confiscated; some volumes were carried to Marrakesh, others were lost or stolen.

The exile lasted fifteen years, from 1593 to 1608. Ahmed Baba was held under restricted conditions in Marrakesh — initially in actual prison, later under a kind of house arrest in a residence on the city's southern edge. He continued to write, to receive visitors, to teach Moroccan students who came to study with him, and to correspond with scholars across the Islamic world. Some of his most important works, including the biographical dictionary of Maliki jurists, were composed or completed during the Marrakesh exile.

His Marrakesh teaching had a paradoxical effect: it transferred a substantial body of Timbuktu scholarship into the Moroccan intellectual world. Ahmed Baba's Moroccan students carried his teaching into the next generation of Maghrebi Maliki jurisprudence. The brain drain that the occupation had inflicted on Timbuktu was partly a brain transfer to Marrakesh. The cultural cost was real, but it was a redistribution rather than a destruction.

Ahmed Baba was finally allowed to return to Timbuktu in 1608, after Sultan al-Mansur's death. He spent the last nineteen years of his life in his home city, teaching the next generation of Sankoré scholars and continuing the literary output. He died in Timbuktu in 1627, near seventy years old. The Ahmed Baba Institute in modern Timbuktu — established in the 1970s as the central archive of Timbuktu manuscripts and devastated by the 2012 jihadist occupation — preserves his name.

---

The **Pashalik of Timbuktu** — the Moroccan-officered military regime that governed the conquered territories — institutionalized within a few years of the conquest.

The Pashalik was nominally subject to Marrakesh, with the Pasha of Timbuktu appointed by the Saadian sultan and reporting back through standard imperial channels. In practice, the Pashalik became autonomous within a generation. The reasons were structural: the trans-Saharan distance made effective central control impossible (it took several months for messages to travel between Marrakesh and Timbuktu), the Moroccan soldiers stationed in the Niger Bend married into local Songhai and Wangara families and developed local interests that diverged from Marrakesh's, and the Saadian dynasty itself collapsed in the 1620s into a long civil war that prevented Marrakesh from maintaining any consistent imperial policy toward its trans-Saharan territories.

The descendants of the Saadian soldiers — known as the **Arma** (from the Arabic *al-rumat*, "the marksmen" or "the gunners") — settled in Timbuktu and Gao, married into local families, and gradually formed an autonomous Mande-Arabic-speaking military caste that ruled the Niger Bend cities into the early nineteenth century. They paid nominal tribute to Marrakesh; they were independent within a generation; they operated more like a creole frontier oligarchy than an imperial garrison. The Arma maintained the gunpowder military advantage — they continued to acquire firearms and gunpowder through trans-Saharan trade — but they did not attempt to rebuild the Songhai Empire's territorial extent. The Arma regime was a contracted, defensive holding operation centered on the Niger Bend cities, not an expansionist successor state.

The Pashalik formally lasted until the early nineteenth century, when the Tuareg confederations and the rising Fulani jihads of the western Sudan swept it aside. The Arma population assimilated into the local Songhai-Mandinka-Berber demographic mix and survives in modern northern Mali as the **Arma** ethnic identity, with its own cultural memory of descent from the 1591 expeditionary force.

---

The **Dendi successor kingdom** maintained the Askia dynasty in exile.

After Tondibi, **Askia Ishaq II** fled south down the Niger to the **Dendi** region — the eastern Niger provinces in what is now the Niger-Benin border zone, far enough from Gao to be beyond the practical reach of the Moroccan occupation. Ishaq was deposed by his own brother **Muhammad Gao** within months and disappears from the historical record. Muhammad Gao established a successor state at the city of **Lulami** (later moved to other capitals as the kingdom shifted across the centuries), which maintained the Askia dynasty and the imperial cultural traditions for another three centuries.

The Dendi kingdom never recovered Songhai's territorial scale. It was a regional power, controlling the Niger frontier between the Hausa city-states and the contracting Mossi kingdoms, but it was not an empire. It maintained the Songhai language, the Askia royal lineage, the administrative titles (the *koi* and *fari* offices), the qadi court system, and many of the religious and cultural institutions of imperial Songhai. The institutional continuity is striking: the Askia dynasty survived in Dendi for roughly the same length of time as the imperial Songhai had existed, finally being absorbed by French colonial conquest in 1901.

The Dendi survival is one of those underappreciated historical patterns: the great medieval imperial dynasties often outlived the empires themselves by remarkable spans, in the form of regional successor states. The Sasanian dynastic memory survived the Arab conquest in various Iranian successor traditions; the Byzantine cultural inheritance survived the fall of Constantinople in the Greek Orthodox Church; the Mali Keita lineage survived in Kangaba (Mali Empire Ch 8). The Askia survival in Dendi belongs to the same pattern. The empire was over; the dynasty was not.

---

The **structural reorientation of West African commerce** was the slow shadow that prevented any successor empire from emerging.

By 1591, the Atlantic slave trade was already replacing the trans-Saharan gold trade as the dominant West African commercial flow. European trading forts dotted the coast — Elmina (Portuguese, 1482), Cape Coast (English, later), Whydah (Dutch, later) — and the African political ecosystem was beginning to reorient toward the coastal trade rather than the Saharan trade. The new commercial centers — Ouidah, Cape Coast, the Niger Delta city-states — were positioned for Atlantic shipping, not for Saharan caravans. The economic basis for a Sahelian empire was being hollowed out from the south.

The Moroccan occupation accelerated this reorientation by destroying the trans-Saharan commercial infrastructure that had survived the conquest. Without the unified Songhai imperial administration, the trans-Saharan trade became more dangerous, more expensive, and less consistently profitable. The merchant networks shifted south toward the coast. The Wangara commercial diaspora reorganized along Atlantic-facing routes. The gold flow that had once moved north across the Sahara to Sahelian markets and then on to Cairo and the Mediterranean increasingly moved south to the coastal forts and across the Atlantic to Lisbon, Amsterdam, and London.

The Sahel was not depopulated, the cities were not abandoned, the cultural traditions did not vanish. But the structural conditions that had made empire-building possible in the western Sahel — the trans-Saharan trade as a concentrated revenue source, the agricultural surplus of the Inner Niger Delta, the gold supply chain running north — were all weakening simultaneously. After Songhai, no successor state would manage to consolidate Sahelian power on a comparable scale.

The Mossi kingdoms persisted into the colonial period. The Bambara kingdoms of Segou and Kaarta emerged in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries as regional powers, never approaching Songhai's territorial extent. The various Fulani jihad states of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries — the Sokoto Caliphate in modern northern Nigeria, the Massina Empire in the central Inner Niger Delta, the Tukulor Empire of al-Hajj Umar Tall — were significant regional polities but operated in a fundamentally transformed commercial environment. None of them matched Songhai's scale.

---

A pause for the broader picture, and the myth-puncturing that needs to happen.

Songhai's destruction is sometimes presented as evidence for "what West African civilization could have been if not for European colonialism." The framing is wrong on several counts.

First, the agent that ended Songhai was not European. It was the Saadian Sultanate of Morocco — an Arab-Berber Islamic state with no European political affiliation, operating on its own strategic calculations. Al-Mansur's invasion was a classic early-modern imperial expansion, motivated by the same calculations of revenue and prestige that drove Suleiman's Hungarian wars or Akbar's Deccan campaigns or Shah Abbas's Caucasus campaigns. It was an Islamic-world geopolitical event, not a European intervention.

Second, the technology that enabled the Saadian victory — the matchlock musket, the artillery train, the disciplined volley-fire infantry — was not European in the sense that "European technology destroyed African civilization" implies. Gunpowder weapons had been developed in China (Tang-Song China), refined across the Mongol-era trade networks (Mongol Empire Ch 8), spread through the Islamic world by the early sixteenth century, and adopted by Mediterranean Europeans in roughly the same timeframe. By 1591, the Ottomans, Safavids, Mughals, Saadians, and Habsburg Spanish were all using essentially the same matchlock-and-artillery military system. The Saadians acquired their gunpowder expertise partly from European Moriscos, but the underlying technology was a transcontinental Islamic-world adoption, not a European import.

Third, what European colonialism did to West Africa was structural rather than conquering. The Atlantic trade reorientation that hollowed out the trans-Saharan gold supply was a slow shadow over the entire seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, gradually starving the Sahelian states of their economic foundation. The actual European colonial conquest of West Africa came in the late nineteenth century — three centuries after Tondibi — and it operated on a Sahel that had already been structurally transformed by the Atlantic shift. The "what could have been" speculation underestimates how long the Atlantic reorientation had been working before European armies actually arrived.

The honest reading of Songhai's fall is that it was destroyed by a combination of three factors. First, internal: the long succession crisis after Askia Dawud's death had visibly weakened the central authority by 1591, making Saadian intervention attractive in a way it had not been a generation earlier. Second, military: the gunpowder gap that Songhai had failed to close during the Dawud reign meant that even a small Moroccan force could defeat a much larger Songhai army in open battle. Third, structural: the Atlantic reorientation was already underway, and even if Songhai had defeated the Saadian invasion in 1591, the long-term economic basis for the Sahelian empire was slowly collapsing.

Songhai was not destroyed by colonial Europe. It was destroyed by the early-modern global gunpowder revolution, applied locally by a Moroccan sultan, against an empire that had been weakened by its own succession problems and was operating in an economy that was gradually being restructured by long-distance commercial shifts none of the participants fully understood. The story is complex enough on its own terms; it does not need to be flattened into a colonial-grievance narrative.

---

# Coda — What Survived

The Songhai Empire ended in 1591. The Songhai people did not, the language did not, the cultural traditions did not, and the manuscript tradition that had been Songhai's greatest intellectual achievement did not.

The **modern Songhai population** — perhaps several million speakers across modern Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso — is the direct demographic descendant of the imperial population. The language is still spoken; the traditional religion still exists in modified form; the river-fishing culture of the Sorko still operates along the Niger; the trading networks of the Wangara diaspora still connect the Niger Bend to the Mediterranean and increasingly to the Atlantic.

The **manuscript tradition** survived in private libraries through the centuries. The 2012 rescue operation, led by Abdel Kader Haidara and a network of volunteers, smuggled tens of thousands of medieval manuscripts out of Timbuktu in metal trunks and rice sacks ahead of the jihadist occupation, hiding them in private homes in Bamako. The operation rescued the largest concentration of pre-colonial sub-Saharan African writing still in existence — and most of what was rescued was Songhai-era Maliki jurisprudence, hadith collections, theological treatises, and the literary output of the Sankoré scholarly tradition. The work of cataloging, digitizing, and translating the rescued manuscripts is still ongoing, decades after it began, and is one of the great unfinished projects of African historical scholarship.

The **Tomb of Askia** at Gao still stands. UNESCO inscribed it as a World Heritage Site in 2004. The mud-brick monument has required continuous maintenance for five hundred years, and the maintenance continues.

The **Dendi kingdom** maintained the Askia dynasty in exile until the French colonial conquest in 1901. The ceremonial regalia of the Askias survived in the Dendi royal compound; some of the symbolic objects from the original Mecca-investiture of Askia Muhammad in 1497 — the green turban, the Prophet's-relic sword — are reportedly still preserved in family hands.

The **three great mosques of Timbuktu** still function as working mosques. They have been re-plastered hundreds of times since they were built. The maintenance is performed annually by the city's religious community, in the traditional Sudano-Sahelian technique that has not significantly changed in seven centuries.

The **trans-Saharan caravan routes** that Songhai had taxed and protected fell into long decline after 1591 but never quite vanished. Salt caravans from Taghaza were still moving south to the Niger as recently as the late twentieth century, when the construction of paved roads and the rise of motor transport finally rendered the camel caravans obsolete. The economic logic of the trans-Saharan trade — salt south from the desert, gold north from the savanna — had outlasted the empires it built by four centuries.

What did not survive was the imperial state. Songhai produced no successor empire on its own scale. The structural reorientation of West African commerce toward the Atlantic prevented any new Sahelian empire from consolidating after 1591. The political vacuum was filled by smaller regional kingdoms — Bambara, Mossi, Hausa, Fulani — that were significant in their own contexts but never approached Songhai's territorial extent or institutional sophistication. The largest continuous territory ever ruled by a sub-Saharan African state, the Songhai imperial peak around 1515, has not been matched by any successor.

What does it mean to call a civilization "destroyed" when the people, the language, the religion, the cultural traditions, and the manuscript archive all survive? Songhai is a useful test case for that question. The empire is gone — five centuries gone, with no real possibility of reconstruction — but the underlying civilization continues. The matchlock at Tondibi ended the imperial state; it did not end the people who built it.

That is most of what can be said. The rest is a matter of cataloging the manuscripts, walking the Tomb of Askia, listening to the modern Songhai musicians who carry the cultural memory forward, reading the Tarikh chronicles in their cramped Arabic script, and trying to imagine what fifteenth-century Timbuktu actually looked like under the Mecca-blessed regime that thought it had built something permanent. The empire built more than it knew. Most of what it built outlasted it.

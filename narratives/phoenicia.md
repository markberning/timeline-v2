# Phoenicia — The People Who Connected the World

**Goal:** Narrative for Phoenicia, the Levantine coastal civilization that never built an empire but built something more durable: the alphabet, the trade routes, the colonies. Every other Mediterranean power conquered territory; the Phoenicians connected it. They gave the world its most consequential invention (the alphabet) and its most dramatic villain (Hannibal), and then vanished so completely that most people today couldn't name a single Phoenician city. This narrative covers roughly 1500 to 146 BCE, from the Bronze Age city-states under Egyptian suzerainty through the invention of the alphabet, the founding of Carthage, the golden age of Tyrian purple and Solomon's Temple, the centuries of subjugation under Assyria and Babylon and Persia, the Carthaginian wars against Rome, and the twin destructions that ended the Phoenician world: Alexander's causeway at Tyre and Scipio's fires at Carthage. The through-line is a civilization that shaped the modern world more profoundly than most empires, and did it without conquering anyone. Phoenicia's alphabet became every alphabet. Phoenicia's colonies became cities that still exist. Phoenicia's trade routes became the arteries of the Mediterranean economy. And Phoenicia itself became a footnote.

# Chapter 1 — The Purple Coast

The Phoenician homeland was a sliver of Mediterranean coast, roughly 200 miles (320 km) long and rarely more than 20 miles (30 km) wide, pinned between the sea on the west and the Lebanon Mountains on the east. It occupied what is now Lebanon plus a narrow strip of coastal Syria and northern Israel. In terms of sheer territory, it was tiny. You could fit the entire Phoenician coast inside Rhode Island twice over. But that narrow strip happened to contain three things that mattered enormously in the Bronze Age world: natural harbors, cedar forests, and a strategic position at the crossroads of three continents.

The harbors came first. The Lebanese coast is rocky and irregular, full of headlands, offshore islands, and protected bays that make natural anchorages. In an era when ships hugged the coastline and needed to pull ashore every night, natural harbors were the most valuable real estate in the Mediterranean. The Phoenicians had dozens of them, and the three best ones became the three greatest Phoenician cities: **Byblos** (BIB-loss; the oldest, roughly 25 miles or 40 km north of modern Beirut), **Sidon** (SY-don; 25 miles or 40 km south of Beirut), and **Tyre** (rhymes with "fire"; 25 miles or 40 km south of Sidon, built partly on the mainland and partly on an offshore island half a mile from shore). These three cities were never unified into a single state. They were independent city-states, each with its own king, its own harbor, its own trade networks, and its own rivalries with the other two. This political fragmentation was both Phoenicia's weakness (they could never resist a determined continental power) and its strength (the competition between cities drove innovation, and if one city fell, the others survived).

The cedar forests were the second asset. The Lebanon Mountains (snow-capped peaks rising to over 10,000 feet, or 3,000 meters, visible from the coastal cities on clear days) were covered in thick stands of **cedar of Lebanon** (Cedrus libani), one of the most prized building materials in the ancient world. Cedar wood is naturally rot-resistant, aromatic, strong, and workable, and in the Bronze Age it was the material of choice for temples, palaces, and ships across the entire eastern Mediterranean. Egypt, which had almost no native timber of any kind, was desperate for Lebanese cedar. Mesopotamia, equally treeless in its alluvial plains, paid enormous sums for it. The cedar trade was Phoenicia's first great industry, and it was old. Egyptian records from around 2600 BCE describe expeditions to Byblos to buy cedar for royal building projects, making the Byblos-Egypt trade route one of the oldest documented commercial relationships in human history. By the time our narrative begins around 1500 BCE, Byblos had been exporting cedar to Egypt for over a thousand years.

---

The third asset was location. The Phoenician coast sat at the intersection of three great Bronze Age civilizational zones: Egypt to the south, Mesopotamia to the east (across the Syrian Desert or down the Euphrates corridor), and the Aegean world to the northwest. Any trade goods moving between these zones could, and often did, pass through Phoenician ports. The Phoenicians were natural middlemen, positioned by geography to broker deals between civilizations that could not easily reach each other directly.

Around 1500 BCE, the Phoenician cities were not yet called "Phoenician." That name comes from the Greek word **phoinikes** (foy-NEE-kays), meaning "purple people," a reference to the famous dye the Phoenicians produced (Chapter 4). The people themselves probably didn't use a single collective name. They identified with their individual cities. A person from Tyre was Tyrian, a person from Sidon was Sidonian, a person from Byblos was Byblian. The Greeks lumped them together because they shared a language, a script, a set of religious practices, and, critically, because they were everywhere the Greeks went in the Mediterranean, selling things.

The language was **Phoenician**, a Semitic language closely related to Hebrew and Aramaic. If you could speak one, you could understand the others with some effort, the way a Spanish speaker can follow Portuguese or Italian. All three belonged to the Northwest Semitic branch, as different from Akkadian (the Semitic language of Mesopotamia) as French is from Romanian. The Phoenicians were also ethnically and culturally part of the broader **Canaanite** world, the collection of Semitic-speaking peoples who inhabited the Levant (the eastern Mediterranean coast, roughly modern Lebanon, Syria, Israel, and Palestine) during the Bronze Age. "Phoenician" and "Canaanite" overlap almost entirely in this period. The distinction is largely a matter of perspective: the Hebrews called their coastal neighbors Canaanites; the Greeks called them Phoenicians; the people themselves called themselves Tyrians or Sidonians.

A note on "firsts" that applies to everything in this narrative. Ancient history is full of claims about who invented what first, and almost every one should be read as "first surviving evidence of" rather than "first time this ever happened." Archaeological luck is uneven. Phoenician cities have been continuously inhabited for thousands of years, meaning their oldest layers are buried under modern Beirut, Sidon, and Tyre and largely inaccessible to excavation. What we know about Phoenicia comes disproportionately from what other people wrote about the Phoenicians (Egyptians, Assyrians, Greeks, Romans), from colonial sites in North Africa and the western Mediterranean, and from the limited excavations possible in Lebanon. The Phoenicians almost certainly wrote their own histories. Every word of them has been lost. Their literature was written on papyrus, which rots, rather than on clay tablets, which survive. Keep that in mind whenever this narrative attributes a "first" to anyone.

---

In 1500 BCE, the Phoenician cities were prosperous trading centers under the political umbrella of **New Kingdom Egypt** (New Kingdom Egypt Ch 1-3). Egypt in this period controlled the Levant as a loose empire, extracting tribute, appointing or confirming local kings, and stationing small garrisons at key points. Egyptian control was not heavy-handed in the Phoenician cities. The pharaohs needed Phoenician trade goods (cedar, olive oil, wine, textiles, and especially the papyrus that Byblos re-exported from Egypt to the rest of the Mediterranean), and the Phoenician kings needed Egyptian protection against rivals and raiders. The relationship was transactional. The Phoenician cities paid tribute and acknowledged Egyptian overlordship; in return, they got access to the Egyptian market and the implicit backing of the most powerful military state in the region.

**Byblos** was the oldest and, in this period, the most important of the Phoenician cities. Its Egyptian connections ran so deep that the city had become the ancient world's clearinghouse for **papyrus** (the writing material made from pressed reeds that was Egypt's chief export commodity). Byblos re-exported Egyptian papyrus across the Mediterranean so thoroughly that the Greeks named their word for "book" after the city: *biblos* became *biblion*, which became "Bible." The word for the book at the center of Western civilization is, literally, "the Byblos thing." Byblos also exported cedar to Egypt on a massive scale, and Egyptian objects found at Byblos (and Byblian objects found in Egypt) demonstrate continuous trade going back to at least 3000 BCE.

The political dynamics of the Phoenician coast in this period are preserved in extraordinary detail thanks to the **Amarna Letters** (a famous archive of diplomatic correspondence found at the Egyptian city of Amarna, dating to roughly 1350-1330 BCE; the archive belongs to the reign of Pharaoh Akhenaten, New Kingdom Egypt Ch 4). Several dozen of these clay tablets are letters from Phoenician kings to the pharaoh, and they paint a vivid picture of local politics: petty rivalries, border disputes, complaints about insufficient Egyptian military support, and, above all, anxiety about the growing power of rivals. **Abimilki** (ah-BIH-mil-kee), king of Tyre, wrote multiple letters to Akhenaten complaining about threats from neighboring kingdoms and pleading for Egyptian reinforcements. **Rib-Hadda** (rib-HAH-dah), king of Byblos, wrote over sixty letters (the most prolific correspondent in the entire Amarna archive) with increasingly desperate appeals for help against the expanding power of the **Amurru** kingdom, a rival Amorite state in the hinterland. Rib-Hadda's letters trace a slow-motion political crisis: a loyal vassal watching his position crumble while his overlord (distracted by religious revolution at home) fails to send aid. His final letters go unanswered, and Rib-Hadda was eventually overthrown and killed.

The Amarna Letters also reveal a critical feature of Phoenician political life: the cities were rivals as much as they were neighbors. Sidon and Tyre competed for commercial supremacy. Byblos feared them both. Local kings formed shifting alliances with inland powers, played off Egyptian authority against local ambitions, and occasionally betrayed each other to gain advantage. This pattern of competitive city-states, each too proud and too commercially successful to accept another's leadership, would persist for the entire lifespan of Phoenician civilization. It meant the Phoenicians never united, never built an empire of their own, and were perpetually vulnerable to any continental power that did.

---

Just to the north of the core Phoenician coast, about 100 miles (160 km) north of Byblos, lay the city of **Ugarit** (oo-GAH-rit; modern Ras Shamra, in coastal Syria). Ugarit was not technically a Phoenician city, but it belonged to the same broader Canaanite cultural world, and it matters enormously for our story because it produced one of the earliest known alphabetic scripts. Around 1300 BCE, Ugaritic scribes developed a **cuneiform alphabet** of 30 letters, wedge-shaped signs pressed into clay tablets, each sign representing a single consonant sound. This was radically different from the thousands of signs used in Mesopotamian cuneiform (which represented syllables and whole words) or the hundreds of signs in Egyptian hieroglyphics (which combined sound-signs with meaning-signs). The Ugaritic alphabet was a simplification so extreme that a person could learn to read and write in days rather than years.

Ugarit also left us the **Baal Cycle**, a set of mythological texts describing the storms, battles, death, and resurrection of the storm god **Baal** (BAH-ahl; meaning "lord" or "master"). The Baal Cycle is the most complete surviving example of Canaanite-Phoenician mythology, and it reveals a religious world that would have been recognizable across the Phoenician coast: Baal as the dynamic young storm god who brings rain and fertility; **El** (meaning simply "god"), the distant creator and father of the gods; **Anat** (ah-NAHT), Baal's fierce warrior sister; **Mot** (meaning "death"), Baal's underworld enemy who kills and devours him each year; and **Yam** (meaning "sea"), the chaotic ocean that Baal must subdue. These gods and their stories formed the core of Phoenician religion. When the Phoenicians later spread across the Mediterranean, they carried these deities with them, adapting their names and cults to local contexts. The cult of **Astarte** (ah-STAR-tay) at Sidon (Chapter 4), the cult of **Melqart** (MEL-kart) at Tyre (Chapter 4), and the cult of **Tanit** (TAH-nit) at Carthage (Chapter 5) all grew from this same Canaanite religious root.

Ugarit will not survive into the next chapter. The city was destroyed around 1185 BCE and never rebuilt. One of the last tablets found in the ruins is a letter from the king of Ugarit, apparently written as the city was under attack, pleading with the king of Cyprus for help. The letter was found still in the kiln, unbaked. It was never sent. The enemy that destroyed Ugarit arrived as part of the catastrophe that swept away most of the Bronze Age world. But the Phoenician cities to the south, for reasons that are still debated, survived what Ugarit did not. The next chapter explains what happened.

# Chapter 2 — Children of the Collapse

Between approximately 1200 and 1150 BCE, nearly every major civilization in the eastern Mediterranean either collapsed outright or contracted dramatically. The **Bronze Age Collapse** (Mesopotamia Ch 9, New Kingdom Egypt Ch 8) remains one of the most devastating civilizational downturns in human history. The Hittite Empire (the great Anatolian power that had been one of Egypt's chief rivals; Hittite Empire Ch 6-7) was destroyed so completely that its very existence was forgotten for three thousand years. Mycenaean Greece (the Bronze Age civilization whose warrior-kings became the heroes of Homer's epics; Mycenaean Civilization Ch 6-7) fell into a dark age that lasted four centuries. Egypt survived but lost its Asian empire and never regained it. The city of Ugarit, the cultural cousin of the Phoenician cities described in Chapter 1, was sacked and burned and never reoccupied.

The causes are still debated, and the current scholarly consensus points not to a single cause but to a cascade of simultaneous stressors: prolonged drought, invasions by the mysterious **Sea Peoples** (coalitions of raiders and displaced populations who attacked coastlines from Anatolia to the Nile Delta), disruption of the tin trade routes that sustained bronze production, internal rebellions, and possibly earthquakes. The most influential modern framework treats it as "systems collapse," the failure of a tightly interconnected international economy overwhelmed by too many shocks at once. The Bronze Age world was surprisingly globalized: Egyptian gold paid for Mesopotamian textiles, Cypriot copper was alloyed with Afghan tin to make bronze tools sold from Greece to Syria, Phoenician cedar beams held up temples from Thebes to Nineveh. When the trade networks that connected these economies broke, everything broke at once.

The Phoenician cities survived. Byblos, Sidon, and Tyre all came through the collapse battered but standing. Why they survived when Ugarit, the Hittites, and Mycenaean Greece did not is one of the persistent puzzles of the period, and scholars have proposed several explanations. Geography may be part of the answer: the Phoenician cities were coastal with strong natural defenses (Tyre's island position made it virtually impossible to capture by land forces), and they were positioned far enough south to avoid the worst of the Sea Peoples' initial onslaught. Flexibility may also have mattered: the Phoenician city-states were small, commercially adaptable, and not dependent on the kind of rigid palace-bureaucracy system that collapsed elsewhere. They did not need an empire to function. They needed trade routes, and trade routes could be rebuilt.

---

The most important consequence of the Bronze Age Collapse for Phoenicia was the vacuum it created. Before the collapse, the eastern Mediterranean had been dominated by three great powers: Egypt, the Hittites, and Assyria (with Babylon and Mycenaean Greece as secondary players). These powers controlled the trade routes, dictated the terms of international commerce, and kept smaller states like the Phoenician cities in a subordinate, tributary position. After the collapse, two of these three powers were gone. The Hittites had vanished entirely. Egypt had retreated behind the Sinai into the Nile Valley and would never again project sustained power into the Levant. Assyria (Assyrian Empire Ch 3) had survived but was crippled, fighting for survival against Aramean migrations and confined to its heartland around the city of Assur. The international system that had governed the Bronze Age world simply ceased to exist.

Into that vacuum stepped the Phoenicians. With no great power to dominate them, no Hittite empire blocking the Anatolian coast, no Egyptian garrisons enforcing tribute payments, and no competing naval powers in the Aegean (the Mycenaean Greeks were gone), the Phoenician cities found themselves in a position they had never occupied before: the dominant maritime traders of the entire Mediterranean. They did not seize this position through military conquest. They seized it by building ships and sailing them to places nobody else was going.

The Phoenicians were already skilled sailors before the collapse. What changed after 1200 BCE was the scale and ambition of their maritime operations. Before the collapse, Phoenician trade had been mostly confined to the eastern Mediterranean: the Nile Delta, Cyprus, the Aegean, Anatolia. After the collapse, the Phoenicians pushed west into waters that no eastern Mediterranean power had regularly penetrated: the central Mediterranean (Sicily, Sardinia, North Africa), the western Mediterranean (southern Spain), and eventually beyond the Strait of Gibraltar into the Atlantic Ocean.

This expansion required ships that could handle open water. **Phoenician shipwrights** pioneered several innovations that made long-distance Mediterranean sailing practical. They developed the **keeled hull** (a structural timber running the length of the ship's bottom, providing stability in open-water swells that would capsize flat-bottomed vessels). They perfected **mortise-and-tenon joinery** (a technique of fitting planks together with precisely carved interlocking wooden pegs, creating hulls so tight that they needed minimal caulking). And they eventually introduced the **bireme** (a galley with two tiers of oars on each side, doubling rowing power for the same hull length, which meant more speed and more cargo capacity). These were not overnight inventions but accumulated improvements over centuries of trial, error, and the unforgiving feedback of the open sea. By the time the Phoenicians were regularly sailing the full length of the Mediterranean, roughly 2,500 miles (4,000 km) from Tyre to Cádiz, their ships were the most advanced vessels in the world.

---

The Phoenicians also built two types of ship that reflected their twin priorities of trade and defense. The **gaulos** (GOW-loss; a wide, round-hulled merchant vessel optimized for cargo capacity over speed) could carry enormous loads of grain, timber, metal, and manufactured goods across open water. The **long ship** (a narrow-hulled warship powered by rowers, the ancestor of the trireme) was faster and more maneuverable, designed to protect the merchant fleet and project power when necessary. The distinction between merchant ships and warships, so fundamental to all later naval history, was already clear in the Phoenician fleet by the early first millennium BCE.

**Tyre** emerged from the collapse as the dominant Phoenician city, eclipsing Byblos, which had been preeminent during the Egyptian-dominated Bronze Age. The reasons for Tyre's rise are partly geographic: the city's core was on a rocky island roughly half a mile (800 meters) offshore, connected to the mainland by a narrow strip of shallows. This made Tyre virtually impervious to the kind of land-based military assault that had destroyed Ugarit and other coastal cities. Any army that wanted to take Tyre had to first command the sea, which no post-collapse power could do. The island location also made Tyre a superb natural harbor, with protected anchorages on both the north and south sides of the island.

Tyre's rise was also driven by leadership. The early post-collapse kings of Tyre (whose names are preserved in garbled form by later Greek historians, most notably **Josephus**, a Jewish historian writing in the first century CE who claimed to be drawing on Tyrian royal archives) pursued an aggressive commercial strategy: establishing trading posts along the Mediterranean coast, securing access to raw materials (metals, timber, grain), and positioning Tyre as the central hub of a maritime trade network that eventually spanned the entire Mediterranean basin.

The Phoenician expansion was never a coordinated imperial project. There was no Phoenician Alexander, no grand strategy of conquest. Individual cities, individual merchants, and individual captains established trading posts where they saw commercial opportunity. **Utica** (YOU-tih-kah; in modern Tunisia, on the North African coast) was founded as early as 1101 BCE according to ancient tradition, though the archaeological evidence is ambiguous and some scholars place the date later. Utica served as a waystation on the route to the western Mediterranean, a place where ships could resupply and trade with local populations before continuing west. Other early outposts appeared on Cyprus (where the Phoenicians established a major presence at **Kition**, modern Larnaca, exploiting the island's rich copper deposits) and along the North African coast.

The most audacious early colony was **Gadir** (GAH-deer; modern Cádiz, in southwestern Spain), traditionally dated to around 1100 BCE, though again the archaeological evidence favors a somewhat later date, probably the ninth or eighth century BCE. Gadir was established beyond the **Pillars of Hercules** (the ancient name for the Strait of Gibraltar, the narrow passage between Spain and North Africa where the Mediterranean meets the Atlantic Ocean). Founding a colony on the Atlantic coast of Spain put the Phoenicians in contact with **Tartessos** (tar-TESS-oss), a wealthy native kingdom in the Guadalquivir River valley of southwestern Spain that sat atop some of the richest silver deposits in the ancient world. The trade that developed between Gadir and Tartessos would make Tyre spectacularly wealthy in the centuries that followed (Chapter 4).

---

Phoenician navigational skill in this period deserves emphasis. Ancient Mediterranean sailing was coastal: ships followed the shoreline, put into harbor each night, and rarely crossed open water. The Phoenicians pushed beyond this conservative approach. They developed techniques for open-water night sailing, navigating by the stars with a precision that no other Mediterranean sailors could match (Chapter 4). Night sailing doubled the effective range of a ship: instead of stopping at sunset and resuming at dawn, a Phoenician crew could sail through the night, covering distances that daytime-only sailors could not match. Combined with the Phoenicians' knowledge of prevailing winds, seasonal weather patterns, and the location of underwater hazards across the entire Mediterranean, this gave them a navigational edge that no competitor could match for centuries.

By roughly 1000 BCE, the Phoenician cities had transformed themselves from Egyptian vassals and regional traders into the dominant commercial power of the Mediterranean. They had achieved this without an army, without an empire, and without conquering a single city. Their weapon was the ship, their strategy was trade, and their timing was exquisite. The Bronze Age Collapse had cleared away every competitor, and the Phoenicians filled the vacuum before anyone else could.

To put this in cross-civilizational context: in 1000 BCE, the Zhou dynasty ruled China (Ancient China Ch 5-6) but had no maritime tradition to speak of. India's Vedic civilization (Vedic Period Ch 2-3) was centered on inland rivers, not ocean trade. Egypt, the great Bronze Age naval power, had retreated from the sea. Greece was still in its post-Mycenaean dark age, centuries away from the colonization wave that would spread Greek settlements across the Mediterranean. The Phoenicians had the sea to themselves, and they used the advantage relentlessly.

But the Phoenicians were about to give the world something more important than purple dye, cedar beams, or silver ingots. They were about to give it the alphabet. Every letter you are reading right now traces its ancestry back to a script invented by Phoenician traders in the early first millennium BCE. It was, by a considerable margin, the most consequential invention in the history of human communication.

# Chapter 3 — The Alphabet

Sometime around 1050 BCE, give or take a century, Phoenician scribes and merchants finalized the writing system that would become the ancestor of virtually every alphabet in the world. The **Phoenician alphabet** consisted of 22 letters, each representing a single consonant sound. No vowels. No syllable signs. No pictograms for whole words. Just 22 simple symbols that could be combined to represent any word in the Phoenician language, and, with minor adaptations, any word in any language on earth.

To grasp why this was revolutionary, you need to understand what writing looked like before the Phoenicians simplified it. In Mesopotamia, the **cuneiform** system (wedge-shaped signs pressed into clay, invented by the Sumerians around 3400 BCE; Mesopotamia Ch 3) used roughly 600 to 1,000 signs, depending on the period and the level of complexity. Some signs represented syllables (a consonant plus a vowel), some represented whole words, some served as "determinatives" (unpronounced markers that told the reader what category of thing was being described). Learning to read and write cuneiform required years of full-time study in a scribal school. Only a tiny professional class of scribes could do it. In Egypt, the **hieroglyphic** system was equally complex: roughly 700 signs in common use, combining sound-signs, word-signs, and determinatives. Again, literacy was confined to a professional scribal elite. In both systems, the barrier to literacy was the sheer number of signs a person had to memorize.

The Phoenician alphabet demolished that barrier. Twenty-two signs, each representing one consonant. A merchant could learn them in a week. A sailor could scratch a shipping manifest on a potsherd. A king could read his own correspondence without relying on a scribal intermediary. The alphabet did not just make writing easier. It democratized it. For the first time in history, literacy was no longer the exclusive property of a priestly or bureaucratic elite. Anyone with a week of instruction and a sharp stick could scratch a message that anyone else with the same week of instruction could read. The implications were staggering, and they played out over centuries: widespread record-keeping, accessible law codes, personal letters, commercial contracts, philosophical treatises, and eventually the great literary traditions of Greece, Rome, and every civilization that inherited an alphabetic script.

---

The Phoenician alphabet did not appear out of nowhere. It had ancestors. The earliest experiments with alphabetic writing (using a small number of signs to represent individual sounds rather than syllables or words) appear to have occurred in the turquoise mining region of the **Sinai Peninsula**, where Semitic-speaking workers employed by Egyptian mining operations scratched a script now called **Proto-Sinaitic** on rock faces sometime around 1800-1500 BCE. Proto-Sinaitic used around 30 signs derived from simplified Egyptian hieroglyphs, each sign representing a single consonant. The principle was acrophonic: the first sound of the Semitic word for the thing the picture showed became the sound value of the letter. So a simplified picture of a house (Semitic *bayt*) represented the sound "b." A simplified picture of water (*mayim*) represented the sound "m." A simplified picture of an ox head (*aleph*) represented the glottal stop at the beginning of the word. That ox head eventually became the Greek letter **alpha**, the Roman letter **A**, and the letter you see at the start of this sentence. The letter A is an upside-down ox head. It has been rotating slowly over the past three thousand years, but the horns are still there if you flip it over.

Proto-Sinaitic evolved into **Proto-Canaanite**, a script attested in scattered inscriptions across the Levant from roughly 1500-1050 BCE. The number of signs gradually stabilized, the letter forms became more standardized, and by around 1050 BCE the script had crystallized into what we call the Phoenician alphabet: 22 consonant letters, written from right to left, each letter a small abstract symbol descended from those original pictographic ancestors. The Phoenicians did not invent the idea of alphabetic writing (the Proto-Sinaitic miners did, at least as far as the surviving evidence tells us). What the Phoenicians did was standardize it, spread it, and make it universal. They carried the alphabet with them on every trade voyage, to every colony, to every port where a Phoenician merchant scratched a price on a pot or carved a dedication on a temple wall. The alphabet traveled on merchant ships.

Byblos remained an important center of Phoenician literacy and royal culture even as Tyre eclipsed it commercially. King **Abibaal** (ah-BEE-bah-al) of Byblos (the exact dates are uncertain, probably mid-to-late tenth century BCE) left an inscription on a statue of the Egyptian pharaoh **Shoshenq I** (ruled approximately 943-922 BCE), demonstrating that Byblos maintained its centuries-old diplomatic relationship with Egypt even in the post-collapse era. The inscription is significant because it is written in the Phoenician alphabet, confirming that the script was already in use for royal and diplomatic purposes by this period.

The oldest substantial inscription in the mature Phoenician alphabet is found on the **Ahiram Sarcophagus** (ah-HEE-ram), the stone coffin of a king of Byblos. The inscription's date is debated (some scholars place it as early as 1000 BCE, others as late as 850 BCE), but it is unquestionably one of the earliest examples of the fully developed Phoenician script. The inscription is a curse: it warns that any king or governor who disturbs the sarcophagus will have his throne overturned and his peace destroyed. The specific content is less important than the fact that the inscription exists at all, in a clear, readable alphabet of 22 consonant letters, carved by someone who expected it to be read by future kings and understood by future readers. The script was already standardized, already functional, already a tool of royal authority.

---

The alphabet had one obvious limitation: no vowels. The Phoenician script was an **abjad** (the technical term for a writing system that records only consonants), and a reader was expected to supply the correct vowels from context. This works tolerably well for Semitic languages, where word roots are built from consonant patterns and vowels are somewhat predictable. In English, if you wrote "bk," the reader might puzzle over whether you meant "book," "back," "bake," or "bike." In Phoenician (and in modern Hebrew and Arabic, which inherited this feature), the consonant skeleton of a word usually narrows the possibilities enough that an experienced reader can infer the correct vowels. But the system is imperfect, and it became a real obstacle when people who spoke non-Semitic languages tried to use the script.

The solution came from the Greeks. Sometime between 850 and 750 BCE, probably through contact with Phoenician traders at ports in the Aegean, on Cyprus, or on the North African coast, the Greeks adopted the Phoenician alphabet wholesale. They borrowed the letter shapes, the letter names, and (originally) the right-to-left writing direction. But the Greek language is Indo-European, not Semitic, and consonant-only writing doesn't work well for Greek. So the Greeks made a crucial modification: they took several Phoenician consonant letters that represented sounds not present in Greek (the glottal stop *aleph*, the pharyngeal sounds *he* and *ayin*, the semi-vowel *yod*, the aspirate *waw*) and repurposed them as vowel signs. *Aleph* became **alpha** (A), *he* became **epsilon** (E), *ayin* became **omicron** (O), *yod* became **iota** (I), and *waw* (with some modification) became **upsilon** (U). The result was the first full alphabet in the modern sense: a script that represents both consonants and vowels, allowing any word in any language to be unambiguously spelled.

The Greeks acknowledged the debt. They called their letters *phoinikeia grammata*, "Phoenician letters." The mythological hero **Cadmus** (KAD-muss), who according to Greek legend founded the city of Thebes and introduced the alphabet to Greece, was explicitly identified as a Phoenician prince from Sidon or Tyre. **Herodotus** (heh-ROD-uh-tuss; a Greek historian writing in the fifth century BCE, often called the "Father of History"), confirmed the tradition: the Greeks got their letters from the Phoenicians, full stop. Modern scholarship agrees. The letter names (alpha, beta, gamma, delta) are meaningless in Greek but are Semitic words: *aleph* means "ox," *beth* means "house," *gimel* traditionally means "camel" (though the early pictograph may represent a throwing stick), *daleth* means "door." The alphabet the Greeks adopted became the Greek alphabet. The Greek alphabet was adapted by the Etruscans in Italy. The Etruscan adaptation became the Latin alphabet. The Latin alphabet is what you are reading right now.

---

Through a separate line of transmission, the Phoenician alphabet also became the ancestor of **Aramaic script** (used across the ancient Near East and eventually by Jesus and his contemporaries), **Hebrew script** (used for the Torah and all subsequent Jewish religious literature), **Arabic script** (used for the Quran and throughout the Islamic world), the **Brahmi script** of India (from which virtually all South and Southeast Asian writing systems descend, though the connection is debated), and dozens of other writing systems. Every alphabet on earth, with the possible exception of the Korean **Hangul** (which was intentionally designed from scratch in the 15th century CE), traces its ancestry back to the Phoenician script.

No other Phoenician invention comes close. The alphabet is, by any measure, one of the two or three most consequential human inventions in history, alongside agriculture and (arguably) the printing press. It made mass literacy possible, even if mass literacy took millennia to achieve. It made literature, philosophy, science, law, and religion transmissible across languages and cultures. It made this sentence possible.

The Phoenicians themselves used the alphabet to write extensively. A semi-legendary author named **Sanchuniathon** (san-koo-nee-AH-thon; from Berytus, modern Beirut), reportedly writing around 1100 BCE (though the date is highly uncertain and some scholars consider him entirely mythical), composed histories and cosmogonies (creation stories) of the Phoenician world. His works survive only in fragments quoted by later Greek authors, particularly **Philo of Byblos** (a Greek-speaking writer of the first-second century CE who claimed to have translated Sanchuniathon's Phoenician originals into Greek). Whether Sanchuniathon was a real person or a literary invention, the fragments attributed to him contain Phoenician mythological and cosmological material that cannot be found elsewhere, including creation narratives that share structural parallels with both Mesopotamian and biblical accounts. The loss of Phoenician literature is one of the great tragedies of ancient history. The Phoenicians wrote on papyrus and parchment, which rot, rather than on clay tablets, which survive. An entire literary tradition, the written heritage of the people who invented the alphabet, has been almost completely erased by time. What survives are fragments quoted by Greeks and Romans, inscriptions carved on stone and metal, and the tantalizing awareness that we are missing most of the picture.

The Phoenicians who standardized and spread the alphabet were not idealistic educators. They were merchants who needed a fast, efficient way to keep business records. The alphabet was a commercial tool that became, accidentally, the foundation of all literate civilization. The next chapter tells the story of what those merchants were actually selling.

# Chapter 4 — Hiram's Golden Age

Around 980 BCE, a king named **Hiram I** (HY-ram; ruled approximately 980-947 BCE) came to the throne of Tyre and turned his city into the richest, most powerful Phoenician state the coast had ever produced. Hiram did not conquer an empire. He built a commercial network so profitable that it made Tyre indispensable to every major power in the region, and he did it during a window of opportunity that would not stay open long.

That window existed because, in the late eleventh and early tenth centuries BCE, none of the great powers were paying attention to the Levant. Egypt was weak, divided between competing dynasties in the north and south. Assyria was confined to its heartland, fighting off Aramean encroachments (Assyrian Empire Ch 3). Babylon was in decline. The Hittites were gone. Into this vacuum, two small but ambitious kingdoms in the Levant were building power: Hiram's Tyre on the coast, and, in the hill country just inland, the **Kingdom of Israel** under Kings David and Solomon. The partnership between Hiram and Solomon became one of the most famous alliances in the ancient world, celebrated in the Hebrew Bible and referenced by virtually every later writer who discussed Phoenicia.

According to the biblical account (and supported by independent historical plausibility, though the exact details are debated), Hiram supplied Solomon with cedar wood, skilled craftsmen, and luxury goods for the construction of the **First Temple** in Jerusalem, the central shrine of Israelite religion. Solomon, in turn, provided Hiram with agricultural products (wheat and olive oil from Israel's hill-country farms) and, more importantly, with land access to the trade routes running south to the **Red Sea** and the wealthy kingdoms of Arabia. The two kings may have jointly operated a fleet based at **Ezion-Geber** (EH-zee-on GEH-ber; a port on the Gulf of Aqaba, at the northern tip of the Red Sea), trading with the land of **Ophir** (location debated: possibly southern Arabia, East Africa, or western India) for gold, precious stones, and exotic goods. The profits from this Red Sea trade made both kings wealthy, and the Temple that Hiram's craftsmen built for Solomon became the defining symbol of Israelite (and later Jewish) religion, destroyed and rebuilt and destroyed again over the following millennium. Hiram's construction crews did not know they were building one of the most symbolically important structures in human history. They were filling a contract.

---

Hiram also built at home. His most significant domestic project was the great **Temple of Melqart** (MEL-kart) at Tyre. Melqart (the name probably means "king of the city") was the patron god of Tyre, a deity associated with the sea, with trade, with colonial expansion, and with an annual death-and-resurrection cycle that echoed the broader Canaanite pattern of dying-and-rising gods (Chapter 1). The Temple of Melqart at Tyre became one of the most important religious centers in the ancient Mediterranean. Greek visitors later identified Melqart with their own hero **Heracles** (Hercules), and the temple attracted pilgrims from across the Mediterranean world for centuries. When the Phoenicians founded colonies, they founded temples to Melqart. The cult of Melqart was, in a sense, the religious glue that held the Phoenician colonial world together, connecting distant outposts like Cádiz and Carthage back to their mother city of Tyre.

The god Melqart's annual ritual was vivid and public. Each year, the god was ritually "killed" (how exactly is debated: some scholars interpret the ritual as an actual burning of an effigy or statue, others as a symbolic death enacted through priestly performance) and then "awakened" or "resurrected" in a spring ceremony called the **egersis** (eh-GAIR-sis). The cycle mirrored the agricultural seasons: the god died with the vegetation in late summer and returned with the rains in spring. Similar dying-and-rising god myths existed across the ancient Near East: the Mesopotamian **Tammuz** (Dumuzi), the Egyptian **Osiris**, and the Byblian **Adonis** (the Greek version of the Phoenician word *adon*, meaning "lord"), whose cult centered at Byblos involved annual mourning rituals during which, according to Greek writers, the river near the city ran red (probably from seasonal iron-rich soil runoff, interpreted as the god's blood). These cults were not carbon copies of each other, but they drew on a shared cultural logic: the frightening annual death of the natural world, and the reassuring promise that it would return.

**Astarte** (ah-STAR-tay), goddess of love, war, and fertility, was the chief deity of Sidon and one of the most widely worshipped Phoenician goddesses across the Mediterranean. She was Sidon's equivalent of Tyre's Melqart: the divine patron, the city's protector, the focus of the most elaborate temple rituals. Astarte was closely related to the Mesopotamian **Ishtar** and the Sumerian **Inanna** (the same fundamental goddess filtered through different Semitic languages and cultural contexts), and Greek visitors identified her with **Aphrodite**. When Phoenician colonists spread across the Mediterranean, they carried the worship of Astarte with them, establishing temples at Kition on Cyprus, at Carthage, at colonies in Sicily, Sardinia, and Spain. Greek and Roman writers described elaborate temple rituals involving sacred prostitution, though modern scholars are deeply divided on whether these accounts reflect actual Phoenician practice or Greek misunderstanding (or deliberate sensationalizing) of foreign religious customs involving female cult personnel.

---

The Phoenician economy in Hiram's era rested on several pillars, and the most famous was **Tyrian purple**. The dye that gave the Phoenicians their Greek name was extracted from **murex** sea snails (specifically *Bolinus brandaris* and *Hexaplex trunculus*), small shellfish found in rocky coastal waters throughout the Mediterranean. The extraction process was laborious, smelly, and enormously profitable. Workers cracked open the shells, removed a tiny mucus-producing gland (the hypobranchial gland, located behind the snail's head), and collected the secretion in stone vats. Fresh, the secretion was yellowish-green and unremarkable. But when exposed to sunlight and air, it underwent a chemical reaction (a photochemical transformation of the molecule 6,6'-dibromoindigo, for the chemically curious) that turned it through stages of green, blue, and finally a deep reddish-purple that was colorfast, meaning it did not fade with washing. The color actually deepened over time, growing more vivid with exposure to light rather than less.

The problem was volume. Each snail yielded a single tiny drop of the precursor fluid. Producing one gram of finished dye required something on the order of 10,000 snails (the exact figure varies in modern reconstructions, but the order of magnitude is consistently staggering). The archaeological evidence for this scale is not subtle: massive deposits of crushed murex shells, some tens of feet deep, have been found along the coast at Sidon and at other Phoenician sites. The smell of rotting snail flesh during the extraction process was apparently atrocious. Ancient writers consistently describe the stench as one of the worst things about Phoenician coastal cities.

The resulting dye was so expensive that it became the ultimate status symbol. Purple cloth was reserved, in practice and eventually by law, for royalty and the highest elites. The Roman Senate would later restrict the wearing of purple to the emperor alone (the phrase "born to the purple" comes from this tradition). The connection between purple and royalty is Phoenician in origin, and it persists today in the symbolic associations of the color. A pound of purple-dyed wool cost roughly as much as a pound of silver. Tyre's virtual monopoly on the highest-quality purple dye made the city fabulously wealthy, and the dye industry remained a Tyrian specialty for centuries. The Byzantine Empire would eventually inherit and continue the Phoenician tradition, making Tyrian purple the exclusive mark of imperial dignity for nine hundred years — the expression "born in the purple" (porphyrogennetos) describing a prince born to a reigning emperor literally referred to the purple-draped imperial birthing room in Constantinople (Byzantine Empire Ch 2 and Ch 6).

But Tyre's economy was diversified well beyond purple. **Sidonian glassmaking** (centered at Sidon but practiced across the Phoenician coast) produced some of the ancient world's finest glass vessels. Phoenician glassworkers pioneered techniques for producing transparent and colored glass, and their products were exported throughout the Mediterranean. Glassblowing (the technique of inflating molten glass on the end of a hollow pipe) would not be invented until much later, around the first century BCE, probably in the Sidon-Tyre region. The earlier Phoenician glass was core-formed: molten glass was wound around a clay core, shaped, decorated with colored glass threads, and then the core was scraped out after cooling. The results were small, intricate, and expensive.

**Phoenician ivory carving** became another prestige export. Phoenician artisans carved elephant ivory (sourced from both African and Syrian elephants, the latter a now-extinct subspecies that once ranged across the northern Levant) into intricate plaques, furniture inlays, boxes, and decorative panels. The style blended Egyptian motifs (lotus flowers, sphinxes, winged sun-disks) with Mesopotamian and local Levantine elements, producing a distinctive hybrid aesthetic that was prized across the ancient Near East. Thousands of Phoenician ivories have been excavated from the Assyrian palaces at Nimrud (Assyrian Empire Ch 4), where they were stockpiled as tribute and war booty. These ivory carvings are some of the finest small-scale artwork to survive from the ancient world.

**Phoenician metalwork** in bronze, gold, and silver was equally sought after. Phoenician smiths produced decorated bronze bowls, incense stands, and ritual vessels that combined Egyptian and Mesopotamian artistic traditions and were exported across the Mediterranean as luxury goods and diplomatic gifts. The Phoenicians were not just traders. They were manufacturers, and their workshops produced a distinctive, high-quality, cosmopolitan style that was recognizable from Spain to Mesopotamia.

This cosmopolitan quality was itself distinctive. Phoenician art blended motifs from every civilization the Phoenicians traded with, creating a hybrid aesthetic that modern scholars sometimes call "international style." An ivory plaque carved in Tyre might combine an Egyptian sphinx, a Mesopotamian sacred tree, and a local Levantine geometric border. A bronze bowl from Sidon might depict an Egyptian pharaoh in a composition framed by Assyrian-style winged genii. The Phoenicians did not simply copy foreign art. They synthesized it, producing objects that belonged to no single tradition and were salable everywhere. This eclecticism was not a lack of originality. It was a commercial strategy: a Phoenician luxury object appealed to customers in Egypt, Assyria, Greece, and the western Mediterranean precisely because it contained visual references that each audience could recognize.

---

After Hiram's death around 947 BCE, Tyre passed through a period of internal instability. Several kings were assassinated or overthrown. Then, around 887 BCE, a priest of Astarte named **Ithobaal I** (ih-THOH-bah-al; sometimes spelled Ethbaal) seized the throne and united the cities of Tyre and Sidon under a single rule for the first time. Ithobaal expanded Phoenician territory, founded new settlements, and pursued an aggressive foreign policy that included marriage alliances with neighboring kingdoms.

The most famous of these alliances was the marriage of Ithobaal's daughter **Jezebel** (JEZ-uh-bel) to **Ahab** (AY-hab), king of the northern **Kingdom of Israel** (the Israelite state had split into two kingdoms, Israel in the north and Judah in the south, after Solomon's death around 930 BCE). Jezebel is one of the most vilified figures in the Hebrew Bible, portrayed as a foreign queen who introduced the worship of Baal into Israel, persecuted the prophets of Yahweh, and ultimately died a gruesome death (thrown from a window, trampled by horses, eaten by dogs, according to the Book of Kings). The biblical authors despised her because she represented the infiltration of Phoenician religion into Israelite society, a threat to the exclusive worship of Yahweh that the biblical writers championed.

From a Phoenician perspective, the marriage was simply standard diplomatic practice. Marriages between royal families cemented alliances, and the alliance between Tyre-Sidon and Israel served both kingdoms' interests: Israel got access to Phoenician trade networks and Mediterranean commerce; Tyre got access to Israel's overland trade routes and agricultural production. Jezebel's promotion of Baal worship in Israel was, from her perspective, the normal expression of royal piety: a queen worshipped her own gods and patronized their cults, as queens had always done across the ancient Near East. That this perfectly conventional behavior made her one of the great villains of biblical literature tells us more about the biblical writers' theological program than about Jezebel herself.

The Phoenicians also innovated in navigation during this period. **Phoenician sailors** navigated by the stars, specifically by **Ursa Minor** (the Little Bear, or the Little Dipper), rather than by **Ursa Major** (the Great Bear, or the Big Dipper) used by Greek sailors. Ursa Minor contains **Polaris** (well, a star very close to where Polaris sits today; the celestial pole shifts slowly over millennia due to the precession of Earth's axis), the star closest to true north, making it a more accurate navigational reference than the larger but less precisely positioned Great Bear. The technique was so identified with the Phoenicians that the Greeks called Ursa Minor "the Phoenician" constellation. This gave Phoenician navigators an edge in open-water sailing: they could hold a more accurate course during night passages, when coastlines were invisible and the stars were the only reference points.

But the golden age was ending. In 842 BCE, an Assyrian king named **Shalmaneser III** (Assyrian Empire Ch 4) forced Tyre and Sidon to pay heavy tribute, beginning a long era of Phoenician subjugation to Mesopotamian empires. The age of Phoenician independence was over. Chapter 6 will trace that subjugation in detail. But before the empires closed in, the Phoenicians had already planted the seeds of their most enduring achievement: a network of colonies that would span the entire Mediterranean and outlast Phoenicia itself. That story is next.

# Chapter 5 — The Great Colonizers

How do you build a civilization across 3,000 miles of sea without an army, a government, or a plan? The Phoenicians answered that question between roughly 1100 and 600 BCE, establishing permanent communities from Cyprus to the Atlantic coast of Spain and Morocco. No king ordered the expansion. No general led it. Individual merchants, ship captains, and refugee families sailed west into waters no eastern Mediterranean power had charted, picked spots where they could trade, and stayed. The result was a network of colonies that stretched farther than the Assyrian Empire at its peak, held together not by garrisons but by kinship, shared gods, and the profit motive. Understanding how they actually did it, what it looked like when a Phoenician crew beached on a foreign shore, requires looking past the list of colony names that ancient writers left us and reconstructing the process itself.

The first step was always the harbor. Phoenician captains chose sites the way their ancestors had chosen Tyre and Sidon: a rocky headland or offshore island with protected anchorage, defensible on the landward side, close to fresh water. The pattern repeats so consistently across the Mediterranean that archaeologists can practically predict where Phoenician remains will turn up. Next came the temple. Before permanent buildings, before city walls, the colonists consecrated a sacred precinct to **Melqart** (the patron god of Tyre, Chapter 4) or **Astarte** (the goddess of Sidon, Chapter 4), establishing a ritual link to the mother city. The temple was not just religious. It was the colony's bank (temple precincts stored communal wealth), its court (oaths were sworn before the god), and its signal to passing ships that this was a Phoenician port where a trader could do business in a familiar language under familiar rules. Then came the negotiation. The Phoenicians were settling coastlines that already had inhabitants, and the archaeological record shows coexistence, not conquest: Phoenician pottery mixed with local wares, intermarriage evidenced by hybrid burial practices, indigenous goods flowing into Phoenician trade networks. The colonists offered metalwork, purple-dyed cloth, wine, and manufactured luxuries. The locals offered food, raw materials, and permission to stay. It was commerce before it was colonization.

The earliest colonies were stepping stones. **Kition** (KIH-tee-on; modern Larnaca, on the southeastern coast of Cyprus) became the chief Phoenician settlement on the island, exploiting Cyprus's rich copper deposits and serving as a midway point between the Levant and the Aegean. From Cyprus, the route ran west along the North African coast, then across to **Sicily**, **Sardinia**, and eventually Spain. Each colony was positioned roughly a day's sail from the next, creating a chain of safe harbors that a Phoenician merchant fleet could follow from one end of the Mediterranean to the other without ever being more than 24 hours from shore.

Along the North African coast, the Phoenicians established **Utica** (YOU-tih-kah; traditionally dated to around 1101 BCE, though the archaeological evidence for this early date is thin; excavated remains confirm a substantial settlement by the eighth century BCE at the latest). Utica sat on the Tunisian coast near the mouth of the Medjerda River, controlling a fertile agricultural hinterland and providing an anchorage for ships traveling the coast route toward the western Mediterranean. It would later be overshadowed by its more famous neighbor, Carthage, but Utica's early founding (if the traditional date is correct) makes it one of the oldest Phoenician settlements outside the Levant.

---

The colony that matters most is **Carthage** (KAR-thij; from the Phoenician *Qart Hadasht*, meaning "New City"). According to ancient tradition, Carthage was founded in 814 BCE by **Dido** (DY-doh), also known as **Elissa** (eh-LISS-ah), a Tyrian princess who fled her homeland after a dynastic crisis. The story, as told by Greek and Roman writers (most famously Virgil in the *Aeneid*, though he was writing literature, not history), goes like this: Dido's brother **Pygmalion** (pig-MAY-lee-on), king of Tyre, murdered Dido's husband **Acerbas** (ah-SER-bass; also called Sychaeus in Latin sources), who was the high priest of Melqart and reportedly enormously wealthy. Pygmalion wanted Acerbas's treasure. Dido, warned in a dream that her brother would come for her next, secretly loaded ships with the treasure and fled with a group of loyal followers. They sailed west along the North African coast and eventually landed in what is now Tunisia, where Dido negotiated with the local **Berber** king (the Berbers were the indigenous peoples of North Africa, spread from the Atlantic coast to western Egypt, with their own languages and tribal structures) for land. The king agreed to sell her as much land as could be enclosed by a single oxhide. Dido cut the hide into thin strips, laid them end to end, and enclosed enough ground to build a citadel. The story is almost certainly legendary (the oxhide trick appears in foundation myths from several Mediterranean cultures), but the founding of Carthage by Tyrian colonists around 814 BCE is broadly accepted by modern historians. And the legend preserves something true about the Phoenician method: negotiation, not invasion. Even in myth, Dido's first act is a real estate deal.

The location was superb. Carthage sat on a peninsula jutting into the Gulf of Tunis, with natural harbors on both sides and a defensible hill (the **Byrsa**, meaning "citadel" in Phoenician, or possibly a Greek pun on *byrsa*, meaning "hide," connecting back to the Dido legend). The city commanded the narrow strait between Tunisia and Sicily, the chokepoint between the eastern and western Mediterranean. Any ship traveling between the two halves of the Mediterranean world passed within sight of Carthage. The city controlled the flow of trade the way Tyre had controlled the Levantine coast.

Carthage grew rapidly. Within two centuries of its founding, it had evolved from a colonial trading post into an independent city-state that rivaled and eventually surpassed its mother city, Tyre. The reasons were partly circumstantial: as the Phoenician homeland fell under Assyrian and then Babylonian domination (Chapter 6), many Tyrian elites and merchants emigrated to Carthage, bringing capital, skills, and commercial networks with them. Carthage was free of the imperial pressures crushing the homeland, and it used that freedom to build its own commercial empire across the western Mediterranean. The pattern of a northern maritime trading hub that grew into an independent commercial empire would be replicated a thousand years later on the other side of Europe: Dublin, founded as a Viking trading post and slave market in the 9th century CE, grew into a similarly dominant hub of the northern Atlantic economy (Viking Age Ch 7).

---

The central Mediterranean islands followed the same pattern. On **Sardinia**, Phoenician settlements clustered along the southern and western coasts, drawn by the island's copper, lead, and silver. The **Nora Stone**, found at one of these colonies, is the oldest known Phoenician inscription outside the Levant (probably ninth or eighth century BCE) and provides the earliest written reference to Sardinia in any language. On **Sicily**, the Phoenicians took the western end of the island, establishing trading posts at **Motya** (MOH-tee-ah), **Panormus** (pah-NOR-muss; modern Palermo), and other sites, while Greek colonists arriving at roughly the same time claimed the east. This east-west division of Sicily would define the island's politics for centuries and eventually spark the wars that drew Carthage into its fatal confrontation with Rome (Chapter 7).

The western colonies also served as gateways to resources that the eastern Mediterranean desperately needed. Beyond Gadir, on the Atlantic coast of Spain, Phoenician merchants traded with **Tartessos**, tapping the Guadalquivir valley's enormous silver deposits. Industrial-scale silver extraction at sites like Rio Tinto (which has been mined almost continuously from Phoenician times to the present day) produced millions of tons of slag and made the Phoenicians the ancient world's silver middlemen. The profits were astronomical. The silver trade explains much of Tyre's wealth in the tenth through eighth centuries BCE and funded the construction of temples, harbors, and fleets that sustained the Phoenician maritime network.

Even further afield, Phoenician merchants reportedly sailed to the **Cassiterides** (kass-ih-TAIR-ih-deez; the "Tin Islands"), a destination that ancient sources located in the far northwest but whose exact identity is debated (probably Cornwall in southwestern Britain, or possibly Brittany in northwestern France, or the Scilly Islands off the Cornish coast). Tin, essential for making bronze, was scarce in the Mediterranean world, and the Phoenicians' willingness to sail into the cold, rough Atlantic to obtain it illustrates both the scale of their ambition and the commercial logic that drove it. Control of the tin supply had been a source of power since the Bronze Age (Chapter 2), and even in the Iron Age, bronze remained important for tools, artwork, and ceremonial objects.

---

The most dramatic Phoenician voyages were exploratory. Around 600 BCE, according to **Herodotus** (writing roughly 150 years later), Phoenician sailors commissioned by **Pharaoh Necho II** (NEH-koh) of Egypt sailed south from the Red Sea, followed the East African coast, rounded the southern tip of Africa, turned north along the West African coast, passed through the Strait of Gibraltar, and returned to Egypt from the west. If the account is accurate, this was the first **circumnavigation of Africa**, a feat not repeated until the Portuguese navigator Vasco da Gama rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1498 CE, over two thousand years later.

Herodotus himself did not believe the story. He reported that the sailors claimed the sun was on their right side (that is, to the north) as they sailed west around the southern tip of Africa. Herodotus found this incredible. Modern readers recognize that it is exactly what you would observe in the Southern Hemisphere: south of the equator, the sun appears to the north. The detail that Herodotus disbelieved is actually the strongest evidence that the voyage happened, because no one in the ancient Mediterranean world would have known to fabricate it. Scholars remain divided, but the majority view leans cautiously toward accepting that the voyage occurred.

Around 500 BCE, a Carthaginian admiral named **Hanno** (HAH-noh) led a large expedition down the West African coast. His account, known as the **Periplus of Hanno** (a *periplus* is a Greek word for a written guide describing a sea voyage), survives in a Greek translation and describes a journey that may have reached as far south as modern Cameroon or Gabon. Hanno describes founding colonies along the Moroccan coast, encountering a volcano (possibly Mount Cameroon, one of the few active volcanoes in West Africa), and observing a group of hairy, aggressive creatures that his interpreters called "Gorillae." These were probably chimpanzees or gorillas, making Hanno's account the earliest known European description of great apes. (When western scientists formally described the gorilla in 1847, they named it after Hanno's creatures.)

At its peak, the Phoenician colonial network stretched over 3,000 miles (4,800 km) from the Levant to the Atlantic. That is roughly the distance from New York to London. And it was held together without a single garrison, a single governor, or a single written law of imperial administration. Each colony was essentially autonomous, connected to the others by trade, by shared language and religion, by the cult of Melqart, and by the informal networks of merchant families who operated across multiple cities. Carthage eventually emerged as the dominant power in the western Mediterranean, but even Carthage ruled more through commercial hegemony than through territorial administration.

The comparison with the Greeks is instructive. The Greeks, who began their own colonization wave roughly a century after the Phoenicians (from roughly 750 BCE onward), founded colonies that were politically independent city-states from the moment of foundation: a Greek colony owed sentimental loyalty to its "mother city" but no political obedience. The Phoenician system was similar in practice, though the relationship between colony and metropolis may have been somewhat closer in the early centuries, with Carthage sending annual tribute to the Temple of Melqart at Tyre long after becoming independent. Neither system resembled the modern European model of colonial empires administered from a distant capital. These were networks, not hierarchies, held together by kinship, religion, and commerce rather than by governors and garrisons.

So how do you build a civilization across 3,000 miles of sea without an army? The answer turns out to be: one temple, one harbor, and one trade deal at a time. The Phoenician colonial model was fragile in ways that an empire is not. It could not resist a determined military power, and it had no mechanism for collective defense. But it was resilient in ways that empires are not. When Tyre fell under Assyrian domination (Chapter 6), the western colonies kept trading. When Carthage was eventually destroyed (Chapter 8), Phoenician-founded cities like Cádiz and Palermo kept existing. The network survived the loss of any single node because no single node controlled it. That structural logic, trade not tribute, connection not conquest, was the Phoenicians' most original contribution to the history of how civilizations expand. It was also about to be tested, because the homeland was losing the independence that had made the whole expansion possible. The empires were closing in.

# Chapter 6 — Under the Empires

In 842 BCE, an Assyrian army under **Shalmaneser III** (Assyrian Empire Ch 4) reached the Phoenician coast and extracted heavy tribute from Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos. The surviving reliefs on the **Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser** (a four-sided pillar found at the Assyrian capital of Nimrud, now in the British Museum) show Phoenician kings alongside other Levantine rulers prostrating themselves before the Assyrian king and presenting gifts: silver, gold, textiles, ivory, and exotic animals. This was not a conquest in the usual sense. The Assyrians did not garrison the Phoenician cities or replace their kings. They imposed tribute: an annual payment of wealth in exchange for not destroying the cities. The Phoenician kings paid because the alternative was Ashurnasirpal's treatment (Assyrian Empire Ch 4), and everyone in the Levant knew what that looked like.

For the next two centuries, the Phoenician cities existed in a state of subordinate autonomy under Assyrian domination. They kept their own kings, maintained their own trade networks, and continued to operate their colonial empire in the western Mediterranean. But they paid, and paid heavily. Assyrian records from the eighth century BCE list tribute from Tyre and Sidon alongside dozens of other Levantine vassal states, and the amounts were substantial: tens of talents of gold and silver, hundreds of bolts of purple-dyed cloth, ivory, ebony, and exotic animals. The Phoenicians were too commercially valuable to destroy and too militarily weak to resist. They adapted, as they had always adapted, to the power that controlled the hinterland.

Some Phoenician kings tested the limits. **Hiram II** (not the famous Hiram I of Chapter 4 — the name recycled across Tyrian dynasties) paid tribute to Assyria's **Tiglath-Pileser III** (Assyrian Empire Ch 5) in the 730s BCE, then joined an anti-Assyrian coalition with Damascus. The gamble failed. Tyre lost control of Sidon, and Hiram was overthrown. The episode demonstrated what every Phoenician king already knew: resistance to Assyria required allies, and allies in the Levant had a habit of losing.

A generation later, **Luli** (LOO-lee), king of Tyre, apparently refused to submit when the Assyrian king **Sennacherib** (sen-NAK-er-ib; Assyrian Empire Ch 6) invaded the Levant in 701 BCE. Luli fled to Cyprus (where he reportedly died), and Sennacherib besieged Tyre's island fortress. The siege reportedly lasted five years (though the evidence is thin and some scholars dispute the duration). Tyre did not fall. The island location that had made Tyre defensible since the Bronze Age still worked: without a fleet capable of blockading the harbor, Sennacherib could not starve the city into submission. He eventually settled for installing a puppet king on the mainland territories and leaving the island defiant. It was a pattern that would repeat. Tyre's island was, for all practical purposes, invulnerable to any army that could not control the sea.

---

The Assyrians squeezed the Phoenician cities for two centuries, but they never destroyed them. The system worked because both sides benefited, even if the benefit was grossly asymmetric. The Phoenicians paid tribute and kept their cities, their commerce, and their colonies. The Assyrians received enormous wealth without having to maintain an expensive garrison force in difficult coastal terrain. When the Neo-Assyrian Empire collapsed spectacularly in the late seventh century BCE (Assyrian Empire Ch 8), the Phoenician cities briefly regained a measure of independence, only to face a new master.

**Nebuchadnezzar II** (neb-uh-kad-NEZ-er) of Babylon, the king who destroyed Jerusalem and its Temple in 586 BCE, turned his attention to the Phoenician coast immediately after. He besieged Tyre beginning in 586 BCE, and according to Josephus (citing Phoenician records through earlier historians), this siege lasted an extraordinary **thirteen years**, finally ending around 573 BCE. Thirteen years. To put that in perspective, Alexander the Great's famous siege of Tyre (Chapter 8) lasted seven months and is considered one of the most remarkable military operations in ancient history. Nebuchadnezzar ground away at the island fortress for thirteen years and still did not capture it in the traditional sense. Tyre eventually came to terms, accepting Babylonian suzerainty and probably a Babylonian-approved king, but the city was not sacked, not looted, and not destroyed. The island had held.

The biblical prophet **Ezekiel**, writing during the Babylonian exile (roughly contemporary with Nebuchadnezzar's siege), composed an extraordinary lament for Tyre that, incidentally, provides one of the most detailed descriptions of Phoenician trade in any ancient source. Ezekiel 27 lists Tyre's trading partners and their goods in a kind of commercial gazetteer: silver, iron, and tin from Tarshish (probably Tartessos in Spain); slaves and bronze vessels from Javan (Greece); horses from Togarmah (Anatolia); ivory and ebony from the coastlands; turquoise, purple cloth, embroidery, fine linen, coral, and rubies from Aram (Syria); wheat, honey, oil, and balm from Judah and Israel; wine from Damascus; wrought iron, cassia, and calamus from Dan and Javan; saddlecloths from Dedan (Arabia); lambs, rams, and goats from Arabia and Kedar. The passage reads like a shipping manifest, and it probably reflects genuine commercial intelligence available to anyone living in the Levantine world. Ezekiel was not praising Tyre. He was prophesying its destruction. But in listing what would be lost, he inadvertently preserved the most complete portrait of the Phoenician trading world that survives in any single ancient text.

---

In 539 BCE, **Cyrus the Great** of Persia conquered Babylon (Persian Empire Ch 2) and absorbed the entire Babylonian Empire, including the Phoenician cities, into the new **Achaemenid Persian Empire** (ah-KEE-meh-nid). For the Phoenicians, the transition was surprisingly painless, and the Persian period may have been the most comfortable era of foreign domination they experienced. The Persians, like the Assyrians and Babylonians before them, valued the Phoenicians primarily for their commercial and naval capabilities. But the Persian approach was less extractive and more cooperative than the Assyrian model. The Achaemenid system of **satrapies** (provincial administration; Persian Empire Ch 2-3) gave the Phoenician cities a degree of self-governance within the imperial structure. Phoenician kings retained their thrones, their commerce continued, and, most importantly, the Persians relied on Phoenician fleets as the backbone of their naval power.

This last point was critical. Persia was a continental land empire with no naval tradition of its own. Its core territories (the Iranian plateau, Mesopotamia, Central Asia) were landlocked. To project power across the Aegean against the Greeks, the Persians needed ships, and the best ships in their empire belonged to the Phoenicians. When Xerxes (ZERK-zeez) assembled the enormous fleet for his invasion of Greece in 480 BCE (Persian Empire Ch 4), Phoenician ships from Sidon, Tyre, and other coastal cities formed the elite core of the Persian navy. At the **Battle of Salamis** (SAL-ah-miss; fought in a narrow strait near Athens in September 480 BCE), the Persian fleet of roughly 600-800 ships faced a smaller Greek fleet of about 370 triremes. The Phoenician contingent was considered the best in the Persian fleet, and Herodotus reports that after the Persian defeat, Xerxes punished his Phoenician captains for their failure while praising others, suggesting the Phoenicians had been expected to carry the battle.

Salamis was a disaster for the Persian invasion force. The narrow strait negated the Persian numerical advantage, and the Greek triremes (light, fast warships with bronze rams designed to punch holes in enemy hulls) outmaneuvered the heavier Phoenician and allied ships in the confined waters. The Persian fleet was shattered, Xerxes retreated to Asia, and the Greek victory at Salamis became one of the defining moments in Western history. For the Phoenicians, the battle was a military humiliation fought in someone else's war. They had no quarrel with the Greeks. They were serving their Persian overlords, and they paid the price for the Persians' strategic miscalculation.

---

**Eshmunazar II** (esh-moo-NAH-zar) of Sidon, who ruled in the early fifth century BCE (perhaps around 490-475 BCE, though the exact dates are uncertain), left behind one of the most important Phoenician texts ever discovered. His sarcophagus, carved from Egyptian black basalt and clearly modeled on Egyptian coffin designs, bears a lengthy inscription in Phoenician that warns against disturbing the tomb: "Whoever you are, any ruler or any man, do not open this resting place and do not search for anything in it, for nothing has been placed in it. Do not remove the coffin from my resting place and do not carry me away from this resting place to another resting place." The inscription also describes building projects that Eshmunazar and his mother, the queen regent **Amoashtart** (ah-moh-ASH-tart), undertook at Sidon, including temples to Astarte and **Eshmun** (esh-MOON), the Phoenician god of healing.

The **Temple of Eshmun** at Sidon, built during this period, was a grand healing sanctuary with monumental terraces, pools, and ritual baths. Eshmun was identified by the Greeks with their own healing god **Asclepius** (as-KLEE-pee-us), and the sanctuary attracted worshippers seeking cures for illness. The temple complex, partially excavated in the 20th century, includes some of the best-preserved Phoenician monumental architecture outside Carthage. Its multi-terraced design, cut into a hillside near the Awali River, blended local Phoenician architectural traditions with Persian and Greek influences, a characteristic Phoenician willingness to absorb and adapt foreign styles.

The Persian period ended in a spasm of violence. In 351 BCE, **Tennes** (TEN-eez), king of Sidon, led a revolt against Persian rule with Egyptian backing. The revolt was part of a broader wave of rebellions across the western Persian Empire. **Artaxerxes III** (ar-tah-ZERK-zeez), the Persian king, responded with overwhelming force. He marched a large army to the Levant, and Tennes, seeing the situation was hopeless, betrayed his own city. He offered to hand Sidon over to the Persians in exchange for his personal safety. Artaxerxes took the city. Sidon was burned to the ground. According to some ancient sources (which may exaggerate), 40,000 Sidonians died, many by their own hands, setting fire to their homes rather than submitting to Persian vengeance. Artaxerxes then executed Tennes anyway. The burning of Sidon in 351 BCE was one of the worst catastrophes in Phoenician history, and it left the Phoenician cities weakened and demoralized on the eve of an even greater upheaval.

The Phoenician experience under empires reveals something fundamental about the nature of Phoenician civilization. They were not a military culture. They did not resist foreign domination through warfare (Tyre's island sieges being the exception, and even those were defensive). They resisted through indispensability. Every empire that conquered the Phoenician coast discovered the same thing: these cities were more valuable intact than destroyed. Their commerce, their shipbuilding, their navigational skill, their manufacturing expertise, and their colonial networks were resources that no conqueror could replicate by simply garrisoning the cities. Destroy the Phoenician economy and you destroyed the revenue stream that made the tribute worth collecting. The Phoenicians survived under the Assyrians, survived under the Babylonians, survived under the Persians, not because they were strong but because they were useful. It was a survival strategy that worked for half a millennium. It would take Alexander the Great, who wanted submission rather than tribute, to finally break it.

Twenty years later, a young Macedonian king named Alexander would arrive at the gates of Tyre with a plan that no previous conqueror had attempted. But before we reach that ending, we need to follow the Phoenician story westward, to the city of Carthage, which had spent the intervening centuries transforming itself from a Tyrian colony into a Mediterranean superpower.

---

# Chapter 7 — The Carthaginian Century

By the fifth century BCE, Carthage had become something its Tyrian founders never imagined: a major military power. The transformation was driven by geography and necessity. Carthage controlled the central Mediterranean chokepoint between Tunisia and Sicily, a position that was commercially valuable but militarily exposed. Greek colonists had been pouring into Sicily and southern Italy since the eighth century BCE, establishing powerful city-states like Syracuse (the largest Greek city in the western Mediterranean) that competed directly with Carthaginian commercial interests. The western Mediterranean was big enough for both Phoenicians and Greeks when trade was the only game. Once territorial control of shipping lanes, harbors, and agricultural hinterlands became the stakes, war was inevitable.

The man who turned Carthage into a military power was **Mago I** (MAH-goh; ruled approximately 550-530 BCE), a general who seized power and founded the **Magonid dynasty** that would dominate Carthaginian politics for over a century. Mago professionalized the Carthaginian army (which had previously relied on citizen militias supplemented by mercenaries), forged an alliance with the Etruscans (a powerful non-Greek civilization in central Italy), and used the alliance to project Carthaginian power across the western Mediterranean. The critical test came at the **Battle of Alalia** (ah-LAH-lee-ah) around 535 BCE, fought off the coast of Corsica. A combined Carthaginian-Etruscan fleet of 120 ships clashed with Phocaean Greeks (colonists from the Ionian Greek city of Phocaea who had established themselves on Corsica) in a brutal naval engagement. The Greeks claimed a tactical victory (they held the battlefield), but their losses were so severe that they abandoned Corsica entirely. Carthage took control of Sardinia, the Etruscans took Corsica, and the western Mediterranean was effectively divided into Carthaginian and Etruscan spheres of influence, with Greeks largely confined to the eastern half and southern Italy. Alalia established the geopolitical framework that would endure until Rome dismantled it.

The catalyst was the **Battle of Himera** (hih-MAIR-ah) in 480 BCE, fought on the northern coast of Sicily in the same year as the Greek naval victory at Salamis. A Carthaginian army under **Hamilcar I** (HAM-il-kar; a Magonid king who had spent years expanding Carthaginian territory in Sicily and Sardinia) invaded eastern Sicily with an enormous force. The Greek city of Himera, allied with Syracuse, was the target. The Greek tyrant **Gelon of Syracuse** (JEL-on) met the Carthaginians with a combined Sicilian Greek army and won a decisive victory. Hamilcar was killed. According to Greek sources, the battle was a bloodbath: the Carthaginian army was destroyed, and Hamilcar either fell in battle or, seeing the day was lost, threw himself into the sacrificial fire he had been tending throughout the fight. (The detail about the fire is colorful enough to be suspicious, but it recurs in multiple sources.)

The defeat at Himera ended Carthaginian military ambitions in Sicily for seventy years. But the humiliation festered. In 409 BCE, Hamilcar's grandson **Hannibal Mago** (not the famous Hannibal of the Alps — the name was common in Carthage) launched a revenge expedition that sacked and razed the city of Himera. It was a vendetta three generations in the making. The Carthaginians dragged 3,000 Greek prisoners to the spot where Hamilcar had died and executed them as a sacrificial offering. Hannibal Mago then went on to attack other Greek cities in western Sicily, beginning a century of intermittent warfare between Carthage and the Greek city-states of Sicily that would only end when both sides were eventually swallowed by Rome.

---

Between the Sicilian wars, Carthage was becoming the commercial capital of the western Mediterranean. The city's population may have reached 300,000 to 400,000 at its peak (estimates vary widely, but even conservative figures place it among the largest cities in the ancient world), and its urban infrastructure was remarkable. The most famous feature was the **Cothon** (KOH-thon), the city's great double harbor. The commercial harbor was rectangular, open to the sea, and large enough to accommodate hundreds of merchant ships. Behind it, connected by a narrow channel, was the circular military harbor, an engineering marvel that could shelter 220 warships in individual covered slips arranged around a central island where the admiralty commanded operations. The military harbor was hidden from the sea, invisible to approaching ships, its entrance concealed and defensible. No ancient city had anything comparable.

Carthage's commercial reach extended from the eastern Mediterranean to the Atlantic coast of Africa. Carthaginian merchants traded in grain (from the rich North African hinterland), gold (from West Africa, obtained through trans-Saharan trade routes), tin (from the Atlantic tin trade), silver (from Spain), salted fish, hides, and the full range of Phoenician manufactured goods: purple-dyed textiles, glass, metalwork, and ivory carvings. The city also developed a substantial agricultural sector in its North African hinterland (the fertile Cap Bon peninsula and the Medjerda River valley), where large estates worked by slave and tenant labor produced grain, olives, grapes, and fruit. A Carthaginian writer named **Mago** (MAH-goh; not the general — the name was common) produced a 28-volume treatise on agriculture that was so highly regarded that when Rome finally destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE, the Roman Senate ordered Mago's treatise translated into Latin, the only Carthaginian literary work they bothered to save from the fires. Everything else Carthage ever wrote was lost.

Carthaginian religion evolved distinctively from its Phoenician roots. While the mother-city cult of **Melqart** remained important (Carthage sent annual tribute to the Temple of Melqart at Tyre for centuries), the primary deities of Carthage became **Baal Hammon** (BAH-ahl HAM-on), a weather and fertility god depicted as a bearded elder with curling ram's horns, and **Tanit** (TAH-nit), a lunar and fertility goddess whose distinctive triangular symbol (a trapezoid body topped by a circle with a horizontal bar between) appears on thousands of Carthaginian votive stelae. Tanit eventually eclipsed Baal Hammon as the most prominent deity in Carthaginian religious life, and her name appears first on inscriptions from the fifth century BCE onward.

The most controversial aspect of Carthaginian religion is the question of **child sacrifice**. Greek and Roman writers (who were, to put it mildly, not objective observers — they were Carthage's enemies) described a practice called the **molk** (or *moloch*): the ritual sacrifice of children, particularly infants, to Baal Hammon in a sacred precinct called a **tophet** (TOH-fet). The Tophet of Carthage, a large open-air sanctuary, has been excavated and contains thousands of urns holding the cremated remains of young children and animals. The archaeological evidence is unambiguous that infants were cremated and their remains deposited in the tophet. What is fiercely debated is whether these children were ritually killed or whether the tophet was a cemetery for children who died of natural causes (infant mortality in the ancient world was appallingly high) and were given a special ritual burial. The scholarly community is genuinely split. Some researchers point to the disproportionate number of very young infants (suggesting they were selected rather than dying randomly), inscriptions that appear to describe vows fulfilled through sacrifice, and the consistent ancient literary testimony. Others argue that the literary sources are propaganda by Carthage's enemies, that the skeletal evidence shows patterns consistent with natural death, and that the urns contain animal remains mixed with human ones (suggesting animal substitution rather than human sacrifice). The honest answer is that the evidence is ambiguous, the debate is ongoing, and anyone who tells you the question is settled is overstating their case.

---

The wars with Rome began over Sicily. The **First Punic War** (264-241 BCE) was triggered when a group of Italian mercenaries occupying the Sicilian city of Messana (modern Messina, at the northeastern tip of Sicily, controlling the strait between Sicily and the Italian mainland) appealed to both Carthage and Rome for help against Syracuse. Both intervened, their forces collided, and a local dispute escalated into a Mediterranean-wide war. The Romans called the Carthaginians *Poeni* (from *Phoinikes*, "Phoenicians"), hence "Punic" Wars.

Rome had no navy at the start of the war. Carthage had the best navy in the western Mediterranean. By any rational calculation, a naval war between a landlocked Italian city-state and the greatest maritime power in the ancient world should have been a mismatch. But the Romans were nothing if not stubborn. According to tradition, they captured a beached Carthaginian warship, reverse-engineered its design, and built a fleet of over 100 quinqueremes (large warships with five rowers per oar section, arranged across two or three tiers) in sixty days. The story may be embellished, but Rome did build a fleet from scratch and then, with characteristic Roman practicality, added a new weapon: the **corvus** (KOR-vuss; literally "crow"), a boarding bridge with a spiked drop-end that could be swung down onto an enemy ship's deck, locking the two ships together and turning a naval battle into a land battle fought at sea. The corvus negated Carthaginian seamanship advantages and let Rome's superior infantry fight the kind of close-quarters combat they excelled at.

The First Punic War lasted 23 years, destroyed fleets on both sides (storms killed as many sailors as battles did — Rome lost over 100,000 men to shipwrecks alone), and ended with a Roman victory. Carthage lost Sicily, paid an enormous indemnity of 3,200 talents of silver over ten years (roughly 80 tons of silver — a staggering sum), and then lost Sardinia and Corsica when Rome seized them during a Carthaginian mercenary revolt shortly after the war. The loss of Sicily was devastating. It had been Carthage's most important overseas possession for centuries.

---

Carthage responded by expanding in Spain. **Hamilcar Barca** (HAM-il-kar BAR-kah; "Barca" meaning "lightning"), one of Carthage's ablest generals, crossed to Spain in 237 BCE with his young son **Hannibal** and spent the next nine years conquering much of the Iberian Peninsula's southern coast. When Hamilcar was killed in battle in 228 BCE (some sources say he drowned crossing a river during a retreat), his son-in-law **Hasdrubal the Fair** (has-DROO-bal) took over, founded **New Carthage** (modern Cartagena, on Spain's southeastern coast) beside rich silver mines, and governed through diplomacy and negotiation. Hasdrubal negotiated the **Ebro Treaty** with Rome, establishing the Ebro River in northeastern Spain as the boundary between Roman and Carthaginian spheres of influence. When Hasdrubal was assassinated by a Celtic slave in 221 BCE, command passed to the twenty-five-year-old Hannibal.

**Hannibal Barca** (HAN-ih-bal BAR-kah; 247-183 BCE) is one of the most famous military commanders in human history, and his campaign against Rome during the **Second Punic War** (218-201 BCE) is one of the most studied military operations ever conducted. Ancient sources claim that before leaving for Spain as a boy, Hannibal's father Hamilcar made him swear an oath at the altar of Baal Hammon to be an eternal enemy of Rome. Whether the story is literally true or not, Hannibal spent his entire adult life trying to destroy the Roman Republic.

In 219 BCE, Hannibal besieged and captured **Saguntum** (sah-GOON-tum), a Spanish city allied with Rome, south of the Ebro River. Rome demanded that Carthage hand Hannibal over for punishment. Carthage refused. Rome declared war. What Hannibal did next is why military academies still study him.

Rather than wait for Rome to bring the war to Spain or Africa, Hannibal took the war to Italy. In the spring of 218 BCE, he marched an army of roughly 50,000 infantry, 9,000 cavalry, and 37 war elephants from Spain, across southern France, and over the **Alps** in late autumn. The Alpine crossing was a nightmare. Rockslides, hostile mountain tribes, freezing temperatures, and the sheer difficulty of moving elephants over 7,000-foot (2,100-meter) passes killed roughly half his army. Hannibal arrived in the Po Valley of northern Italy with about 26,000 men and most of his cavalry intact but only a handful of surviving elephants. He had lost half his force before fighting a single battle. It didn't matter.

Over the next three years, Hannibal inflicted a series of devastating defeats on Roman armies. At the **Trebia River** (TREH-bee-ah; December 218 BCE), he used a hidden cavalry force to ambush the Roman flank after luring them across an icy river. At **Lake Trasimene** (trah-sih-MEE-nee; June 217 BCE), he executed a perfect ambush, concealing his army in the morning fog along the lakeshore and destroying an entire Roman army of roughly 25,000 men, the largest ambush in military history.

Then came **Cannae** (KAN-eye; August 216 BCE), the masterpiece. On a flat plain in southeastern Italy, Hannibal faced a Roman army of roughly 80,000 men (some estimates run higher) with only about 50,000 of his own. The Romans had massed the largest army they had ever assembled, intending to overwhelm Hannibal through sheer numbers. Hannibal's response was one of the most famous tactical maneuvers in military history: the **double envelopment**. He placed his weakest infantry in the center of his line and his strongest (Libyan heavy infantry) on the flanks, with cavalry on both wings. As the battle began, the Roman infantry pushed forward against Hannibal's deliberately weak center, which slowly gave ground, bowing inward in a concave arc. The Romans pressed deeper and deeper into what they thought was a collapsing enemy line. When the center had retreated far enough, the strong Libyan flanks wheeled inward, closing like the jaws of a trap, while the Carthaginian cavalry routed the Roman cavalry, swung around behind the Roman army, and sealed the encirclement. The Roman army, packed too tightly to use their weapons effectively, was slaughtered where they stood. Ancient sources report between 50,000 and 70,000 Roman dead (modern historians tend to revise downward to 40,000-50,000, but even the lower estimates make it the worst single-day military disaster Rome would suffer in its entire history). To this day, "Cannae" is military shorthand for a battle of annihilation.

---

But Hannibal did not win the war. His strategic problem was the same problem that every invader of Italy would face for the next two thousand years: Italy is long, peninsular, and full of fortified cities, and even a brilliant field commander cannot conquer every city one by one. Rome refused to negotiate after Cannae. Most of Rome's Italian allies, whom Hannibal had hoped would defect, stayed loyal (or were too afraid of Roman reprisal to switch sides). And Rome, despite losing battle after battle, kept raising new armies. Rome's population base, citizen and allied combined, was vastly larger than Hannibal's expeditionary force, and the Roman political system proved psychologically immune to the kind of shock defeats that would have forced most ancient states to sue for peace. After Cannae, the Romans adopted a strategy of exhaustion: avoiding pitched battles, shadowing Hannibal's army, raiding his supply lines, and attacking his Italian allies one by one. Hannibal remained undefeated in Italy for fifteen years, but he could never force the decisive political outcome he needed.

The war turned when a young Roman general named **Publius Cornelius Scipio** (SKIP-ee-oh; later called **Scipio Africanus**; 236-183 BCE) took the war to Carthage's source of power. Scipio conquered Carthaginian Spain, cutting off Hannibal's reinforcements. Then, in 204 BCE, Scipio invaded North Africa directly, threatening Carthage itself. The Carthaginian senate recalled Hannibal from Italy to defend the homeland. In 202 BCE, Scipio and Hannibal met at the **Battle of Zama** (ZAH-mah), about 75 miles (120 km) southwest of Carthage. This time the tactical genius was on Scipio's side. He used Numidian cavalry (North African horsemen who had switched their allegiance from Carthage to Rome) to envelop the Carthaginian flanks, turning Hannibal's own signature maneuver against him. Hannibal was decisively defeated for the first and only time in his career.

The peace terms were crushing. Carthage surrendered its entire fleet except ten ships, gave up all its overseas territories, paid an indemnity of 10,000 talents of silver over fifty years (roughly 260 tons — more than three times the First Punic War indemnity), and agreed never to wage war without Roman permission. Carthage was reduced from a Mediterranean superpower to a regional city-state, commercially viable but politically neutered. Hannibal served briefly as a civic reformer in post-war Carthage before being driven into exile by Roman pressure. He spent his remaining years as an adviser to various eastern Mediterranean kings who were also threatened by Rome, and eventually killed himself by poison in 183 BCE, reportedly saying, "Let us relieve the Romans from the anxiety they have so long experienced, since they think it tries their patience too much to wait for an old man's death."

Scipio Africanus, ironically, died in the same year, also embittered and marginalized by the Roman political system he had saved.

---

# Chapter 8 — The End of Phoenicia

In 332 BCE, **Alexander the Great** (Alexander III of Macedon, the young king who was in the process of conquering the entire Persian Empire) arrived at the gates of Tyre. Most Phoenician cities had surrendered to him without resistance. Byblos opened its gates. Sidon, still bitter over its destruction by the Persians in 351 BCE (Chapter 6), actively welcomed Alexander and helped him against their old rival Tyre. **Aradus** (AR-ah-duss; a Phoenician city on a small island off the northern coast) submitted. The Phoenician fleet, which had been serving the Persian navy, began defecting to Alexander ship by ship.

But Tyre refused. The Tyrians proposed neutrality: they would not fight against Alexander, but they would not surrender their city either. Alexander demanded entry to the island city to sacrifice at the Temple of Melqart (he identified Melqart with Heracles, from whom the Macedonian royal family claimed descent). The Tyrians refused to admit a foreign army. Alexander decided to take the city by force.

The problem was the same one that had defeated Shalmaneser and frustrated Nebuchadnezzar: Tyre's island location. The city sat half a mile (800 meters) offshore, surrounded by water on all sides, protected by walls that rose directly from the sea to a height of 150 feet (45 meters) on the landward side according to ancient sources (a figure that may be exaggerated but conveys the scale). No army in history had taken the island by assault. Alexander, characteristically, decided to do what no one had ever done: build a road across the sea.

The **causeway** (or mole) that Alexander constructed across the half-mile strait between the mainland and the island of Tyre was one of the most extraordinary engineering projects of the ancient world. His army demolished the abandoned mainland city of Old Tyre and used the rubble as fill. They drove wooden pilings into the seabed, packed the gaps with stone, earth, and debris, and built the causeway outward toward the island foot by foot. The work was brutally dangerous. Tyrian warships and fire-ships attacked the workers. Divers reportedly cut the ropes anchoring the pilings. The Tyrians mounted catapults on their walls and rained missiles down on the construction crews. At one point, the Tyrians launched a fire-ship that destroyed two siege towers Alexander had built on the end of the causeway.

Alexander responded by widening the causeway (to make it harder to attack from one side) and assembling a fleet of his own, largely from Phoenician cities that had surrendered and from Cypriot allies. With naval superiority established, Alexander blockaded Tyre's harbors and mounted an amphibious assault. His engineers built siege towers on ships and floated them up to the island's walls. Battering rams on barges pounded the base of the fortifications. After seven months of siege (January to August 332 BCE), the walls were breached, and Alexander's troops stormed the city.

---

The sack of Tyre was savage. Alexander was furious at the long resistance, and the aftermath was intended as a message. Ancient sources (primarily the historians Arrian and Diodorus, writing centuries later to glorify Alexander, and the rhetorician Curtius Rufus, known for dramatic embellishment) report that 8,000 Tyrians were killed in the fighting, 2,000 military-age men were crucified along the beach (a practice the Phoenicians themselves had reportedly used against captured enemies), and 30,000 civilians were sold into slavery. The specific figures should be treated with caution — ancient battle accounts routinely inflated numbers for dramatic effect — but the scale of the atrocity is not disputed. The city was not completely destroyed (Alexander did sacrifice at the Temple of Melqart, as he had originally proposed), but it was devastated. The causeway that Alexander built silted up over the centuries and permanently connected the island to the mainland. The site of ancient island Tyre is today a peninsula. The sea that had protected the city for millennia was filled in by a twenty-six-year-old Macedonian king in seven months.

The siege of Tyre effectively ended Phoenician independence. The Phoenician cities continued to exist as urban centers under Hellenistic and later Roman rule, but they lost their political autonomy, their distinct cultural identity gradually dissolved into the broader Greco-Roman Mediterranean world, and the Phoenician language was slowly replaced by Greek and later Aramaic. By the time of the Roman Empire, the Phoenician homeland was simply part of the province of Syria. The old cities were still prosperous, still commercially active, but no longer Phoenician in any meaningful sense. The Phoenician language survived longest in North Africa, where Punic (the western dialect of Phoenician spoken at Carthage and its colonies) was still spoken in some rural areas as late as the fifth century CE, roughly a thousand years after the fall of Tyre.

The western Phoenician story ended even more violently. After the Second Punic War (Chapter 7), Carthage was stripped of its military power but remained commercially prosperous. Roman hardliners viewed Carthage's continued prosperity as a threat. The Roman senator **Cato the Elder** (234-149 BCE), an archconservative who distrusted Carthage on principle, reportedly ended every speech in the Senate, regardless of the topic, with the phrase **"Carthago delenda est"** ("Carthage must be destroyed"). Whether or not the anecdote is literally true (it is widely attested in ancient sources, but some scholars consider it a later embellishment), it captures the pathological Roman anxiety about a city that had once come terrifyingly close to destroying them.

---

In 149 BCE, Rome manufactured a pretext for war. Carthage had fought a defensive war against the Numidian king **Masinissa** (mah-sih-NISS-ah), a Roman ally who had been encroaching on Carthaginian territory for decades, knowing that Rome would back him. The war technically violated the treaty ending the Second Punic War (which prohibited Carthage from waging war without Roman permission), and Rome used this technicality to declare the **Third Punic War**.

The war was a siege, and it lasted three years (149-146 BCE). Carthage, despite having been disarmed, resisted with extraordinary determination. The city's population forged new weapons from melted-down household metal. Women cut their hair to braid into bowstrings. The Carthaginians fought from the walls, from the rooftops, from the streets. But the outcome was never in doubt. Rome sent its best general, **Scipio Aemilianus** (SKIP-ee-oh ay-mil-ee-AH-nus; the adopted grandson of Scipio Africanus, the victor of Zama), and in the spring of 146 BCE, the Roman army broke through the walls and fought street by street through the city for six days.

The destruction of Carthage was total. The city was systematically demolished. Buildings were pulled down, temples were looted, and the population was killed or enslaved. Ancient sources claim that 50,000 survivors were sold into slavery and that the city burned for seventeen days. The persistent legend that the Romans plowed the ruins and sowed them with salt to ensure nothing would ever grow there is probably apocryphal (the salt detail appears in no ancient source and is first attested in the 19th century), but the spirit of the legend is accurate: Rome intended to erase Carthage from the map. The city's territory became the Roman province of Africa. Carthage would eventually be refounded by the Romans as a Roman colony, becoming one of the great cities of the Roman Empire, but as a Latin-speaking Roman city, not a Phoenician one.

In one of history's coincidences, the same year that Rome destroyed Carthage, 146 BCE, it also sacked and destroyed Corinth, the great Greek commercial city. The dual destructions of Carthage and Corinth in 146 BCE are conventionally taken as the moment when Rome became the undisputed master of the entire Mediterranean world.

The sacred precinct at Carthage known as the **Tophet** (TOH-fet) was buried under the rubble. The great **Cothon** harbor, with its 220 covered ship sheds, was demolished. The **Byrsa** citadel, where Dido had legendarily negotiated for land with a cut-up oxhide, was leveled and later rebuilt as a Roman forum. The six-century arc from Dido's flight from Tyre to Scipio Aemilianus's fires was complete. The greatest colony the Phoenicians ever founded was gone.

But the site of Baalbek (BAH-al-bek; in the Beqaa Valley of modern Lebanon), originally a Phoenician sacred precinct dedicated to Baal, survived the end of Phoenician independence and was transformed into one of the largest temple complexes in the ancient world under Roman rule. The Romans renamed it **Heliopolis** ("City of the Sun") and built colossal temples to Jupiter, Bacchus, and Venus atop the older Phoenician foundations. The Temple of Jupiter at Baalbek contains the largest hewn stone blocks in the ancient world, including the famous **Trilithon** (three stones each weighing over 800 tons). Baalbek is a fitting symbol of the Phoenician legacy: the Romans built their most ambitious architecture on a site the Phoenicians had been worshipping at for centuries, and the foundation stones are still Phoenician.

---

The Phoenicians left no empire, no unified state, no grand architectural monuments on the scale of Egypt's pyramids or Mesopotamia's ziggurats. They left something more durable. Every letter in every alphabet from Iceland to Indonesia traces back to the 22 consonant signs that Phoenician traders standardized and spread across the Mediterranean three thousand years ago. The trade routes they pioneered became the arteries of every subsequent Mediterranean civilization. The cities they founded (Cádiz, Cagliari, Palermo, Tunis, Málaga) are still inhabited today. The navigation techniques they developed (celestial navigation by Polaris, the keeled hull, mortise-and-tenon joinery) became the foundation of all subsequent Mediterranean seamanship.

They also left the most dramatic military narrative in ancient history. Hannibal's crossing of the Alps with war elephants, his double envelopment at Cannae, his fifteen years of undefeated campaigning in Italy, and his tragic end in exile are studied in every military academy and retold in every survey of ancient history. The Carthaginian general became, paradoxically, more famous than any other Phoenician precisely because he fought against Rome, and Rome won, and the winners write the history. We know Hannibal through the eyes of the people who feared him most.

The Phoenicians connected the ancient world. They did it not through armies or ideologies but through ships, trade goods, and 22 simple letters scratched on pots and sarcophagi and temple walls. They built the infrastructure of civilization and then vanished into it, absorbed by the Greek and Roman cultures that inherited everything the Phoenicians had created. The alphabet you are reading is their monument. It is more durable than any pyramid.

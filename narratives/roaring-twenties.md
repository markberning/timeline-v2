# Roaring Twenties — The Boom and the Backlash

**Goal:** Narrative for the United States from the armistice of November 1918 to the Wall Street Crash of October 1929: eleven years in which the country invented the modern consumer economy, the broadcast media, the car culture, and the celebrity, and, on the very same calendar, ran the largest peacetime nativist movement in its history and a national experiment in banning alcohol. The central thesis of this book is that these were not two decades happening near each other but one decade, and that you cannot understand the music, the money, and the movies without understanding the Klan, Prohibition, the immigration quotas, and the Red Scare, because they were a single argument about what kind of country a modern, urban, mass-media America was going to be. The other thesis is structural and simple: the boom that gave the decade its name contained the Crash that ended it, and almost nobody with the power to say so would. "The Roaring Twenties" is itself a label applied after the fact, and like "antebellum" it hides as much as it reveals. It roared for some people, in some places. For farmers, for the coal and textile regions, for Black Americans under Jim Crow (the system of legally enforced segregation, disfranchisement, and lynching the white South built after the Civil War, the subject of the preceding Reconstruction narrative), and for the South generally, the decade did not roar at all. This narrative tells you what the records say, where the numbers are genuinely uncertain, and which version of a contested story is the scholarly consensus and which is nostalgia or propaganda dressed as history.

A note on dates and measurements: this is a U.S.-focused narrative for a U.S.-based reader, so distances are given in miles first, kilometers in parentheses, and large numbers are anchored against things a reader can feel. A note on sources: the twenties are one of the best-documented decades in American history up to that point, newspapers, the new tabloids, government statistics, radio recordings, film, the sociology of "Middletown," and a flood of memoirs. The risk is not a lack of evidence but the opposite: a thick, glamorous, much-mythologized record produced mostly by and for the people the decade was good to. The flapper, the speakeasy, and the ticker tape are the parts that got remembered; this book also covers the parts that were edited out.

A note on the politics: a few things in this story are genuinely contested by historians (whether Sacco and Vanzetti committed the robbery; exactly how many died at Tulsa; whether the Crash "caused" the Depression) and the text says so and gives the range. Most of the rest is not contested. That the second Klan was a national mass movement and not a Southern fringe, that the immigration quotas were written to be racially selective and said so openly, that Prohibition enriched organized crime, and that the boom was real but narrow and debt-fueled, these are the documented consensus, not one side of a live academic argument, and the text states them plainly. The decade's own nostalgia is itself a historical artifact, and this book treats it as one.

# Chapter 1 — The Word and the Wreckage

Start with the label, because it is selling you something.

**"The Roaring Twenties."** Say it and you already see the picture: a flapper with bobbed hair doing the Charleston (the era's frantic, kicking signature dance, Chapter 14) on a speakeasy table, a Model T, a stock ticker, Gatsby's parties, jazz. It is one of the most successful brand names any decade ever got, and it was applied, like a movie poster, after the show was over, mostly in the 1930s and after, by people looking back at the years before the Great Depression with a mixture of envy and a hangover. That is the first thing to be suspicious of. The people living through 1918, 1919, and 1920 did not feel like they were roaring. They were burying flu victims, striking against wage cuts, dodging deportation, and in some American cities being burned out of their homes by mobs. The decade did eventually produce a real, world-changing boom. But the name flattens eleven very different years into one cocktail party, and it flattens out, almost entirely, the roughly half of the country the party never reached, the farm, the mill town, the mine, and the Jim Crow South (Chapter 5 sets out exactly who and why). Hold the suspicion. "Roaring" is a real description of part of this decade for part of this country, and it is also a piece of nostalgia doing a job, and this book will keep telling you which is which.

One blanket caveat, because this book makes a lot of "first" claims (the first broadcast nation, the first mass consumer economy, the first modern celebrity): "first" here means "first at national scale" or "first in the form we'd recognize," not first ever anywhere. Radio existed before KDKA, cars before the Model T, fame before Lindbergh; what changed was scale, speed, and reach. Chapter 5 takes up the biggest of these claims and names who was second.

Now the bridge, because there is a forty-year hole between the end of the last narrative and the start of this one, and you need it filled in or nothing here makes sense. This is the recap, and it is a long one, but it is the only one.

The story before this story ends in 1877, when **Reconstruction** collapsed. Reconstruction is the subject of the narrative that comes right before this one in the chain, so the compressed version: after the Civil War (Antebellum America), the federal government spent about a decade trying, with real but incomplete commitment, to remake the defeated South into a place where the four million people freed from slavery could be citizens, vote, own land, and be safe. The Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments wrote birthright citizenship and Black male suffrage into the Constitution. For roughly a decade this was not a paper promise but a working, if embattled, multiracial democracy: Black men voted across the South in large numbers, held legislative seats, and built their own churches, schools, and fraternal orders. It was not abandoned so much as overthrown. Organized white paramilitary violence (the *first* Ku Klux Klan, then the rifle clubs and the White League) systematically murdered Black officeholders and voters while the Supreme Court gutted the federal laws meant to stop it, and a disputed presidential election in 1876 was settled by a bargain in which the last federal troops were withdrawn from the South in 1877. The Reconstruction narrative tells this as the deliberate, violent counter-revolution it was, and that distinction matters here: Jim Crow was not what the South always was, it was what a defeated experiment was replaced with by force. With that, the South was handed back to its white ruling class, who spent the next quarter-century building the system this book calls Jim Crow and will keep calling Jim Crow: a body of state laws and customs that segregated nearly everything by race, stripped Black men of the vote they had constitutionally been guaranteed (through poll taxes; "literacy" tests on which a white registrar, who alone judged the answers, could pass an illiterate white applicant and fail a Black college graduate at will; and outright violence), locked Black Southerners into sharecropping (a form of farm tenancy that kept the cropper perpetually in debt to the white landowner), and enforced the whole arrangement with lynching, extrajudicial murder, usually by hanging or burning, often public, of which there were thousands between the 1880s and the 1920s. The Supreme Court blessed it: in 1896 it ruled, in *Plessy v. Ferguson*, that "separate but equal" facilities were constitutional, which made segregation the law of the land. The movement that finally dismantled this system is the last narrative in the chain, the Civil Rights Era; the specific instrument was the Voting Rights Act of 1965 (Civil Rights Era narrative, Ch 12), ninety-five years after the Fifteenth Amendment had supposedly already guaranteed the vote. This matters for the twenties because Jim Crow is the force that drives the **Great Migration** (Chapter 13), the movement of more than a million and a half Black Americans out of the rural South and into the cities of the North and Midwest, which begins in earnest around the World War and which builds the Harlem and the Chicago and the Detroit where this decade's most lasting culture gets made.

While the South was rebuilding white supremacy, the rest of the country was industrializing at a violent pace. The decades from roughly 1877 to 1900 are usually called the **Gilded Age** (a phrase from a Mark Twain novel: a thin gold plating over cheap base metal, glittering on the surface and corrupt underneath). It was the age of the transcontinental railroads, of steel and oil, of vast new fortunes (Carnegie, Rockefeller, the railroad and banking dynasties) and the giant monopolistic corporations called **trusts** that those fortunes built, and of enormous, often desperate, immigrant labor. A trust was a specific legal trick: the stock of many competing companies was handed over to a single board of trustees, who then ran the supposedly separate firms as one combine, fixing prices and crushing rivals while technically remaining many companies, which is why "trust" became the era's word for monopoly and "trust-busting" the word for breaking one up. The factories and the corporate organization built in the Gilded Age are the machine this decade's consumer economy will run on; the Gilded Age learned how to *make* enormous quantities of things, and the twenties will learn how to *sell* them to everyone. Against the Gilded Age's inequality and corruption came a reaction, roughly 1900 to 1917, called the **Progressive Era**: a broad, contradictory reform movement that produced the federal income tax, the **Federal Reserve** (the national central bank created in 1913, which will matter enormously in Chapter 15), the direct popular election of senators, the regulation of food and drugs, the breakup of some trusts, and, crucially for this book, two of the constitutional amendments central to this decade's culture war: the Eighteenth, which bans alcohol (Chapter 11), and the Nineteenth, which gives women the vote (Chapter 2). A word on what a central bank does, because the term will recur and the ending depends on it: the Federal Reserve is the bankers' bank, it holds the reserves of ordinary banks, lends to them in a crisis (the "lender of last resort"), and, above all, can make borrowing money across the whole economy cheaper or more expensive by lowering or raising the interest rate it charges banks, which is the single most powerful lever any government has over a boom. Hold that; Chapter 15 turns on it. The twenties will spend the whole decade living off Progressive machinery while loudly repudiating Progressive politics, a tension worth holding onto.

Two more pieces. First, **immigration**. Between roughly 1880 and 1924 something like 25 million people immigrated to the United States, the largest movement of people in the country's history, and the *source* shifted: where earlier waves had come mostly from Britain, Ireland, Germany, and Scandinavia, the new wave came overwhelmingly from southern and eastern Europe, Italians, Poles, Russians, Greeks, and millions of Jews fleeing the pogroms of the Russian Empire (a pogrom was an organized mob attack on a Jewish community, looting, burning, and killing, frequently tolerated or quietly encouraged by the imperial authorities of Tsarist Russia). They were disproportionately Catholic and Jewish in a country that thought of itself as Protestant, they packed into the industrial cities, and they are the population the 1920s will slam the door on (Chapter 9) and that much of the decade's nativist politics is *about*. The 1920 census was the first in American history to find more Americans living in cities than on farms, by a hair, and the rural half of the country experienced that as a loss of the nation it thought it had.

Second, the **World War**. The United States stayed out of the European war that began in 1914 for almost three years, then entered in April 1917, mobilized four million men, shipped two million of them to France, fought hard in the last six months, and lost about 116,000 dead (roughly 53,000 in combat, the rest mostly to disease, much of it the flu of Chapter 2). By the armistice of November 11, 1918, the U.S. had been transformed: it had a huge war-built industrial plant, it was no longer a debtor but the world's leading *creditor*, owed billions by Britain and France, and it had run, at home, a startling apparatus of repression. The wartime Espionage Act of 1917 and Sedition Act of 1918 made antiwar speech a federal crime: the laws made it a felony to say or print anything that obstructed military recruiting or, under the 1918 expansion, anything "disloyal" about the government, the flag, or the armed forces, so a public speech urging men to resist the draft was itself the prosecutable act, no sabotage or spying required. The Socialist leader Eugene Debs went to prison for one such speech and would run for president from his cell. Government propaganda and quasi-official vigilante groups had whipped up "100% Americanism," anti-German hysteria (German measles became "liberty measles," and German-Americans were harassed), and a habit of treating dissent as treason. The war ended; the machinery did not get dismantled. It was sitting there, fully built, when the Russian Revolution gave it a new target. That is where Chapter 3 picks up.

That is the inheritance. A country that had let the formerly enslaved be forced back down into Jim Crow and then told itself it hadn't; that had built the world's largest industrial machine and the inequality to match; that had reformed itself and then gotten tired of reform; that had taken in 25 million immigrants and was about to decide it had made a mistake; and that had just won a war by becoming, for the duration, a surveillance state, and had not turned the apparatus off. Now the eleven years.

One operational point before we start, because it changes how to read everything that follows. The danger with the twenties is not too little evidence but too much of the wrong kind: the gorgeous, much-mythologized record (magazines, movies, advertising, the *novels*) was made by and for the slice of the country the decade was good to, while the Tulsa massacre, the lived mechanics of sharecropping, and the immigrant neighborhoods just before the door shut had to be dug back out, sometimes against active suppression, decades later. When this book and the brand disagree, that gap is usually why. The text will say so when it happens.

# Chapter 2 — The Plague and the Panic

The war ended at 11 a.m. on November 11, 1918, on the eleventh day of the eleventh month, and Americans poured into the streets to celebrate. They were celebrating in the middle of the deadliest disease event in modern American history, and many of the people in those crowds would be dead within weeks, because that is what celebrating in a crowd did in the autumn of 1918.

The **1918 influenza pandemic** was a strain of influenza that did something flu almost never does: it killed healthy young adults at high rates, not just the very old and the very young. It is usually called the "Spanish flu," not because it came from Spain but because wartime Spain, being neutral, had an uncensored press that reported it freely while the warring countries suppressed the news. The likeliest explanation, though the details are still argued, is that it turned the body's own defenses against it: in a young adult with a vigorous immune system, the response to the virus could spiral out of control and flood the lungs with fluid and inflammatory cells until the person essentially drowned, so the very strength that protected the old and the young from this strain is what killed the people in between. It moved along the tracks the war had laid: troopships, crowded training camps, men packed into trenches and transports. It came in waves, the second, in the autumn of 1918, by far the worst. It killed roughly **675,000 Americans** in a little over a year. Sit with that number against something you can feel: it is more Americans than died in combat in the World War, the Second World War, Korea, and Vietnam *combined*. It killed more people worldwide, estimates run from 50 to 100 million, than the war that was ending as it peaked. Philadelphia held a giant Liberty Loan war-bond parade on September 28, 1918, after public-health voices had warned against mass gatherings; within weeks the city's hospitals were overwhelmed and the dead outran the supply of coffins. Cities that closed schools, theaters, and churches early and kept them closed (St. Louis is the textbook case) fared measurably better than cities that did not (Philadelphia), a contrast that public-health historians still cite.

And then it receded, and the country more or less decided not to talk about it. This is one of the genuinely strange facts of the decade and worth flagging because it bears on the sources problem from Chapter 1: an event that killed two-thirds of a percent of the entire population in a year left almost no mark on the decade's novels, its movies, or its popular memory. The Roaring Twenties brand has no room for it. It was recovered as a major subject of historical attention mostly much later, which tells you something about how the decade chose to remember itself.

While the flu was still burning through, the economy fell off the war footing with no plan. During the war the government had effectively run the economy: it set prices, allocated steel and coal, ran the railroads, guaranteed contracts. The armistice cancelled the war contracts almost overnight, the price controls came off, and several things happened at once. First, a short, sharp burst of inflation: with controls gone and consumers spending wartime savings, prices jumped roughly 15 percent in 1919 and into 1920. Second, the soldiers came home: demobilization meant the army simply mustered roughly four million men out as fast as it could, discharging them in waves with a small bonus and a train ticket and no program to find them work, into a labor market the shutting-down war industries were emptying at the same time. Third, the inflation meant that wages which had been frozen during the war now bought far less than they had, while corporate profits stayed high. Workers who had been told not to strike for patriotic reasons during the war were now poorer in real terms and no longer at war.

So they struck. **1919 was the largest strike year in American history**: roughly four million workers, about one in five in the entire workforce, walked out at some point. This was not, mostly, revolution, whatever the newspapers said (Chapter 3 is about what the newspapers said). It was wage-and-recognition unionism, workers wanting their pay to catch up to prices and wanting their unions recognized, happening at enormous scale all at once. But two of the strikes became national symbols, and the symbols are what mattered politically.

In February 1919 the entire union workforce of **Seattle**, some 65,000 workers, walked out in sympathy with striking shipyard workers. It was the first **general strike** in American history, meaning a strike not by one trade but by essentially all *organized labor* in a city at once (organized labor being the workers who belonged to unions, the bargaining associations that negotiated collectively with employers, as opposed to the unorganized majority who bargained alone and lost), with the explicit aim of shutting the city down. Seattle's strike was disciplined and almost eerily peaceful; the strikers organized their own milk delivery and kept order, and it ended in a few days without violence. But "the workers ran the city for a week" landed in a country watching Bolsheviks (the Russian communist revolutionaries who had seized power in 1917, Soviet Union Ch 1) take over a continent, and the mayor of Seattle built a national career on having "broken" a revolution.

Then, in September 1919, the **Boston police** struck. This one was genuinely alarming in a way Seattle was not: when the police walked off the job over the right to affiliate with a national union, Boston had several nights of looting and disorder, because the thing that had walked off the job was the thing that prevents looting and disorder. The strike was broken hard. The Massachusetts governor, a tight-lipped Vermont-born Republican named **Calvin Coolidge**, sent in the State Guard (the emergency state militia a governor can call up when the regular National Guard is unavailable, here used as armed strikebreakers), refused to rehire the strikers, and issued a single sentence that made him a national figure overnight: "There is no right to strike against the public safety by anybody, anywhere, any time." It was the perfect line for the moment, it put his name on every front page in the country, and it is the reason an obscure state governor is on the 1920 Republican ticket and in the White House by 1923 (Chapters 4 and 5).

There was also the **Great Steel Strike**, about 350,000 steelworkers, the largest single strike to that point, broken over months with private guards, strikebreakers, court injunctions (judicial orders forbidding the union from picketing or organizing, so that continuing the strike became contempt of court and the strikers could be jailed by a judge without a jury), and a relentless press campaign painting it as a foreign Bolshevik plot (its lead organizer was in fact a radical, which the steel companies used for everything it was worth), and a national coal strike that a federal court shut down with an injunction. Almost all of the 1919 strikes failed. Union membership, which had risen during the war, fell through the entire prosperous decade that followed. One of the quiet, important facts about the Roaring Twenties is that the boom was not shared with organized labor; it was, in significant part, built on labor's defeat in 1919.

One thing did not fail in this stretch, and it had been fought for since before the Civil War. In August 1920 the **Nineteenth Amendment** was ratified, barring the denial of the vote "on account of sex." Tennessee put it over the top when a 24-year-old legislator, Harry Burn, switched his vote to yes after a letter from his mother urging him to "be a good boy" and help ratify, the kind of detail popular history loves and usually rounds off, though here the letter is real and Burn himself credited it. Something like 26 million American women became eligible to vote (the round figure that circulates; the exact count depends on which census and which state rules you use), in time for the November 1920 presidential election, the first in which women voted nationwide. This is the seventy-year suffrage movement's victory and it is real and it is enormous, and it needs an immediate honest qualification, because the brand version of the twenties presents 1920 as the year American women got the vote, full stop, and that is not what happened. The Nineteenth Amendment removed *sex* as a legal barrier. It did nothing about the *race* barriers the Southern states had built, which is why most Black women in the South, like most Black men, still could not register to vote in 1920 or for decades after, they were blocked by the same poll taxes, rigged literacy tests, and violence that blocked Black men. Suffrage in 1920 enfranchised, in practice, white women and Northern Black women. Black women in the South had to wait for the movement Chapter 13 starts and the later narrative finishes. The amendment also did not, by itself, change much about women's economic and legal position fast; the campaigning split immediately into the moderate League of Women Voters and the militant wing that drafted, in 1923, the first **Equal Rights Amendment**, which then went nowhere for fifty years. The vote was a real, hard-won, partial victory, which is the most honest sentence you can write about a lot of the twenties.

So the decade opened on a country burying flu victims, watching prices outrun wages, watching one in five workers walk off the job, hearing every strike described as the opening of a Bolshevik revolution, and, in the middle of all of it, doubling its electorate. It was frightened, and frightened countries do things they regret. The next chapter is the things it did.

# Chapter 3 — The Fear

In November 1917 the Bolsheviks seized power in Russia and announced they intended to spread their revolution to the entire world. To a great many Americans in 1919, looking at four million workers on strike, at general strikes and police strikes, at a wave of bombs, and at millions of immigrants from exactly the part of Europe the revolution was coming out of, it did not look like a foreign event. It looked like the beginning. The result was the **First Red Scare**, and it is one of the clearest cases in American history of a real but limited threat being inflated into a panic, and the panic doing far more damage than the threat.

There was a real threat, and honesty requires starting with it, because the standard simplification is that the Red Scare was pure hysteria over nothing, and that is not quite true. A small movement of Italian-American anarchists who followed **Luigi Galleani**, an Italian agitator who had landed in the United States in 1901, edited an incendiary anarchist newspaper for immigrant workers, and openly preached "propaganda of the deed" (political murder and bombing as a tool), genuinely was committed to revolutionary violence. In the spring of 1919 they mailed roughly thirty mail bombs to prominent men (most were intercepted before they could go off; one that did maimed the household of a Georgia senator) and then set off a coordinated round of larger bombs in several cities on a single June night, one of which destroyed the front of the Washington house of the U.S. Attorney General, **A. Mitchell Palmer**. The bomber blew himself up on Palmer's step; among the neighbors on the same Washington block, by their own later accounts, were the future Supreme Court justice Felix Frankfurter (a Vienna-born Harvard law professor and civil-liberties advocate) and Franklin Roosevelt, then the assistant secretary of the Navy and, twelve years later, the president who would govern the Depression this book ends at. In September 1920 a wagon packed with dynamite and iron sash-weights was detonated in front of the J.P. Morgan bank on Wall Street, killing about 38 people and wounding hundreds, the **Wall Street bombing**, the deadliest such attack on American soil to that point, never solved, generally attributed to the Galleanists. So: real bombs, real dead people, a real if tiny violent movement.

What turned that into a Scare was the decision, by Palmer, by much of the press, by employers fighting the strikes, by patriotic and veterans' organizations, to treat the tiny violent fringe and the enormous nonviolent rest (labor unionists, Socialists, immigrants, anyone foreign and to the left) as one undifferentiated revolutionary mass. The mechanism is worth naming because it recurs in American history: a genuine security threat is used to license a sweeping crackdown on a much larger population that mostly has nothing to do with it, and the crackdown is most attractive to people who had other reasons to want that population suppressed, employers wanting to break unions, nativists wanting to expel immigrants, politicians wanting a national profile. The Early American Republic narrative shows the first run of exactly this play, the Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798, when a war scare with France was used to jail newspaper editors and lengthen the residency wall against immigrant voters; the Palmer Raids are that 1798 machinery turned, this time, on immigrant radicals instead of opposition politicians.

Palmer wanted to be president. In late 1919 and January 1920 his Justice Department, working through a 24-year-old aide named **J. Edgar Hoover** running a new "radical division" (he ran the federal investigative bureau that became the **FBI**, the Justice Department's national police and domestic-intelligence arm, for the next half-century, keeping secret files on Americans the whole time), carried out the **Palmer Raids**: mass arrests of suspected radicals in dozens of cities, by the most-cited estimate around 6,000 people though counts vary, mostly immigrants, mostly seized without warrants, many beaten, many held for weeks in appalling conditions, many arrested simply for belonging to an organization or being in the wrong meeting hall. Hundreds of non-citizens were deported, because deporting a non-citizen radical required no criminal trial, only an administrative hearing (an in-house government proceeding before an immigration official, with no jury, no requirement to prove a crime, and few of the protections a criminal court must give), a legal shortcut Palmer used hard. In December 1919 the army transport *Buford*, which the press gleefully called the "Soviet Ark," sailed for Soviet Russia with around 250 deported radicals aboard, among them **Emma Goldman**, the Russian-born anarchist writer and orator who had lived in the United States since 1885 and was, after decades of agitation on labor, free speech, and birth control, perhaps the most notorious radical in the country. The Palmer Raids are now a standard example, in American law and history, of a civil-liberties catastrophe, and they were recognized as one fairly quickly: a group of prominent lawyers (Frankfurter among them) published a damning report on the Justice Department's lawlessness, and the **American Civil Liberties Union** was founded in 1920, specifically in response to the wartime sedition prosecutions and the raids, to do the thing no organization had reliably done before, supply lawyers and bring court challenges on behalf of people whose speech or association the government was punishing. Palmer then destroyed his own presidential hopes by loudly predicting a giant revolutionary uprising for May 1, 1920, which did not happen, leaving him looking like a man who had wrecked thousands of lives over a prediction that flopped.

Out of this atmosphere came the decade's longest-running legal drama. In May 1920 two Italian immigrant anarchists, **Nicola Sacco** and **Bartolomeo Vanzetti**, were arrested for a payroll robbery and double murder in South Braintree, Massachusetts. They were Galleanists, they were armed when arrested, and they lied to the police. They were also tried in 1921 in front of a judge who privately called them "anarchist bastards," in a courtroom where their politics and foreignness were front and center, on physical evidence that was contested then and is contested now. Their case became a worldwide cause; for seven years, appeals, demonstrations, and protests on multiple continents argued that two immigrant radicals could not get a fair trial in Red Scare America. They were executed on August 23, 1927. Here is the honest state of it, because this is one of the genuinely unresolved questions in the book: modern scholarship is divided on whether Sacco fired the fatal shots (ballistics analysis, the forensic matching of a recovered bullet to the specific gun that fired it by the marks the barrel leaves, was primitive in 1921 and has been re-run several times since, with results read both ways, and some historians now think Sacco was probably guilty and Vanzetti probably not), but it is *not* divided on whether the 1921 trial was fair. The consensus is that it was not, that the defendants' anarchism and immigrant status were used against them in a way that denied them due process, whatever the truth about the gun. "We may have shot a guilty man unfairly" is a harder and more honest summary than either side's slogan, and it is the summary the evidence supports.

The Red Scare's victims were overwhelmingly immigrants and the left. But 1919 was also one of the worst years of anti-Black violence in American history, and it is essential not to file it as a footnote to the Red Scare, because it was its own thing and in human terms it was worse. In the summer and autumn of 1919, white mobs attacked Black communities in more than two dozen cities and counties, Chicago, Washington D.C., Knoxville, Omaha, and Elaine, Arkansas, where a white force of hundreds, in some accounts joined by federal troops, killed somewhere between dozens and well over a hundred Black sharecroppers who had tried to organize a union. This was not random mob fury; it was the same instrument that had overthrown Reconstruction running again. The Reconstruction narrative documents the template, deputized white paramilitaries, the destruction of prosperous Black institutions, no prosecutions, the record actively suppressed, and 1919 followed it almost step for step. The writer James Weldon Johnson, an official of the **NAACP** (the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People, founded in 1909 to fight segregation and lynching through lawsuits, lobbying, and publicity, and the Black counterpart, at almost the same moment and with almost no coordination, to the white-and-radical civil-liberties work the ACLU was about to take up), named the season the **"Red Summer,"** *red* for blood. In Chicago, eight days of violence, touched off when a Black teenager was stoned and drowned for drifting across an unwritten color line at a Lake Michigan beach, left, by the figures most often cited, around 38 dead and well over a thousand Black families burned out of their homes. A crucial difference from earlier waves of racist violence: many of the Black men in these cities were veterans who had just been to France, were armed, and fought back. The NAACP's anti-lynching campaign (Chapter 13) gained national urgency from the Red Summer, and the violence did not stem the migration north (Chapter 13); it sharpened it, because the alternative, the rural South, produced, two years later, the single worst episode.

On May 31 and June 1, 1921, in **Tulsa, Oklahoma**, a white mob, deputized, and in some cases armed, by the city itself, with airplanes reportedly used to drop incendiaries, destroyed the Greenwood district, a Black neighborhood prosperous enough to be called "Black Wall Street." The trigger was the usual one: a young Black man accused, on essentially no evidence, of assaulting a white woman in an elevator, and the threat of a lynching, which armed Black veterans came to the courthouse to prevent. The number killed is one of the genuinely uncertain figures in this book and the uncertainty is itself part of the story: contemporary official counts said about three dozen, but later investigations, including a 2001 state commission and ongoing mass-grave excavations, estimate the dead at up to about 300, and the reason the number is unknown is that the city of Tulsa actively suppressed the event for fifty years, no prosecutions, no insurance payouts, the records buried, the massacre kept out of textbooks and local memory until survivors and historians forced it back into the record near the end of the century. Ten thousand Black residents were left homeless. The **Tulsa Race Massacre** is the sharpest single illustration of two things this book keeps returning to: that the twenties did not roar for Black Americans, and that the glossy record of the decade is glossy partly because the ugliest parts were deliberately erased and had to be dug back up, sometimes literally.

The Scare burned out fast, Palmer's failed prophecy, the courts pushing back, the economy recovering, the country simply tiring of fear. By the end of 1921 Harding had even commuted the sentence of Eugene Debs, the Socialist still in federal prison for an antiwar speech, and invited him to the White House for a chat. But the Scare left two permanent residues. One was a federal apparatus and a young man named Hoover who had learned how to surveil and file Americans, and who kept doing it. The other was the political fuel for the next chapter's two great projects: shutting the immigration door (Chapter 9), and electing a government that promised, above everything, to make all of this *stop*.

# Chapter 4 — Normalcy

In May 1920 a Republican senator from Ohio named **Warren Gamaliel Harding** gave a speech in Boston with a sentence in it that has outlived everything else about him: "America's present need is not heroics but healing, not nostrums but normalcy, not revolution but restoration." *Normalcy* was not, as legend later had it, a word Harding invented by mistake, it was rare but real, but the legend stuck because it fit him: a slightly fuzzy word for a slightly fuzzy promise, which was, precisely, the promise to make the previous three years stop. No more war, no more flu, no more strikes, no more Bolsheviks, no more crusading president telling the country it had a moral mission in the world. After Woodrow Wilson, the Progressive moralist who had taken the country into the war, fought himself to a stroke trying to get the U.S. into his League of Nations (the international body the 1919 Treaty of Versailles created; the Constitution requires a two-thirds Senate vote to ratify any treaty, and a bloc of senators who would not accept a binding commitment to defend other countries denied Wilson that two-thirds in a long and bitter fight, so the U.S. never joined the body it had invented), and left office a broken invalid, the country wanted, above all, to be left alone. It got Harding by the largest popular-vote landslide to that point, roughly 60 percent of the vote, about 16 million to 9 million, in the first election in which women voted nationwide, a thumping repudiation not just of the Democrats but of the whole Progressive, internationalist, reforming temper of the previous twenty years.

Harding was an Ohio newspaper publisher of genuine personal warmth, limited ability, and disastrous judgment about friends. He is routinely ranked at or near the bottom of American presidents, and the ranking is fair, but the simple "worst president, total joke" version misses what is interesting, which is that his administration contained, simultaneously, some of the most capable men of the era and some of the most corrupt, and that the policies the capable ones set in motion are the policies the boom ran on.

Start with the capable men, because their policies are the ones the boom ran on. As Treasury Secretary, Harding appointed **Andrew Mellon**, a Pittsburgh banker and aluminum, oil, and steel magnate who was one of the three or four richest men in the country. Mellon's program, pursued under Harding and then under Coolidge for the rest of the decade, was the *supply-side* argument in its first full American form. Supply-side means cutting taxes on the people and firms that supply capital and goods (the rich, investors, corporations) rather than putting money in ordinary consumers' pockets, on the theory that high top rates merely drove the rich to hide money in tax-exempt shelters, while lower rates would pull that money back into productive, taxable investment and grow the economy enough that total revenue would not fall. Over the decade, in a series of Revenue Acts (1921, 1924, the big one in 1926), the top marginal income-tax rate was cut from 73 percent to 24 percent and the estate tax was gutted. Whether it "worked" is still argued and probably always will be, but the honest thread to pull is this: the things the policy promised, high revenue and a boom, did happen, and the things it did not promise happened too. The income gains piled up at the very top while the farm, the mine, and the mill (Chapter 5) got nothing, and the prosperity it presided over turned out to be sitting on a hollow floor (Chapter 15). Mellon unambiguously got what he wanted, a decade of falling taxes on high incomes and a deliberate federal retreat from using the tax code to redistribute; whether the country got what *it* was promised is the question the rest of the book answers. As Commerce Secretary, Harding appointed **Herbert Hoover**, the famous engineer who had run Belgian war relief and the wartime Food Administration and was probably the most admired administrator in America. Hoover turned the sleepy Commerce Department into the most dynamic agency in the government, promoting industrial standardization, trade associations, commercial aviation, and the regulation of the new radio spectrum (the finite band of broadcast frequencies on which radio signals travel; without a referee assigning each station its own frequency, powerful stations simply drown out weaker ones and nobody can be heard, which is the chaos Chapter 8 describes). His philosophy, that government should *organize and coordinate* private enterprise rather than run it or leave it alone, is its own distinct thing, neither Progressive regulation nor pure laissez-faire, and it is why he, not Mellon, ends up the decade's defining administrator and its last president (Chapter 15). As Secretary of State, Harding had **Charles Evans Hughes**, who hosted the 1921–22 **Washington Naval Conference**, which produced the first major international arms-limitation treaty by capping the battleship tonnage of the leading naval powers (the United States, Britain, Japan, France, and Italy), a real achievement and a marker of how thoroughly the country had recoiled from the war into disarmament and staying home.

The same administration was also the most corrupt the country had yet seen, and that is the part Harding is remembered for, because it was spectacular. He had brought to Washington a crowd of Ohio political cronies and poker companions that the press came to call the **Ohio Gang**, and they treated the federal government as a thing to be sold off in pieces. The pardon power is the president's constitutional authority to wipe out a federal conviction; the Ohio Gang sold it, taking cash to arrange pardons and paroles for convicted men. They sold immunity from Prohibition enforcement and access to the government's confiscated liquor. They sold government contracts. Charles Forbes, put in charge of the brand-new Veterans' Bureau, the agency meant to care for the men disabled in the World War, looted it through kickbacks on hospital construction and the corrupt sale of supplies; the waste was commonly estimated at around $200 million, the kind of large round figure that should be read as an order of magnitude rather than an audited total, and he went to prison. But the scandal that gave the decade its byword for corruption was **Teapot Dome**. The Secretary of the Interior, Albert Fall, secretly leased two naval oil reserves, one at Teapot Dome, Wyoming, one at Elk Hills, California, to two private oilmen, without competitive bidding (the legal requirement that government contracts be opened to rival sealed offers so the public gets the best price and no official can simply hand a contract to a friend, the safeguard Fall deliberately bypassed), in exchange for something on the order of $400,000 in "loans," cash, and cattle, the documented value varying with how each gift is counted. A Senate investigation, one of the great congressional investigations in American history, dragged it into the light over several years. Fall became, in 1929, the first U.S. cabinet officer ever imprisoned for crimes committed in office. In a verdict that became a national joke about justice for the rich, the oilman convicted of nothing was acquitted of bribing the man who was convicted of taking the bribe.

Harding seems to have begun to grasp, in 1923, that his friends were destroying him. The line most often hung on him, recorded secondhand through the journalist William Allen White and polished in the retelling, has him saying he had no trouble with his enemies but that his "goddamned friends" were the ones keeping him walking the floor at night. The exact words are uncertain; the sentiment is well attested. On a western speaking tour, in San Francisco, on August 2, 1923, he died suddenly, almost certainly of a heart attack, before the worst of the scandals had broken publicly. Because he died beloved and the corruption surfaced afterward, the country mourned him enormously, then spent the next several years discovering what he had presided over. His historical reputation went into the basement and stayed there.

And his death did one more thing, the thing that mattered most for the decade. It handed the presidency, at the precise moment the economy was about to take off, to the one man in the administration with no whiff of the corruption on him at all: a silent, frugal, scandal-proof Vermonter who had been kept so far from the action as vice president that he learned he was president while on vacation at his father's farm, by lamplight, in the middle of the night.

# Chapter 5 — The Silent Man

The scene is almost too good, and it is true, which is worth saying because a lot of the best twenties stories are improved in the retelling and this one did not need it. **Calvin Coolidge**, vice president, was visiting his father's farmhouse in Plymouth Notch, Vermont, in the first days of August 1923. The farmhouse had no electricity and no telephone. Word that Harding had died came up the valley by messenger in the dark. Coolidge's father, John, was a notary public, a local official with the legal power to administer oaths, and so at 2:47 a.m. on August 3, 1923, by the light of a kerosene lamp, in the sitting room of a Vermont farm with no electric light, a father swore in his son as President of the United States. Then, by his own account, Coolidge went back to bed.

It is the perfect origin story for the man, because Coolidge's entire political brand was that he was the opposite of everything the previous five years had been: not heroic, not corrupt, not eloquent, not ambitious-seeming, not modern. He was famously, deliberately, almost aggressively silent, "Silent Cal", and the silence was a strategy, not just a temperament. The famous exchange, a dinner companion betting she could get more than two words out of him, his reply "You lose," is told too many ways to be taken as transcript, and is best treated as the kind of legend that attaches only to a man whose silence was already proverbial. He slept eleven hours a day and napped in the afternoon, and he understood, better than almost any politician of the era, that in a decade when the press, the radio, and the public mood all wanted the government to *stop doing things and let the boom happen*, the most popular thing a president could do was very little, very quietly, and let people get rich.

His actual philosophy got compressed, in January 1925, into the sentence the whole decade is remembered by, almost always shortened to "the business of America is business," which is punchier than what he actually said ("the chief business of the American people is business") and drops the more idealistic qualifications that surrounded it, but the misquote stuck because it captured the policy exactly. The policy was: continue Mellon's tax cuts (the big 1926 Revenue Act, top rate down to 24 percent, is the Coolidge-era centerpiece), keep federal spending and the federal government small, stay out of the economy, stay out of the world, and take credit for the prosperity. He twice vetoed the **McNary–Haugen** farm-relief bill, whose mechanism was that the federal government would buy up surplus crops at a high domestic "fair" price and dump them abroad for whatever the world market would pay, eating the loss to keep American farm prices up; Coolidge killed it on the grounds that propping up prices was not the government's business, a decision that tells you both the consistency of the philosophy and exactly who the philosophy was not for. He ran for a full term in 1924 on the slogan "Keep Cool with Coolidge," against a Democratic Party that had just spent its convention tearing itself to pieces (Chapter 10) and a Progressive third-party candidate, Robert La Follette, who pulled nearly five million votes in protest and carried exactly one state, his own Wisconsin. Coolidge won comfortably. The country had a booming economy and a president who promised to do nothing to it, which was, in 1924, precisely the offer the country wanted to accept.

Now the boom itself, in outline, because Chapters 6 through 8 are going to take it apart system by system and this chapter just needs to establish the shape. Between roughly 1922 and 1929 the American economy did something genuinely new in the history of the world. Industrial output and worker productivity rose dramatically. The gross national product, the total money value of everything the country produced in a year and the standard yardstick of the size of an economy, grew by something on the order of 40 percent across the decade (the figure varies with how it is measured, which is why the careful phrasing). And, this is the new part, the part that makes it the *first* of its kind, the gains, for the urban industrial middle and skilled working class, showed up not mainly as more food and rent but as *things*: a car, a radio, an electric refrigerator, a washing machine, a vacuum cleaner, a phonograph, store-bought clothes, packaged food, movie tickets, all bought new, many bought on credit, all advertised relentlessly. This had never happened at this scale anywhere, and the obvious candidate for who *should* have done it first sharpens the point. Britain was the first industrial nation and had the world's largest empire and mass factory production by the 1880s, yet it did not produce a mass consumer economy then, and the reasons are precisely the ingredients America had and Britain lacked: British industrial wages were lower and far more unequally spread, so most workers had no surplus to spend on durable goods; consumer installment credit was underdeveloped, so a worker could not buy ahead of his wages; advertising was still primitive; and British industry was oriented toward exporting to the empire rather than selling at home. America was the first place all of those came together at once, which is what "first" means here. Germany was a great industrial power but its 1920s were the catastrophe of hyperinflation and then a fragile, debt-propped recovery. The Soviet Union, in this same decade, was a peasant economy beginning a state-driven industrialization at enormous human cost (Soviet Union Ch 2). Japan was industrializing fast but nowhere near American consumer levels (Japanese Economic Miracle is the chapter where Japan reaches them, decades later). The United States in the 1920s is the first society on earth where a mass consumer economy, make enormous quantities of branded goods, pay workers enough and lend them enough to buy them, sell them through national advertising, became the way the whole system worked. The America that comes after is, in large part, the one assembled in this decade, and it has never been disassembled.

This is the place to set down, once and at full length, who the boom left out, because the brand version stops at the paragraph above and the rest of the book will only need to point back here. The boom was real and it was narrow, and three very large groups mostly did not get it. **Farmers**, close to a third of the country, had been pushed by wartime demand to expand and borrow; the demand vanished after the war, crop prices collapsed and stayed collapsed all decade, and something like a quarter of American farmers lost their land, so the rural depression the 1930s would merely nationalize was already running in the countryside the entire time. The **"sick industries"**, coal, textiles, New England manufacturing, the railroads, stagnated or shrank as oil displaced coal, the South and synthetics undercut New England textiles, and labor's 1919 defeat (Chapter 2) kept wages down where the work survived; the **Battle of Blair Mountain** in 1921, when some ten thousand West Virginia coal miners took up arms and marched on the southern counties to break the operators' private gunmen and forced company towns and were stopped only when the U.S. Army moved in against them, the largest armed labor uprising in American history, and the brutal 1929 Gastonia, North Carolina textile strike are bookends on a region the boom never reached. And **Black Americans**, the great majority still in the Jim Crow South, were locked out by law and violence.

The three are usually told as parallel but separate stories, and the deepest point of this chapter is that for the largest group the lines actually cross. The Black farm South was not a different misfortune from the white farm depression; it was the same crop economy with a far more brutal floor under it. The Reconstruction narrative lays out the mechanism: when emancipation did not come with land redistribution, the formerly enslaved were forced into sharecropping and the crop-lien system, raising someone else's cotton on credit advanced at usurious terms, the debt rolled over and growing every year, with no possibility of accumulating land or capital. Sixty years later, in the 1920s, that trap was still closed. When cotton prices collapsed, a white owner-farmer lost equity he had built; a Black sharecropper, who had been allowed to build none, had nothing between him and ruin but the road north. That is why the farm crisis and the Great Migration of Chapter 13 are one story, not two: the migration is the failure of Reconstruction's land promise, sixty years deferred, finally cashing out. The "Roaring Twenties," in short, is largely the experience of the urban, white, industrial wage-and-salary class during a real boom that did not reach the farm, the mine, the mill town, or the cotton field. That asterisk now stands; later chapters will point to it rather than rebuild it.

Coolidge, in the end, had the impeccable timing of a man who leaves the party at exactly the right moment. On August 2, 1927, the fourth anniversary of the lamplit oath, he handed reporters a slip of paper with one sentence on it and walked away without answering questions: "I do not choose to run for President in nineteen twenty eight." Historians have argued ever since about whether he sensed the boom was hollow; the more mundane likeliest reading is that he was tired and grieving (his teenage son had died in 1924) and the famously laconic man was being laconic. Whatever the reason, the effect was the same as Harding's death in reverse: the man who presided over the boom walked out the door before the bill arrived, and handed the presidency, and the Crash, to the famous engineer who had spent the decade being the most admired man in the government (Chapter 15).

# Chapter 6 — The Machine That Made the Decade

If you want one object that explains the decade, it is not the cocktail or the saxophone. It is the **automobile**, and specifically the way it was *built*, because the way the car was built is the way everything in the consumer economy was eventually built, and the way the car reshaped the country is the way the consumer economy reshaped American life.

Start with **Henry Ford**, who did not invent the car (Europeans did, in the 1880s) and did not even invent the moving assembly line in any pure sense, but who in 1913, at his Highland Park plant in Michigan, put the pieces together at a scale and to an effect nobody had managed before. The **assembly line** as Ford perfected it works on a single principle: instead of skilled workers moving to a stationary car and building the whole thing, the car moves, on a powered conveyor, past stationary workers, each of whom does one small task, attach this bolt, fit this wheel, over and over, all day. Break the job into the smallest possible repeated motions, never make the worker walk or fetch or think, and the time to build a car collapses. By the standard figures, the time to assemble a Model T chassis fell from somewhere over twelve hours to roughly an hour and a half, and at peak a Ford plant was completing a car about every half-minute, the kind of vivid production statistic that gets quoted as a single number but really describes a particular plant at a particular moment. The price of the **Model T**, the one model Ford built (famously available, he said, "in any color, so long as it is black," because black paint dried fastest and the line could not wait), fell year after year as volume rose, bottoming under $300 in the mid-twenties, within reach, for the first time, of a skilled worker's roughly annual earnings rather than only a rich man's. By 1927 Ford had built around 15 million Model Ts over the model's nineteen-year run; for much of the early-to-mid twenties Ford alone built roughly half the cars made anywhere in the world, a dominance no single carmaker has matched since, though the exact share depends on how and when you count.

The line had a problem, and Ford's solution to it is the hinge of the whole consumer economy, so slow down here. The work was so monotonous and grinding that workers quit constantly; by the company's own figures, which are the main source for the number and worth flagging as such, annual turnover at Highland Park ran several hundred percent, meaning Ford had to hire many times over his entire workforce every year just to keep the plant staffed, which was enormously expensive. In 1914 Ford did something that looked insane to other industrialists: he roughly doubled the basic wage to **five dollars a day** and cut the workday to eight hours. The immediate purpose was to stop the turnover and the labor unrest (it worked; men lined up around the block). But the deeper logic, which Ford himself articulated and which became the founding idea of the mass-consumption economy, was this: pay the workers who build the cars enough that they can *buy* the cars. A worker who is also a customer is not just a cost; he is the market. This is the loop the entire decade runs on, mass production making goods cheap enough for the masses, and mass *wages* (plus, soon, mass *credit*, Chapter 7) putting buying power in the masses' hands to absorb the output. It is genuinely a new economic model, and it is the exact inversion of the labor system the previous narratives in this chain were built on. The antebellum and post-emancipation cotton South ran on the opposite principle, extract the maximum labor at the minimum cost and keep the worker with no purchasing power at all, the logic of slavery and then of sharecropping. Ford's logic, that the worker is the market and the system fails unless he is paid enough to buy back what he makes, is why the consumer economy was Northern and urban and the cotton field stayed poor: the two halves of the country were running on opposite answers to the same question. It is the first time a major economy is consciously organized around the idea that the worker and the consumer are the same person and the system only works if both halves are fed. The brand version stops at "Ford was generous." The real story is that mass production without mass purchasing power is just a warehouse full of unsold cars, and Ford saw it first.

Ford's weakness was that he saw it first and then stopped thinking. He believed the formula was permanent: one cheap, unchanging, reliable car forever. The man who saw what came next was **Alfred Sloan**, who took over **General Motors** in 1923. GM could not beat Ford on price, so Sloan refused to compete on price. Instead he built what became the modern American corporation and the modern American marketing strategy at the same time. GM offered "a car for every purse and purpose", a ladder of brands from cheap Chevrolet up to luxury Cadillac, so that as a family's income rose it could "trade up" within GM. GM introduced the **annual model change**, restyling the car every year so that last year's car, though mechanically fine, looked old, which manufactured a reason to buy a new one before the old one wore out (the technique now called planned, or really *perceived*, obsolescence). And GM, through its financing arm GMAC, pushed buying the car **on installment credit** (Chapter 7), which Ford disdained and which turned out to be how most Americans actually bought cars. By 1927 GM's strategy had overtaken Ford's single cheap car so decisively that Ford had to shut his plants for roughly half a year, idle tens of thousands of workers, and completely retool from the Model T to the new **Model A**, the moment even Henry Ford had to admit that styling, choice, and credit had beaten cheapness and durability. That defeat is one of the most important business stories of the century, because the model that won, segmented brands, annual restyling, consumer credit, marketing as the core of the enterprise, is the model the American economy has run on ever since.

Now what the car *did*, because the object reshaped the country as profoundly as the way it was built. By the late twenties roughly one American family in two owned a car. That sounds modest until you set it against the rest of the world: in Britain and France in 1929 a private automobile was still an upper-middle-class luxury, owned by a thin sliver of the population, and mass car ownership did not arrive in most of Europe or in Japan until well after the Second World War. The American "first" here is not the car, which Europeans built, but the ordinary family's car, and the gap between America and everyone else was a generation wide.

A country where ordinary families have cars is physically a different country, and the change worth opening up is the **suburb**. The car severed the old tie between where you lived and where you worked: you no longer had to live within a streetcar ride of the factory or the office. That released a new kind of place, the planned, low-density, car-dependent residential development (Beverly Hills, Kansas City's Country Club District) built around the automobile and protected by **zoning**, the new municipal power, spreading fast in the twenties, to dictate by law what could be built where, banning shops and apartments and factories from designated single-family districts so the suburb stayed exactly the kind of place its developers were selling. Zoning is what made the suburb a permanent legal form rather than just a fashion, and the suburb is the single most consequential thing the car built, the template for how Americans would live, and segregate, for the rest of the century. The rest followed quickly and can be named more briefly: the federally funded numbered-highway system (the **Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1921** put the national government into road-building dollar-for-dollar with the states, and the 1926 system included the storied Chicago-to-Los-Angeles **Route 66**, whose length is given variously around 2,400 miles depending on the era's routing); a whole roadside economy that had not existed (the gas station, the motel, the diner, the billboard); the national park and the family road trip as middle-class rituals; and a quiet revolution in courtship, a private space on wheels away from the parlor and the parents, which scandalized the older generation precisely because they understood what it was for. When two sociologists ran the first deep sociological portrait of a typical American town, published in 1929 as **Middletown** (the town was Muncie, Indiana; the study, which recurs in Chapters 7 and 14, became the standard close reading of how the consumer decade actually changed ordinary life), they found families who would "go without clothes" before they would give up the automobile. In one decade an object went from luxury to necessity, and the country physically rebuilt itself around it.

# Chapter 7 — Buy Now, Pay Later

A country can only consume what it can pay for, and in 1920 most American families could not, out of current wages, pay cash for a car, a refrigerator, a radio, and a washing machine all at once. The thing that squared that circle, that turned the productive capacity of Chapter 6 into the consumer society the decade is remembered as, was a change less visible than the assembly line and at least as important: the normalization, for ordinary middle-class and working families, of **going into debt to buy things**.

Buying on credit was not new; what was new was its respectability and its scale. For most of American history, consumer debt carried a moral stigma, debt was something the poor fell into and the prudent avoided, and "neither a borrower nor a lender be" was a value, not just a line from a play. In the twenties that stigma was deliberately dissolved, mostly by the people selling the goods, through the **installment plan**: buy the thing now, take it home now, and pay it off in fixed monthly amounts over months or years, with interest, the seller (or a finance company like GM's GMAC) keeping the right to repossess if you missed payments. By the late twenties, on the economic historians' usual estimates, somewhere in the range of one in ten to one in seven of all retail sales, and the clear majority of sales of cars, radios, furniture, and major appliances, were on installment. Consumer debt became, for the first time, a permanent structural feature of the American economy rather than an emergency. The phrase that did the cultural work was "buy now, pay later," and the genius and the danger of it are the same fact: it let a family consume at the level of its expected *future* income rather than its current cash, which hugely expanded demand on the way up and meant that a great deal of the decade's prosperity was, quite literally, borrowed. Hold that thought; it does not stay abstract. It is the same logic as buying stock "on margin" (Chapter 15), and the decade ends when the future everyone had borrowed against fails to arrive.

Credit gave people the money. **Advertising** gave them the wanting. The modern advertising industry, the agency, the slogan, the brand, the campaign, was substantially built in this decade, much of it on Madison Avenue, the Manhattan street where so many big agencies clustered that "Madison Avenue" became, and remains, shorthand for the advertising business itself. What it figured out was that you do not sell a product by describing the product. You sell it by attaching it to a fear or a desire the buyer already has: the fear of social failure, of being unattractive, of falling behind the neighbors; the desire for romance, status, modernity, youth. The textbook case is Listerine, a mild antiseptic that the Lambert company turned into a mass product in the early twenties by pushing an obscure quasi-medical word for bad breath, "halitosis" (the word predates the campaign; Listerine's advertising is what burned it into the language), and selling the mouthwash as protection against a private flaw that was costing you romance and marriage. Soap, cigarettes, cars, and refrigerators were sold the same way, not as objects but as the gap between the life you had and the life you feared you were missing. The decade's emblematic advertising text is a 1925 bestseller by the ad executive Bruce Barton, *The Man Nobody Knows*, which recast Jesus of Nazareth as the founder of modern business, a supreme executive and advertising man who, in Barton's telling, took twelve obscure men and forged them into an organization that conquered the world (the book's argument, paraphrased; the phrasing here is not a verbatim quotation). It was meant admiringly. It is also, read now, an almost too-perfect confession: in a decade whose loudest public fights were about religion (Chapters 10 and 12), the thing actually being preached to the largest national congregation, every day, through magazines and soon the radio, was consumption.

Then there was the question of where you bought it, and the answer changed the American main street permanently. The old general store ran on the storekeeper: he knew you, carried you on credit through a bad month, fetched the goods from behind the counter, and bought in small lots at prices a small buyer pays. The **chain store** killed all of that on purpose. A&P in groceries opened thousands of identical stores; Woolworth standardized the five-and-dime; Piggly Wiggly invented the self-service aisle, where the customer, not a clerk, pulled a labeled package off a shelf, which is the moment the brand on the package replaced the word of the clerk. Buying for a thousand stores at once, a chain could undersell any single merchant and never lose, and the independent storekeeper, with him the informal neighborhood credit and the face-to-face economy, began to disappear. The trade-off the chain store introduced at national scale, cheaper goods bought at the cost of the locally owned main street, was new in the twenties and has never been resolved since.

And underneath all of it was the wire that made the modern home a place worth filling with appliances: **electrification**. In 1918 only a minority of American homes had electric power; by the end of the twenties roughly two-thirds did, though the figure conceals a brutal divide, because the two-thirds was overwhelmingly *urban*, and the great majority of American *farms* still had no electricity at all, which is one more way the boom stopped at the city limits. But for the electrified urban home the change was total. Electric light ended the rhythm of the kerosene lamp (the one Coolidge took his oath by, a detail that was already nostalgic in 1923). The electric refrigerator began replacing the icebox and the daily ice delivery. The electric washing machine and vacuum cleaner began replacing the most backbreaking parts of domestic labor, and, in one of the decade's sharper ironies, the same advertising that sold the labor-saving appliance also raised the standard it was measured against, so the time saved was substantially spent meeting the higher standard. The vacuum cleaner is the clean example: the carpet that had been beaten outdoors a few times a year was now expected to be vacuumed weekly, and the wife who had once sent the wash out or done it on a fixed wash-day now, with a machine at home, was expected to send the family out in fresh clothes far more often. The labor was not saved; it was reassigned and intensified, a pattern historians of housework have documented carefully. Refrigeration plus the chain store plus the car plus national brands together rebuilt the American diet and the American kitchen inside a single decade.

Put the pieces together and you have the machine the whole decade ran on: factories that could make almost unlimited quantities of standardized goods (Chapter 6); wages and, above all, credit that put buying power in ordinary hands; advertising that manufactured the desire to spend that power; chain distribution that delivered the goods cheaply everywhere; and an electrified home to put them in. This is the first **mass consumer economy** in the history of the world, not the first place with rich people who bought a lot, but the first place where the *system itself* depended on tens of millions of ordinary people continuously buying new manufactured things, much of it on borrowed money, in response to national advertising. It produced a genuinely higher material standard of life for the urban middle and skilled working class than any large society had ever had. It also built in a structural fragility that the brand version never mentions and Chapter 15 collects on: an economy that runs on continuous mass purchasing fueled by debt and confidence has a floor made of debt and confidence, and floors made of confidence do not bend. They break.

# Chapter 8 — The Broadcast Nation

On the night of November 2, 1920, a Westinghouse engineer in a shack on a Pittsburgh rooftop read out election returns, Harding was beating Cox, into a microphone, over a station with the call letters **KDKA**, to an audience of perhaps a few hundred people with homemade receivers. It is conventionally counted as the first commercially licensed radio broadcast in the United States. Within roughly two years there were on the order of five hundred licensed stations (the exact count moves around in the records of the new regulators). Within the decade, radio went from a hobbyist's crystal set to a piece of furniture in roughly a third of American homes, and in doing so it did something that had genuinely never happened in human history: it let millions of people, scattered across a continent, hear the *same voice, the same music, the same joke, and the same advertisement at the same instant*.

That is worth stopping on, because it is one of the decade's true firsts and the comparison sharpens it. Every previous mass medium had a delay and a copy. A newspaper or a book or a phonograph record reached many people, but each reader read a separate physical object, at a separate time. Radio was simultaneous and shared: a nation of strangers, in the same moment, having the same experience. No society had ever had that. It is the technological substrate of the modern shared culture, the national audience, and the United States in the 1920s is where it first existed at scale. Britain built its national broadcaster, the BBC, in this same decade, deliberately as a non-commercial public monopoly funded by a license fee, the opposite institutional choice from the American one. That contrast is worth holding, and not only because it shaped each country's media for the rest of the century: because American radio was paid for by advertisers chasing the largest possible audience, the most popular, most profitable, nationally distributed content included the demeaning imitation of Black culture that this chapter ends on, while the BBC, beholden to no sponsor and run as an instrument of national uplift, carried nothing like it. The American structure did not merely permit broadcast minstrelsy; the advertiser model actively rewarded it.

The American structural choice was made fast and it was commercial. The early chaos, hundreds of stations stamping on each other's signals, was resolved by the **Radio Act of 1927**, which created a Federal Radio Commission to license broadcasters in "the public interest" and established the principle that the airwaves were public property the government would parcel out. But the content and the money were private and were quickly nationalized into networks. In 1926 RCA launched the **National Broadcasting Company** (NBC), the first permanent national radio network, which linked local stations together with leased telephone lines so a single program could go out across the country at once. **CBS** followed in 1927 and was bought the next year by William Paley, then in his late twenties, who built it into NBC's great rival. The two-network structure created by 1928, and the model of paying for it all by selling the audience's attention to advertisers, is, recognizably, the structure of American broadcasting for the rest of the century, television included. Radio was where the consumer economy of Chapter 7 got its national pulpit: the singing commercial, the sponsored program (whole shows were named for their sponsors), the brand jingle, all invented here. It was also where national politics changed: the 1924 conventions were the first broadcast, and Coolidge's flat, dry, unemotional radio voice turned out to suit the new medium far better than the booming open-air oratory of the old stump speakers, an early sign that the medium would start choosing its politicians.

The other engine of the new mass culture was already running in a Los Angeles suburb. By the early twenties the American film industry had consolidated into the **studio system**: a handful of large companies, many of them founded by Eastern European Jewish immigrants who had been shut out of older American industries and built a new one (a fact worth stating plainly, because it becomes a target of the nativism of Chapters 9 and 10), that controlled production, distribution, *and* the theaters, an integrated structure that let them dominate from the script to the ticket booth. Movies became the country's fourth-largest industry and, more than that, its dominant storyteller: by the end of the decade weekly movie attendance ran into the many tens of millions (the figures most often cited put it well over half the population going every week, though the estimates vary widely), in a building unlike anything most of them had at home. The lavish **movie palace**, the largest of them seating six thousand under marble and gilt, turned moviegoing into an opulent mass ritual, a cathedral of the new religion of Chapter 7.

The same machine that made the modern celebrity also made the modern celebrity scandal, and the scandals matter here mostly for what they triggered. The comedian Roscoe "Fatty" Arbuckle was tried three times in 1921–22 over a woman's death at a party (the third jury acquitted him and apologized; his career was destroyed anyway), the template for the celebrity-destroying media frenzy. When the romantic idol **Rudolph Valentino**, an Italian immigrant, died suddenly in 1926 at thirty-one, the public grief was genuinely enormous, a crowd estimated in the tens of thousands mobbed the New York funeral home and the press reported scenes of mass hysteria, the lurid details of which (riots, fan suicides) grew in the retelling and are hard to verify, but the phenomenon, mass mourning for a stranger known only through media, was real and new. The studios, frightened that this drumbeat of scandal would bring government censorship down on them, censored themselves first: in 1922 they hired the Republican politician Will Hays to police screen morality, and his office grew into the **Production Code** that dictated what American movies could show until the 1960s. So the decade's culture war (Chapters 10 and 12) ran inside the movies too, the moralists quietly winning the screen even as they lost the cities, which is the same split the chapter's ending turns on.

And then, in October 1927, the medium ate its own silent self. Warner Bros. released ***The Jazz Singer***, the breakthrough feature with synchronized singing and a few lines of spoken dialogue, the most quoted of them Al Jolson's ad-libbed "you ain't heard nothin' yet" (earlier shorts and the 1926 *Don Juan* had carried synchronized sound, so the precise "first words" claim is contested; what is not is that *The Jazz Singer* is the film that killed the silent era). Within about three years the silent film, a global, wordless art form that had let a Chaplin be understood in any country on earth, was extinct. It is a clean, brutal case of a new technology destroying a mature art form in a few years, and it carries an emblem the brand version never mentions: the headline performance of the first sound feature was a white star, Jolson, in **blackface**, performing the minstrel caricature of Black music and Black people. Blackface minstrelsy was not a 1920s invention. It was the most popular form of American mass entertainment before the Civil War, part of the cultural apparatus that had manufactured the fiction of the contented slave (the antebellum and Reconstruction narratives trace it), and it had survived emancipation entirely intact. What the twenties did was put it on the new national machines: *The Jazz Singer* on the movie screen and network radio's first national smash, *Amos 'n' Andy* (a comedy built on two white performers voicing exaggerated Black "dialect" characters), broadcast the same demeaning imitation, now to the whole country at once, of the very Black culture that, uptown in Harlem, was producing the decade's most original and lasting art (Chapter 13). The broadcast nation was born; whose voice it carried, and whose it imitated and sold, is one of the things this decade was fighting about.

# Chapter 9 — The Door Closes

For roughly a century the United States had kept its door, for Europeans, essentially open. There were exceptions, and they had been racial from the start (the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 had already barred Chinese laborers), but the basic fact of the country, the thing it told itself about itself, was that it took people in. About 25 million had come between 1880 and the World War. In a four-year span in the 1920s, Congress slammed the door, and, this is the part the brand version of the decade omits entirely, it did so explicitly, openly, on stated grounds of race and national origin, with the science of the day cited in the committee reports. The immigration restriction of the twenties is not a shameful subtext you have to read between the lines for. It is text.

The fuel was the **nativism** of Chapter 3's aftermath: the postwar fusion of anti-immigrant, anti-Catholic, anti-Jewish, anti-radical, and anti-Black feeling into a single movement that called itself **"100% Americanism."** Define that phrase, because it is the decade's organizing political emotion outside the cities and it powers this chapter and the next two. "100% Americanism" held that there was one real American type, white, Protestant, of northern or western European "old stock", and that everyone else (the Catholic and Jewish immigrants of the new wave, the radicals, Black Americans, eventually the wets and the evolutionists) was a diluting, disloyal, or dangerous contaminant of it. It is a politics of purity and of grievance, and it ran the Klan (Chapter 10), much of Prohibition's enforcement coalition (Chapter 11), the fundamentalist side of the culture war (Chapter 12), and, most consequentially in law, immigration restriction. This is not the first American instance of the form. The Antebellum America narrative covers its direct ancestor, the Know-Nothings of the 1850s, a mass anti-Catholic, anti-immigrant secret order with rituals, lodges, and real electoral power at the state level, which rose during the Irish and German wave and collapsed only when the larger crisis of slavery pulled the floor out from under it. The pattern recurs almost exactly seventy years later, a rapid immigration wave, a surge of Protestant anxiety, a mass fraternal organization with secret rituals and state-level power, and a sudden collapse when a bigger scandal hits (Chapter 10). The second Klan is the Know-Nothing structure running a second time.

The legal mechanism was the **quota**, and the way the quota was designed is the whole point, so follow it. The first move, the **Emergency Quota Act of 1921**, capped immigration from each country at 3 percent of the number of people of that nationality already living in the U.S. as of the **1910** census. Tying the quota to the existing population mathematically favored the groups already numerous and disfavored the newer arrivals, but the restrictionists thought 1910 was still too generous, because the new-wave immigrants had already been arriving in force by then. So the permanent law, the **Immigration Act of 1924** (the **Johnson–Reed Act**), did two things. It cut the percentage to 2 percent, and, the decisive move, it pegged the quota to the **1890** census, deliberately reaching back to a year *before* the great southern- and eastern-European wave, so that the "national origins" of the 1890 population (overwhelmingly British, German, Irish, Scandinavian) would set the quotas and the Italians, Poles, Greeks, Russians, and Jews of the new wave would be cut to a trickle. The arithmetic was the policy. The 1924 act's quota gave Great Britain and Germany the lion's share and gave Italy a few thousand a year against the hundreds of thousands who had been coming. And the act's "aliens ineligible to citizenship" clause barred essentially all immigration from Japan and the rest of Asia outright. The clause worked through an older racial law: a 1790 statute had limited naturalization to "free white persons," and a pair of 1920s Supreme Court rulings held that Japanese and South Asian immigrants did not legally count as white and so could never become citizens, which the 1924 act then used as the trigger to bar them from entering at all. It extended the 1882 Chinese exclusion to the whole continent, an insult that poisoned U.S.–Japan relations for a generation (Japanese Economic Miracle traces where that road eventually led). The full **National Origins Formula** took final effect in 1929, fixing total immigration at roughly 150,000 a year. Anchor that against what it replaced: in 1921 alone over 800,000 immigrants had entered, so the new ceiling cut the flow by something like four-fifths and was deliberately apportioned to keep America's ethnic composition frozen at 1890's. It governed who could enter the United States until 1965.

The intellectual cover for this was a science that was entirely mainstream in the twenties and is one of the decade's hardest things to look at squarely: **eugenics**. Eugenics was the belief, taught in universities and high-school biology textbooks, endorsed by respectable scientists, foundations, and politicians, and built into the immigration debate as expert testimony, that human worth was substantially hereditary, that some "stocks" were genetically superior to others, and that the state could and should improve the population by encouraging the "fit" to breed and preventing the "unfit." It was not a fringe. Its prestige is exactly why the House and Senate immigration committees, the congressional panels that drafted the quota laws, could put eugenicists in the witness chair as expert authorities, and why, in 1927, the United States Supreme Court itself, in *Buck v. Bell*, upheld the forced sterilization of a young woman deemed "feebleminded," with Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes writing the sentence that should be carved over the decade's self-image: "Three generations of imbeciles are enough." Roughly sixty thousand Americans would be forcibly sterilized under such laws across the century, the practice accelerating after the *Buck* decision blessed it; the ruling has never been formally overturned. This needs saying without softening, and it also needs the specific historian's note this book owes you: it sounds, to a modern reader, like the most extreme possible projection backward of later horrors, and the natural suspicion is that the text is exaggerating for effect. It is not. The quotation is verbatim from the Supreme Court's opinion, the law was real, the sterilizations happened, and the connection drawn at the time between American eugenics and immigration policy was explicit and proud, not hidden. The reader's instinct to disbelieve it is itself a measure of how thoroughly the decade's nostalgia has done its editing.

One countercurrent runs the other way and belongs here for honesty's sake, though it was less an act of generosity than a closing of a different loose end. The **Indian Citizenship Act of 1924** finally granted U.S. citizenship to all Native Americans born in the country, many of whom had been excluded from it for the entire history of the republic. It is real and it mattered, and it needs its own asterisk in the same breath: several states promptly found other ways to keep Native people from actually voting, in some cases for decades, by the same poll-tax and residency tricks used against Black Southerners. The same Congress that was racially sorting the world's immigrants also, almost in passing, admitted the continent's original inhabitants to a citizenship many states would then quietly hollow out, which is, in miniature, the whole pattern of rights in this decade: granted on paper, qualified in practice, and never quite for everyone at once.

# Chapter 10 — The Invisible Empire

In August 1925, somewhere between 30,000 and 50,000 men in white hoods and robes marched in formation down Pennsylvania Avenue in Washington, D.C., past the Capitol and the federal buildings, in daylight, openly, as a show of strength. They were the **Ku Klux Klan**, and the single most important thing to understand about them, the thing the brand version of the twenties either omits or files under "the South", is that this was a *national* organization at the peak of a *national* mass movement, and the men marching were as likely to be from Indiana, Oregon, or Colorado as from Georgia.

This requires care, because there were three different Klans in American history and conflating them muddles everything. The *first* Klan was the Reconstruction-era terrorist insurgency of the 1860s–70s, and the crucial point the Reconstruction narrative establishes is that it did not merely "dissolve", it *won*: its murder campaign against Black voters and officeholders helped overthrow the multiracial democracy of Reconstruction, after which it could stand down because its work was done. The *third* Klan is the small, violent, segregationist resistance to the civil-rights movement of the 1950s–60s (the later narrative in this chain). The Klan of *this* book is the **second Klan**, a different kind of animal from either. It was refounded at a 1915 ceremony on Stone Mountain, Georgia, on cultural ground already prepared: the same year, the film *The Birth of a Nation* had rewritten the first Klan as heroic saviors of the white South, and it was wildly popular because it was a technical landmark of early cinema, was marketed as a prestige event with orchestral scores in major theaters, and was endorsed at the highest level (it was screened at the White House). It did not invent that flattering story; it dramatized the version of Reconstruction that the Lost Cause and the academic Dunning School had already installed in textbooks and respectable opinion, the version the Reconstruction narrative dismantles. The second Klan grew in exactly that soil, and after 1920 a pair of professional promoters, Edward Young Clarke and Elizabeth Tyler, who took a cut of every membership fee and ran the recruiting like a sales business, turned it into a slick, mass-marketed, multi-million-member national fraternal organization.

Its hatreds were broader than the first Klan's. The second Klan was anti-Black, yes, but its day-to-day politics in the North and Midwest, where most of its members were, were at least as much anti-Catholic, anti-Jewish, and anti-immigrant, it was, in essence, "100% Americanism" (Chapter 9) with a costume, a fee schedule, and a national sales force. It presented itself to its millions of members not primarily as a lynching society but as a Protestant fraternal lodge, a temperance enforcer (it loved Prohibition and saw itself as its vigilante arm, Chapter 11), a defender of "traditional morality" against the flappers and the movies and the speakeasies, and a booster of "old-stock" Americans against the Catholic and Jewish newcomers, all of which made it socially respectable in a way the first and third Klans were not. Its violence was real, especially against Black and Catholic targets, but its *mass* appeal in the twenties was as a movement of cultural grievance and "decency" for ordinary white Protestants who felt the modern, urban, immigrant, wet, jazz-playing America of this very book was taking their country away from them. Which is to say: the Klan is not a digression from the story of the Roaring Twenties. It is the backlash half of the decade organized into a membership organization, and it has to be told beside the jazz, not after it.

How big was it? The honest answer includes the uncertainty: the Klan lied about its numbers and the records were deliberately destroyed, so estimates of peak membership range roughly from three to six million, with four to five million the usual figure, meaning, at the mid-decade peak around 1924–25, something like one in every fifteen or twenty native-born white Protestant adult men in the country. Numbers alone can make the second Klan sound like a respectable lodge that just happened to be huge, which is the mistake; the violence was real and worth showing rather than only stating. In 1921, in Mer Rouge, Louisiana, two men who had spoken against the local Klan were seized by hooded men, tortured, and murdered, their crushed bodies surfacing in a lake months later; the state could not get a conviction because the Klan controlled the local courts. Across the decade the organization ran floggings, tar-and-featherings, cross-burnings as terror, and economic boycotts against Black, Catholic, Jewish, and "immoral" targets, mostly in places where, as at Mer Rouge, no jury would convict it. That is the thing the membership table leaves out.

It converted the membership into raw political power, especially at the state level. It effectively controlled the government of **Indiana**, elected backed governors and senators across the Midwest and West, ran much of the politics of **Oregon** and **Colorado**, and was strong enough nationally to shape a presidential nominating convention. A party's national convention adopts a *platform*, the official statement of what the party stands for, voted on by the assembled state delegates; at the 1924 Democratic convention in New York (so deadlocked it took a record 103 ballots over two weeks to pick a nominee), a proposed plank that merely *named and condemned the Klan* was fought over furiously and defeated by a margin of about one vote out of more than a thousand fractional delegate votes. Read that twice. In 1924 one of the two major American political parties could not bring itself, by the narrowest possible margin, to officially disapprove of the Ku Klux Klan, because too many delegates either belonged to it or feared its voters. This was not a sudden anomaly. The Reconstruction and Civil Rights narratives show the same party's complicity with organized white-supremacist violence running in an unbroken line from the 1870s Redemption paramilitaries through 1924 and on into the 1950s; the convention vote is one data point on a fifty-year curve, not a freak.

And then it collapsed almost as fast as it had risen, and the way it collapsed is a parable. **D. C. Stephenson** held the title of Grand Dragon, the Klan's chief officer for Indiana and several neighboring states, and he was the second Klan in microcosm: he had built a personal political machine that elected the governor and much of the legislature, he posed as the guardian of white Protestant womanhood and public morality, and he was, privately, a violent predator. In 1925 he abducted and savagely assaulted a young woman named Madge Oberholtzer; she took poison, lingered, gave a detailed deathbed statement, and died of the combined injuries and poison. Stephenson, to whom popular history attaches the boast "I am the law in Indiana" (a line that fits him exactly and is repeated everywhere, though its sourcing is journalistic rather than documented, so it is best read as emblem rather than transcript), was convicted of second-degree murder in November 1925. The conviction shattered the moral pretense the whole organization had been sold on, and when Stephenson, feeling abandoned by the politicians he had bought, began handing prosecutors his lists of bribed officials, the political machine came apart too. Membership, already softening, collapsed within about two years; by the end of the decade the second Klan was a husk. Its sudden disappearance is one reason the decade could later be remembered as roaring rather than seething: the largest hate movement in the country's peacetime history rose, briefly ran the politics of several states and nearly captured a national party, and then imploded so completely, on a single sordid crime, that the nostalgic memory of the twenties could simply leave it out. This book does not leave it out, because it was, for several years, as much "the twenties" as the Charleston was.

# Chapter 11 — The Great Thirst

On January 17, 1920, the United States did something no other major modern country has matched in scope: it wrote a total, nationwide ban on the manufacture, sale, and transport of alcoholic beverages into its Constitution. The comparison is what makes the choice strange rather than inevitable. Several countries had the same Protestant temperance movement and acted on it, but none went this far. Britain tightened licensing and pub hours; Canada ran prohibition province by province and mostly abandoned it within a few years; the Nordic countries used punishing taxes and a state liquor monopoly, and Sweden settled on individual rationing books rather than an outright ban. America alone reached for the most drastic instrument available, a constitutional amendment binding the whole country at once, which is exactly why it became the textbook case. The **Eighteenth Amendment** is the only amendment in American history that took a personal freedom away rather than securing one, and it is the only one that was ever repealed. The thirteen years it lasted are the single best illustration in this book of a recurring twenties truth: a law passed to make the country more moral, virtuous, and orderly instead handed a multi-billion-dollar industry to criminals, corrupted the police and the courts that were supposed to stop them, and made the lawbreaking glamorous.

First, how a whole country bans drinking, because the mechanism explains a lot. Prohibition was not imposed by a tipsy majority changing its mind one morning; it was the final victory of the most effective single-issue pressure group in American history to that point, the **Anti-Saloon League**. The League's innovation, studied by political scientists ever since, was to ignore everything except the one issue, endorse or destroy candidates of either party solely on their "wet" or "dry" votes, and build an interdenominational Protestant church network into a disciplined voting machine. It rode a genuine and not-crazy reform impulse (the pre-Prohibition saloon really was, often, a squalid and exploitative institution, and drink really did wreck families in an era with no social safety net), fused it with rural Protestant suspicion of the Catholic, immigrant, urban America where the saloons mostly were, and got the World War as a final gift: anti-German feeling could be turned against German-American brewers (Pabst, Schlitz, Anheuser-Busch), and grain was needed for the war, not for beer. The amendment passed; the **Volstead Act**, the law that defined and enforced it, set the threshold of "intoxicating" absurdly low (anything over one-half of one percent alcohol) and then funded enforcement so feebly that for a country of 120 million people stretching across a continent the entire federal Prohibition force never amounted to more than a couple of thousand agents at a time, paid little enough that bribing them was trivial.

What followed is almost a controlled experiment in the limits of law. Prohibition did reduce overall drinking, especially at first and especially among the poor who could not afford bootleg prices, and this is the part the "Prohibition was a total failure" cliché gets wrong and should be said honestly. But it did not stop drinking; it drove it underground and made it criminal, profitable, and chic. The **speakeasy**, an illegal bar entered by password ("speak easy" at the door), replaced the saloon, and there were more of them than there had been saloons. The often-quoted figure for New York City, "somewhere between 30,000 and 100,000," really means nobody knew: the lower end is the Prohibition Bureau's own guess and the upper end is press exaggeration, and the honest takeaway is only that there were a great many. An entire illegal supply chain materialized to feed them. Ships anchored on **"Rum Row"** just outside the three-mile limit of U.S. jurisdiction and offloaded liquor onto fast boats; the Detroit "Purple Gang" ran Canadian whiskey across the river; rural Americans distilled "moonshine" and "bathtub gin." Some of it was lethal, and not always by accident: industrial alcohol is legally manufactured for solvents and fuel, and to stop bootleggers from rinsing it and selling it as drink the federal government ordered it "denatured", deliberately adulterated with poisons such as methanol, which causes blindness and death, and bootleggers redistilled it imperfectly anyway, so the government knowingly presided over the poisoning of thousands of its own citizens, a fact the jolly-speakeasy brand never includes. And because the souped-up car built to outrun federal agents on back roads became a Southern subculture of its own, stock-car bootleg running is one of the documented roots (though NASCAR's own promotion has burnished the legend) of what became NASCAR, the stock-car racing circuit that is now one of the most-watched spectator sports in the United States. A law against a drink rebuilt the country's relationship to cars, courts, and crime.

The decisive consequence, the one that outlived Prohibition itself, was **organized crime**. Before Prohibition, criminal gangs were mostly local, small, and limited to gambling, extortion, and vice. Prohibition handed them a product with insatiable demand, no legal competitor, and colossal margins, and to exploit it they had to build something new: large, coordinated, business-like criminal organizations that ran manufacturing, smuggling, distribution, and the systematic bribery of police, prosecutors, judges, and city governments necessary to protect all of it. The modern American crime syndicate was, quite precisely, financed and organized by twenties bootleg money. Its emblem is **Al Capone**, who took over the Chicago "Outfit" in 1925 and built a bootlegging, gambling, and vice empire grossing, by common estimate, around $60 million a year, while effectively owning Chicago's police and many of its politicians. Capone was not a hidden figure; he was a celebrity, gave interviews, sat at ballgames, and styled himself a businessman serving a public that wanted what he sold, which in a sense he was. The gang wars over territory were vicious, and the one that turned the public decisively against him was the **St. Valentine's Day Massacre** of February 14, 1929: Capone gunmen, some dressed as police, lined seven members of a rival gang against a Chicago garage wall and machine-gunned them. The shock of it (and of the realization that men disguised as the law were doing the killing) was a turning point in public opinion. And the way Capone was finally brought down is the perfect closing irony of Prohibition: not for murder, not for bootlegging, not for owning a city, but, in 1931, for failing to file an income-tax return on the illegal millions, the one law a bootlegger could not buy, intimidate, or shoot his way around.

By the mid-twenties the failure was visible to anyone honest. Juries in wet cities routinely refused to convict; respectable people drank openly in defiance; the poisoned-alcohol deaths mounted; the corruption was a national scandal; and a counter-movement was forming, including, devastatingly for the dry argument, Pauline Sabin's Women's Organization for National Prohibition Reform, which blew up the claim that Prohibition was what women wanted. The dry coalition kept hardening the law as its support drained, passing the 1929 Jones Law that turned even first-offense bootlegging into a felony (a serious crime carrying more than a year in prison, as opposed to a minor "misdemeanor"), now punishable by five years. And in 1929 Hoover appointed the Wickersham Commission to study enforcement. Its 1931 report is the episode's perfect punchline and deserves to be said plainly: after exhaustively documenting that Prohibition was unenforceable and failing, the commission recommended that it be kept anyway, several commissioners attaching individual notes that contradicted the official conclusion. Even the body convened to defend the law could not honestly defend it and could not bring itself to say so. It was repealed in 1933, by the Twenty-first Amendment, in the depths of the Depression, partly because a desperate government wanted the liquor tax revenue. But the part of Prohibition that did not get repealed is the part that matters most going forward: it had built the modern crime syndicate, normalized the wholesale bribery of police, prosecutors, and city halls, and taught a generation of Americans that the law itself could be a racket. That machinery did not vanish with the saloons. It went into gambling, labor, and politics, and it is one of the things the country carried, fully assembled, out of the twenties and into everything that came after.

# Chapter 12 — God and the Monkey

In July 1925, in a small town in eastern Tennessee, a young schoolteacher was put on trial for the crime of teaching that human beings evolved from earlier animals, though, as it happens, whether he had actually taught the lesson at all was never really the point and is still debated; the trial was arranged. The **Scopes trial** lasted eight days, was carried live on the radio (the first American trial broadcast nationally), drew the most famous lawyer and the most famous orator in the country to a sweltering courthouse, and settled, legally, almost nothing. It mattered because it was the moment the decade's deepest argument, the one underneath the Klan and Prohibition and the immigration quotas, was staged in the open, in front of the whole listening country, as a single fight between two old men: the argument over whether modern America, with its science and its cities and its relativism and its jazz, was progress or apostasy.

Set the religious scene first, because the reader needs the terms. American Protestantism in the early twentieth century was splitting along a fault that the decade widened into a canyon. On one side were the **modernists**, who held that Christian faith could and must be reconciled with modern science and modern scholarship, that the Bible could be read as morally true without being literally and scientifically accurate, and that evolution and an old earth were simply facts a thinking Christian accommodated. On the other side were the **fundamentalists**, a word that comes from a series of cheap doctrinal pamphlets, "tracts," short, free, mass-printed booklets of religious argument, called *The Fundamentals*, funded by two wealthy oilmen and mailed by the millions to ministers and seminarians across the country in the 1910s, which insisted on a set of non-negotiable doctrines, above all the literal, inerrant truth of the Bible: the world created as Genesis says, the virgin birth, the physical resurrection, miracles as historical events. Fundamentalism in the twenties was not a fringe and was not stupid; it was a large, organized, intellectually serious (on its own premises) reaction by millions of mostly rural and small-town Protestants who experienced the modern, urban, scientific, secular culture of this entire book as a direct assault on the truths their lives were built on. The **fundamentalist–modernist controversy** tore through the major denominations all decade, worst among the Presbyterians and Baptists. (A denomination is an organized Protestant church body with its own governance, doctrine, and clergy, Presbyterian, Baptist, Methodist, and so on; "which denomination" mattered because the fight was over which of these large institutions, and their colleges and pulpits, the modernists or the fundamentalists would control.) Evolution in the public schools became its battlefield because the public school was where the state itself decided which account of human origins every child would be taught, which made it, unavoidably, a fight over whose worldview the government would endorse.

Tennessee fired the first legal shot with the **Butler Act** of March 1925, which made it a crime to teach in a public school "any theory that denies the story of the divine creation of man as taught in the Bible." The American Civil Liberties Union (founded in the Red Scare, Chapter 3) advertised for a teacher willing to be a test case. Boosters in the town of Dayton, openly hoping the publicity would put their fading town on the map, recruited a young, mild substitute science teacher and football coach named **John Scopes**, who agreed to be the defendant precisely because there was little risk: everyone, including Scopes, understood the case to be a staged test of the law, the penalty was a small fine, and the ACLU had promised to pay for his defense, so being prosecuted cost him almost nothing and made him briefly famous. Then the case caught fire far beyond anyone's plan, because of the two men who came to argue it. For the prosecution: **William Jennings Bryan**, three-time Democratic presidential nominee, former Secretary of State, the greatest popular orator in America for thirty years, a sincere Christian and economic progressive who had come to see Darwinism as the cruel ideology behind the eugenics of Chapter 9 and the social-Darwinist defense of the very business inequality he had spent his life fighting. (That is the part the famous version flattens: Bryan's anti-evolutionism was tangled up with a genuine moral horror at how "survival of the fittest" was being used to justify both racial pseudoscience and unrestrained capitalism, which does not make him right about biology but does make him something more interesting than a buffoon.) For the defense: **Clarence Darrow**, the most famous courtroom lawyer in the country, a militant agnostic who, the year before, had saved Nathan Leopold and Richard Loeb, two wealthy Chicago university students who had murdered a fourteen-year-old boy in 1924 to prove they could commit a perfect crime, from the gallows in a sensational trial, and who came to Dayton explicitly to put fundamentalism itself on trial.

The trial's substance was thin (Scopes had clearly taught evolution; the law clearly forbade it; he was always going to be convicted, and was, with a token hundred-dollar fine later thrown out on a technicality). The trial's theater was historic, and it peaked when the judge would not let Darrow call scientists as witnesses and Darrow, improvising, called Bryan himself to the stand as an expert on the Bible. Bryan, vain and unable to resist the platform, accepted. Under Darrow's questioning, in front of the national radio audience, Bryan was pushed into defending biblical literalism in detail (where did Cain get his wife; was the whale literal; was the earth made in six twenty-four-hour days) and grew confused, contradictory, and angry. This is the moment the legend was minted, and it is worth watching it being minted rather than simply repeating it. The urban press, led by **H. L. Mencken**, the ferociously witty Baltimore newspaper columnist and magazine editor who was the most quoted American writer of the decade and made a career out of mocking what he saw as American provincialism, filed copy treating the trial as civilization's war on the "yokels" of what he was popularizing as the "Bible Belt", and wrote the story as a total modernist rout and made Dayton a national shorthand for rural idiocy. That is the version that hardened into memory, and it is, crucially, the *winners'* version, written in real time by the side it flattered. Hold it at arm's length, because the next paragraph is going to take it apart. Five days after the trial ended, still in Dayton, Bryan died in his sleep, and his death was widely read, by the same press, as the symbolic death of agrarian Protestant America itself, which made the legend feel complete and was wrong.

But here is where the brand version, the *Inherit the Wind* version, is most misleading, and the chapter has to say so. The modernists won the *press* and lost the *country*. Scopes's conviction stood. Tennessee's law stayed on the books until 1967. Far from being discredited, anti-evolution laws spread: Mississippi passed one in 1926, Arkansas in 1928, and, more quietly and more effectively, the major textbook publishers, frightened of the Southern market, simply removed or muted Darwin from American high-school biology books for decades. The fundamentalists did not vanish in humiliation in 1925; they withdrew from the prestige institutions that had mocked them and spent the next half-century building their own, parallel network of churches, colleges, publishers, and, crucially, broadcasters, the new technology of Chapter 8 turned to the old-time religion. The era's pioneering religious broadcaster was the Los Angeles revivalist **Aimee Semple McPherson**, who built an enormous temple seating well over five thousand and one of the first radio ministries, and who became a national tabloid sensation in 1926 when she vanished for weeks, reappeared with a story of having been kidnapped that almost nobody believed (a grand jury investigated, the evidence was contradictory, no charges ultimately stuck, and the truth was never established), and emerged, scandal and all, more famous than before, which was itself the lesson about broadcasting and celebrity religion. The marriage of mass broadcasting and revivalism that would shape American religion and politics for the rest of the century was performed in this decade. Scopes is remembered as the moment science laughed fundamentalism out of the room. What actually happened is that fundamentalism walked out of that room, built its own, and came back later, larger. The decade's culture war did not end at Dayton. It went underground, exactly like the liquor, and for similar reasons.

# Chapter 13 — The New Negro and the New Sound

The most lasting thing the Roaring Twenties made was not the Chrysler Building or the Model A or *The Great Gatsby*. It was a body of music and writing created, in a few square miles of upper Manhattan and on the South Side of Chicago, by exactly the Americans the rest of this book has shown the decade's politics trying to suppress. The supreme irony of the twenties is that its permanent cultural legacy was disproportionately Black, made by the children of the people Jim Crow had built a regime to keep down and the Klan had built a movement to terrorize, and that white America consumed it ravenously while refusing its makers a seat at the table, sometimes literally.

It starts with a migration, and the migration starts in the unpaid bill of Chapter 5. The reason Black Southerners were still trapped in sharecropping in 1916, with no land and no capital to cushion them, is that Reconstruction's promise of land was made in 1865 and broken within months; the migration is that sixty-year-old failure finally forcing people onto the road. The push was Jim Crow, debt, and lynching; the pull came when the World War and then the 1924 immigration restriction (Chapter 9) choked off the European immigrant labor Northern factories had run on, opening industrial jobs to Black workers at scale, and when the Black press, above all the *Chicago Defender*, advertised the North as a promised land. The *Defender* had to be smuggled because white planters and many Southern towns banned or confiscated it and threatened the Black agents who distributed it, precisely because it told sharecroppers there was somewhere else to go; Pullman porters (Black railroad workers, of whom more below) carried bundles of it south and dropped them along the line to be passed hand to hand. The result was the **Great Migration**: between roughly 1916 and 1930, on the conservative estimates more than 1.5 million and by higher counts well over that, Black Americans left the rural South for the cities of the North and Midwest, the largest internal migration in American history to that point. It did not deliver the promised land, residential segregation, hostile white neighbors, the Red Summer (Chapter 3), and worse pay for worse jobs were waiting, but it concentrated, in Harlem and Chicago's "Black Belt" and Detroit, hundreds of thousands of Black Americans with wages, institutions, newspapers, churches, and audiences, at a measure of distance from the Southern terror, and out of that concentration came an explosion.

That explosion had a literary and a political wing and a musical one, and they were not equal in reach. The writing and the organizing concentrated and trained a leadership; the music is what conquered the world, so the music is where this chapter is going to spend its weight. But the supporting cast has to be on the stage first, briefly and in order.

The literary wing is usually called the **Harlem Renaissance**. Its manifesto was *The New Negro*, a 1925 anthology assembled by Alain Locke, a Howard University philosophy professor and the first Black American Rhodes Scholar, whose authority and connections made him the movement's impresario; it argued that a new generation of Black Americans, urban, assertive, and unwilling to ask for white approval, was remaking American culture from Harlem outward. Its defining poet, **Langston Hughes**, insisted that Black artists draw without apology on jazz, the blues, and Black speech rather than imitate white models; **Zora Neale Hurston**, trained as an anthropologist, salvaged the rural Southern folklore the migration was leaving behind; the Jamaican-born **Claude McKay** had given the movement its angriest early voice in "If We Must Die," written in the blood of the Red Summer. The political wing, which the prettified version of "the Harlem Renaissance" tends to drop, ran in parallel. **Marcus Garvey**'s Universal Negro Improvement Association built the largest Black mass movement in American history on racial pride, economic self-help, and a return to Africa (its 1920 New York convention drew a crowd the UNIA put at 25,000, a figure that comes mostly from the movement's own claims and is probably high), before Garvey was convicted of mail fraud tied to his steamship line and deported. The **NAACP** ran the long campaign against lynching, pushing an anti-lynching bill through the House in 1922 only to watch Southern senators kill it with a **filibuster**: in the Senate a senator who has the floor can keep talking, literally holding it by refusing to yield, and unless a supermajority votes to force an end to debate the bill simply never comes to a vote, so a determined Southern minority used it to strangle every federal anti-lynching measure for the next forty years. That same weapon, the Southern bloc's filibuster, is the obstacle the movement finally breaks in 1964, when the cloture votes end the longest filibuster in Senate history and the Civil Rights Act passes (the Civil Rights Era narrative, Ch 10). That weapon was not new in 1922; the Reconstruction narrative shows the same Senate machinery being used to block federal protection of Black citizens since the 1870s, which is the point, the 1922 defeat was the continuation of a fifty-year system, not a fresh failure. And in 1925 **A. Philip Randolph** founded the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, organizing the all-Black workforce that made up the sleeping-car attendants of the Pullman Company (the firm that built and operated the nation's railroad sleeping cars and was the largest single employer of Black men in America, which is why a union of porters was both possible and explosive) into the first Black-led union to win a major contract. Mark that name and that year: the Civil Rights Era narrative identifies this same Randolph, sixteen years later, as the man whose threatened 1941 mass march on Washington forced the first federal order against employment discrimination (Civil Rights Era narrative, Ch 1), and again in 1963 as the organizer of the March on Washington (Ch 9), making him the one figure whose career directly links the Harlem Renaissance, the New Deal, and the Second Reconstruction. The pipeline runs straight from the 1925 porters' union to that.

Now the spine. The musical wing reached incomparably more people and traveled incomparably faster than the books or the rallies, because it had the two new machines of Chapter 8 to carry it, the phonograph record and the radio, and because one man turned a regional folk music into a fine art almost single-handedly. **Jazz** had come up the river out of New Orleans's Black musical world to Chicago and then everywhere, and the figure who, more than any other, remade what it could be was **Louis Armstrong**. The numbers in "Hot Five" and "Hot Seven" refer literally to the size of the band, the small studio groups Armstrong led on a run of recordings from roughly 1925 to 1928, and the point is what he did on them: jazz had been a collective music in which a front line of horns improvised together, and Armstrong pulled the improvising soloist out in front of the band as the music's center of gravity, the structural move (a brilliant individual voice taking flight over a supporting group) that organized not only jazz but most American popular music after it. The framing oversimplifies a change many musicians were part of, but no other single player's recordings carried it as far. Around him the rest: **Duke Ellington**'s orchestra began its residency at Harlem's **Cotton Club** in late 1927, and the club's nightly radio wire carried his sound and his name into the entire country; **Bessie Smith**, the "Empress of the Blues," sold records by the hundreds of thousands into the segregated "race records" market (records made by Black artists for Black buyers) and was, by the usual accounts, the best-paid Black performer of the decade. Through records, radio, and the migration that had concentrated the talent in the first place, a music made by the descendants of the enslaved became, inside a single decade, the sound of modern America, and of modern Europe, where "le jazz" took Paris.

And the Cotton Club is the symbol this chapter has to end on, because it is the decade in one room. It was the most famous nightclub in America. It presented the greatest Black musical artists of the age, Ellington's among them, in lavish, often grotesquely "primitive"-themed shows, to an audience that was, by the club's strict policy, whites only. The performers entered through the back. The decade's most original and lasting art was made by Black Americans; the decade's economy and law were structured so that white Americans profited from it and its makers largely did not, and could not even watch it from the good seats. That is the Roaring Twenties with the asterisk attached, and it is the truer sentence than the one with the saxophone and no asterisk. The justice did not come in this decade. What this decade built instead was the base, the porters' union and the NAACP, the migrant cities and the money and the audiences, the confidence of a generation that had made the century's defining American music while being shut out of the room, and that base is precisely what the movement two narratives down this chain would be launched from.

# Chapter 14 — The Lost Generation and the New Woman

The twenties got its other name, "the **Jazz Age**," from a young novelist who was twenty-three when he became famous and who spent the rest of his short life chronicling, and being destroyed by, the decade he had named. **F. Scott Fitzgerald** published *This Side of Paradise* in 1920, became overnight the voice and the mascot of postwar youth, coined "the Jazz Age" for the era, and in 1925 published *The Great Gatsby*, which took the decade's bootleggers, brand-new money, parties, and hollow glamour and turned them into the novel by which the whole period is now most often understood. It is worth a true note that cuts against the brand: *Gatsby* sold modestly in the twenties and Fitzgerald died in 1940 thinking himself a failure; the book became "the great American novel" only afterward, which is itself a lesson about how this decade was remembered versus how it was lived. The point of the Lost Generation writers is that they are the decade's own internal dissent. While the advertising of Chapter 7 told the country it had never been happier, its best young writers were saying, in the country's best prose, that the boom was spiritually empty and the official optimism was a fraud.

"The **Lost Generation**" is the writer Gertrude Stein's phrase, made famous by **Ernest Hemingway**, who used it as an epigraph to *The Sun Also Rises* (1926), the novel that gave the disillusioned, war-scarred postwar generation its portrait and gave American writing a new stripped-down style that influenced it for the rest of the century. Many of these writers physically left, congregating in 1920s Paris (where the dollar was strong, the censorship was light, and the wine was legal) around Gertrude Stein's salon and the bookshop of Sylvia Beach, the expatriate American whose Left Bank shop, Shakespeare and Company, was the expatriates' clubhouse and lending library and who in 1922 published James Joyce's *Ulysses* when no commercial house would touch it: Hemingway, Fitzgerald, the poet Ezra Pound, and the rest. At home, the role of professional skeptic was filled by **H. L. Mencken**, the Baltimore critic of Chapter 12, the most quoted American writer of the decade, who founded *The American Mercury* in 1924 and conducted a daily war on Prohibition, the Klan, fundamentalism, and what he called the "booboisie", the self-satisfied conformist middle class that the novelist Sinclair Lewis had skewered in *Babbitt* (1922), a book that put a new word for that exact type into the language. Across the decade, the people best at using its new mass media to describe it were largely in revolt against it, which is one more reason the decade's self-image and the decade's reality need to be held apart.

The decade's emblem of social change was a young woman, and she needs the same careful handling as everything else in this book. The **flapper**, bobbed hair, hemlines at the knee, cosmetics in public, cigarettes in public, a cocktail in a speakeasy, the Charleston (the frantic dance, popularized by the 1923 Black musical *Runnin' Wild*, that traveled, like the music, from Black culture into the white mainstream), was the decade's image of liberated young womanhood, and there was something real underneath the image. The Nineteenth Amendment (Chapter 2) had enfranchised women; more women than ever worked for wages; the open revolt against Victorian rules about female bodies, behavior, and desire was genuine, and it was a lasting change. But the brand sells the image as the reality, and the reality was narrower. The flapper was largely an urban, young, white, middle-class figure and substantially a media and advertising construction; most American women, especially in the rural South and among the immigrant working class and Black women under Jim Crow, lived nothing like her. The new women in the paid workforce were overwhelmingly funneled into low-paid "pink-collar" jobs, typist, telephone operator, salesgirl, clerk, and most were still expected to quit at marriage. The vote had not delivered economic or legal equality; the Equal Rights Amendment (Chapter 2) introduced in 1923 went nowhere; and the era's contraception movement, led by Margaret Sanger, which genuinely expanded women's control over their lives, was, like much twenties reform, entangled with the eugenics of Chapter 9. The "New Woman" was a real and permanent loosening of one set of constraints, sold by the culture industry as a total liberation that had not, in fact, occurred.

Hold those two facts side by side, because the chapter's real subject is the tension between them. The same decade that produced its own sharpest critics, the writers above, telling the country its prosperity was hollow, also built the most efficient machine yet invented for manufacturing the opposite message: the modern **celebrity**, the famous person produced at industrial scale and national speed by the tabloids, the newsreels, the radio, and the ghostwritten book. The dissenters and the manufactured heroes are not two separate topics; they are the decade arguing with itself, the Lost Generation writing the indictment while the celebrity machine cranked out the rebuttal, and the machine had the bigger circulation. Sport built the prototypes (**Babe Ruth**, whose 60 home runs in 1927 made him the closest thing to a globally famous athlete the world had yet seen and turned baseball into a power spectacle, with Dempsey, Grange, and Jones close behind), and one of them complicates the brand: **Gertrude Ederle**, who in 1926 became the first woman to swim the English Channel and did it nearly two hours faster than any man had, came home to a vast New York parade, the crowd put in the millions by the era's wildly unreliable estimates, for proving a woman could be a national sports hero. (Her contemporary, the heavyweight Jack Dempsey, gave the decade its emblem of the sports *gate*, the total ticket money a fight drew: his 1927 rematch with Gene Tunney, the famous "Long Count" bout, in which a delayed count let a knocked-down Tunney recover and win, grossed somewhere over $2 million, among the first prizefights to do so, the precise "first" depending on where the threshold is set.) The apotheosis of the whole machinery was **Charles Lindbergh**. On May 20–21, 1927, the 25-year-old airmail pilot flew a tiny single-engine plane, the *Spirit of St. Louis*, alone, nonstop, about 3,600 miles (5,800 km) from New York to Paris, in thirty-three and a half hours, with no radio and a periscope because the fuel tank blocked his forward view. He was a genuinely brave man who did a genuinely extraordinary thing, and then the media did to him what they had learned to do: a crowd estimated at around four million, the standard figure, repeated everywhere and impossible to verify, turned out for his New York parade. Lindbergh is the purest case of the decade's last invention, fame itself as an industry, the same radio-tabloid-newsreel machine that sold the cars and the soap, turned now to manufacturing a global hero overnight out of one quiet man and one good flight. The country that could do that to Lindbergh in May 1927 was the same country that, two and a half years later, would discover what else its machinery of confidence could manufacture, and could not.

# Chapter 15 — The Bubble

The decade ended where its psychology had been pointing all along: in a crowd of ordinary people who had borrowed against a future they were certain would arrive, discovering, over about eleven days in October 1929, that it would not. The Crash is the perfect ending to this book not because it caused everything that came after (that is argued, and the chapter will say so) but because it was the moment the decade's organizing belief, that the boom was permanent and the prosperity general, met the arithmetic, and lost.

There was a dress rehearsal, and it is worth a paragraph because it taught the wrong lesson. From about 1923 to 1925, **Florida** had a real-estate mania. Land in and around Miami and the new town of Coral Gables was bought, often sight-unseen, often with a small "binder" down payment, and flipped within weeks at a profit to the next buyer, who flipped it to the next, on the universal assumption that the price could only rise. It was a textbook speculative bubble, the price detached from any use the land could be put to and floating purely on the belief that someone else would pay more tomorrow. It deflated in 1926, and then a catastrophic hurricane in September 1926 killed hundreds and physically destroyed the boom's epicenter. The lesson available was the one American history had already taught at least three times: the Early American Republic and Antebellum America narratives walk through the speculative panics of 1819, 1837, and 1857, each the same arc of boom, easy credit, collapse, and a banking class insisting all the way down that the fundamentals were sound. The 1929 Crash is the fourth run of a known structural failure, not a bolt from the blue. The lesson actually drawn from Florida was narrower and fatal, that *Florida* specifically had been a bad bet, and the speculative money, undeterred, simply moved north to a bigger and apparently sounder market, the New York Stock Exchange.

Into this came the decade's last president. **Herbert Hoover**, the famous engineer-administrator of Chapters 4 and 5, the most admired man in the government, won the 1928 election in a third consecutive Republican landslide, beating the Democrat Al Smith (the first Catholic ever nominated by a major party, against whom the campaign unleashed open anti-Catholic bigotry, the "100% Americanism" of Chapter 9 in a presidential election) on a platform that the boom was not only permanent but nearly complete; accepting the nomination in 1928 he declared, in words reproduced with minor variations across sources but consistent in substance, that America was nearer to the final triumph over poverty than any land had ever been. He took office in March 1929. The crash came in October. The cruel-timing reading, that fate ambushed a decent man, is the easy one and it is the wrong one for this book. Hoover's boosterism was not bad luck; it was the decade's central refusal speaking through its most capable spokesman. For eleven years almost nobody with power and a platform would say the floor might be hollow, because the entire system, the credit, the advertising, the politics, the stock market, depended on no one saying it. Hoover believing and broadcasting that poverty was nearly beaten in the summer of 1929 is not ironic timing; it is the purest possible expression of the thing this whole book has been describing, and he was as much its product as the shoeshine boy with the stock tips.

Now the machine, because the Crash is not a mood, it is a mechanism, and the mechanism is the same one the whole consumer decade ran on, applied to stocks instead of cars: **borrowing against the future**. Two devices did the damage. The first was buying **on margin**. An investor could buy stock by putting down as little as ten percent of the price in cash and borrowing the other ninety percent from the broker, pledging the stock itself as *collateral*, the asset the lender can seize and sell if the loan goes bad. On the way up this multiplied gains spectacularly (put down $1,000, control $10,000 of stock, and a ten percent rise doubles your money). But it built in a trap: if the price fell, the broker issued a "margin call" demanding more cash to cover the loan, and an investor who could not pay had his pledged stock sold out from under him immediately, at whatever the price now was. Broker loans of this kind ran to something on the order of $8.5 billion by 1929 (estimates vary, but the order of magnitude is firm). The second device was the **investment trust**, a company whose only business was owning shares in other companies, often itself bought on borrowed money and often owning yet other trusts. This is *leverage*, the general name for using borrowed money so that a small base of real value controls a much larger pile of assets: it multiplies the return on the way up and the loss on the way down by exactly the same factor, and stacking leveraged trusts on top of leveraged trusts meant a single dollar of genuine value was propping up many dollars of paper claims. Both devices did the identical thing: they magnified gains on the way up and guaranteed forced, cascading, accelerating selling on the way down. The market roughly doubled between early 1928 and September 1929 on this borrowed fuel and on a flood of new amateur investors. "Everybody Ought to Be Rich" ran the title of a 1929 magazine article by a prominent financier; the cliché of the era, true enough to stick, was the shoeshine boy giving stock tips.

A few people said so, and the way they were treated is the chapter's bleakest point. The economist Roger Babson predicted a crash in September 1929 and was publicly mocked. On October 17, 1929, about a week before the collapse began, Yale's Irving Fisher, one of the most celebrated economists in the country, declared that stock prices had reached "what looks like a permanently high plateau", a line that has followed his considerable reputation ever since. The experts who reassured the public were believed; the ones who warned it were dismissed as cranks, which is what almost always happens at the top of a bubble and is exactly why bubbles get as big as they do. The **Federal Reserve** (the central bank of Chapter 1) saw the speculation and was paralyzed by a real dilemma it never resolved in time. Its main tool was the interest rate it charged banks to borrow: raise that rate and borrowing money gets more expensive everywhere, which would have starved the stock speculators of the cheap loans feeding the mania, but the same dearer credit would also choke ordinary business and farming, which were not booming and could not stand it. Caught between popping the bubble and crushing the real economy, the Fed dithered between its regional banks and its Washington board through 1928 and 1929, moving late and indecisively. The debate over whether the Fed could and should have acted differently has never ended; what is not in dispute is that it did not act in time.

The market peaked on September 3, 1929, at a Dow Jones Industrial Average of 381. The Dow is an index, a single running number built from the share prices of a small set of large companies, used as a rough thermometer for the whole stock market; the figure itself is meaningless in isolation (a modern Dow above 40,000 is not "a hundred times bigger," it is a different basket compounded over a century), so what matters is not 381 but how far and how fast it then fell from it. It drifted and slid nervously for seven weeks while almost everyone called it a healthy correction. Then it broke. On **Black Thursday**, October 24, panic selling hit the floor; a pool of leading bankers publicly bought stocks that afternoon to show confidence, and the gesture steadied prices for one weekend. It was the last time confidence worked. On **Black Monday**, October 28, the Dow fell about 13 percent with no rescue in sight. On **Black Tuesday**, October 29, 1929, the dam broke completely: roughly 16 million shares were dumped in the worst single day in the exchange's history, the ticker ran hours behind the actual prices, margin calls forced more selling which forced more margin calls, the leverage of the whole structure now running violently in reverse. Over the worst stretch of October somewhere on the order of tens of billions of dollars in paper wealth evaporated (the figure most often quoted is around $30 billion, and like all such totals it is an estimate of unrealized losses that moves with the dates you pick). Anchor it loosely: it was many times the entire annual federal budget of the day and comparable to the whole cost of the World War to the United States. The decade that had named itself for its own roar ended in eleven days of falling numbers.

Two honest qualifications close the book, because the brand version oversimplifies the ending as badly as it oversimplifies everything else. First: the Crash did not, by itself, cause the **Great Depression**. This is genuinely debated among economists, but the broad consensus is that the Crash was a trigger and a symptom, and that the catastrophe of the 1930s came from deeper structural faults the brand decade had hidden, the farm depression that had run the whole decade (Chapter 5), the "sick industries," the extreme concentration of the decade's income gains at the top, an under-regulated banking system of roughly 25,000 mostly small, undiversified banks with no deposit insurance to stop a panic becoming a collapse, and the unstable circular structure of international war debt. To these the early 1930s would add a set of specific policy failures, the Federal Reserve letting the money supply contract sharply as banks failed in waves, and the 1930 Smoot–Hawley tariff choking world trade, which are named here only so the gap is not a blank, and are the proper subject of the next narrative, not this one. The Crash is where the Roaring Twenties ends; the Depression is where the next story begins, and this book deliberately stops at the cliff edge rather than climbing down into it. Second, and finally: even on the way down, the men in charge would not say it. Hoover and the bankers insisted prosperity was "just around the corner"; the market even rallied somewhat into the spring of 1930 before collapsing for real, taking, this time, the banks and the factories and the farms with it.

That is the Roaring Twenties: a decade that genuinely invented the modern consumer economy, the broadcast nation, the car culture, and the celebrity, and made, in Harlem and Chicago, a body of art that outlived everything else it produced, and that ran, on the exact same calendar, the largest peacetime nativist movement in the country's history, a failed war on alcohol that built organized crime, a racially engineered closing of the nation's doors, and a stock mania it could not bring itself to call a mania until it had ended. The boom was real and it was narrow and it was borrowed, and it contained the Crash, and almost nobody with the power to say so would. The Depression and the world war that followed reshaped American society from top to bottom, and returning Black veterans, the heirs of the migration and the institutions Chapter 13 described, the "Double-V" generation, helped spark the civil-rights movement that would transform the country again. That is the next narrative in this chain, the Civil Rights Era, whose runway opens exactly here, 1929–1954 (Ch 1). This one ends on October 29, 1929, with the ticker running hours behind the truth, which is, in the end, the most accurate possible image of the entire decade.

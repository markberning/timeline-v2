# High Medieval Europe — From the Year 1000 to the Mongol Shock

**Goal:** Narrative for the High Middle Ages of Latin Europe (roughly 1000–1300 CE), the three-century stretch when the institutions of the modern West — the chartered town, the corporate university, the constitutional charter, the international bank, the parliament — were invented in their first recognizable form. Across these three hundred years Europe doubled its population, rebuilt its cities, recovered the lost works of Aristotle through Arabic translation, marched 2,500 miles overland to take Jerusalem, raised cathedrals to heights nothing in northern Europe had ever attempted, and forced a king at Runnymede to put his seal to a document that the rest of the world would still be quoting eight centuries later. The High Middle Ages are also when popes excommunicated emperors and emperors stood barefoot in the snow, when knights from Rouen and Lyon and Cologne ate each other in besieged Antioch, when a Venetian merchant boy named Marco Polo set out for the court of Kublai Khan, and when, in 1241, an entirely different shock arrived from the steppe and very nearly ended the whole project.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

A note on names and pronunciation: the High Middle Ages were polyglot. Latin was the language of the Church and the universities, French the language of the courts and the new vernacular literature, Italian the language of money, German the language of the eastern frontier. Names appear in their most common English form, with phonetic guides on first use for the unfamiliar ones. Dates are CE.

The reader who wants the full sweep can read all three. This volume covers three hundred years that contained more institutional invention per century than any comparable stretch of European history before the Industrial Revolution.

# Chapter 1 — The Year 1000 and the Take-Off

For most of the early Middle Ages, western Europe had been a backwater. The Roman cities had shrunk into provincial market towns or disappeared into farmland. The Roman roads were still there but no one was repairing them. The aqueducts were broken, the public baths were ruins, the great libraries of Antioch and Alexandria and Constantinople were either lost (Alexandria) or out of reach (Constantinople). A Frankish bishop in 700 had to write to Cassiodorus's old monastery in southern Italy to ask for a copy of a basic geometrical text he could not find in his own library. The civilization that had been the western half of the Roman Empire was running on inheritance and not generating much new of its own.

Then, somewhere around the year 1000, something changed.

The change was not a single event. It was the accumulation of small improvements — a heavier plow, a better collar for the draft horse, a third field added to the crop rotation, a watermill on every workable stream — that compounded into a transformation of the European countryside. Population grew. Surplus food fed the cities. The cities grew. New cities were chartered. Long-distance trade revived. Money came back into common use. Stone construction picked up. Latin literacy spread. By 1100, western Europe had been quietly turned into a different kind of place.

This chapter is about the slow undramatic infrastructure layer that made everything else in this book possible. The Crusades, the cathedrals, the universities, Magna Carta — none of it happens without the agricultural revolution that fed the people who built and fought and studied. Civilizations run on calories. The High Middle Ages got more calories per acre than any previous European civilization had ever managed.

---

To understand the take-off, start with the climate.

Between roughly 950 and 1250 CE, average temperatures in northern Europe ran about half a degree to one degree Celsius warmer than the medieval baseline. This sounds small. It was not. A temperature shift of that size, sustained over three centuries, lengthens the growing season at northern latitudes by two to four weeks a year, lifts the elevation at which grain will ripen, reduces the frequency of catastrophic frost, and pushes the boundary of viable agriculture significantly further north and higher into the mountains. This is the **Medieval Warm Period** (the standard name; some climatologists prefer "Medieval Climate Anomaly" because the warmth was real in northern Europe but not globally uniform). Vineyards reached as far north as southern England — wine production at Ely Cathedral in eastern England in 1086 is recorded in the Domesday Book — and rye could be grown in Norway. Greenland was actually green enough in the early 1000s for the Norse to plant farms there (Viking Age Ch 7).

The warming did not cause the take-off, but it provided the floor. Crops that struggled in colder centuries became reliable. Marginal land became productive. Famines, while they still happened, became less frequent and less catastrophic. A peasant in 1100 could expect more reliable harvests than his great-great-grandfather had managed in 900.

This is the kind of background condition historians often skip past on the way to the more glamorous narrative. It deserves attention. The same warming that let the Norse settle Greenland was letting French peasants double-crop their fields in Normandy, German colonists push into eastern Europe with rye and oats, and English shepherds graze sheep in the Scottish borderlands. A few hundred years later, when the climate cooled again at the start of the **Little Ice Age** around 1300, the European agricultural system would be running into hard limits and the demographic boom would stall — but that is the Late Medieval story, not this one.

---

The four technological innovations that powered the agricultural revolution all reached western Europe in usable form between roughly 800 and 1100 CE.

First, the **heavy mouldboard plow**. The light wooden ard plow inherited from the Mediterranean Romans worked fine in the dry sandy soils of southern Europe but barely scratched the wet heavy clay soils that covered most of northern France, the Low Countries, England, and Germany. The heavy plow — iron-shod, with a vertical knife (the coulter) cutting the sod, a horizontal blade (the share) slicing it from below, and a curved board (the mouldboard) turning it over — could break clay soils that had been effectively unfarmable. Once you have the heavy plow, vast tracts of northern Europe become arable for the first time. Forests that had stood since the end of the last Ice Age were cleared. Land area under cultivation expanded by an estimated factor of three to five over the next two centuries.

Second, the **three-field crop rotation**. The older Mediterranean two-field system left half the cropland fallow each year to recover. The three-field system divided the cropland into three: one field of winter wheat or rye (planted in autumn, harvested in summer), one of spring oats or barley (planted in spring, harvested in late summer), one fallow. This raised the productive share of farmland from one half to two thirds and added the spring-planted oats — a crop that could feed both humans and the new draft horses. Three-field rotation spread across northern Europe between 900 and 1200, slowly enough that no single date marks its arrival but cumulatively enough to roughly double the food output of the average farm.

Third, the **rigid horse collar**. The classical world had used the throat-and-girth harness, which let an ox pull a plow by the chest but choked a horse by the windpipe whenever it tried to pull seriously. The rigid padded horse collar — apparently invented in China by the third century CE and transmitted west across the Eurasian steppe over the next several hundred years — let a horse pull from the shoulders, transferring all its weight into the load. Combined with horseshoes (which protected the hooves from soft European mud) and the shoulder yoke for two-horse teams, the new harness made the horse roughly twice as efficient as the ox for plowing and hauling. The horse moves faster, works longer hours, and handles uneven terrain better than the ox. The substitution of horses for oxen across northern Europe between 1000 and 1300 effectively tripled the available draft power on the medieval farm.

Fourth, the **watermill**. The watermill is older than the High Middle Ages — Romans had them, eighth-century monasteries had them — but the diffusion explodes after 1000. The 1086 Domesday survey commissioned by William the Conqueror counted **5,624 watermills** in England alone, roughly one for every fifty households. By 1300 there were more than 10,000 in England and probably 30,000 across France. Watermills were used to grind grain (the original application), full cloth (pounding wet wool to thicken it), saw timber, hammer iron, crush ore, work bellows for furnaces, and run a dozen other industrial processes. The High Medieval watermill was the first widespread non-animal industrial power source in human history. The closest contemporary parallel was the much later Song dynasty's use of water-powered industry in eleventh- and twelfth-century China (Tang-Song China Ch 8), running on a similar logic but applied to silk and iron rather than wool and grain.

To these four core innovations the High Middle Ages added the **post mill** — a wooden windmill mounted on a central post so the entire body could be rotated to face the wind, first attested in Yorkshire and Normandy around 1180 — adding wind power to the mechanical inventory wherever streams ran dry.

The cumulative effect of all this was that a peasant family in 1200 could feed itself on roughly half the land its great-great-grandfather had needed in 900. The other half — and the new land brought under cultivation by the heavy plow — produced the marketable surplus that fed the cities, supported the merchants, paid the masons of the cathedrals, and freed enough young men from farm labor to fill the new universities.

---

The demographic effect was enormous.

Best modern estimates put western Europe's population at roughly 35–40 million around the year 1000 and roughly 70–80 million by 1300. This is a doubling in three centuries. The figures are extrapolations from incomplete tax and church records, hearth counts, and archaeological surveys, and the error bars are wide — but every serious estimate points to the same general magnitude. The High Middle Ages were the most demographically expansive period in European history before the Industrial Revolution.

Where did the extra people go? Some onto newly cleared land — the great forests of Germany, Poland, and Hungary were rolled back by axe and plow over these three centuries. Some east, into the German colonization (the **Ostsiedlung**) of Slavic lands across the Elbe. Some south, into the Christian advance through Iberia (Chapter 8). And, most consequentially for the rest of this book, some into the cities.

The cities of western Europe in 1000 were small. Paris had perhaps 20,000 people. London perhaps 10,000. The largest cities of the early-medieval West were dwarfed by their contemporaries elsewhere — Constantinople (Byzantine Empire Ch 5) at perhaps 400,000, Córdoba in Muslim Spain at over 100,000, Baghdad (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1) at perhaps 800,000 at its eighth-century peak. By 1300 the picture had shifted. Paris had perhaps 200,000 residents, Venice and Milan around 150,000, Florence and Genoa around 100,000. These are still small compared to Hangzhou (Tang-Song China Ch 8) at over a million, but they are the largest western European cities since Rome's fourth-century population peak. And they kept growing.

The cities were also organizationally new. The high-medieval city was not the Roman survival of the early Middle Ages but a chartered self-governing **commune** with its own institutions: an elected council, a defined legal jurisdiction (the famous formula was that "city air makes you free" — a serf who lived in a chartered city for a year and a day was no longer a serf), guilds organizing crafts and trade, taxation by consent. The northern Italian cities went furthest, becoming effectively independent republics by the twelfth century. The German "free imperial cities" — Cologne, Lübeck, Hamburg, Frankfurt, Augsburg — answered only to the emperor and not to any local lord. The Flemish cloth cities (Bruges, Ghent, Ypres) became some of the wealthiest urban centers in Europe. France and England kept tighter royal supervision, but even there the chartered town with elected officers and its own court was the standard model by 1200.

This was something genuinely new. The cities of the ancient world had been governed by the same aristocracies that governed the countryside. The High Medieval city had its own merchant-and-artisan elite that owed nothing to the rural lords. It is one of the structural inventions of the period and the foundation of everything else in the urban story — the merchants of Chapter 4, the masons of Chapter 6, the students of Chapter 7.

---

Cross-civ check: the European take-off of 1000–1100 is one of those moments where it helps to look around the world.

In **China**, the **Song dynasty** had been founded in 960, and the eleventh century saw the most spectacular economic and technological efflorescence in pre-modern world history — printed books in millions of copies, paper money, industrial-scale iron production, the magnetic compass, gunpowder weapons, water-powered textile machinery (Tang-Song China Ch 6 and Ch 8). The Song economy in 1100 was probably four to five times the size of the entire western European economy combined. Most of what western Europe was inventing for the first time, China had had for centuries.

In the **Islamic world**, the eleventh century was the late golden age — al-Ghazali was writing in Baghdad, Avicenna had finished his philosophical and medical synthesis a generation earlier, the great hospitals of Cairo and Damascus were operating, Cordoba in Muslim Spain held more books than every Christian monastery in Europe combined (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4 and Ch 7). The translations that would carry Aristotle back to the Latin West (Chapter 5) were waiting on the shelves of libraries the West had not yet learned to read.

In **India**, the Chola maritime empire was at its peak, conducting naval expeditions across the Bay of Bengal and dominating the trade between South India and Southeast Asia (Medieval India Ch 5). South Indian temple-cities were producing some of the most sophisticated stone architecture in the world, with thousand-pillared halls and 200-foot gopuram gate towers — at exactly the same time the first Romanesque cathedrals of western Europe were being built with much more modest engineering ambitions.

In **Japan**, the Heian court was producing *The Tale of Genji* (around 1010), the world's first psychological novel, written by the woman court official Murasaki Shikibu (Ancient Japan Ch 8) — at exactly the moment the rural aristocrats of northern France were largely illiterate and getting their letters dictated by clerks.

In the **Americas**, **Cahokia** in the Mississippi valley was approaching its early-twelfth-century peak with perhaps 20,000 residents — the largest pre-Columbian city north of Mexico. The **Maya** of the southern lowlands had collapsed in the eighth and ninth centuries (Maya Civilization Ch 5), but Postclassic Maya kingdoms continued in the Yucatán under Chichén Itzá and later Mayapán (Maya Civilization Ch 6). The **Wari** highland empire of Peru was in its final decline (Middle Horizon Empires Ch 8), and the next great Andean polity, the Chimú on the north coast, was just beginning to consolidate.

The European take-off of 1000–1100 was therefore not Europe leaping ahead of the world. It was Europe catching up. The Latin West in 1000 had been a back province of Eurasia; by 1300 it was at least back in the conversation. By 1500 — which is the territory of the Late Medieval and Early Modern volumes — it had begun to diverge in the directions that would eventually produce the modern world. But the take-off in this chapter is a recovery, not a sprint.

---

A few words about how this gets paid for, because the rest of the book sometimes takes the institutional infrastructure for granted.

The medieval economy ran on land. A king or great lord granted land to a vassal in exchange for military service; the vassal granted land to lesser knights in exchange for the same; the knights granted plots to peasants in exchange for labor and a share of the crop. This stack of obligations is what historians since the seventeenth century have called **feudalism** (FYOO-duh-lizm), from Latin *feudum*, meaning a fief — a grant of land. The actual term "feudalism" did not exist in the Middle Ages and the system was much messier than the textbook diagrams suggest, but the basic logic — land in exchange for military and labor service — held across most of western Europe between roughly 900 and 1300.

The man at the bottom of the stack was the **serf** (SURF, from Latin *servus*, slave). The serf was not a slave in the classical sense — he could not be sold separately from the land, he had legal rights, he could marry and pass property to his children — but he was bound to the manor of his lord, owed labor on the lord's fields a fixed number of days per week, and could not leave without permission. The High Medieval centuries saw two contradictory trends. On the one hand, agricultural intensification made the serf's labor more valuable and gave many serfs better terms. On the other, the rise of the urban economy gave serfs a way out — the chartered town's "year and a day" rule that made fugitives free was the safety valve that drained the most ambitious labor off the manor and into the city.

The **manor** itself — the basic unit of medieval rural economy — was a self-contained working estate with the lord's house at the center, the peasants' village around it, and the cultivated fields, common pastures, woodland, and watermill organized as a single working system. The Domesday Book of 1086 catalogues English manors in extraordinary detail: how many plows, how many peasants, how many mills, how many fishponds, how the population had changed since 1066 (most often: it was now smaller, because William's conquest had been brutal). The manorial economy is the engine room of the High Medieval take-off. The cathedrals and the universities are the steeple and the tower; the manor is the foundation.

By 1300, most of the institutional pieces of the High Middle Ages were in place. The next chapter turns to the political and religious confrontation that defined the eleventh century — the long war between the popes who were now claiming supremacy over kings and the kings who refused to accept it.

# Chapter 2 — Pope vs. Emperor

In late January 1077, the Holy Roman Emperor Henry IV stood barefoot in the snow for three days outside the gate of the castle of **Canossa** (kah-NOH-sah) in northern Italy. He was wearing only a coarse woolen pilgrim's robe over his shirt. He was waiting for Pope Gregory VII to come to the gate and lift his excommunication. Eventually Gregory did. Henry kissed his ring, was readmitted to the Catholic Church, and rode home.

This is one of the most famous scenes in medieval history, and it deserves explanation. Why was the most powerful secular ruler in western Christendom standing in the snow in his nightshirt waiting for permission from a sixty-year-old monk with a cane to be allowed back into a Church he himself was supposedly the protector of? What had they been fighting about? And what did it actually settle?

The answer is the **Investiture Controversy**, the long contest from roughly 1075 to 1122 over who could appoint bishops in western Europe — the king or the pope. It sounds technical. It was technical. It was also one of the structural turning points of the entire medieval period, the moment when the western Church established itself as a power independent of the secular state. Everything that follows in this book — the Crusades, the universities, Magna Carta, the conflict between civil and religious authority that would still be live in the Reformation and the Enlightenment — runs out of the precedent that Gregory VII set against Henry IV.

This chapter explains what was at stake.

---

Some setup. Through most of the early Middle Ages, the western Church had been comfortably entangled with secular rulers. Bishops were major landholders, and their lands came with feudal obligations to the king who granted them. When a bishop died, the king typically chose his replacement from among candidates suggested by the cathedral chapter, then "invested" him by handing him the symbols of his office — a ring (representing his marriage to the diocese) and a crozier or staff (representing his pastoral authority). The new bishop also received from the king the lands and revenues of his see, which often made him one of the wealthiest and most powerful men in the kingdom. The king got a loyal noble in a key position; the bishop got land and rank; the Church got an institutional presence. Everyone, on the surface, was happy.

There were two problems with this arrangement that grew worse through the tenth and eleventh centuries.

The first was **simony** (SY-muh-nee), the buying and selling of church offices, named after Simon Magus from the New Testament who tried to buy spiritual power from the apostles. By 1000, simony was endemic. Bishoprics were openly sold to the highest bidder. Wealthy noble families bought the bishopric of, say, Reims for a younger son who had no particular religious vocation but who would administer the see's revenues for the family's benefit. The Church's spiritual authority and its temporal wealth had become so entangled that the spiritual side was visibly being prostituted to the temporal.

The second was **clerical marriage**. The early Church had not strictly required priests to be celibate, but by the eleventh century the Roman Church was moving toward an absolute requirement of clerical celibacy. This was less a matter of theology than of property: a married priest had children, and his children inherited church property as if it were family property, draining the institution. The reformers wanted celibate clergy partly for spiritual reasons (the model was the monk) and partly to keep church property from leaking out into hereditary noble lineages.

Underneath both of these specific abuses was a deeper structural tension. If the Church's spiritual authority was higher than the king's temporal authority — and the entire medieval theology of kingship insisted that kings derived their power from God through the Church — then how could the king appoint the bishops who administered that spiritual authority on God's behalf? It looked like the river running uphill. The reform movement wanted the river to flow the right way: bishops would be chosen by the Church, not by the king.

---

The reform began in the monasteries. The abbey of **Cluny** (kloo-NEE) in Burgundy, founded in 910, had been chartered with the unusual provision that it answered directly to the pope and not to any local lord or bishop — a kind of jurisdictional independence that let it pursue strict religious reform without political interference. The Cluniac model spread. By the late eleventh century there were over a thousand Cluniac priories across western Europe, all loosely linked to the mother house, all observing a stricter version of the Rule of Saint Benedict (the sixth-century template for monastic life, Mongol Empire era predecessor of much medieval institutional thinking — actually written in early-medieval Italy, the foundational Western monastic charter). The Cluniacs produced a generation of reform-minded clerics who eventually started getting elected pope.

The reformers worked their way to the top of the institution between roughly 1046 and 1073. **Pope Leo IX** (1049–1054) issued the first systematic decrees against simony and clerical marriage. **Pope Nicholas II** (1059–1061) issued the bull *In Nomine Domini* in 1059 that took the election of the pope itself out of the hands of the Roman aristocracy and the German emperor and gave it exclusively to the **College of Cardinals** — the papal electors who still choose the pope today. (The 1059 reform is one of the Church's most consequential institutional moves and it is often skipped in popular histories. Without it, the popes would have remained essentially appointees of whoever held Rome.)

The third major figure was **Hildebrand of Sovana**, an Italian Cluniac monk who had been advising successive popes for twenty years before he was elected as **Gregory VII** in 1073 at the age of about sixty. Gregory was the most ambitious pope of the medieval West. In 1075 he issued the **Dictatus Papae** — the Dictates of the Pope — a list of twenty-seven propositions claiming sweeping papal authority. A few examples will give the flavor: the pope alone is universally called pope; the pope can depose emperors; all princes shall kiss the pope's feet; the pope may absolve the subjects of unjust rulers from their oaths of loyalty.

This was new. Previous popes had claimed religious supremacy. Gregory was claiming political supremacy too. He was saying, in effect: I can fire your king.

It would have been provocative under any circumstances. But Gregory had picked an opponent in **Henry IV**, the young Holy Roman Emperor, who was not going to take it lying down.

---

**Henry IV** of the Salian dynasty had inherited the throne at age six in 1056 and reached his majority around 1065. The German emperor was theoretically the protector of the western Church and held the right to "invest" bishops within his vast empire (which then included Germany, the Low Countries, much of northern Italy, and Burgundy). When the bishopric of Milan came open in late 1075, Gregory and Henry both insisted on appointing different candidates. Gregory threatened excommunication. Henry, in January 1076, summoned a council of German bishops at Worms that issued a counter-blast declaring Gregory deposed and demanding that he descend from his usurped throne.

Gregory's reply in February 1076 was overwhelming. He **excommunicated** Henry — formally cutting him off from the sacraments, which any medieval Christian believed meant being damned if he died — and, more devastatingly, he **released Henry's subjects from their oaths of loyalty**. The second move was the new move. It said, in effect: the political loyalty you owe your king depends on his being in good standing with the Church, and he is no longer in good standing.

The German nobility seized on it instantly. Henry had been struggling with rebellious vassals throughout his reign; many of them had been waiting for an excuse to disobey him. With the pope's release of obligations in hand, they convened at the Diet of Tribur in October 1076 and gave Henry an ultimatum: get the excommunication lifted by February 1077 or be deposed.

Henry crossed the Alps in midwinter — by the chronicle accounts, with great difficulty, his small entourage sliding down icy mountain passes on hides and ropes — and arrived at the castle of Canossa, where Gregory was staying as the guest of Countess Matilda of Tuscany, in late January 1077. The chronicles say Henry stood barefoot in the snow, wearing only a hair shirt, for three days outside the castle gate before Gregory would receive him. On the fourth day, Gregory accepted his penance and lifted the excommunication. Henry kissed the pope's ring and returned to Germany.

In the short term, **Canossa** was a victory for Gregory. The pope had brought an emperor to his knees. In the medium term, it was a victory for Henry — by removing the excommunication, Gregory had taken away the legal basis for Henry's deposition, and Henry went back to Germany and quickly defeated the rebellious barons. In the long term, the question of who had actually won would not be settled for another fifty years.

The personal struggle continued. Gregory excommunicated Henry again in 1080. Henry invaded Italy, captured Rome in 1084, installed an antipope (Clement III), and forced Gregory to flee south to Salerno, where Gregory died in 1085 reportedly muttering, "I have loved justice and hated iniquity, therefore I die in exile." Henry's descendants kept fighting his successors. The contest finally ended with the **Concordat of Worms** in September 1122, signed between Pope Calixtus II and Henry's son Henry V, which compromised the question by separating the spiritual investiture (the ring and staff, by the pope) from the temporal investiture (the lands and office, by the emperor). Bishops would be elected by their cathedral chapters under both papal and royal supervision.

It was a compromise. But the compromise was on the new ground that Gregory had cleared. The Church now genuinely was a power independent of the secular state, with its own legal system (canon law), its own judicial procedures (the church courts), its own hierarchy answerable only to itself, and its own financial system. The Investiture Controversy did not put the pope above the king in any practical sense. It put the pope alongside the king, as a parallel sovereign in a shared territory. This dual sovereignty — two parallel power structures with overlapping jurisdictions — is one of the structural inventions of the High Middle Ages and one of the things that distinguishes the European political tradition from most of its contemporaries.

---

The structural parallel worth pausing on is the **Great Schism of 1054**, which had happened twenty-one years before the Investiture Controversy began, on the eastern side of the Christian world.

On July 16, 1054, Cardinal Humbert, a papal legate visiting Constantinople, walked into the Hagia Sophia during the liturgy and laid on the altar a bull of excommunication against the Patriarch Michael Cerularius. The patriarch returned the favor a few days later. It was, on the surface, a procedural quarrel about doctrinal differences (the use of unleavened bread in the Eucharist, the *filioque* clause in the Nicene Creed, papal supremacy) that had been simmering for centuries. Neither side considered it permanent at the time. Mutual excommunications had happened before and been retracted.

This one was not retracted. The 1054 split between the **Roman Catholic Church** in the Latin West and the **Eastern Orthodox Church** in the Greek-speaking east hardened over the following decades and centuries, deepened by the linguistic and cultural drift between Latin Europe and the Byzantine world (Byzantine Empire Ch 7), the Crusader sack of Constantinople in 1204 (Chapter 3), and the failure of every subsequent attempt at reunion. As of 2026, the Catholic and Orthodox churches are still formally in schism, though the mutual 1054 excommunications were lifted by Pope Paul VI and Patriarch Athenagoras in 1965.

The 1054 schism and the 1075–1122 Investiture Controversy were related episodes in the same larger story — the western Church becoming a more aggressive, centralized, jurisdictionally ambitious institution under the reform popes. The same Cardinal Humbert who delivered the excommunication at Hagia Sophia in 1054 was a Cluniac reformer who had drafted some of the early reform decrees. The reform that produced the Investiture Controversy and the reform that produced the schism with the East were the same reform. The Church that the High Middle Ages would build — disciplined, centralized, jurisdictionally aggressive — was being assembled at exactly this moment.

Cross-civ check: while Gregory VII and Henry IV were fighting over investiture, the **Seljuk Turks** had just defeated the Byzantines at Manzikert in 1071 (Byzantine Empire Ch 7), opening Anatolia to Turkic settlement and triggering the Byzantine appeals for western military help that would shortly become the First Crusade. In **al-Andalus**, the Cordoban caliphate had just collapsed (1031) and Christian armies were beginning the long advance south (Toledo would fall in 1085). In **China**, the Song dynasty was at its eleventh-century peak, with Wang Anshi pushing through the most ambitious reform program in pre-modern Chinese history (Tang-Song China Ch 6). In **India**, the Chola navy was at the height of its Bay of Bengal expansion (Medieval India Ch 5). The 1070s and 1080s are an unusually busy decade for institutional restructuring across Eurasia, and the European share of it is the Investiture Controversy.

---

The longer-term consequence was the **medieval papacy** as a continuous political institution. By 1100 the popes had:

- Their own electoral process (cardinals, since 1059)
- Their own legal system (canon law, codified by Gratian's *Decretum* around 1140)
- Their own court system (the diocesan courts and ultimately the papal curia)
- Their own diplomatic corps (the legates)
- Their own international taxation system (the tithe, Peter's Pence, and the various papal levies)
- Their own ideological program (the reform movement)
- And their own demonstrated willingness to confront kings and emperors

The thirteenth-century popes (Chapter 8) would push this institutional power further than Gregory had imagined possible. **Innocent III** (1198–1216) would place England under interdict to bring King John to heel, depose the Holy Roman Emperor Otto IV, call the Fourth Crusade and the Albigensian Crusade, approve the Franciscans and Dominicans, and preside over the Fourth Lateran Council that codified medieval Catholic doctrine and discipline for the next three centuries. By 1216 the medieval papacy was the most institutionally developed political organization in Latin Europe.

It was also still the Church. This is one of the things that makes the medieval papacy genuinely strange to modern eyes. It was simultaneously a religious institution claiming to be the body of Christ on earth, and a political-administrative apparatus collecting taxes from England to Sicily and adjudicating property disputes between Sienese banking houses. The medieval Church's spiritual mission and its earthly machinery never separated. They could not separate, because the institutional theory of medieval Christendom assumed that they were the same thing. Gregory VII's reform had been about making the spiritual mission control the earthly machinery rather than the other way around. By 1216 the popes had largely won that fight. By 1300 they had begun to lose it again to the rising centralized monarchies of France and England, in ways the Late Medieval volume picks up.

The Investiture Controversy's most important legacy was therefore not a specific outcome but a structural principle: that there are two parallel sovereignties in Christian Europe, the spiritual and the temporal, neither of which is reducible to the other. This is not the modern doctrine of separation of church and state — neither side in 1077 would have recognized that idea, and both would have rejected it. But it is the medieval foundation on which the eventual modern doctrine could be built. The dual sovereignty of pope and emperor, fought over for fifty years and compromised at Worms in 1122, is one of the structural inheritances of the modern western political tradition.

The next chapter turns to the most spectacular thing the reformed papacy did with its new authority: it called Christendom to war.

# Chapter 3 — The Crusades

On November 27, 1095, Pope Urban II delivered a sermon in an open field outside the town of **Clermont** (klair-MOH(N), in central France) that called the Christian knights of western Europe to march two thousand five hundred miles overland to the Holy Land and recover Jerusalem from Muslim rule. Within a year, tens of thousands of people — peasants as well as knights, including women, children, and the elderly — had set out east. Within four years, an army of European knights had captured Jerusalem and slaughtered most of its Muslim and Jewish inhabitants. Within two centuries, four Crusader states had been established and largely lost again, the eastern Christian capital at Constantinople had been sacked by western Christian crusaders (1204), and the entire enterprise had ended with the fall of the last Crusader stronghold at Acre in 1291.

The Crusades are one of the most familiar episodes in medieval history and one of the most thoroughly mythologized. This chapter tries to do a few things at once: explain why the Crusades happened, narrate what actually happened, address some of the most persistent myths, and assess what the Crusades did and did not accomplish.

---

Several long-term developments converged at Clermont in 1095.

The first was the **Seljuk shock**. In 1071 a Seljuk Turkish army under Sultan Alp Arslan had destroyed the Byzantine field army at the Battle of **Manzikert** (Byzantine Empire Ch 7), capturing Emperor Romanos IV and opening the Anatolian plateau to Turkic settlement. Within a decade the Seljuks had pushed almost to the walls of Constantinople. The Byzantines had been losing territory for four hundred years, but Manzikert was a different kind of loss — the Anatolian heartland that had supplied half the empire's tax revenue and most of its soldiers was gone in a single campaign season.

The second was the **changing political situation in the Levant**. The Seljuks had also taken Jerusalem in 1073 from the Fatimid caliphate of Egypt (Islamic Golden Age Ch 6 covers the Fatimid context). Christian pilgrims to the Holy Land — a stream that had been steady through the early Middle Ages, occasionally tolerated and occasionally harassed under successive Muslim rulers — found the Seljuks less hospitable than the Fatimids had been. Stories of mistreatment of pilgrims circulated in western Europe in the 1080s and 1090s. Some of the stories were exaggerated. The general impression was not.

The third was the **Byzantine appeal for help**. Emperor Alexios I Komnenos sent ambassadors to Pope Urban II in 1095 asking for western military assistance against the Seljuks. Alexios was hoping for a few thousand mercenary knights to bolster the Byzantine army. He got something quite different.

The fourth was the **reformed papacy** of the previous chapter, looking for ways to channel the violent energies of the Frankish warrior aristocracy into something more useful than fighting each other. The **Peace of God** and **Truce of God** movements had been trying since the late tenth century to limit aristocratic violence by declaring various people, places, and days off-limits for warfare. A military expedition to the Holy Land — directed against non-Christians, sanctioned by the pope, framed as a pilgrimage with spiritual rewards — was the logical next step.

---

The sermon at Clermont survives in five different versions written by different chroniclers years later, none of them verbatim, and historians have argued for centuries about what Urban actually said. All five versions agree on the basic structure: Urban described the suffering of eastern Christians under Seljuk rule, called the Frankish knights to undertake a military pilgrimage to liberate Jerusalem, and promised that participants would receive remission of sins for their journey. The crowd reportedly responded with shouts of **Deus vult** — "God wills it" — that became the slogan of the First Crusade.

The response far exceeded anything Urban had planned for. He had expected to send a knightly army of several thousand under disciplined noble leadership. What he got was a vast popular movement.

The first wave was the **People's Crusade** of spring 1096 — an unauthorized expedition of perhaps 40,000 mostly unarmed peasants and townsfolk, including significant numbers of women, children, and the elderly, led by a charismatic preacher called **Peter the Hermit** and an impoverished knight named **Walter Sans Avoir** (san-zah-VWAR — "Walter the Penniless"). They marched east through Germany and the Balkans toward Constantinople, sustaining themselves by foraging and by extorting supplies from the towns they passed through. Most never reached the Holy Land. A large fraction died of disease, hunger, or fighting along the way. The remnant that arrived at Constantinople in summer 1096 was hustled across the Bosporus by the appalled Byzantine emperor and was almost entirely massacred by Seljuk Turks at Civetot in October 1096.

The People's Crusade also produced the first significant act of organized antisemitic violence in medieval Europe. As bands of crusaders moved through the Rhineland in May, June, and July 1096, mobs attacked the Jewish communities of Speyer, Worms, Mainz, Cologne, Trier, and other cities, killing several thousand people and destroying centuries-old communities. Some of the killings were carried out by crusader bands; others by local townspeople using the crusade as a pretext. The bishops of several Rhineland cities tried to protect their Jewish populations and were mostly unable to. The **Rhineland massacres** of 1096 are the largest single episode of antisemitic violence in Europe before the Black Death pogroms of 1348–1349 (Late Medieval Europe Ch 4) and they established a pattern — crusade preaching producing local antisemitic violence — that would recur on every subsequent crusade.

The official **First Crusade** set out in autumn 1096, several months after the People's Crusade had largely already destroyed itself. Four major contingents marched separately: **Godfrey of Bouillon** with the Lotharingians, **Raymond of Toulouse** with the Provençals, **Bohemond of Taranto** with the Norman-Sicilians, and **Robert of Normandy** with the Anglo-Normans. They converged on Constantinople in late 1096 and early 1097, where the alarmed Emperor Alexios extracted oaths from the leaders to return any reconquered former Byzantine territory before ferrying them across the Bosporus.

The march south through Anatolia was brutal — the summer of 1097 saw thousands die of heat and thirst — but the First Crusade reached Antioch in October 1097 and besieged it for eight months. Inside Antioch the situation was desperate; outside, the crusaders ran short of food, with chronicle accounts describing them eating their horses, dogs, and (in some accounts) the corpses of dead Muslims after battle. The city finally fell in June 1098 by treachery — a Christian merchant inside the walls let a small party in by night, who opened a gate. Two days later a Turkish relief army under Kerbogha of Mosul arrived and besieged the besiegers. The crusaders, starving and outnumbered, were rescued (according to crusader chroniclers) by the discovery of the Holy Lance — the spear that had pierced Christ's side at the Crucifixion — buried under the floor of Antioch's cathedral. The discovery, almost certainly orchestrated by the visionary Peter Bartholomew, gave the crusaders enough morale to sortie out and defeat Kerbogha's army on June 28, 1098.

Antioch was kept by Bohemond as the **Principality of Antioch**, in violation of his oath to Alexios. The breach of trust would poison Byzantine-Crusader relations for the next two centuries.

The march south through Syria took a year. Jerusalem was finally besieged in June 1099. The first assault failed. The crusaders built two siege towers from timber stripped from the surrounding hills, and on July 15, 1099, they breached the walls in two places and entered the city. Over the next two days they slaughtered most of the Muslim and Jewish inhabitants — Muslims who had taken refuge on the roof of the al-Aqsa mosque, Jews who had gathered in the main synagogue (which the crusaders set on fire). Western chroniclers boasted that the blood ran ankle-deep in the streets; Muslim sources quoted similar figures. Modern estimates of the death toll run from 3,000 to 30,000.

The **massacre at Jerusalem** in July 1099 is one of the most notorious atrocities of the High Middle Ages. It is also somewhat anomalous — most crusader sieges did not end this way, because most cities surrendered on terms before the wall was breached, and once a wall was breached, medieval law of war considered the inhabitants forfeit. The massacre would be remembered by later Muslim chroniclers (notably Ibn al-Athir in the early thirteenth century) as the founding act of the Crusader presence in the Levant. It would be contrasted, repeatedly, with the very different terms on which Saladin would retake the same city eighty-eight years later.

---

The First Crusade established the four **Crusader states** in the Levant: the **Kingdom of Jerusalem**, the **Principality of Antioch**, the **County of Edessa**, and the **County of Tripoli**. These were Christian states ruled by a French-speaking aristocracy of crusader veterans and their descendants, with Italian merchant communities (Venice, Genoa, Pisa) running the port cities and the maritime trade, and a much larger native population — Muslim, Christian, and Jewish — providing the labor and most of the inhabitants. They survived in increasingly reduced form for nearly two centuries.

The Crusader states are sometimes presented in popular accounts as a kind of medieval European colonial project. The comparison is partial. They were ruled by European crusader veterans, they were religiously legitimized by the Roman Church, and they were demographically dominated by indigenous populations. But they were also genuinely indigenous in some ways. The crusader nobility intermarried with local Christian and even Muslim families. They adopted Levantine architecture, food, dress, and some social customs. Visiting western European pilgrims in the late twelfth century were sometimes appalled at how "Easternized" the **Outremer** (oo-truh-MAIR — French for "overseas," the standard medieval name for the Crusader states) French had become. The crusader states were neither pure transplants nor pure indigenous polities; they were something hybrid, like the Norman kingdom in Sicily (with which they shared many characteristics) but more politically precarious because of the constant Muslim military pressure from the surrounding Levantine and Egyptian states.

---

The crusades did not end with the First.

The **Second Crusade** (1147–1149) was launched after the County of Edessa fell to the Muslims in 1144. It was preached across Europe by **Bernard of Clairvaux** (Chapter 5) — the most influential preacher of his generation — and led by Louis VII of France and Conrad III of Germany. It collapsed at the disastrous siege of Damascus in July 1148, when an ill-advised four-day attack on the city's walls failed and the crusader army withdrew in chaos. The failure damaged crusader prestige in the Holy Land, Bernard's reputation in Europe, and the credibility of the crusading idea. Bernard spent the rest of his life writing apologies for what had gone wrong.

The **Third Crusade** (1189–1192) was triggered by the most consequential single event in twelfth-century crusader history — the recapture of Jerusalem by **Saladin** (sah-lah-DEEN, properly Salah al-Din ibn Ayyub, the Kurdish Muslim general who had unified Egypt and Syria under his Ayyubid dynasty). On July 4, 1187, Saladin's army destroyed the assembled crusader field army at the **Horns of Hattin** in Galilee — luring the heavily armored crusader force across waterless terrain in summer heat, then surrounding and methodically destroying them. On October 2, 1187, Jerusalem surrendered to Saladin after a brief siege.

Saladin's treatment of conquered Jerusalem was conspicuously different from the crusaders' treatment of conquered Jerusalem in 1099. Christians who could ransom themselves were permitted to leave with their property; those who could not were enslaved, but Saladin and his brother al-Adil paid the ransoms of thousands themselves. The Holy Sepulchre church was preserved, with Christian access negotiated. The contrast — Christian crusaders massacring Muslims and Jews in 1099 versus Muslim Saladin protecting Christians in 1187 — was widely commented on at the time and is one of the reasons Saladin became one of the very few non-Christian figures to be admired in medieval European literature, including in Dante's *Divine Comedy*.

The fall of Jerusalem triggered the Third Crusade under three of the most famous monarchs of the era: **Frederick Barbarossa** of the Holy Roman Empire, **Philip II Augustus** of France, and **Richard the Lionheart** of England. Frederick drowned crossing the Saleph river in Anatolia in June 1190 and his German army largely went home. Philip and Richard reached the Levant by sea in 1191. Together they recaptured Acre after a two-year siege. Then Philip went home, leaving Richard to fight Saladin alone for another year. Richard won the Battle of Arsuf in September 1191 and twice came within sight of Jerusalem but never tried to retake the city — knowing that even if he could capture it, he would not have the manpower to hold it after the bulk of the crusader army went home. The **Treaty of Jaffa** in September 1192 left Jerusalem in Saladin's hands while granting Christian pilgrims access to the city.

It was a draw, on terms that left the Crusader states reduced but viable. Saladin died the next year, in March 1193, at age fifty-six. Richard was captured on the way home by Duke Leopold of Austria (whom he had insulted at Acre), held for ransom by Henry VI of the Holy Roman Empire, and finally freed in 1194 after the English crown paid a ransom equal to roughly two years' English royal revenue.

---

The **Fourth Crusade** (1202–1204) is the worst of them.

The plan, agreed in 1201, was an attack on Egypt — the strategic move that would split the Ayyubid sultanate and create conditions for retaking Jerusalem. Pope Innocent III approved. The crusader army gathered at Venice in 1202 to be ferried east. The Venetians, who had built an enormous fleet for the expedition at significant expense, expected to be paid the contracted price in full. The crusader army that arrived was much smaller than projected and could not pay the bill.

The Doge of Venice, **Enrico Dandolo** — at this point in his eighties and famously blind — proposed a deal: the crusaders could work off their debt by helping Venice recapture the rebellious Adriatic city of Zara from the king of Hungary (a Catholic Christian) on the way east. The crusaders did so in November 1202, sacking the Christian city. Pope Innocent III excommunicated the entire expedition. They proceeded anyway.

At Zara they were met by ambassadors of **Alexios IV Angelos**, the son of the deposed Byzantine emperor Isaac II, who proposed an even more lucrative side trip. If the crusaders would help him take Constantinople and put him on the Byzantine throne, he would pay the crusaders' Venetian debt, fund the rest of their expedition to Egypt, place the Byzantine Church under Roman authority, and provide ten thousand Byzantine troops for the crusade. The crusaders accepted.

The crusader fleet arrived at Constantinople in summer 1203, took the city in July with limited fighting, installed Alexios IV as co-emperor with his father Isaac II, and waited to be paid. Alexios IV could not pay — the Byzantine treasury was nearly empty, the population hated him as a Latin puppet, and the promised Church union enraged the Orthodox clergy. He was overthrown and killed in late January 1204 by Alexios V Doukas Mourtzouphlos.

The crusaders, denied their payment and now openly at war with the new Byzantine government, decided to take Constantinople for themselves.

They sacked the city on April 13, 1204. The sack was the worst urban disaster in Constantinople's history — worse than anything the Persians, the Arabs, the Bulgarians, or the Russians had ever managed. The crusader army looted the city for three days, methodically. They stripped the great churches of their altar plate. They stole the bronze horses from the Hippodrome (which still stand in front of the Basilica of San Marco in Venice). They smashed the imperial tombs and scattered the bones. The city's libraries, with their irreplaceable Greek classical manuscripts, were burned or looted for parchment. Nuns were raped in the convents. The patriarchal throne in the Hagia Sophia was occupied by a drunken prostitute who sang bawdy songs from it.

The crusaders established the **Latin Empire of Constantinople** that lasted until 1261, when the Byzantine successor state at Nicaea retook the city under Michael VIII Palaiologos (Byzantine Empire Ch 9). The Byzantine Empire was permanently broken — when Constantinople was recovered in 1261 it was a smaller, poorer, less populous shadow of the city that had fallen in 1204, and the empire never recovered its former territory or military strength. The Fourth Crusade is one of the great catastrophes of European medieval history. Pope Innocent III, who had explicitly forbidden the attack, had no power to stop it; when he learned of the sack he reportedly wept, then conditionally approved the Latin Empire because the schism with the Greek Church was, in his view, now provably the Greeks' fault for resisting the crusader-installed Latin Patriarch.

The lasting consequence was the permanent embittering of the Greek-Catholic schism. Greeks who might have considered the 1054 schism (Chapter 2) a procedural quarrel could not consider 1204 anything but a devastation by the Latin Church. The schism's emotional foundation in popular memory was set by the sack, not by the doctrinal disputes that had supposedly caused it. To this day, eight centuries later, the 1204 sack is still cited in Orthodox-Catholic dialogue as the wound that has not healed.

---

A few words about the popular myths.

The **Children's Crusade** of 1212 — the legendary expedition of thousands of children who supposedly set out for Jerusalem and were sold into slavery by treacherous merchants — is largely a later embroidery. Two unrelated movements in 1212, a French one led by a shepherd boy named Stephen of Cloyes who marched to Saint-Denis, and a German one led by a boy named Nicholas of Cologne who led a procession south toward the Mediterranean, were merged in fifteenth-century chronicles into the legend. Modern historians regard the original participants as mostly poor adolescents and unemployed adults from the wandering bands of the early thirteenth century. The slavery story is largely later invention. The legend persisted because it fit the medieval and post-medieval imagination of childhood as innocent and crusading as corrupted.

The **Crusades as a "civilizing mission"** — the modern claim, sometimes asserted on both political wings, that the Crusades represented an effort to civilize a barbaric Muslim world — is essentially backward. In 1095, the Islamic civilization that the Crusaders confronted in the Levant was wealthier, more urbanized, more literate, and in most measurable cultural respects more sophisticated than the Latin Europe they came from. Damascus was a larger city than any in northern Europe. Cordoba and Cairo had public hospitals, public lighting, public baths, paved streets, and pharmacies; Paris in 1100 did not. Arabic was the language of Aristotle and Galen and Euclid; Latin was a much smaller intellectual world that had only just begun, through the Toledo translations (Chapter 5), to recover what Arabic readers had had for three hundred years. The cultural traffic between the Crusader states and the surrounding Muslim world ran overwhelmingly Islamic-to-European, not the other way around. The crusaders learned Arabic medical practice, Levantine cuisine, irrigation techniques, glassmaking, sugar production, and much else, and brought a great deal of it back to western Europe. They taught the Muslim world relatively little.

The **Inquisition**, often imagined as a feature of the crusader era, was actually only beginning at the very end of this period. Pope Gregory IX issued the bull *Excommunicamus* in 1231 establishing the **medieval Inquisition** — papal inquisitors, mostly Dominicans, deployed to hunt down surviving Cathars and Waldensians in southern France and northern Italy. This is the institutional ancestor of the much later Spanish and Roman Inquisitions of the early modern period. The medieval Inquisition was smaller, more legally constrained, and less catastrophically violent than its later descendants — but it was a real and growing apparatus by the late thirteenth century, and the **Albigensian Crusade** of 1209–1229 had already inflicted brutal religious violence on the Cathar populations of Languedoc.

---

What did the Crusades actually accomplish?

Militarily: very little, in the long run. The Crusader states established by the First Crusade survived for less than two centuries; the last stronghold at **Acre** fell to the Mamluks on May 18, 1291, ending the European military presence in the Levant for six hundred years. None of the four cities the First Crusade captured — Jerusalem, Antioch, Edessa, Tripoli — has been continuously in Christian hands since.

Politically: the Crusades reinforced the medieval papacy's claim to lead Christendom but also exposed its limits. The pope could call a crusade; he could not control what the crusade did once it left. The Fourth Crusade was the dramatic demonstration of the limit. The popes kept calling crusades into the late thirteenth century and beyond, but the response shrank steadily.

Economically: the Crusades vastly expanded the Italian maritime cities — Venice, Genoa, Pisa — that financed and supplied the expeditions and that established trading colonies in the Crusader ports. The commercial revolution of Chapter 4 has the Crusades as one of its triggers. The wealth flowing back to western Europe from Levantine trade, with sugar, spices, silk, and dyestuffs at the center, transformed the European luxury economy.

Culturally: the Crusades intensified European contact with the eastern Mediterranean and accelerated the cultural transfer that the Toledo translations had begun. New foods, new technologies, new literary themes (the romance of the East, the figure of Saladin), new architectural styles, new diseases, and a much larger geographic imagination flowed back to Europe with returning crusaders and their followers.

Religiously: the Crusades produced the permanent embitterment of European Christian–Jewish relations through the Rhineland massacres and their successors; the permanent embitterment of Catholic-Orthodox relations through 1204; and a long persistence of holy-war thinking in European Christianity that would resurface periodically into the early modern period.

Demographically: the Crusades killed enormous numbers of people, on both sides, including very large numbers of crusader participants who never returned. The total death toll across all the major crusades cannot be reliably estimated; figures of one to five million dead across the two-century arc have been argued.

Morally: the Crusades have been condemned and defended in every century since they ended, and they will be condemned and defended in every century to come. The honest summary, from this distance, is that they were a religiously motivated military enterprise that achieved their stated short-term goal (reaching Jerusalem) at enormous cost, did not achieve their long-term goal (holding Jerusalem), inflicted horrendous violence on Muslim, Jewish, and Eastern Christian populations along the way, and left the world they encountered substantially less changed than they themselves were changed by encountering it.

The next chapter follows the money — the commercial revolution that the Crusades helped accelerate.

# Chapter 4 — The Commercial Revolution

While the warriors of Chapter 3 were marching east, the merchants of Chapter 4 were quietly building something more permanent. Between roughly 1100 and 1300, western Europe shifted from a barter-and-land economy run by lords and manors to a money economy run by merchants and banks. The Italians invented the bill of exchange. The Florentines invented double-entry bookkeeping. The Hanseatic Germans built the first international free-trade zone. The Champagne fairs in northern France became the central exchange between Mediterranean and Baltic Europe. By 1300, a Florentine banker could issue a credit instrument in his Florence office, have it cashed in London or Bruges or Acre weeks later, and earn a profit on the currency conversion that technically did not violate the canon law prohibition on usury.

This is one of the most consequential structural inventions of the High Middle Ages. The commercial institutions developed between 1100 and 1300 — international banking, bills of exchange, double-entry bookkeeping, joint-stock investment, international trade fairs, chartered merchant cities, a body of merchant law — are the direct ancestors of the financial system that runs the world in 2026. Most of them were invented in Italy.

---

The setting was the recovery of long-distance trade, which had largely collapsed in western Europe with the Roman Empire and only began to revive in earnest after 1000.

The trade flowed along three main axes.

The first was the **Mediterranean axis** between the Italian maritime cities and the Levant, North Africa, and Byzantium. **Venice** and **Genoa** were the dominant players, with **Pisa** important until its naval defeat by Genoa at Meloria in 1284. The Italian cities shipped European silver, woolen cloth, timber, and slaves east; they brought back silk, spices, sugar, dyestuffs, alum, and the exotic luxury goods that the European aristocracy increasingly wanted. The Crusader states (Chapter 3) gave the Italian merchants permanent ports and trading colonies in the Levant. The Fourth Crusade (1204) gave Venice an outright commercial empire in the eastern Mediterranean — a network of fortified bases on the islands of Crete, Negroponte (Euboea), and the Greek mainland that Venice held for the next four centuries.

The second was the **North Sea–Baltic axis** dominated by the German **Hanseatic League**. The Hanse merchants traded Russian furs, Baltic amber, Polish grain, Norwegian dried fish, English wool, Flemish cloth, and Rhenish wine across the entire northern European coast from Novgorod to London. Founded informally in the twelfth century and formalized by the 1241 mutual defense treaty between Lübeck and Hamburg, the **Hanseatic League** grew over the next century into a self-governing trading network of more than a hundred north German and Baltic cities, with major foreign trading posts (the **Kontore**) at Bergen, London, Bruges, and Novgorod. At its peak in the fourteenth century the Hanse fielded its own navy, ran its own coinage, and could blockade or wage war against entire kingdoms (it forced the king of Denmark to accept humiliating commercial terms in 1370).

The third was the **continental land-trade axis** between the two, running through France and Germany. The principal exchange point was the **Champagne fairs**.

---

The **Champagne fairs** were six great trade fairs held in rotation in the towns of **Lagny**, **Bar-sur-Aube**, **Provins** (proh-VAN), and **Troyes** (TWAH) in the Champagne region of northern France between roughly 1150 and 1300. Each fair lasted six to eight weeks. The cycle ran continuously around the year. At any given time, there was a major Champagne fair in progress somewhere in northern France, attended by Italian merchants from Florence and Venice, Flemish merchants from Bruges and Ghent, German merchants from Cologne, English wool merchants, and Iberian merchants from Barcelona and Valencia.

The Champagne fairs did several things at once. They were the principal commercial meeting point of medieval Europe, where Italian merchants exchanged Mediterranean goods for Flemish cloth and northern raw materials. They were the principal financial settlement point — the last week of each fair was reserved for clearing accounts, with merchants who owed each other money settling their balances by transferring credits, often with no actual coin changing hands. They were the principal site for the development of European commercial law: the **lex mercatoria** (Latin for "law merchant"), a body of customary commercial principles enforced by the fair courts, became the foundation of European commercial law and the ancestor of much of modern contract law.

The Champagne fairs also nurtured a sustained relationship between Italy and northern France that exposed Italian merchants to the institutional habits of the Flemish and German trading cities, and exposed northern merchants to the financial instruments the Italians had been developing. The technology transfer ran both ways and was continuous for a century and a half.

The fairs declined after 1300 as the Italian merchants increasingly bypassed them — first by sailing directly from Italy to Bruges and London via the Strait of Gibraltar after 1277 (the Genoese galley pioneer voyage that opened the route around Iberia), and later by relocating their main northern operations to Bruges and then Antwerp. By 1400 the Champagne fairs were a shadow of what they had been. By 1500 they were a memory.

---

The Italian banking innovations of the thirteenth century are worth pausing on because they shaped the financial architecture of the entire later western world.

Start with **bills of exchange**. The bill of exchange is a written order by which a merchant deposits money with a banker in one city and has it paid out in another currency in a different city. A Florentine wool merchant could deposit 100 florins in his Florence bank, receive a bill payable in London in pounds sterling, hand-carry the bill to London, present it at the Florence bank's London office, and collect his pounds. This is a money-transfer instrument like a modern wire transfer. It also let the bank earn interest on the time-and-currency conversion in a way that technically did not constitute **usury** — the canon-law prohibition on lending at interest that limited Christian financial activity throughout the medieval period.

This last point matters. The medieval Catholic Church considered any loan at interest a form of theft (you were extracting payment for the use of money, which the Church held to be barren of natural increase). Jewish moneylenders, who were not bound by canon law, supplied much of the small-scale lending market in northern Europe — and were repeatedly persecuted, robbed, and expelled when their royal protectors decided their debts were too inconvenient. Christian bankers had to find ways around the usury ban. The bill of exchange was one of the great workarounds. The interest income was disguised as a foreign-exchange profit, which was permissible. By the late thirteenth century the Italian merchant-bankers had built an entire international financial system on this single innovation.

Next, **double-entry bookkeeping**. The systematic recording of every transaction as both a debit in one account and a credit in another — the foundational accounting practice that lets a business track its own financial position in real time — was developed by Italian merchants in the late thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries and codified in 1494 by the Franciscan mathematician **Luca Pacioli** (loo-kah pah-CHOH-lee) in his *Summa de Arithmetica*. Pacioli's exposition is fifteenth-century, but the practice he was describing was thirteenth-century. Double-entry bookkeeping is the conceptual technology that made modern corporate finance possible.

Next, **branch banking**. The great Florentine houses of the High Middle Ages — the **Bardi** (BAR-dee), the **Peruzzi**, the **Acciaiuoli** (ah-chah-YOH-lee), and later the **Medici** — operated branches in London, Paris, Avignon, Naples, Constantinople, and Cyprus, all coordinated through the Florentine head office. They handled foreign exchange, deposits, loans, papal tax collection, royal financing, and private investment, all at once. The Bardi and Peruzzi houses were the principal bankers of King Edward III of England in the early fourteenth century — and were both bankrupted in the 1340s when Edward defaulted on his Hundred Years' War loans, one of the trigger events of the late-medieval Italian banking crisis (Late Medieval Europe Ch 1).

Finally, **commercial partnerships** — the **commenda** in Venice and the **societas** in Florence — that pooled investment from multiple parties for a single trading voyage, with returns distributed proportionally. These are the medieval ancestors of the joint-stock company.

The cumulative effect of all this was to make Florence and Genoa and Venice the financial capitals of late-medieval Europe and the source of most of the institutional vocabulary the rest of the world would inherit. The word **bank** comes from the Italian *banca* — the bench on which the moneychanger conducted business. The word **bankrupt** comes from *banca rotta* — the broken bench, the public destruction of an insolvent moneychanger's bench in the Florentine market. **Credit** is *credito* — what is believed about a borrower. **Florin**, the gold coin first minted in Florence in 1252, became the standard high-value coin of late-medieval Europe and the first European gold coin in regular international circulation since the fall of Rome.

---

Cross-civ check: the High Medieval European commercial revolution did not invent international banking from nothing. It learned heavily from the much older and more sophisticated Islamic commercial system.

The Islamic world had been operating long-distance commercial networks since the Abbasid period, with sophisticated financial instruments — the **suftaja** (a written order for payment in another city, the direct ancestor of the bill of exchange), the **hawala** (an informal value-transfer system still in use in 2026), the **mudaraba** (a commercial partnership pooling capital and labor), and an extensive network of merchant communities from Andalusia to China (Islamic Golden Age Ch 8). The Geniza documents from Cairo's Ben Ezra synagogue, accidentally preserved in a storage chamber for nine centuries and discovered in the 1890s, show eleventh- and twelfth-century Jewish merchants in Cairo conducting international trade with India, Spain, Sicily, and Yemen using exactly the kind of credit instruments and partnership arrangements that Italians would be using two centuries later.

The transmission of Islamic commercial techniques to Italy ran through Sicily, southern Spain, the Crusader states, and the Italian merchant colonies in Alexandria and Tunis. Italian merchants learned the Islamic financial instruments, modified them to fit Christian canon law, and built them into the European banking system. The **suftaja → bill of exchange** translation is well-documented; many of the other innovations follow the same pattern.

In **China** at the same time, the Song dynasty had pioneered **paper money** — the first widely circulating government-issued paper currency in human history — with the imperial *jiaozi* notes from 1024 (Tang-Song China Ch 6). The Song economy ran on paper currency, deep credit markets, and large-scale state-guaranteed merchant guilds. Western Europe would not have widely circulating paper money until the eighteenth century. The Italian banking innovations of the thirteenth century brought western Europe much closer to Song-era Chinese commercial sophistication, but the Chinese had still been doing it earlier and at greater scale.

The High Medieval commercial revolution was therefore not a European leap into uncharted territory. It was Latin Europe catching up to commercial techniques the Islamic world and China had been using for centuries. What was distinctive about the European version was the institutional scaffolding — the chartered merchant cities, the lex mercatoria, the professional notarial profession, the banking partnerships organized around extended families — that wrapped around the Islamic-derived financial instruments and made them durably embedded in European urban political life.

A few more cross-currents are worth flagging here. The Mediterranean trading economy the Italians were building ran on **West African gold**, channeled north across the Sahara through Berber caravan networks to the North African ports — gold whose source would shortly be the rising Mali Empire under Sundiata's successors (Mali Empire Ch 1 and Ch 4). The Italian commercial system that financed the European take-off was therefore physically dependent on a Sub-Saharan African political economy that contemporary European writers barely understood. Eastward, the **Indian Ocean trading system** the Italians tapped into through the Mamluk-controlled Red Sea routes already had its own deep institutional architecture: the Chola maritime empire of medieval South India dominated the Bay of Bengal between roughly 985 and 1120 (Medieval India Ch 5), with naval campaigns reaching Sumatra and Sri Lanka and trade networks that wound from East Africa through Yemen and the Malabar coast to Southeast Asia. By the late thirteenth century, the Pax Mongolica across Eurasia made overland travel from Venice to the **Yuan** court at Dadu unprecedentedly secure — which is why Marco Polo could make the trip and come back in one piece. The same Mongol-protected corridors would carry the Black Death plague west out of Central Asia in the 1340s, but that catastrophe is the Late Medieval volume's territory.

---

A note on the **Knights Templar**, who deserve their own paragraph in any commercial revolution chapter.

Founded in 1119 by **Hugues de Payens** to protect pilgrims to Jerusalem, the **Knights Templar** were a religious-military order — knights who had taken monastic vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience while remaining trained warriors. They grew rapidly: papal endorsement in 1129, donations of land across western Europe, exemption from local taxation and ecclesiastical jurisdiction. Within a century they were one of the wealthiest organizations in Latin Christendom, with an international network of preceptories from Ireland to Cyprus.

The Templars also turned out to be excellent bankers. Their network of preceptories doubled as a network of secure deposit vaults. A pilgrim traveling from Paris to Acre could deposit money at the Paris Temple and withdraw it at the Acre Temple on a Templar credit slip, avoiding the risk of carrying large amounts of coin across Europe. By the late twelfth century the French royal treasury was being kept in the Paris Temple. Several French kings used the Templars as their personal financiers. The Templar banking operation pioneered some of the same instruments — deposits, transfers, loans — that the Italian houses would soon be doing on a larger scale.

The Templars met a famously bad end. King Philip IV of France, deep in debt to them and resenting their independence, arrested every Templar in France in a single day on October 13, 1307, on charges of heresy, sodomy, and idolatry. Most confessed under torture. Philip pressured Pope Clement V to dissolve the Order, which Clement did at the Council of Vienne in 1312. The last Grand Master, Jacques de Molay, was burned at the stake in Paris on March 18, 1314. The Templars' lands were transferred to the Knights Hospitaller (their parallel order, founded around 1099 to operate a hospital for pilgrims), but most of the cash disappeared — almost certainly into Philip's treasury.

The Templar destruction is technically early-fourteenth-century, just outside this volume's window. But the High Medieval banking apparatus they had built was thirteenth-century, and the precedent of using a religious organization as a transnational financial network outlived them.

---

The commercial revolution had social consequences that ran beyond the merchant class.

The rise of the urban economy gave serfs (Chapter 1) a way out of the manor, and the cumulative drain on rural labor through the High Middle Ages helped erode serfdom across western Europe. By 1300, serfdom had largely disappeared from Italy and was visibly weakening in France and England. (Eastern Europe followed a different trajectory — Polish, Hungarian, and Russian serfdom would actually intensify in the late medieval and early modern centuries, as the eastern European nobility tightened control over peasants who were increasingly producing grain for the western market. The "second serfdom" of eastern Europe is one of the most striking divergences in late-medieval continental development.)

The commercial revolution also produced new conceptual problems. If a merchant could become rich without owning land, what was his place in the medieval social order, which had been structured around the three estates of those who fight (the warrior nobility), those who pray (the clergy), and those who work (the peasantry)? The answer, which the late medieval centuries spent working out, was the rise of a fourth estate — the **bourgeoisie**, the chartered townsmen, the merchants and artisans and bankers and lawyers and notaries and clerks. By 1300 this fourth estate was numerous enough, wealthy enough, and politically organized enough to demand a place in the new representative assemblies (Chapter 8) that were beginning to appear across western Europe. The commercial revolution and the political constitutionalism of the thirteenth century were the same revolution, viewed from two different angles.

The next chapter turns to the mind.

# Chapter 5 — The Twelfth-Century Renaissance

In 1100, an educated cleric in Paris had access to perhaps a few dozen books of classical Latin literature, no Greek philosophy except what had survived in Boethius and a few late-Roman handbooks, no Arabic science, no Roman law in any systematic form, no medical treatises beyond a thin surviving Latin tradition, and a body of Christian theology that had been worked over for centuries but had not had a serious infusion of new material since Augustine in the fifth century. By 1200, that same Parisian cleric had access to the complete works of Aristotle in Latin translation, the major works of Plato, Euclid's *Elements* in full, Ptolemy's *Almagest*, the medical writings of Galen and Hippocrates, the philosophical works of the great Islamic synthesizers Avicenna and Averroes, the great Jewish philosopher Maimonides, the complete Roman law of Justinian's *Corpus Juris Civilis*, and a vastly expanded Christian theological literature including the systematic theology of Peter Lombard's *Sentences*, the systematic canon law of Gratian's *Decretum*, the first European logical treatises since antiquity, and the first vernacular literature in Provençal, Old French, German, and Italian.

This is the **twelfth-century Renaissance** — the recovery of the classical and Islamic learning that had been lost or out of reach for the preceding seven centuries, and the explosion of original European thought that the recovery enabled. It was the most consequential intellectual transformation in European history before the printing press.

The phrase "twelfth-century Renaissance" was coined by the American medievalist Charles Homer Haskins in 1927 to insist that the high-medieval centuries deserved the same honorific that historians had previously reserved for the fifteenth-century Italian Renaissance. The phrase has stuck. Modern scholars sometimes prefer to talk about the "long twelfth century" running from roughly 1075 to 1225, but the basic claim is unchanged. Something significant happened to European intellectual life between Anselm and Aquinas, and most of it happened in the twelfth century.

---

The key mechanism was **translation**.

When the western Roman Empire collapsed in the fifth century, knowledge of Greek was largely lost in the Latin West. The Greek-speaking eastern Empire continued to read its own literature, and the Islamic world that conquered most of the eastern Mediterranean in the seventh and eighth centuries (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1) inherited and translated the Greek scholarly tradition into Arabic. By 900, almost the entire surviving corpus of Greek philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, and medicine existed in Arabic translation in the great libraries of Baghdad, Cordoba, Cairo, and Damascus.

The Latin West, meanwhile, had Boethius's partial Latin translations of Aristotle's logical works (the so-called "old logic"), some Cicero, some late-Roman compendiums and encyclopedias, and very little else of Greek thought. Latin Europeans who wanted to read Aristotle's *Metaphysics* or *Physics* or *Ethics* could not, because no Latin translation existed.

The translation movement that recovered this material happened in three main centers between roughly 1080 and 1200.

The first was **Toledo** (toh-LAY-doh), the great Visigothic capital of Spain that had been under Muslim rule since the early eighth century and had been recaptured by the Christian king Alfonso VI of León-Castile on May 25, 1085. Toledo's surviving Arabic libraries, with their accumulated centuries of translation work, became the principal channel through which Aristotle, Greek mathematics, Indian numerals, and the work of Islamic scholars like Avicenna and Averroes were translated into Latin. The translators were a polyglot mix of Spanish Christians, Mozarabic Christians (Arabic-speaking Christians from al-Andalus), Spanish Jews, and visiting Latin clerics from across Europe. The most prolific was **Gerard of Cremona** (chreh-MOH-nah, an Italian who relocated to Toledo around 1145 and stayed until his death in 1187), who produced more than seventy translations including Ptolemy's *Almagest* (the most influential single text in medieval European astronomy), the medical canon of Avicenna, and Aristotle's *Physics* and *On the Heavens*.

The second was **Sicily**, where the Norman kings (Norman Sicily, founded 1091, see Viking Age Ch 7 for the long Norman context) ruled a multilingual Christian-Muslim-Greek kingdom and patronized translations directly from Greek into Latin without going through Arabic. The Sicilian translations were technically more accurate (less filtered through intermediate languages) but smaller in volume. The translation of Plato's *Phaedo* and *Meno* into Latin was Sicilian. Much of Ptolemy's *Optics* was Sicilian. The court of Roger II in twelfth-century Palermo was one of the great cultural crossroads of the Mediterranean — Greek bureaucrats, Arab geographers, Norman knights, and Latin clerics all serving the same king.

The third was **Antioch** in the Crusader states (Chapter 3), where Latin clerics had access to Arabic libraries in the Levant. Antiochene translations were less systematic than Toledan ones but contributed important scientific and medical material.

The cumulative result of all this translation activity was that by 1200 the Latin West had recovered most of what the Islamic world had inherited from the Greeks, plus a great deal of original Islamic scholarship. Aristotle was back. Euclid was back. Ptolemy was back. Galen was back. The medical canon of **Ibn Sina** (called Avicenna in Latin, ah-vih-SEN-uh) and the philosophical commentaries of **Ibn Rushd** (called Averroes in Latin, ah-VER-oh-eez) — two of the great Islamic synthesizers who had themselves been working on Aristotle (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4 and Ch 7) — were available in Latin and were being read alongside the classical texts they were commenting on.

This created a problem that would absorb European theology for the next century: how to integrate this newly recovered classical and Islamic thought with Christian theology. Aristotle's *Metaphysics* contained claims (the eternity of the world, the unity of the active intellect across humanity) that contradicted Christian doctrine. Averroes's commentaries on Aristotle were even more troubling, since Averroes had pushed the Aristotelian system in directions (the impersonal nature of intellectual immortality) that were openly incompatible with Christian belief. The integration project — what to do with Aristotle, how much of him to accept, how to read him compatibly with Christianity — is most of what the next chapter (Chapter 7) on scholasticism is about.

---

The other great recovery of the twelfth century was **Roman law**.

Around 1080 a complete manuscript of the **Corpus Juris Civilis** — the great sixth-century compilation of Roman law produced by Emperor Justinian (Byzantine Empire Ch 2) — was rediscovered in northern Italy, possibly at Pisa or Ravenna. The Corpus Juris Civilis is enormous: it includes the *Digest* (a fifty-book compilation of opinions by classical Roman jurists), the *Codex* (the imperial laws then in force), the *Institutes* (a textbook), and the *Novels* (Justinian's own legislation). Together they represent a thousand years of Roman legal thinking, systematically organized, in usable form.

The Corpus came to **Bologna**, where the lawyer **Irnerius** (ihr-NEHR-ee-us) and his successors built a school of legal scholarship around it. The Bologna jurists treated the Corpus as a complete and internally consistent system that could be reconciled by close reading and dialectical analysis. They invented the **gloss** — the marginal commentary on the legal text — and built up over the next century a layered commentary apparatus that became the foundation of European civil law. The Bologna school produced lawyers who staffed the courts and chanceries of every European kingdom, the papal curia, and the new town governments. It also produced (Chapter 7) the first university.

The Bologna recovery of Roman law was paralleled by the codification of canon law. **Gratian** (an obscure monk about whom almost nothing is known) compiled the *Decretum* (or *Concordia Discordantium Canonum* — *Concordance of Discordant Canons*) around 1140, a systematic compilation of nearly four thousand church-law texts organized by topic, with apparent contradictions resolved by Gratian's own dialectical commentary. The *Decretum* became the foundational textbook of canon law for the rest of the Middle Ages and the institutional armature of the medieval Church's legal system.

By 1200, Latin Europe had two parallel and increasingly sophisticated legal traditions — the civil law (Roman, secular) and the canon law (ecclesiastical) — and a professional class of legally trained men staffing both. This is the legal substrate of the Magna Carta moment in Chapter 8.

---

The figures of the twelfth-century intellectual world are worth meeting briefly.

**Anselm of Canterbury** (1033–1109), abbot of Bec in Normandy and then Archbishop of Canterbury, formulated the **ontological argument** for God's existence in the *Proslogion* around 1078: God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived; existing in reality is greater than existing only in the understanding; therefore the greatest conceivable being must exist in reality. The argument is the most influential single piece of medieval philosophy and is still a live question in modern philosophy of religion. Anselm also wrote *Cur Deus Homo* (*Why God Became Man*), the foundational medieval treatise on the theology of the Atonement. As Archbishop he clashed with William Rufus and Henry I over the same investiture question that had launched the Investiture Controversy in Germany (Chapter 2), and was twice forced into exile.

**Peter Abelard** (1079–1142) was the most controversial logician of the early twelfth century. A Breton-born brilliant young teacher who took on his own teachers in Paris and bested them in disputation, Abelard was famous (and notorious) for his confidence, his sharp tongue, and his ability to make logic sing. He also famously fell in love with the brilliant young **Heloise** (HEH-loh-eez), the niece of a Paris cathedral canon named Fulbert, in whose household he was tutoring her around 1117. They had a son. Heloise's uncle had Abelard castrated by hired men in retaliation. Abelard withdrew to the abbey of Saint-Denis as a monk; Heloise became a nun. They corresponded years later from separate monasteries, and their letters became one of the great romantic correspondences in European literature — Heloise's particularly so, with her unflinching meditations on the difference between obligation and desire, between conventional virtue and what she calls the love that survives despite everything. Abelard's theological treatise *Sic et Non* (*Yes and No*) collected 158 contradictory statements from authoritative Christian sources on theological questions and offered no resolutions, on the principle that the reader's own intellectual work in resolving them was the point. Abelard's dialectical method became the foundation of the scholastic disputation that would dominate the next century's universities.

**Bernard of Clairvaux** (1090–1153), Cistercian abbot from 1115 and the most influential churchman of his generation, was Abelard's principal antagonist and the most important religious figure of the twelfth century not to wear the papal tiara. Bernard preached the Second Crusade. He founded sixty-eight Cistercian monasteries. He mediated between popes and kings. He produced sermons and treatises that shaped western mysticism for centuries. The papacy of his former student Eugene III ran effectively on Bernard's advice. Bernard's hostility to Abelard's logical theology — to the application of dialectical reason to questions of faith — represented a strand of medieval thought that would lose institutional ground over the next century to the scholastics, but never disappeared.

**Hildegard of Bingen** (1098–1179), Benedictine abbess of Rupertsberg in the Rhineland, was the most extraordinary intellectual woman of the twelfth century and one of the most extraordinary intellectuals of any gender. Her works include theological visions (the *Scivias*, the *Liber Vitae Meritorum*, and the *Liber Divinorum Operum*), a treatise on natural medicine (*Causae et Curae*), a treatise on the medicinal properties of plants and animals (*Physica*), the morality play *Ordo Virtutum*, the largest surviving body of medieval music by a single composer (seventy-seven liturgical songs and an oratorio), and a private correspondence with popes, emperors, and Bernard of Clairvaux. She invented her own language (the *Lingua Ignota*) with over a thousand words and an alphabet. She advised four popes and wrote sharply to the German emperor Frederick Barbarossa when she disapproved of his church policy. She is one of the small set of medieval intellectuals whose authority — institutional and personal — actually transcended the gender constraints of the period; she was widely regarded in her lifetime as a saint, was read continuously through the late Middle Ages, and was finally formally canonized in 2012 and named a Doctor of the Church.

The new monastic movements of the twelfth century — most importantly the **Cistercian Order**, founded in 1098 at the abbey of **Cîteaux** (see-TOH) in Burgundy by reformers seeking a stricter return to the Rule of Saint Benedict — provided much of the institutional support for the intellectual revolution. The Cistercians built sixty-eight monasteries in Bernard's lifetime and over five hundred by 1300. They were austere, agricultural, and organized around a constitutional charter (the *Carta Caritatis*) that linked daughter houses to the mother in a federal structure that anticipated some later medieval institutional designs. They pioneered medieval estate management, hydraulic engineering, and large-scale sheep farming, becoming some of the most economically dynamic landlords in Europe. They also produced theological writing and devotional literature that fed the new urban universities.

---

The vernacular literature of the twelfth century is worth noting because it represents Latin's first systematic competition for European literary attention.

Until roughly 1100, all serious writing in western Europe had been in Latin. Vernacular languages — Old French, Old English, Old High German, Provençal, Italian — were spoken everyday tongues, used for some practical writing, but not considered fit for literature. This changed in the twelfth century.

The change began in southern France with the **troubadours** (TROO-buh-dorz, from Provençal *trobador*, "finder" or "composer"). Beginning around 1100 with **Duke William IX of Aquitaine** (Eleanor of Aquitaine's grandfather, Chapter 8), the troubadours composed Occitan-language lyric poetry around the new theme of **fin'amor** (fee-na-MOR — "refined love" — what later French and English literature would call "courtly love"). The basic conventions: the lover is a knight; the beloved is a noble lady, often married to a more powerful lord; the love is intense, refined, often unrequited; the lover serves the lady through the lyric song. The convention spread north to the **trouvères** (troo-VAIR) of northern France writing in Old French, east to the German **Minnesänger** (MIN-eh-zeng-er), and eventually south to Italy, where it would become one of the foundations of Dante's *Divine Comedy* and the vernacular Italian literary tradition.

Northern France in the late twelfth century also produced the great **Arthurian romances**. **Geoffrey of Monmouth's** Latin *History of the Kings of Britain* (1136) seeded the Arthur legend; **Chrétien de Troyes** (kray-TYAN duh TWAH) developed it in the 1170s and 1180s into the cycle of Lancelot, Guinevere, Perceval, and the Holy Grail. The Arthurian cycle became the dominant prose-romance form of late-medieval European literature.

By 1200, vernacular literature was no longer a peripheral activity. Latin remained the dominant language of theology, law, philosophy, and the Church, but French was the dominant language of romance and chivalric literature, German of the courtly epic, Italian of the new lyric, and Provençal of love poetry. The thirteenth century would extend this to include Castilian (with Alfonso X's prose chronicles and law codes) and Middle English (which would not produce major literature until Chaucer in the fourteenth century, just outside this volume's window).

---

Cross-civ check: the twelfth-century European intellectual recovery is best understood by comparing it to the intellectual systems it was learning from.

The **Islamic world** was running a much more mature philosophical-theological synthesis through exactly the same period. **Al-Ghazali** (al-gha-ZAH-lee, d. 1111) had just published his *Incoherence of the Philosophers* — the great Islamic critique of the Greek philosophical tradition — and his autobiography *Deliverance from Error*, which together largely redirected Islamic intellectual life away from Hellenistic philosophy and toward Sufi spirituality. **Ibn Rushd** (Averroes, d. 1198) responded with *The Incoherence of the Incoherence*, defending Aristotelian rationalism against al-Ghazali, and produced commentaries on Aristotle that became more influential in Christian Europe than they were in the Islamic world after his death. The Latin West that was first encountering Aristotle was therefore encountering him via Islamic commentators who had already been arguing about him for centuries.

The **Jewish world** of the twelfth century produced **Maimonides** (my-MON-uh-deez, properly Moshe ben Maimon, 1138–1204), the great rabbinic scholar and physician born in Cordoba, exiled by Almohad persecution to Cairo, where he served as personal physician to Saladin's vizier. Maimonides's *Guide for the Perplexed* (written in Arabic around 1190) attempted the same synthesis between Aristotelian philosophy and revealed religion that Averroes was attempting from the Islamic side and Aquinas would attempt from the Christian side. It was rapidly translated into Hebrew and then Latin and read across Christian Europe within a generation. The three great twelfth- and thirteenth-century religious-philosophical syntheses — Aquinas's Christian, Averroes's Islamic, Maimonides's Jewish — were genuinely parallel projects working on the same problem (how to integrate Aristotle with revealed monotheism), within the same intellectual conversation, and largely reading each other.

In **India**, the twelfth century saw the great **Vedanta** synthesizers continuing the work of Shankara (eighth century) — most importantly Ramanuja (d. 1137) and later Madhva (thirteenth century) — building elaborate philosophical systems around the Upanishads (Medieval India Ch 7). The temple-cities of South India in the same period were producing some of the most sophisticated stone architecture in the world (Medieval India Ch 6), with thousand-pillared halls and 200-foot gopuram gate towers. The Cholas at this point were the dominant maritime power in the Bay of Bengal, with naval campaigns reaching Sumatra and Sri Lanka.

In **China**, the eleventh and twelfth centuries saw the **Neo-Confucian** synthesis under thinkers like Cheng Yi, Cheng Hao, and culminating in **Zhu Xi** (joo SHEE, d. 1200), the great Song dynasty philosopher whose systematic reading of the Confucian classics became the official philosophical framework of Chinese imperial civilization for the next seven centuries (Tang-Song China Ch 8). Zhu Xi and Aquinas died within a century of each other. They were both responding to the same kind of intellectual problem — how to systematize a textual tradition into a coherent philosophical theology — in cultures that had no contact with each other.

The European twelfth-century Renaissance was therefore one of three or four parallel intellectual revolutions happening across Eurasia in the eleventh and twelfth centuries. What was distinctive about the European version was that it was happening in a culture that had recently been intellectually impoverished and was building from a much lower base. The Latin West in 1200 was still well behind the Islamic and Chinese intellectual establishments. By 1300 it had largely caught up to Islamic philosophy through the scholastic synthesis. By 1500 (which is the Late Medieval volume's territory) it would be visibly diverging in directions that the others were not.

The next chapter turns to the visible monument of the twelfth-century recovery — the cathedrals.

# Chapter 6 — Cathedrals and the Gothic Revolution

In 1140, the Abbot **Suger** (soo-ZHAY) of the royal abbey of **Saint-Denis** outside Paris began rebuilding the choir of his church in a new way. He used **pointed arches** instead of round Romanesque ones. He used **ribbed vaults** that channeled the weight of the ceiling onto specific load points. He pierced the walls with **large stained-glass windows** that filled the interior with colored light. He left a written explanation of what he was doing — one of the very few surviving statements by a medieval architect about his own building philosophy — that argued the bright colored light entering the church was a direct experience of divine illumination, the worshipper's soul being drawn upward through the material light to the immaterial light of God himself. The technical and the theological program were the same program.

Saint-Denis is conventionally dated as the birth of **Gothic architecture**. The actual technical innovations Suger combined had been developed individually elsewhere: pointed arches existed in earlier Romanesque buildings, ribbed vaults existed in Norman architecture, large windows existed at scattered earlier sites. What Suger did at Saint-Denis was combine them all into a coherent architectural and theological system. Over the next century and a half, his combination spread across northern Europe and became one of the most successful architectural styles in human history.

This chapter is about how a small abbey choir on the outskirts of Paris turned into the dominant European architectural style for three centuries.

---

A bit of background on what Gothic was reacting against.

The dominant architecture of Latin Europe between roughly 1000 and 1140 had been **Romanesque** — a style derived from the surviving examples of Roman building, characterized by massive stone walls, round arches (the standard Roman form), barrel vaults or groin vaults, small windows, and a generally fortress-like solidity. The Romanesque cathedral was built like a stone box: thick walls bore the weight of the heavy stone vaults above, with the windows kept small to preserve the wall's structural strength. The interiors were dark and ponderous, decorated with painted murals because there was little light to display anything else. The great Romanesque churches — Speyer in Germany, Pisa in Italy, the Norman cathedrals at Caen and Durham, the abbey at Cluny in Burgundy (which at its third rebuilding around 1100 was the largest church in Europe) — were imposing and theologically purposeful but structurally limited. You could not raise the vault much above sixty or seventy feet without the walls beginning to bow outward under the lateral thrust, and you could not pierce the walls with large openings without compromising the structure.

Gothic solved both problems with the three innovations Suger combined.

The **pointed arch** allowed builders to vary the proportions of an arch independently — the same span could be arched at any height, by adjusting the angle of the point — which meant that vaults could be raised to whatever ceiling height the builders wanted without proportionally widening the arch. This let Gothic vaults climb to 100, 130, 150 feet — heights that Romanesque round arches could not reach in stone.

The **ribbed vault** concentrated the structural weight of the ceiling onto a skeleton of stone ribs that channeled it down to specific load-bearing points — the columns and pier bundles of the nave — rather than distributing it across the entire wall as Romanesque vaults did. This meant the walls between the load points could be hollowed out, removed, replaced with windows or with mere stone tracery, without any structural consequence.

The **flying buttress**, which appeared at Notre-Dame de Paris around 1180, completed the system. The flying buttress is an external arch that transfers the outward thrust of a high vault to a free-standing pier well outside the wall of the church. It looks (and looks better in nineteenth-century photographs than in person) like the cathedral has stone arms reaching out in all directions to brace itself. The mechanical genius of the flying buttress is that it lets the wall of the church be reduced to almost nothing — the structural work is being done outside, by the buttresses, and the wall is freed to be replaced by stained glass.

The combination — pointed arch plus ribbed vault plus flying buttress — produced a building that looked nothing like Romanesque. The Gothic cathedral interior is high, light, and skeletal. Stone columns run up the inside walls and branch into the rib pattern of the vault overhead. Between the columns, the wall is replaced almost entirely by stained-glass windows. Light pours in through the colored glass and fills the interior with intentional theological color — blue for heaven, red for the blood of Christ, gold for divine light. The Romanesque cathedral was a stone fortress. The Gothic cathedral was a stone cage of light.

---

The major cathedrals of the High Gothic period, in roughly chronological order:

**Saint-Denis** (begun 1140) — Suger's abbey church just north of Paris, the prototype. The choir survives largely as Suger built it; the nave was rebuilt in the High Gothic style in the next century.

**Notre-Dame de Paris** (begun 1163) — Construction began in 1163 under Bishop Maurice de Sully on the Île de la Cité in Paris. It took roughly two centuries to complete. Notre-Dame was one of the first cathedrals to use the flying buttress, around 1180. The fire of April 15, 2019, destroyed the spire and most of the medieval roof timbers; the stone vault and the great rose windows survived, and the cathedral was reopened on December 7, 2024.

**Chartres Cathedral** (rebuilt after fire 1194) — When fire destroyed most of the older church on the night of June 10–11, 1194, the bishop and chapter took the unusual decision to rebuild faster and grander than before. The new cathedral was substantially complete by 1220 — twenty-six years from foundation to working liturgy, an extraordinarily fast pace for a building of this size. Chartres has the densest surviving medieval glass collection on earth: 167 windows, including the deep blue glass that scholars still cannot fully reproduce (the recipe involved cobalt added during glassmaking; the exact medieval method has been lost). The Chartres west portal sculptures and the south transept Bible windows together form the most important coherent visual program of medieval theology that survives intact in any single building.

**Reims Cathedral** (begun 1211) — The coronation church of every French king from the thirteenth century to Charles X in 1825. The Reims west front sculpture is one of the great High Gothic sculpture cycles.

**Amiens Cathedral** (begun 1220) — The largest interior space in northern Europe, with a 138-foot vault — taller than a fourteen-story modern building. Amiens represents High Gothic at its most engineered.

**Beauvais Cathedral** (begun 1225) — Beauvais pushed Gothic engineering past its limits. The choir vault was raised to 153 feet (the highest vault in any Gothic cathedral) and partially collapsed in 1284. The cathedral was repaired, the central tower added in 1569, and that tower then collapsed in 1573. The rest of the cathedral was never built. Beauvais is what Gothic looks like when ambition outruns the engineering budget.

**Sainte-Chapelle** (1242–1248) — King Louis IX of France built Sainte-Chapelle on the Île de la Cité in Paris to house his collection of Passion relics, including the Crown of Thorns purchased from the Latin Emperor of Constantinople in 1238 for what was reportedly more than half a year's French royal revenue. The chapel's upper level is essentially a glass box held up by stone, the most spectacular surviving demonstration of **Rayonnant Gothic** — the more delicate, more glass-dominated phase of the style that succeeded the High Gothic of Chartres and Amiens.

**Cologne Cathedral** (begun 1248) — Construction began in 1248 to house the relics of the Three Magi (taken from Milan in 1164 by Frederick Barbarossa's chancellor). The building was abandoned in the sixteenth century with construction roughly half complete; the choir, the south wall, and the foundations of the nave were finished. It was not completed until the nineteenth century, when the original medieval plans were rediscovered in a granary and followed almost exactly. Cologne Cathedral as it stands today is therefore both a medieval and a nineteenth-century building, on a thirteenth-century plan.

**Salisbury Cathedral** (begun 1220) — The major English cathedral entirely built in a single coherent Gothic style, over a remarkably short thirty-eight-year period to its main consecration in 1258. Most other English cathedrals (Canterbury, York, Durham, Westminster) are stylistic patchworks of Romanesque, Early English, Decorated, and Perpendicular Gothic from successive rebuilding campaigns.

The High Gothic technical peak is reached at Amiens around 1240 and at Beauvais (briefly, before the collapse) around 1284. After about 1300, French Gothic shifts into the more decorative **Rayonnant** and then **Flamboyant** phases that the Late Medieval volume picks up; English Gothic shifts into **Decorated** and then **Perpendicular**. The structural innovation is largely complete by 1280; what comes after is elaboration on the established system.

---

The construction of a Gothic cathedral was an enormous undertaking — the largest sustained capital project most medieval cities ever attempted.

A typical High Gothic cathedral required somewhere between twenty thousand and a hundred thousand cubic meters of cut stone, quarried from sites that were sometimes hundreds of miles away and shipped by river barge. It required tens of thousands of square feet of stained glass, made in specialized regional workshops. It required tens of thousands of tons of timber for scaffolding, centering for the arches during construction, and the eventual roof framing. It required the labor of hundreds of skilled stonemasons, glaziers, carpenters, and ironworkers, plus thousands of unskilled laborers, organized into traveling guilds that moved from cathedral project to cathedral project across the High Medieval period. It typically took fifty to two hundred years to complete; many cathedrals were started in one century and finished in another by builders working from the original master plan with whatever modifications successive generations imposed.

The financing came from a combination of episcopal endowments, royal grants, papal indulgences, private donations from wealthy townsmen, and the collection plates of pilgrims. Pilgrim relics — fragments of the True Cross, pieces of Mary's veil at Chartres, the Three Magi at Cologne, the Crown of Thorns at Sainte-Chapelle — were both spiritual focuses of the building and economic engines that drew the donations and pilgrim spending that funded ongoing construction. The cathedral was the center of the medieval city's religious, cultural, and economic life all at once.

A common popular myth is worth addressing here: the medieval cathedrals were *not* built by anonymous communal volunteer labor in some kind of pre-modern collective religious fervor. They were built by professional specialist guilds, paid in cash for skilled work, on contracts negotiated and supervised by the cathedral chapter and the master mason. The medieval guilds — organized fraternities of masons, carpenters, glaziers, sculptors, and ironworkers — were a real and central feature of cathedral construction, and the master mason was a paid professional architect-engineer who ran the project on a contractual basis, not a pious enthusiast. The "everyone helped to build the cathedral" image is a nineteenth-century romantic invention, not a medieval reality. What is true is that the unskilled hauling and carrying labor sometimes drew on local volunteers as a form of pilgrimage, especially during the famous outbursts of building enthusiasm in the 1140s and again at Chartres after 1194, when chronicle accounts describe townspeople voluntarily harnessing themselves to wagons hauling stone from quarries. But the skilled construction itself was always paid professional work.

---

The Gothic cathedral was not just a building. It was a **comprehensive theological program** rendered in stone, glass, and sculpture.

The **plan** of the cathedral was a Latin cross — a long nave intersected by transepts, with the altar at the eastern end pointing toward Jerusalem. The **west portal** sculptures presented the Last Judgment, with Christ as judge, the saved on his right, the damned on his left, and the resurrection of the dead in the lintel below. The **north transept** sculptures typically presented the Old Testament: Adam and Eve, Moses, the prophets. The **south transept** sculptures presented the New Testament: the Annunciation, the Nativity, the apostles. The **interior columns and capitals** displayed scenes from the lives of saints, the virtues and vices, sometimes the labors of the months and the signs of the zodiac. The **stained-glass windows** ran a continuous biblical narrative from Genesis to the Last Judgment in pictures that the largely illiterate congregation could read directly. A medieval worshipper walking through Chartres or Amiens was walking through the entire Christian theological program rendered in three dimensions.

Some of the iconography is technical enough to be worth pausing on. The **rose window** — the great circular stained-glass window typically placed above the western door, with smaller rose windows above each transept — was visual theology: the rose was a symbol of the Virgin Mary (the "rose without thorns" of the Marian tradition), and the geometric structure of the window radiating from a central medallion was understood as a diagram of the divine order radiating from the centerpoint of God. The **labyrinth** inset in the floor of the nave at Chartres and several other cathedrals was a meditative pilgrimage path that the faithful could walk on their knees as a devotional substitute for going to Jerusalem. The **vault keystones** at the crossing of each rib pattern were often carved with sacred images that were understood to bind the building together both structurally and theologically. The **gargoyles** on the exterior — grotesque demonic figures projecting from the corners of the roofline — were both functional rainwater spouts and theological warnings: the demonic remained outside the consecrated interior of the church.

The **pilgrimage circuit** around the eastern end of the choir, with radiating chapels each containing a different relic and altar, was designed to let the increasing pilgrim traffic of the High Middle Ages flow through the cathedral without disrupting the daily liturgy at the main altar. This is a piece of practical traffic engineering that the architects of Saint-Denis essentially invented and that became standard High Gothic practice.

---

Cross-civ check: while the Gothic cathedrals were going up across northern Europe, comparable monumental religious architecture was being built in other civilizations with very different structural and theological logics.

In **South India** during exactly the same period, the Chola, Hoysala, and later Vijayanagara empires were building massive **temple complexes** — Brihadeeswarar at Thanjavur (1010), Hoysaleswara at Halebidu (1121), the temple-city of Madurai — characterized by massive carved-stone gopuram gate towers reaching 200 feet, thousand-pillared halls (the Chola "Thousand-Pillared Hall" at Madurai actually has 985 pillars, but the figure is conventional), and façades carved with thousands of sculpted figures depicting the entire Hindu mythological corpus (Medieval India Ch 6). The South Indian temple was an attempt at total visual representation of the divine cosmos using continuous figural sculpture. The Gothic cathedral was an attempt at the same goal using glass, light, and structural skeleton. Both produced spectacular monuments. Both were, in their respective traditions, the highest forms of religious art.

In the **Islamic world** of the same period, monumental architecture took a very different direction. The Great Mosque of Cordoba had been completed in stages between the eighth and tenth centuries; the Almohad-era mosques of Marrakech and Seville (the latter's minaret survives as the Giralda tower, completed 1198) were being built during the High Gothic period. Islamic religious architecture preferred horizontal expansion (vast open prayer halls), geometric pattern decoration (because figural representation was generally avoided in religious buildings), and dome-and-minaret silhouettes inherited from Byzantine and Sassanian models. The Gothic vertical aspiration toward stone-and-glass walls of light is essentially absent from Islamic religious architecture, which had its own quite different aesthetic priorities.

In **China** under the Song dynasty, monumental religious architecture was built in wood — the great Buddhist temples and pagodas of the period (the Yingxian wooden pagoda of 1056 still survives, 220 feet tall, the oldest surviving wooden tower in the world) used elaborate timber-bracket construction (dougong) to achieve heights and spans comparable to Gothic cathedrals using completely different structural logic. Chinese sacred architecture generally avoided stone for religious building because of the cultural preference for wood and the philosophical preference for harmony with the natural materials of the site. Tang-Song Chinese pagodas and temples could be as tall and ambitious as Gothic cathedrals; they just looked nothing alike.

In **Mesoamerica**, the **Postclassic** period (roughly 900–1521) saw the construction of major Maya complexes at Chichén Itzá and Mayapán (Maya Civilization Ch 6), with continued pyramid-building in the long Mesoamerican tradition. The Aztec capital at Tenochtitlán would not be founded until 1325 — slightly outside this volume's window — but the Toltec-influenced architecture that would shortly inspire it was being built at Tula in central Mexico through the eleventh and twelfth centuries.

The High Gothic cathedral is therefore best understood not as a uniquely European achievement but as one of several great civilizational responses, in roughly the same period, to the problem of representing the divine in monumental architecture. What was distinctive about the Gothic version was the structural engineering — the achievement of unprecedented vault heights and unprecedented wall transparency through the pointed-arch / ribbed-vault / flying-buttress system. Most of the Gothic cathedrals would still be standing 800 years later. So would most of the South Indian temples, and the Yingxian pagoda, and the Cordoba mosque. Monumental religious architecture, when well-built, lasts.

---

The High Gothic moment was also the high-water mark of **medieval craft guilds** as economic and cultural institutions.

The guild was a voluntary association of practitioners of a single craft — masons, glaziers, weavers, dyers, goldsmiths, illuminators — that regulated training, set standards, controlled quality, restricted entry, and provided mutual aid for sick or destitute members. The medieval guild was simultaneously a labor organization, a trade association, a mutual-aid society, a religious confraternity (most guilds had a patron saint and a guild altar in the local cathedral), and a political institution within the chartered city. By 1300, most western European cities were governed by some combination of patrician magnates and the guilds of the leading crafts. The cathedral construction industry sat at the high end of the guild economy: the master masons and the leading sculptors were among the most highly paid and most internationally mobile professionals of the medieval world.

A famous master mason — like **Villard de Honnecourt**, whose surviving sketchbook from the 1230s contains drawings of cathedral details, geometric problems, mechanical devices, and architectural elements from his travels across northern France and Germany — could expect employment offers from across the continent. The cathedral master masons were the international rock stars of medieval applied science.

# Chapter 7 — Universities and Scholasticism

The medieval university is one of the most consequential institutional inventions of the High Middle Ages and one of the very few medieval institutions that has survived continuously into the present. The universities of 2026 — Bologna, Paris, Oxford, Salamanca, Cambridge — are the same institutions, in the same cities, doing recognizably the same kind of work, that opened in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. They are older than any other continuously existing secular institution in the western world. They are older than every modern nation-state. The University of Bologna in 2026 is celebrating its 938th anniversary.

The university was something genuinely new in the world. The classical world had had philosophical schools (Plato's Academy, Aristotle's Lyceum), the early-medieval cathedral schools had taught Latin and basic theology to clerics, and the great Islamic *madrasas* of the eleventh century were teaching Sharia and theology in formally constituted institutions. But the medieval European university was something different from all of these — a self-governing chartered corporation of teachers and students, organized as a guild with its own legal identity, granting credentials recognized across western Christendom, with a defined multi-year curriculum, in subjects that ranged from law and medicine to theology and philosophy. By 1300, every kingdom in Latin Europe wanted one.

This chapter is about how the universities were invented, what they taught, and what they did with the rediscovered classical and Islamic learning of the previous chapter.

---

The first formal European university was **Bologna**, traditionally dated to **1088**.

Bologna grew out of the recovery of Roman law (Chapter 5). The lawyer **Irnerius** had begun teaching Justinian's *Corpus Juris Civilis* there around 1080. By 1088 there was a recognizable school of legal studies attracting students from across Europe. By 1158, the Holy Roman Emperor Frederick Barbarossa issued the *Authentica Habita*, granting Bologna's students legal protection from local jurisdiction during their studies — one of the first imperial charters of academic privilege. By 1200, Bologna was the leading school of Roman and canon law in Europe and the model that other universities tried to copy.

The peculiar feature of Bologna was that it was a **student-run university**. The **universitas** ("the whole" — Latin for "guild" or "corporation," from which the word university derives) at Bologna was a guild of students, not a guild of professors. The students collectively hired their own professors, set the curriculum, fixed the tuition rates, fined professors for showing up late or skipping difficult material, and could collectively withdraw their patronage if they were dissatisfied. This sounds modern but produced very different institutional dynamics — the Bologna professors were essentially professional services hired and fired by their students, who were mostly older men preparing for careers in canon law or imperial administration, often already in lower clerical orders, often with money and political connections.

The second great early university was **Paris**, formalized around the mid-twelfth century.

Paris grew out of the cathedral school of Notre-Dame, which had been a serious teaching institution since at least the time of Peter Abelard (Chapter 5) in the early twelfth century. The masters teaching theology and the liberal arts in Paris organized themselves into a guild of teachers, distinct from the Bologna model. The Paris **universitas** was a guild of professors who admitted students into a defined curriculum and granted them credentials at the end. King Philip II issued the first royal recognition in 1200; Pope Innocent III formally recognized the university in 1215. By 1230 Paris was the leading school of theology and philosophy in Europe and the model for most northern European universities.

**Oxford** emerged in the mid-twelfth century from the migration of English students after Henry II banned them from Paris in 1167. **Cambridge** broke off from Oxford in 1209 after a town-and-gown riot. The two English universities followed the Paris model of master-led institutions and developed the distinctive English **collegiate system** in the thirteenth century, when wealthy donors began endowing residential colleges (Merton at Oxford in 1264, Peterhouse at Cambridge in 1284) as residential foundations for poor students.

**Salamanca** in Spain was founded by King Alfonso IX of León in 1218 and reaffirmed by Alfonso X in 1254, becoming the leading university of the Iberian peninsula. Salamanca was distinctive for its connections to the Toledo translation movement and its role in transmitting Arabic and Hebrew scholarship into the Latin academic mainstream.

By 1300, the major university map of Latin Europe looked roughly like this: Bologna for law, Paris for theology and philosophy, Oxford and Cambridge for theology and the liberal arts, Salamanca for Iberian scholarship, **Salerno** in southern Italy for medicine (Salerno had been a leading medical school since the late ninth century, with a distinctive Greek-Arabic-Latin synthesis going back to the eleventh century), **Padua** founded in 1222 (after a secession from Bologna) as a rival law school, and **Naples** founded by Frederick II in 1224 as the first explicitly state-founded university. Other university foundations followed across the late thirteenth and fourteenth centuries — Coimbra, Toulouse, Montpellier, Prague, Vienna, Heidelberg, Cracow — but the High Medieval template had been set by 1300.

---

The medieval university curriculum was inherited from late antiquity and elaborated in the cathedral schools.

A student arrived at the university typically around age fourteen or fifteen, after preparatory study in a cathedral or grammar school, and entered the **arts faculty** for a course of study in the **seven liberal arts** organized in two groups: the **trivium** (TRIH-vee-um — grammar, logic, rhetoric — the three verbal arts) and the **quadrivium** (kwah-DRIH-vee-um — arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, music — the four mathematical arts). Students completed the trivium for the **bachelor's degree** (typically three to four years), then continued through the quadrivium for the **master's degree** (another two to three years), at which point they were licensed to teach the liberal arts themselves. Most students stopped at this point and went off to careers in the church bureaucracy, royal administration, or the towns.

The most ambitious students continued into one of the three **higher faculties**: theology (at Paris and Oxford), law (at Bologna and Padua), or medicine (at Salerno and Montpellier). The higher degrees took another five to fifteen years, depending on the field. A doctorate of theology from Paris in the thirteenth century took thirteen to sixteen years of study after the master of arts and required defending a series of public disputations on disputed theological questions. The medieval universities took a long time.

The teaching method was the **lecture**, the **disputation**, and the **commentary**. The **lecture** (Latin *lectio*, "reading") was literally the master reading a designated authoritative text aloud, sentence by sentence, with explanatory commentary — Aristotle's *Physics*, Justinian's *Digest*, Peter Lombard's *Sentences*, depending on the faculty. Students copied down the lecture as fast as they could write. The **disputation** (Latin *disputatio*, "argument") was a structured oral debate on a specific question, with one master defending a thesis, opponents raising objections, and a master determining the answer. The **commentary** was the written exposition of an authoritative text, with the master's reading of difficult passages and his resolution of apparent contradictions.

The pedagogical assumption was that authoritative texts were internally consistent and that apparent contradictions could be resolved by close reading and dialectical analysis. This is the method that Peter Abelard had pioneered in *Sic et Non* (Chapter 5) and that became the standard scholastic technique. The student was not learning to think original thoughts; the student was learning to master the authoritative texts and to defend orthodox positions in formal disputation. This is a more constrained intellectual ideal than the modern university aspires to. It was also extraordinarily effective at producing the trained cadre of lawyers, theologians, doctors, and administrators that staffed the institutions of late-medieval Europe.

---

The new institution that revolutionized university teaching in the thirteenth century was the **mendicant order** — specifically, the **Dominicans** and the **Franciscans**.

**Dominic of Caleruega** (1170–1221) was a Spanish canon who founded the Order of Preachers (the Dominicans) in 1216 with papal approval, organized for educated preaching against the Albigensian heresy in southern France (Chapter 3). The Dominicans took theological learning as their core institutional identity from the start. They sent their best students to the universities, established Dominican houses in every university town, and within a generation were occupying many of the chairs of theology at Paris and Oxford.

**Francis of Assisi** (1181–1226) had a different starting point. Born to a wealthy cloth merchant in central Italy, Francis renounced his inheritance in a public scene around 1206 (he stripped naked in the town square and gave his clothes back to his father) and founded the Franciscan Order in 1209 around radical voluntary poverty, preaching to ordinary people in their own languages, and care for the sick and the outcast. Within a decade the Franciscans were one of the largest religious orders in Europe. The original Franciscan emphasis had been on simple itinerant preaching rather than scholarly study; this changed in the second generation, when the Franciscans, like the Dominicans, moved into the universities and began producing some of the period's most original theology.

Both orders together — the Dominicans (the **Order of Preachers**, dedicated to learned preaching) and the Franciscans (the **Order of Friars Minor**, dedicated to apostolic poverty) — are called the **mendicant orders** because they were originally supposed to live by alms (Latin *mendicare*, to beg) rather than by endowed monastery lands. The mendicant orders transformed medieval religious life by relocating the most ambitious preachers and theologians from rural monasteries into the new urban universities, where they took most of the chairs of theology at Paris and Oxford in the thirteenth century. The two greatest scholastic theologians of the High Middle Ages — Albertus Magnus and Thomas Aquinas — were both Dominicans. The two most influential Franciscan thinkers of the period — Bonaventure and (just outside this volume's window) Duns Scotus — produced a parallel Franciscan synthesis that disagreed with the Dominican one on several major points.

---

**Thomas Aquinas** (1225–1274) is the central intellectual figure of the High Middle Ages.

Born to a noble family in southern Italy, Thomas was educated at Monte Cassino, then at the new University of Naples, and joined the Dominican Order around 1244 against the violent objections of his family (who locked him up at home for over a year trying to make him change his mind). He went to Paris to study under **Albertus Magnus** (Albert the Great, the German Dominican who was the first major Christian thinker to engage seriously with the recently translated Aristotle) and followed Albert to Cologne. He returned to Paris as a master of theology in 1256, taught there in two extended periods, and spent the rest of his life lecturing, writing, and producing the systematic theology that would shape Catholic intellectual life for the next seven centuries.

Aquinas's central project was the **synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology**. The recently recovered Aristotle (Chapter 5) had been controversial at Paris in the 1210s and 1220s — Aristotelian texts had been formally banned by the Paris faculty in 1210 because of their apparent incompatibility with Christian doctrine on points like the eternity of the world and the personal immortality of the soul. By Aquinas's time the bans were lapsing, but the integration problem remained. Aquinas attempted to show that Aristotle's philosophy and Christian theology were not just compatible but mutually illuminating — that natural reason (Aristotle) and revealed faith (Christianity) led to the same God by different paths.

The result was the **Summa Theologiae** (begun 1265, unfinished at his death in 1274 — a working manuscript of roughly 1.8 million words). The Summa's structure is the formal scholastic disputation taken to its furthest extension: Aquinas poses thousands of theological questions, presents the strongest objections to his preferred answer, gives his answer, then responds to the objections individually. The five "Five Ways" (the five proofs for the existence of God in question 2 of part 1) are the most famous single section. The whole work is a vast Gothic cathedral of structured argument, reading every theological question through both natural reason and revealed scripture and arguing for the harmony of the two.

Aquinas's project was not universally welcomed at the time. Several of his specific theses were condemned by the Bishop of Paris in 1277, three years after Aquinas's death. The Franciscan school under Bonaventure favored a more Augustinian, Plato-influenced theology that distrusted Aristotle as much as Bernard of Clairvaux had distrusted Abelard. There was a continuing thirteenth-century debate, never fully resolved, about how much Aristotle Christian theology could safely absorb.

What is now obvious in hindsight, but was not obvious at the time, is that Aquinas had won. Aquinas was canonized in 1323 and named a Doctor of the Church in 1567. The Council of Trent (1545–1563) placed his Summa on the altar alongside the Bible during deliberations. Pope Leo XIII's 1879 encyclical *Aeterni Patris* declared Thomism the official philosophical framework of Roman Catholic theology — which it remains in 2026, eight centuries after Aquinas first arrived in Paris as a student.

---

The structural parallel worth pausing on is that the Christian-Aristotelian synthesis Aquinas was building had two contemporary parallels — one Islamic, one Jewish — working on exactly the same problem in exactly the same generation.

**Ibn Rushd** (Averroes, 1126–1198), the great Andalusian Muslim philosopher, had attempted a parallel synthesis of Aristotle with Islamic theology (Islamic Golden Age Ch 7). His position was more radical than Aquinas's would be — Averroes accepted some Aristotelian conclusions (like the eternity of the world) that Aquinas would reject as incompatible with revealed religion, and his commentaries on Aristotle were so extensive and so influential that he was simply called "the Commentator" by the medieval Latins. Averroes's positions on the unity of the active intellect across humanity (which seemed to imply that personal immortality was an illusion) became the focus of one of the great intellectual debates of thirteenth-century Paris, with the so-called **Latin Averroists** (most prominently Siger of Brabant) defending Averroes's reading and Aquinas writing his treatise *On the Unity of the Intellect against the Averroists* (1270) to refute it.

**Maimonides** (Moshe ben Maimon, 1138–1204), the great Jewish philosopher writing in Cairo (Chapter 5), had attempted a parallel synthesis of Aristotle with Jewish theology in his **Guide for the Perplexed** (composed in Arabic around 1190, rapidly translated into Hebrew and then Latin). Maimonides's position was closer to what Aquinas would eventually argue: that revealed religion and natural reason were ultimately consistent, and that apparent contradictions between scripture and Aristotle could usually be resolved through allegorical reading of the difficult biblical passages. Aquinas read Maimonides extensively and cited him by name as "Rabbi Moses" repeatedly in the Summa.

The three great twelfth- and thirteenth-century religious-philosophical syntheses — Christian (Aquinas), Islamic (Averroes), Jewish (Maimonides) — were therefore parallel projects working on the same problem, within the same intellectual conversation, reading each other across linguistic and religious boundaries that did not stop the exchange of ideas. This is one of the most striking features of high-medieval intellectual life and one of the things modern accounts often underplay. The medieval Mediterranean was not the sealed religious-civilizational compartments that later periods would imagine; it was a connected intellectual ecology in which Spanish Jews translated Arabic Aristotle for Italian Christians, and Aquinas at Paris cited Maimonides at Cairo on a question Averroes at Cordoba had also addressed.

---

A common modern myth is worth addressing: the medieval university was not "open to all" in any sense the modern reader would recognize.

The student body of the medieval university was almost entirely male. The very few documented exceptions are exceptional precisely because they are exceptional. Women were formally excluded from matriculation across nearly all medieval universities (Salerno's medical faculty admitted some women, including the famous Trotula in the eleventh century, as the most documented exception). The exclusion of women was not just about the formal student body but about the entire institutional structure — the universities were organized around clerical orders, since faculty members had to be in lower clerical orders even if not actually ordained priests, and women were canonically barred from clerical orders.

The student body was also socially restricted. Latin literacy was the entry barrier — and Latin literacy was acquired in cathedral schools and grammar schools that mostly served the children of the clergy, the lesser nobility, and the urban patriciate. A peasant boy might in theory rise into the universities (the old chronicle accounts treasure the occasional case), but in practice the social filter was heavy. The medieval universities served a clerical-bourgeois-noble male population, not a representative cross-section of medieval society.

The universities also had a complicated relationship with the city populations around them. The medieval **town-and-gown** conflict — the recurrent tension between the privileged exempt-from-local-jurisdiction student population and the resentful local townspeople — was a regular feature of university towns from the start. The 1209 Cambridge secession from Oxford was triggered by a town-and-gown brawl. The 1229 Paris student strike (after a tavern brawl in which several students were killed by royal troops) led to a two-year suspension of teaching and a permanent reorganization of the relationship between the university and the French crown. The 1265 Oxford riots saw the killing of a number of students by townspeople. The privileged status of medieval university students made them frequently arrogant, sometimes violent, and recurrently resented by the cities they lived in.

What is true is that the medieval universities created, for the first time in Europe, a credential — the master's degree — that conferred a set of legal privileges (exemption from local taxation, the right to teach anywhere in Christendom, the right to be tried in church courts rather than secular ones) recognized internationally. A student who completed his master's at Paris could go on to teach at Oxford or Bologna without re-credentialing. This is a small thing that turned out to be very consequential. The international academic credential is a medieval invention.

# Chapter 8 — Magna Carta to Mongol Shock

On June 15, 1215, in a meadow at **Runnymede** (RUN-ee-meed) on the south bank of the Thames near Windsor, **King John of England** put his seal to a charter that his rebellious barons had forced on him. The charter — the **Magna Carta** ("Great Charter") in Latin — established that the king was bound by law, that no free man could be imprisoned or dispossessed without the lawful judgment of his peers, that taxation required the consent of those taxed, that the church had certain liberties from royal interference, and a long list of more specific provisions about wardship, inheritance, foreign mercenaries, and the conduct of royal officials.

John repudiated it within ten weeks. He was dead within fifteen months. His nine-year-old son Henry III's regency reissued Magna Carta in 1216 and again in 1225, and the reissued versions became the foundation of the English constitutional tradition. The 1215 document itself is a contingent piece of medieval baronial politics. The institutional principle it established — that even the king is under the law — became one of the most consequential ideas in western political history.

Twenty-six years after Runnymede, in April 1241, two Mongol armies under generals **Batu Khan** and **Subutai** annihilated a Polish-Moravian-Templar force at the Battle of **Legnica** (LEHG-neet-sah, in modern Poland) on April 9 and a Hungarian royal army at the Battle of **Mohi** (MOH-hee, in modern Hungary) on April 11 — two complete victories two days apart. King Béla IV of Hungary fled to the Adriatic coast. The Mongols devastated central Europe for a year. They turned back in spring 1242 only because of the death of the Great Khan **Ögedei** (UH-guh-day) in Mongolia, which recalled Batu to the steppe for the succession kurultai (Mongol Empire Ch 4). The reprieve was political and accidental. There was nothing in central Europe in 1242 that could have stopped the Mongols if they had decided to keep coming.

This chapter is about the two parallel structural shifts of the thirteenth century — the constitutional shift inside western Europe, exemplified by Magna Carta and the rise of representative bodies, and the geopolitical shock from outside, exemplified by the Mongol arrival. By 1300, both shifts had reshaped the political landscape of Latin Europe in ways the rest of the medieval period would be living with.

---

Start with the constitutional story.

**King John of England** (reigned 1199–1216) was the youngest of the four legitimate sons of Henry II and Eleanor of Aquitaine and one of the worst kings ever to reign in England. He was also unlucky. He inherited the throne from his older brother Richard the Lionheart in 1199 with most of the resources of the Angevin Empire — England, Normandy, Anjou, Maine, Touraine, Aquitaine — and a hostile Philip II of France actively working to dismember it.

The dismemberment proceeded faster than anyone expected. Between 1202 and 1204, Philip II's armies overran Normandy, Anjou, Maine, and Touraine, leaving John holding only England, Ireland, and parts of Aquitaine. The lost territories included most of the wealthy continental French heartland of the Plantagenet dynasty. John spent the next decade trying to recover them and failing repeatedly. His attempt at a major continental coalition with the Holy Roman Emperor Otto IV ended at the **Battle of Bouvines** on July 27, 1214, where Philip II destroyed the coalition army in northern France. John was personally absent from Bouvines (he was running a parallel campaign in southwestern France that also went badly), but Bouvines confirmed the loss of the continental Angevin territories and triggered the political crisis at home that produced Magna Carta the following year.

John's domestic problems were just as bad as his foreign ones. He had quarreled with **Pope Innocent III** over the appointment of the Archbishop of Canterbury — when Innocent insisted on his own candidate, Stephen Langton, against John's preferred choice, John refused to accept Langton; Innocent excommunicated John in 1209 and placed England under **interdict** (suspension of all church services, including baptisms, marriages, funerals, and the Mass) from 1208 to 1213. John eventually backed down and submitted to Innocent in May 1213, accepting Langton, paying compensation, and formally surrendering England as a fief of the papacy — which he then held back from the pope in vassalage. The submission was humiliating but politically clever; it made Innocent John's defender against subsequent baronial revolt. The barons would later complain that the pope was protecting an unfit king against their legitimate grievances.

The grievances themselves were real. John had taxed England punishingly to fund his failed continental wars. He had abused royal wardship and marriage rights to enrich himself at the expense of noble families. He had imposed arbitrary fines, sold royal offices to the highest bidder, and repeatedly violated feudal custom. He had personally killed his nephew Arthur of Brittany (the rival claimant to the throne) in 1203, in circumstances that everyone in Europe believed were murder. And after Bouvines, he had returned home wanting to launch yet another war that would require yet more taxation.

The barons revolted in spring 1215 under **Robert Fitzwalter**, captured London in May, and forced John to negotiate at Runnymede in June. The document they presented him is what we now call **Magna Carta** — sixty-three clauses, a mixture of specific feudal grievances and broader principles of restraint on royal power. The most famous clauses are clause 39 (no free man shall be imprisoned or dispossessed except by the lawful judgment of his equals or by the law of the land) and clause 40 (to no one will we sell, to no one will we deny or delay right or justice). These two clauses, in slightly modified form, would become the foundation of the English common-law principle of **due process** and the foundation of the right against arbitrary detention (**habeas corpus**, technically a later development).

John repudiated Magna Carta within ten weeks, on the grounds that it had been extorted from him under duress. He persuaded Pope Innocent III to annul it in August 1215. The barons rebelled again, invited the French Prince Louis (the future Louis VIII of France) to invade England and claim the throne, and were saved from the awkward consequences only by John's sudden death from dysentery in October 1216, at age forty-nine.

The regency of John's nine-year-old son **Henry III** was much more politically skilled than John had been. The regents under William Marshal reissued Magna Carta in November 1216 in modified form, as a goodwill gesture to settle the rebellion, drove out the French invasion, and reissued the charter again in 1217 and definitively in 1225 in the version that became permanently English constitutional law. Henry III repeatedly confirmed the 1225 reissue throughout his sixty-six-year reign (1216–1272). His son **Edward I** confirmed it again in 1297, in the *Confirmatio Cartarum*. Magna Carta was a settled part of English law by 1300.

---

The institutional consequence of Magna Carta was the gradual emergence of a **representative assembly** in England.

Clause 14 of Magna Carta required the king to summon "the archbishops, bishops, abbots, earls, and greater barons" and the lesser tenants-in-chief through the sheriffs to consent to extraordinary taxation. This was the seed of **Parliament**. The principle — that taxation required consent — required a body to give the consent. The body that emerged over the thirteenth century was Parliament, an assembly of the king with the magnates, prelates, and (eventually) elected representatives of the shires and the towns.

The crucial expansion was **Simon de Montfort's Parliament** of January 1265.

**Simon de Montfort**, Earl of Leicester, was an Anglo-French baron who had led a baronial revolt against Henry III, defeated the king at the Battle of Lewes in 1264, and effectively governed England as a kind of medieval parliamentary regent for fifteen months. To shore up support against royalist resistance, de Montfort summoned a parliament in January 1265 that for the first time included representatives of the **shires and the boroughs** alongside the magnates and clergy. This is the first English assembly to include elected commoners and is conventionally regarded as the institutional origin of the House of Commons. De Montfort was killed in battle at Evesham on August 4, 1265, but his model became the structural basis of the English Parliament under Edward I (1272–1307), whose **Model Parliament** of 1295 — including bishops, abbots, peers, two knights from each shire, two burgesses from each borough, and elected representatives of the lesser clergy — became the standard form of English parliamentary representation for centuries.

The English development was paralleled, with significant variations, across other parts of western Europe.

The **Cortes** in Iberia were assembling representatives of the noble, ecclesiastical, and urban estates from the late twelfth century — the Cortes of León in 1188 was the first European assembly to include burghers from the towns, predating Simon de Montfort's parliament by seventy-seven years. The Cortes of Castile and the Cortes of Aragon developed alongside.

The **Estates-General** of France would not be formally summoned until 1302 (slightly outside this volume's window), but provincial estates assemblies had been operating in various French regions throughout the thirteenth century.

The **Diet of the Holy Roman Empire** had been operating since the early twelfth century but was an assembly of the major imperial princes rather than a representative body in the constitutional sense; the imperial cities would not gain formal representation until the later fourteenth century.

The **Hungarian Diet** secured its own constitutional charter, the **Golden Bull of 1222**, only seven years after Magna Carta — establishing similar restraints on royal power and the right of the nobility to resist unlawful royal acts. The 1222 Golden Bull is the parallel Hungarian document to Magna Carta and developed into the constitutional foundation of the medieval Kingdom of Hungary.

By 1300, every major western European kingdom had some form of representative or quasi-representative assembly with at least conditional power to constrain royal taxation and (in some cases) royal legislation. This is the medieval foundation of the modern parliamentary tradition. It was not democracy in any modern sense — the franchise was extremely narrow, the assemblies were dominated by the noble and ecclesiastical estates, and the king retained vast prerogative powers. But it established the principle that taxation requires consent and that there must be some formal body to give that consent. That principle would be the seed of what would eventually grow into modern constitutional government.

---

A note on the figure who pulled the thirteenth-century papal apparatus to its peak.

**Pope Innocent III** (1198–1216) was elected at thirty-seven and pope for eighteen years, the most politically ambitious pope of the Middle Ages. His record reads like a list of medieval institutional landmarks: he placed England under interdict and brought King John to heel; he called the Fourth Crusade and the Albigensian Crusade; he approved the new Franciscan and Dominican orders; he presided over the **Fourth Lateran Council** of November 1215; and he pushed the medieval papacy to the height of its institutional power.

The **Fourth Lateran Council** is worth pausing on. Innocent's largest single legislative act, the council issued seventy decrees that defined transubstantiation as Catholic doctrine, required annual confession and communion of every Catholic, mandated identifying clothing for Jews and Muslims (one of the dark precedents of European antisemitism — the requirement that Jews wear distinguishing dress was repeatedly enforced and reissued through the late Middle Ages and beyond), condemned the Albigensian heresy, and codified much of the medieval church's pastoral and disciplinary apparatus for the next three centuries. The Fourth Lateran is the institutional equivalent of Magna Carta on the religious side — a single act of assembly that defined the framework for everything that came after.

After Innocent, the medieval papacy began to decline relative to the rising centralized monarchies of France and England. **Pope Boniface VIII** (1294–1303) tried to repeat Gregory VII's claims of papal supremacy over kings; his clash with King Philip IV of France ended in his humiliation at Anagni in 1303, his death three weeks later, and the relocation of the papal court to **Avignon** in 1309 — beginning the **Avignon Papacy** that the Late Medieval volume picks up. The thirteenth century was the high tide of the medieval papacy. After 1300 the centralized monarchies began to eat the papacy's institutional space.

---

The other thirteenth-century strand worth following is the long Christian advance through the Iberian peninsula.

The **Reconquista** ("reconquest" in Spanish) was the long Christian recovery of Iberia from Muslim rule — conventionally beginning with the Battle of Covadonga around 722 (where the Visigothic refugee Pelagius defeated an Umayyad raiding force in the Asturian mountains) and ending with the fall of Granada in 1492 (Late Medieval Europe Ch 8). The thirteenth century was its most spectacular phase.

The trigger event was the Battle of **Las Navas de Tolosa** on July 16, 1212. A coalition of the Christian kingdoms of Castile, Aragon, Navarre, and Portugal under Alfonso VIII of Castile destroyed the army of the Almohad caliphate (the Berber dynasty that had been ruling al-Andalus and Morocco since the 1140s) in southern Spain. The Almohad collapse opened the way for rapid Christian advance over the next half-century.

**Córdoba** — the former capital of the Umayyad caliphate of al-Andalus, one of the great cultural centers of the medieval Islamic world (Islamic Golden Age Ch 7) — fell to **Ferdinand III of Castile** on June 29, 1236.

**Valencia** — the great port city on the Mediterranean coast — fell to **James I of Aragon** on September 28, 1238.

**Seville** — the largest remaining Muslim city in Spain and the Almohad administrative capital — fell to Ferdinand III on November 23, 1248 after a sixteen-month siege.

By 1250, four-fifths of the Iberian peninsula was under Christian rule. The remaining Muslim territory was the small **Emirate of Granada** in the southern mountains of Andalucia, a tributary state under the Nasrid dynasty that would survive for another 244 years before falling in January 1492.

The high-medieval Reconquista was both a military and a cultural project. As territory was conquered, Arabic libraries were absorbed into Christian Iberia (the Toledo translation movement of Chapter 5 was the most consequential single result). Muslim and Jewish populations under the new Christian regimes initially retained substantial legal protections — the **Mudéjars** (moo-DAY-har, Muslims under Christian rule) and the **Mozarabs** (moh-ZAH-rab, Arabic-speaking Christians) maintained their religions and communal autonomy under royal protection. The Spanish-Jewish community of medieval Iberia was the largest and culturally most productive Jewish community in medieval Europe.

The protections eroded over the following centuries. The Almohad-era ferocity that had driven Maimonides into exile in Cairo was paralleled by increasing Christian intolerance after the great thirteenth-century conquests. By 1300 there were intermittent forced conversions, anti-Jewish riots, and pressure on Muslim communities to convert or emigrate. The full collapse of medieval Iberian religious coexistence into the **Spanish Inquisition** of 1478 and the Jewish expulsion of 1492 is post-medieval and belongs to the Late Medieval volume. But the trajectory had been set during the great thirteenth-century conquests.

The Mongol shock arrives now.

The Mongol Empire under **Genghis Khan** had unified the steppe in the early thirteenth century and expanded into Central Asia and northern China by Genghis's death in 1227 (Mongol Empire Ch 2 and Ch 3). His grandsons divided the empire and continued the expansion. **Batu Khan**, son of Genghis's eldest son Jochi, took on the western expansion. Between 1237 and 1240 his armies systematically destroyed the cities of the **Rus' principalities** — Ryazan in 1237, Vladimir in 1238, Kyiv on December 6, 1240. The destruction of Kyiv was particularly thorough: contemporary accounts and modern archaeology agree that the city was substantially leveled. The Mongol overlordship of Rus', known to Russian historians as the **Tatar Yoke**, lasted in formal terms until 1480 and reoriented Russian politics decisively away from the Byzantine cultural sphere and into the eastern, steppe-oriented orbit (Mongol Empire Ch 7).

After Kyiv, Batu turned west. In late 1240 and early 1241, Mongol scouting parties raided into Poland, Hungary, and the Balkans. The main invasion force came in spring 1241.

The Mongols divided into two main columns. The northern column under **Baidar** and **Kadan** swept through Poland to draw away the northern European armies that might otherwise have reinforced Hungary. The southern and main column under **Batu** and **Subutai** marched directly on Hungary.

The northern column met the assembled Polish-Moravian-Templar force under Duke Henry II the Pious of Silesia at the Battle of **Legnica** on April 9, 1241. The Mongols used their standard tactic of feigned retreat and envelopment to draw the Polish chivalry into pursuit, then closed around them with their reserve heavy cavalry. The Polish force was annihilated. Henry was killed; the chronicles say his head was paraded on a Mongol lance. The Polish chivalry was destroyed as a fighting force.

Two days later, on April 11, 1241, the southern column under Batu and Subutai met the Hungarian royal army under **King Béla IV** at the Battle of **Mohi** on the Sajó river in eastern Hungary. The Mongols crossed the river by night, surrounded the Hungarian camp at dawn, deliberately left a gap in their encirclement to encourage the Hungarians to flee through it, and then methodically destroyed the disorganized fugitives over thirty miles of pursuit. The Hungarian royal army was effectively annihilated. Béla escaped to the Adriatic coast and spent the next year as a refugee on Croatian islands while the Mongols devastated Hungary.

The devastation was thorough. Mongol contingents reached the Adriatic coast and threatened Vienna. The chronicles record the burning of villages, the slaughter of populations, and the enslavement of survivors across the Hungarian plain. Modern estimates put the Hungarian population loss at fifteen to twenty-five percent in the year of Mongol occupation.

Then, in spring 1242, the Mongols withdrew. The reason was not Christian military resistance — there was no Christian military resistance — but Mongol internal politics. The Great Khan **Ögedei** had died in Mongolia in December 1241, and Batu had to return for the succession kurultai that would eventually elect Güyük as the new Great Khan (Mongol Empire Ch 4). The Mongol withdrawal from central Europe in 1242 was an accident of imperial succession, not a victory by Christian arms.

The reprieve was permanent in central Europe — the Mongols never returned in force, though raids continued into Poland and Hungary through the late thirteenth century. But the lesson stuck. The Mongols had demonstrated that a steppe army with disciplined cavalry tactics, gunpowder weapons (Mongol Empire Ch 3), and competent generalship could defeat any European army the kingdoms could field, and could devastate any country they chose to attack. There was nothing in 1241 between the Mongol field armies and the Atlantic coast of France that could have stopped them. The fact that they did not keep coming was contingent — a matter of imperial succession politics, not Christian capability.

This is one of the strangest counterfactuals in medieval history. If Ögedei had not died in December 1241, the Mongol invasion would almost certainly have continued through 1242 and 1243 into the Holy Roman Empire and France, and the political shape of late-medieval and early-modern Europe would have been very different. The High Middle Ages as a coherent period might have ended at Mohi in 1241 rather than at the Black Death in 1347.

---

The Mongol shock had a second echo in the Levant. **Hülegü Khan**, another grandson of Genghis, sacked Baghdad on February 10, 1258, ending the Abbasid caliphate (Islamic Golden Age Ch 10). The Mongol westward advance was finally stopped at **Ayn Jalut** on September 3, 1260, by the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt — the only major army to defeat the Mongols in a major field battle in the thirteenth century (Mongol Empire Ch 5). The Mamluk victory at Ayn Jalut saved Egypt and the surviving Crusader states and fixed the Mongol western frontier roughly along the Euphrates.

It also produced one of the great might-have-beens of medieval European diplomacy. The Mongol Ilkhans of Persia, who had taken the Levant up to the Egyptian frontier, were openly courting Christian Europe in the late thirteenth century as potential allies against the Mamluks. Successive Ilkhans sent embassies to the popes and the kings of France and England proposing joint Mongol-Christian campaigns against Mamluk Egypt. Some of the Ilkhans personally were sympathetic to Christianity (their wives and mothers were often Christian, from the Christian populations of Central Asia). Several rounds of negotiation between the Ilkhans and the Crusader-supporting western powers came close to producing a joint campaign. None ever did. The opportunity for a Mongol-European alliance against Mamluk Egypt expired with the Ilkhanid conversion to Islam under Ghazan in 1295. This is the closest the medieval Latin West came to genuinely allying with a non-Christian Eurasian power against another, and it is a measure of how far the Mongol shock had pushed European political thinking.

The most famous European to take up the Mongol opening commercially rather than diplomatically was **Marco Polo**. The Venetian merchant Marco Polo traveled overland with his father and uncle to the Mongol court of **Kublai Khan** in 1271, spent seventeen years in China, and on his return dictated **The Travels of Marco Polo** while imprisoned in Genoa after capture in a Venetian-Genoese sea battle around 1298 (Mongol Empire Ch 6). The book became the most influential European description of Asia for the next two centuries and, two centuries later, gave Christopher Columbus the vague mental geography that motivated his westward voyage looking for the Indies. Polo is technically late-thirteenth-century, just inside this volume's window, but his cultural impact is mostly fourteenth- and fifteenth-century and belongs to the Late Medieval volume.

A few words about the legacy of the High Middle Ages, since this volume ends here.

By 1300, the institutional inventory of western Europe included things that had not existed in 1000 and that the rest of the world would eventually inherit: the chartered self-governing town, the international banking corporation, the corporate university, the constitutional charter, the representative parliament, the international religious order, the Gothic cathedral, and a body of recovered classical and Islamic learning that the rest of the medieval period would build on. The institutions were not modern in their content (the parliament had a tiny franchise; the university excluded women; the banks ran on Catholic-canon-law workarounds; the cathedrals were funded by indulgences). But their institutional logic — chartered corporate identity, formal credentials, written constitutional documents, separation of church and state, taxation by consent, due process — was a recognizable ancestor of what the modern western political and intellectual tradition would eventually become.

The High Middle Ages also have a legacy of darker things that the rest of medieval Europe would also build on: the antisemitic violence of 1096 and its successors, the religious-war ideology of the Crusades, the Inquisition's emerging machinery for prosecuting heresy, the gendered exclusions of the universities and the religious orders, the European idea of itself as a uniquely Christian civilization with a divine right to expand outward. Most of these darker features would intensify in the late medieval and early modern centuries before they began to give way under modern critique.

The Late Medieval Europe volume picks up the story in 1300 with the four catastrophes of the fourteenth century — the climate cooling that started the Little Ice Age, the failure of the Italian banking houses, the Hundred Years' War, and the Black Death. The High Middle Ages had been a long boom. The Late Middle Ages would be a long shock. But the institutional inheritance from the boom — the cathedrals, the universities, the parliaments, the chartered cities — would survive the shock, and would be the platform from which everything afterward was built.

Cross-civ check: while western Europe was processing its constitutional and Mongol shocks of the 1200s, the rest of Eurasia was managing its own version of the Mongol arrival. **China** under the Southern Song would fall to the Mongols in 1279, ending the Song dynasty and inaugurating the Yuan (Mongol Empire Ch 6). **Persia** had been under Mongol rule since the 1250s under the Ilkhanate, which would convert to Islam in 1295 and dissolve into successor states in the 1330s — the political vacuum that gave the Safaviyya order at Ardabil room to grow into what would eventually become Safavid Persia (Safavid Persia Ch 1). **Mali** in the western Sudan was just beginning its rise under the founder Sundiata around 1235, the empire that would peak under Mansa Musa a century later (Mali Empire Ch 1 and Ch 4). **Aksum** had long since dissolved into the medieval Ethiopian states, where the Solomonic dynasty would come to power in 1270 and consolidate the Christian Ethiopian kingdom that the European myth of "Prester John" — a legendary Christian king of the East whom the Crusaders kept hoping to make contact with — was vaguely groping toward (Kingdom of Aksum Ch 8). In **Mesoamerica**, the Postclassic Maya kingdoms continued under the regional power of Mayapán (Maya Civilization Ch 6); in the **Andes**, the Wari empire had collapsed and the regional successor states were consolidating around Chimor on the Peruvian coast (Andean Kingdoms Ch 8 and Middle Horizon Empires Ch 8). In the **Pacific**, the Polynesian voyaging culture had reached the easternmost limits of its expansion at Aotearoa (New Zealand) and Rapa Nui (Easter Island) sometime in the thirteenth or fourteenth centuries (Polynesian Voyagers Ch 4 and Ch 6). The Mongol shock and the High Medieval European boom were both real, but they were two episodes in a much larger thirteenth-century world history that was simultaneously absorbing the Mongol arrival, building the Mali Empire across the Sahara, settling the eastern Pacific, and continuing the long Mesoamerican and Andean cultural traditions.

The High Middle Ages had been one Eurasian civilization recovering from a long slump and rebuilding its institutional base. By 1300, Latin Europe was a player on the Eurasian stage again. Whether the Late Middle Ages would consolidate that recovery or break it down was an open question — and the answer would turn out to be both. But that is the next volume.

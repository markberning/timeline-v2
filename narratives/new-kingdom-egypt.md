# New Kingdom Egypt — Three Reinventions and a Collapse

**Goal:** Narrative for the third TL in the Nile Valley chain, following Early Dynastic Egypt (predynastic through Second Dynasty) and Old Kingdom Egypt (Third Dynasty through First Intermediate Period + reunification). Covers roughly 2055–1069 BCE — from the Middle Kingdom's classical literary renaissance through the Hyksos occupation, the explosive imperial expansion of the 18th Dynasty, Akhenaten's religious revolution, Ramesses II's monumental reign, and the final collapse under the Sea Peoples and the Bronze Age crisis. The central thesis is that Egypt reinvented itself three times — as a literary renaissance after collapse, as a chariot empire after foreign occupation, and as a monumental superpower under the Ramessids — and each reinvention was more ambitious and more brittle than the last. The Middle Kingdom gave Egypt its classical culture. The Hyksos gave Egypt its weapons. The New Kingdom gave Egypt its empire. And the Bronze Age Collapse took it all away.

## Chapter 1 — Egypt's Classical Age

The Old Kingdom Egypt narrative ended with a man named Mentuhotep II standing in his terraced temple at Deir el-Bahri, looking out over a country that had been broken and rebuilt (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 8). The First Intermediate Period, roughly 130 years of civil war, famine, and competing warlords, had shattered the system that built the pyramids. Mentuhotep II put it back together, reunifying Egypt around 2055 BCE from his base at Thebes, 400 miles (640 km) south of the old capital at Memphis. He founded the Middle Kingdom.

What came next was not what you might expect. After a century of chaos and famine, the reunified Egypt did not immediately start building giant monuments or conquering foreign lands. Instead, it did something rarer and in many ways more impressive. It produced a literary golden age.

The Middle Kingdom, roughly 2055 to 1650 BCE, is the period the Egyptians themselves would later regard as the classical age of their civilization. Egyptian scribes for the next fifteen hundred years copied Middle Kingdom texts the way Renaissance Europeans copied Cicero. The prose style, the vocabulary, the literary forms invented during this period became the standard against which all later Egyptian writing was judged. If the Old Kingdom proved that faith could be converted into engineering (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 1), the Middle Kingdom proved that crisis could be converted into art.

---

The transition from the Eleventh Dynasty to the Twelfth Dynasty happened with the kind of awkwardness that Egyptian records prefer to smooth over. Mentuhotep II's successors held things together for a couple of generations, but the last king of the Eleventh Dynasty, **Mentuhotep IV** (who reigned only about seven years), was apparently overthrown by his own vizier, a man named **Amenemhat** (pronounced "ah-MEN-em-hat"). Amenemhat took the throne around 1991 BCE and founded the Twelfth Dynasty, becoming **Amenemhat I**.

This was a coup, and Amenemhat knew it looked like one. His response was to do what every usurper in the ancient world did: build legitimacy through competence and propaganda, and move the capital so that the old court's power networks couldn't reach you.

Amenemhat I built a new capital called **Itjtawy** (pronounced "it-jeh-TAH-wee," meaning "Amenemhat is the one who seizes the Two Lands"), located somewhere near the entrance to the **Faiyum** (fy-OOM), the large oasis depression southwest of Memphis. The exact location of Itjtawy has never been found — it lies buried under Nile alluvium — but its strategic logic is clear. It sat between Memphis (the ancient administrative center) and Thebes (the Eleventh Dynasty's power base), close to the rich agricultural land of the Faiyum, and far enough from both older capitals to give the new dynasty room to build its own identity. It was a political capital designed to say: this is a new beginning.

The Faiyum itself became one of the dynasty's great projects. The oasis is fed by a side channel of the Nile called the Bahr Yusuf (named much later by Arab settlers), and the Middle Kingdom pharaohs, especially **Amenemhat III** (reigned roughly 1860–1814 BCE), engineered an ambitious irrigation system to control the water flow into the Faiyum basin, reclaiming marshland for agriculture and creating one of Egypt's most productive farming regions. The scale of this project impressed ancient visitors for millennia. Herodotus, the Greek historian who visited Egypt around 450 BCE, described a vast artificial lake in the Faiyum and a labyrinthine temple beside it that he considered more impressive than the pyramids. The irrigation works have been rebuilt and modified so many times since the Middle Kingdom that their original form is debated, but the achievement was real: the Twelfth Dynasty turned the Faiyum from a seasonal marshland into a permanent breadbasket.

---

Amenemhat I's reign ended the way it began: with violence. Around 1962 BCE, he was assassinated in his own palace, apparently by members of his own court. The assassination is not directly described in any historical text, but it is the unmistakable context behind one of the most remarkable documents in Egyptian literature.

The **Instructions of Amenemhat** is a literary text in which the dead pharaoh's ghost addresses his son and successor, **Senusret I** (pronounced "SEN-oo-sret"), from beyond the grave. The ghost describes the assassination attempt in chilling detail: "It was after supper, night had come. I was lying on my bed, resting, for I had become weary. As I began to drift into sleep, weapons were brandished against me. I acted like a snake of the desert." The pharaoh — or rather, the author putting words in the pharaoh's mouth — warns his son to trust no one: not friends, not brothers, not advisors. The tone is bitter, exhausted, and utterly unlike the confident declarations carved on temple walls.

Is it real? Almost certainly not a literal message from the dead. It was probably composed during or shortly after Senusret I's reign, as a piece of political literature designed to justify the new king's legitimacy and explain the violent transition. But as literature, it is extraordinary. The Instructions became one of the most-copied texts in Egyptian education for the rest of pharaonic history. Schoolboys practiced their hieratic handwriting by copying its sentences. It survived in dozens of papyrus fragments and writing-board exercises, which is how we know it was a core text in the Egyptian literary curriculum.

This is what makes the Middle Kingdom's literature remarkable: these are not simple inscriptions boasting about military victories or listing offerings to the gods. These are complex fictional narratives with unreliable narrators, political subtext, and psychological depth. The Middle Kingdom invented literary fiction in Egypt, and arguably produced some of the earliest literary fiction in the world.

---

The masterpiece of the era is the **Story of Sinuhe** (pronounced "sih-NOO-hey"), and it is worth spending some time with because it explains something important about what the Middle Kingdom was.

Sinuhe is a court official who is present when Amenemhat I is assassinated. In the confusion, he panics and flees Egypt, running northeast into the Levant (the coastal region of the eastern Mediterranean, roughly modern Syria, Lebanon, Israel, and Palestine). He ends up in the kingdom of a Bedouin chief called Ammunenshi, who gives him land, a wife, and a comfortable position. Sinuhe prospers. He fights a champion in single combat and wins. He raises children. He becomes a man of importance in a foreign land.

And he is miserable. Not because his life is bad, but because he is not in Egypt. The entire emotional engine of the story is Sinuhe's longing for home — for the Nile, for the court, for the sound of the Egyptian language, for the assurance that when he dies he will be properly mummified and buried in Egyptian soil rather than wrapped in a sheepskin and dropped in a hole in some foreign wilderness. When a letter from the pharaoh finally reaches him, offering him a pardon and a return, Sinuhe's response is overwhelming. He faints. He weeps. He goes home, and the pharaoh receives him, and the story ends with Sinuhe getting his Egyptian tomb and his Egyptian funeral and his Egyptian eternity.

The Story of Sinuhe is not a simple adventure tale. It is a meditation on what it means to be Egyptian. Sinuhe can live comfortably among foreigners, speak their language, lead their armies, marry their women — but he will never be one of them, because being Egyptian is not about competence or success. It is about belonging to the land, to the river, to the gods who live along the Nile and nowhere else. The story's emotional power comes from the fact that Sinuhe is not fleeing persecution or poverty. He is fleeing his own identity, and the story insists that you cannot do that. Egypt is not something you can leave behind.

This is profoundly political literature. In the aftermath of the First Intermediate Period's collapse, when Egypt had fractured into warring provinces and the idea of a unified nation nearly died, the Story of Sinuhe is an argument that Egypt is eternal and that belonging to Egypt is the highest good a person can aspire to. It is, as scholars have noted, a delightful piece of royal propaganda wrapped in a genuinely moving personal narrative. The Middle Kingdom was good at that combination.

Other major literary works from this period include the **Prophecy of Neferti** (a text that "predicts" the First Intermediate Period and the rise of Amenemhat I — written after the fact, making the prophecy conveniently accurate), the **Eloquent Peasant** (a story about a peasant who delivers a series of increasingly elaborate speeches on justice to a corrupt official, becoming so eloquent that the official keeps robbing him just to hear the next speech), and the **Satire of the Trades** (a father's advice to his son about why being a scribe is better than every other profession, with vivid descriptions of how miserable other jobs are — the washerman washes the clothes of menstruating women, the fisherman is eaten by crocodiles, the bricklayer's fingers are rotting off). These texts were funny, sharp, politically engaged, and sophisticated. They were also consciously literary in a way that earlier Egyptian texts were not. The authors knew they were making art.

For comparison: around the same time (roughly 1800 BCE), Mesopotamia was producing the great Old Babylonian literary texts, including the most complete version of the **Epic of Gilgamesh** and the **Code of Hammurabi** (Mesopotamia Ch 8). China would not produce comparable literary fiction for another millennium. The Indus Valley civilization, which was roughly contemporary with the early Middle Kingdom, left no readable literary texts at all — their script remains undeciphered (Indus Valley Ch 6). Greece's earliest literary works, the Iliad and Odyssey, would not be composed for another eight hundred years.

---

The Middle Kingdom's political achievement was equally impressive, and it centered on one pharaoh: **Senusret III** (reigned roughly 1878–1839 BCE).

Senusret III is one of the most important pharaohs in Egyptian history, and one of the least known to casual readers. He solved the problem that had destroyed the Old Kingdom.

The problem was the **nomarchs** — the hereditary governors of Egypt's forty-two provinces (nomes) who had accumulated too much independent power during the Old Kingdom, maintained their own armies, and eventually became the warlords of the First Intermediate Period (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 7). The Middle Kingdom's earlier pharaohs had kept the nomarchs in check through a combination of loyalty and vigilance, but the system was always fragile. Senusret III ended the fragility by ending the nomarchs. He replaced the old provincial system with a centralized bureaucracy answerable directly to the crown, with three new administrative departments (for Upper Egypt, Lower Egypt, and the "Head of the South") run by appointed officials rather than hereditary lords. After Senusret III's reforms, the great nomarch tombs — the elaborate rock-cut chambers that provincial governors had been building for themselves in Middle Egypt — simply stop. The nomarchs didn't die out physically, but their independent political power was broken.

The mechanism was administrative, not military (no texts record mass executions or wars against the provinces). Senusret III apparently achieved this through a combination of bureaucratic restructuring, royal appointments that bypassed the old hereditary claims, and the sheer gravitational pull of a strong central government that made independent provincial power irrelevant. It was one of the most effective political reorganizations in the ancient world, and it held for two centuries.

---

Senusret III was equally decisive in Nubia, and here the evidence is written in stone — literally.

The Middle Kingdom's relationship with Nubia was defined by the **fortress system**: a chain of at least thirteen massive mudbrick fortresses built along the Nile between the First and Second Cataracts, stretching about 250 miles (400 km) into what is now northern Sudan. The fortresses — **Buhen** (pronounced "BOO-hen"), **Semna** (pronounced "SEM-nah"), **Kumma**, **Uronarti**, and others — were military installations of a sophistication that would not be matched in the ancient world for centuries. Buhen alone had walls 16 feet (5 meters) thick, bastions at regular intervals, a dry moat, loopholes for archers, and a glacis (a sloped stone surface at the base of the wall designed to prevent scaling). These were not outposts. They were a militarized frontier zone designed to control the flow of trade, people, and information between Egypt and the powerful kingdom of **Kerma** (Ancient Nubia Ch 3-4), which was growing in strength about 200 miles (320 km) further south, at the Third Cataract.

Senusret III campaigned into Nubia at least four times, pushing the Egyptian frontier to the Second Cataract and establishing a boundary marker at Semna with an inscription that is startling in its directness: "Any son of mine who shall maintain this boundary which My Majesty has made, he is my son. But as for whoever shall abandon it, and shall not fight for it, he is not my son, and was not born to me." The text goes on to describe the Nubians in terms that are bluntly racist by any standard: "They are not people one respects; they are wretches, craven-hearted." Whether Senusret III actually believed this or was performing the rhetoric of imperial ideology is debatable, but the rhetoric tells us something about how the Middle Kingdom justified its southern expansion: as a civilizing mission defending Egypt's natural border against people who were less than fully human.

The reality, as the Ancient Nubia narrative covers in detail, was rather different. The Kerma kingdom was a sophisticated state with its own monumental architecture, complex social hierarchy, and long-distance trade networks. The Middle Kingdom's obsession with fortifying the Second Cataract was not the response of a superpower casually swatting a nuisance. It was the response of a superpower that was genuinely worried about what was growing on its southern border.

---

The Middle Kingdom also produced the **Rhind Mathematical Papyrus**, the most extensive mathematical document to survive from ancient Egypt: 84 problems covering arithmetic, algebra, geometry, and the Egyptian system of unit fractions (Egyptians expressed all fractions as sums of fractions with numerator 1, a system that seems cumbersome but worked effectively for practical calculations). It was copied around 1650 BCE from an earlier original, and it gives us a window into how Egyptian engineers calculated pyramid slopes, field areas, bread rations, and beer distributions. In a different register, the **Edwin Smith Papyrus**, copied during the same period, is the world's oldest known surgical treatise: 48 case studies describing injuries with remarkably rational, anatomical approaches. "If you examine a man having a wound in his head penetrating to the bone, you should place your hand upon it. If you find his skull uninjured, you should say: An ailment I will treat." The tone is clinical, systematic, and startlingly modern.

The Middle Kingdom's last major figure was its most unusual. **Sobekneferu** (pronounced "so-BEK-nef-eh-roo," meaning "beauty of Sobek"), who reigned around 1806–1802 BCE, was the first confirmed female pharaoh in Egyptian history. Other women had held power before — Merneith had ruled as regent during the First Dynasty (Early Dynastic Egypt Ch 6), and Nitocris of the Sixth Dynasty is mentioned in later king lists but may be legendary. Sobekneferu appears in the Turin King List with her own regnal years and her own cartouche. She was the daughter of Amenemhat III and apparently came to the throne when the male line ran out. Her reign was short, and the Twelfth Dynasty ended with her. Her successor founded the Thirteenth Dynasty, and the long, slow decline toward the Second Intermediate Period began.

The Thirteenth Dynasty produced a rapid succession of rulers — over fifty kings in about 150 years, many of them reigning only briefly and some of them possibly of non-royal origin. Central authority weakened. The provinces drifted. The military frontier in Nubia was abandoned, and the Kerma kingdom expanded into the territory the Egyptian fortresses had controlled. The Faiyum irrigation works fell into disrepair. The treasury shrank.

And in the northeast Delta, in a city called **Avaris** (pronounced "AV-ah-ris"), a community of Levantine settlers — traders, craftspeople, and pastoralists who had been living in Egypt for generations — was growing stronger, richer, and more independent. They were about to take over.

## Chapter 2 — The Uninvited Guests

The word **Hyksos** is a Greek mangling of an Egyptian phrase, **heka khasut**, meaning "rulers of foreign lands." It was originally a title of respect, used for Levantine chieftains. By the time it entered the historical record as a group name, it had become something uglier: the label the Egyptians slapped on the foreign dynasty that ruled northern Egypt for about a century, and whose memory they would try to erase, rewrite, and demonize for the next thousand years.

The Hyksos were not, as Egyptian propaganda later insisted, a sudden barbarian horde that swept out of the eastern desert and conquered Egypt by force. The archaeological evidence tells a more complicated and more interesting story.

---

The roots of the Hyksos takeover go back to the Middle Kingdom itself. Throughout the Twelfth and Thirteenth Dynasties, the northeastern Nile Delta had been home to a growing population of Semitic-speaking settlers from the Levant. These were people whose language belonged to the **Semitic language family** (a completely different language group from Egyptian, as different as Chinese and English; Semitic includes what would later become Hebrew, Aramaic, Phoenician, and Arabic). They came from the coastal cities and inland towns of modern Syria, Lebanon, and Palestine, and they had been drifting into Egypt for centuries — as traders, as mercenaries, as laborers, as refugees from periodic famines in the Levant.

The settlement pattern was gradual, not sudden. Excavations at the site of **Tell el-Dab'a** (pronounced "tell el-DAB-ah"), identified as the Hyksos capital of Avaris, show a continuous Levantine presence going back to at least the late Twelfth Dynasty. The earliest layers show Egyptian-style burials alongside Levantine pottery, Levantine weapons, and Levantine-style houses. The people at Tell el-Dab'a were not invaders. They were immigrants, living inside Egypt, participating in Egyptian society, and maintaining their own cultural identity at the same time.

The Austrian archaeologist Manfred Bietak, who excavated Tell el-Dab'a starting in the 1960s, has traced the transformation step by step. In the early layers, the Levantine settlers adapted to Egyptian customs. In the middle layers, the proportion of Levantine material increases: more Syrian pottery, more donkey burials (a Levantine practice, not an Egyptian one), more weapons in tombs. In the later layers, the settlement becomes a Levantine-style city with monumental architecture, palatial compounds, and temples featuring Canaanite religious elements. The transformation was not a conquest. It was a demographic and cultural shift, happening over generations, in which a growing foreign community gradually consolidated power in a region where central Egyptian authority was weakening.

By around 1650 BCE, the Thirteenth Dynasty in Memphis had become so weak — kings reigning for months rather than years, the treasury depleted, the bureaucracy fraying — that the Levantine rulers at Avaris were effectively independent. At some point in the mid-seventeenth century BCE (the exact date is debated), the Hyksos leaders dropped the pretense of subordination and declared themselves pharaohs. The Fifteenth Dynasty, as Manetho later numbered it, was a foreign dynasty ruling from the Delta.

---

What did the Hyksos actually bring to Egypt? This is where the story gets interesting, because the answer is: weapons that would change the world.

The **horse-drawn chariot** was the single most transformative military technology of the Bronze Age. Chariots had been developed on the steppes of Central Asia and had spread to Mesopotamia, the Hittite kingdoms of Anatolia (modern Turkey), and the Levantine city-states by roughly 1800 BCE. They reached Egypt via the Hyksos. The chariot was not a heavy tank. It was a lightweight two-wheeled platform, pulled by two horses at a gallop, carrying a driver and an archer (or a driver and a spearman). Its purpose was mobility: chariot forces could outflank infantry, harass supply lines, pursue routed enemies, and deliver archers to optimal firing positions faster than any foot soldier could respond. In flat, open terrain — exactly the kind of terrain you find in the Nile Delta, the Sinai, and the Levantine coastal plains — chariots were devastating.

The Hyksos also brought the **composite bow**, a weapon made from layers of wood, horn, and sinew glued together under tension, which could shoot arrows further and with more force than the simple wooden self-bows the Egyptians had been using since the predynastic period. The composite bow was smaller (critical for shooting from a moving chariot), more powerful, and more expensive to manufacture. Combined with the chariot, it created a weapons system that made the Hyksos militarily superior to any Egyptian force that tried to fight them on open ground.

Other Hyksos introductions included improved bronze-working techniques, the **khopesh** (pronounced "KOH-pesh"), a curved sickle-sword that became a signature Egyptian weapon for the next five centuries, new pottery forms, new textile techniques, and possibly the vertical loom. The Hyksos were not barbarians. They were urbanized, cosmopolitan Levantines who were thoroughly connected to the broader Bronze Age world of international trade and technological exchange — a world that the Middle Kingdom Egyptians had been somewhat insulated from by their self-contained Nile Valley geography.

For cross-civilizational context: the chariot revolution was happening across the entire Near East at roughly the same time. The **Hittites** in Anatolia were building their empire on chariot warfare (Hittite Empire Ch 2-3). The **Mitanni** (a Hurrian-speaking kingdom in northern Syria that would become one of the great powers of the Late Bronze Age) were famous for their horse-breeding and chariot-training manuals. In **Shang Dynasty China**, chariots would appear around 1200 BCE, probably transmitted across the Eurasian steppe from west to east (Shang Dynasty Ch 3). The horse-drawn chariot was the Late Bronze Age's defining technology, and Egypt adopted it later than its neighbors — because the Hyksos had to bring it in.

---

The Hyksos ruled the Delta and possibly much of Middle Egypt for about a century, from roughly 1650 to 1550 BCE. This is the **Second Intermediate Period** — Egypt's second great fracture, echoing the First Intermediate Period that had followed the Old Kingdom's collapse (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 7).

The country was divided. The Hyksos Fifteenth Dynasty controlled the north from Avaris. A vassal dynasty (the Sixteenth, composed of minor Hyksos rulers and local chiefs) controlled parts of Middle Egypt. And in the south, the **Seventeenth Dynasty** at Thebes maintained an independent Egyptian kingdom, controlling Upper Egypt from roughly the First Cataract to somewhere north of Thebes.

Further south, beyond the cataracts, the Kerma kingdom in Nubia had exploited the collapse of the Middle Kingdom's frontier fortresses to expand northward, becoming one of the most powerful states in the Nile Valley (Ancient Nubia Ch 4). The Thebans were squeezed between the Hyksos in the north and the Nubians in the south — a strategic nightmare.

And yet the Thebans did not simply accept their situation. The Hyksos period was not just foreign occupation. It was a military education. The Thebans observed the Hyksos weapons. They studied the chariot. They learned to breed horses. They adopted the composite bow. And they began to plan.

Two things about the Hyksos period are worth noting because later Egyptian propaganda distorted them. First, the Hyksos were not destroyers of Egyptian culture. They adopted Egyptian royal titles, used hieroglyphs, worshipped Egyptian gods (alongside their own, particularly **Baal** and **Anat**, Levantine storm and war deities that they identified with the Egyptian god **Set**). The Hyksos pharaoh **Apepi** (also called Apophis, though he should not be confused with the chaos serpent of the same name) was a patron of Egyptian scribal culture — the Rhind Mathematical Papyrus (Chapter 1) was copied during his reign. The Hyksos period was more complex than "foreigners oppressed Egypt."

Second, the later Egyptian accounts of the Hyksos expulsion were written by the winners. The story of the liberation war, as it has come down to us, is almost entirely from the Theban side. The Hyksos left few of their own texts, and what they did leave was systematically destroyed by the victorious Eighteenth Dynasty. We are hearing one side of a two-sided war.

---

The war began, according to a much later and possibly legendary account, with an insult. The story (preserved in a Ramesside-era papyrus, written centuries after the events) claims that the Hyksos king Apepi sent a message to the Theban ruler **Seqenenre Tao** (pronounced "seh-keh-NEN-ray TAH-oh") complaining that the hippopotami in the sacred pool at Thebes were keeping him awake at night. Since Avaris was 400 miles (640 km) away, this was obviously not a sincere complaint about noise pollution. It was a diplomatic provocation, a coded demand for submission. The text breaks off before recording the response.

Whether or not the hippo story is historical, the war was real. And the physical evidence for what happened to Seqenenre Tao is among the most visceral artifacts in all of Egyptology.

His mummy, discovered in the Deir el-Bahri cache in 1881, shows massive wounds to the head: an axe blow above the left eye, a second axe blow that caved in the left cheekbone, a dagger wound behind the left ear, and a blow from a mace or club to the right side of the forehead. These are not the injuries of a man who died peacefully. They are the injuries of a man who was attacked from multiple angles, possibly while lying down or kneeling. Whether he died in battle or was assassinated in his bed is debated, but the wounds are consistent with Hyksos-style weapons — the types of axes found at Tell el-Dab'a. Seqenenre Tao appears to have died fighting the Hyksos, or at least died at their hands.

His successor, **Kamose** (pronounced "kah-MOH-seh"), continued the war and pushed it further north than anyone expected. Two stelae from Karnak record Kamose's campaigns in extraordinary detail, including a captured letter from the Hyksos king Apepi to the ruler of Kerma, proposing a joint attack on Thebes from north and south simultaneously. The fact that the Hyksos and the Nubians were coordinating against Thebes shows how precarious the Theban position really was — trapped between two enemies, fighting for survival.

Kamose raided deep into Hyksos territory, reaching the outskirts of Avaris itself, but he died before completing the conquest. The final act would belong to his brother — or possibly his son (the exact relationship is debated) — a young man who would become one of the most consequential rulers in Egyptian history.

## Chapter 3 — Liberation

**Ahmose I** (pronounced "AH-moh-seh") came to the throne as a child, possibly as young as ten. His mother, **Ahhotep I** (pronounced "ah-HOH-tep"), served as regent during his minority, and her own tomb would later yield military decorations — golden flies, the Egyptian award for valor in battle — suggesting she played an active role in the war effort, not just a ceremonial one. When Ahmose came of age and took command, he did what his predecessors had been building toward for a generation.

He expelled the Hyksos from Egypt.

The final campaigns, around 1550 BCE (the exact date varies by a decade depending on which scholarly chronology you follow), saw Ahmose's forces assault Avaris itself. The archaeological evidence at Tell el-Dab'a shows destruction layers consistent with a military conquest in this period: burned buildings, abandoned storerooms, sudden cessation of occupation. Ahmose didn't stop at the border. He pursued the Hyksos into southern Palestine, besieging and capturing the fortress of **Sharuhen** (pronounced "shah-ROO-hen"), probably to prevent the defeated Hyksos from regrouping and counterattacking. The siege of Sharuhen lasted three years, according to the autobiographical inscription of **Ahmose son of Ibana** (a naval officer from El-Kab whose tomb inscription is our best source for the liberation wars), which gives some indication of how difficult and prolonged the campaign was.

With the Hyksos expelled, Ahmose also campaigned southward into Nubia, reasserting Egyptian control over the territory the Middle Kingdom had held and that the Kerma kingdom had absorbed during the Second Intermediate Period. He regarrisoned the old Middle Kingdom fortresses at the Second Cataract and pushed the frontier further south. By the end of his reign, around 1525 BCE, Egypt was unified again — one pharaoh, one kingdom, from the Mediterranean to deep into Nubia.

This was the founding of the **Eighteenth Dynasty** and the beginning of the **New Kingdom**, roughly 1550 to 1069 BCE: the era of Egypt's greatest power, its largest empire, its most famous pharaohs, and its most spectacular monuments. Everything that follows in this book — Hatshepsut, Thutmose III, Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, Ramesses the Great — happens because Ahmose I drove out the Hyksos and turned Egypt from a defended kingdom into an offensive empire.

---

The transformation was not just military. The Hyksos experience fundamentally changed Egyptian psychology. For the first time in their history, the Egyptians had been conquered by foreigners. The Middle Kingdom had feared the Nubians and fortified the southern border; the Old Kingdom had occasionally raided Libyans and Bedouin. But no foreign power had ever ruled Egypt itself. The Hyksos did. And the lesson the New Kingdom drew from that experience was: never again. The best defense is an offense so deep that no enemy can reach the homeland.

This is why the New Kingdom became an empire. The Old Kingdom and Middle Kingdom had been powerful but essentially defensive. They controlled the Nile Valley, traded with their neighbors, and occasionally campaigned abroad when threatened. The New Kingdom was different. It pursued conquest as policy, establishing permanent garrisons, appointing Egyptian governors, extracting tribute, and projecting military power hundreds of miles beyond Egypt's borders. The Hyksos invasion was the trauma. The New Kingdom empire was the response.

Ahmose's immediate successors continued the transformation. **Amenhotep I** (reigned roughly 1525–1504 BCE) consolidated the reconquest, campaigned in Nubia, and is traditionally credited with founding the artisans' village of **Deir el-Medina** (pronounced "dare el-meh-DEE-nah") on the west bank of Thebes. Deir el-Medina would become one of the most important archaeological sites in Egypt — a walled village housing the workers who built and decorated the royal tombs in the nearby **Valley of the Kings** and **Valley of the Queens**. Because the village was occupied continuously for nearly five hundred years, and because its literate inhabitants threw their written correspondence, legal documents, love letters, laundry lists, and medical prescriptions into communal rubbish heaps that survived in the dry desert air, Deir el-Medina provides the richest single record of daily life in the ancient world. We will return to it throughout this narrative, and its final chapter (Chapter 9) is one of the most poignant endings in ancient history.

---

**Thutmose I** (pronounced "thoot-MOH-seh," reigned roughly 1506–1493 BCE), the third pharaoh of the Eighteenth Dynasty, took the empire to a new level. He campaigned deep into Nubia, pushing the Egyptian frontier past the Second Cataract all the way to the area around the **Fourth Cataract**, roughly 750 miles (1,200 km) south of the Egyptian border — further into Africa than any pharaoh had ever gone. He appointed the first **Viceroy of Kush** (a title that literally meant "King's Son of Kush," though the holder was rarely an actual prince), an Egyptian official who governed Nubia as a colonial territory, extracting gold, labor, and tribute for the Egyptian crown. Nubia was the engine of New Kingdom wealth. The gold mines of the Eastern Desert and Upper Nubia (Kingdom of Kush Ch 1-2) produced a flow of gold that made Egypt the richest power in the Bronze Age world and gave the pharaoh the means to finance his armies, his temples, and his diplomacy.

Thutmose I also campaigned in the opposite direction, marching north through the Levant and reaching the **Euphrates River** in modern Syria — the first Egyptian army to see the great river of Mesopotamia. The Egyptians were so disoriented by a river that flowed from north to south (the opposite of the Nile) that they called it "that inverted water." Thutmose I set up a victory stele on the bank of the Euphrates, marking the northernmost point of Egyptian power to date. The stele was a statement of intent: Egypt was now a player in the great-power politics of the Near East, competing for influence with the Mitanni kingdom (a Hurrian-speaking state in northern Syria), the Hittites in Anatolia (Hittite Empire Ch 3), and the Kassite rulers of Babylon (Mesopotamia Ch 10).

Thutmose I was also, probably, the first pharaoh buried in the **Valley of the Kings**, the narrow, hidden wadi (dry valley) on the west bank of Thebes that would serve as the royal cemetery for the next five hundred years. The Valley of the Kings was the opposite of the pyramids in philosophy: where the Old Kingdom pharaohs had built visible, monumental tombs designed to be seen for miles, the New Kingdom pharaohs carved their tombs into a hidden canyon, concealed them underground, and separated the tomb from the mortuary temple (which was built on the plain below). The change reflected a hard lesson. The pyramids had been robbed. Every single one of them. The Pyramid Texts had not protected the pharaoh's body; the stone walls had not stopped the thieves. The New Kingdom solution was secrecy: hide the body, and put the conspicuous temple somewhere else so the thieves look in the wrong place.

It didn't work. Nearly every tomb in the Valley of the Kings was also robbed in antiquity. But the strategy lasted five centuries before its final, spectacular failure (Chapter 9).

---

One more figure from this founding generation deserves attention. **Ahmose-Nefertari** (pronounced "AH-moh-seh nef-er-TAR-ee"), the wife of Ahmose I and mother of Amenhotep I, became one of the most revered women in Egyptian history. She held the title **God's Wife of Amun**, a priestly position at the temple of Karnak that gave its holder significant religious authority and independent wealth. The title had existed before, but Ahmose-Nefertari transformed it into a major political institution — the God's Wife of Amun would become one of the most powerful positions in the Egyptian state, especially during periods when royal power wavered. After her death, Ahmose-Nefertari was deified and worshipped as the patron goddess of the Theban necropolis. Her images in tomb paintings show her with black skin — probably a symbolic choice representing fertility and resurrection rather than a literal depiction, though this is debated.

The early Eighteenth Dynasty established the patterns that would define the New Kingdom for the next five centuries: aggressive military expansion, Nubian gold financing everything, the Amun priesthood at Karnak as the religious center of the state, the Valley of the Kings as the royal cemetery, Deir el-Medina as the workers' village, and queens wielding real political and religious power under the umbrella of institutional titles. All of these elements were in place by 1493 BCE, when Thutmose I died and was buried somewhere in the Valley of the Kings.

What happened next was something no one in the Egyptian court expected. A woman took the throne — not as regent, not as queen consort, not as a placeholder for a child king. She took it as pharaoh, in her own right, with a full royal titulary and a beard.

---

## Chapter 4 — The Woman Who Was King

**Hatshepsut** (pronounced "hat-SHEP-soot") was the daughter of Thutmose I and his Great Royal Wife, Ahmose. When Thutmose I died, the succession passed to his son by a secondary wife: **Thutmose II**, who married Hatshepsut (his half-sister, in keeping with the Egyptian royal practice of sibling marriage to concentrate divine blood). Thutmose II reigned for roughly thirteen years (the exact length is debated), and his health appears to have been poor — his mummy shows signs of a skin disease and he died relatively young. He left behind a daughter by Hatshepsut, named **Neferure** (pronounced "nef-eh-ROO-ray"), and a son by a secondary wife, a boy who would become **Thutmose III**.

The boy was too young to rule. Hatshepsut became regent, governing Egypt on behalf of her stepson. This was not unprecedented — Ahhotep I had done something similar for the young Ahmose I, and Merneith had served as regent during the First Dynasty (Early Dynastic Egypt Ch 6). What was unprecedented was what happened next.

Around the seventh year of the regency, roughly 1473 BCE, Hatshepsut stopped being a regent and became pharaoh. She adopted the full five-fold royal titulary, including a throne name (Maatkare, "Truth is the Ka of Ra"). She was depicted in temple reliefs wearing the traditional pharaonic regalia: the nemes headdress, the false beard, the kilt, the ceremonial bull's tail. In some scenes she appears as a woman wearing male royal insignia. In later scenes she appears fully male — broad-chested, flat-breasted, in the conventional pose of a male king. The transformation was not a personal gender expression. It was a political necessity. The pharaonic institution was coded male. The rituals, the titles, the costumes, the theological framework — the pharaoh was Horus, and Horus was male. To function as pharaoh, Hatshepsut had to become, at least in the ritual and artistic vocabulary, a king.

She did not displace Thutmose III. He remained officially co-regent throughout her reign, and his name appears alongside hers on many monuments. But there was no ambiguity about who was running things. Hatshepsut was pharaoh. Thutmose III was, for twenty-two years, a junior partner.

---

Hatshepsut's reign was not a period of military conquest. It was a period of building, trade, and consolidation — and it was spectacularly successful.

The defining event of her reign, at least in the monumental record, was the **Expedition to Punt**. **Punt** (the exact location is debated but probably somewhere on the coast of modern Eritrea, Somalia, or eastern Sudan) was a semi-legendary trading partner that the Egyptians called "God's Land," a source of luxury goods — frankincense, myrrh, ebony, gold, ivory, exotic animal skins, and live animals including baboons and possibly giraffes — that Egyptian expeditions had been visiting sporadically since the Old Kingdom. Hatshepsut's expedition, around 1470 BCE, was the most elaborately documented trading voyage in Egyptian history.

The wall reliefs at Hatshepsut's mortuary temple at **Deir el-Bahari** depict the expedition in extraordinary detail: the five ships sailing down the Red Sea coast, the arrival at Punt, the meeting with the chief of Punt (named Parehu) and his wife (shown in the reliefs as remarkably corpulent — ancient fat-shaming or accurate portraiture, scholars disagree), the trading of Egyptian goods for trees of myrrh transplanted in baskets, gold, baboons, and panther skins. The ships are shown loaded to the waterline on the return voyage. The reliefs insist that this was a divine mission, ordered by the god **Amun** himself: "Sailing in the sea, beginning the goodly way towards God's Land, journeying in peace to the land of Punt."

The Punt expedition was politically important for Hatshepsut because it demonstrated that a female pharaoh could maintain Egypt's prestige and trading networks without waging war. The reliefs at Deir el-Bahari make the expedition's success a central argument for Hatshepsut's legitimacy — as if to say: a pharaoh who can bring the treasures of God's Land to Amun's temple has earned the throne.

---

The temple itself is Hatshepsut's greatest surviving monument, and one of the most beautiful buildings in Egypt.

The **Mortuary Temple of Hatshepsut** at Deir el-Bahari is built directly into the cliff face on the west bank of Thebes, immediately adjacent to the much older mortuary temple of Mentuhotep II (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 8). The design echoes Mentuhotep's terraced plan but on a grander scale: three ascending colonnaded terraces connected by central ramps, rising from the desert plain to the cliff face, with the innermost sanctuary cut directly into the rock. The columns are clean, elegant, and proportionally refined in a way that later Egyptian architecture rarely matched. The overall effect, when you approach from the floodplain, is of a building that grows out of the landscape rather than sitting on top of it.

The architect was almost certainly **Senenmut** (pronounced "SEN-en-moot"), a man of non-royal birth who held an extraordinary accumulation of titles under Hatshepsut: steward of the estate of Amun, overseer of all works of the king, and tutor to the princess Neferure. Senenmut's relationship with Hatshepsut has generated endless speculation — were they lovers? One piece of graffiti found in an unfinished tomb near Deir el-Bahari appears to depict a man and a woman in a sexual position, and some scholars have interpreted it as a crude caricature of Senenmut and Hatshepsut. The graffiti is ambiguous at best, and the romantic interpretation may say more about modern assumptions than ancient Egyptian reality. What is unambiguous is that Senenmut held more power than any non-royal individual since Imhotep (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 1), and that his power depended entirely on Hatshepsut. When she died, he vanished from the record.

Hatshepsut also expanded the great temple of **Karnak** (pronounced "KAR-nak"), the vast religious complex at Thebes that was the administrative and ceremonial center of the Amun cult. She erected two pairs of **obelisks** (tall, tapering stone pillars capped with a gilded pyramidion, quarried from single blocks of granite at Aswan and transported hundreds of miles downriver) at Karnak, one pair of which included the tallest obelisk ever erected in Egypt at that time: nearly 97 feet (29.5 meters) high. The surviving obelisk still stands at Karnak today, inscribed with texts proclaiming Hatshepsut's devotion to Amun and her right to rule.

---

Hatshepsut died around 1458 BCE, after about twenty-two years as pharaoh. She was approximately fifty years old — a respectable age for the period. The cause of death is unknown. There is no evidence of violence.

What happened after her death is one of the most debated questions in Egyptology.

At some point after Hatshepsut's death, her successor Thutmose III ordered her images systematically defaced. Her cartouches were chiseled off temple walls. Her statues were smashed, pulled down, and buried. Her name was removed from king lists. Her obelisks at Karnak were walled up behind masonry (which ironically preserved them — the walling protected the inscriptions from weathering). The intention was clear: to erase Hatshepsut from the record, to make it look as if she had never ruled, to eliminate the precedent of a female pharaoh.

But here is the puzzle. Thutmose III did not do this immediately. The erasure campaign began roughly twenty years after Hatshepsut's death — near the end of Thutmose III's own reign, when he was preparing to pass the throne to his son, **Amenhotep II**. Why wait two decades?

The most widely accepted explanation among modern scholars is that the erasure was not about personal revenge. If Thutmose III had hated Hatshepsut, he could have begun the destruction immediately upon her death. The twenty-year delay suggests that the motivation was dynastic, not personal: Thutmose III was preparing the succession for his son, and he wanted to clean up the historical record so that the line of succession ran cleanly from Thutmose I to Thutmose II to Thutmose III to Amenhotep II, without the awkward interruption of a female pharaoh who had no place in the standard theological model. The erasure was not about punishing Hatshepsut. It was about making the succession look tidy.

An older interpretation — that Thutmose III acted out of decades-long resentment at being sidelined by his stepmother — is still popular in some accounts but has fallen out of favor with most Egyptologists. The evidence suggests that Thutmose III held Hatshepsut's administrators in high regard, retained many of her officials, and never erased her mortuary temple (which would have been easy to demolish). He erased her *name*, not her *buildings*. That distinction matters.

Hatshepsut's body may have been found. In 2007, a mummy from the Valley of the Kings (designated KV60A) was identified as Hatshepsut through a DNA comparison with a tooth found in a canopic jar bearing her name. The identification is widely accepted but not universally settled. If correct, Hatshepsut was a large woman who suffered from diabetes, bone cancer, and bad teeth — an unglamorous end for one of Egypt's most remarkable rulers.

---

## Chapter 5 — Napoleon of Egypt

Thutmose III had spent twenty-two years watching someone else run his kingdom. When Hatshepsut died and the full power of the pharaonic state finally came into his hands, he used it to build the largest empire Egypt had ever known.

Modern scholars sometimes call Thutmose III the "Napoleon of Egypt," a label he earned by conducting at least seventeen military campaigns over roughly twenty years, expanding the Egyptian Empire from the Fourth Cataract in Nubia to the banks of the Euphrates in Syria, and documenting the whole thing with a precision that makes his campaigns among the best-recorded military operations in the ancient world. Napoleon himself would have appreciated the comparison. Both men were relatively short (Thutmose III's mummy stands about 5 feet 4 inches), both were brilliant strategists, and both left extensive written records of their campaigns. The analogy breaks down in one important respect: Napoleon lost. Thutmose III won.

---

The defining battle came in year 22 of his reign (counting from the beginning of his co-regency with Hatshepsut), roughly 1457 BCE. A coalition of Canaanite and Syrian city-states, led by the king of the city of **Kadesh** (pronounced "KAH-desh," an important fortress city on the Orontes River in modern Syria, which will reappear spectacularly in Chapter 8), had rebelled against Egyptian control. The coalition assembled its forces at **Megiddo** (pronounced "meh-GID-oh"), a fortified city in the Jezreel Valley of modern Israel that controlled a key pass through the Carmel Ridge — one of the most strategically important locations in the ancient Near East.

The **Battle of Megiddo** (1457 BCE) is the earliest battle in recorded history for which we have a detailed tactical account. Thutmose III's scribes, writing in his **Annals** on the walls of the temple of Karnak, describe the approach march, the war council, and the pharaoh's decision with a specificity that reads more like a military after-action report than a royal inscription.

The Egyptian army reached the Carmel Ridge and faced a choice: three routes through the mountains to the Jezreel Valley beyond. The northern and southern routes were safe but long. The central route, through the narrow **Aruna Pass**, was the most direct but also the most dangerous — the pass was so narrow that the army would have to march in single file, vulnerable to ambush. Thutmose III's generals argued for one of the safe routes. The pharaoh overruled them: "My Majesty shall proceed on this road of Aruna. Let him who will among you go on those roads you have mentioned, and let him who will among you come in the following of My Majesty."

The gamble paid off. The coalition forces had expected the Egyptians to take one of the flanking routes and had positioned their army accordingly. When the Egyptian army emerged from the Aruna Pass directly in front of Megiddo, the coalition was caught off-guard. The battle was a rout. The coalition forces broke and ran, and the garrison of Megiddo hauled the survivors up over the walls on ropes made from knotted clothing.

The Annals note, with a hint of frustration, that the Egyptian soldiers stopped to loot the enemy camp instead of pursuing the fleeing army immediately, which allowed the defenders to close the gates of Megiddo and forced a seven-month siege. But the outcome was decisive. When Megiddo finally fell, Egypt controlled the entire Levantine coast and the inland routes to Syria.

---

Over the next twenty years, Thutmose III conducted at least sixteen more campaigns in the Levant and Syria. The Annals record them with varying levels of detail — some are described at length, others are little more than lists of tribute received. The overall trajectory is clear: systematic expansion, consolidation, and the construction of a permanent imperial infrastructure.

At its maximum extent under Thutmose III, the Egyptian Empire stretched from the **Fourth Cataract** in Nubia (roughly 750 miles/1,200 km south of the traditional Egyptian border at the First Cataract) to the Euphrates River in northern Syria (roughly 400 miles/640 km northeast of the Egyptian Delta). The total span from the southern to the northern frontier was over 1,100 miles (1,800 km) — comparable in length to the distance from New York to Miami, though the territory was a narrow corridor along the Nile and the Levantine coast rather than a filled-in landmass.

The imperial system worked like this. In Nubia, Egypt governed directly. The **Viceroy of Kush** administered the territory through a hierarchy of Egyptian officials, temples, and garrisons. Nubian gold flowed north; Egyptian culture flowed south. Temples like the one Thutmose III built at **Jebel Barkal** (pronounced "JEB-el BAR-kal," a sacred mountain near the Fourth Cataract that was central to both Egyptian and later Kushite religion — Kingdom of Kush Ch 2) projected Egyptian religious authority deep into Africa. Nubian elites were educated in Egyptian schools, given Egyptian names, and integrated into the administration. This was cultural colonialism of a thoroughness that would not be matched until the Roman Empire.

In the Levant, the system was different. Egypt did not attempt to rule the Syrian and Palestinian city-states directly. Instead, it kept local kings in power as vassals, required them to pay tribute, send their sons to Egypt as hostages (where they were raised at court and returned home as pro-Egyptian rulers), and provide military support when called upon. Egyptian garrisons were stationed at key strategic points — **Gaza**, **Joppa** (modern Jaffa), **Byblos**, **Sumur** — but the day-to-day governance of the Levantine territories was left to local rulers. The system worked well when the pharaoh was strong and attentive. When he wasn't — as would become devastatingly clear under Akhenaten (Chapter 7) — the vassal states were on their own.

Thutmose III also received tribute and diplomatic exchanges from powers he did not directly control: Babylon, Assyria, the Hittites, and the Mitanni. The Annals record gifts of lapis lazuli from Babylon, horses from the Mitanni, and silver from Hatti (the Hittite heartland in central Anatolia). These were not acts of submission — Babylon and the Hittites were fully independent great powers. They were the diplomatic language of the Late Bronze Age international system, in which great kings addressed each other as "brother" and exchanged prestige goods as markers of status and alliance. This system would reach its peak under Amenhotep III (Chapter 6) and collapse spectacularly a century later.

---

Thutmose III's domestic achievement was equally important: he made the temple of **Karnak** into the greatest religious complex in the ancient world.

Karnak had existed since the Middle Kingdom (Chapter 1), but Thutmose III expanded it on a scale that transformed it from an important temple into an institution rivaling the pharaoh himself. He built the **Festival Hall** (Akh-menu, meaning "Effective of Monuments"), a unique columned hall whose stone pillars are carved to resemble tent poles — a deliberate reference to the military campaign tent, bringing the king's battlefield triumphs into the sacred precinct. He added pylons (monumental gateways flanked by tapering towers), obelisks, new sanctuaries, and enclosure walls. His Annals — the detailed campaign records — were carved on the walls of the inner chambers, turning the temple into both a place of worship and a national archive.

The Amun priesthood at Karnak benefited enormously from Thutmose III's conquests. A significant share of the war booty and annual tribute from the empire was donated to the temple: gold, silver, livestock, slaves, and land. Over the coming centuries, the Amun temple would accumulate so much wealth and land that it would become a rival center of power to the pharaoh himself — a development that would have profound consequences during the New Kingdom's decline (Chapter 9).

Thutmose III died around 1425 BCE, after a sole reign of roughly thirty-three years (fifty-four years from the start of his co-regency with Hatshepsut). He was buried in the Valley of the Kings, in a tomb (KV34) decorated with the earliest complete version of the **Amduat** (the underworld book describing Ra's twelve-hour nocturnal journey through the realm of the dead, a key New Kingdom funerary text). His mummy was later moved to the Deir el-Bahri cache, where it was found in 1881 — ironically, stored in the same cliff bay as the mortuary temple of the stepmother he had tried to erase.

His son **Amenhotep II** (reigned roughly 1427–1401 BCE) continued the military campaigns, though on a reduced scale. Amenhotep II was famous for his physical prowess — his inscriptions boast of his abilities as an archer (he could drive an arrow through a copper target from a speeding chariot, or so the texts claim), a horseman, and a runner. He conducted campaigns in Syria and Nubia, brought back captives and tribute, and maintained the empire his father had built. But the era of expansion was essentially over. What followed was the era of diplomacy.

---

## Chapter 6 — The Sun King

**Amenhotep III** (pronounced "ah-MEN-hoh-tep," reigned roughly 1391–1353 BCE) inherited the most powerful kingdom on Earth and spent thirty-eight years enjoying it.

That sounds dismissive, but it is not meant to be. Amenhotep III's achievement was to turn military supremacy into cultural and diplomatic dominance. He fought almost no wars. He didn't need to. The empire his grandfather Thutmose III had built was stable, the tribute was flowing, the Nubian gold mines were producing at peak capacity, and the international diplomatic system was functioning smoothly. Amenhotep III used the resulting wealth not for conquest but for architecture, art, diplomacy, and the projection of an image of kingship so magnificent that it influenced every subsequent Egyptian pharaoh — including his grandson Ramesses II, who spent sixty-six years trying to match it (Chapter 8).

---

The key to Amenhotep III's reign was **Queen Tiye** (pronounced "TEE-yeh"), his Great Royal Wife, and one of the most politically influential women in the ancient world. Tiye was not of royal blood. Her father, **Yuya** (pronounced "YOO-yah"), was a provincial official and military commander from the town of Akhmim in Upper Egypt. Her mother, **Thuya**, held priestly titles. The marriage of a pharaoh to a non-royal woman was unusual enough that Amenhotep III publicized it with a series of large commemorative scarabs (beetle-shaped carved stones used for official announcements — the press releases of ancient Egypt), distributing them across the empire.

Tiye's political importance went beyond the ceremonial. She appears in diplomatic correspondence alongside the pharaoh, and foreign kings addressed letters directly to her — a remarkable acknowledgment of her authority in a world where royal women were typically invisible in diplomatic exchanges. After Amenhotep III's death, the king of Mitanni, **Tushratta** (pronounced "too-SHRAT-ah"), wrote to Tiye asking her to ensure that the friendly relations between their kingdoms continued under her son. "You know," Tushratta wrote, "all the words that I spoke with your husband. You are the one who knows them all. No one else knows them." This is not the language a king uses with a passive consort. Tiye was a policy maker.

---

Amenhotep III's reign was the apex of the Late Bronze Age international system, a network of diplomatic relationships connecting the great powers of the Near East in a web of correspondence, gift exchange, royal marriages, and mutual recognition that historians sometimes call the **Amarna system** (after the site where the correspondence was discovered).

The **Amarna Letters** are a cache of roughly 380 cuneiform tablets found in 1887 at the ruins of Tell el-Amarna in Middle Egypt (the site of Akhenaten's capital, Chapter 7). They represent the diplomatic archive of the Egyptian court during the reigns of Amenhotep III and Akhenaten, roughly 1380–1330 BCE. The letters are written in **Akkadian** (the diplomatic lingua franca of the Near East, much as French was the language of European diplomacy in the 18th century), and they include correspondence with the kings of Babylon, Assyria, Mitanni, the Hittite Empire (Hittite Empire Ch 5), Arzawa (a kingdom in western Anatolia), Alashiya (Cyprus), and dozens of Levantine vassal rulers.

The correspondence between great kings is extraordinary reading. They address each other as "brother" and exchange gifts that are more like competitive displays of wealth than practical commerce. The Babylonian king Kadashman-Enlil complains that Amenhotep III sent him too few gold items and suspects they were gold-plated rather than solid: "Gold is like dirt in your country — one can simply gather it up. Why is it valued so dearly?" The Mitanni king Tushratta sends his daughter to marry Amenhotep III and then spends letter after letter demanding the return gifts he was promised. The Hittite king cautiously approaches Egypt for diplomatic contact. These letters read like the minutes of a very expensive poker game played by men who were acutely conscious of their relative status.

The system also included **diplomatic marriages**: foreign princesses sent to the Egyptian court to seal alliances. Amenhotep III married daughters of the kings of Babylon, Mitanni, and Arzawa. He did not, however, send Egyptian princesses abroad. When the Babylonian king asked for an Egyptian princess, Amenhotep III's recorded response was blunt: "From of old, a daughter of the king of Egypt has not been given to anyone." Egypt took princesses from everyone else but shared none of its own. The policy reflected Egypt's conviction of its own superiority — a conviction that was, at this point in history, well supported by reality.

For a cross-civilizational snapshot: while Amenhotep III was exchanging gold and princesses with Babylon and Mitanni, the **Shang Dynasty** in China was at its height, producing the finest bronze vessels in the world and practicing oracle-bone divination in their capital at Yinxu (Shang Dynasty Ch 6). In the **Andes**, the Paracas culture was creating elaborate textile art on the south Peruvian coast, an ocean and a world away from the Bronze Age diplomatic circuit (Early Andean Ch 8). In **Greece**, the Mycenaean palatial civilization was in its prime, with fortress-palaces at Mycenae, Tiryns, and Pylos (Mycenaean Civilization Ch 4-5) — and Mycenaean pottery has been found in Egyptian contexts from this period, suggesting indirect trade contacts.

---

Amenhotep III was the greatest builder of the Eighteenth Dynasty, and possibly of any Egyptian dynasty before the Ramessids.

The **Temple of Luxor**, begun under Amenhotep III and later expanded by Ramesses II (Chapter 8), was dedicated not to a specific god but to the concept of divine kingship itself — a temple celebrating the pharaoh's divine right to rule, where the annual **Opet Festival** procession would carry the sacred barque (ceremonial boat) of Amun from Karnak to Luxor in a spectacular public ritual. The temple's colonnade, with its massive papyrus-bundle columns, remains one of the most impressive spaces in Egyptian architecture.

Amenhotep III's mortuary temple on the west bank of Thebes was the largest ever built in Egypt. Almost nothing of it survives — it was dismantled in antiquity for its building stone — but its entrance was guarded by two colossal seated statues of the pharaoh, each about 60 feet (18 meters) high, carved from single blocks of quartzite transported 400 miles (640 km) from quarries near Cairo. These are the **Colossi of Memnon**, which stand in the floodplain today as lonely sentinels guarding nothing, the temple behind them entirely gone. The statues became a famous tourist attraction in the Roman period — the northern colossus, damaged by an earthquake in 27 BCE, emitted a musical humming sound at dawn (probably caused by temperature-induced cracking of the stone as it heated in the morning sun), which the Greeks attributed to the mythical Ethiopian king Memnon greeting his mother Eos, the goddess of the dawn. When Emperor Septimius Severus repaired the statue around 199 CE, the sound stopped.

Amenhotep III also built extensively at Karnak, at Soleb in Nubia (a temple complex that rivaled Luxor in refinement), and at his palace complex at **Malkata** on the west bank of Thebes — a sprawling palace city with painted floors, audience halls, an artificial harbor, and separate quarters for the harem. The palace was decorated in a style that Egyptian art historians consider the peak of Eighteenth Dynasty aesthetics: naturalistic, fluid, and luxurious.

The art of Amenhotep III's reign deserves mention in its own right. Sculptors produced portrait statues of a refinement and psychological depth unmatched before or after — faces with heavy-lidded eyes, slight smiles, and an air of serene confidence that conveys both divinity and personality. The **glass industry** reached its Egyptian peak during this period, producing polychrome vessels, jewelry, and decorative objects in brilliant blues, greens, yellows, and whites. Egyptian glass was a luxury export — glass vessels from this period have been found across the eastern Mediterranean and as far away as Mycenaean Greece.

Amenhotep III died around 1353 BCE, probably in his fifties, after the longest and most prosperous reign in the Eighteenth Dynasty. His successor would undo nearly everything he had built. The smooth diplomatic system, the stable empire, the balanced relationship between pharaoh and priesthood, the confident cultural self-assurance — all of it was about to be torn apart by a pharaoh who decided that every other god in Egypt was false, and that only the sun disk was real.

## Chapter 7 — The Heretic Pharaoh

No pharaoh in Egyptian history has generated more scholarly debate, more popular fascination, and more wildly contradictory interpretations than **Akhenaten** (pronounced "ahk-eh-NAH-ten"), born Amenhotep IV, who came to the throne around 1353 BCE and spent seventeen years attempting to reshape Egyptian civilization around the worship of a single god: the visible sun disk, called the **Aten**.

Was Akhenaten a visionary monotheist, a precursor of Moses, a philosophical genius centuries ahead of his time? Or was he a fanatical authoritarian, a delusional megalomaniac who wrecked a functioning empire because he was in love with an idea? The honest answer is that the evidence supports elements of both interpretations and neither one entirely. What is not debated is that his revolution was the most radical departure from tradition in three thousand years of Egyptian civilization, and that it failed completely.

---

Akhenaten began his reign as **Amenhotep IV**, using his birth name and following conventional religious practices. The change happened gradually over the first few years and then accelerated dramatically.

The **Aten** (pronounced "AH-ten") was not a new god. The Aten — the visible disk of the sun — had been venerated as an aspect of the sun god Ra since the Middle Kingdom, and Amenhotep III (Akhenaten's father) had shown particular devotion to the Aten alongside the traditional Amun cult. What Akhenaten did was different in kind, not just degree.

First, he elevated the Aten above all other gods. Then he declared that the Aten was the only true god. Then he changed his name from Amenhotep ("Amun is satisfied") to Akhenaten ("Effective for the Aten"), erasing the name of Amun from his own identity. Then he ordered the name of Amun — and eventually the names of most other gods — chiseled off every monument in Egypt. Thousands of inscriptions were mutilated. Temple revenues were redirected. The Amun priesthood at Karnak, one of the wealthiest and most powerful institutions in the country, was shut down.

This was not a philosophical disagreement about the nature of divinity. This was a head-on assault on the economic and political power of the priestly establishment — an establishment that controlled enormous landholdings, commanded thousands of workers, and had accumulated centuries of institutional authority. Whether Akhenaten was driven by genuine religious conviction, a desire to break the power of the Amun priesthood, or both, the practical effect was the same: he centralized all religious authority (and its associated wealth) in himself, as the sole intermediary between humanity and the Aten.

The theology of Atenism, as expressed in the **Great Hymn to the Aten** (probably composed by Akhenaten himself, or at least attributed to him), is remarkable by any standard:

"How manifold are your works! They are hidden from the face of man. O sole god, like whom there is no other! You created the world according to your desire, while you were alone: all men, cattle, and wild beasts, whatever is on earth, going upon its feet, and what is on high, flying with its wings."

The parallels to Psalm 104 in the Hebrew Bible have been noted by scholars since the nineteenth century. Whether there is a direct connection (some scholars have proposed that Atenism influenced early Israelite monotheism, though this is debated and the evidence is circumstantial) or a parallel development, the Great Hymn to the Aten is one of the earliest expressions of a worldview in which a single divine intelligence created and sustains all living things. It is also, notably, a warm and celebratory text — focused on light, growth, fertility, and joy — in contrast to the darker, more anxiety-driven funerary literature of traditional Egyptian religion with its demons, serpents, and weighing of hearts.

---

In the fifth year of his reign, Akhenaten did something even more radical than changing the religion. He abandoned Thebes entirely and built a new capital from scratch on virgin ground.

The site he chose was a barren stretch of desert on the east bank of the Nile in Middle Egypt, roughly 250 miles (400 km) north of Thebes, where the cliffs curve back from the river to create a broad natural amphitheater. He named it **Akhetaten** (pronounced "ahk-et-AH-ten," meaning "Horizon of the Aten"), and it is known today as **Tell el-Amarna** or simply **Amarna**. Within a few years, a complete city had been built: palaces, temples, government buildings, workers' housing, a royal road wide enough for chariot processions, and suburbs spreading across the desert plain. The population may have reached 30,000 to 50,000 at its peak.

The Aten temples at Amarna were unlike any Egyptian temple before or since. Traditional Egyptian temples were roofed, dark, enclosed spaces — the innermost sanctuary was a small chamber accessible only to the highest priests, perpetually shrouded in incense and lamplight. Akhenaten's temples were open to the sky, flooded with sunlight, with altars piled high with food offerings visible to all worshippers. The architecture was the theology made physical: the Aten was the visible sun, and its temple had to be open to the sun's light. No darkness. No hidden sanctuaries. No mystery.

The art of the Amarna period was equally revolutionary. Traditional Egyptian art followed rigid conventions that had changed little in two thousand years: the pharaoh was depicted in idealized form, broad-shouldered, narrow-waisted, serene, eternal. Amarna art depicted Akhenaten with an elongated face, thick lips, a narrow chest, wide hips, a sagging belly, and spindly limbs. Nefertiti and the royal daughters were shown with similarly exaggerated features — elongated skulls, thin necks, soft bodies. Whether this represents a genuine medical condition (Marfan syndrome, Froehlich's syndrome, and other diagnoses have been proposed and debated for a century), a deliberate artistic distortion meant to convey the pharaoh's otherness, or simply a new aesthetic that Akhenaten imposed on his artists is unresolved. What is clear is that the Amarna style was a conscious rejection of everything Egyptian art had been.

The most famous product of Amarna art is the painted limestone bust of **Nefertiti** (pronounced "nef-er-TEE-tee"), discovered in 1912 by the German archaeologist Ludwig Borchardt in the workshop of the sculptor Thutmose at Amarna. The bust is a masterwork of ancient portraiture — elegant, symmetrical, and hauntingly lifelike — and it has become one of the most recognized images from the ancient world. It currently resides in the Neues Museum in Berlin, and Egypt has repeatedly requested its return.

---

Nefertiti's role in Akhenaten's revolution went far beyond being a beautiful queen. She appears in temple reliefs performing rituals that were normally reserved for the pharaoh — smiting enemies, making offerings to the Aten, driving a chariot. In some scenes, she is depicted wearing the pharaonic crown. Some scholars have argued that Nefertiti was effectively a co-ruler, wielding power equal to or even exceeding Akhenaten's, at least in the public representation of the regime.

What happened to Nefertiti is one of the great mysteries of Egyptian history. She disappears from the record around year 12 of Akhenaten's reign. Did she die? Was she exiled? Was she elevated to an even higher position under a new name? Some Egyptologists believe that a shadowy figure called **Neferneferuaten** (a ruler who may have briefly co-reigned with Akhenaten or succeeded him) was Nefertiti under a new throne name. If true, she may have ruled Egypt as pharaoh in her own right, however briefly, before or after Akhenaten's death. The evidence is fragmentary and fiercely debated.

What is less debated is what Akhenaten's religious revolution did to Egypt's empire. The answer is: nothing good.

The Amarna Letters (Chapter 6) include a series of increasingly desperate messages from Egypt's Levantine vassal kings, begging the pharaoh for military help against external threats — the Hittites pressing south, local rivals encroaching on loyal cities, bands of **Habiru** (a term for displaced or semi-nomadic people in the Levant, sometimes tentatively linked to the early Hebrews, though the connection is debated) raiding the countryside. The most anguished letters come from **Rib-Hadda**, the king of Byblos, who wrote dozens of letters pleading for Egyptian troops and accusing his neighbors of treason: "All my cities have been taken from me, yet there is no one who extends a hand to me." The Egyptian court, consumed by the religious revolution, either did not respond or responded inadequately. The empire in the Levant did not collapse entirely under Akhenaten, but it frayed badly, and the loss of prestige and territory would take decades to recover.

---

Akhenaten died around 1336 BCE, after seventeen years of rule. His immediate successor may have been Neferneferuaten (possibly Nefertiti) or the shadowy figure of **Smenkhkare** (pronounced "smenk-KAR-eh"), who may or may not be the same person — the sequence of rulers between Akhenaten and Tutankhamun is one of the most confused patches in Egyptian chronology.

What is clear is that a boy named **Tutankhamun** (pronounced "toot-ank-AH-moon"), probably Akhenaten's son by a secondary wife, came to the throne around 1332 BCE at roughly nine or ten years of age. His original name was Tutankhaten ("Living Image of the Aten"), and the first act of his reign — almost certainly directed by his advisors rather than the child king himself — was to change it to Tutankhamun ("Living Image of Amun"). The name change was a public declaration: the Aten revolution was over. Amun was back.

The **Restoration Stela**, erected at Karnak early in Tutankhamun's reign, describes the state of affairs the boy king inherited: "The temples of the gods and goddesses had fallen into ruin. Their shrines had collapsed and become overgrown mounds. The land was in a state of chaos, and the gods had turned their backs on this land." The text is politically motivated — it was designed to justify the restoration and discredit Akhenaten — but the underlying reality was real. The Aten revolution had disrupted the temple economies, alienated the priesthood, and (according to the Restoration Stela) caused the gods to abandon Egypt, resulting in military failure abroad.

Tutankhamun's court restored the old gods, reopened the temples, and began the slow process of repairing the damage. The capital was moved back to Memphis and Thebes. Amarna was abandoned and would eventually be demolished, its stone blocks recycled into other buildings.

Tutankhamun died around 1323 BCE, at approximately eighteen or nineteen years of age. The cause of death has been debated for decades — malaria, a broken leg, genetic disorders resulting from inbreeding (his parents were siblings), or some combination. CT scans have revealed multiple health problems, and DNA analysis has confirmed that he was the product of an incestuous union.

In death, Tutankhamun became the most famous pharaoh in history — not for anything he did in life, but because his tomb (designated KV62, discovered by Howard Carter in 1922) was the only royal tomb in the Valley of the Kings found nearly intact, with its spectacular golden burial goods, including the iconic golden death mask, still in place. The tomb's contents, now in the Egyptian Museum in Cairo, are the single most famous collection of ancient artifacts in the world.

---

One of the most remarkable documents from this chaotic period is a letter from Tutankhamun's widow, **Ankhesenamun** (pronounced "ank-es-en-AH-moon"), to the Hittite king **Suppiluliuma I** (pronounced "soo-pee-loo-lee-OO-mah," the most powerful Hittite king, who was actively expanding his empire at Egypt's expense — Hittite Empire Ch 5-6). The letter, preserved in the Hittite archives at Hattusha, reads: "My husband has died. I do not have a son. But to you, they say you have many sons. You might give me one of your sons, and he might become my husband."

The letter is extraordinary. An Egyptian queen was asking the king of Egypt's greatest rival to send a prince to become pharaoh. The implications were staggering. Suppiluliuma was initially suspicious — this had to be a trap — and sent an envoy to verify. After confirming the offer was genuine, he sent his son, Prince **Zannanza** (pronounced "zan-NAN-zah"), to Egypt.

Zannanza was murdered on the way, almost certainly on the orders of the Egyptian court. The Hittite archives record Suppiluliuma's fury: "They killed him!" The murder may have been orchestrated by **Ay** (pronounced "EYE"), an elderly courtier who married Ankhesenamun and took the throne himself, reigning for about four years. Ankhesenamun disappears from the record after the marriage, and her fate is unknown.

Ay was followed by **Horemheb** (pronounced "hor-em-HEB"), a military commander who had served under Tutankhamun and who systematically dismantled what remained of the Amarna legacy. Horemheb was the cleanup man. He rebuilt temples, restored military discipline, reorganized the administration, and — critically — erased the names of Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, Ay, and Nefertiti from the official king lists, reassigning their monuments to himself. In the official chronology maintained by later Egyptians, Horemheb followed directly after Amenhotep III, as if the entire Amarna period had never happened.

When Horemheb died around 1292 BCE without an heir, the throne passed to his vizier, a military officer named **Paramessu**, who became **Ramesses I** — the first king of the Nineteenth Dynasty and the grandfather of the most famous pharaoh in history.

---

## Chapter 8 — The Greatest Name

There is a joke among Egyptologists that wherever you dig in Egypt, you find the name of Ramesses II. He carved it on temples he built. He carved it on temples other pharaohs built. He carved it on statues that weren't his. He put his cartouche on obelisks, on cliff faces, on the walls of conquered cities, and on objects so old that the original maker was already forgotten. No pharaoh before or after inscribed his name on as many monuments across as much territory as **Ramesses II** (pronounced "RAM-eh-seez"), and the cumulative effect, three thousand years later, is that his name is everywhere.

This is not an accident. Ramesses II reigned for sixty-six years (roughly 1279–1213 BCE), making his the second-longest independently verified reign in Egyptian history (after Pepi II of the Old Kingdom, whose ninety-plus-year reign — Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 6 — is debated). He fathered at least eighty children by multiple wives. He built more temples, erected more statues, and produced more inscriptions than any pharaoh in history. He was also, by the standard of his own time, a genuinely important ruler whose military campaigns, diplomatic achievements, and building program defined Egypt's image of itself for the next thousand years. The Ramessid era — the Nineteenth and Twentieth Dynasties, roughly 1292–1069 BCE — takes its name from the dynasty he dominated.

But the Ramessid story starts with his father.

---

**Seti I** (pronounced "SET-ee," reigned roughly 1294–1279 BCE) was the son of Ramesses I and the true founder of the Nineteenth Dynasty's power. Where Ramesses I had reigned only briefly (about two years), Seti I had time to rebuild.

He campaigned in the Levant, reasserting Egyptian control over territories that had drifted during the chaos of the late Eighteenth Dynasty. He fought the **Hittites**, who had expanded their sphere of influence southward into Syria during the Amarna period's neglect (Hittite Empire Ch 5-6). He campaigned against the Libyans on Egypt's western border and against the Shasu (Bedouin nomads in the Sinai and Negev). His military record was solid if not spectacular — he restored Egypt's position without achieving the total dominance of a Thutmose III.

Seti I's lasting legacy is architectural. His temple at **Abydos** (the ancient sacred city where the earliest pharaohs had been buried — Early Dynastic Egypt Ch 5) is one of the finest buildings in Egypt, with relief carvings of such exquisite detail and delicacy that they are considered among the masterpieces of Egyptian art. The temple contains the famous **Abydos King List**, a sequence of cartouches listing every pharaoh from Menes (the legendary unifier) to Seti I himself. The list is selective — it omits the Hyksos, Hatshepsut, and the Amarna pharaohs — but it is one of the primary sources for the chronology of Egyptian kingship.

Seti I also began building the **Great Hypostyle Hall** at Karnak, the most jaw-dropping space in Egyptian architecture: a forest of 134 columns arranged in sixteen rows, the central twelve columns rising to 69 feet (21 meters) and topped with open papyrus-flower capitals wide enough for a hundred people to stand on. The hall covers an area of about 54,000 square feet (5,000 square meters) — larger than the interior of Notre-Dame de Paris. Seti I built the northern half; Ramesses II completed the southern half and, characteristically, carved his name over much of his father's work.

---

Ramesses II came to the throne around 1279 BCE, probably in his early twenties. He would not leave it for sixty-six years. During that time, he fought the most famous battle of the ancient world, signed the oldest known peace treaty, built the most iconic monuments in Egypt, and produced so many children that his dynasty dominated Egyptian politics for a century after his death.

The defining military event of his reign was the **Battle of Kadesh** (pronounced "KAH-desh"), fought in roughly 1274 BCE against the **Hittite** king **Muwatalli II** (pronounced "moo-wah-TAL-ee") at the city of Kadesh on the Orontes River in modern Syria.

Kadesh was the largest chariot battle in recorded history. Ramesses II's army included roughly 20,000 soldiers and 2,000 chariots. The Hittite force was comparable in size and included chariot contingents from across the Hittite Empire's vassal network, including troops from the kingdom of Amurru, Ugarit, and other Syrian states (Hittite Empire Ch 6). The strategic stakes were enormous: control of Syria and the balance of power in the entire Near East.

What actually happened at Kadesh is obscured by the fact that our most detailed account is Ramesses II's own version, inscribed in exhaustive detail on the walls of five different temples (Karnak, Luxor, the Ramesseum, Abu Simbel, and Abydos). In Ramesses' telling, the battle was a personal triumph. The Hittites ambushed the Egyptian army while it was divided into four marching columns, overwhelmed the leading division, and trapped Ramesses with a small bodyguard. The pharaoh then personally rallied his forces, charged into the Hittite chariots virtually alone, and turned the battle by sheer heroism: "His Majesty was alone, no one with him. He found himself in the midst of two thousand five hundred chariots."

The reality was probably less dramatic. The Egyptian army was ambushed due to poor intelligence (Ramesses had trusted false reports from captured spies), and the initial phase of the battle was a near-disaster. Reinforcements from the coastal route (the Ne'arin, a separate Egyptian force marching from the direction of Amurru) arrived at a critical moment and turned the tide. The battle ended in something close to a tactical draw — the Egyptians held the field but failed to capture Kadesh, and the Hittites retained control of the city and the surrounding territory.

Ramesses II's propagandistic account of Kadesh as a personal victory is perhaps the most elaborate example of spin in the ancient world. It is worth noting that the Hittite accounts, preserved at Hattusha, also claimed victory. Both sides were lying, or at least embellishing heavily. The battle was a stalemate.

---

But the aftermath of Kadesh was genuinely historic.

In 1259 BCE, roughly fifteen years after the battle, Ramesses II and the Hittite king **Hattusili III** (pronounced "hat-too-SEE-lee," who had come to power through a coup against Muwatalli II's successor — Hittite Empire Ch 7) signed a formal peace treaty. The **Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty** is the oldest known peace treaty in world history — the oldest surviving document in which two sovereign powers formally agree to end hostilities, respect each other's borders, extradite fugitives, and provide mutual military assistance.

The treaty was written in both Akkadian (the diplomatic language) and Egyptian, and copies were exchanged between the two courts. The Hittite version, written on a silver tablet, is described in Egyptian texts but has not survived. The Egyptian version is inscribed on the walls of Karnak and the Ramesseum. A Babylonian-language copy of the Hittite version was found in the archives at Hattusha. A replica of the treaty hangs in the United Nations headquarters in New York — a symbol of the idea that even the most implacable enemies can negotiate peace.

The treaty was reinforced by a diplomatic marriage: Ramesses II married a Hittite princess around 1246 BCE. She was given an Egyptian name, **Maathorneferure**, and appears in inscriptions at Abu Simbel. The marriage sealed the alliance, and the two empires maintained peaceful relations for the rest of their coexistence — until the Hittite Empire was destroyed by the Bronze Age Collapse about fifty years later (Chapter 9).

---

Ramesses II's building program was on a scale that no pharaoh before or after could match, largely because no other pharaoh had sixty-six years to build.

**Abu Simbel** is his masterpiece. Located in Nubia, about 180 miles (290 km) south of the First Cataract, the site consists of two temples carved directly into a sandstone cliff face on the west bank of the Nile. The Great Temple's facade features four colossal seated statues of Ramesses II, each about 66 feet (20 meters) high, flanked by smaller statues of his family members. The interior extends deep into the rock, with a series of halls decorated with reliefs of the Battle of Kadesh and other military triumphs, culminating in an innermost sanctuary containing four seated statues of gods (including a deified Ramesses himself). The temple was oriented so precisely that twice a year — around February 22 and October 22 — sunlight penetrated the full 185 feet (56 meters) of the interior and illuminated the faces of the three gods at the back of the sanctuary, leaving the fourth (the underworld god Ptah) in shadow. This solar alignment was a deliberate engineering feat, and it still functions today — though when the entire temple was relocated in the 1960s to save it from the rising waters of Lake Nasser behind the Aswan High Dam, the alignment shifted by about one day.

The smaller temple at Abu Simbel was dedicated to the goddess Hathor and to Ramesses II's favorite wife, **Nefertari** (not to be confused with Nefertiti, Akhenaten's wife — similar names, different women, different centuries). Nefertari's own tomb in the Valley of the Queens (QV66) is the most beautifully decorated tomb in all of Egypt — its wall paintings, depicting the queen in the company of the gods, are preserved in vivid color and are considered the finest achievement of New Kingdom tomb painting.

The **Ramesseum**, Ramesses II's mortuary temple on the west bank of Thebes, included a colossal seated statue of the pharaoh estimated to have weighed over a thousand tons. The statue now lies in fragments on the temple floor. The English Romantic poet Percy Bysshe Shelley saw (or heard about) a fragment of this statue and wrote "Ozymandias" (1818), one of the most famous poems in English, about a shattered colossus in the desert: "Look on my works, ye Mighty, and despair!" Ozymandias is a Greek rendering of Ramesses' throne name, User-maat-re.

Ramesses II also built or expanded temples at Karnak (completing the Great Hypostyle Hall), Luxor (adding the entrance pylon, the great courtyard, and the colossal statues and obelisks), Abydos, Memphis, and dozens of sites across Nubia. His name, as noted, is everywhere.

---

Ramesses II's last years were long. He outlived at least twelve of his sons. His successor was his thirteenth son, **Merneptah** (pronounced "mer-NEP-tah"), who came to the throne around 1213 BCE already in his sixties. Merneptah's reign was short (about ten years) but produced one of the most historically significant artifacts in Egypt: the **Merneptah Stele**, also known as the "Israel Stele."

The stele (a large stone slab inscribed with hieroglyphic text, found in Merneptah's mortuary temple at Thebes) is primarily a victory inscription celebrating Merneptah's defeat of a Libyan invasion. But in the last few lines, it includes a list of defeated peoples in the Levant, and among them is a single line: "Israel is laid waste; his seed is no more." This is the earliest known reference to **Israel** in any ancient text — the first time the name appears in the historical record. The reference is brief and uninformative (it tells us that a people called Israel existed in Canaan around 1208 BCE and that Merneptah claimed to have defeated them, nothing more), but its significance for the history of the Abrahamic religions is enormous. The Merneptah Stele is one of the few points where Egyptian history and biblical history touch each other in the archaeological record.

The stele also provides a useful chronological anchor. Around 1208 BCE, as Egypt was already beginning its slow decline, the peoples who would later produce the Hebrew Bible were establishing themselves in the hill country of Canaan. Two civilizations whose stories would shape the modern world were briefly in the same frame — one at the end of its imperial power, the other at the beginning of its historical existence.

Ramesses II's death around 1213 BCE, after sixty-six years on the throne, did not end the dynasty, but it ended the era. The Nineteenth Dynasty continued for another generation, through Merneptah and a confused sequence of short-lived successors (Seti II, Amenmesse, Siptah, and the female regent **Twosret**, the last ruler of the Nineteenth Dynasty). By 1186 BCE, the Nineteenth Dynasty was finished, and the Twentieth Dynasty took over under a new pharaoh who took the name Ramesses III — deliberately echoing the greatest name in Egyptian history. He would need all the prestige that name could carry, because the Bronze Age world was about to end.

---

Before leaving the Ramessid golden age, one unexpected product of the era deserves mention: Egyptian love poetry. The New Kingdom produced a body of lyric poetry — preserved on papyri and ostraca (inscribed pottery fragments) from Deir el-Medina and elsewhere — that is among the most beautiful in the ancient world. The poems are frank, sensual, and emotionally direct in ways that might surprise readers who think of ancient Egypt only in terms of mummies and stone monuments.

"My heart flutters hastily when I think of my love for you; it lets me not act sensibly, it leaps from its place. It lets me not put on a dress, nor wrap my scarf around me. I put no paint upon my eyes, I'm even not anointed."

These are real voices from three thousand years ago — not royal propaganda, not priestly incantation, but ordinary people writing about desire, jealousy, longing, and the physical ache of being separated from someone you love. The love poetry of the New Kingdom stands alongside the Song of Solomon and the poetry of Sappho as one of the great lyric traditions of the ancient world.

---

## Chapter 9 — The Last Stand

The end of the Bronze Age is one of the great catastrophes of human history. Between roughly 1200 and 1150 BCE, a cascade of destruction swept across the eastern Mediterranean, destroying or severely damaging nearly every major civilization in the region. The **Hittite Empire** collapsed entirely — its capital, Hattusha, was burned and abandoned around 1180 BCE (Hittite Empire Ch 8). The **Mycenaean** palace-states of Greece were destroyed or abandoned (Mycenaean Civilization Ch 7). The great trading city of **Ugarit** on the Syrian coast was sacked and never reoccupied. The Kassite dynasty of Babylon was overthrown. The kingdom of **Amurru** vanished. Cyprus was devastated. The entire international trading system that had connected these civilizations — the network of diplomacy, gift exchange, and commercial shipping that the Amarna Letters document — ceased to function.

The causes of the **Bronze Age Collapse** are debated and were probably multiple: climate change (drought conditions have been detected in the archaeological record), earthquakes (the eastern Mediterranean is seismically active), internal rebellions, disruption of trade routes, and systems failure in societies that had become too complex and too interconnected to absorb multiple simultaneous shocks. The collapse was not a single event but a cascading series of failures, each one making the next worse, like dominoes falling across a map.

At the center of the military destruction were the **Sea Peoples**, a confederation of migrating and raiding groups whose exact origins are still debated. Egyptian texts name several groups — the **Peleset** (probably the Philistines), the **Tjeker**, the **Shekelesh**, the **Denyen**, and the **Weshesh** — but these names do not map neatly onto known peoples, and the Sea Peoples may have included displaced populations from Mycenaean Greece, western Anatolia, the Aegean islands, Sardinia, Sicily, and other regions. They were not a unified army under a single command. They were a migration — whole populations on the move, with families, oxcarts, and livestock, driven by the same forces of climate change and civilizational collapse that were destroying the world they came from.

Egypt was the only major Bronze Age power that survived the Sea Peoples. And the pharaoh who saved it was **Ramesses III** (reigned roughly 1186–1155 BCE), the second king of the Twentieth Dynasty and the last great warrior pharaoh.

---

Ramesses III fought the Sea Peoples in at least two major engagements, around 1178 BCE — one on land and one at sea, both depicted in vivid relief carvings on the walls of his mortuary temple at **Medinet Habu** (pronounced "med-EE-net HAH-boo"), on the west bank of Thebes.

The land battle was fought somewhere along the Levantine coast or the Egyptian Delta frontier (the exact location is uncertain). The Medinet Habu reliefs show the Sea Peoples arriving in ox-drawn carts, accompanied by women and children — confirming that this was a migration, not just a military raid. The Egyptian army, including both infantry and chariot forces, engaged the Sea Peoples in a pitched battle. The reliefs show the Egyptian chariots crashing into formations of Sea Peoples warriors fighting on foot, with the distinctive horned and feathered helmets that are one of the few visual identifiers of the Sea Peoples in the archaeological record.

The naval battle is even more remarkable. The Medinet Habu reliefs depict one of the earliest representations of a naval engagement in history. The Sea Peoples' ships, propelled by sails, have entered the mouths of the Nile. The Egyptian fleet, composed of war galleys with both oars and sails, engages them in shallow water, with Egyptian archers on both the ships and the shore pouring arrows into the enemy vessels. The scenes show capsized ships, drowning warriors, and bound captives being dragged from the water. Whether the naval battle was a single engagement or a series of actions at different Nile mouths is unclear, but the outcome was decisive: the Sea Peoples were defeated and turned back.

Ramesses III's victory inscription is appropriately grandiose: "I slew the Denyen in their isles, while the Tjeker and the Peleset were made ashes. The Shekelesh and the Weshesh of the sea were made nonexistent, captured all together and brought in captivity to Egypt." The language is exaggerated — the Sea Peoples were not annihilated — but the core claim is true. Egypt stopped them. No other power managed the same feat.

Some of the defeated Sea Peoples were settled as military colonists along the Egyptian frontier. The **Peleset** were settled in the southern coastal strip of the Levant — the region that would later be known, after them, as **Palestine**. The Peleset/Philistines would appear centuries later in the Hebrew Bible as the enemies of the Israelites, their name eventually giving a region its permanent label.

---

But saving Egypt from the Sea Peoples was not the same as saving the Egyptian Empire. The Bronze Age international system was gone. Egypt's Levantine vassal network had collapsed. The Hittites, Egypt's former rivals and then treaty partners, no longer existed. The trade routes that had brought copper from Cyprus, tin from Afghanistan, lapis lazuli from the Hindu Kush, and cedar from Lebanon were disrupted or destroyed. The gold from Nubia continued to flow, but the markets and diplomatic partnerships that gave it value were disappearing.

Ramesses III's reign was a long rearguard action, and the cracks showed in places the propaganda couldn't reach.

Around 1170 BCE, the workers at **Deir el-Medina** — the village that had housed the royal tomb builders for three hundred years (Chapter 3) — did something unprecedented. They walked off the job. Their grain rations, which constituted their wages (the Egyptian economy was based on redistribution, not money), had not been paid for weeks. The workers crossed the walls of their village, marched to the nearby mortuary temples, and staged a sit-in, refusing to return to work until they were paid.

This is the first recorded **workers' strike** in human history. It is documented in several papyri from Deir el-Medina, including the **Turin Strike Papyrus**, which records the workers' complaints and the officials' responses in bureaucratic detail. The immediate cause was unpaid rations, but the underlying cause was systemic: the Egyptian treasury was running out of grain. The costs of fighting the Sea Peoples, combined with declining trade revenues and the increasing expense of maintaining a vast temple economy, had outpaced the state's ability to collect and distribute resources.

The workers were eventually paid — at least partially — and returned to work. But the strike happened again, and again, in subsequent years. The strikes were a symptom. The engine that had driven the New Kingdom for five centuries — Nubian gold paying for an army that protected trade routes that generated wealth that funded temples that legitimized the pharaoh — was breaking down.

---

The final decades of the New Kingdom were defined by two additional crises: tomb robbery and the rise of the Amun priesthood.

The **Great Tomb Robbery trials**, documented in a series of papyri from the reign of **Ramesses IX** (about 1126–1108 BCE), record the investigation and prosecution of organized gangs of thieves who had been systematically looting the royal and noble tombs on the west bank of Thebes. The papyri describe the interrogation of suspects (including the use of beating to extract confessions), the opening and inspection of tombs to assess damage, and the political infighting between officials responsible for the necropolis. The robberies were not the work of casual grave-robbers. They involved the complicity of tomb guards, local officials, and possibly high-ranking administrators. Gold was being stripped from coffins, jewelry was being melted down, and royal mummies were being unwrapped for their amulets.

The tomb robberies were a sign that the system was failing at its most fundamental level. The entire purpose of the Valley of the Kings — the reason it had been created, the reason Deir el-Medina existed, the reason generations of artisans had spent their lives cutting and painting tombs in the desert heat — was to protect the pharaoh's body for eternity. If the state could no longer protect its own dead kings, it could no longer fulfill its most basic theological obligation. The contract between pharaoh and people — I maintain ma'at, you maintain my cult — was broken.

Meanwhile, the Amun priesthood at Karnak had been accumulating power and wealth for centuries. The temple of Amun owned vast landholdings, controlled thousands of workers, and operated as a quasi-independent economic institution. **Papyrus Harris I**, compiled at the death of Ramesses III, is the longest papyrus in existence (133 feet/41 meters) and records the pharaoh's donations to the temples of Egypt. The totals are staggering: the Amun temple alone controlled an estimated 81,000 workers, 421,000 head of cattle, 691,000 acres (280,000 hectares) of agricultural land, and 65 towns. These numbers, if accurate, meant that the Amun priesthood controlled a larger share of Egypt's economic output than the pharaoh himself.

By the late Twentieth Dynasty, the High Priest of Amun at Thebes was effectively an independent ruler of Upper Egypt, while the pharaoh in the Delta controlled Lower Egypt. The state was splitting in two — not through invasion or civil war, but through the slow migration of economic and political power from the throne to the temple.

---

The Twentieth Dynasty limped through a succession of increasingly powerless Ramessids: Ramesses IV through Ramesses XI, most of whom reigned briefly and accomplished little. **Ramesses XI** (reigned roughly 1099–1069 BCE) was the last. During his reign, a powerful figure named **Herihor** (pronounced "HAIR-ee-hor"), who held both the positions of High Priest of Amun and Viceroy of Kush, effectively ruled Upper Egypt as an independent potentate, while the pharaoh controlled only the Delta. Herihor had himself depicted on temple walls at Karnak in the same scale and the same poses as the pharaoh — a visual declaration that he considered himself the pharaoh's equal. Whether he ever formally claimed the royal title is debated, but the practical reality was clear: Egypt was divided again.

When Ramesses XI died around 1069 BCE, the New Kingdom died with him. What followed was the **Third Intermediate Period**: a fragmented era of competing dynasties, Libyan warlords, and eventually Nubian pharaohs — the kings of Kush who conquered Egypt from the south and founded the Twenty-Fifth Dynasty, ruling the same country that had once colonized their ancestors (Kingdom of Kush Ch 4-5).

---

The New Kingdom, in its five centuries of existence, had been the most powerful civilization on Earth. It had produced the Valley of the Kings, the temples of Karnak and Luxor, Abu Simbel, and the Ramesseum. It had conquered an empire stretching from the Fourth Cataract to the Euphrates. It had given the world Hatshepsut, Thutmose III, Akhenaten, Nefertiti, Tutankhamun, and Ramesses the Great. It had invented the diplomatic peace treaty, produced the oldest surgical manual, weathered the most devastating geopolitical catastrophe of the ancient world, and witnessed the first workers' strike in recorded history.

And it ended the way its predecessor periods had ended: fractured, exhausted, and divided. Egypt reinvented itself after the Old Kingdom's collapse, and again after the Hyksos occupation, and again after the Amarna crisis. Each reinvention was more ambitious and more brittle than the last. The Middle Kingdom had been a literary renaissance. The early New Kingdom had been a military explosion. The Ramessid era had been a monumental spectacle. And each time, the system grew more complex, more expensive, more dependent on the continuous flow of wealth from a shrinking resource base.

The ideas survived, as they always did. The pharaonic theology, the temple rituals, the concept of ma'at, the divine kingship — all of it continued through the Third Intermediate Period, through the Libyan dynasties, through the Nubian reconquest, through the Assyrian invasion, through the Persian conquest, through Alexander, through the Ptolemies, all the way to Cleopatra and the Roman absorption. Egypt's ideas lasted three thousand years. Its empires lasted about five hundred.

But that is not this story. This story ends in 1069 BCE, at Thebes and in the Delta, with a country that had survived every crisis the Bronze Age could throw at it and was still, despite everything, recognizably Egypt — fractured, impoverished, its tombs robbed and its empire gone, but still performing the rituals, still writing the texts, still insisting that the pharaoh was Horus and that ma'at would be maintained. The pyramids were already two thousand years old. The Story of Sinuhe was still being copied. And somewhere in the Levantine hills, a people called Israel, first named on an Egyptian stele, were beginning to write their own book — one that would outlast even the pharaohs.

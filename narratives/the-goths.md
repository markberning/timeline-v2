# Chapter 1 — A People From an Island

The previous book ended with a sentence pointing straight at this one. The fuse had burned for four hundred years, the small peoples of the cold north had clumped into a handful of huge confederations, and the last of those confederations to enter the story was the largest. The fuse had burned to its end. The explosion belonged to the Goths.

This is that explosion. It is the story of how a people remembered as having come from an island in the far north ended up sacking Rome, killing emperors on the battlefield, ruling Italy from a marble palace, translating the Bible into an alphabet that did not exist before they needed it, and finally being ground out of existence by a twenty-year war that left the country they had governed a depopulated ruin. No other Germanic people did more to end the ancient world. None of them did it with stranger contradictions: barbarians who quoted Roman law, heretic Christians who copied scripture in silver ink, illiterate horse-warriors who within two centuries produced a king who restored aqueducts and a philosopher whose prison notebook became one of the most-read books of the Middle Ages.

But before any of that, a confession, the same kind of confession the previous book opened with, because the same problem shapes everything here too: almost everything written about the early Goths was written long after the fact, often by people with reasons to shape the story, and the Goths' own account of where they came from is a legend that a modern historian cannot take at face value and cannot quite throw away either.

---

The legend goes like this. Long ago, in the deep northern past, the Goths lived on a great island called **Scandza** (pronounced "SKAN-zah"). It was a cold, crowded place, "a hive of nations," and one day a king named Berig loaded his people into three ships and sailed south across the water. They landed on the southern shore of the Baltic Sea at a place they called **Gothiscandza** (pronounced "GOTH-ih-SKAN-zah"), which means roughly "the Gothic coast" or "the Goths' Scandza." From there, over generations, they pushed inland and then drove south and east across the rivers of eastern Europe until they reached the Black Sea, where the real history starts.

"Scandza" is almost certainly Scandinavia, and "an island" is what Scandinavia looks like from the south if you have never been told otherwise: a vast land mass across the sea whose far side nobody has reached. The slowest ship in the legend, the one that fell behind, supposedly gave its passengers the mocking name *Gepidae* ("the sluggards"), the origin story for the Gepids, a real and closely related people who appear later in this book. This is the kind of detail that tells you what you are dealing with. It is not a chronicle. It is an origin myth doing what origin myths do: explaining who counts as us, who counts as the embarrassing cousins, and why we have a right to the land we are standing on. It is, to borrow a phrase this series likes, a delightful piece of royal propaganda.

The reason the legend survives at all, and the reason every word of it has to be handled with tongs, is a single book by a single writer, and he needs introducing properly because this entire chapter, and large parts of several others, rest on him.

---

The writer was **Jordanes** (pronounced "jor-DAY-neez"). He was not a Roman historian of the classical age. He wrote in Constantinople around 551 CE, near the very end of this book's story, more than four hundred years after the events Chapter 1 is describing. He says of himself that he was of Gothic descent and had worked as a secretary, and that he was a Christian. His book is called the *Getica* (pronounced "GET-ih-kah"), short for *On the Origin and Deeds of the Goths*, and it is the only sustained narrative history of the Goths to come down to us from anywhere near antiquity. Without it we would have almost no Gothic story at all, only the disconnected references of Greek and Roman writers describing Gothic armies hitting their cities.

Here is the problem, stated plainly because the rest of the book depends on the reader holding it. Jordanes says he was working in a hurry from memory, abridging a much longer, now-lost history of the Goths written a few decades earlier by a Roman senator named **Cassiodorus** (pronounced "kass-ee-oh-DOR-us"), who served the Gothic kings of Italy and had every political reason to make his employers look like an ancient and noble people with a glorious pedigree, ideally one as old and dignified as Rome's own. So the *Getica* is a sixth-century summary, made partly from memory, of a lost sixth-century book, written to flatter a Gothic dynasty, about events stretching back to a legendary migration centuries before anyone was writing any of it down. Every link in that chain is a place where the story could bend.

Modern scholars are genuinely divided about how much of Jordanes to believe. The harshest reading treats the Scandza migration as essentially invented, a learned construction stitched together from older Greek geography and the political needs of a Gothic court, the way a modern nation invents a heroic founding. A more moderate reading allows that a real folk memory of a northern origin probably lies underneath the literary embroidery, because a tradition that a people came from somewhere else is the kind of thing oral memory does preserve, even when it scrambles the details. This book will take the moderate line and keep flagging it: the Goths *remembered* coming from the north, that memory mattered to them, and we should neither swallow the three ships whole nor pretend the memory was nothing. And one consequence of all this holds for the whole book, so it is worth saying once and then not repeating: for the early centuries the Goths left no writing of their own at all, so every "first," every date, every confident claim about them means "as far as their enemies' panic, their graves, and a Christian secretary's hurried abridgement of a flatterer's history will allow." Where the ground is soft, this book will say so.

---

So where does the *archaeology* put them, as opposed to the legend? Here the picture is firmer, though it comes with its own warning that runs through the whole field: a pot is not a person. Archaeologists can trace a style of brooch or a way of burying the dead across a map and across the centuries, but a shared material culture is not the same thing as a shared people who would have called themselves Goths, and pinning a named people from a sixth-century book onto a scatter of excavated cemeteries is exactly the kind of move modern scholars argue about hardest. With that caution loaded, the standard reconstruction runs as follows.

In the first and second centuries CE, in the basin of the **Vistula** (pronounced "VISS-tyoo-lah"), the major river of what is now Poland running north to the Baltic, there was a distinctive archaeological culture that scholars call the **Wielbark culture** (pronounced "VEEL-bark," named after a site near modern Malbork). Wielbark is the best candidate for "the early Goths, on the ground, before the story starts," and the reasons it is a good candidate are worth seeing, because they show how this kind of history is actually done.

Wielbark cemeteries have a habit that sets them apart from their neighbors: the people buried their dead both by cremation and by laying the body in the ground unburned, and, strikingly, they buried them with almost no weapons, even in the graves of people who were clearly wealthy and important. That is unusual. Most Iron Age societies in the region put a man's spear and shield in the grave with him; the Wielbark people, for whatever reason of belief we cannot recover, mostly did not. They did bury jewelry, and a great deal of it: bronze and silver brooches, and amber, the fossilized tree resin that washes up on the Baltic shore and that the Roman world prized for amulets and ornament far out of proportion to its weight. The amber is a clue in itself. These communities sat at the northern end of the **Amber Road**, the long-distance route that had carried Baltic amber south to the Mediterranean for centuries, and through that route came Roman coins and Roman goods, which is how we know these were not isolated forest people but prosperous communities plugged into a trade network that reached the empire. They smelted their own iron from bog ore, farmed, and lived in unwalled villages. This is the world the Gothic story comes out of: not nomads, not savages, but settled, reasonably rich Baltic-zone farmers and traders, four hundred years before anyone wrote them a history.

---

These were Germanic-speaking people, and that single fact deserves slowing down for, because the reader who has not read the previous book needs it and the reader who has still benefits from the anchor. **Germanic** is a branch of the enormous **Indo-European** language family, the same family that produced Latin, Greek, the Celtic languages, Persian, and Sanskrit. "Same family" badly misleads if it makes you picture dialects of one tongue. By the time the Goths enter recorded history, the Germanic branch had been evolving separately from Latin for well over a thousand years. A Goth and a Roman meeting at the Danube were about as mutually intelligible as a modern English speaker and a modern Russian speaker: distantly, structurally related, sharing a buried common ancestor, and unable to understand a spoken word of each other. The Goths were not "northern Romans" or barbarian Celts; the Celtic peoples to their west spoke a different Indo-European branch again, as foreign to Gothic as Gothic was to Latin ([[celtic-cultures]]). They looked, to a Mediterranean writer, broadly like the other tall, fair, trousered northerners, and that resemblance fooled outsiders into lumping them together. The look was similar. The speech was foreign in every direction.

Gothic itself belongs to a sub-branch called East Germanic, and it carries a distinction this book will not cash in until Chapter 5, but is worth planting now: of all the languages in this enormous family, the one with the oldest substantial surviving text is not English or German or Norse but Gothic, and the text is a Bible, made by the people this book is about. The reason that is so strange, and what it means for everything from the Vikings to modern linguistics, waits for the chapter where Ulfilas builds the alphabet to do it ([[viking-age]], [[germanic-tribes]]). For now just hold the oddity: the first long writing in the ancestral tongue of half of Europe was produced by fourth-century steppe Goths.

---

Sometime around the late second and early third centuries, the Wielbark world moved. The archaeology shows the Wielbark style expanding southward and eastward, and absorbing or displacing an earlier neighboring culture (scholars call it the Oksywie culture) as it went. The legend tells the same thing as a story: the long trek from Gothiscandza, through the lands of other peoples, fighting and pushing, generation after generation, toward the Black Sea.

Why move at all? The honest answer is the same engine that drove the whole previous book and will drive this one. In the Baltic zone a family farmed a fixed amount of workable land with iron tools and a short growing season; when good years pushed the population up, there was no spare land to split among the extra sons, and the surplus people had to go somewhere. South and east lay better-watered soil, and beyond that lay the Roman world, whose coins, wine, and metalwork had been travelling up the Amber Road into these villages for generations, so the Goths did not have to imagine the empire's wealth in the abstract: they had been handling pieces of it their whole lives, and they knew which direction it came from. Land pressure pushed; Roman wealth, already physically present in their own settlements, pulled. The detailed sequence is genuinely uncertain, the dates are soft, and "the Goths migrated" almost certainly means "many small groups moved south at different times for different reasons, and the survivors later remembered it as one people's heroic journey," which is precisely how the Cimbri and the great migrations of the earlier book worked too ([[germanic-tribes]]). What is not in doubt is the destination and roughly when they arrived there in force. By the early-to-mid third century, peoples the Roman world was now calling Goths were on the steppe north of the Black Sea, on the very edge of the Roman frontier, and they had arrived with appetite.

The empire on the other side of that frontier was the richest state in the world, and it was about to have the worst century in its history. The Goths arrived to find the door not just unlocked but coming off its hinges.

---

# Chapter 2 — The Black Sea World

The Goths did not migrate into an empty country, and they did not crash straight onto Rome out of a featureless barbarian dark, the way the Roman sources and a great deal of later storytelling have it. The country they reached first was not Rome at all. It was the **Pontic steppe**, the open grassland north of the Black Sea spanning what is now Ukraine, Moldova, and Romania, and it was one of the most ethnically mixed and economically connected places in the ancient world. To understand the Gothic raids on Rome, you have to first understand that the Goths spent two centuries marinating in this world, and came out of it transformed.

The steppe was the western end of the great Eurasian grassland highway that runs for thousands of miles east to Mongolia, the homeland and road of mounted nomads (it is the highway down which the Huns will come in Chapter 6). For centuries before the Goths arrived, its dominant people had been the **Sarmatians** (pronounced "sar-MAY-shunz"), an Iranian-speaking confederation of horse nomads, and before them the related **Scythians** (pronounced "SITH-ee-unz"), who get their own book in this series ([[scythians]]). "Iranian-speaking" means a different branch of Indo-European again: as foreign to Gothic as Gothic was to Latin, the speech of peoples whose distant relatives ruled Persia. The Goths were Germanic newcomers settling among, fighting, intermarrying with, and learning from Iranian-speaking horsemen who had owned this grass for half a millennium. That contact is not decoration. It is where the Goths learned to fight from horseback in the heavy, lance-armed style that would shatter a Roman emperor's army at Adrianople (Chapter 7). The single most lethal Gothic military asset was, in a real sense, a loan from the Sarmatians.

And along the Black Sea coast itself there was a third world entirely: old Greek colonial cities, founded a thousand years earlier and now under Roman influence, full of merchants and grain ships and stone temples. The steppe in the Gothic period was a layered place: Greek-descended townsfolk on the coast, Iranian nomads on the open grass, settled farming peoples in the river valleys, and now Germanic Goths moving in among all of them and, increasingly, over them.

---

The archaeological signature of this mixed world has a name, and it is the single most important thing to take from this chapter, because it overturns the picture most people carry of "barbarian Goths." It is called the **Chernyakhov culture** (pronounced "chair-NYAH-koff," after a site in Ukraine). From roughly the third century to the late fourth, an enormous zone across modern Ukraine, Moldova, and Romania shows the same distinctive package of goods and settlements over and over, and the next paragraph spells out exactly what that package is and why it is a surprise. Most scholars connect its core to the Goths and the peoples they dominated, with the usual loud caveat that an archaeological culture is not an ethnic label.

What Chernyakhov sites actually contain is the surprise. Not the camps of wandering raiders. Substantial permanent villages, with long timber houses, sunken-floored workshops, and granaries for stored grain. Fields worked with iron tools. And, above all, *wheel-thrown pottery*, made on a fast potter's wheel and fired in proper kilns to a quality that earlier local pottery never reached. That detail does an enormous amount of work, so it is worth saying why. Hand-built pottery is what every village can make. Wheel-thrown, kiln-fired pottery at scale implies specialists who do nothing but make pots, a market to sell them in, and a society organized enough to support people who do not grow their own food. The Chernyakhov Goths had iron smelting, bronze and silver smiths turning out belt fittings and brooches, bone-comb makers, and pottery workshops. This is a working economy with craft specialization, not a horde.

The mechanism behind the prosperity is the one this series keeps returning to, and here it ran at full strength. Down the rivers and across the frontier markets came the manufactured wealth of the empire: Roman wine and olive oil in their tall pottery amphorae, fine red Roman tableware, glass, bronze vessels, silver coin. Up the other way went grain, hides, and slaves. The Goths sat astride this exchange and grew rich on it, and the wealth bought what wealth in this world always bought: bigger followings of armed men for the chiefs who controlled the trade. The same prestige-goods pump that had enriched the chiefs of the previous book was now running on the Black Sea, and it was building Gothic war-leaders into something the small chieftains of two centuries earlier had never been.

A grimmer engine ran alongside the trade, and the honesty rule means not skipping it. A major Gothic export was people. Gothic raiding, once it started, took Roman captives by the tens of thousands and brought them back to the steppe, where they were settled as agricultural labor and craft workers. Some of those captives were skilled artisans and Christian clergy, and their forced presence among the Goths turns out to matter later: they are one of the channels by which both Roman skills and the Christian religion first reached Gothic communities (Chapter 5). The Gothic economy was built partly on the unfree, and some of the most consequential ideas in Gothic history arrived in the heads of slaves.

---

Now the raiding. Around 238 CE, in the political chaos that followed the murder of the Roman emperor Maximinus Thrax, Gothic groups crossed the lower Danube into the Roman province of Moesia (roughly modern Bulgaria and Serbia) and discovered something intoxicating: it worked. The empire was distracted, its frontier troops were thin, and a successful raid came home loaded with silver, slaves, and prestige. In a society where a war-leader's followers stayed loyal exactly as long as he could supply gold and glory, a discovery like that is not a one-off. The raiders went back across the Danube, and the next year a larger band followed them, drawn by the same loot and the same easy success, and the year after that another.

The Goths had spent two hundred years becoming a settled, prosperous, militarily formidable people on the edge of the richest empire on earth, and just as the raids were becoming a habit that empire fell into the deepest political collapse of its entire history. The wolf had found the threshold; the house was about to set itself on fire from the inside.

---

# Chapter 3 — The Gothic Storm

For about thirty years in the middle of the third century, the Goths did to the Roman Balkans and the Roman Aegean something that had not been done to the Roman heartland since Hannibal, and they did it during the single worst stretch the empire ever survived. The two facts feed each other: Rome was normally untouchable in its own interior, and for these decades it had almost entirely stopped being Rome.

The backdrop is the **Crisis of the Third Century** (conventionally about 235 to 284 CE), and the previous book sketched it from the German side; this is what it looked like from the Black Sea. Emperors were murdered after months in power, sometimes after weeks. The silver coinage was debased, the mints stretching their silver by mixing in ever more base metal until the coins were nearly silverless, and as everyone realized the money was worth less, prices spiraled. Plague swept through. The empire briefly split into three separate pieces. The frontier armies were repeatedly pulled away to fight each other in civil wars instead of guarding the border. To a Gothic war-leader watching from across the Danube, this was not a crisis. It was the greatest opportunity in living memory, and the Goths took it with both hands.

---

The first serious blow fell under a leader the sources call **Cniva** (pronounced "kuh-NEE-vah"), the first Gothic individual whose campaigns we can follow in any detail, and even he is known only through Roman writers describing the man who beat their armies. Around 250 CE Cniva led a large Gothic force deep into the Balkans and besieged **Philippopolis** (pronounced "fih-LIP-oh-poh-lis," modern Plovdiv in Bulgaria), one of the great cities of Roman Thrace. The city fell. The sack was enormous; the Roman tradition speaks of vast plunder and many thousands of captives carried off, and although ancient casualty and captive numbers are routinely inflated and should be read as "a catastrophe" rather than as audited figures, the scale of this one is not seriously doubted. A major Roman provincial city had been taken and gutted by a barbarian army operating hundreds of miles inside the empire.

The Roman response was to send the emperor himself. **Decius** (pronounced "DEE-shus") was a Balkan-born senator and military man who had seized the throne in 249 in one of the period's many army coups, and restoring the battered Danube frontier was exactly the kind of soldier's task that had made him emperor in the first place, so he led the campaign in person to throw the Goths back across the river. In 251 CE he caught Cniva's army as it withdrew, heavy with plunder, and forced a battle at a place called **Abritus** (pronounced "AB-rih-tus," in the marshy country of modern northeastern Bulgaria). The battle went catastrophically for Rome. By the surviving accounts the Goths drew the Roman army onto bad ground, swampy terrain where Roman formations could not hold together, and destroyed it. Decius was killed there, and his son and co-emperor with him.

This is usually flagged as the first time a Roman emperor was killed in battle against a foreign enemy, and the claim is worth handling carefully, exactly the way this series handles every "first." It is true in the strict and important sense: no previous reigning Roman emperor is recorded as having died in combat against a foreign foe on the battlefield. The honest qualifications are these. Emperors had been killed before, constantly, but by their own soldiers, by usurpers, by assassins, the ordinary murderous machinery of Roman politics. Earlier Roman commanders had died fighting foreign enemies, the consuls slaughtered by Hannibal at Cannae most famously ([[ancient-rome]]), but they were not reigning emperors, because there were no emperors yet. And Decius's death is sometimes muddied in the sources by hints of Roman treachery on the day, which would make it less a clean barbarian triumph than a battle lost partly from within. With all that loaded: a sitting Roman emperor was destroyed, in the field, by a Gothic army, and the shock of it is real and was felt as such. It was a category of disaster the empire had not previously had a slot for.

---

Decius's death did not stop the storm; it accelerated it. Through the 250s and 260s the Goths and the peoples riding with them learned a second trick that made them far worse than land raiders. They took to the sea. The Goths had no seafaring tradition of their own, but they did not need one: the Greek-descended coastal cities and the local boat-building populations they now dominated did, and Gothic war-leaders supplied the muscle and the captives to crew vessels built or seized along the Black Sea's northern shore. From there the fleets sailed south and squeezed through the narrow straits that link the Black Sea to the Aegean, the Bosphorus and the Dardanelles, the same chokepoint Constantinople would later sit astride, and out into the open Greek seas. For a terrifying decade the cities of Greece and Asia Minor, places that had not feared a foreign army in centuries because they sat safely deep inside the empire, were sacked from the water.

The raids reached the richest targets imaginable. **Ephesus** (pronounced "EFF-eh-sus"), one of the great cities of Roman Asia Minor, was struck, and the famous Temple of Artemis there, one of the wonders of the ancient world, was plundered. Worst, in symbolic weight, was the sack of **Athens** around 267 CE. Athens, the intellectual capital of the classical world, the city of Plato and the Parthenon, was taken and burned by a seaborne raid; the destruction layer archaeologists have found in the lower city and the old marketplace area is severe, though how much of the city was sacked rather than abandoned, and whether the Acropolis itself held, the evidence does not let us say with confidence. There is a famous, much-loved story attached to this raid, that the Goths gathered the city's books to burn them and were talked out of it by one of their own who argued, dryly, that as long as the Greeks had their libraries they would keep reading instead of training for war. The story is repeated in a later source and is almost certainly too neat to be true, a literary anecdote rather than a recorded event, and this book flags it as such rather than serving it up as fact. But the sack itself happened, and the meaning is not in the anecdote. The meaning is that Athens, of all cities, could be burned by Goths who had arrived by ship.

---

The empire clawed its way back, and the man who turned the tide has a name that became a title. Around 269 CE the emperor **Claudius II** met a vast Gothic host at the **Battle of Naissus** (pronounced "NICE-us" or "NAH-iss-us," modern Niš in Serbia). The victory was not a single clean stroke but a grinding campaign: a great battle that broke the main host, followed by Roman cavalry hunting the scattered Gothic columns through the Balkan mountains all the way back toward the Danube, harrying the survivors, and finishing the work that disease in the crowded Gothic camps had begun. By the end, an enormous Gothic force had been killed, captured, or dispersed, and the great seaborne raids were broken for a generation. He took the victory name **Gothicus**, "conqueror of the Goths," and Roman writers thereafter called him Claudius Gothicus. The respite he bought was real, but he barely lived to enjoy it; Claudius himself died of plague the next year, and the Senate declared him a god, which is what Rome did with emperors it wished to honor. Many of the defeated Goths were not slaughtered or expelled but settled inside the empire as *coloni*, tied agricultural tenants, on Balkan land that decades of raiding, plague, and flight had emptied of its farmers, so that resettling them filled fields that would otherwise have gone to waste. That detail is easy to skip and should not be. It is the same policy the previous book watched Marcus Aurelius improvise: when you cannot keep them out, you let them in on conditions, and you hope the conditions hold. Rome was already, in 270, doing to the Goths the thing that would eventually undo it.

Two years later, in 271 CE, the emperor **Aurelian**, having beaten the Goths again and stabilized the frontier by hard fighting, made a cold strategic decision that tells you how the balance had shifted. He abandoned the province of **Dacia** (pronounced "DAY-shah," roughly modern Transylvania, north of the Danube, the territory Rome had conquered a century and a half earlier in its last great expansion). Aurelian pulled the army and the administration back across the Danube, evacuated the Roman settlers and townspeople who would go, leaving behind the farms, towns, and the dead of a century and a half of Roman Dacia, and shortened the frontier to something he could actually defend. This was presented as strategy, and as strategy it was sound. But it was an emperor of the people who had spent four hundred years only ever taking land formally signing a piece of the empire away, and naming it Dacia Aureliana on the safe side of the river as if relabeling the loss could cushion it. It was the first time Rome had voluntarily given up a major province to barbarian pressure. The river, not the old conquered land beyond it, was now the line, and the Goths had pushed it there.

The great storm was over. Rome had survived, as it kept surviving, by a hair and at a cost. But the Goths who came out the other side of the third century were not the migrants who had reached the Black Sea a hundred years before. They were richer, more organized, militarily blooded against Roman armies, and now in possession of an entire abandoned Roman province. They were also, by the end of the century, starting to split into the two peoples who carry the rest of this book.

# Chapter 4 — Two Peoples, One Name

By the end of the third century there were two Goths, not one, and the split is the structural fact that organizes everything that follows, so it has to be set out carefully and with its uncertainties showing, because this is one of the places where the late sources and the modern scholarship are most at odds.

The two peoples are conventionally called the **Visigoths** and the **Ostrogoths**, and almost everyone has heard those names and almost no one has been told what they actually meant or when. Here is the careful version. By around 290 CE the Roman sources are describing two distinct large Gothic groupings on either side of the **Dniester** (pronounced "NEE-ster"), the river running south to the Black Sea through modern Moldova and Ukraine. The western group, between the Danube and the Dniester, the sources call the **Tervingi** (pronounced "TER-vin-ghee"). The eastern group, out on the steppe beyond the Dniester toward the Don, they call the **Greuthungi** (pronounced "GROY-thoong-ghee"). The Tervingi are the ancestors of what later writers called the Visigoths; the Greuthungi, of the Ostrogoths.

The familiar names need a flag, because they have a backstory that is itself a lesson in how history gets written. "Visigoth" and "Ostrogoth" are often glossed as "western Goths" and "eastern Goths," and that gloss is roughly right geographically and almost certainly wrong etymologically. The names appear to mean something more like "the good and noble Goths" and "the Goths of the rising sun," honorific labels rather than compass directions, and they were popularized in their tidy west/east form by Jordanes and Cassiodorus in the sixth century (Chapter 1), working for an Ostrogothic court that wanted a clean, dignified, ancient pedigree. The neat two-tribe scheme is partly a sixth-century retrojection imposed on a third- and fourth-century reality that was messier, with many shifting groups rather than two fixed nations. This book will use Tervingi and Greuthungi for the early period and Visigoth and Ostrogoth once the peoples themselves have hardened into those identities, and it will keep reminding the reader that the line was never as sharp as the names make it sound.

---

What was Gothic society actually like on the inside, in the century between the great raids and the Hunnic catastrophe? The honest answer leans heavily on archaeology and on scattered, hostile, or much-later sources, so the picture is real but soft at the edges. With that said, the surviving evidence supports a reasonably clear structure.

Gothic society had roughly three tiers. At the top was an aristocratic warrior class; below them, free farmers, the broad mass of the people, who worked their own land and fought when called; and beneath them, a dependent and enslaved population, much of it Roman captives taken in the raids and their descendants, doing the agricultural labor. This was not a society of equals and it was not nomadic. The Chernyakhov villages (Chapter 2) show it was overwhelmingly a settled farming people, with the warrior aristocracy sitting on top of a working agrarian economy, not a camp of horsemen.

The political structures of the two peoples diverged, and the divergence matters because it shapes how each of them dies and survives in the rest of the book. The Tervingi were led not by a king in any strong sense but by a council of nobles whose individual leaders were called **reiks** (pronounced "rakes," a Gothic word related to the Latin *rex* and the root in modern words like "Reich"), with, in times of crisis, a single paramount leader chosen from among them. The sources call that paramount Tervingi leader by the title of "judge," not "king," and that word choice is doing real work, not just decorating the page. A judge is a leader by consent and arbitration whose authority is bounded; a king claims to rule by right and by blood. The Tervingi system was deliberately the former: flexible, non-hereditary, a coalition of proud noble houses that would follow a war-leader but would not be owned by one. It is, almost exactly, the political pattern the previous book traced through the whole Germanic world, the confederation of equals that follows a winner but will not be ruled by one of its own ([[germanic-tribes]]).

The Greuthungi went the other way. Out on the steppe they developed a more centralized, more genuinely royal kingship, and it attached itself to a single ruling house, the **Amali** (pronounced "AH-mah-lee"), the dynasty that legitimized its power with a pedigree of heroic and semi-divine ancestors. That pedigree survives mainly through Jordanes (Chapter 1), and an official royal genealogy written down by a court historian is the single purest case of his core problem: it is a story built to flatter the family that commissioned it, so the deep generations of the Amali line should be read as dynastic propaganda, not record. The Amali line is the one that, generations later, produces Theodoric the Great, the Gothic king who ruled Italy from a marble palace (Chapter 10). The political seed of that was planted here, on the steppe, in the choice the Greuthungi made to be ruled by kings while the Tervingi chose to be led by judges.

---

The most powerful Greuthungi king of this era, and the one the sources make the most of, was **Ermanaric** (pronounced "ER-man-ar-ik"). According to the tradition, mostly Jordanes again, with all the caveats that name now carries, Ermanaric built an enormous steppe domain in the mid-fourth century, subjugating his neighbors one after another, the Heruli, the Slavic peoples Jordanes calls the Venethi, and Finnic and other groups toward the north and east, until he ruled, in the legend's telling, a Gothic empire stretching across much of the Pontic steppe. Jordanes compares him to Alexander the Great, which is exactly the kind of comparison a court historian inflating a founding dynasty would reach for, and modern scholars accordingly treat the size and unity of "Ermanaric's empire" with heavy skepticism: there was probably a real and powerful Greuthungi king named Ermanaric who dominated a real if looser network of subject peoples, and the rest is epic. What is not in doubt is that Ermanaric became a legend. The story that grew up around his death is gruesome enough to show why it was sung for a thousand years: in the heroic version, the old king has a woman of a subject people torn apart by horses for a supposed betrayal, and her brothers come for him in revenge and leave him crippled by sword-wounds, dying slowly as the Huns close in. Whether any of that happened is unrecoverable, but the tale itself, in recognizable form, surfaces centuries later and far from the steppe in Old Norse and Old English verse, which tells you something true even when the facts are gone: the Goths of this period were already generating the heroic stories that other Germanic peoples would still be singing in the Viking age ([[viking-age]], [[germanic-tribes]]).

Ermanaric's importance to this book is the manner of his fall, because it is the hinge of everything after. The tradition says that when an unstoppable new enemy came out of the eastern steppe and overwhelmed his domain, Ermanaric, old and unable to face it, died, by his own hand in some accounts, in battle in others, as his kingdom collapsed around him. The enemy was the Huns, and their arrival is the single most consequential event in this entire book. But before the Huns reach the Goths, one Goth has to do something that will outlast every kingdom in this story, every battle, and the Huns themselves. He has to give his people a Bible.

---

# Chapter 5 — The Bishop Who Built an Alphabet

In the middle of the fourth century, while the Goths were still pagan steppe-dwellers raiding and trading at the edge of the empire, one man invented an alphabet, used it to translate the Bible, and in doing so made the Goths the first Germanic people with a written literature and, for the next two centuries, a Christian people of the wrong kind. The consequences of that second part, the wrong kind, run through the rest of this book and out the far side of it, all the way to Spain. This is the most important chapter that does not contain a battle.

The man was **Ulfilas** (pronounced "UL-fih-las," also written Wulfila, which means "little wolf" in Gothic). The previous book pointed straight at him; this is where he arrives. We know more about him than about any earlier Goth, because he operated inside the Roman Christian world and that world wrote things down. He was born around 311 CE, a date that rests on a single later church historian, Philostorgius, and the strongest tradition about his background is that he descended from Roman Christian captives, the people taken in the great third-century raids (Chapter 3) and resettled among the Goths. That captive-descent reading is one scholarly reconstruction among a few, but it is the best-attested one. That detail is the mechanism, not a coincidence: Christianity first reached the Goths not by missionaries crossing the frontier but by Christian prisoners dragged across it, who kept their faith in captivity and passed it to their children and their captors' neighbors. The religion entered Gothic society from the bottom, through the unfree, decades before any bishop was involved.

Around 341 CE Ulfilas traveled to the Roman world and was consecrated bishop, with a formal church mandate to evangelize the Goths, by a churchman named **Eusebius of Nicomedia** (pronounced "yoo-SEE-bee-us"). That name is not decoration; it is the whole story in a single fact, and it requires a detour into the most consequential theological argument in the history of the late Roman Empire, because the Goths are about to be permanently marked by it.

---

The argument was about the precise nature of Christ, and a modern reader needs it laid out plainly, with no assumption of prior knowledge, because everything political in the back half of this book turns on it.

By the early fourth century Christianity had become a major and soon the favored religion of the Roman Empire. But Christians were ferociously divided over a question that sounds abstract and was treated as anything but: what exactly *was* Jesus Christ, in relation to God the Father? One camp, following a priest from Alexandria named **Arius** (pronounced "AIR-ee-us"), held that Christ the Son was created by God the Father, was subordinate to him, and was not co-eternal with him; there was, in the slogan of the position, "a time when the Son was not." This is **Arianism**. The opposing camp held that the Son was of the very same divine substance as the Father, equally eternal, equally God, not created at all. This is the position that won.

It won at a specific, datable, decisive event: the **Council of Nicaea** in 325 CE, the first great empire-wide council of the church, convened and presided over by the emperor Constantine himself, which condemned Arius's position as heresy and produced the creed (the Nicene Creed, still recited in churches today) that affirmed the Son was "of one substance with the Father" ([[ancient-rome]]). The reader should mark why an emperor cared about a theologians' quarrel, because the answer is the engine of two centuries of conflict: an empire whose unity now ran partly through a single shared church could not tolerate that church being split down the middle over its central claim. Religious division was political division. Nicaea was an attempt to make the empire's Christianity, and therefore the empire, one thing.

It did not take cleanly. For decades after Nicaea the argument raged on, emperors switched sides, councils contradicted councils, and for a long stretch in the mid-fourth century the imperial court itself leaned Arian. And this is the fact that detonates across the rest of the book: the man who consecrated Ulfilas, Eusebius of Nicomedia, was a leading figure of the Arian side, and the form of Christianity Ulfilas carried to the Goths was Arian Christianity. The timing was almost accidental; it happened to be the version in imperial favor when the Goths happened to be converting. But its consequences were not accidental at all.

---

Now the alphabet. The Arian question is the consequence that detonates across the rest of the book, but the alphabet is the part that is unambiguously, technically astonishing in its own right, and it is the payoff Chapter 1 promised and held back.

To translate the Bible into Gothic, Ulfilas needed to write Gothic down, and Gothic had no adequate script. The Germanic peoples had the runes, the angular **Elder Futhark** carved on weapons and brooches that the previous book described ([[germanic-tribes]]), and there were Gothic runic inscriptions, so the Goths were not strictly unlettered. But runes were a script for short charms, names, and maker's marks, not for copying out a long sacred text, and they carried pagan associations a Christian bishop wanted nowhere near scripture. So Ulfilas did something almost no one in history gets to do: he built a writing system on purpose, from scratch, for a specific job. The **Gothic alphabet** has somewhere around twenty-five to twenty-seven letters, depending on which contested characters scholars count, and Ulfilas assembled it like an engineer, taking most of the letterforms from the Greek alphabet he knew, borrowing a few from Latin, and reaching for a handful of runes for the Germanic sounds Greek and Latin had no letters for. It was not an evolution. It was a designed instrument, and the act of designing it simultaneously created Gothic literacy where there had been none.

With that instrument he produced the **Gothic Bible**, and here the distinction Chapter 1 deliberately withheld can finally be cashed in full. It is the earliest substantial text in any Germanic language, by a margin of roughly three centuries over the next-oldest. Sit with what that means concretely: the oldest long document in the entire family that includes English, German, Dutch, Frisian, Yiddish, and all the Scandinavian languages is not an English poem or a German law or a Norse saga but a fourth-century Gothic Gospel. The Beowulf manuscript is some six hundred years younger; the Norse Eddas, younger still ([[viking-age]]). And because Gothic was written down so early, before the family had drifted as far apart as it later would, it is the closest surviving window scholars have onto what early Germanic actually sounded like, which is why a heretic bishop's Bible became the cornerstone text of Germanic philology, the discipline that reconstructs the deep history of these languages. Ulfilas did not just give the Goths a book. He accidentally gave linguists their single best fossil of the common ancestor of every reader of this sentence's language.

There is a charming, much-repeated tradition that Ulfilas deliberately left the violent Books of Kings untranslated because his people were warlike enough without scriptural encouragement to fight. Like the Athens library story (Chapter 3), this is a good anecdote that the evidence does not actually secure, and this book flags it as the kind of too-perfect detail to enjoy but not to trust.

The translation did not vanish with the Goths. Its most famous surviving witness is the **Codex Argenteus** (pronounced "ar-JEN-tee-us," Latin for "the silver book"), a luxury manuscript of the Gothic Gospels produced more than a century after Ulfilas in Ostrogothic Italy (Chapter 10), and it is worth picturing rather than just naming. Its parchment was dyed deep imperial purple, and the entire text was written not in ink but in silver, with the opening lines and the holy names in gold, so that the book is a sheet of metal script glittering on a royal-purple ground, an object made to look as splendid and as legitimate as anything in a Catholic cathedral. It survives, battered and incomplete, today, and almost everything we have of Ulfilas's own Gothic words comes from it and a few other fragments. A barbarian translation that the Roman church had condemned as heretical is, by sheer accident of survival, one of the great treasures of European manuscript history.

---

So how did a people actually convert, as opposed to a bishop arriving with a book? The mechanism matters more than the date, and the rule of this series is to describe the process, not just announce the result.

It went in layers, over generations, and against resistance. The bottom layer was the Christian captives and their descendants, already there before Ulfilas. The next was Ulfilas's own mission, preaching and organizing a Gothic-language church among the Tervingi near the Danube. Then came the violent reaction, which is the part that proves the conversion was a real contested social process and not a tidy switch. The Tervingi paramount leader of the 360s and 370s was **Athanaric** (pronounced "ah-THAN-ar-ik"), who held the title of judge (Chapter 4), and Athanaric saw Gothic Christians, with their loyalty to a foreign god and their entanglement with the Roman enemy, as a political threat to Gothic independence. Around 370 he launched a persecution, and the way it was carried out is worth seeing, because it shows that this was a war over Gothic identity, not just belief. The sources describe an image of the ancestral Gothic gods, the old pagan powers Gothic communities had always sacrificed to, set on a wagon and wheeled from settlement to settlement like a loyalty test on the move. Each household was made to bow and offer sacrifice to it as it passed. Those who refused, the Christians, were marked out by that refusal in front of their own neighbors and killed for it. One account remembered for centuries afterward had a congregation of Gothic Christians burned alive inside the tent-church where they had gathered to worship, the building fired around them while they sang. The Gothic church got its martyrs, and a persecuted minority church with martyrs is a church that grows. The mechanism is not mysterious: public executions advertise the faith to everyone watching, they prove the believers think it worth dying for, and a religion that can produce people who will sing while they burn is far harder to stamp out than one nobody will suffer for. Persecution that does not utterly exterminate a faith tends to spread it. That is the mechanism. Christianity took hold among the Goths not as a royal decree from the top but from the bottom up, through captives, through preaching, and through the blood of Goths killed by other Goths for refusing to sacrifice.

By the time the Goths entered the empire as a people in the 370s, they were largely Christian, and crucially they were Arian Christian, exactly the kind the empire had condemned at Nicaea. Sit with the consequence, because it is one of the load-bearing facts of European history. When the Goths later ruled Romans, in Gaul, in Spain, in Italy, ruler and ruled would worship the same God, read the same scripture, and belong to bitterly opposed churches that each considered the other heretical. The Goths arrived as masters who were religiously foreign in a way no previous conqueror had been. That problem will help kill the Visigothic kingdom in Gaul, isolate the Ostrogothic kingdom in Italy, and not be resolved for more than two hundred years, when a Visigothic king in Spain finally converts his people to Catholicism long after this book has otherwise ended. That Spanish afterlife runs past the edge of this book and into a separate one ([[al-andalus]]); it is flagged here only so the reader knows the Arian problem outlives every kingdom in these chapters.

But all of that assumes the Goths get inside the empire at all. They were still on the steppe in 375, Christian and divided and prosperous, when the thing came out of the east that broke their world in a single year.

---

# Chapter 6 — The Year the World Ended

In 376 CE an entire people stood on the north bank of the Danube and begged the Roman Empire to let them in. They were not raiders and they were not an army on campaign. They were a nation in flight, men, women, children, the old, the sick, with whatever they could carry, pressed up against the river with something behind them they could not fight. What the empire did with that request, and how it went wrong, produced within two years the worst military catastrophe Rome had suffered in six centuries. This is the hinge of the entire book. Everything before it is approach. Everything after it is consequence.

The thing behind them were the **Huns**. The Huns get the fuller treatment in the wider story of the age ([[migration-period]]); what matters here is what they were to the Goths, which was the end of the world as the Goths had known it. They were mounted nomads who came out of the eastern steppe, the same grassland highway that had brought the Sarmatians (Chapter 2), now delivering something far worse. They came from so far east, beyond the deepest country any Roman geographer had mapped or named, that to the empire they seemed to have appeared out of nothing, a people with no place of origin anyone could point to. Their military edge was specific and is worth naming because it explains why the Goths could not simply fight them. The Huns fought from horseback with the composite reflex bow, a short, powerfully recurved bow built up of layered wood, horn, and sinew that packed the power of a far longer weapon into something a man could shoot from a galloping horse. A Gothic warrior carried a plain self-bow cut from a single stave, useful but no match for it, and a Gothic army was built to stand in an infantry line and to deliver one heavy noble cavalry charge. The Huns did neither thing the Goths were built to beat. They would not stand to be charged; they shot you to pieces from beyond reach, rode off the moment you advanced, swarmed back the instant you tired, and used the feigned retreat to pull a disciplined line into chaos. Against that, Gothic strength was close to useless, and the Goths knew it. The Greuthungi out on the open steppe took the blow first and were shattered; this is where Ermanaric's domain collapsed and Ermanaric died (Chapter 4). What reached the Tervingi to the west was not just news but survivors, people who had watched the eastern Gothic world come apart and could not say how to stop it, and it was that, the visible proof that the strongest Goths alive had been destroyed by something they could not fight, that drove the western Goths to a decision no Germanic people in this whole series had made before in quite this way: the only safe place left on earth was inside the Roman Empire, and they went to the river to beg for it.

---

The leader who led them to the Danube was **Fritigern** (pronounced "FRIT-ih-gern"), a Tervingi chieftain, and the most important Goth of the fourth century after Ulfilas. The reigning eastern emperor was **Valens** (pronounced "VAY-lenz"), who ruled the eastern half of the empire from Constantinople and who was, conveniently and fatally, an Arian Christian himself. Fritigern understood the religious chessboard and played it: he presented himself and his people as Arian Christians, the emperor's kind of Christians, a calculated diplomatic move that aligned the refugees with imperial sympathy and made Valens more inclined to say yes. The religious split that Chapter 5 set up is already, here, a working political instrument in Gothic hands.

And Valens said yes. From his point of view it even looked like a good deal. A whole people, settled on Roman land south of the Danube, would mean farmers paying taxes and, above all, a vast pool of military recruits, fighting men he badly needed for his other wars. This was the old logic of the *foederati*. A *foederati* arrangement meant admitting a barbarian group onto Roman soil under a formal treaty, a *foedus*, that bound them to supply soldiers in return for land and provisions. Rome had done it for small war-bands for generations ([[germanic-tribes]]); the new thing was scaling that bargain up from a war-band to an entire nation in a single stroke. In the autumn of 376 the Tervingi were ferried across the Danube into Roman territory. The numbers are unknowable, the sources say tens of thousands, possibly far more, and as always read the figure as "more people than the local Roman administration could possibly handle" rather than as a census.

That is exactly what went wrong, and the going-wrong is one of the most damning episodes in Roman administrative history, recorded by the Romans themselves. The local Roman commanders responsible for receiving, feeding, and resettling the refugees were two officials whose names the sources preserve in infamy, Lupicinus and Maximus, and they treated the catastrophe as a business opportunity. The Goths, disarmed in theory and starving in fact, were not fed. Roman officials sold them food at extortionate prices, and when the Goths ran out of money and possessions, the price became their children, sold into slavery to buy grain. One detail the sources give, and it is the one nobody forgets, is that the Romans sold the starving Goths dog meat, at a rate of one dog per Gothic child taken as a slave. Whether every specific is literally accurate or sharpened in the retelling, the broad picture, deliberate, profiteering cruelty toward a desperate people the empire had formally agreed to protect, is what the Roman sources themselves report, which is the strongest possible form of the claim: this is the conquerors' own confession, not the victims' complaint.

---

Our source for nearly all of this needs introducing properly, because the quality of the witness is unusually high and the reader should know it. He is **Ammianus Marcellinus** (pronounced "am-ee-AH-nus mar-sell-EYE-nus"), a Greek-speaking former Roman army officer who wrote a long history of the empire in Latin in the late fourth century, within living memory of these events and partly from his own military experience. He is, by a wide consensus, the best and most reliable narrative historian of the late empire, the nearest thing this period has to a clear-eyed eyewitness rather than a flatterer or an abridger. When Ammianus, a Roman patriot and old soldier, lays the blame for what came next squarely on Roman greed and Roman incompetence, that judgment carries weight precisely because it costs his own side.

What came next was inevitable. A starving, cheated, armed nation, and they were still effectively armed, the disarming had been bungled along with everything else, does not stay quiet. The breaking point came when Lupicinus invited Fritigern and the other Gothic leaders to a banquet and attempted to have them murdered there, a treacherous feast meant to decapitate the Gothic leadership at a stroke. It failed. Fritigern talked, or fought, his way out and escaped back to his people, and the revolt that the Roman officials' cruelty had been building exploded across the province of Thrace. The Tervingi rose, the surviving Greuthungi who had also crossed joined them, and they were swelled by escaped Gothic slaves and by gangs from the Thracian mines, where Goths sold into bondage had been worked underground, all of them with nothing to lose, and what had been a refugee crisis became a war inside the empire, fought by a people with nothing left to lose, on Roman soil, against a Rome that had created the catastrophe itself.

For two years Fritigern's Goths burned and plundered their way through the Balkans while local Roman forces failed to contain them. The emperor Valens, away on the eastern frontier, gathered his field army and marched west to deal with the problem personally. He was confident. He had a real Roman field army and the Goths were, in his mind, a rabble of refugees. He marched it toward a Thracian city called Adrianople in the high summer of 378, and what happened outside its walls is the reason this book exists.

---

# Chapter 7 — Adrianople

On the ninth of August, 378 CE, outside the city of **Adrianople** (pronounced "ay-dree-an-OH-pul," modern Edirne in European Turkey), a Roman emperor at the head of a Roman field army was destroyed by the Goths. It was not supposed to be possible. Rome lost battles all the time, lost whole armies, lost three legions in a German forest within living cultural memory, but it did not lose a reigning emperor and his field army together to a barbarian people in a pitched battle and then prove unable to come back from it. That had simply never been the kind of thing that happened to Rome. Adrianople made it the kind of thing that happened to Rome, and the empire never fully put the pattern back together.

The setup was a sequence of bad decisions, and the reader should track them because the disaster was not bad luck, it was a series of choices. Valens (Chapter 6) had a sound plan: wait at Adrianople for his nephew, the western emperor Gratian, who was marching east with the western field army to join him. Two emperors and two armies against the Goths was a near-certain win. But Valens had reasons to want the victory for himself. His nephew Gratian had recently won a glittering victory of his own on the Rhine, and a court full of officers who resented sharing the credit with the western army pushed Valens to settle the Goths before Gratian arrived and the glory had to be split. His scouts badly underestimated the size of the Gothic force, and the chance to win it all himself was dangled in front of him. He took it. On a brutally hot August day he marched his men out from the city, in their armor, without water, several miles to the Gothic position, and engaged them tired, thirsty, and alone.

There is one more decision, and it belongs to Fritigern, and it is the kind of detail that shows the Goths were not the rabble Valens thought. Fritigern's Tervingi infantry were drawn up in a defensive ring around their wagons, the laager, the circled-wagon camp that protected the noncombatants and gave the foot soldiers something to anchor on. But the Greuthungi cavalry, the heavy lance-armed horsemen of the eastern Goths (the steppe inheritance of Chapter 2), were not there yet; they were out foraging. Fritigern played for time, sending envoys, dragging out negotiations, while a Roman army stood in the sun, because every hour he stalled was an hour closer to his cavalry returning. It is the same patience Cniva had used at Abritus and the same the Goths kept showing: they were not a horde, they were soldiers who managed a battlefield.

---

The battle itself was short and total. Fighting broke out before Valens was ready, while envoys were still going back and forth, and the Roman line went in against the Gothic wagon-ring in the heat of the late afternoon. Then the thing Fritigern had been stalling for happened. The Greuthungi heavy cavalry, thousands of armored lancers, came back from foraging at exactly the wrong moment for Rome, crested the ground behind the fight, took it in at a glance, and drove straight down into the exposed flank of an army that was already fully committed forward and could not turn to face them. That is the hinge of the hinge. The Roman infantry, pinned against the wagons in front and smashed by horsemen on the side, was crushed inward on itself, packed so tight in the press, the sources say, that men could not raise their weapons, could not swing a sword, could not even fall down when they were killed. It became a slaughter rather than a battle. The eastern Roman field army, the main military force of the entire eastern empire, was annihilated in an afternoon. Ammianus, our reliable old soldier (Chapter 6), writes that barely a third of the army escaped, and he explicitly names the defeat as a disaster comparable to Cannae, the worst in Roman history. The often-quoted figure that "two-thirds of the army died" is a modern paraphrase of his account rather than a number he tallies; what he actually conveys is annihilation on the Cannae scale, and on that the sources do not disagree.

That comparison is the one to dwell on, and it should be made carefully, the way this book makes every superlative. Roman writers, Ammianus among them, explicitly reached for **Cannae** to describe Adrianople. Cannae was the battle in 216 BCE in which Hannibal of Carthage encircled and destroyed the largest army Rome had ever fielded, killing tens of thousands in a single day, the deepest military wound in the Republic's history ([[ancient-rome]], [[carthage]]). The Adrianople comparison is doing precise work: not "a bad defeat" but "the worst kind of defeat there is, the kind we name our catastrophes after." And the comparison also reveals the deeper change, the thing that makes Adrianople arguably worse than Cannae for what it meant. After Cannae, Rome refused to negotiate, raised new armies, fought on for fourteen years, and won total victory. After Adrianople, Rome could not do that. The manpower was not there, the army that died at Adrianople could not simply be replaced, and the empire's answer was not reconquest but accommodation. Cannae was a wound Rome survived by being Rome. Adrianople was a wound Rome survived by ceasing, in a structural way, to be the Rome that had survived Cannae.

And the emperor died. **Valens** was killed on the field, and the manner of his death became one of the haunting images of the age precisely because it was so undignified and so uncertain. He simply disappeared in the rout; his body was never found. The story that hardened in the tradition is that wounded Valens was carried into a peasant farmhouse, that the Goths, not knowing the emperor was inside, set the building alight to burn out the defenders, and that the master of the Roman world burned to death anonymously in a barn, unrecognized and unburied. The honesty rule requires saying that this is the dramatic version and the truth is simply unknown; what is certain is only that the emperor went into the battle and never came out, his corpse lost among the thousands. He was the first reigning Roman emperor to die at the hands of a foreign enemy since Decius at Abritus a hundred and twenty-seven years before (Chapter 3), and the symmetry was felt at the time: as far as the record runs, no other foreign people had brought down even one sitting emperor in the field, and the Goths had now done it twice.

---

What Rome did next is the truly decisive part, more decisive than the battle, because the battle could in principle have been avenged and was not. The empire could not raise another field army to destroy the Goths, so it made a deal with them instead, and the deal changed what the Roman Empire was.

The new eastern emperor was **Theodosius I** (pronounced "thee-oh-DOH-shus"), a capable general handed a broken army and an unbeatable enemy inside the borders. He spent years not defeating the Goths but containing them, recruiting hard, including recruiting Goths to fight, accelerating the process the previous book traced of an army filling up with the very people it was fighting. And in 382 CE he concluded the settlement that ends this phase of the story and opens the next: the **foedus of 382**, the treaty that let the Goths stay.

The terms were genuinely without precedent, and the precise novelty is the point, so it has to be stated exactly. Earlier barbarian groups had been settled inside the empire before, going back to Marcus Aurelius and Claudius Gothicus (Chapter 3); that part was not new. What was new was that the Goths were settled inside the empire as a *people*, with their own leaders, fighting in their own units under their own commanders, on land collectively granted them in the Balkans, and not broken up, not distributed as individual recruits, not absorbed. They were an armed, organized, semi-autonomous nation living permanently inside the Roman frontier, owing military service but governing themselves. No barbarian people had ever lived inside the empire on those terms. The frontier the entire previous book had been about, the line between Roman and barbarian, did not get crossed and pushed back this time. It got dissolved, by treaty, with an armed nation now sitting on the Roman side of it by right.

Theodosius held it together while he lived, partly by using the Goths hard. In 394 CE he marched west to put down a usurper, Eugenius, a Roman official propped up on the western throne by a general named Arbogast, in a civil war that was also, in the religious temper of the age, framed as a clash between Theodosius's Christianity and a last gasp of the old pagan order. At the **Battle of the Frigidus**, a river crossing in the eastern Alps, he threw his Gothic federate troops into the front of the line as shock infantry on the brutal first day, where roughly half of them are said to have been killed while his regular Roman troops stood behind them and watched. The Goths noticed. They had been admitted to the empire to be saved and were instead being spent to win an emperor's civil war, sent to die first so Romans would not have to, and the resentment of that day fed straight into what happened the moment Theodosius died. He died in 395 CE, the last man to rule the whole Roman Empire as one, and at his death the empire split permanently between his two young, weak sons, east and west. The Goths inside the empire, unpaid, leaderless, freshly bled at the Frigidus, and now with no strong emperor left to fear, did the obvious thing. They chose a new leader of their own and went looking, in arms, for what the empire had promised them and never paid. His name was Alaric.

# Chapter 8 — The Unthinkable

In 410 CE the city of Rome was sacked. The sentence is short and the reader who has just arrived may not feel the weight of it, so this chapter has to do something the others have not needed to do as urgently: explain, before anything else, why this one event sent a shock through the entire ancient world that people were still writing about, arguing about, and building theology around for the next thousand years.

Rome had not fallen to a foreign enemy in eight hundred years. Not since a Gaulish war-band had taken the city around 390 BCE, when Rome was still a small central-Italian city-state centuries before it ruled an empire, had a foreign army entered Rome as a conqueror ([[ancient-rome]], [[celtic-cultures]]). Eight centuries. In that span Rome had been the center of the world, the city that conquered everywhere and was never itself taken, that had survived Hannibal at the gates after Cannae without the city falling, that had grown from a town to the capital of an empire spanning from Britain to the Euphrates. "Rome is eternal" was not a slogan; for eight hundred years it had been a simple description of observed reality. To the people of 410, Rome being sacked was not like a military defeat. It was like being told the sun had been put out. The contemporary Christian writer Jerome, translating scripture in his cell in Bethlehem, wrote in a letter that the news struck him dumb, that for a long while he could not speak for grief, and produced the line that has echoed ever since: "the city which had taken the whole world was itself taken." The image of his voice failing is the sense of his letter rather than a phrase he wrote; the captured-city line is verbatim his. That is the emotional scale. Now the story of how a Goth did it.

---

The Goth was **Alaric** (pronounced "AL-a-rik"), and he is one of the most misunderstood figures in this whole series, so it is worth getting him right. He was not a wild barbarian who hated Rome and wanted to destroy it. He was a product of the *foedus* (Chapter 7), a Gothic noble who had grown up inside the Roman system, fought in Rome's wars, including bleeding at the Frigidus in 394, and whose entire career was a long, increasingly furious attempt to extract from the Roman state the thing it had promised his people and kept not delivering: secure land, regular supplies, and a recognized, paid, honorable place inside the empire. Around 395, in the chaos after Theodosius's death, the Goths in the Balkans raised Alaric as their king, uniting the federate Goths into a single mobile people-in-arms under one leader. For the next fifteen years he was less an invader than a man with an army the size of a nation, marching it around the Mediterranean world trying to be paid.

His chief opponent and occasional partner for most of that time embodies the era's central irony. He was **Stilicho** (pronounced "STIL-ih-koh"), the supreme military commander of the western empire and the man effectively running it for the boy-emperor Honorius, and Stilicho was himself of barbarian descent, the son of a Vandal father. The defense of Rome against the Goths was led by a half-Vandal general; the empire was now so thoroughly run by the people it had spent four centuries fighting that the distinction between Roman and barbarian had become, at the top, almost meaningless. Stilicho repeatedly out-generaled Alaric, beat him at the **Battle of Pollentia** in 402, capturing Alaric's wife along with the Gothic baggage train, the wagons that carried the families, plunder, and supplies of an entire people on the move, on Easter Sunday, and forced him out of Italy more than once, but he never destroyed him, partly because Stilicho wanted Alaric's army intact as a tool for his own political schemes. The two men circled each other for years, sometimes at war, sometimes negotiating, Alaric at one point even being given a legitimate Roman command in **Illyricum** (the strategic Balkan region along the Adriatic's eastern hinterland, the contested seam between the eastern and western halves of the empire), an official posting that gave his army the legal right to draw weapons from the state arms factories that supplied Roman troops. The line between the empire's enemy and the empire's general was that blurred.

Then the western court destroyed its own defense. In 408 CE the emperor Honorius, weak, frightened, and listening to courtiers who hated the powerful half-Vandal general, had **Stilicho arrested and executed** on a charge of treason. It was a catastrophic act of self-harm. The one general who had repeatedly out-fought Alaric was dead, the army was purged of his supporters, and in the aftermath Roman soldiers massacred the families of the barbarian federate troops, driving tens of thousands of those soldiers straight into Alaric's army. Honorius had simultaneously removed the only man who could stop Alaric and handed Alaric a flood of new recruits, then retreated behind the marshes of **Ravenna**, the swamp-protected city on the Adriatic coast that the western court had made its capital precisely because it was hard to reach, and refused to negotiate seriously.

---

What followed was not one sack but a slow, three-part strangulation, and the sequence matters because it shows Alaric trying, almost to the last, *not* to do it. He marched on Rome and besieged it in 408, cutting the grain supply until the city, which had not faced this in living memory, was reduced to starvation and reportedly to talk of cannibalism, then lifted the siege in exchange for an enormous ransom. The Greek historian Zosimus, writing about a century later, gives the figure as gold, silver, silk, dyed hides, and three thousand pounds of pepper, an oddly specific commodity that has become the famous detail of the whole episode. The exact contents vary between the surviving accounts and the pepper in particular may owe as much to legend as to any audited tally, but whatever the precise list, the point it makes is solid: even besieged and starving, Rome could still produce a king's ransom in luxury goods, including a spice that had travelled from India. He besieged it again in 409, this time installing a puppet emperor of his own to pressure Honorius into a deal. Through all of it Alaric's demands were strikingly modest: land for his people and the rank of a senior Roman general for himself. He kept asking the empire to make him legitimate. The empire, safe in its swamp, kept refusing.

In August 410 CE, after the negotiations finally and definitively collapsed, the **Sack of Rome** happened. The gate was opened, by treachery or by exhaustion, the sources are not certain, and for three days the Goths plundered the city. By the brutal standards of ancient sacks it was restrained, which is itself historically telling and should not be sanded into a Hollywood image. Alaric was an Arian Christian (Chapter 5), his men were Christians, the great basilicas of Saint Peter and Saint Paul were respected as places of sanctuary, and there was no general massacre of the population. It was looting, not annihilation. Rome was not destroyed; it was robbed and humiliated. But the physical damage was beside the point. The point was that it had happened at all, that the unsackable city had been sacked, by a people who eight hundred years of Romans had been raised to think of as the definition of the inferior outsider.

The intellectual shockwave produced one of the most important books in Western history, and it has to be named here because the event is incomplete without it. Pagans across the empire said the obvious thing: Rome fell because it had abandoned its ancient gods for Christianity, and the old gods had withdrawn their protection. In direct answer to that charge, the North African bishop **Augustine of Hippo**, the most influential Christian thinker of the entire era, began writing **The City of God**, a vast work arguing that no earthly city, not even Rome, was ever eternal or the true home of a Christian, that the only enduring city was the City of God, and that the fall of a mere human empire proved nothing against the faith. It became the foundational political theology of the entire Middle Ages, a framework for thinking about church, state, and the meaning of history that shaped Europe for a thousand years. A Gothic sack of Rome produced the book that taught medieval Europe how to think about Rome's fall.

---

Alaric did not enjoy his triumph. Within months of the sack he was dead, of fever, in southern Italy, while leading his people toward the grain ships of Africa, having achieved the thing no one had achieved in eight centuries and gained from it nothing he had actually wanted, no land, no title, no settlement. His burial became the most famous legend of the age, and this book treats it as a legend and says so. The story, from Jordanes (Chapter 1), with all the warning that name carries, is that his men diverted the course of the river **Busento** near Cosentia, buried Alaric and a great treasure in the dry riverbed, let the river flow back over the grave, and then killed the slaves who had done the work so no one would ever know where the conqueror of Rome lay. It is a perfect story, which is exactly why it should be held at arm's length; it is the kind of too-symmetrical tale that grows around great deaths, and no grave has ever been found.

What came next mattered more than the sack, and it set the direction for the rest of the book. Alaric's successor was his brother-in-law **Athaulf** (pronounced "AH-tah-wulf"), and Athaulf articulated something genuinely new in a remark the sources preserve: he said he had once dreamed of replacing the Roman world with a Gothic one, "Gothia" where "Romania" had been, but had concluded that the Goths could never make a functioning state without Roman law and order, and that his ambition now was not to destroy Rome but to use Gothic arms to restore it. He married a Roman princess, **Galla Placidia**, the captured sister of the emperor himself, at Narbonne in 414 in a full Roman-style ceremony, a Gothic king and a Roman imperial princess, an image of the ambiguous fused world that was being born. And he led his people out of Italy and into southern Gaul (modern France), looking for the thing Alaric had died still wanting: somewhere to actually settle and stay. They were about to get it, and in getting it they built the first lasting barbarian kingdom on Roman soil.

---

# Chapter 9 — The Kingdom in the West

In 418 CE the Roman government formally granted the Visigoths land to settle and govern in **Aquitaine** (pronounced "ACK-wih-tane"), the rich region of southwestern Gaul around the Garonne valley, with the city of **Toulouse** (pronounced "too-LOOZE") as their seat. With that grant the Goths stopped being a people in motion and became a kingdom with an address, and a thing happened that the previous four hundred years of this series had been building toward without ever quite delivering: a Germanic people set up a permanent, functioning state inside the western Roman world and ran it. The **Kingdom of Toulouse** is the first enduring barbarian successor kingdom in the West, and the way it worked is the template for the medieval Europe that follows it.

The mechanism of the settlement deserves attention because it was not a conquest in the burn-it-down sense, and the difference is the whole character of the new order. The Visigoths were settled under a Roman system called **hospitalitas** (pronounced "hoss-pih-TAL-ih-tas," literally "hospitality"), the rules by which Roman soldiers had long been billeted on civilian landowners. Adapted for the Goths, it meant, in theory, that Roman estate-owners surrendered a defined share of their land or its revenue to support a settled Gothic warrior, who became their armed neighbor rather than their destroyer. Scholars argue, genuinely and unresolved, about whether what changed hands was the land itself or just the tax revenue from it, and this book flags that as an open question rather than picking a side. But the shape is clear and it is important: the Roman landowning class of Aquitaine was not exterminated or dispossessed wholesale. It kept its estates, its Latin, its villas, and its place. A Gallo-Roman aristocrat went on collecting rents, going to law before courts that still used Roman procedure, writing literary letters to other aristocrats, and serving in the kingdom's administration, only now the man with the soldiers down the road and the king above him both happened to be Gothic. Gothic warriors and Roman senators lived as armed neighbors rather than as conquerors and conquered, the Roman administrative and legal furniture kept running, and the kingdom was, from the start, a Gothic-Roman hybrid rather than a barbarian replacement.

It was also, from the start, religiously divided in exactly the way Chapter 5 promised. The Visigoths were Arian Christians ruling a population of Catholic Gallo-Romans. Both worshipped Christ; each church considered the other heretical; and that fault line ran underneath the whole kingdom, a permanent structural weakness that the Goths' enemies would eventually learn to exploit as a weapon.

---

The kingdom's defining military moment came in 451 CE, and it is one of the strangest alliances in this entire series. **Attila** (pronounced "AT-il-uh"), king of the Huns, the same people whose arrival had shattered the Gothic world in 375 and driven the Goths into the empire in the first place (Chapter 6), invaded Gaul at the head of a vast multiethnic army. The man who organized the defense was the last great Roman general of the West, **Aetius** (pronounced "ay-EE-shus"), and the core of the army he assembled to stop Attila was the Visigothic kingdom of Toulouse. Refugees from the Huns, three generations on, now stood as the main barbarian pillar of the Roman defense *against* the Huns. They met Attila at the **Battle of the Catalaunian Plains** (pronounced "kat-a-LAW-nee-an," somewhere in the Champagne region of eastern France; the exact site is unknown), one of the largest and most consequential battles of the age, fought between two coalitions each more barbarian than Roman. Attila was checked and forced to withdraw, which is as close to a defeat as the Huns suffered in the West. The price for the Goths was their king. **Theodoric I** (a Visigothic king in Gaul, not to be confused with the later Ostrogothic Theodoric the Great of Chapter 10, a different man in a different country) had by 451 ruled the Visigoths for more than three decades, the long reign that turned the Aquitaine settlement into a genuine independent power with Toulouse as a real capital and a real army. He died on the field at the Catalaunian Plains, killed in the fighting against Attila, and his son **Thorismund** was acclaimed king on the battlefield amid the dead. The fuller story of Attila and the Huns belongs to the wider convulsion of the age ([[migration-period]]); what it means here is that the Visigothic kingdom was now strong enough, and Roman enough, to be the thing that saved Roman Gaul.

---

By the 460s and 470s the Visigothic kingdom had stopped pretending to be a Roman client and become an independent power, and the king who completed that transformation was **Euric** (pronounced "YOO-rik"), who took the throne in 466 by murdering his brother Theodoric II, who had himself taken it by murdering their brother Thorismund, the new king acclaimed at the Catalaunian Plains. Violent succession was less an aberration than the Gothic norm: with kingship non-hereditary in any fixed sense and the throne going to whoever could hold it, killing the incumbent was a recognized, almost routine, route to it, and this book will mostly stop remarking on it. Euric did two things that define the kingdom's maturity. First, he conquered, expanding aggressively until the Visigoths controlled most of southern Gaul and a large part of **Hispania** (pronounced "his-PAH-nee-ah," the Iberian Peninsula, modern Spain and Portugal), at exactly the moment the western empire was visibly dying around him, in the 470s. Second, and more lasting, around 475 he issued the **Code of Euric**, a written law code for his kingdom.

The Code of Euric deserves its weight stated precisely. It is one of the earliest written law codes produced by any Germanic people, and its character is the whole point of this chapter: it was not a code of pure Gothic tribal custom. It was Gothic law written down in Latin, in Roman legal form, blending Germanic practice with Roman jurisprudence, issued by a barbarian king governing a mixed population through written, recognized, Roman-style law. Compare this to where the previous book left the Germanic world: a society that had possessed the runes for centuries and pointedly never used writing for law, administration, or records, because it ran on the spoken oath and the assembly and did not have a state ([[germanic-tribes]]). Two generations after Adrianople, a Gothic king is issuing a Latin law code. That is the measure of the transformation this whole series has been tracking. The Goths did not just inherit the Roman West; they took up its single most characteristic instrument, written codified law, and made it theirs. Euric's grandson Alaric II went further still, issuing in 506 the **Breviary of Alaric**, a systematic digest of *Roman* law compiled for the kingdom's Roman subjects, a barbarian king publishing an authoritative edition of Roman law for the Romans he ruled.

---

The Toulouse kingdom ended where Gothic kingdoms tended to end, on a battlefield, and the way it ended carried the religious fault line to its logical conclusion. To the north a new power had risen: the **Franks**, the Germanic confederation the previous book introduced as forming on the lower Rhine ([[germanic-tribes]]), now united and expanding under their king **Clovis** (pronounced "KLOH-vis"). Clovis had done the thing the Visigoths had not: he and his Franks had converted directly to *Catholic* Christianity, not Arianism. The difference came down to timing and contact. The Goths had been converted in the mid-fourth century, when the imperial court itself leaned Arian and the missionaries who reached them carried Arian Christianity (Chapter 5); the Franks were still pagan a century and a half later, and when Clovis converted around 500 the form on offer from the Catholic Gallo-Roman bishops around him, in a now firmly Catholic empire, was Catholic. The same Roman-barbarian contact produced opposite churches because the two peoples plugged into it at opposite phases of the Arian controversy. That accident made Clovis, in the eyes of the Catholic Gallo-Roman population and the Catholic church, the orthodox champion against the heretic Goths, and Clovis used it deliberately, framing his war on the Visigoths as something close to a holy war and counting on the Catholic subjects of the Gothic kingdom to prefer a Catholic conqueror. In 507 CE, at the **Battle of Vouillé** (pronounced "voo-YAY," near Poitiers in western France), the Franks crushed the Visigoths and the Visigothic king Alaric II was killed in the fighting. A later Frankish tradition, recorded by the bishop Gregory of Tours roughly seventy years afterward, has Clovis striking him down in person; most historians treat the personal duel as the kind of heroic flourish that grows around a founding king and accept only that Alaric II died at Vouillé. The kingdom of Toulouse collapsed. The Visigoths were driven out of almost all of Gaul, keeping only a coastal strip around Narbonne called Septimania, and the heretical-conqueror problem that Chapter 5 had predicted had now, precisely as predicted, helped destroy a Gothic kingdom.

But the Visigoths did not end. They retreated across the Pyrenees and rebuilt, re-centering the kingdom on the Iberian Peninsula with its capital eventually at **Toledo** (pronounced "toh-LAY-doh," in central Spain). The Visigothic Kingdom of Toledo would rule most of Spain for another two centuries after this book otherwise closes, and its long afterlife, including the eventual conversion of the whole Gothic people away from Arianism and the kingdom's destruction by an Arab and Berber army in 711, runs on into a separate book ([[al-andalus]]); this one only points the way. A people that had crossed the Danube as starving refugees a century and a quarter earlier now crossed the Pyrenees as a kingdom in retreat but intact, carrying its king, its law code, and its heretic church south into Spain, where it would outlast the western empire itself. That is the western Goths settled. The eastern Goths were meanwhile walking into the actual heart of the old empire, Italy, to try something stranger than building a kingdom on its edge: running Rome itself.

---

# Chapter 10 — Theodoric's Italy

Between roughly 493 and 526 CE, the Italian peninsula, the literal heartland of the Roman Empire, was ruled by a Gothic king who had been raised as a hostage in Constantinople, who governed through the Roman Senate, restored Roman aqueducts, sponsored Roman philosophy, and built himself a tomb that still stands. His reign is the strangest and, in some ways, the most poignant thing in this entire book, because for thirty years it looked as though the answer to four hundred years of Roman-barbarian collision might be neither conquest nor collapse but fusion, a Gothic-Roman Italy that worked, and then it did not, and the failure is the door into the catastrophe of the final chapter.

To get there the West has to formally end, and it does, almost as a footnote. By the 470s the western Roman Empire was a hollow shell, a series of boy-emperors controlled by barbarian generals while the actual provinces were run by barbarian kingdoms. In 476 CE a Germanic commander of the Italian army named **Odoacer** (pronounced "OH-doh-AY-ser") deposed the last of these figurehead western emperors, a boy named, with almost unbearable historical irony, Romulus Augustulus, "little Augustus," combining the name of Rome's founder and its first emperor in a child puppet. Odoacer did not appoint a replacement. He sent the imperial regalia to Constantinople, declared he would rule Italy as king under the nominal sovereignty of the eastern emperor, and that was that. This date, 476, is the one traditionally called "the fall of the Western Roman Empire," and the honesty rule means saying clearly that historians argue endlessly about whether it deserves the weight, because no one at the time experienced it as the world ending; it was the quiet formalization of something that had already happened. Odoacer then ruled Italy for over a decade, and ruled it well, which matters because it is what Theodoric would inherit. He kept the Senate sitting, the law courts open, the tax system and grain dole functioning, and the whole Roman civil administration staffed by Romans, governing as a king who left the machinery of Roman Italy essentially intact and simply ran it. By 489 the peninsula Theodoric marched into was not a wrecked country but a working one, a going concern in Roman administrative order, and that inheritance is half the reason his own reign could become what it did. The empire in the West did not fall in a day. It was administered, by barbarians, into a different thing.

---

The Goth who replaced Odoacer was **Theodoric the Great** (pronounced "thee-OH-dor-ik"), of the Amali royal house of the Ostrogoths (the Greuthungi dynasty of Chapter 4), and his life is the perfect distillation of everything this book is about. As a boy he had been sent to Constantinople as a hostage, a guarantee of his people's good behavior, and he grew up there, in the greatest Roman city in the world, absorbing Roman government, Roman culture, and Roman political thinking. He was a Gothic king raised inside the Roman state, the same paradox as Alaric and Stilicho, but taken further than either: Alaric had grown up in Roman armies and Stilicho served the empire, yet neither had been schooled from boyhood inside its imperial capital, and neither ended up actually governing Roman territory through the Roman Senate. Theodoric did both. He is the paradox not just embodied but enthroned.

How he got Italy is itself a study in how the post-Roman world worked. The eastern emperor in Constantinople, **Zeno**, had a problem: Theodoric and his Ostrogoths were a dangerous, restless armed nation loose in the Balkans, too strong to destroy and too disruptive to ignore. Zeno's solution, around 488, was elegant and cynical: he commissioned Theodoric to invade Italy and overthrow Odoacer, in the emperor's name. This solved two problems at once. It removed the dangerous Goths from the Balkans by pointing them at Italy, and it replaced Odoacer, a ruler Constantinople had never properly recognized, with one who at least notionally held his authority from the emperor. Theodoric marched an entire people across the Alps into Italy, beat Odoacer in a string of battles, at the Isonzo river and at Verona in 489 and again at the Adda in 490, and then ran into the obstacle every conqueror of Italy in this period ran into: **Ravenna**, the swamp-girdled capital, effectively unstormable. Theodoric besieged it for three years. It fell only by negotiation, not assault, in 493, with an agreement that the two men would rule Italy jointly.

That agreement lasted about ten days. At a banquet held to seal the peace, Theodoric personally killed Odoacer with his own sword. Jordanes (Chapter 1), writing decades later and never one to waste a good scene, adds a grim warrior's joke, that surveying the body Theodoric remarked the man "had no bones in him," that the blow had cut clean through. That line is exactly the kind of too-perfect anecdote a court-flattering source invents, and its historicity is essentially nil; the murder, however, is not in doubt. It was treachery, plain and unflinching, and this book does not dress it up; it is the founding act of Ostrogothic Italy, and it tells you that the philosopher-king's regime began, like every Gothic kingdom in these pages, with a murder.

---

What Theodoric then built over the next three decades is the reason he is called "the Great," and it is genuinely extraordinary. He ran Italy as a deliberate dual state. The Goths were the army, holding military command and the profession of arms; the Romans ran everything civil, the Senate, the law courts, the tax administration, the bureaucracy, almost entirely unchanged and staffed by Romans. The two peoples were kept legally and religiously separate, Arian Goths and Catholic Romans, soldiers and civilians, but yoked into one functioning kingdom under a king who presented himself, carefully, as a restorer of Roman civilization rather than its conqueror. He issued a law code, the **Edictum Theodorici**, applying to Goths and Romans alike to reduce friction between them. The great Roman senatorial families, the Anicii and the Symmachi, the old aristocratic dynasties whose wealth, landholdings, and inherited offices had run Rome's civil life for generations, served as his consuls and prefects and found, to their own evident surprise, that the Gothic regime was a stable and reasonably generous patron of the traditional Roman order. Cassiodorus's letters (the same Cassiodorus whose lost Gothic history Jordanes abridged, Chapter 1) record the regime ordering the maintenance of the great roads like the Via Appia, the repair of aqueducts, the restoration of public baths, the management of the grain supply, the protection of Rome's ancient monuments. This was a barbarian king treating the upkeep of Roman civilization as a central duty of government and saying so loudly, framing it as proof that the Goths were carriers of civilization, not its destroyers. It is the most thorough refutation in this whole series of the word "barbarian."

The physical proof still stands at Ravenna, and architecture in this world was always purposeful, so the why matters as much as the what. Theodoric rebuilt the royal palace, and he raised two structures that survive. One is the basilica of **Sant'Apollinare Nuovo** (pronounced "sant ah-pol-ee-NAH-reh"), built as his palace church, its walls lined with mosaics of a quality that puts it among the finest buildings of the early medieval world; it was an Arian church, built to proclaim that the Gothic king's faith was as splendid and as legitimate as the Romans' own. The other is his own **Mausoleum**, a massive circular tomb of pale stone whose most astonishing feature is its roof: a single colossal slab of limestone, roughly 33 feet (10 meters) across and weighing on the order of 230 tons, set in place as one piece, a dome that is one monolithic rock rather than masonry. Where the slab was quarried is debated, with limestone from the Istrian coast across the Adriatic the leading candidate and a closer Apennine source also argued; how a stone that size was moved and lifted at all is the part nobody has fully explained. The choice was a statement. A Roman dome is mortared masonry, the empire's signature technology; Theodoric's is a single Gothic boulder doing the same job by sheer scale, a barbarian king's monument that borrows the Roman form and announces, in stone, that it is something else. It still stands outside Ravenna, the only thing in this entire book the reader could go and touch.

---

The intellectual life of Theodoric's Italy produced one book that outlasted the kingdom by a thousand years, and the way it was produced is the dark turn that ends the reign. The finest scholar at Theodoric's court was **Boethius** (pronounced "boh-EE-thee-us"), a Roman aristocrat from the Anicii who had already translated parts of Aristotle's logic and written textbooks on arithmetic and music, and who had set himself a larger project: to render all of Plato and Aristotle into Latin and reconcile them, a one-man rescue mission to keep Greek philosophy alive in a West that was losing the ability to read Greek at all. He served Theodoric as head of the civil administration. And then it turned. When a senator was accused of treasonous correspondence with Constantinople and Boethius spoke up to defend him, the accusation swung onto Boethius himself: that he too had been plotting to bring back imperial rule. The charge fell into Theodoric's last and most fearful years, and around 523 Boethius was arrested, imprisoned, and in 524 executed without the full hearing his rank should have guaranteed, condemned, as he saw it, for loyalty to the very Roman order his king had spent thirty years claiming to restore. In prison, waiting to die, stripped of office, wealth, and reputation, he wrote **The Consolation of Philosophy**, a dialogue in which a figure called Philosophy visits the condemned man in his cell and argues him out of despair, on fortune, on suffering, and on the goods that no king and no executioner can confiscate. It became one of the most widely read and beloved books of the entire Middle Ages, copied and translated across Europe for the next thousand years, the last great work of the classical mind written, fittingly, by a Roman dying at the hands of a Gothic state that thought of itself as Rome's protector. Theodoric also executed Boethius's father-in-law, the revered senator **Symmachus**, on related charges.

These executions were the regime's self-inflicted wound, and the reason cuts to the heart of the book. Theodoric's whole system depended on the trust of the Roman aristocracy and the toleration of the religious divide. In his last years that trust curdled. The eastern empire under a new, aggressively Catholic regime was persecuting Arians, the rift between the Arian Gothic king and his Catholic Roman subjects widened into mutual suspicion, and Theodoric, aging and increasingly fearful of Roman disloyalty and a Constantinople plot, turned on the very Roman elite his rule had courted. He even imprisoned the pope, John I, after a failed diplomatic mission, where the pope died. The philosopher-king ended as a frightened old man killing his best Roman servants over their faith and their loyalty, and the goodwill that had made the dual state work died with them. Theodoric died in 526, leaving the throne to his young grandson under the regency of his daughter.

That daughter was **Amalasuntha** (pronounced "ah-mah-lah-SOON-tah"), and she was the most thoroughly Romanized of all the Amali, fluent in Latin and Greek, schooled in literature and philosophy, and determined to keep her father's dual state and his careful peace with Constantinople alive. She ruled in her young son Athalaric's name and tried to raise him as an educated prince in the Roman manner, with tutors and books. The Gothic nobility took this as exactly the wrong lesson: they wanted a king raised hard, among warriors, at the hunt and the drill ground, and they forced her to hand the boy back to a soldier's upbringing, under which he drank and sickened and died as a teenager. With him died her only legitimate claim to rule, and the nobles who resented the drift toward Rome now had her cornered. Politically exposed, she took a cousin, **Theodahad**, as co-ruler to shore up her position; he turned on her at once, had her imprisoned on an island, and in 535 had her murdered there, strangled in her bath. It was the worst possible decision at the worst possible moment, because the murder of the Romanized Gothic queen handed the most powerful man in the world the legal and moral pretext he had been waiting for. In Constantinople, an emperor who had sworn to reconquer the lost Roman West now had his cause.

# Chapter 11 — Justinian's War

The Ostrogothic kingdom was not conquered quickly and cleanly. It was killed slowly, over twenty years, in a war so destructive that the country both sides were fighting over, Italy, the original heartland of the Roman Empire, came out of it depopulated, plague-ridden, its cities broken and its aqueducts dry, never to recover its ancient prosperity. The grim final irony of this entire book is that the people who did the most physical destruction to Roman Italy were not the Goths who ruled it but the Romans who came to take it back. This is the story of how the Goths ended, and of what it cost.

The man who launched the war was **Justinian** (pronounced "jus-TIN-ee-an"), emperor of the eastern Roman Empire from Constantinople, whose great consuming ambition was the *renovatio imperii*, the reconquest of the western provinces lost to the barbarians and the restoration of the Roman Empire to one whole ([[byzantine-empire]]). He had already, by his general's hand, destroyed the Vandal kingdom in North Africa and retaken Carthage ([[carthage]]). The murder of Amalasuntha in 535 (Chapter 10), a princess Justinian had cultivated as a friendly contact, gave him exactly the casus belli he wanted. In 535 CE he sent an army to take Italy from the Ostrogoths, and the **Gothic War** began.

The general was **Belisarius** (pronounced "bel-ih-SAIR-ee-us"), the finest soldier of the age and Justinian's instrument in every reconquest. The opening went almost embarrassingly well for the empire. Belisarius took Sicily in 535 against little resistance, crossed to the mainland, captured Naples in 536 by a famous ruse, sending a party into the city along a disused aqueduct channel to surprise the defenders, and entered the city of **Rome** itself later that year as the Gothic garrison simply withdrew. Almost everything we know about this war comes from **Procopius** (pronounced "proh-KOH-pee-us"), a Greek-speaking secretary who served on Belisarius's own staff and wrote the standard contemporary history of Justinian's wars. The aqueduct story is his, and it is good to remember that a secret-aqueduct entry is also a stock dramatic set piece in ancient history-writing, so the detail may be sharpened in the telling even if the city did fall to a surprise infiltration. For a moment it looked like the whole thing might be over in two years.

It was not, because the Goths found a king. The feeble Theodahad was killed and replaced by a war-leader named **Witigis** (pronounced "WIT-ih-gis"), who gathered the Gothic army and in 537 laid siege to Belisarius inside Rome. The siege lasted over a year. Belisarius held the city using the great walls of Aurelian, the third-century fortifications built during the crisis of Chapter 3, now defending Rome against the Goths who had once helped make those walls necessary. He held, eventually went over to the offensive, and in 540 took Ravenna itself, the impregnable swamp-capital, not by storm but by a ruse: he let the Goths believe he would accept their offer to make *him* their western emperor, walked into the city under that pretense, and then took it for Justinian instead. Witigis was shipped to Constantinople as a prisoner. In 540 the war looked won, the Ostrogothic kingdom apparently finished.

---

It was not finished, and the reason is the structural flaw that runs through Justinian's whole project. Belisarius had won with a small army that was never properly reinforced or paid, because Justinian, fighting Persians in the east and suspicious of any general too successful, would not pour resources into Italy once it looked won. He recalled Belisarius, left the conquest half-garrisoned, and let the heavy taxation begin before the country was actually pacified. Into that gap the Goths recovered. What followed is the part of the war that makes it a tragedy rather than a conquest, and it centers on one man. The Goths, beaten and leaderless, raised a new king who turned out to be one of the most capable leaders in their whole history: **Totila** (pronounced "TOH-tih-lah"). From near-total defeat in 541, Totila reconquered almost all of Italy over the next decade and held it against the full weight of the eastern empire, and he did it with a strategy that was politically brilliant and that the Roman sources, hostile as they are, cannot quite hide.

Totila understood that the war was being lost by the *Romans* among their own people. Justinian's reconquest came with a tax regime far heavier than Theodoric's, run by imperial finance officials, the most notorious named Alexander "the Scissors" for the way he clipped soldiers' pay and squeezed the provincials, who treated newly retaken Italy as a source of revenue to be wrung dry rather than a population to be won over. The ordinary people of Italy, the poor, the enslaved, the dispossessed, were discovering that imperial liberation was worse than Gothic rule. Totila exploited this directly. He freed slaves who joined him, cancelled or redirected the rents the great landlords extracted, recruited the dispossessed into his army, and presented the Gothic cause not as barbarian resistance but as the liberation of ordinary Italians from a predatory Roman aristocracy and a predatory imperial tax collector. He wrote letters to the Roman Senate making exactly this argument, sophisticated political propaganda in full Roman rhetorical form, from a Gothic king, denouncing Constantinople's exploitation of Italy. He even put together a Gothic *fleet*, the same way the third-century Goths had taken to the sea (Chapter 3), by controlling Italian ports and harnessing the local shipwrights and sailors he now ruled, and used it to raid Sicily and the Adriatic coast, a people of the inland steppe two centuries earlier now fighting a naval war in the Mediterranean. He took Rome itself, more than once; the city changed hands several times in this war, was partly demolished, was at one point left almost entirely empty of inhabitants. Totila's revival is the proof that the Ostrogoths, even at the end, were not a spent barbarian remnant but an adaptive, politically inventive power that nearly won.

---

Justinian ended it by changing the general. He recalled Belisarius and in 551 sent the man who finished the war: **Narses** (pronounced "NAR-seez"), and the choice was as strange as anything in this book. Narses was a castrated palace official, a eunuch, the class of men who in Constantinople staffed the most trusted positions around the emperor precisely because, barred from founding dynasties of their own, they were thought safe to hold real power. He was by now around seventy-five, had spent his life in the treasury and the court rather than the field, and had no conventional military training at all. What he did have was Justinian's complete confidence, and with it the one thing Belisarius had been denied through fifteen years of war: not brilliance, which Belisarius had in abundance, but a large, fully supplied, fully paid army that did not melt away for want of food and wages. The greatest reconquest in Roman history was finished not by its finest soldier but by an old palace administrator who was finally given enough money to do the job. The two armies met in 552 at the **Battle of Taginae** (pronounced "ta-JEE-nay," also called Busta Gallorum, in the central Apennines). Narses out-thought Totila completely, anchored his line with massed dismounted archers, broke the Gothic cavalry charge, and won decisively. Totila was killed fleeing the field. The Gothic king who had nearly reversed the entire war was dead, and with him the Ostrogothic cause's last real chance.

The Goths fought on, briefly, under one more king, Teia, who made a last stand in 553 at the **Battle of Mons Lactarius** (pronounced "monz lak-TAH-ree-us," near Mount Vesuvius) and died there too. The surviving Goths negotiated terms; most of those who were left crossed the Alps out of Italy, and the Ostrogoths as an organized people, a kingdom, an army, a name with a state attached, ceased to exist. The Amali dynasty, the heroic Gothic royal line that Jordanes had traced back to semi-divine ancestors on the steppe (Chapter 4), simply ended. There was no more Ostrogothic Italy.

In 554 CE Justinian issued the **Pragmatic Sanction**, the decree that formally reimposed imperial Roman law over Italy, returned confiscated estates to their pre-war owners, and erased the Ostrogothic legal order as though the kingdom had never been. On paper it was the triumphant restoration of the Roman Empire in Italy, Justinian's life's ambition achieved. In reality it was a death certificate written over a grave. Twenty years of siege, famine, scorched earth, and the **Plague of Justinian** had wrecked the peninsula. That plague was the catastrophic pandemic, probably the first great outbreak of the bubonic plague, that swept the Mediterranean from the 540s and reached Italy in the middle of the war. Bubonic plague kills fast and in appalling numbers, swelling the lymph nodes into the black "buboes" that name it and carrying off a large fraction of an exposed population within weeks, and in a peninsula already emptied by war it removed the farmers, soldiers, and townspeople who might otherwise have rebuilt. Cities were depopulated, the aqueducts that Theodoric had so carefully maintained were broken, the agricultural economy was shattered, Rome itself was a fraction of its former self. Italy would not recover its pre-war population or prosperity for centuries. And the restoration did not even last: just fourteen years later, in 568, a new Germanic people walked into a northern Italy that the war had stripped of garrisons, defenders, and people, and took most of it almost unopposed. These were the **Lombards**, a frontier confederation from the middle Danube of much the same kind the Goths themselves had once been; some of them had actually fought in Narses's army against the Goths, and now they helped themselves to the prize. The empire had bled itself white winning a country it no longer had the strength to hold, and a fresh set of barbarians simply walked into the vacuum. Justinian destroyed the Goths and, in doing so, destroyed the thing the Goths had been preserving.

---

That is the end of the Goths as a power in Italy, and it is the place to step back and say what this whole book has been.

The Goths were the people who did the most to end the ancient world, and they did it not as the simple destroyers of Roman propaganda but as something far more interesting and far more consequential: the empire's most capable heirs. They produced the first written literature in any Germanic language. They issued some of the first written Germanic law codes, in Latin, in Roman form. They killed two Roman emperors in the field, sacked the unsackable city after eight hundred years, and then turned around and ran Roman Italy with more care for its aqueducts and its philosophers than its own last emperors had managed. They were Christians of the losing side, Arian heretics ruling Catholic subjects, and that single fact, planted by one bishop's mission in the fourth century, shaped and broke their kingdoms for two hundred years.

The Ostrogoths end here, in 554, completely. But the Goths do not entirely end, and the honest close of this book has to point both forward and outward, the way the previous book pointed at this one. The Ostrogothic destruction is one act in a much larger drama, the whole churning shattering of the Roman West, the Huns and the Vandals and the Franks and the fall of the empire, that is the subject of the wider story of the age ([[migration-period]]). And the western Goths did not die at all. The Visigothic Kingdom of Toledo (Chapter 9) ruled Hispania for another century and a half after the Ostrogoths were gone. In 589 a Visigothic king, Reccared, finally did the thing no Gothic ruler had done in two and a half centuries: at the Third Council of Toledo he converted his entire people from Arianism to Catholicism, healing at last the religious fracture Ulfilas had opened in the fourth century, and merging Goth and Hispano-Roman into a single Catholic people. That kingdom of Catholic Visigoths ruled Spain until 711, when an army crossed from North Africa and took the whole peninsula in a few years, opening the next great chapter of Iberian history ([[al-andalus]]).

The Goths who sacked Rome are gone. But the law they wrote down, the alphabet a bishop built for them, the idea that a barbarian king could be a restorer of civilization, and the political map their kingdoms drew across Gaul and Spain and Italy, all of that outlived them by a thousand years. They were not the end of Rome. They were, more than anyone, the bridge from it to everything that came next.

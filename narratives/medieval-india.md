# Medieval India — Temple Kingdoms and the Slow Road to Delhi

**Goal:** Narrative for the Indian Subcontinent chain entry covering the long, awkwardly named medieval period — roughly 550 CE, when the Gupta Empire fragmented, to 1206, when Qutb al-Din Aibak proclaimed himself sultan at Lahore and the Delhi Sultanate began. Six and a half centuries. No single empire holds the subcontinent for any of it; the closest anyone comes is Harsha's brief forty-one-year reunification in the seventh century. What the period lacks in unifying political narrative it makes up for in temples (Brihadeeswara, Khajuraho, Kailasa, Konark), philosophers (Shankara, Ramanuja, Madhva), naval expeditions (Rajendra Chola sailing to Sumatra), and devotional poetry (the Tamil Alvars and Nayanars, the Sanskrit Gita Govinda). It is also the era when Buddhism slowly disappeared from the land that invented it, when the Bhakti movement transformed Hindu worship from priest-mediated ritual into ecstatic personal devotion, and when the first wave of Turkic invasions ended Hindu political control of north India for the next seven centuries. None of this fits in a single tidy arc, which is why this book is about regional kingdoms doing very different things in parallel.

A note on names and pronunciations: medieval Indian sources used Sanskrit at the courts, Tamil and Kannada in the south, Persian and Arabic for the foreign visitors, and a dozen regional vernaculars in the temples. Names will be given in their most common English form, with pronunciations on first use. Dates are CE unless noted otherwise.

A note on "first" claims, as in every TL in this project: "first" means "first surviving evidence of." Medieval India is full of cases where a particular dynasty, technique, or text is the first attested example in surviving evidence but is almost certainly building on earlier work that did not survive. Royal patronage shapes which inscriptions get carved on stone, which palm-leaf manuscripts get recopied, and which temples get rebuilt rather than abandoned. The dynasties that dominated court chronicles are the ones we know about. The smaller polities, the marginal traditions, the working women — they show up as fragments in inscriptions and saint-biographies and not much more.

A note on the term "medieval India": it is a periodization invented by nineteenth-century European historians who imported the European tripartite scheme of Ancient / Medieval / Modern. Indian historians today often prefer "early-medieval" for this 550–1200 period (with "medieval" reserved for the Delhi Sultanate and Mughal eras), which is a useful clarification but does not change what the period actually contained. Whatever you call it, what happens between the Guptas and the Delhi Sultanate is the long middle of Indian civilizational history, and it happens overwhelmingly through regional dynasties, temples, and the slow work of philosophical and devotional culture rather than imperial unification.

# Chapter 1 — The Aftermath of Gupta

The Gupta Empire, classical India's golden age (Gupta Empire Ch 8), did not collapse in a single dramatic event. It fragmented gradually over a century, the way an old political order usually does — provincial governors stopped sending tribute, regional kings declared independence, dynastic infighting weakened the center, foreign pressure peeled off the borderlands, and at some point in the early sixth century the imperial fiction stopped being worth maintaining.

The trigger was the **Hephthalite Huns** — a Central Asian people, also called the **White Huns** or in Sanskrit sources the **Hunas** (HOO-nahz), who pushed south through Afghanistan into the upper Indus and Punjab regions in the late fifth century. They were not the same Huns who had attacked the Roman Empire under Attila a generation earlier (those were the **European Huns**, the people Attila led across the Danube), but they came from the same general Central Asian world and used the same kind of mounted-archer warfare. The Hephthalites had already destroyed the Sasanian Persian king Peroz I in 484 (Persian Empire Ch 9), and their westernmost expansion was now pressing on the Gupta northwest frontier.

The Gupta emperor **Skandagupta** (skahn-dah-GOOP-tah, ruled roughly 455–467) defeated the first wave of Hun raiders, but the campaigns drained the Gupta treasury — surviving Skandagupta-era gold coins are noticeably debased, with a higher base-metal content than the pure gold issues of his predecessors (Gupta Empire Ch 7). His successors faced a second, larger wave under the Hun kings **Toramana** (toh-rah-MAH-nah) and his son **Mihirakula** (mih-hee-rah-KOO-lah). By 510, the Huns controlled most of the Punjab and were operating as far southeast as Gwalior.

The reprieve came from an unexpected direction. Around **528 CE**, a king named **Yashodharman** (yah-shoh-DHAR-mahn) of **Malwa** (the central Indian region around modern Mandsaur) defeated Mihirakula at the **Battle of Sondani** — and either killed the Hun king or captured and humiliated him; later sources differ. Yashodharman's victory pillar at Mandsaur survives, with a Sanskrit inscription describing the battle in characteristically grandiose terms ("the Hun king bowed his head before the king of Malwa, who alone made him prostrate"). The political reality is that Yashodharman was not a Gupta loyalist — he was an ambitious local king filling the vacuum the Guptas had left — but his victory ended the Hun threat to north India and made the regional patchwork of the next century possible.

The Guptas themselves limped on for another two generations. The last attested Gupta emperor, **Vishnugupta**, ruled around 540–550 CE over a much-reduced territory in eastern India centered on Magadha, the original Gupta heartland. After him, Gupta inscriptions stop. The dynasty did not officially abolish itself; it just faded out, the way a senator's family in late antique Rome might fade out into private gentry without ever issuing a formal renunciation.

What replaced the Guptas was not another empire but a constellation of regional kingdoms, each holding a piece of the former Gupta territory and each treating itself as the natural local successor. To understand the medieval period that follows, you have to know the cast.

---

In the **western Ganges plain** — the rich agricultural region around modern Uttar Pradesh — the **Maukhari** (mow-KAH-ree) dynasty rose to prominence from the late sixth century. Their capital was **Kannauj** (kuh-NOWJ), the great cosmopolitan trade-center city on the upper Ganges that had been a regional hub for centuries and would now become the symbolic prize of north Indian politics for the next four hundred years. Kannauj sat at the junction where the trade routes from the northwest (Punjab and Afghanistan) met the routes down the Ganges to Bengal and the Bay of Bengal. Holding Kannauj meant holding the connection between north India's two halves. Three successive medieval power blocs would bleed themselves white fighting for it (Chapter 4).

To the **northwest**, in modern Haryana, the **Pushyabhuti** (push-yah-BHOO-tee, also called the **Vardhana**) dynasty established itself at **Thanesar** (THAH-neh-sar), an old pilgrimage town near modern Karnal. Pushyabhuti dynasties tend to look insignificant in retrospect — their early kings are barely attested in inscriptions — but the family produced **Harshavardhana** (har-shah-var-DHAH-nah, just **Harsha** for short), the last great north Indian emperor before the Turkic invasions, whose forty-one-year reign is the subject of the next chapter.

In **Gujarat**, the **Maitraka** (MAY-trah-kah) dynasty ruled from **Vallabhi** (VAL-ah-bee), a port city on the Saurashtra peninsula that handled the bulk of the western Indian trade with the Sasanian Persian Gulf. The Maitrakas were originally Gupta provincial governors who declared independence in the late fifth century; their kingdom lasted until Arab raids destroyed Vallabhi in the 770s.

In **Bengal**, the post-Gupta political situation was even more fragmented. The eastern Ganges delta split into two regional kingdoms — **Gauda** (GOW-dah) in the north and **Vanga** (VAHN-gah) in the south — with various smaller polities filling the spaces between them. A Gauda king named **Shashanka** (shah-SHAHN-kah) emerged briefly in the early seventh century as the most powerful figure in eastern India and would become Harsha's main rival.

In the **Deccan** plateau (south-central India, from roughly the Narmada River in the north to the Tungabhadra River in the south, the great upland that separates the northern plain from the Tamil south), the **Chalukyas** (chah-LOOK-yahz) of **Vatapi** (VAH-tah-pee, modern Badami in Karnataka) had built up a regional kingdom by the mid-sixth century and would shortly become the dominant southern power.

In the **Tamil south** — the deep peninsular region below modern Bangalore — three ancient kingdoms were resurfacing after centuries of obscurity: the **Pallavas** (PAH-lah-vahz) of **Kanchipuram** (kahn-chee-POO-ram), the **Pandyas** (PAHN-dee-ahz) of **Madurai** (MAH-doo-rai), and the **Cheras** (CHEH-rahz) of the Malabar coast in modern Kerala. The fourth ancient Tamil power, the **Cholas** (CHOH-lahz), would not reappear as a major player until the ninth century, but when they did they would build south India's first true empire (Chapter 5).

This is the cast: a half-dozen regional dynasties, each pursuing local ambitions, none of them strong enough to subordinate the others. The medieval period in India is the long chronicle of what they did with the situation.

---

A pause to address the obvious question. Why did the Gupta Empire collapse, and why did nothing comparable replace it for six and a half centuries?

The standard textbook answers are several, none of them entirely satisfying. The Hun invasions exhausted the imperial treasury and military, which is true but does not explain why no Indian dynasty reconsolidated after the Hun threat ended. The economic shift from imperial trade to localized rural agriculture — the so-called "feudalization" thesis — has been argued by some Indian historians (especially the late R. S. Sharma) but contested by others; the documentary evidence is real but its interpretation is sharply debated, and the comparison with European feudalism is almost certainly forced. The rise of regional cultural identities — Tamil south, Kannada Karnataka, Bengali east, Marathi Maharashtra — made imperial unification harder once it had broken, the way it would later be hard for any single power to reunify the post-Roman successor states of Western Europe.

The simplest honest answer is that nobody knows for sure. India has a recurring pattern in which large empires (Maurya, Gupta, Mughal, British) are followed by long periods of political fragmentation that nonetheless produce extraordinary cultural flowering. Whether this pattern reflects something structural about the subcontinent's geography (too big, too varied, too defended by mountains and oceans for any single state to hold) or something contingent about the timing of dynastic collapses (the Guptas happened to fall before any natural successor was ready) is a question generations of historians have argued about and not resolved. What is observable is the pattern: imperial unification is the exception in Indian history, regional fragmentation the rule.

The cross-civ comparison that helps is the post-Roman west. The Western Roman Empire fell in 476 CE, and what replaced it was not another empire but a constellation of regional kingdoms — Visigoths in Spain, Franks in Gaul, Anglo-Saxons in Britain, Ostrogoths in Italy. Western Europe stayed politically fragmented for the next thousand years until the early-modern nation-states slowly coalesced. India in 550 CE was beginning a similar process, except that the Indian subcontinent is roughly the same size as Western Europe, and the Indian regional kingdoms turned out to be better at building monumental temples than their European counterparts at building monumental cathedrals — at least until the Gothic age finally got underway in the twelfth century, by which time the Brihadeeswara had already stood for two hundred years.

---

The transition from Gupta to post-Gupta also produced one of the most consequential demographic changes in Indian history: the **gradual disappearance of Buddhism** from north India.

This is a process, not an event, and it took roughly seven hundred years to complete. In 500 CE, Buddhism had been the dominant religion of north India for nearly a thousand years. The great monastic universities at **Nalanda** (nah-LAHN-dah; founded under late Gupta patronage as one of the world's first true residential universities, with up to 10,000 students from across Asia at its medieval peak), **Vikramashila** (vik-rah-mah-SHEE-lah; a Pala-era Buddhist university founded around 800 CE), and **Odantapuri** (oh-dahn-tah-POO-ree; a third major monastic center) flourished through the entire period this book covers. Faxian and Xuanzang both visited as Chinese pilgrims, took back Sanskrit texts, and translated them into Chinese — a process that established Chinese Buddhism as a major world religious tradition (Han Dynasty Ch 7 and Six Dynasties Ch 6).

Yet by 1300 CE, Buddhism had nearly vanished from India. It survived in Sri Lanka, in Tibet, in Southeast Asia, in China, in Japan and Korea — but not in the land that invented it. A handful of small Buddhist communities persisted in the Himalayan foothills and along the Bay of Bengal coast, but the major monasteries were gone, the lay community had been reabsorbed into Hinduism, and the religion that had shaped Indian intellectual life for fifteen centuries had effectively disappeared from its homeland.

How and why this happened is one of the major puzzles of Indian religious history, and Chapter 7 takes it up in detail. For now the relevant point is that the disappearance was already underway during the period this book covers — partly through gradual royal patronage shifts (kings who had once funded monasteries now funded Hindu temples), partly through Hindu philosophical absorption of Buddhist ideas (Shankara's Advaita Vedanta in the eighth century essentially argued that the Buddhists were right about a lot of things but had reached the wrong final conclusions), and finally through the destruction of the great Pala monastic universities in the Turkic invasions around 1200 (Chapter 8). The post-Gupta political fragmentation was the macro-level frame inside which this religious shift happened.

---

One more piece of context, before the chronological narrative starts.

The medieval period is when the **temple** consolidates as the central institution of Indian religious life — economically, socially, and architecturally. This is genuinely new. The Vedic period worshipped through fire sacrifices conducted in open-air enclosures, with no permanent temple buildings (Vedic Period Ch 3). Early Buddhism worshipped at stupas (hemispherical relic mounds) and in monasteries, not in temples in the later Hindu sense. The Mauryans and Guptas built some temples but on a modest scale; most surviving Gupta-era temples are small structures with single sanctuaries.

What changes in the medieval period is that temples become huge. They become economically powerful (with extensive landholdings, hundreds of staff, daily ritual employment for entire towns). They become architecturally elaborate (the soaring **shikhara** towers of the north, the stepped **vimana** towers of the south, the dense external sculpture covering every surface). They become political instruments (a king demonstrates his legitimacy by building or restoring temples; a victorious king plunders or destroys his enemy's principal temple; a temple sponsorship inscription is a piece of permanent royal advertising). And they become community centers — the temple complex includes assembly halls, ritual bathing pools, kitchens for distributing food to pilgrims, schools, and dance pavilions, with all of this activity organized around the central deity in the sanctum.

Almost every famous Indian temple — the ones on postcards and travel websites — was built during the medieval period. Brihadeeswara at Thanjavur (1010, Chola). Khajuraho in central India (950–1050, Chandela). Kailasa Temple at Ellora (770, Rashtrakuta). Konark Sun Temple in Odisha (1250, Eastern Ganga). The Hoysala temples at Belur and Halebidu (twelfth century). The Lingaraja at Bhubaneswar (eleventh century). The early Pandya phases of the Meenakshi Temple at Madurai. The Pallava complex at Mahabalipuram. The Modhera Sun Temple in Gujarat (early eleventh century, Chaulukya). All of them medieval. The temple-as-monument is one of the great cultural products of the period this book covers, and it is the visible, physical thing the period left behind.

The cross-civ comparison is the cathedral-building era of medieval Western Europe. The first stage of Romanesque cathedrals starts around 1000; the great Gothic age runs from roughly 1140 (Saint-Denis) to 1500. Indian temple-building begins earlier (the Pallava rock-cut temples at Mahabalipuram are seventh-century, comparable to Charlemagne's palace chapel at Aachen), peaks earlier (Brihadeeswara in 1010 is contemporary with the early Romanesque), and continues until the Mughal era. Both traditions are doing the same fundamental thing: deploying massive architectural and economic resources to express religious devotion, dynastic legitimacy, and civic identity in stone. Both produce monuments that survive their builders by a millennium.

The political and religious chapters of this book will keep returning to the temples. Hold on to the framework.

Cross-civ check: the period this chapter covers — roughly 550 to 650 CE, the immediate aftermath of the Gupta collapse — overlaps with **Justinian's reconquest** of the Western Mediterranean (Byzantine Empire Ch 2), the rise of the **Sui** and early **Tang** dynasties in China (Sui–Tang–Song Ch 1), the founding of **Islam** in the Arabian Peninsula (Muhammad's preaching begins around 610 CE, Islamic Golden Age Ch 1), the **Maya Classic period** flowering at Tikal and Palenque (Maya Civilization Ch 5), the **Maitraka** Persian Gulf trade peak under the late **Sasanian Empire** (Persian Empire Ch 9), and the very end of the **Visigothic Kingdom** in Spain. The world of the late sixth and early seventh centuries was a world reorganizing after Late Antiquity — and India was one of the regions doing that reorganization most visibly.

# Chapter 2 — Harsha and the Last North-Indian Empire

Among the regional kings who emerged from the Gupta wreckage, one stood out from the rest. **Harshavardhana** of the Pushyabhuti dynasty came to the throne at Thanesar in **606 CE**, at the age of sixteen, under circumstances so dramatic that they generated the first surviving Sanskrit historical biography of an actual Indian king.

The story is told by Harsha's court poet **Banabhatta** (BAH-nah-BHAHT-tah, or just **Bana**), who composed the **Harshacharita** ("Deeds of Harsha") around 640 CE while Harsha was still alive. The Harshacharita is the first surviving Sanskrit royal biography in anything like the modern sense — earlier Sanskrit literary genres had dealt with mythological kings (the Mahabharata and Ramayana), legendary kings (the Buddhist Jatakas), or kings as supporting characters in religious texts, but no surviving earlier work attempts a sustained narrative biography of a contemporary historical individual. The Harshacharita is propaganda, written by an employee of the king, and it should be read with the same skepticism you would apply to any official biography. But it is also detailed, internally consistent, partially confirmed by other inscriptions and the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang's independent eyewitness account, and the closest thing we have to a campaign chronicle for any seventh-century Indian king.

Harsha came to the throne because his older brother **Rajyavardhana** (rah-jyah-var-DHAH-nah) was assassinated.

The background was a marriage alliance. Harsha's older sister **Rajyashri** had been married to the Maukhari king **Grahavarman** of Kannauj (Chapter 1), making the Pushyabhuti and Maukhari dynasties allies through the upper Ganges plain. Around 605 CE, the Gauda king **Shashanka** of Bengal — Harsha's eventual archnemesis — invaded Kannauj, killed Grahavarman, imprisoned Rajyashri, and threatened to extend his power across north India. Harsha's father, the Pushyabhuti king Prabhakaravardhana, had recently died. His older brother Rajyavardhana led a punitive expedition against Shashanka, defeated him in battle, but was then assassinated — the Harshacharita says by treachery at a parley, though the details are murky. Bana describes Harsha receiving the news, taking a vow of vengeance, and being persuaded by his ministers to accept the throne rather than abdicate to a religious life. He was sixteen years old. The year was 606 CE.

The first thing he did was take an army down the Ganges to find his sister.

The campaign that followed is the central drama of the Harshacharita. Harsha pursued Shashanka, recovered Rajyashri (Bana describes her in the Vindhya Mountains, about to commit ritual self-immolation when her brother's army arrived in time to stop her), drove Shashanka back to Bengal, and over the next several years systematically extended his control across the upper and middle Ganges plain. By 612 CE he ruled Thanesar, Kannauj (which he made his new capital), and most of modern Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and parts of Punjab. By 620 he had pushed east to incorporate parts of Bengal and Odisha. By the 630s, he was the unchallenged ruler of north India.

He never quite controlled the south. Around 618 CE he tried to extend his empire across the **Narmada River** — the traditional geographic frontier separating north India from the Deccan plateau — and was decisively defeated by **Pulakeshin II** (POO-lah-keh-shin), the great Chalukya king of Vatapi. Pulakeshin's victory inscription at **Aihole** (eye-HOH-lay, the Chalukya temple-experimentation site in modern Karnataka), composed by the court poet **Ravikirti** in elegant Sanskrit verse, describes Harsha's defeat in characteristically gloating terms: "his elephants, smitten by Pulakeshin's army, ran wild." The historical reality is that Harsha's expansion stopped at the Narmada and the Chalukyas held the Deccan unchallenged for another century. The two empires coexisted along the Narmada line for the rest of Harsha's reign — north and south India were politically distinct, and the medieval period would mostly see them stay that way.

What is striking about Harsha is that he was the only seventh-century north Indian king to manage even a partial reunification, and he was barely able to hold it together. His empire required his constant personal attention — the Harshacharita and Xuanzang both describe him conducting endless military progressions across his territory, never settling in Kannauj for long, dispensing justice from a portable royal camp. When he died in 647 CE without an heir, his empire dissolved within months. There was no Pushyabhuti dynasty after Harsha. Even the centralized state he built turned out to be his personal achievement, not a durable institution.

---

The most extraordinary firsthand account of Harsha's India comes from a foreign visitor: the Chinese Buddhist monk **Xuanzang** (shwen-DZAHNG, sometimes spelled **Hsüan-tsang**, ~602–664 CE).

Xuanzang spent **fifteen years** traveling across India between 630 and 645 CE, primarily to collect Buddhist scriptures and study at the great monastic universities. His travelogue, the **Records of the Western Regions** (Chinese: *Da Tang Xiyu Ji*), is the single most detailed eyewitness description of seventh-century Indian society from any source — Indian or foreign — and it is the closest thing the medieval period offers to the kind of historical anchor that, in other civilizational traditions, you take for granted.

The biographical setup is worth pausing on, because it illustrates the lengths a determined seventh-century traveler had to go to. Xuanzang was a Chinese monk born in Henan province who became dissatisfied with the inconsistent Chinese translations of Sanskrit Buddhist texts then in circulation. He decided around 627 CE to travel to India in person, learn Sanskrit, and bring back authentic copies. The Tang government refused him a travel permit (China was at the time fighting the Eastern Turks and travel into Central Asia was restricted; Six Dynasties Ch 7 sets the scene). Xuanzang left without permission, smuggling himself across the Jade Gate and through the Tarim Basin oases, surviving sandstorms in the Taklamakan Desert, traversing the Tian Shan mountain passes, and reaching Samarkand and Bactria in modern Uzbekistan and northern Afghanistan. He then crossed the Hindu Kush into Gandhara (the old Buddhist heartland in modern northwestern Pakistan and eastern Afghanistan), spent several years studying at monasteries there, and finally entered the Indian subcontinent proper around 631.

He spent the next decade traveling. He visited the Kushan-era stupa at Peshawar. He climbed up to the rock-cut Buddhas of Bamiyan in central Afghanistan (the same colossal Buddhist sculptures destroyed by the Taliban in 2001 — Xuanzang's eyewitness description from 630 is one of the most detailed surviving accounts of how they originally looked). He went to **Sarnath** near Varanasi, where the Buddha had given his first sermon. He visited **Bodh Gaya**, the place of the Buddha's enlightenment. He spent five years studying at **Nalanda** under the abbot **Shilabhadra**, mastering Sanskrit and the major Mahayana philosophical schools. He traveled south through the Deccan and reached the Pallava country in the Tamil region. He returned north along the western coast through the Maitraka kingdom of Gujarat. He visited Harsha's court at Kannauj and was honored with a major religious assembly attended by twenty subordinate kings. And finally in 645 he returned to China — twenty years after he had left, with **657 Sanskrit manuscripts**, several Buddha relics, and a head full of detailed observations.

The Tang emperor Taizong (Sui–Tang–Song Ch 1), who had refused Xuanzang's original travel permit, now welcomed him as a celebrity and assigned him a translation bureau. Xuanzang spent the rest of his life translating Sanskrit Buddhist texts into Chinese — a project that eventually produced 1,335 volumes and established the philosophical foundations of the Chinese Yogacara and Faxiang schools of Buddhism. He also dictated his travelogue, the *Records of the Western Regions*, at Taizong's request as a geographic and political reference for the Tang court's interest in Central Asia.

What makes the *Records* invaluable for medieval Indian history is its method. Xuanzang was a careful, methodical observer with no political stake in the kingdoms he visited. He gives administrative details (the size of cities, the strength of armies, the names of kings, the systems of taxation), religious details (the number of monasteries in each region, the dominant Buddhist school, the relative strength of Buddhism vs. Hinduism vs. Jainism), economic details (the goods produced, the trade routes, the prices), and cultural details (food, clothing, ceremonies, mortuary practices, conversational customs). He does this for over a hundred kingdoms across the Indian subcontinent and the surrounding regions. No comparable Indian source from the period exists. Xuanzang's *Records* are the basic geographical reference for seventh-century India.

His description of Harsha himself is detailed and admiring. Xuanzang describes a king who personally attended to military, judicial, and religious affairs from a portable camp; who patronized Buddhism and Hindu sects equally without favoring one; who held a great religious assembly at **Prayag** (modern Allahabad, the confluence of the Ganges and Yamuna) every five years and ceremonially distributed his entire treasury to monks, Brahmins, and the poor; who had a personal scholarly interest in Mahayana Buddhism and patronized Nalanda generously; who personally honored Xuanzang at the Kannauj assembly and arranged for his safe return journey to China. The portrait is favorable — Xuanzang owed Harsha hospitality and protection — but the specific details (the religious assemblies, the Nalanda patronage, the Prayag distributions) are confirmed by independent Indian inscriptions and by Bana's Harshacharita.

The single sentence that probably captures the man comes from Xuanzang: "He forgot sleep and food in his devotion to good works." The Chinese pilgrim may have been polishing his royal patron's reputation, but the account is consistent with everything else we know about Harsha — a serious, dutiful, religiously eclectic king who maintained his empire through personal effort and saw kingship as a moral vocation.

---

Harsha was also a writer. Three Sanskrit plays survive under his name — **Ratnavali** (the love story of a king and a princess in disguise), **Priyadarshika** (a similar courtly romance), and **Nagananda** (a more serious Buddhist work about a prince who sacrifices himself to save the snake-people from being eaten by Garuda, the eagle-mount of Vishnu). Whether Harsha personally composed these plays or commissioned them from court poets writing under his name is debated; the standard scholarly view is that he was at least the active patron and probably contributed substantively. The plays are competent if not first-rate Sanskrit drama — the *Nagananda* in particular has been periodically revived as the most theologically interesting work of Sanskrit Buddhist theater. That a reigning emperor was producing literary work, or at least taking active credit for it, says something about the cultural standards of seventh-century court life.

Bana's **Harshacharita** itself is even more important as a literary monument. It is written in **kavya** (KAH-vyah) — the elaborate, ornate style of Sanskrit court poetry that prizes complex compound nouns, layered metaphor, and dense ornamentation. Reading Bana is genuinely difficult; even fluent Sanskritists report that his prose requires constant reference to commentary. But the difficulty is part of the point — kavya was meant to demonstrate the highest possible Sanskrit literary skill, and Bana was showing off for an audience that included other accomplished poets at Harsha's court.

Bana also wrote a second work, the **Kadambari**, which is a long Sanskrit prose romance about love across multiple lifetimes. He died before finishing it, and his son Bhushanabhatta completed it. The *Kadambari* is more famous in modern India than the *Harshacharita*; in many Indian university Sanskrit programs it is the standard introduction to Bana and to mature kavya prose. Both works survive complete. Bana is, by some distance, the most accomplished Sanskrit prose writer of the medieval period, and the fact that he flourished under Harsha is one of the best advertisements for the cultural environment Harsha created.

---

When Harsha died in **647 CE**, his empire fell apart almost immediately.

He had no son. The throne passed briefly to a minister named **Arjuna**, who attempted to seize power for himself — and who promptly attacked a Tang Chinese diplomatic mission that happened to be traveling through Magadha at the time. The leader of the mission, a Chinese ambassador named **Wang Xuance**, escaped, fled to Tibet, raised a Tibetan and Nepalese auxiliary force, returned, defeated and captured Arjuna, and brought him back to China as a prisoner of Emperor Taizong. The episode is one of the genuinely strange diplomatic incidents of the seventh century and is documented from the Chinese side in Tang court records. From the Indian side, the Pushyabhuti collapse is too rapid even to leave good evidence — within five years of Harsha's death, his empire was gone, the regional kingdoms had reasserted themselves, and the political situation had returned to the patchwork of competing dynasties that the Pushyabhutis had briefly transcended.

What did Harsha leave behind?

Politically, almost nothing. His empire dissolved in months. No durable institutional framework survived him. His capital city of Kannauj would be fought over by other dynasties for the next four centuries (Chapter 4) but the Pushyabhuti dynasty itself was extinct.

Culturally, more than the political record suggests. The Harshacharita established a Sanskrit historical biography genre that influenced subsequent court literature across India. The cultural environment at Kannauj attracted scholars and poets who dispersed across the regional kingdoms after his death and carried that environment with them. His religious eclecticism — patronizing Buddhism, Shaivism, Vaishnavism, and the sun cult equally — set a model that subsequent Indian kings often emulated, the implicit theory being that royal legitimacy was best displayed through pluralistic patronage of all the kingdom's religious traditions rather than identification with any one of them. The Prayag assemblies he sponsored became a recurring institution of north Indian religious life that survived in various forms into the modern period.

And the Harshacharita / Xuanzang documentation pair gives us a window into seventh-century India that we have for almost no other moment in the medieval period. After Harsha, the documentary record gets thinner. Inscriptions continue, sculpture and architecture survive, religious literature accumulates — but the kind of dense political-administrative-cultural documentation that the Harshacharita and the *Records of the Western Regions* together provide is essentially unique to the early seventh century.

After Harsha, nobody else manages to unite north India until Babur in 1526 (Mughal Empire Ch 1). Eight hundred and seventy-nine years.

Cross-civ check: Harsha's reign (606–647) overlaps almost exactly with the lifetime of **Muhammad** (~570–632, Islamic Golden Age Ch 1), the founder of Islam. While Harsha was building his north Indian empire and presiding over Buddhist religious assemblies at Prayag, Muhammad was preaching in Mecca, fleeing to Medina, conducting the early Arab conquests, and laying the foundations of an empire that would shortly transform Eurasian history. The early Caliphate (the **Rashidun**, "Rightly Guided") expanded out of Arabia in the 630s and 640s — exactly the years Xuanzang was traveling through India. The two events were geographically and culturally distant from each other; neither figure was aware of the other. But the same decades that gave India its last great pre-Sultanate Hindu emperor were giving the Middle East the religion that would, six centuries later, end Hindu political dominance in north India entirely. The historical irony is too perfect to leave unmarked.

# Chapter 3 — The South Rises

While the post-Gupta north fragmented into competing regional kingdoms, the south was doing something different. From the late sixth century forward, three southern dynasties — the **Pallavas** of Kanchipuram, the **Pandyas** of Madurai, and the **Chalukyas** of Vatapi — built temple-states that produced the architectural traditions and political institutions that the Cholas would later inherit and scale up. The chapter's central claim is that the most important medieval Indian innovations of the seventh and eighth centuries — Hindu temple architecture as a fully developed tradition, royal patronage of Tamil devotional poetry, the Vesara hybrid temple style of the Deccan, the world's largest monolithic religious sculpture — all happened in the south, while the north was still working through the political consequences of the Gupta collapse.

---

The **Pallava dynasty** had ruled the Tamil northeast (centered on **Kanchipuram**, kahn-chee-POO-ram, an old religious city in the modern Indian state of Tamil Nadu about forty miles southwest of Chennai) since the late third or early fourth century CE. Their early history is poorly attested — they appear in inscriptions as regional kings paying tribute to the Satavahanas (Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 6) and then to local successor dynasties, and only emerge as an independent power in the sixth century. Around 575 CE under **Simhavishnu** (sim-hah-VISH-noo), the Pallavas began their classical phase: an aggressive expansion along the Tamil coast, formal Sanskrit court culture, and the first stages of a major temple-building program that would last for the next three centuries.

The architectural breakthrough came under **Mahendravarman I** (mah-hen-drah-VAR-mahn, ruled ~600–630). Mahendravarman was, like Harsha, an unusually literate king: he wrote a Sanskrit farce called the **Mattavilasa Prahasana** ("The Drunkard's Sport"), a satire on Buddhist and Shaivite mendicants that survives complete and is one of the only specimens of Sanskrit comedy from any period. He was also the first Pallava king to commission **rock-cut temples** carved directly into living rock — initially small cave-shrines at sites like Mahendravadi and Mandagapattu in the Tamil hills, where the unwary visitor today can still find inscriptions praising the king for having built a temple "without timber, without mortar, without bricks, without metal."

The rock-cut technique was not a Pallava invention. It went back to the Buddhist cave-monasteries of Maharashtra (the famous caves at Ajanta, mostly Gupta-era; Gupta Empire Ch 4) and Karnataka, which had been carved as monastic dormitories and prayer halls since the second century BCE. What the Pallavas did was adapt the technique to free-standing Hindu temple structures — chambers with sanctuaries, mandapas (pillared halls), and external sculpture, all carved from a single rock face rather than constructed from quarried blocks. The chairs of construction are reversed: instead of building up, you carve down, removing material to leave the desired form.

This led to the period's most ambitious architectural project, at a coastal site about forty miles south of Chennai called **Mahabalipuram** (mah-hah-BAH-lee-POO-rahm, also spelled **Mamallapuram**, since it took its name from the epithet "Mamalla" of King **Narasimhavarman I**, mah-LAH).

---

**Narasimhavarman I** (nah-rah-sim-hah-VAR-mahn, ruled ~630–668) was Mahendravarman's son and successor, and he was the king who turned the Pallava state into the dominant southern power for a generation. His military reputation was made by the wars against the Chalukyas of Vatapi. The Chalukya king **Pulakeshin II** (Chapter 2) had been the dominant Deccan king of the early seventh century — he had defeated Harsha at the Narmada around 618, accepted homage from kings as far north as Maharashtra, and conducted his own raids into Pallava territory. Around 642, Narasimhavarman finally turned the tables, marched north into Chalukya territory, and **sacked Vatapi** itself. Pulakeshin II was killed in the fighting. The Pallava king added the title **Vatapi-konda** ("conqueror of Vatapi") to his royal style and brought back south Indian sculptors who had been trained on Chalukya architectural projects.

The Mahabalipuram complex was developed under Narasimhavarman and his successors over roughly the next eighty years. It includes:

The **Pancha Rathas** (PAHN-chah RAH-thahz, "Five Chariots"), a row of five free-standing monolithic shrines carved out of solid granite outcrops. Each is named after a Mahabharata character (the Pandava brothers and their wife Draupadi) — though the names were probably attached centuries later by local tradition, not by the Pallava builders. The five Rathas are the same building experiment in five different architectural styles, side by side: a small flat-roofed shrine, a barrel-vaulted shrine, a multi-tiered pyramidal shrine, and so on. They were never consecrated for actual worship — they appear to be a kind of architectural sketchbook in stone, built so the Pallava court could compare the styles and choose which to develop further. The pyramidal Dharmaraja Ratha became the prototype for the Dravida south Indian temple style that would eventually produce Brihadeeswara at Thanjavur four centuries later.

The **Descent of the Ganges** (also called **Arjuna's Penance**), the largest open-air bas-relief in India and possibly in the world. It covers a granite cliff face about ninety feet long and forty-five feet high, with a natural vertical fissure running through the middle of the composition. The sculpture depicts a scene from Hindu mythology — either the descent of the Ganges River from heaven (with the river figured as falling water through the cliff fissure) or the legendary austerities of the Pandava prince Arjuna (who stood on one leg in penance to obtain a divine weapon from Shiva), depending on whose interpretation you accept; the sculpture allows both readings. It contains over a hundred figures: gods, sages, animals, celestial beings, and a famous family of life-sized elephants near the bottom. The technical sophistication and sheer scale put it in a class by itself among premodern bas-reliefs anywhere in the world.

The **Shore Temple**, built around 700 CE under Narasimhavarman II, is one of the earliest free-standing structural stone temples in south India. (Earlier south Indian "temples" had mostly been rock-cut into living rock; the Shore Temple was built up from quarried stone blocks like a northern Nagara temple.) It stands on the Bay of Bengal beach with the surf coming in around the base — historically there were probably six other shore temples in a row that have since been eroded by the ocean — and its three-tower configuration is one of the iconic images of medieval Indian architecture.

The whole Mahabalipuram complex is a UNESCO World Heritage site. It is also an active tourist destination today, easily reached as a day trip from Chennai, and it is the best single place to walk through the early development of medieval south Indian temple architecture.

---

The **Chalukyas of Vatapi**, even after losing Pulakeshin II to Narasimhavarman in 642, recovered and continued ruling the Deccan for another century. Their major architectural legacy is the temple complexes at three closely spaced sites in modern Karnataka: **Aihole** (eye-HOH-lay), **Pattadakal** (paht-tah-dah-KAHL), and **Badami** (bah-DAH-mee, the modern name for ancient Vatapi).

What makes these sites remarkable is that they document, in stone, the experimental development of Hindu temple architecture in the Deccan. The Chalukya kings patronized temple builders who tried out both the **Nagara** north-Indian style (curvilinear shikhara towers) and the **Dravida** south-Indian style (stepped pyramidal vimana towers), often building them side by side in the same complex so the patrons could see the difference. The hybrid that eventually emerged from this experimentation, called the **Vesara** (VEH-sah-rah) style, combined elements of both — a stepped tower with northern-style curving profiles and miniature shikhara motifs, the Deccan compromise between the two parent traditions. The Hoysala temples at Belur and Halebidu (Chapter 6) would later carry the Vesara style to its decorative peak.

The Chalukya site at **Pattadakal** is the canonical place to see this experiment in progress. Ten major temples cluster around a central courtyard. Four are built in the Nagara north-Indian style. Six are built in the Dravida south-Indian style. The two traditions stand side by side, each with its own characteristic plan, tower form, and sculptural program, all built within the same century by the same dynasty's patronage. UNESCO World Heritage; one of the most architecturally illuminating temple complexes anywhere in India.

The Chalukyas were finally overthrown in **753 CE** by their own subordinates, the **Rashtrakutas** (rash-trah-KOO-tahz) of **Manyakheta** (mahn-yah-KHEH-tah, modern Malkhed in Karnataka).

---

The **Rashtrakuta** dynasty was the most powerful early-medieval Indian power. They ruled most of the Deccan for two and a half centuries (753–982 CE), at their peak demanded tribute from the Pratiharas of north India and the Palas of Bengal alike, and conducted the most ambitious architectural project in medieval Indian history — the **Kailasa Temple at Ellora**.

The Rashtrakuta founder **Dantidurga** (dahn-tee-DOOR-gah, ruled 735–757) overthrew his Chalukya overlords through a combination of military skill and strategic marriage alliances. His successor **Krishna I** (KRISH-nah, ruled ~757–774) consolidated the Rashtrakuta state and commissioned the Kailasa Temple. The greatest Rashtrakuta king was **Govinda III** (GOH-vin-dah, ruled 793–814), who conducted the most successful military campaigns of the period — defeating the Pala emperor Dharmapala and the Pratihara emperor Nagabhata II in a single campaign and briefly making Kannauj a Rashtrakuta dependency. **Amoghavarsha I** (ah-MOH-ghah-VAR-shah, ruled 814–878) was a long-reigning, highly cultured king who patronized Jainism, founded the new capital at Manyakheta, and is sometimes credited (probably loosely) with the Sanskrit poetic theory text **Kavirajamarga**.

The Arab traveler **al-Masudi** (vis-ah-LOO-dee, "the Herodotus of the Arabs," who wrote in the early tenth century after travels across the Indian Ocean world) called the Rashtrakuta king of his day **one of the four great kings of the world** — alongside the Abbasid caliph in Baghdad, the Byzantine emperor in Constantinople, and the emperor of China. The classification is revealing of how an outside observer with a Eurasian comparative perspective ranked the political powers of the early tenth century. The Rashtrakutas were operating in the same imperial league as the Abbasids and the Tang.

But their lasting monument is the temple at Ellora.

---

The **Kailasa Temple at Ellora** — Cave 16 in the cave-temple complex on the Aurangabad-Manmad highway in modern Maharashtra — is one of the genuine wonders of the world.

It is a full-sized Hindu temple to **Shiva** (in his form as **Kailasanatha**, "lord of Kailasa," referring to the sacred Himalayan mountain that Hindu mythology identifies as Shiva's earthly abode). Architecturally it is a complete temple complex: a central sanctum, a multi-pillared mandapa hall, two subsidiary shrines, a Nandi pavilion (housing the bull-mount of Shiva), surrounding cloistered galleries with smaller shrines, and elaborate external sculpture covering the towers and walls. The central tower rises 96 feet. The entire complex measures roughly 165 feet long, 109 feet wide, and 100 feet tall.

What makes the Kailasa Temple unique is that it was **carved downward out of a single basalt cliff**. Conventional construction works upward from foundations — you quarry stone elsewhere, transport it to the site, and assemble it. The Rashtrakuta builders did the opposite. Starting at the top of the basalt outcrop, they cut a vertical trench around the rectangular footprint of the future temple, isolating a great rectangular block of rock. They then carved downward into that block, removing rock to leave the temple's external form — towers, walls, doorways, statues — all in their finished positions. They then hollowed out the interior chambers. Then they covered every surface, internal and external, with detailed sculpture. Then they carved the surrounding cloister galleries and subsidiary shrines into the surrounding cliff face.

The total quantity of basalt removed is conservatively estimated at **200,000 tons**. The work probably took roughly twenty years to complete, with hundreds of stonecutters, sculptors, and supervisors organized around the project. Modern engineering analyses have noted that the carvers had no possibility of correcting structural mistakes — one bad cut on a primary load-bearing element and the entire edifice could have been ruined — and that the geometric precision and structural soundness of the finished temple are remarkable given the irreversibility of every step in the process.

It is the largest single monolithic structure in the world. It is the largest rock-cut temple anywhere. It is one of the great engineering feats of the ancient or medieval period from any civilization. And it stands today essentially intact, carved out of the same cliff it was carved out of in 770 CE, available to walk through and photograph on a regular tourist day-trip from Aurangabad. UNESCO World Heritage, of course.

The standard cross-civ comparison is **Petra** (the rock-cut Nabataean city in modern Jordan, mostly second century BCE through second century CE) — but Petra's facades, however dramatic, are mostly tomb-fronts carved into cliff faces, not full free-standing buildings carved from isolated blocks. The Kailasa Temple is a full Hindu temple complete in three dimensions, in the round, on a scale none of the Petra structures attempt. The closer comparison is probably **Lalibela** in Ethiopia (the rock-hewn churches built around 1200 by Emperor Gebre Mesqel Lalibela), which uses the same isolate-and-carve-downward technique — but Lalibela's largest structure (Bete Medhane Alem) is roughly 110 feet long and 80 feet wide. Kailasa is bigger. Lalibela also came roughly four hundred years after Kailasa, suggesting that the Ethiopian builders may have been independently rediscovering a technique the Rashtrakutas had already perfected, or possibly that knowledge of the Kailasa technique had circulated through Indian Ocean trade and influenced the Ethiopian project. The historical connection is unproven; the technical similarity is unmistakable.

---

The **Pandya dynasty** of Madurai, the third great early-medieval southern power, gets less coverage in standard histories than the Pallavas or Cholas because their inscriptional record is thinner and their architectural legacy got built over and rebuilt repeatedly through later centuries. But they were continuously powerful in the deep Tamil south — modern southern Tamil Nadu and parts of Kerala — for most of the medieval period. Their pearl fisheries on the **Gulf of Mannar** coast (between south India and Sri Lanka) had been famous since Roman times (Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 7) and remained one of the world's principal sources of pearls through the medieval and early-modern periods. Their patronage of Tamil literature was generous and continuous. The early phases of the **Meenakshi Temple** at Madurai — though most of the visible temple today is the result of much later Nayak-era construction in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries — go back to the Pandya period.

The **Cheras** of the Malabar coast (modern Kerala) were the third member of the ancient Tamil triumvirate, ruling the spice-producing west coast where pepper, cardamom, and ginger grew. Throughout the medieval period, Arab and Persian merchants dominated the Kerala spice trade with the Persian Gulf and Red Sea, and the Chera kings — who took the dynastic title **Kulasekhara** in their medieval phase — grew rich on customs revenues from the foreign merchant communities at Calicut, Quilon, and Cochin. The Chera kingdom is also the legendary setting for one of the more interesting religious establishments of the period: the **Cheraman Juma Mosque** at Methala in northern Kerala, traditionally said to have been founded around 629 CE by the Chera king Cheraman Perumal after he converted to Islam through contact with Arab traders. Modern scholars debate the founding date — the present mosque structure is later, and the conversion narrative has obvious legendary elements — but the consensus is that the Cheraman Juma is genuinely ancient and is probably the oldest mosque in the Indian subcontinent. It is also, importantly, a mosque founded peacefully through trade, not through Turkic conquest. The two distinct stories of Indian Islam — the peaceful trader's mosque on the Malabar coast, and the soldier's mosque built from temple rubble in Delhi — both have roots in the medieval period, and they have continued to coexist ever since.

---

The cumulative point of this chapter is that the Tamil south and the Deccan in the period 600–850 CE were doing the kind of monumental temple-building, philosophical-poetic flowering, and dynastic state-building that the textbook image associates with later medieval India — and they were doing it while the north was still working through its post-Gupta political problems. The Pallavas at Mahabalipuram were carving the Descent of the Ganges around 700 CE, a generation after Harsha's empire had collapsed. The Rashtrakutas were excavating the Kailasa Temple in 770 CE, while the post-Gupta political situation in the north was still patchwork. The Chera and Pandya ports were handling the pepper trade with the Arab world before the Arab armies had reached Sindh.

The center of gravity of medieval Indian civilization was, for the seventh and eighth centuries, in the south. It would shift northward again in the ninth century when the Pratiharas, Palas, and Rashtrakutas began the Tripartite Struggle for Kannauj — and then shift south again in the eleventh century when the Cholas built the Brihadeeswara Temple. The pattern of regional shifts in cultural and political weight is one of the underlying rhythms of the medieval period. There is rarely a single center; there is a moving center.

Cross-civ check: the period this chapter covers (~600–850 CE) is the great early-medieval southern flowering. Outside India, this is also the moment of **Tang China**'s peak — the Tang capital at Chang'an (modern Xi'an) was the largest city in the world around 750, with possibly a million inhabitants (Sui–Tang–Song Ch 2). It is the period of the **Umayyad** and early **Abbasid** caliphates expanding across Persia, Mesopotamia, North Africa, and Spain (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). It is the period of the **Carolingian** Frankish Empire under Charlemagne (crowned Holy Roman Emperor in 800, the same generation as Krishna I building Kailasa). It is the period of the **Maya Late Classic** at Copán and Tikal (Maya Civilization Ch 6). The medieval Indian south was operating in the same imperial-architectural league as Tang Chang'an and Abbasid Baghdad, and its monumental output — the Kailasa Temple, the Mahabalipuram complex — stands comparison with anything those contemporaries produced.

# Chapter 4 — The Tripartite Struggle for Kannauj

By the middle of the eighth century, three regional dynasties had emerged as the dominant powers of north India, and they spent the next two and a half centuries fighting a three-way war for control of one symbolic city — Kannauj on the upper Ganges. The war is conventionally called the **Tripartite Struggle**. The three contestants were the **Gurjara-Pratiharas** (goor-jah-rah-prah-tee-HAH-rahz) of Rajasthan and the western Ganges, the **Palas** (PAH-lahz) of Bengal and Bihar, and the **Rashtrakutas** of the Deccan. None of them ever held Kannauj long enough to translate dominance into a durable north-Indian empire. The chronic warfare exhausted all three by the end of the tenth century, leaving the field open for the Turkic invasions that began with Mahmud of Ghazni in 1001 (Chapter 8).

This is one of the medieval period's structural pathologies. Three roughly equal regional powers, each strong enough to defeat one of the other two but not strong enough to defeat both at once, fight a chronic three-way war for a prize none of them can actually hold. The cumulative effect over multiple generations is that all three weaken themselves and the political vacuum eventually gets filled by a fourth party from outside.

---

The **Gurjara-Pratihara** dynasty originated in modern Rajasthan, in the desert and semi-desert zone northwest of the Indo-Gangetic plain. The name **Gurjara** refers to a regional people of probably Central Asian origin who had settled in northwestern India between the fifth and seventh centuries and gradually been Hinduized. (Modern Indian historians differ on whether the Gurjaras were a tribe, a caste, or a regional designation; the surviving inscriptions are not specific.) The **Pratihara** branch traced descent from a mythological ancestor named Lakshmana (the brother of Rama in the Ramayana epic) and styled themselves as Kshatriya warriors and protectors of the Hindu social order against the Arab raids that had been pushing eastward out of Sindh since the Umayyad conquest in 711 CE.

The dynasty's founder was **Nagabhata I** (NAH-gah-BHAH-tah, ruled ~730–760), who repulsed Arab incursions into the Avanti region (modern western Madhya Pradesh) in the 730s and established the Pratihara state as the eastern bulwark against further Arab expansion. His successors gradually extended Pratihara power eastward across modern Rajasthan, Gujarat, and into the western Ganges plain.

The dynasty's greatest king was **Mihira Bhoja** (mih-HEE-rah BHOH-jah, ruled 836–885), whose long reign coincided with the peak Pratihara empire. Mihira Bhoja's territory at its peak extended from Sindh in the west to Bengal in the east and from the Himalayan foothills in the north to the Narmada River in the south — essentially all of north India outside the Pala-controlled east. His army was reportedly the strongest cavalry force in the subcontinent, partly because the Pratiharas controlled the western Indian horse trade with the Arab world and could continuously replenish their cavalry from imported stock.

The Arab geographer **Sulayman al-Tajir** ("Sulayman the Merchant"), who wrote around 851 CE based on his own travels in the Indian Ocean trade routes, described Mihira Bhoja's kingdom in unusually positive terms for an Arab observer reporting on a Hindu state. He praised the security of the Pratihara roads (a reliable indicator of effective royal control), the discipline of Mihira Bhoja's army, the strength of his cavalry, and — interestingly — the equity of his court justice. Sulayman's Arab merchant contemporaries operated extensively in Pratihara territory, and the Pratiharas seem to have welcomed the trade despite being theoretically the eastern frontier defenders against Arab military expansion. The two regimes maintained a workable relationship: the Pratiharas held the military line at the desert, the Arab merchants traded peacefully across it.

The Pratiharas captured Kannauj from the Maukhari successors around the late eighth century and held it through most of the ninth and tenth, making it their capital. At its zenith in the late ninth century, Kannauj under Pratihara rule was probably the largest and richest city in north India — a major center of Sanskrit learning, Hindu temple sponsorship, and luxury trade. It had also become, through Pratihara cultivation, the symbolic capital that any north Indian power needed to claim in order to be taken seriously as an emperor.

---

The **Pala dynasty** of Bengal and Bihar was the eastern competitor. The Palas emerged in the mid-eighth century from a chaotic political situation in the eastern Ganges delta — the post-Shashanka Bengal region had collapsed into civil war between rival warlords for nearly a century, with no stable government emerging. Around 750 CE, an assembly of leading Bengal nobles reportedly elected a man named **Gopala** (goh-PAH-lah) as king, partly in desperation to end the chronic warfare and partly because Gopala's personal reputation for courage and judgment was widely respected. The election story is reported in Pala inscriptions and in the Tibetan historian Taranatha's much later account; some modern scholars treat it as legendary, but the Pala dynasty's foundation was, at minimum, unusually consensual for a medieval Indian king-making.

Gopala's son **Dharmapala** (dahr-mah-PAH-lah, ruled ~781–821) was the real architect of Pala power. He extended Pala control across Bengal, Bihar, and parts of Odisha, and around 790 he attempted the ambitious move of capturing Kannauj from the Pratiharas — and succeeded, briefly. Pala sources describe a ceremony at Kannauj in which Dharmapala installed a client king (a Maukhari prince named Chakrayudha) on the Kannauj throne while subordinate kings from across north India attended in homage. This was the high-water mark of Pala power and the moment the Tripartite Struggle visibly began as a three-way contest. The Pratiharas under Nagabhata II soon counterattacked, recovered Kannauj, and the see-saw began.

Dharmapala's other major contribution was the founding of **Vikramashila Monastery**, the second great Pala-era Buddhist university (after Nalanda, which the Palas continued to patronize lavishly). Vikramashila — somewhere on the Ganges in modern eastern Bihar, the exact site is contested but probably near modern Bhagalpur — focused especially on Tantric Buddhist studies and produced many of the great Tantric scholars whose work would shortly migrate to Tibet (Chapter 7 for the slow disappearance of Indian Buddhism). Vikramashila operated for about four centuries, until the Turkic invasions destroyed it around 1200.

The Palas were unusual among medieval Indian dynasties in being formally Buddhist for most of their existence. Most Pala kings personally followed Mahayana Buddhism, sponsored Buddhist monasteries on a massive scale, and minted coinage with Buddhist iconography. They also patronized Hindu and Jain establishments, in line with the standard medieval Indian royal practice of pluralistic patronage, but their primary religious identity was Buddhist. The Pala period was the last great age of Indian Buddhism, and the Pala-era monastic universities were the institutional backbone that kept Indian Buddhism intellectually alive into the eleventh and twelfth centuries.

---

The **Rashtrakuta** dynasty (Chapter 3) was the third leg of the triangle, attacking Kannauj from the south. The Rashtrakutas had to cross the Vindhya Mountains and the Narmada River to reach the upper Ganges, which made their northern campaigns logistically difficult and chronically expensive — but they conducted them anyway, repeatedly, throughout the ninth and tenth centuries, because Kannauj was the prize that distinguished an emperor from a regional king.

The most successful Rashtrakuta king militarily was **Govinda III** (Chapter 3), who in a single campaign around 800 CE defeated both the Pala emperor Dharmapala and the Pratihara emperor Nagabhata II. Govinda then briefly captured Kannauj and installed his own client. He could not hold the city — his army's logistical situation in the upper Ganges plain was unsustainable, and he had to withdraw south to defend the Deccan from a Pallava attack — but the campaign demonstrated Rashtrakuta military superiority over both rivals at the same time.

Subsequent Rashtrakuta kings repeated the pattern. **Indra III** (in-DRAH, ruled ~914–928) sacked Kannauj around 916, defeating the Pratihara king Mahipala in the process. **Krishna III** (krish-NAH, ruled 939–967) led the most sweeping Rashtrakuta northern campaign of the tenth century, defeating both the Pratiharas and Palas and establishing temporary Rashtrakuta dominance over north India.

The fundamental problem the Rashtrakutas faced was the same problem the Palas and Pratiharas faced from their own directions: holding a distant capital by force required a permanent army of occupation that was ruinously expensive, and any prolonged absence from the dynasty's actual heartland invited revolt back home. The Rashtrakutas' Deccan-based capital at Manyakheta needed continuous attention. The Pratiharas' Rajasthan-Western-Ganges base needed continuous defense against Arab pressure. The Palas' Bengal-Bihar base was constantly threatened by smaller successor dynasties in the eastern frontier zones. None of the three could maintain Kannauj as a permanent imperial capital because none of the three could afford to leave its actual heartland undefended for long.

---

The Tripartite Struggle ground on until the late tenth century. By 982 CE, the Rashtrakuta dynasty had been overthrown by its own subordinates (the Western Chalukyas of Kalyani recovered the Deccan throne), the Pratiharas were in serious decline (Mihira Bhoja had been the last truly powerful Pratihara king, and his successors had been increasingly defeated by the rising Chandela and Chaulukya regional dynasties), and the Palas were facing increasingly assertive Sena rivals in Bengal. Kannauj, the prize all three had fought for, was under Pratihara rule but Pratihara power was a shadow of what Mihira Bhoja had wielded a century earlier.

Then in **1019 CE**, **Mahmud of Ghazni** (Chapter 8) sacked Kannauj. The Pratihara king Rajyapala fled the city without fighting — a humiliation his Chandela neighbor Vidyadhara avenged by killing him shortly afterward. Kannauj never recovered its political weight after the 1019 sack. The city physically continued to exist (and exists today as a moderate-sized town in western Uttar Pradesh) but it ceased to be the symbolic prize of Indian politics. Whatever the next great north Indian power was going to be, it would build its capital somewhere else.

The cumulative cost of two and a half centuries of three-way war was that all three competing dynasties had spent themselves. None of them had built the kind of durable institutional foundations — administrative system, professional army, fiscal apparatus — that could withstand serious external pressure. When Mahmud's mounted archers came south through the Khyber Pass in the early eleventh century, the regional dynasties of north India were not in a position to put up coordinated resistance. The Pratiharas were already collapsed. The Palas were preoccupied with the Senas. The Rashtrakutas were gone. The Rajput successor dynasties — Chandelas, Chaulukyas, Paramaras, Chauhans — fought Mahmud individually as he raided their territories and were individually defeated. None of them tried to organize a north-Indian coalition. None of them probably could have, given the chronic distrust that the long Tripartite Struggle had built up.

This is one of the genuinely tragic patterns of medieval Indian history. The two and a half centuries of wasted three-way war did not just fail to produce a stable north Indian empire. They actively eroded the political fabric that any future stable empire would need to build on. By the time the Turkic invasions came, the regional kingdoms had been fighting each other so habitually for so long that the idea of unified resistance had stopped being plausible.

---

Two more pieces of medieval north Indian history are worth setting down before we leave this chapter, because they happened in parallel with the Tripartite Struggle and shaped what came next.

The first is the rise of the **Chandela** Rajputs of Bundelkhand (the central plateau region of modern Madhya Pradesh, between the Yamuna and Narmada rivers). The Chandela dynasty emerged in the late ninth century as a vassal of the Pratiharas, then declared independence as Pratihara power waned in the tenth. Their capital was at **Khajuraho** (kah-joo-RAH-hoh), a small town in the Bundelkhand interior, and over a century from roughly 950 to 1050 CE they built one of the most spectacular temple complexes in Indian architectural history (Chapter 6 takes up Khajuraho in detail). The Chandelas were one of the few medieval Rajput dynasties to win a defensive engagement against Mahmud of Ghazni — Vidyadhara Chandela's army apparently caused Mahmud enough trouble in 1019 that the Ghaznavid sultan withdrew without a decisive battle.

The second is the rise of the **Chaulukyas** (or **Solankis**) of **Gujarat**, who emerged in the late tenth century at the expense of the declining Pratiharas. The Chaulukya capital was at **Anhilwara** (ahn-hil-WAH-rah, modern Patan). Their kingdom controlled the rich western Indian merchant ports at **Cambay**, **Bharuch**, and **Somnath** — making them probably the wealthiest north Indian dynasty of the eleventh century, since the Indian Ocean trade with the Arab world flowed through their territory. The Chaulukya kings were enthusiastic patrons of Jainism (a high proportion of the Gujarati merchant class was Jain, and the patronage was politically as well as religiously useful), and their support funded the marble-clad **Dilwara temples** on Mount Abu in modern Rajasthan — among the most exquisitely detailed examples of medieval Jain temple architecture anywhere.

The Chaulukyas, the Chandelas, the Paramaras of Malwa under King **Bhoja** (boh-JAH, the polymath Rajput king who became a Sanskrit cultural reference for the ideal scholar-king), the Tomaras of Delhi, the Chauhans of Ajmer — all of these are Rajput successor dynasties that filled the political space in north India after the Pratihara collapse. They are, collectively, the kings whose regional armies Mahmud of Ghazni and Muhammad of Ghor would face. None of them was strong enough to stop the Turkic invasions individually. None of them organized a coalition. The political fragmentation that had been the dominant feature of north India since the Gupta collapse was now, six centuries later, about to have its predictable consequences.

Cross-civ check: the Tripartite Struggle for Kannauj (~750–1000 CE) overlaps with the **Carolingian** division of Charlemagne's empire among his grandsons (Treaty of Verdun, 843), which produced a similar long-running multi-way war for control of the central Frankish territories that no single successor could decisively win — and which similarly created the political conditions for later Norse, Magyar, and Saracen raids to penetrate deep into formerly imperial territory. It also overlaps with the **An Lushan** rebellion (755–763) and the long Tang decline that followed (Sui–Tang–Song Ch 3), with the **Abbasid** weakening in Baghdad, with the **Macedonian Renaissance** in the Byzantine Empire (Byzantine Empire Ch 5), and with the early **Viking Age** raids across northern Europe (Viking Age Ch 2). The late ninth and tenth centuries were a period of imperial weakening in many of the major Eurasian civilizations simultaneously. The Indian version produced no Viking analog — no externally-based raiding power emerged to take advantage of the situation, until the Turkic invasions of the eleventh century. But the structural pattern is recognizable.

# Chapter 5 — The Chola Maritime Empire

While the north was exhausting itself in the Tripartite Struggle, the south was building south India's first true empire. The **Chola** dynasty had been extinct as a major power since the early centuries CE, when the ancient Tamil Cholas (familiar to readers of Tamil Sangam-era poetry, Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 7) had declined into local irrelevance. Around 848 CE, a regional chieftain named **Vijayalaya** (vi-jah-yah-LAH-yah) seized control of the small town of **Thanjavur** (THAHN-jah-voor, also spelled Tanjore) in the central Tamil region, claimed Chola descent, and founded what is now called the **medieval Chola** dynasty. Over the next two centuries, his successors built the most powerful state south India had ever seen — and one that projected naval power across the Bay of Bengal in a way no Indian state had attempted before or would attempt again until modern times.

The medieval Cholas peaked in the early eleventh century under two extraordinary kings, **Rajaraja Chola I** (rah-jah-RAH-jah, ruled 985–1014) and his son **Rajendra Chola I** (rah-JEN-drah, ruled 1014–1044). What they built in fifty-nine years is the central subject of this chapter.

---

The Chola revival began with conquest of the immediate Tamil neighborhood. Through the late ninth and tenth centuries, successive Chola kings absorbed the declining Pallavas (the Pallava dynasty was extinct by about 897), pushed back the Pandyas to a southern rump kingdom around Madurai, and gradually consolidated Tamil Nadu under a single throne. By the time **Parantaka I** (pah-RAHN-tah-kah, ruled 907–955) was on the throne, the Cholas were the dominant Tamil power. Parantaka extended Chola rule into Sri Lanka — an expansion that would matter enormously a century later — but suffered a serious defeat by the Rashtrakutas at the Battle of Takkolam around 949 that briefly checked Chola northward expansion.

Rajaraja came to the throne in **985 CE** and immediately started doing things on a different scale.

His military campaigns over the next twenty-nine years extended Chola rule across all of south India — the deep Tamil south (incorporating the Pandya kingdom), the western Chera coast in Kerala, the Karnataka highlands (taking territory from the Western Chalukyas of Kalyani), and the entire island of Sri Lanka (the Chola army captured the old Sinhala capital at **Anuradhapura** around 993 and held the northern half of the island as a Chola province). The Chola navy also conducted operations in the **Maldives**, projecting Chola power into the Indian Ocean for the first time. By the early eleventh century, Rajaraja ruled a state stretching from the Tungabhadra River in the north to Sri Lanka in the south and from the Arabian Sea to the Bay of Bengal — south India's first genuine empire.

Rajaraja was also a meticulous administrator. He commissioned the first detailed land survey of the Tamil country, recording field-by-field measurements, soil quality, irrigation status, and tax assessments. He standardized the Chola currency, the **kasu** copper coin and the **kalanju** gold weight. He restructured the provincial administration, dividing the empire into **mandalams** (provinces), **valanadus** (subdivisions), **nadus** (districts), and **kurrams** (sub-districts), with a clear hierarchy of officials and a documented appeals procedure for tax disputes. The medieval Chola administrative system was probably the most sophisticated of any Indian state of its period, and it was the institutional foundation that enabled what came next.

What came next was the **Brihadeeswara Temple at Thanjavur**, which Rajaraja consecrated in **1010 CE** to mark the twenty-fifth anniversary of his accession.

---

The **Brihadeeswara Temple** ("Great Lord's Temple," dedicated to Shiva) is, by general consensus, the apex of medieval south Indian architecture and one of the greatest single buildings of the medieval world.

The numbers, first. The central tower (**vimana**) rises **216 feet** in fourteen stepped stories, capped by a single 80-ton dome stone (which according to local tradition was hauled up a four-mile-long earthen ramp from a site outside the city, but the actual mechanism is reasonably contested). The temple is built almost entirely of **granite** — an estimated 130,000 tons of it, hauled from quarries at least thirty miles away (granite is not native to the Cauvery delta around Thanjavur; Rajaraja had it transported in deliberately, presumably for its durability and prestige value). The main sanctum houses a 12-foot-tall stone Shiva lingam (the abstract sculptural form representing Shiva), one of the largest in any Indian temple. The temple complex covers about 25 acres, surrounded by a double-walled enclosure with elaborate gateway towers (gopurams).

When Brihadeeswara was completed in 1010, its central vimana at 216 feet was probably the **tallest structure in India** and possibly the tallest Hindu temple in the world. (For context, the contemporary **Imperial Cathedral at Speyer** in the Holy Roman Empire, completed around 1060, was about 230 feet tall to the top of its towers — Speyer is slightly taller but was completed half a century later. **Old St. Paul's Cathedral** in London, built in the late eleventh century after the Norman Conquest and destroyed in the 1666 Great Fire, was eventually about 489 feet tall to its spire — but St. Paul's reached that height through later medieval and Tudor additions. Brihadeeswara at 1010 was, on a global scale, an exceptionally tall building.)

The architectural sophistication is more interesting than the raw height. The vimana's structural system is essentially a stone version of the Pallava-era stepped-pyramid form developed at Mahabalipuram (Chapter 3), scaled up by an order of magnitude. Each of the fourteen stories diminishes precisely as it rises, producing the temple's characteristic tapering profile. The interior is a hollow shaft with internal galleries that allow access to the upper levels — the temple is not a solid pile of stone but a carefully engineered hollow tower. The exterior is covered with sculptural niches at every level, each containing a bronze or stone deity figure, with detailed iconographic programs identifying each figure's mythological identity.

The temple also carries the most extensive surviving inscriptional record of any medieval Indian building. The granite walls are densely covered with Tamil inscriptions recording the original endowments — the lands assigned to support the temple, the personnel hired (from the chief priest down to the temple drummers and the women who bathed the deity), the gold and silver vessels donated for ritual use, the daily food allocations, the rules for tax-free temple lands, the procedures for replacing broken ceremonial objects. The Brihadeeswara inscriptions are one of the most detailed economic and administrative documents from any medieval institution anywhere in the world. They tell us, for example, that the temple supported about 600 personnel directly and was entitled to revenue from approximately 250 villages in the Cauvery delta. The temple was not just a religious monument; it was a major economic institution operating on a quasi-permanent basis with explicit royal endowments.

Brihadeeswara is a UNESCO World Heritage site — formally, "Great Living Chola Temples," a complex of three Chola-era temples (Brihadeeswara, plus the temple at Gangaikonda Cholapuram built by Rajendra and the Airavatesvara Temple built by Rajaraja II at Darasuram). The Brihadeeswara has been in continuous active use since 1010, has survived a millennium of Sri Lankan raids, Vijayanagar, Maratha, French, and British rule, and is still a functioning Hindu temple where daily worship continues exactly as Rajaraja's inscriptions specify. It is one of the few medieval architectural wonders of the world that you can still walk through on a normal Tuesday afternoon and watch in active operation.

---

Rajaraja's son **Rajendra Chola I** (ruled 1014–1044) inherited the throne and immediately set out to surpass his father's achievements.

His first major campaign was a land expedition across north India to the Ganges. This was unusual. South Indian kings rarely campaigned in the north — the logistical difficulty of marching an army across the Vindhya Mountains and the central Indian plateau was prohibitive — but Rajendra did it anyway, partly to demonstrate Chola power and partly to bring back symbolically charged Ganges water for a new temple ritual. The campaign in the late 1010s defeated the late Pala king Mahipala in Bengal (the political situation in the north was already chaotic enough by this point that Rajendra did not face coordinated resistance) and brought the Chola army to the Ganges itself. Rajendra had vessels of Ganges water carried back to the Cauvery delta, where he founded a new capital city named **Gangaikonda Cholapuram** (gahn-gai-KON-dah CHOH-lah-poo-rahm, literally "the Chola city that took the Ganges") and built a second great temple there, the **Brihadisvara Temple at Gangaikonda Cholapuram**, completed around 1030. The new temple was nearly as large as his father's at Thanjavur, with a slightly shorter but more decoratively elaborate vimana. The campaign earned Rajendra the title **Gangaikonda Chola** ("Chola who took the Ganges"), one of the more memorable royal epithets of medieval India.

Rajendra's second great campaign was the **naval expedition against Srivijaya** around **1025 CE**. This is the famous one. It is also one of the most extraordinary single military operations of the medieval world.

---

**Srivijaya** (sree-vee-JAH-yah) was a Buddhist maritime kingdom based in modern Sumatra (the great island in western Indonesia), with a capital probably at **Palembang** (PAH-lem-bang) and tributary control extending across the **Strait of Malacca** to the Malay Peninsula. Srivijaya had been the dominant maritime trading power in Southeast Asia since the seventh century, controlling the strategic chokepoint between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea through which all trade between India and China had to pass. Their navy patrolled the Strait of Malacca and the surrounding waters; their customs revenues from merchants passing through the Strait were the foundation of the kingdom's wealth.

Srivijaya was, in commercial terms, in an awkward symbiotic relationship with the Cholas. Tamil merchant guilds — most famously the **Ainurruvar** (eye-noo-ROO-var, "the Five Hundred"), a powerful merchant corporation operating across south India and Southeast Asia — used Srivijayan ports for their Bay of Bengal trade. Chola diplomatic missions had visited Srivijaya in the early eleventh century. The Srivijayan king had even, around 1005, sponsored the construction of a Buddhist temple at Nagapattinam (a Chola port on the Tamil coast) for the use of Buddhist pilgrims traveling between Sumatra and the south Indian Buddhist sites.

What turned this commercial partnership into war is unclear. The Chola inscriptions are propagandistic and not specific about causes; the Srivijayan side of the story is poorly documented. The most plausible reconstruction is that Srivijaya had begun extracting unfavorable tolls or trade restrictions from Tamil merchant guilds operating through the Strait of Malacca, and the Chola merchant community had requested royal intervention. There may also have been a religious dimension — Srivijaya was Buddhist and the Cholas were Shaivite, and Rajendra's reign was generally a high-water mark of Shaivite triumphalism. There was almost certainly also a sheer demonstration-of-power dimension. Whatever the precipitating cause, around 1025, Rajendra dispatched a Chola war fleet across the Bay of Bengal to attack Srivijaya directly.

The expedition crossed approximately **1,200 nautical miles** of open ocean — from the Tamil coast across to Sumatra — using monsoon wind patterns to make the crossing efficient. (The southwestern monsoon, blowing from late spring through early autumn, would carry sailing vessels eastward from India to Southeast Asia; the northeastern monsoon, blowing from late autumn through early spring, would carry them back. Indian Ocean trade had used these wind patterns for over a millennium by Rajendra's time, but military expeditions of this scale were unprecedented.) The Chola fleet captured Palembang, took the Srivijayan king prisoner, looted the royal treasury, and then proceeded to attack a series of additional Srivijayan tributary ports along the Malay Peninsula and the Sumatran coast. Chola inscriptions list fourteen captured cities, including modern **Kedah** in Malaysia and other ports across the region.

The expedition then returned home. The Cholas did not attempt to permanently occupy Srivijayan territory — that would have required sustaining an army across an ocean indefinitely, which was logistically impossible. What they accomplished was the demonstration of Chola naval power, the temporary disruption of Srivijayan trade dominance, and the establishment of a clear Chola sphere of commercial influence across the eastern Indian Ocean. Tamil merchant guilds operated unimpeded in Southeast Asia for the next century. Srivijaya recovered, but its dominance of the Strait of Malacca was permanently weakened, and successor states (the Khmer Empire on the mainland, Majapahit in Java) eventually absorbed the regional power.

The Chola raid on Srivijaya is **the only confirmed pre-modern Indian transoceanic military expedition**. No other ancient or medieval Indian state attempted anything comparable. The Mauryan, Gupta, and Mughal states all had powerful armies but never used them across open oceans on this scale. Rajendra's expedition was, by some distance, the single most ambitious naval operation any Indian state ever conducted before the modern period. The cross-civ comparison that helps is the **Athenian Sicilian Expedition** of 415 BCE — comparable in scale, comparable in ambition, comparable in the specific challenge of projecting force across open water — except that the Athenians lost catastrophically and the Cholas won. The other comparison is the early-fifteenth-century **voyages of Zheng He** under the Ming dynasty (Ming Dynasty), which were larger in personnel and ship count but were primarily diplomatic missions, not military expeditions. Rajendra's was a war fleet.

---

The Chola Empire continued to flourish for another century after Rajendra. **Kulottunga I** (koo-LOT-toon-gah, ruled 1070–1122) — actually a member of the Eastern Chalukya dynasty who inherited the Chola throne by marriage — extended Chola influence into the Andhra region, sent diplomatic embassies to Song China in 1077 (Tang–Song Ch 4), and presided over a long, prosperous, generally peaceful reign that Chola inscriptions remember as a golden age. The Tamil merchant guilds expanded their operations across maritime Southeast Asia. The Chola bronze sculpture tradition reached its mature peak, producing the iconic **Nataraja** (Shiva as Lord of the Dance) bronzes that now grace museum collections on every continent.

But the long Chola decline began with Kulottunga's successors in the twelfth century, accelerated under chronic warfare with the rising Hoysalas of Karnataka, and was effectively complete by 1279, when the last Chola king Rajendra III was overthrown by the Pandyas of Madurai. The Cholas were south India's first true empire and remained for centuries the cultural reference point — the standard by which subsequent Tamil dynasties (Vijayanagar, the Nayaks, the Marathas) measured themselves. Their bronze sculptures, their Brihadeeswara Temple, their merchant guild networks, and their naval reach across the Bay of Bengal became foundational elements of the Tamil cultural imagination that persist into the present.

Cross-civ check: the Chola peak under Rajaraja and Rajendra (985–1044) overlaps with the **Northern Song** dynasty in China (Tang-Song Ch 5), which was the wealthiest contemporary power and the destination of Chola diplomatic missions. It overlaps with the **Norman Conquest** of England (1066), with the **First Crusade** (1095–99, Byzantine Empire Ch 7 and Islamic Golden Age Ch 9), with the **Pala-Sena transition** in Bengal, with the **Khmer Empire** at Angkor under Suryavarman I and Suryavarman II (the latter would soon commission Angkor Wat), and with the **Fatimid Caliphate** in Cairo at its peak. The eleventh century is one of the genuinely dense moments of medieval Eurasian history — Norman cathedrals going up in England, Romanesque temples in northern Europe, Brihadeeswara in Tamil Nadu, the Hagia Sophia mosaics in Constantinople, and Khmer Bayon-style temples in Cambodia all overlapping. The medieval Chola world was not isolated. It was part of a connected eleventh-century cosmopolis stretching from London to Phnom Penh.

# Chapter 6 — Bhakti and the Temple Kingdoms

The Chola, Pratihara, Pala, and Rashtrakuta dynasties built the political frame. What filled the frame was a religious and cultural transformation that reshaped Hindu practice across the subcontinent and produced most of the temples on modern tourist itineraries. The transformation has two interrelated parts: the rise of the **Bhakti** (BHAHK-tee, literally "devotion") devotional movement, which reframed worship as personal love between devotee and deity, and the consolidation of the **temple** as the central institution of Indian religious, social, and economic life. Both processes were underway throughout the medieval period; both reached their first peak in the seventh through twelfth centuries; both would shape Hindu religious practice from the medieval period to the present.

---

The Bhakti movement began in the **Tamil south** in the seventh century with two parallel groups of poet-saints: the **Alvars** (AHL-vahrz), twelve devotees of Vishnu, and the **Nayanars** (nah-yah-NAHRZ), sixty-three devotees of Shiva. The two groups composed an enormous body of Tamil-language religious poetry between roughly 600 and 900 CE — the Vaishnava poetry collected in the **Divya Prabandham** (DIV-yah prah-BAHN-dahm, "Divine Compositions") and the Shaiva poetry collected in the **Tirumurai** (tih-roo-MOO-rai, "Sacred Texts"). The combined corpus runs to thousands of hymns. It is one of the great corpora of religious literature in any language.

What made the Bhakti poetry distinctive — and revolutionary in the context of contemporary Hindu practice — was its theological stance.

Mainstream Hindu worship in the early medieval period was conducted primarily through priestly ritual. **Brahmin** (BRAH-min) priests, the hereditary priestly caste, performed elaborate Sanskrit-language ceremonies on behalf of the laity, who attended but did not personally interact with the deity. The deity was approached through ritual purity, correct procedure, prescribed offerings, and the mediating expertise of the priest. Worship was a transactional relationship — you sponsored the ritual, the priest performed it, the deity received it, and merit accrued to you in measurable quantities. The system was elaborate, expensive, and accessible primarily to the well-off.

The Bhakti poets attacked this entire framework. Their poetry insisted that the deity was directly approachable by any devotee who loved him sincerely — without priestly mediation, without elaborate ritual, without Sanskrit learning, without caste qualifications. The Tamil Alvar **Nammalvar** (NAHM-AHL-vahr, ninth century, the most important of the twelve Alvars) wrote thousands of verses in vernacular Tamil addressed directly to Vishnu in tones of intimate personal love — sometimes as a child to a parent, sometimes as a lover to a beloved, sometimes as a friend to a friend, sometimes as a devotee to a master. The Shaiva Nayanar **Sambandar** (SAHM-bahn-dahr, seventh century) composed Tamil hymns to Shiva at hundreds of specific temple sites, recording his pilgrimages around the Tamil country and his personal devotional experiences at each. The Bhakti poets did not deny that priestly ritual had value, but they insisted that personal devotion was the higher path and was available to anyone willing to cultivate it.

The most striking single example is **Andal** (AHN-dahl, ninth century), the only female Alvar. Her two surviving works — the **Tiruppavai** (tih-roo-PAH-vai, a sequence of thirty Tamil hymns ostensibly describing a religious vow undertaken by young women in the month of Margali) and the **Nachiar Tirumozhi** (NAH-chee-yar tih-roo-MOH-zhee) — are sustained love poems addressed to Vishnu in the voice of a young woman who is in love with the deity and wishes to be married to him. Tradition holds that Andal was so devoted to Vishnu that she refused all human husbands and was finally married to the deity directly at the temple of Srirangam (where the local tradition reports that her body merged into the deity's image). The Tiruppavai is still recited every December in Tamil and Telugu temples; Andal is still considered, in the Sri Vaishnava tradition, one of the most exemplary devotees in all of religious history.

The Bhakti movement was therefore, among other things, an early egalitarian challenge to caste-bound religious hierarchy. Several of the most important Bhakti poets came from low-caste backgrounds. **Tiruppan Alvar** was a member of the Panar caste of bards (a low caste in the social hierarchy of medieval Tamil society), and his hymn praising Vishnu at Srirangam is one of the most cherished Vaishnava devotional texts. **Tirumular**, the author of the great Shaiva work **Tirumantiram**, was a non-Brahmin sage. **Karaikkal Ammaiyar** was a married woman who became a Shaiva ascetic. The Bhakti tradition explicitly held that caste and gender were irrelevant to devotional capacity — the deity loved the devotee, not the social category.

(This egalitarian dimension can be overstated. The Bhakti movement did not actually overthrow caste, and many Bhakti traditions in subsequent centuries gradually accommodated themselves to caste hierarchy. But the founding poetic moment was, on the surviving evidence, genuinely radical for its period, and this radical streak would continue to surface in Bhakti revivals through the medieval and early-modern periods. The fifteenth-century weaver-saint **Kabir** in north India and the seventeenth-century cobbler-saint **Ravidas** are direct theological descendants of the early Tamil Bhakti tradition.)

The Bhakti movement spread northward over centuries. In Karnataka, the twelfth-century **Lingayat** (LIN-gah-yaht) tradition founded by **Basava** (BAH-sah-vah) carried Bhakti devotion into Kannada-language religious literature with a similarly egalitarian theological streak. In Maharashtra, the thirteenth-century **Varkari** tradition (centered on the temple at Pandharpur and the saints **Jnanadeva**, **Namdev**, and later **Tukaram**) developed a Marathi-language devotional poetry around the deity Vithoba (a regional form of Krishna). In Bengal, **Jayadeva**'s twelfth-century Sanskrit poem **Gita Govinda** brought a fully developed Bhakti aesthetic into elite Sanskrit literature. In north India proper, the medieval Bhakti tradition would later (fifteenth century onward) produce **Mirabai** of Rajasthan, **Kabir** of Banaras, **Tulsidas** of Awadh, and **Surdas** of Braj. The vernacular devotional traditions of every Indian regional language ultimately trace back to the seventh-century Tamil beginning.

---

The other half of the medieval transformation was the consolidation of the temple as institution.

This is genuinely new in scale. Vedic religion had no temples; worship was conducted at fire altars in open enclosures (Vedic Period Ch 3). Buddhism worshipped at stupas (relic mounds) and in monastery compounds, not in temples in the later Hindu sense. Early Hindu temples, from the Gupta era and earlier, were modest structures — single-cell sanctuaries with small porches, sometimes built of brick or wood and only later replaced with stone. The really big temples — the multi-acre complexes with multiple sanctums, surrounding cloisters, ritual bathing tanks, processional halls, and elaborate towers — are a medieval-period development.

The medieval Indian temple, at its most fully realized form, is several institutions at once.

It is **a religious sanctum**. The central garbhagriha ("womb-house") contains the principal deity image, accessed through specific ritual procedures by qualified priests. Daily worship sequences (aratis, abhishekas, prasada distributions) follow procedures often documented in inscriptions on the temple walls. Major festivals — the deity's birthday, the spring or autumn festivals tied to the agricultural calendar, the deity's processional outings on chariot-floats — bring tens or hundreds of thousands of pilgrims through the complex annually.

It is **a major economic institution**. Royal endowments granted villages, lands, and revenue rights to support the temple's operations. The temple paid for its own staff (priests, drummers, dancers, cooks, scribes, security, maintenance workers — sometimes hundreds of people), its own ritual supplies (flowers, food, oil, cloth, sandalwood, gold and silver vessels), and its own ongoing construction and repair. Brihadeeswara Temple's inscriptions document the scale: roughly 600 personnel directly supported by the temple, 250 villages providing the supporting revenue, an annual budget on the order of tens of thousands of kalanjus of gold. A medieval temple at this scale was operating a significant fraction of the local economy.

It is **a community center**. The temple complex housed pilgrim hostels, kitchens that distributed food daily to pilgrims and the poor, schools for Brahmin students learning Sanskrit, music and dance schools for the temple's resident performers (the **devadasis**, female temple dancers — a deeply complicated medieval institution), and assembly halls for political and judicial proceedings. Town meetings, royal proclamations, marriage ceremonies for the local elite, and major commercial transactions all happened at or near the temple.

It is **a piece of dynastic propaganda**. The king who built the temple (or his descendants who completed it) expected the inscription on the entrance pylon to credit him by name. The temple's tower, visible from miles away, was a permanent visual statement of royal piety and wealth. Plundering or destroying an enemy's principal temple, or conversely sponsoring a major restoration after a victory, was understood across medieval India as an act of high political symbolism — the religious equivalent of capturing or recapturing a national capital.

The medieval temple was, in short, the central institution around which medieval Indian society organized itself. The cathedral comparison from Western Europe is partly apt — the Gothic cathedrals were also religious-economic-civic institutions on a comparable scale — but the medieval Indian temple complex was usually denser in personnel, more economically productive, and more central to local political life than the European equivalent. Brihadeeswara at Thanjavur, Khajuraho's Kandariya Mahadeva, the Lingaraja at Bhubaneswar, the Belur and Halebidu Hoysala temples — these are the institutions through which medieval Indian society lived.

---

The single best place to understand medieval temple architecture in concentrated form is **Khajuraho** (kah-joo-RAH-hoh), the Chandela dynasty's capital region in modern Madhya Pradesh. The Chandelas built **eighty-five temples** at Khajuraho between roughly 950 and 1050 CE, of which **about twenty-five survive** in good condition. They are the canonical examples of the **Nagara** north Indian temple style at its mature peak — a square sanctum surmounted by a curvilinear shikhara tower that rises in a single sweeping curve, often with smaller subsidiary towers (urusringas) clustered around the main one to create a mountain-like aggregate profile.

The largest and most accomplished of the Khajuraho temples is the **Kandariya Mahadeva** ("Cave-of-Shiva of the Great Lord"), built around 1030 CE under the Chandela king Vidyadhara. It stands 102 feet tall. Its central shikhara is composed of eighty-four miniature shikhara peaks aggregated into the main tower's profile, producing the characteristic visual effect of a stylized Himalayan mountain range. Every external surface of the temple is densely covered with sculptural figures — gods, goddesses, attendant deities, celestial beings, dancers, animals, and (notoriously) the **erotic sculptures** that have given Khajuraho its global reputation.

The erotic sculptures are worth pausing on, because they have been the subject of more popular misunderstanding than almost any other aspect of medieval Indian art. Approximately 10% of the Khajuraho external sculptures depict explicitly sexual scenes — couples, groups, sometimes elaborate compositions involving multiple figures and various coupling positions. The remaining 90% of the sculptural program is religious iconography, divine attendants, courtly scenes, and decorative figures with no sexual content. The erotic figures appear primarily on the external walls in specific architectural zones (most often around the entrance and on the southern face), not at the deity sanctum, and they share visual conventions with broader genres of medieval Indian sculpture that depict the full range of human experience.

The interpretive question is what these sculptures meant in their original religious-architectural context. Multiple theories exist. One emphasizes the Tantric current within medieval Hinduism, which held that the energies of human sexuality could be channeled toward spiritual realization through specific ritual practices — the erotic sculptures may depict the Tantric rituals or the metaphysical principles those rituals invoked. A second emphasizes the auspiciousness of the sexual couple (**maithuna**) in Hindu iconographic tradition, which long predates Khajuraho — the union of male and female principles is an ancient Hindu symbolic frame for cosmic creativity, and erotic figures appear on Hindu temples back to the early medieval period as auspicious motifs. A third emphasizes the broader medieval Indian literary tradition of treating sexual pleasure as one of the four legitimate goals of human life (the others being **dharma** — moral duty; **artha** — material prosperity; and **moksha** — spiritual liberation; together called the **purusharthas**). Sexual desire was a religiously sanctioned dimension of human experience, and depicting it was not regarded as obscene in the medieval framework. The Vatsyayana **Kama Sutra** had been composed in the Gupta era as a serious technical treatise on these matters (Gupta Empire Ch 5), and its assumptions about the legitimacy of erotic life were broadly shared in medieval literate culture.

What is clear is that the Khajuraho sculptures were not regarded as transgressive by their original Chandela patrons. They were carved openly, on the most prestigious temples in the dynasty's capital, by master sculptors working in standard workshops, with the full sponsorship of the ruling family. Whatever embarrassment the sculptures have caused subsequent observers (especially during the British colonial period, when Victorian moralism collided abruptly with medieval Indian sculptural traditions and produced both prurient fascination and considerable censorship) is a function of those later observers, not of the medieval understanding.

The Khajuraho temples are a UNESCO World Heritage site. Like Brihadeeswara, they are still actively used for Hindu worship and are accessible for tourists today. They are also, despite the dramatic external sculpture, primarily experienced as serious religious monuments by the great majority of pilgrims who visit them.

---

The other great medieval temple-building dynasties of north and central India deserve at least brief mention.

The **Eastern Ganga** dynasty of Kalinga (modern Odisha) built the great temples on the Bay of Bengal coast — the **Lingaraja** at Bhubaneswar (eleventh century, the largest medieval temple in eastern India), the **Jagannath Temple** at Puri (twelfth century, one of the most sacred Hindu pilgrimage sites in eastern India), and the spectacular **Sun Temple at Konark** (mid-thirteenth century, just past the medieval period proper, but the climactic work of the Kalinga temple tradition). The **Konark Sun Temple** was built by King **Narasimhadeva I** as a colossal stone chariot of the sun god Surya — twelve carved wheels representing the months of the year, seven horses representing the days of the week, and a sanctum that originally rose perhaps 230 feet before its tower collapsed in unclear circumstances centuries later. The temple's surviving sculptural program is one of the great achievements of medieval Indian art, and the partial ruin (the main tower is gone, but the assembly hall, the carved wheels, the sun god statues, and the elaborate external sculpture all survive) is one of the most photographed structures in India. UNESCO World Heritage.

The **Hoysala** dynasty of southern Karnataka (eleventh through thirteenth centuries) built their characteristic star-shaped sanctuaries with dense soapstone friezes covering every external surface. The most famous Hoysala temples — **Chennakesava** at **Belur** (consecrated 1117 by King Vishnuvardhana to commemorate his military victory over the Cholas), **Hoysaleswara** at **Halebidu** (twelfth century, the dynasty's grandest temple), and **Keshava** at **Somnathpur** (1268, the most complete surviving Hoysala temple) — are the most ornately sculptured medieval Indian buildings, with thousands of figures carved into each square meter of external surface using the soft locally-quarried soapstone that allowed extraordinary fineness of detail.

The **Chaulukyas** of Gujarat sponsored the marble-clad **Dilwara temples** on Mount Abu — five Jain temples built between roughly 1031 and the thirteenth century, the most important being the **Vimal Vasahi** (built 1031 under the merchant-minister Vimal Shah) and the **Luna Vasahi** (built 1230). The Dilwara temples are technically Jain rather than Hindu, but they belong to the same medieval tradition of dynastic temple sponsorship and architectural elaboration. Their interior carving — every column, ceiling panel, and door frame densely worked with detailed figural and floral sculpture in white marble — is, by general consensus, the most exquisitely refined stonework of medieval India.

The **Western Chalukyas** of Kalyani (Chapter 4) developed the Vesara hybrid style at sites including the great temple at **Lakkundi** in modern Karnataka. Their work was the immediate aesthetic predecessor of the Hoysala style.

In the deep south, the Pandyas continued building and rebuilding the Madurai **Meenakshi Temple** complex through the medieval period (most of the visible structure today is later, especially Nayak-era from the seventeenth century, but the medieval Pandya phases established the basic plan and dedicated the temple to the goddess Meenakshi as the principal local form of the divine). The **Sri Ranganathaswamy** Temple at **Srirangam** (an island in the Cauvery River near modern Tiruchirappalli, the most important Vaishnava pilgrimage site in south India) was developed across centuries by the Cholas, Pandyas, and Vijayanagar dynasties into one of the largest temple complexes in the world by area.

This is, taken together, the visual signature of medieval India. Almost every large stone temple you can see in a tourist photograph today is medieval. The temple-building tradition continued under later dynasties (Vijayanagar in the south through the sixteenth century, the Nayaks in the seventeenth, various smaller polities into the eighteenth) but the core of the tradition — the developed architectural styles, the elaborate sculptural programs, the institutional template of the temple-as-economic-and-civic-center — is medieval.

Cross-civ check: the temple-building peak of medieval India (~950–1250) overlaps almost exactly with the **Romanesque** and early **Gothic** cathedral-building eras in Western Europe. Speyer Cathedral was completed around 1060. The Cluny Abbey reached its largest extent around 1100. Saint-Denis (the first true Gothic) was begun in 1135. Notre-Dame de Paris was begun in 1163. Chartres was rebuilt after 1194. Salisbury Cathedral was begun in 1220. The architectural monumentalism of the high medieval period is global — Hindu India, Catholic Europe, Buddhist Burma (Pagan), and Khmer Cambodia (Angkor) are all building extraordinary religious monuments simultaneously, with no causal connection between them but with strikingly similar institutional logics. Religious monumentalism was, apparently, what state-sponsored civilizations produced as their characteristic high-medieval cultural artifact.

# Chapter 7 — Vedanta, Jainism, and the Slow Disappearance of Buddhism

The medieval period was the great age of Indian philosophical synthesis. Three philosophers — **Adi Shankara** in the eighth century, **Ramanuja** in the late eleventh and early twelfth, and **Madhva** in the late thirteenth — each built a complete metaphysical system out of the older Upanishadic and Vedic textual tradition (Vedic Period Ch 5), and the systems they built defined the dominant strand of Hindu intellectual life for the next millennium and beyond. In parallel, Jainism flourished under royal patronage in Karnataka and Gujarat, producing some of the most refined religious literature and architecture of the period. And Buddhism, the religion that had defined Indian intellectual life for fifteen centuries, slowly disappeared from the land of its birth.

This chapter is mostly about ideas. The architecture and the politics happen in adjacent chapters; this one is the philosophical heart of the medieval Indian world.

---

To understand what Shankara and his successors were doing, you have to understand what they were inheriting.

The core texts of mainstream medieval Hindu philosophy were a body of writings called the **Vedanta** (veh-DAHN-tah, literally "end of the Vedas"). The texts have three primary sources, called collectively the **prasthanatrayi** ("triple foundation"):

The **Upanishads** (oo-PAH-nih-shahdz), a body of philosophical dialogues composed between roughly 800 and 200 BCE, in the late Vedic and post-Vedic period (Vedic Period Ch 5). The Upanishads explore questions about the nature of ultimate reality (**Brahman**, brah-MAHN, the cosmic principle underlying all existence), the nature of the individual self (**Atman**, AHT-mahn), and the relationship between them. The major Upanishads — the **Brihadaranyaka**, the **Chandogya**, the **Katha**, the **Mundaka**, the **Mandukya**, and others — contain some of the deepest and most sophisticated philosophical writing of the ancient world, and they are open-ended enough that they can be read in support of multiple theological positions.

The **Bhagavad Gita** (BHAH-gah-vad GEE-tah, "Song of the Lord"), an interpolated philosophical episode in the Mahabharata epic, composed in roughly its present form between the second century BCE and the second century CE (Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 1). The Gita is a long dialogue between the warrior-prince Arjuna and his charioteer Krishna (who is revealed mid-dialogue to be the supreme deity in human form) on questions of ethical action, devotion, and ultimate liberation. Like the Upanishads, the Gita is open enough to support multiple theological readings.

The **Brahma Sutras** (BRAH-mah SOO-trahz), a terse and highly compressed text of 555 aphorisms attributed to a sage named **Badarayana** (bah-DAH-rah-yah-nah), probably composed between the second century BCE and the fourth century CE, that attempts to systematize the philosophical content of the Upanishads. The Brahma Sutras are so terse that they are essentially unreadable without a commentary — and the commentaries on the Brahma Sutras by later Vedanta philosophers became the canonical statements of the rival philosophical schools.

What the medieval Vedanta philosophers did was write commentaries on these three foundational sources, each commentator arguing that his interpretation was the correct one and the rivals' interpretations were wrong. The commentaries are book-length philosophical treatises in their own right. The interpretive disagreements among them turn out to involve some of the deepest questions about the nature of reality, the self, and the divine that any philosophical tradition has ever addressed.

---

**Adi Shankara** (AH-dee SHAHN-kah-rah) was the first and most consequential of the medieval Vedanta commentators. He lived for about thirty-two years — the traditional dates are 788–820 CE, though the actual dates have been debated and an alternative dating to the early seventh century has been argued by some scholars. He was born in **Kalady** in Kerala, traveled across India debating philosophical opponents, and died at **Kedarnath** in the Himalayan foothills.

His philosophical position is called **Advaita Vedanta** (ad-VAI-tah veh-DAHN-tah, literally "non-dual Vedanta"). The core claim is that **Brahman** (ultimate reality) and **Atman** (the individual self) are not two but one. The apparent multiplicity of the world — the apparent existence of separate selves, separate objects, separate processes — is a kind of cognitive illusion (**maya**, MAH-yah, "illusion" or more technically "superimposition") that arises from ignorance (**avidya**, ah-VID-yah, "non-knowledge"). When the underlying ignorance is overcome, the apparent multiplicity is recognized as illusory and the underlying unity is realized directly. **Liberation** (**moksha**, MOH-kshah, "release") is not a state attained by ritual or by accumulating merit but a recognition of what has always been the case — the individual self is, and always was, identical with ultimate reality.

This is a radical position. It implies that the world we ordinarily experience — with its distinct objects, its causation, its time and space — has only a kind of provisional or pragmatic reality, like the apparent existence of a snake when you misperceive a coiled rope in dim light. The snake never existed; you just thought it did. Similarly, on Shankara's reading, the multiplicity of the world never existed in the deepest sense; we just thought it did because we were ignorant.

It is also a position that aligns Shankara with certain strands of Mahayana Buddhism — particularly the **Madhyamaka** ("middle way") school developed by **Nagarjuna** (nah-GAHR-joo-nah) in the second century CE (Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 5), which had argued that all phenomena are empty of inherent existence (**shunyata**, SHOON-yah-tah). Shankara's Hindu critics, both in his own time and afterwards, accused him of being a "crypto-Buddhist" — taking Madhyamaka conclusions and dressing them in Vedantic language. Shankara's response was that he was correctly interpreting the Upanishads, which had said all this long before the Buddhists got around to it. The accusation is interesting partly because Shankara's philosophical maturity coincided with the late phase of organized Indian Buddhism and may have contributed to Buddhism's eventual absorption into Hindu philosophical frameworks. On Shankara's reading, the Buddhists had been roughly right about the illusory nature of conventional reality but had drawn the wrong final conclusion (annihilation rather than identity with Brahman). Hinduism could thus claim everything Buddhism had to offer and more.

Shankara's other contributions were institutional. He founded **four monastic centers** (**mathas**, MAH-thahz) at the four geographic corners of India: **Sringeri** in the south (Karnataka), **Dvaraka** in the west (Gujarat), **Jagannath Puri** in the east (Odisha), and **Jyotirmath** in the north (Uttarakhand). The four mathas continue to operate today and are headed by lineal successors of Shankara called **Shankaracharyas**, who function as the highest religious authorities of the Smarta Brahmin tradition that traces itself to Shankara. The four-corner placement of the mathas was deliberate symbolic geography: it made the institutional Vedanta tradition pan-Indian rather than regional.

Shankara also reformed the practice of Hindu monasticism by establishing the **Dashanami** (dah-shah-NAH-mee, "ten names") order of Hindu sannyasis (renouncers) — providing an organized structure for Hindu monastics that paralleled the Buddhist sangha and that gave Hinduism a permanent institutional monastic presence it had previously lacked. The Dashanamis still exist; they are a major component of the Hindu religious establishment.

Shankara is, by some distance, the most consequential Indian philosopher of the medieval period. The intellectual framework he built has remained the dominant theoretical position in Hindu philosophical discussions for over a thousand years. When modern Indians think about the relationship between the individual soul and the divine, they are usually working — knowingly or not — within Shankara's framework.

---

The first major reaction against Shankara came from **Ramanuja** (rah-MAH-noo-jah, ~1077–1157 CE), the great philosopher of the **Sri Vaishnava** (shree VAI-shnah-vah) tradition, based at the temple at **Srirangam** in the Tamil south.

Ramanuja was uncomfortable with Shankara's monism for both theological and devotional reasons. Theologically, he did not accept that the apparent multiplicity of the world was illusory; he thought the world was real, that individual souls were real, that God was real, and that they were all in genuine relationship to each other rather than collapsing into a single undifferentiated unity. Devotionally — and Ramanuja was a leading inheritor of the Tamil Bhakti tradition (Chapter 6) — he thought Shankara's framework eliminated the possibility of love between devotee and deity. If the devotee and the deity are ultimately identical (as Shankara held), then the love is in some sense self-love, and the rich devotional tradition of the Alvars and Nayanars becomes a temporary stage on the path to a final non-dual realization rather than the ultimate goal in itself.

Ramanuja's alternative position is called **Vishishtadvaita** (vih-shish-tahd-VAI-tah, "qualified non-dualism"). He held that ultimate reality is the personal God Vishnu, that individual souls are real and distinct from God but eternally united with him in the way attributes are united with a substance, and that liberation consists not of dissolving into Brahman but of an eternal devotional relationship between the individual soul and Vishnu. The technical metaphysics is dense — Ramanuja worked out elaborate accounts of the relationship between God, souls, and matter that read as serious philosophical treatises — but the practical upshot is that personal devotion to a personal deity is the highest spiritual path, and that the personal devotee retains his or her individuality eternally rather than dissolving into divine unity.

Ramanuja's philosophical school provided the intellectual foundation for the Vaishnava devotional tradition that has flourished across south India ever since — and that would later spread to north India through later Vaishnava saints (**Chaitanya** in fifteenth-century Bengal, the various Krishna-bhakti movements of the medieval and early-modern Hindi-speaking regions). Most modern Indian religious practice that emphasizes personal devotion to a personal deity is operating within a theological framework Ramanuja systematized.

---

The third great medieval Vedanta philosopher was **Madhvacharya** (MAHD-vah-CHAR-yah, ~1238–1317 CE), who took the reaction against Shankara even further than Ramanuja had.

Madhva was based at **Udupi** in coastal Karnataka and founded the school called **Dvaita Vedanta** (DVAI-tah, "dualist Vedanta"). His position was that God (Vishnu), individual souls, and the material world are three permanently distinct categories of reality — the strict opposite of Shankara's monism. Souls and God are eternally distinct; souls do not become God under any circumstances; salvation consists of the soul's eternal blissful subordination to God, never of identification with God. Even in liberation, the boundary between divine and human remains absolute.

Madhva was also the only major Indian philosopher to systematically reject the doctrine that all souls are ultimately destined for liberation. He argued, controversially, that some souls are eternally damned — destined for a kind of ongoing hell — and that liberation is not universally available. This is closer to certain strands of Christian theology than to mainstream Hindu thought, and the parallel has not gone unnoticed. (Some scholars have argued for direct Christian influence on Madhva via the Syriac Christian community on the Malabar coast, though the evidence is circumstantial. Most scholars now think Madhva developed the position independently.) The Madhva tradition centered on Udupi remains the third major Vedanta school, less widespread than the Shankara or Ramanuja schools but very influential in coastal Karnataka and parts of the Marathi-speaking region.

Together, **Shankara, Ramanuja, and Madhva** constitute the three great medieval Vedanta schools. They span the full philosophical spectrum from radical monism to radical dualism, with Ramanuja's qualified non-dualism in the middle. They define the conceptual landscape that Indian philosophical discussion has inhabited ever since, and the language they developed — Brahman and Atman, maya and avidya, jiva and Ishvara, dvaita and advaita — is the standard vocabulary of Hindu metaphysical discourse to this day.

---

In parallel with the Vedanta schools, **Jainism** flourished under royal patronage across medieval Karnataka and Gujarat.

Jainism is one of the ancient Indian religious traditions, founded (in the standard narrative) by **Mahavira** in the sixth century BCE — roughly contemporary with the Buddha — though Jain tradition holds that Mahavira was the twenty-fourth and last in a long line of teachers (**tirthankaras**, "ford-makers") stretching back into mythological antiquity. Jainism's distinctive doctrines include:

A radical commitment to **non-violence** (**ahimsa**, ah-HIM-sah), extending not just to humans and animals but to insects and (in some Jain traditions) even microscopic organisms. Strict Jain monks sweep the path before them as they walk to avoid stepping on insects, strain water before drinking to avoid swallowing tiny organisms, and (in the strictest tradition) eat no root vegetables because pulling them up kills the plant.

A doctrine of **karma** as a quasi-physical substance that adheres to the soul and weighs it down — reincarnation continues until karma is purged through ascetic discipline. Liberation is achieved by burning off accumulated karma through extreme asceticism, especially fasting (in the most extreme Jain tradition, **sallekhana**, the ritual death by fasting at the end of life).

A pluralistic epistemology (**anekantavada**, ah-NEH-kahn-tah-VAH-dah, "the doctrine of many-sidedness") that holds that reality is too complex to be captured by any single perspective — multiple partial views can each be valid in their own context.

Jainism split early in its history into two main branches: the **Digambara** (dig-AHM-bah-rah, "sky-clad") branch, whose male monks practice nudity as a form of detachment from possessions; and the **Shvetambara** (shveh-TAHM-bah-rah, "white-clad") branch, whose monks wear white robes. The two branches differ on doctrinal points (the Digambaras hold that women cannot achieve liberation in their current bodies; the Shvetambaras disagree) and operate on largely separate territorial bases. The Digambaras predominated in Karnataka and central India; the Shvetambaras predominated in Gujarat, Rajasthan, and the western Indian merchant communities.

Throughout the medieval period, Jainism flourished under the patronage of regional dynasties whose courts were sympathetic. The **Western Ganga** dynasty of Karnataka was the most important Digambara patron — under their minister **Chamundaraya**, the **Gomateshwara** monolithic statue of the Jain saint Bahubali was carved at **Shravanabelagola** (shrah-vah-nah-bel-LAH-go-lah) in 981 CE. The statue stands **57 feet tall**, carved from a single granite block, and is the tallest free-standing monolithic statue in India. The ritual bathing of the statue (**Mahamastakabhisheka**) is held once every twelve years and remains one of the most spectacular religious events in modern India.

The **Hoysalas** continued Western Ganga patronage of Jainism in Karnataka. The **Chaulukyas** of Gujarat heavily patronized Shvetambara Jainism — most importantly through the **Dilwara temples** on Mount Abu (Chapter 6), the most exquisitely refined Jain temple architecture anywhere. The Chaulukya minister **Vimal Shah** built the first of the Dilwara temples in 1031 CE; subsequent temples in the complex were funded by other Jain merchant patrons through the medieval period.

Jainism's medieval flourishing was helped by its strong association with the merchant class. Jain ethical doctrines — non-violence, vegetarianism, business honesty, frugality, charitable giving — produced a distinctive merchant culture that accumulated capital efficiently and reinvested it in temple sponsorship and community charity. The Jain merchant communities of Gujarat and Rajasthan would become, over subsequent centuries, the most economically sophisticated trading networks in India. Modern Indian commercial dynasties from the Marwari Jain trading families to the Birlas and the Adanis trace their commercial cultures back to medieval Jain origins.

---

And then there was Buddhism, slowly disappearing.

The disappearance of Buddhism from India is one of the genuinely puzzling phenomena of medieval Indian history. Buddhism had been the dominant religious tradition of north India through the Maurya, Kushan, and Gupta periods. The great Buddhist monastic universities at Nalanda, Vikramashila, and Odantapuri operated continuously through the entire medieval period until the Turkic destruction around 1200. Chinese pilgrims (Faxian in the early fifth century, Xuanzang in the seventh, Yijing in the late seventh, Wu Kong in the eighth) all reported flourishing Buddhist communities across north India. The Pala dynasty patronized Mahayana Buddhism on a massive scale through the entire ninth, tenth, and eleventh centuries.

And yet by 1300 CE, Buddhism had effectively vanished from India. A handful of small Buddhist communities persisted in the Himalayan foothills (where Tibetan Buddhism was a continuing presence) and along parts of the Bay of Bengal coast. The major monasteries were gone. The lay Buddhist community had been reabsorbed into Hindu practice. The religion that had defined Indian intellectual life for fifteen centuries was no longer a significant presence in the subcontinent.

Multiple factors contributed, in proportions historians still debate:

**Royal patronage shift**. As medieval kings shifted their religious sponsorship from monasteries to temples, the financial foundation of organized Buddhism eroded. Buddhist monasteries had depended on royal endowments to support their large monastic populations; without continuing royal sponsorship, the monastic establishments shrank and their intellectual leadership migrated elsewhere (most importantly to Tibet, where the Buddhism-centered political establishment was happy to receive Indian scholars). Pala royal patronage kept eastern Indian Buddhism alive into the eleventh and twelfth centuries; with the Pala collapse, the patronage collapsed too.

**Hindu philosophical absorption**. Shankara's Advaita Vedanta absorbed many of the philosophical positions Buddhism had developed, in a Hindu theological framework that did not require separation from the broader Hindu tradition. A medieval Indian intellectual interested in Madhyamaka-style emptiness or Yogacara-style consciousness analysis could increasingly find versions of those ideas inside Hindu Vedanta, without needing to commit to the institutional Buddhist sangha. This made the philosophical contribution of Indian Buddhism less distinctive over time and reduced the intellectual incentive to maintain Buddhist institutional identity.

**Devotional competition**. The rise of Hindu Bhakti devotional traditions (Chapter 6) provided ordinary lay people with intensely personal religious experiences that Buddhist monasticism, focused on monastic practice and philosophical study, could not match. The Buddhist lay community had always been dependent on the monastic community for its religious framework; when the monastic community shrank, the lay community had nowhere natural to go except into the increasingly devotional Hindu tradition.

**The Turkic destruction**. The final blow came in the early thirteenth century when **Bakhtiyar Khalji**'s Turkic cavalry overran Bihar and Bengal around 1200–1205 (Chapter 8). Khalji's army destroyed Nalanda, Vikramashila, and Odantapuri, killing the resident monks and burning the libraries. The Persian historian Minhaj-i Siraj reports the destruction of Nalanda in detail. The Tibetan historian Taranatha, writing centuries later but using earlier sources, gives an even fuller account of the Buddhist religious establishment being systematically wiped out across eastern India. The intellectual leadership of Indian Buddhism had been concentrated in these few major institutions; their physical destruction without functioning patron-states to rebuild them was the death blow to organized Buddhism in the subcontinent.

After 1200, what was left of Indian Buddhism gradually faded out. Some monks fled to Tibet and Nepal, carrying the Sanskrit Buddhist textual tradition with them. Some lay communities continued for several generations in attenuated form. By 1500 CE, Buddhism had essentially vanished as a significant presence in India, surviving only in the Himalayan border regions where it would later be assimilated into Tibetan Buddhism's mature institutional form. The religion that India had given to the world had stopped being practiced in any substantial form at home.

The cross-civ irony is striking. Indian Buddhism survived everywhere it had been transplanted — Sri Lanka, Tibet, China, Korea, Japan, Vietnam, Cambodia, Burma, Thailand, and the Indonesian islands — and disappeared only in the land of its origin. The land that produced Buddhism became, by 1500, the only major Asian civilization without a significant Buddhist presence. Modern Indian Buddhism (the **Dalit Buddhist movement** founded by **B. R. Ambedkar** in 1956, which converted millions of Dalits to a renewed Buddhism in protest against caste oppression) is a twentieth-century revival, not a continuous tradition.

---

A final note on medieval Indian science.

The Gupta-era mathematical tradition (Aryabhata, the decimal place-value system, the concept of zero) continued through the medieval period, extended by a series of remarkable mathematician-astronomers based at the **Ujjain** observatory in central India. The most important medieval figure is **Bhaskara II** (BHAH-skah-rah, also called **Bhaskaracharya**, 1114–1185 CE), whose **Siddhanta Shiromani** ("Crown Jewel of Mathematical Treatises," 1150) is the most sophisticated medieval Indian mathematical-astronomical work.

Bhaskara II worked out principles of differential calculus several centuries before Newton and Leibniz — including a recognition that the rate of change of a function at a point can be defined as the limit of an average rate of change as the interval shrinks toward zero, and an early version of what would later be called Rolle's Theorem. He computed the length of the sidereal year (the time for the Earth to complete one orbit relative to the fixed stars) to within minutes of the modern value. He posed and solved problems in indeterminate equations (Diophantine equations) that European mathematicians would not match until the seventeenth century. His textbook **Lilavati** ("Lila," named for his daughter), written in elegant Sanskrit verse, became the standard arithmetic textbook of medieval and early-modern India.

The cumulative achievement of medieval Indian mathematics was substantial. The decimal place-value numeral system, including the symbol for zero as a number rather than just a placeholder, was passed to the Arab world through Persian polymaths like **al-Khwarizmi** (al-KWAH-riz-mee, ~825 CE, the Khwarazmian mathematician at the Abbasid court who wrote the first systematic Arabic treatise on Indian arithmetic — Islamic Golden Age Ch 3). Al-Khwarizmi called the system "Hindi numerals" and credited the Indian source. Arab mathematicians extended and refined the system; eventually it was passed from Arabic to Latin Europe (largely through the Italian mathematician **Fibonacci** in the early thirteenth century and his **Liber Abaci** of 1202), where Europeans called the digits "Arabic numerals" because they had received them from Arabic translations. The Indians had been writing them this way since the Gupta era and refining them continuously throughout the medieval period.

This is one of the largest cross-civilizational intellectual debts in world history, and it is recognized in the modern formal name of the system — **Hindu-Arabic numerals** — even though the everyday English usage just says "Arabic numerals" and forgets the Indian source. The number you use to count, to measure, to do arithmetic, to write programs, to do science, is in its essential structure a medieval Indian invention.

Cross-civ check: the Vedanta synthesis happened simultaneously with the **Scholastic** synthesis in Western Europe (Anselm in the eleventh century, Thomas Aquinas in the thirteenth) and with the Islamic philosophical synthesis (al-Ghazali in the late eleventh century, Ibn Rushd in the twelfth — Islamic Golden Age Ch 5). All three civilizational traditions were doing the same fundamental work in the same era: taking inherited philosophical and scriptural materials and building rigorous systematic interpretations that would define their respective intellectual landscapes for centuries afterward. The medieval Indian Vedanta tradition is part of the same global eleventh-thirteenth-century philosophical moment as Scholasticism in Paris and Falsafa in Cordoba.

# Chapter 8 — The Ghaznavid and Ghurid Invasions

The medieval period that this book has been describing — six and a half centuries of regional Hindu kingdoms, monumental temple architecture, devotional poetry, and philosophical synthesis — ended in a series of military defeats spread across two centuries. The first wave came under **Mahmud of Ghazni** in the early eleventh century. The second and decisive wave came under **Muhammad of Ghor** in the late twelfth. By 1206, when **Qutb al-Din Aibak** proclaimed himself sultan at Lahore, north India was under Turkic Muslim rule and the Delhi Sultanate was beginning. The medieval Hindu period was over.

This is one of the genuine watersheds of Indian history. From 1206 forward, the political dominance of north India would belong to Muslim-ruled imperial states for the next seven centuries — first the Delhi Sultanate (Delhi Sultanate Ch 1), then the Mughal Empire (Mughal Empire Ch 1), and after the Mughal collapse in the eighteenth century, the British East India Company, which inherited and modified the institutional frameworks the Mughals had built. Hindu political power did not disappear — the Vijayanagar Empire dominated the south through the fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, the Marathas built a major regional empire in the seventeenth and eighteenth, and various smaller Hindu kingdoms persisted across the subcontinent throughout the period — but the dominant imperial state was, from 1206 onward, no longer Hindu.

How this transition happened is the subject of this chapter.

---

To understand the Turkic invasions, you have to understand what Central Asia had become by the year 1000.

The story starts in the eighth and ninth centuries, when the Abbasid caliphate, recognizing that its native Arab armies were insufficient to defend the empire's enormous territories, began purchasing **Turkic slave-soldiers** (**ghulams** or **mamluks**) from the steppe peoples north of Khorasan. These Turkic soldiers were converted to Islam, trained from childhood in royal military service, and proved spectacularly effective on the battlefield. The Abbasids built up substantial Turkic military elites who, over generations, became the dominant power in the eastern caliphate. By the late ninth and tenth centuries, regional governors of Turkic origin were establishing semi-independent states across the eastern Islamic world. The most successful of these was the **Samanid** dynasty, which ruled most of Khorasan and Transoxiana from the late ninth through the late tenth century with a sophisticated court at Bukhara.

When the Samanids weakened in the late tenth century, their Turkic generals seized power. **Sebuktigin** (sah-BOOK-tih-gin, ruled 977–997), a Turkic former Samanid slave-soldier who had risen to become governor of the city of **Ghazni** (GHAZ-nee, in modern eastern Afghanistan), declared himself an independent sultan and founded the **Ghaznavid** dynasty. His son **Mahmud of Ghazni** (mah-MOOD, ruled 998–1030) inherited the dynasty and made it briefly the most powerful state in the eastern Islamic world.

Mahmud's military project had two phases. The first was consolidating his control of Khorasan, eastern Iran, and parts of Central Asia — defeating his various Samanid and Karakhanid rivals and incorporating their territories. The second was systematic raiding into northern India.

He conducted **seventeen raids** into India between 1001 and 1027 CE. The raids were not attempts at permanent conquest. They were, explicitly, extraction operations. Mahmud would assemble a mounted army in the autumn (after the monsoon rains had ended and the rivers had subsided), march down through the Khyber Pass and across the Punjab, attack a series of targets — usually wealthy Hindu temples or Rajput capitals — for several months, accumulate as much portable wealth as possible (gold, silver, jewels, slaves), and march back to Ghazni before the next monsoon. The raids generated enormous wealth, which Mahmud spent on building a magnificent capital at Ghazni and patronizing the great Persian poet **Ferdowsi** (fer-DOW-see) and the polymath scholar **al-Biruni** (more on whom shortly).

The most famous of the raids was the 1025 sack of **Somnath**.

---

The **Somnath Temple** (SOM-nahth) was the great Shiva temple on the **Saurashtra** peninsula of modern Gujarat, perched on the Arabian Sea coast and dedicated to Shiva in his form as **Somnatha**, "lord of the moon." It was one of the **twelve jyotirlingas** — the twelve most sacred Shiva pilgrimage sites in India — and one of the wealthiest Hindu temples in the subcontinent, accumulated through centuries of pilgrim donations and royal endowments. The temple was a major economic institution: Arab and Persian travelers' accounts describe it as housing thousands of Brahmin priests, hundreds of dancers (the temple **devadasis**), a substantial treasury, and a constant flow of pilgrims from across north India.

Mahmud reached Somnath in early 1025, at the head of an army of perhaps 30,000 cavalry, after a difficult march across the Thar Desert. The defending Hindu force was reportedly substantial — the local Chaulukya king Bhima I and his allies fielded tens of thousands of soldiers — but the defense was inadequately organized. Mahmud's army assaulted the temple, broke through the outer defenses, and sacked the inner sanctum. Persian sources describe Mahmud personally smashing the central Shiva lingam with his sword. The destruction of the lingam was a deliberately symbolic act — Hindu sources record that Mahmud's army then carried away the temple's accumulated treasure, including reportedly several thousand pounds of gold and silver, and sold tens of thousands of captured pilgrims and temple personnel into slavery.

The 1025 sack became one of the most traumatic episodes in subsequent Hindu memory. It would be invoked over and over across the next thousand years as the foundational image of Muslim violence against Hindu sacred sites. The temple was rebuilt by the Chaulukyas after Mahmud withdrew; it was sacked again by Alauddin Khalji's army in 1297, rebuilt; sacked by Aurangzeb in 1665, rebuilt; and most recently rebuilt in 1951 after Indian independence as a deliberate act of national-religious affirmation. The current Somnath Temple is the seventh or eighth structure on the site, depending on how you count.

The historical complexity that subsequent Hindu nationalist narratives have flattened is real, however. Mahmud's raids were not motivated by religious ideology in the simple sense — he raided for treasure, and any wealthy Hindu temple was a worthwhile target whether or not the religious symbolism was particularly charged. He also raided wealthy Muslim cities in eastern Iran when they were politically expedient targets. His court patronized Persian poetry that included substantial Hindu mythological content (Ferdowsi's Shahnameh contains pre-Islamic Iranian material that overlaps with Indian mythological material in ways that Ferdowsi did not censor). And the Hindu communities Mahmud raided rebuilt rather than fled — Somnath's continuous reconstruction across a thousand years is, in its way, evidence of religious resilience as much as of religious vulnerability.

What Mahmud's seventeen raids did decisively accomplish was the demonstration that Hindu north India was militarily vulnerable to organized Turkic cavalry. The regional Rajput kingdoms — Chandelas, Chaulukyas, Paramaras, Chauhans — could fight off small raiding parties but could not coordinate against systematic invasions. The political fragmentation that the Tripartite Struggle had produced (Chapter 4) had not been corrected. The military lesson of the Mahmud raids was registered. The implications would be drawn out a century and a half later by Muhammad of Ghor.

---

Before we get to Muhammad of Ghor, the figure of **al-Biruni** (al-bee-ROO-nee, 973–1048 CE) deserves attention, because he is the most thoughtful outside observer of medieval Indian civilization that any source produced.

**Abu al-Rayhan al-Biruni** was a Khwarazmian polymath — that is, from the region of **Khwarazm** in modern Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan — born in the city of **Kath** in 973 CE. He was a mathematician, astronomer, geographer, ethnographer, comparative religionist, and historian, and he was unusual among medieval scholars for working seriously across all of these fields rather than specializing in one. He had been a court scholar under several Khwarazmian and Central Asian dynasties before being captured by Mahmud of Ghazni when Mahmud annexed Khwarazm around 1017. Al-Biruni was brought back to Ghazni essentially as a prize — Mahmud had a habit of collecting talented intellectuals from his conquests — and made a court astronomer.

What al-Biruni did with his Ghazni captivity was learn Sanskrit. He spent years studying with Indian scholars (some of them captured during Mahmud's raids), traveled extensively in northwestern India during periods when the Ghaznavid army was campaigning there, read the Indian astronomical and mathematical literature in the original Sanskrit, and eventually wrote — around 1030 CE, the year of Mahmud's death — the book that became his masterpiece on Indian civilization. The book is called **Kitab fi Tahqiq ma li-l-Hind** ("Book of Verifying What India Has," usually called **Tarikh al-Hind**, "History of India," in shortened form).

The Tarikh al-Hind is one of the most remarkable works of medieval cross-cultural scholarship. Al-Biruni systematically describes Indian religion, philosophy, astronomy, mathematics, geography, social customs, literature, and history, drawing on Sanskrit primary sources he read in the original. He compares Indian intellectual traditions to Greek (he had read Aristotle and the Greek astronomical-mathematical tradition in Arabic translation), Persian, and Islamic equivalents. He is unusually frank about the difficulties of cross-cultural understanding, including his own struggles with Sanskrit philosophical vocabulary and his frustration with the rigidity of caste rules that excluded foreign scholars from many Hindu pedagogical settings. He criticizes both Indian and Islamic prejudices fairly. He praises Indian astronomical sophistication where it exceeds the Greek tradition (which he is in a position to judge accurately) and notes Indian errors where they exist. The tone throughout is of a serious scholar genuinely trying to understand a civilization on its own terms.

The Tarikh al-Hind is, by some distance, the best outsider account of medieval Indian society from any source. The fact that it was written by a scholar working in the entourage of the very Sultan whose army was simultaneously sacking Indian temples is one of the more interesting ironies of medieval Indian intellectual history. Al-Biruni was, in modern terms, both an embedded observer with the invading army and a serious cross-cultural ethnographer who genuinely respected the civilization he was observing. His book is the closest medieval India comes to having an external mirror held up to it. The internal Indian sources of the period rarely attempt the kind of systematic civilizational self-description that al-Biruni produces; the Tarikh al-Hind fills a gap that the Indian sources leave open.

Al-Biruni died in Ghazni in 1048. His reputation in the Islamic world remained high for centuries — al-Biruni was one of the four or five great polymath scholars of the Islamic Golden Age, alongside al-Razi, Ibn Sina (Avicenna), and Ibn al-Haytham (Islamic Golden Age Ch 4). His reputation in Indian historiography went through a long eclipse and has been revived in the modern period; modern Indian historians regularly cite the Tarikh al-Hind as a major source for eleventh-century Indian society.

---

The Ghaznavid empire collapsed surprisingly quickly after Mahmud's death. His descendants ruled a reduced version of the empire from Ghazni and then from Lahore for another century and a half, but the dynasty was no longer the dominant Central Asian power. The new dominant power was the **Seljuk** Turks, who conquered Persia in the 1040s, defeated the Byzantines at Manzikert in 1071, and built one of the major Eurasian empires of the eleventh century (Islamic Golden Age Ch 9, Byzantine Empire Ch 7).

The Ghaznavids continued holding Lahore as a frontier outpost, but their major presence in the Indian heartland was over. For a hundred and fifty years after Mahmud, north India had a respite. The regional Rajput dynasties extended themselves across the Ganges plain — the **Chauhans of Ajmer** under King **Visaladeva** (twelfth century) became the most powerful, eventually capturing Delhi from the **Tomaras** around 1150. The **Gahadavalas** ruled Kannauj. The **Senas** displaced the Palas in Bengal. The Chola Empire was in slow decline in the south. The political situation looked, superficially, like a continuation of the medieval pattern.

What was different was that a new Turkic dynasty was rising in central Afghanistan. The **Ghurids** (GOOR-idz) — originally a Tajik dynasty based at the city of **Ghor** (GOR), in the central Afghan highlands between Ghazni and Herat — had been Ghaznavid vassals. In the mid-twelfth century they began expanding at Ghaznavid expense. By 1186 they had captured Lahore from the last Ghaznavid prince and inherited the Ghaznavid Indian frontier. The Ghurid sultan **Ghiyath al-Din Muhammad** ruled the central empire from his capital at **Firuzkuh**; his younger brother **Muhammad of Ghor** (born **Muizzuddin Muhammad ibn Sam**, ~1149–1206 CE) was given responsibility for the Indian campaign.

---

Muhammad of Ghor was a different kind of invader from Mahmud of Ghazni. Mahmud had raided for plunder and returned home each spring; Muhammad set out, systematically, to conquer.

His first major Indian campaign in 1175 captured Multan and consolidated Ghurid control over Sindh and southern Punjab. Through the 1180s he extended Ghurid territory across northern Punjab. By 1190, he was ready to attack the great Rajput kingdoms of the Indian plains.

The principal Rajput ruler of north India was **Prithviraj Chauhan** (PRIT-vee-rahj CHOW-hahn, ruled 1166–1192), king of the Chauhan dynasty of Ajmer and Delhi. Prithviraj was the great hero-king of Hindi-Rajasthani folklore — a young, brave, romantically charismatic figure whose court attracted poets, his marriage to the Kannauj princess Sanyogita became the subject of an entire literary cycle (the twelfth-century Hindi epic **Prithviraj Raso** by the bard **Chand Bardai**), and his military reputation made him one of the few Rajput kings genuinely respected across regional dynastic boundaries. He was also, despite the legendary aura that subsequent centuries built around him, an actual capable military commander.

Muhammad of Ghor's first invasion of Rajput territory met Prithviraj at the **First Battle of Tarain** in 1191. Tarain was a plain near modern Karnal in Haryana, about 80 miles north of Delhi — strategic ground controlling the road from Punjab into the Ganges plain. The Rajput coalition that Prithviraj led included contingents from multiple regional Rajput dynasties (Chauhans, Tomaras, possibly Chandelas) and reportedly outnumbered the Ghurid army considerably. Prithviraj's heavy cavalry charge broke the Ghurid line; Muhammad of Ghor was personally wounded and very nearly killed; the Ghurid army was driven from the field. The defeat was the first major Hindu military victory over the Turkic invaders in two centuries.

It also turned out to be the last.

Muhammad returned to Ghor, spent a year reorganizing his army, recruiting reinforcements, and studying what had gone wrong at Tarain. The next year, **1192**, he came back. The **Second Battle of Tarain** was fought on essentially the same ground, but the tactical setup was different. Muhammad of Ghor used his cavalry archers more flexibly — withdrawing in the face of Rajput charges rather than meeting them head-on, harassing the Rajput cavalry with sustained arrow fire, and maneuvering the Rajputs into prolonged action where their heavier formations exhausted themselves. By the end of the day, the Rajput army was broken. Prithviraj Chauhan was captured. The Chauhan kingdom of Ajmer surrendered. Within months, Ghurid forces had occupied Delhi.

What happened to Prithviraj Chauhan after his capture is murky. Persian sources say he was executed in Ghazni shortly after the battle. The legendary tradition (the Prithviraj Raso) gives him a more dramatic ending — he is brought to Ghazni in chains, blinded by Muhammad, and then asked to demonstrate his archery skills in the sultan's court; using his memory of Muhammad's voice, he draws a bow and shoots Muhammad through the throat, then commits suicide along with his loyal poet Chand Bardai. The legendary version is almost certainly invented, but it captures the cultural significance of Prithviraj's fate in subsequent Hindu memory: the last Hindu emperor of Delhi, defeated and killed by the founder of Muslim rule, becomes the romantic-tragic figure around whom the larger story is organized.

The Second Battle of Tarain in 1192 is the conventional dividing line in Indian historiography between "medieval" and "Sultanate" India. It opened the road to Delhi for the Ghurid forces. Within months, Muhammad of Ghor's general **Qutb al-Din Aibak** (kuh-TOB al-DEEN AY-bak), a Turkic slave-soldier whom Muhammad had purchased and trained, occupied Delhi and began converting it into a Turkic military headquarters.

---

Over the next decade, Ghurid forces under Muhammad of Ghor and his lieutenants — most importantly Aibak in north India and **Bakhtiyar Khalji** in Bengal — completed the conquest of the Indo-Gangetic plain.

Aibak captured Kannauj from the Gahadavala king Jayachandra around 1194. He sacked Banaras (the holy city on the Ganges, also called Varanasi, the most sacred Hindu pilgrimage site in north India). He pushed eastward across the Doab. He suppressed Rajput revolts when they arose. He began the construction of the **Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque** ("Might of Islam") in Delhi, the first congregational mosque in the city, built deliberately on the site of demolished Hindu and Jain temples — twenty-seven of which were torn down, and their carved stone columns reused in the mosque's construction. Adjacent to the mosque, Aibak began construction of the **Qutb Minar**, a 240-foot fluted brick-and-sandstone victory tower, which would be completed by his successors over the following century. The Qutb Minar is still standing — UNESCO World Heritage, the tallest brick minaret in the world, and a deliberately ambiguous monument that has been read alternately as celebrating Islamic conquest, demonstrating Indo-Islamic architectural synthesis, or commemorating an interreligious complexity that has no clean ideological narrative.

Meanwhile, **Muhammad bin Bakhtiyar Khalji** (BAHKH-tee-yar KHAL-jee), a Khalji Turkic warlord in Ghurid service, conducted the conquest of Bihar and Bengal. The campaign was completed in a series of dramatic raids around 1200–1205 that overran the great Pala-era monastic universities at Nalanda, Vikramashila, and Odantapuri. The Persian historian **Minhaj-i Siraj**, writing his **Tabaqat-i Nasiri** about fifty years later, gives the standard account: Bakhtiyar's troops stormed Nalanda, killed the resident Buddhist monks who refused to convert, and burned the libraries — the Buddhist Sanskrit philosophical and religious texts accumulated over six centuries went up in smoke. The Tibetan historian Taranatha (writing in 1608, but using earlier Tibetan sources) gives an even fuller account of the systematic destruction of Buddhist institutions across eastern India during this period.

The destruction of Nalanda is one of the great cultural losses in human history. The library held perhaps tens of thousands of Sanskrit manuscripts — Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain — accumulated through six centuries of Indian intellectual life. What survived was what had already been copied and exported to Tibet, Sri Lanka, China, and Southeast Asia. The bulk of medieval Indian Buddhist philosophical literature exists today only because Tibetan monks had translated it into Tibetan in the centuries before the destruction. The Sanskrit originals that the Tibetans were translating from no longer exist. Modern scholars are reconstructing major Indian Buddhist works from Tibetan back-translation, supplemented by a few surviving Sanskrit fragments.

The Bakhtiyar Khalji destruction of Nalanda is the death blow to organized Buddhism in north India. Buddhist communities continued in attenuated form for several generations afterward, but the institutional infrastructure — the great monastic universities, the trained teaching lineages, the Sanskrit textual scholarship — was effectively wiped out in a few years of military campaigning. The religion that had been the dominant intellectual tradition of north India for fifteen centuries lost its institutional foundations. The slow disappearance traced in Chapter 7 had its sudden, violent ending here.

---

**Muhammad of Ghor** himself was assassinated in **1206 CE** at the river crossing of Damyak in modern Pakistan, while traveling between Ghazni and Lahore. The assassins were probably members of an obscure Indian Hindu sect (the **Khokars**, an ethnic group in the Punjab who had been raided by Muhammad's army several times) — though the details are debated. He left no son. The Ghurid Empire collapsed within a few years of his death, fragmenting among rival successor commanders.

In India, his slave-general **Qutb al-Din Aibak** proclaimed himself sultan at Lahore in 1206, founding the **Mamluk** ("Slave") dynasty — the first dynasty of what would become the Delhi Sultanate. The transition was as much an institutional as a personal one. Aibak was a talented Turkic military leader, but the legitimacy of his sultanate did not derive from any traditional claim — he was a manumitted slave-soldier, with no royal lineage and no traditional Indian basis for rule. What he was building was a new institutional template: a Turkic military aristocracy ruling India through its own administrative system, with its own religion (Sunni Islam), its own language (Persian, with Arabic religious vocabulary), and its own legal and architectural traditions, all imported from the eastern Islamic world.

The **Delhi Sultanate** that Aibak founded would last from 1206 to 1526 — three centuries of Turkic and Afghan dynastic succession (the Mamluks, the Khaljis, the Tughluqs, the Sayyids, the Lodis), each with its own peculiarities, but all sharing the basic framework Aibak had established. The Delhi Sultanate is the subject of Delhi Sultanate Ch 1 and the chapters that follow. What is relevant here is that by 1206, the medieval period of regional Hindu kingdoms was over. The next chapter of north Indian history would be written in Persian and conducted from a Turkic-Muslim throne in Delhi.

---

What did the medieval period leave behind?

The temples. Almost every famous Indian temple — Brihadeeswara, Khajuraho, Ellora's Kailasa, Konark, Belur, Halebidu, Madurai's medieval foundations, Srirangam, Bhubaneswar's Lingaraja, the Dilwara temples at Mount Abu — was built during this period. They are still standing. They are still in active use. They are the visible, physical inheritance of medieval Indian civilization and they will probably outlast all of us.

The philosophical schools. Shankara's Advaita, Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita, Madhva's Dvaita — the three main Vedanta schools that define modern Hindu metaphysics — all crystallized during this period. The intellectual frame that contemporary Indian religious discourse uses, on questions of God, soul, world, and liberation, was substantially worked out in medieval commentaries on Upanishads written between Banaras and Srirangam.

The Bhakti devotional tradition. The Tamil Alvars and Nayanars established a template — vernacular devotional poetry directly addressed to a personal deity, accessible to all castes, theologically egalitarian — that would spread across India over subsequent centuries and shape the popular religious life of the subcontinent ever since. Modern Indian devotional practice is overwhelmingly Bhakti-influenced, even when the Bhakti origins have become invisible.

The decimal numeral system, in its medieval refined form. Bhaskara II at Ujjain, the Sanskrit mathematical textbook tradition, the systematic treatment of zero and place-value notation — passed to the Arab world through al-Khwarizmi and from there to Europe through Fibonacci, the system that you use to write numbers on a piece of paper today is a medieval Indian product.

The temple-as-civic-institution model. The medieval Indian template — the temple as religious sanctum plus economic engine plus community center plus piece of dynastic propaganda — survived the medieval period and continued shaping Indian social organization through the Sultanate and Mughal eras (when Hindu temples continued to be built in many regions outside direct imperial Muslim control), through the British colonial period, and into the present. Modern Indian temple management organizations operate on institutional templates with medieval origins.

And the political pattern. The medieval period demonstrated, with depressing clarity, what happens to a politically fragmented subcontinent when faced with externally organized military invasion. The lesson was registered. The Vijayanagar Empire (which would emerge in south India in the fourteenth century, partly as a Hindu defensive response to the Sultanate's southern incursions) was deliberately built as a centralized Hindu state of a kind the medieval period had not produced. The Maratha Empire of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries was similarly an attempt at centralized Hindu power. Modern Indian nationalism, when it constructs a usable Hindu past, draws very selectively from medieval India — usually emphasizing the temple and philosophical achievements, often glossing over the political fragmentation that proved so consequentially fatal.

The medieval Indian period is, taken together, one of the great cultural flowerings of any civilization in any era. Its political record is genuinely tragic — the long Tripartite Struggle, the failure to integrate after the Gupta collapse, the inability to coordinate against the Turkic invasions. Its cultural and religious record is unmatched. The temples, the philosophy, the devotional poetry, the mathematics, the maritime expeditions, the synthesis of Hindu and Buddhist and Jain religious traditions in a long mutual conversation — these are extraordinary achievements that coexisted with the political tragedy, and they did not prevent the political outcome.

After Tarain, north India would be ruled by Muslim dynasties for the next seven centuries. The medieval period was over. The Sultanate was beginning. And the temples that the medieval period had built would still be standing, mostly intact, when the British finally lowered their flag at the Red Fort in 1947 and the modern Indian republic was born.

Cross-civ check: 1206 CE, the founding year of the Delhi Sultanate, is the same year Genghis Khan was proclaimed Great Khan of the Mongols at the kurultai on the banks of the Onon River (Mongol Empire Ch 2). The two events would not connect for another fifty years — the Mongols would later raid the Delhi Sultanate's Punjab frontier through the thirteenth century, and the eventual Mughal dynasty would trace descent from both Genghis Khan and Timur — but the medieval period this book has covered ended in the same year that the most consequential Eurasian empire of the next century began. The world that the medieval Indian dynasties had inhabited — a world of regional kingdoms, monumental temple-building, devotional poetry, philosophical commentary, and the Sanskrit cosmopolis stretching from Afghanistan to Java — was, simultaneously across Eurasia, ending.

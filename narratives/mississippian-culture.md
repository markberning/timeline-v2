# Mississippian — The Mound Builders

**Goal:** Narrative for the second TL in the North American Indigenous chain. The Mississippian peoples left no written records, not even an undeciphered script. Everything we know comes from archaeology, the chronicles of one Spanish invasion in the 1540s, the eyewitness accounts of French colonists who lived among one surviving Mississippian society in the 1700s, and the oral traditions of the descendant nations who carry the story still. The narrative adapts to this: the rediscovery frame, the landscape as protagonist, the archaeological record as detective story, and the living descendant nations as the epilogue. Central thesis: in the river valleys of eastern North America, Native peoples built a civilization of cities, monumental earthen pyramids, sacred kingship, and continent-spanning trade (without metal tools, without draft animals, without the wheel, without writing) and when nineteenth-century white Americans found the ruins, they invented a racist myth that a vanished non-Indian race built them, because admitting the truth would have indicted what the United States was doing to the descendants of the people who actually built them. The mound builders did not vanish. Their descendants are citizens of sovereign nations today.

**Status:** Full draft, 10 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — The Mound Builder Myth

Somewhere around the year 1810, a Tennessee farmer running a plow through his field hit something that wasn't a rock. When he dug it out, it was a small pot, and inside the pot were human bones. He was not surprised. Half the farms in the Mississippi and Ohio valleys turned up bones and broken pottery and carved shell when the land was first cleared, because the land was full of **mounds** (deliberately built hills of piled-up earth, not natural rises, ranging from low domes to flat-topped earthen platforms the size of small pyramids). Tens of thousands of them dotted the landscape, some small enough to plow flat in an afternoon, some a hundred feet tall and covering more ground than a city block. They were everywhere. They were obviously made by people. And almost every white American who looked at them refused, flatly and on principle, to believe that the people in question were Indians.

A note on words before we go further. This book uses "Indians," "Native Americans," and "Native peoples" to mean the same thing: the indigenous peoples of North America and their descendants. "Indian" is the older term (Columbus's geographic mistake, frozen into the language) and the one the nineteenth-century sources used; "Native American" and "Native peoples" are the modern usages. They are not different groups. Where this book later names specific nations (the Cherokee, the Muscogee, and so on), those are particular peoples within that whole.

This is where we have to start, because the lie came first. Before the archaeology, before the careful excavation, before anything we now know about the people who built the mounds, there was a story that white Americans told themselves for most of a century, and it is worth understanding that story in detail, because the truth is partly the story of how it was demolished.

The story was called the **Mound Builder Myth**, and its core claim was simple: a vanished race of people (not Native Americans, but someone else, someone "civilized") had once lived in North America, built these magnificent earthworks, and then been exterminated by the ancestors of the Indians, who were cast as savage latecomers incapable of such works. The identity of the vanished race was filled in differently by different enthusiasts, and the list is a tour of nineteenth-century fantasy: they were the lost tribes of Israel; they were Vikings who had sailed down from a Norse colony; they were refugees from sunken Atlantis; they were the Toltecs (a real central-Mexican people who built the city of Tula and dominated the region around 900–1150 CE, well known to nineteenth-century writers from Spanish colonial accounts) or some other civilized Mexican people who had passed through on their way south; they were Welsh, Phoenician, Hindu, or the survivors of a "white" race annihilated by a "red" one. The specifics did not matter. What mattered was the load-bearing assumption underneath all of them: that Native Americans could not possibly have done this.

It is important to be precise about why this myth was so popular, because it was not an innocent error. The decades when the Mound Builder Myth was at its strongest, roughly the 1820s through the 1840s, were exactly the decades when the United States was forcibly removing the Native nations of the eastern woodlands from their land. That is not a coincidence of timing; the myth peaked when it did because it was useful when it did. (The groundwork was laid earlier, in Washington's scorched-earth Sullivan Campaign and the shattering of Tecumseh's confederacy: Early American Republic Ch 3 and Ch 15.) As white settlement pushed west into the Southeast in the early 1800s, the federal government and southern states wanted the land occupied by five large, organized, treaty-holding Native nations: the **Cherokee** of the southern Appalachians, the **Muscogee (Creek)** of Georgia and Alabama, the **Chickasaw** and **Choctaw** of Mississippi, and the **Seminole** of Florida, all of them in the Southeast, all of them with their own governments and farms and (in several cases) written constitutions. The argument for taking that land needed a moral cover, and the mounds, sitting all over the contested ground, supplied one.

The **Indian Removal Act** was the 1830 federal law that authorized the result: the government negotiated (and coerced) treaties under which these nations were expelled from the Southeast and marched a thousand miles west to what is now Oklahoma. The forced march, during which thousands died of exposure, hunger, and disease, is remembered as the **Trail of Tears** (its full story, the removal's legal mechanism and the Cherokee court cases, is told in the American narrative; Antebellum America Ch 4). It was being debated and executed while Americans were standing in front of the mounds insisting that no Indian could have built them. The two facts are connected, and not subtly. Walk the logic the way its believers did: the mounds prove a civilized people once lived here; Indians are savages who could not have built them; therefore Indians must have arrived later and exterminated the real builders; therefore the Indians are themselves usurpers who took this land by slaughter; therefore taking it back from them is not theft but the recovery of a stolen inheritance. Every step is false, but stacked together they convert dispossession into restitution. If the Indians had exterminated the noble Mound Builders, then removing the Indians was not theft. It was, in a grotesque inversion, justice. The myth was not a harmless antiquarian hobby. It was the dispossession's alibi, and it was repeated from pulpits, in school textbooks, and on the floor of Congress.

---

The dismantling of the myth was slow, and it began, improbably, with a future president and a hole in the ground.

In 1784, **Thomas Jefferson** (the Virginia planter and revolutionary, later the third president of the United States, here a forty-one-year-old gentleman scientist) owned land near a small earthen mound on the Rivanna River in central Virginia. Jefferson did something with that mound that essentially nobody in the Western world had thought to do with an ancient monument before: instead of digging straight down into the middle to grab whatever treasure was inside, he cut a trench through the side and looked at the *layers*. He observed that the mound was built of distinct strata, that bones occurred at multiple levels in different states of decay, and that the bodies had clearly been added over a long period rather than all at once. From the layering he reasoned about the sequence of events that had produced the mound. This sounds obvious. It was not. Reading a site by its layers, what archaeologists call **stratigraphy** (the principle that material laid down later sits on top of material laid down earlier, so the vertical sequence is a record of time), is the foundational technique of modern archaeology. Jefferson's Rivanna trench is widely cited in histories of archaeology as the first stratigraphic excavation conducted in the Americas and one of the earliest deliberate uses of the layered approach anywhere; the precise "world first" claim is hard to defend strictly (a few European antiquarians were also digging and observing layers in the eighteenth century), but the standard reading is that Jefferson's dig was a remarkably early and methodologically modern instance. He published his findings in *Notes on the State of Virginia*, and crucially, he concluded that the mound had been built by the ancestors of the Native peoples of the region. He was right. Almost nobody listened. For comparison, careful stratigraphic excavation would not become standard archaeological practice in Europe, at sites like Troy and Pompeii, for nearly another century.

The next major step was a survey. In 1848, the brand-new **Smithsonian Institution** in Washington published the inaugural volume of its scholarly series, *Contributions to Knowledge*. (The Smithsonian is a federally chartered research and museum organization, founded in 1846 with a bequest from the British scientist James Smithson, today the world's largest museum and research complex.) That volume was titled *Ancient Monuments of the Mississippi Valley*, and it was the work of two Ohio men, **Ephraim Squier**, a newspaper editor, and **Edwin Davis**, a physician. Over several years they had measured, mapped, and surveyed more than two hundred mound sites and earthwork complexes across the Ohio and Mississippi valleys, producing precise scale drawings of geometric earthworks so large and so exactly laid out that the drawings themselves became the primary record, which mattered, because most of the sites they drew were plowed away within the next two generations. The detail in those surveys is what makes the persistence of the myth so striking. At the **Newark Earthworks** in Ohio they recorded a circle joined to an octagon, each side of the octagon hundreds of feet long, the whole figure laid out with a regularity that implies a unit of measure and a survey method. Squier and Davis put a near-perfect geometric monument on the page, in ink, at scale, and still leaned toward the vanished-race interpretation, because the cultural pressure to do so was nearly irresistible. That is the cognitive dissonance the myth demanded: you could draw the evidence with your own hand and refuse to read what it said. The myth survived rigorous mapping. It would take the federal government setting out, deliberately, to settle the question.

---

The pressure Squier and Davis bent under was not a vague mood; it had concrete teeth. A scholar who credited the mounds to Indians was contradicting the political consensus of the country that funded the scholarship, the textbooks that taught it, and the churches that moralized it. The vanished-race story was the respectable position, taught in schools and preached from pulpits; the contrary position aligned a researcher with the inconvenient and unpopular claim that the people the United States was busy expelling had built the nation's most impressive ancient monuments. It cost reputation and standing to say so, and it threatened the comfortable national story almost everyone wanted to keep believing. That is the kind of pressure that makes careful men report what their data did not show.

The break came at the **Bureau of Ethnology**, the Smithsonian's research arm devoted to **ethnology**. (Ethnology is the systematic study and comparison of human cultures; in this period the Bureau was focused on documenting Native American languages, customs, and history under the wrong assumption that they were vanishing.) In the 1880s, Congress appropriated money specifically to resolve the mound question once and for all, and the work was handed to a man named **Cyrus Thomas**. Thomas is one of the quiet heroes of this story, and what makes him interesting is that he started out a believer. When he took the job he was confident the mounds had been built by a vanished race. His team then spent years excavating mounds across the eastern United States systematically, comparing the construction methods, the artifacts, the burial practices, and the pottery against everything known about the **historic** Native peoples of the same regions. ("Historic" here means the nations that lived there in the period of European written records, as opposed to **prehistoric**, the eras before any written documentation.) If the mound builders had been a different, vanished people, their work should look unrelated to the historic nations'. It did not; the techniques and artifacts lined up. The evidence pointed in exactly one direction. In his enormous 1894 report for the Bureau, Thomas stated the conclusion without hedging: the mounds were built by the ancestors of the American Indians, the same peoples Europeans had encountered when they arrived. There was no vanished race. There never had been. Thomas had been hired to prove the myth and had instead, honestly, killed it. Among scholars, the question was effectively closed by 1894. In the popular imagination it would limp along for decades more, and a diluted version of it (the vague sense that the mounds are "mysterious" and that "nobody really knows" who built them) survives in roadside lore to this day. They are not mysterious in that way. We know.

Thomas won the scholarly argument on paper at almost the exact moment the physical evidence was being hauled away in carts. By the time he published, much of what he was racing to record was already gone. The destruction of the mounds is its own grim chapter, and the clearest example is a city. The site of modern St. Louis, Missouri, was so thick with earthen mounds that its nineteenth-century nickname was **"Mound City."** The largest of them was a colossal riverfront earthwork called the **Big Mound**. By nineteenth-century descriptions it was a long ridge-top mound on the order of 300 feet long and roughly 30 feet high, a small city block of piled earth. It was leveled in the 1860s, and the dirt, human burials and artifacts included, was carted off as construction fill during the city's expansion (the destruction is variously attributed in the sources to railroad grading and to street and lot leveling; the common thread is that the largest mound in St. Louis was sold off by the cartload). Across the Mississippi and Ohio valleys, thousands of mounds were plowed flat for farming, quarried for road and rail construction, or simply bulldozed for towns. We will never know what was in most of them. The myth helped here too: it is psychologically much easier to bulldoze a "mysterious old hill" than the pyramid of a known people.

---

So here is the thesis of this book, stated plainly so the rest of it can argue the case.

In the river valleys of eastern North America, between roughly 800 and 1600 CE, Native peoples built a civilization. It had cities. It had monumental architecture: flat-topped earthen pyramids larger at the base than the Great Pyramid of Giza, raised basket-load by basket-load by people with no metal tools, no wheel, no draft animals, and no writing. It had hereditary sacred rulers, ranked classes, organized warfare, a shared art and religion that spanned half a continent, and trade networks that moved seashell, copper, and stone across thousands of miles. When white Americans found the wreckage of it, they could have read it correctly (Jefferson did, in 1784) but instead they built a myth that erased the builders, because the myth was useful to a project of dispossession. The builders did not vanish. They were devastated by a catastrophe we will come to (Chapter 9), they reorganized under new names (Chapter 10), and their descendants are citizens of sovereign Native nations in the United States right now. The mounds still stand. People who can trace their ancestry to the people who raised them are alive and can tell you so.

---

One methodological note before the story, and it governs everything that follows.

The Mississippian peoples had no writing system. Not a script, not even an undeciphered one like the **Indus Valley seals**. (The seals are short strings of pictorial signs carved on stone stamp seals by the Bronze Age cities of the Indus River, in modern Pakistan and India: a real script that nobody has been able to read; Indus Valley Ch 6.) The Mississippians did not even have that. They left no inscriptions, no records, no names of their own kings written by their own hands. This means that, unlike Mesopotamia or Egypt or China, we cannot quote a single sentence a Mississippian person said. The contrast inside the Americas is sharper still: the **Maya**, far to the south in Mexico and Central America, had a fully developed writing system that modern scholars have largely deciphered, the only fully readable indigenous script in the pre-Columbian Americas. While Maya scribes were carving dated dynastic histories into stone in the same centuries (Maya Ch 2), the Mississippian world produced monumental cities and a continent-spanning sacred art and not one readable word. Everything in this book therefore comes from exactly four kinds of evidence, and it is worth knowing them up front so you can weigh each claim as it arrives. They are flagged here because they are the spine of the chapter title and they govern every page after this one.

The first is **archaeology**: the mounds, the houses, the pottery, the bones, the trash, the seeds, the postholes, the physical residue of life, read with techniques like stratigraphy and **tree-ring dating** (matching the distinctive pattern of wide and narrow annual growth rings in a wooden beam to a master ring sequence built from living and dead trees, which can pin a piece of timber to the exact year it was cut). This is the bulk of what we have, and it is silent on names, words, and motives.

The second is the **de Soto chronicles**: four accounts written by members or contemporaries of a Spanish military expedition led by Hernando de Soto, the same conquistador who had grown rich helping plunder the Inca Empire in Peru (Chapter 9), which marched through the Southeast from 1539 to 1543 while Mississippian chiefdoms were still functioning. These are eyewitness, which is priceless, and they are also the testimony of an invading army that was robbing, enslaving, and killing the people it described, which colors everything.

The third is the **French ethnography of the Natchez**: detailed accounts written by French colonists in the early 1700s who lived alongside the Natchez of the lower Mississippi, the last society to practice something recognizably like the old Mississippian way of life into the era of European observers with notebooks (Chapter 7). This is the one window we have onto the inside of a living Mississippian-style society, but it is a single, late, contact-altered society, and projecting it back five centuries onto Cahokia is a temptation the careful reader must resist, as the careful scholars do.

The fourth is **descendant oral tradition**: the migration stories, clan histories, and ceremonial knowledge of the Native nations of the Southeast and the eastern woodlands (the Muscogee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Cherokee, Caddo, and others) whose ancestors built and inherited this world. These are not decoration and they are not folklore in the dismissive sense. They are the testimony of the people whose history this is, and where they can be checked against the archaeology they have repeatedly turned out to be carrying accurate information across centuries.

And a final caveat that inoculates the entire manuscript, because this book will say "first" and "earliest" and "largest" many times. In archaeology, "first" never means "the first time this was ever done." It means "the first instance for which we have found surviving evidence." Evidence survives by luck: by climate, by what got plowed under versus what got protected, by which sites a railroad happened to miss. The eastern woodlands are humid; organic material rots; the dry desert preservation that makes the record of the **Ancestral Puebloans** so rich does not exist here. (The Ancestral Puebloans were the Native farming peoples of the arid Southwest, the builders of the great houses of Chaco Canyon and the cliff dwellings of Mesa Verde, ancestors of today's Pueblo nations, and the subject of this chain's previous book; Ancestral Puebloans Ch 1. This book will compare the two repeatedly: two very different North American civilizations running at roughly the same time, one on river floods in the east, one on desert ingenuity in the west.) So when this book says the largest earthwork in the Americas, or the first city north of Mexico, read it as "the largest and first *that we have found and can date*," and hold the rest of the continent's unrecovered past in mind.

# Chapter 2 — The Eastern Woodlands

Forget, for this chapter, the people. Start with the water.

If you stand on a bluff on the east bank of the Mississippi River just below where it meets the Missouri, near modern St. Louis, you are looking at the outflow of a **drainage basin** that covers roughly forty percent of the continental United States. A drainage basin is the entire region whose rainfall and snowmelt eventually run downhill into a single river system; the Mississippi's basin reaches from the Rockies to the Appalachians, so a creek in Montana and a creek in Pennsylvania can both, in the end, feed the same river. The **Mississippi River** runs about 2,300 miles (3,700 km) from Minnesota to the Gulf of Mexico, which is comfortably longer than a drive from New York to Los Angeles, and it is not really one river but the trunk of a colossal branching tree. The **Missouri** comes in from the west out of the Rockies. The **Ohio** comes in from the east, draining the western slopes of the Appalachian Mountains. The **Arkansas**, the **Red**, the **Tennessee**, the **Illinois**, and a hundred lesser rivers feed the system. Almost every place that matters in this book sits on one of these rivers, because the rivers were the highways, the farmland, and the reason cities could exist at all.

The rivers built the farmland by flooding. Every spring, swollen with snowmelt and rain, the Mississippi and its tributaries overran their banks and laid down a fresh sheet of silt across the low ground beside the channel. That low ground is the **floodplain** (the flat valley bottom a river inundates and rebuilds with sediment), and a floodplain is, agriculturally, a renewable gift: the flood that destroys also fertilizes, refreshing the soil for free, year after year. The single most important piece of floodplain in this entire story is a stretch of the Mississippi bottomland directly across the river from modern St. Louis, an unusually wide, fertile, well-watered expanse that archaeologists call the **American Bottom**. Hold that name. The largest city in the ancient United States was built there (Chapter 4), and it was built there because the dirt was that good.

Cross-civ context: this is the same logic that built the Old World's first civilizations, and the comparison is worth making precisely. Mesopotamia rose on the silt of the Tigris and Euphrates, Egypt on the annual flood of the Nile, the Indus cities on the Indus and the monsoon, the Chinese dynasties on the Yellow River: every one of them a civilization grown from a river that flooded and refertilized predictable farmland (Mesopotamia Ch 1; New Kingdom Egypt Ch 1). The eastern woodlands belong to that same family of river-valley origins. But not every early civilization ran on a flooding grain river, and two contrasts are worth holding alongside it. One is desert: the Ancestral Puebloans of the Southwest (Chapter 1; Ancestral Puebloans Ch 1) built a complex society with *no* such river, on less than ten inches of unreliable rain a year, by sheer water-management ingenuity. The other is the sea: the earliest civilization in the Americas, the Norte Chico centers on the desert coast of Peru around 3000 BCE, was funded not by grain at all but largely by an enormous marine-protein surplus, anchovies hauled from one of the richest fisheries on Earth and traded inland for cotton and crops (Early Andean Civilizations Ch 2). The Mississippian model is the classic river-valley-grain type; it is worth knowing it is a type, not the only road into complexity. North America by itself ran the river-valley path in the east and the desert-ingenuity path in the west at the same time, and got a complex society out of each.

The whole eastern half of the continent, before Europeans cleared it, was the **Eastern Woodlands**: a near-continuous deciduous and mixed forest of oak, hickory, chestnut, and pine, running from the Atlantic coast to the edge of the prairies, threaded by the rivers and dense with deer, turkey, fish, freshwater mussels, nuts, and wild seeds. It was not empty wilderness. People had been actively managing it for thousands of years before our story starts. They deliberately set low fires to clear the **understory** (the tangle of shrubs, saplings, and brush growing beneath the tall forest canopy), which killed competing brush, fertilized the soil with ash, and produced open, grassy ground that drew deer and turkey to graze and made them easier to hunt. They favored the nut trees, oak, hickory, and chestnut, by clearing competitors away from them and burning around them, so that the groves yielded heavier, more reliable mast (the fallen nut crop) that fed both people and the game they hunted. None of this is farming in the planted-field sense, but it is not passive foraging either. It was a worked landscape long before it was a farmed one, and that distinction is itself a quiet rebuttal of the idea that the people here did nothing with the land until Europeans showed them how.

---

People had been doing remarkable things in this landscape for an extremely long time before the first Mississippian mound was raised, and the depth of that background is itself a correction to the Mound Builder Myth, so it is worth walking through.

Long before farming, the **Archaic** peoples were already building monuments. "Archaic" is archaeology's broad label for the post–Ice Age, pre-agricultural Native cultures of North America: the hunters, gatherers, and fishers who moved through the woodlands and along the rivers after the last glaciers retreated. The Ice Age ended, very roughly, around 11,500 years ago (about 9500 BCE), so the Archaic spans thousands of years of foraging life before crops. The headline site is **Poverty Point**, in the lower Mississippi valley in what is now northeastern Louisiana. Built between roughly 1700 and 1100 BCE by hunter-gatherers (people with no agriculture and no pottery worth speaking of) Poverty Point is a vast complex of concentric earthen ridges and large mounds, the biggest of which, **Mound A**, holds a famously large volume of earth, all of it carried in baskets. The most-cited modeled estimate is on the order of 238,000 cubic yards (different studies put the figure somewhat higher or lower, because earthwork volumes are reconstructions, not measurements); that is enough fill to bury a football field under a layer roughly 50 yards deep, moved one basket at a time. The cross-civ anchor here should stop you for a second: 1700 BCE. When the people at Poverty Point were piling up Mound A, the Great Pyramid of Giza in Egypt was already over eight centuries old (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 4), Mesopotamia had had cities for two thousand years (Mesopotamia Ch 3), and the Shang dynasty was about to begin in China (Shang Dynasty Ch 1). Monumental construction in North America is not a late, borrowed idea. It is older than the Roman Republic, older than the oldest written portions of the Hebrew Bible (the foundational scriptures of Judaism, the core of the Christian Old Testament, whose earliest texts were composed roughly in the centuries after 1000 BCE), and it was done by foragers. Poverty Point predates Cahokia by more than two thousand years, a longer gap than the one between us and the founding of Rome.

These peoples were also farmers in waiting, in a way that the story usually leaves out. North America is one of only a handful of places on Earth where humans independently invented agriculture, the others being the Near East, China, Mesoamerica, the Andes, and a couple of smaller hearths. There is a sharp pattern hiding in that list: not all of these hearths were grain-based. The Andean hearth (Early Andean Civilizations Ch 1) domesticated the potato and other tubers rather than a cereal, and the eastern woodlands hearth, the one we are standing in, domesticated oily and starchy seed plants, not a true grain. Both the Andes and the eastern woodlands went on to build their early complex societies before maize, a grain, arrived from Mexico. The reflexive assumption that civilization needs a wheat-or-rice-type cereal is contradicted twice over inside the Americas alone.

The eastern woodlands version is called the **Eastern Agricultural Complex**: starting around 3000–1000 BCE, woodland peoples domesticated their own native plants, with no help from anyone else's crops. The roster includes **goosefoot** (a leafy seed plant whose tiny black seeds were cooked into mush or ground into flour; it is a close relative of the South American grain quinoa, itself a high-protein seed crop, and of garden spinach), **sumpweed** (also called marsh elder, grown for its oily, sunflower-like seeds, eaten for their fat and calories), **little barley**, **erect knotweed**, **maygrass**, and a thick-walled **squash/gourd**, plus the one survivor everyone has eaten, the domesticated **sunflower**. Native people in eastern North America were deliberately breeding crops from wild plants while the Bronze Age was happening in the Old World. That this rarely makes it into the national story is itself a small monument to the durability of the Mound Builder mindset.

---

Then come the mound-building cultures that lead directly into the Mississippian, and there are two big ones.

The **Adena** culture (roughly 800 BCE to 100 CE) was centered on the Ohio valley and reached, in its mound-building practice, across a broad swath of the upper Ohio drainage, parts of present-day Ohio, West Virginia, Kentucky, Indiana, and Pennsylvania. Its signature is the conical **burial mound**: an important person interred in a log tomb, the tomb covered with a heap of earth, and often more burials added over time, the mound growing with each one, which is, of course, exactly the layered structure Jefferson read off his Virginia trench in 1784 (Chapter 1). These were not small. The **Grave Creek Mound** in present-day West Virginia, the largest surviving Adena burial mound, stands about 62 feet high (19 m) and roughly 240 feet across at the base, a cone of earth raised entirely by basket over generations of accumulating burials. The Adena were Woodland-period people: they hunted, gathered, and tended the Eastern Agricultural Complex crops, lived in scattered settlements, and invested enormous communal effort in the dead, a thousand years before anyone here grew enough corn to feed a city.

The **Hopewell** culture (roughly 100 BCE to 500 CE), also centered in the Ohio valley but extending its influence vastly farther, is one of the genuine wonders of ancient North America and almost nobody outside the field has heard of it. The Hopewell built geometric earthworks of staggering scale and precision: embankments forming perfect circles, squares, and octagons, some of the figures hundreds of yards across, some of them aligned to the rising and setting points of the moon over its 18.6-year cycle (the same lunar standstill cycle the Chacoans tracked on the other side of the continent; Ancestral Puebloans Ch 3). The **Newark Earthworks** in Ohio originally covered more than four square miles, an area roughly the size of two thousand football fields, all of it laid out as connected circles, an octagon, and a square in surveyed geometry. The **Great Serpent Mound**, also in Ohio, is a quarter-mile-long earthen snake that contains no burials and was not a tomb; it appears to have been a purely ceremonial monument, a sacred image written into the landscape and a gathering place rather than a cemetery, its head aligned to the summer solstice sunset. The **Mound City** group (a different Mound City than St. Louis; this one is a Hopewell ceremonial site in Ohio) packed two dozen burial mounds inside an earthen enclosure.

What makes the Hopewell jaw-dropping is the reach of their exchange. Archaeologists call it the **Hopewell Interaction Sphere**: not an empire, not even a single people, but a continent-spanning web through which exotic materials flowed to the Ohio ceremonial centers, **obsidian** (volcanic glass) from the Rocky Mountains more than a thousand miles west, **marine shell** and shark teeth from the Gulf of Mexico, **copper** from the Great Lakes, **mica** from the southern Appalachians, and **grizzly bear teeth** from the far west. Hopewell artisans worked these into some of the most refined art made anywhere in the pre-Columbian north: delicate copper falcons, mica cut into the shape of a human hand, stone smoking pipes carved as astonishingly lifelike animals. The materials did not travel by anything resembling a merchant caravan, because there was no money, no market, no draft animal, and no wheel. They moved hand to hand and group to group, by gift, by exchange between ritual leaders, and by the journeys of people coming to the great ceremonial centers, an object passing through many hands across the distance rather than being shipped by one trader. This is the same kind of non-market prestige exchange that, at almost exactly the same centuries, ran jade, obsidian, and marine shell across the Maya world to the south through its own network of ceremonial cities (Maya Ch 1–2). Two continental exchange systems, neither using money or markets, both moving sacred and prestige materials through webs of ceremony rather than trade for profit. The mechanism gets its full explanation where it matters most, in the Mississippian world (Chapter 6).

---

There is one more ingredient, and it is the one that changes everything: corn.

**Maize** is the crop selectively bred over thousands of years from **teosinte** (pronounced "tay-oh-SIN-tay"), a wild Mexican grass with a tiny, hard, almost unrecognizable cob; the slow domestication of teosinte into corn happened in Mexico, and corn then spread outward from there. The same crop, traveling along the same northward diffusion route, fed the Maya and the Aztecs in Mesoamerica and reached the Ancestral Puebloans of the Southwest (Chapter 1; Ancestral Puebloans Ch 1). It reached eastern North America surprisingly early but mattered surprisingly late. Maize did not arrive by conquest or mass migration; it spread the way crops usually do, passed from one farming people to the next along the existing exchange and contact networks, a few seed ears at a time, generation by generation, until it had crossed thousands of miles from its Mexican origin. Traces of maize appear in the eastern woodlands by around 200 CE, in the Hopewell era, but for centuries it was a minor garnish. People grew a little of it alongside their native Eastern Agricultural Complex crops; it was not the staple, and society was not built around it. The reason is partly genetic: early maize was not well adapted to the shorter growing seasons and longer summer days of the temperate east, and it took centuries of local selection to breed a hardy, productive **flint corn** (a hard-kerneled variety that matured fast enough for a short northern season and stored well as dried grain) that thrived there.

That breakthrough, productive cold-tolerant maize grown intensively on the rich river floodplains, does not pay off until after about 900 CE. When it does, the consequences are not gradual. A floodplain like the American Bottom, planted in this improved maize, could feed far more people per acre than wild foods or the old native crops, and could generate a *storable surplus*: granaries of dried corn that carried a community through winter and freed labor from the daily food hunt. Surplus is the hinge on which everything in the next eight chapters turns. Surplus means population can rise. Rising population concentrated on the best floodplain means villages become towns. A storable surplus can be controlled, gathered, and redistributed by someone, which means it can underwrite leaders, specialists, ceremony, and the mass labor to pile up a hundred-foot mountain of earth. There is a continental coincidence worth pausing on: the maize that crossed the continent set off surplus-funded, monument-building society on both ends of it at almost the same time. The Ancestral Puebloans' great-house era in the Southwest and the Mississippian emergence in the eastern river valleys both ignite after roughly 900 CE, on the same crop, on opposite sides of North America, neither aware the other existed (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 1, 4). Around 900 to 1000 CE, on the American Bottom, all of those switches got thrown at roughly the same time. The result has a name.

# Chapter 3 — The Mississippian Emergence

The word **Mississippian** is going to do a lot of work in this book, and it does not mean what the parallel words usually mean in history, so it is worth being careful.

"Mississippian" is not the name of an empire, the way "Roman" names an empire. It is not the name of one people, the way "the Maya" names a people, or one language, or one dynasty. There was never a Mississippian emperor, never a Mississippian capital that ruled the rest, never a Mississippian nation. The word is an archaeologist's label for a *shared way of living* that appeared across the river valleys of the eastern and southeastern United States starting around 900 CE and lasting, in places, until European contact and beyond. Many different peoples, speaking languages from several unrelated families, independently adopted and adapted the same recognizable bundle of practices over a few generations. These language families are as different from one another as English is from Arabic, not regional accents but separate linguistic stocks: **Muskogean** (the family of the Choctaw, Chickasaw, Creek, and Natchez of the Deep South), **Siouan** (spoken by nations of the mid-continent and the Ohio and Mississippi valleys, related to the Plains Sioux), **Caddoan** (the family of the Caddo of the Red and Arkansas river country to the west), and others. Picture less "the Roman Empire" and more "the medieval Christian world": a Norwegian and a Sicilian in 1200 CE shared a religion, an architecture of cathedrals, a calendar of feasts, and a web of trade and pilgrimage, while belonging to entirely different kingdoms and speaking unrelated tongues. There is an even closer parallel inside the Americas: the broad Mesoamerican cultural sphere, in which the Maya, the later Aztecs, and their neighbors shared a ritual ballgame, a feathered-serpent religious iconography, a 260-day sacred calendar, and a common stock of sacred imagery inherited ultimately from the Olmec, while remaining politically fragmented and speaking mutually unintelligible languages (Maya Ch 1). A shared ceremonial package spreading across unrelated peoples with no single ruler over them is a recurring American pattern, and Mississippian is the eastern-woodlands instance of it. Mississippian is that kind of word.

So what was in the bundle? Six things, and it is worth seeing each one concretely rather than as a checklist.

**Platform mounds and plazas.** This is the most visible marker and the one the mound builders are named for. Note the change from what came before: the earlier Adena and Hopewell mounds (Chapter 2) were mostly conical **burial mounds**, dome-shaped heaps raised over tombs, monuments to the dead. The signature Mississippian mound is a different thing entirely, a **platform mound**: a large, flat-topped earthen pyramid, not pointed like an Egyptian pyramid but truncated, like a pyramid with the top sliced off to make a raised stage. It was built not as a tomb but as a foundation for the living. On the flat summit stood the most important buildings: the chief's house, a temple, a charnel house holding the bones of noble ancestors. The mounds were arranged around a deliberately flattened open **plaza**, a community-scale public square for ceremony, games, markets, and assembly. A Mississippian town center was a designed civic space, not a random cluster of huts.

**Intensive maize agriculture.** Floodplain cornfields, worked with stone, shell, and wooden hoes, producing the storable surplus that paid for everything else (Chapter 2).

**Shell-tempered pottery.** Mississippian potters mixed crushed mussel shell into their clay before firing. **Temper** is any gritty material added to raw clay to keep a pot from cracking as it dries and bakes; potters had long used crushed rock or sand. Switching to crushed shell was a genuine technical advance. It let them make thinner, stronger vessels that better survived **thermal shock**, the sudden stress of being heated and cooled, which is what cracks an ordinary cooking pot set directly on a fire. Shell-tempered pots could be set on the coals and reheated again and again without shattering, which matters for a society cooking a lot of corn. Because the trait spread along with the rest of the package, a shell-tempered potsherd in the dirt is one of the quickest field signals that you are standing on a Mississippian site.

**Ranked chiefdoms.** Hereditary inequality, organized around a sacred ruling line. This needs its own treatment; see below.

**The chunkey game.** A specific, named sport played across the entire Mississippian world: a polished stone disk rolled across a smooth court while players hurled long poles to land where the disk would stop. It was not just a game; it was a continent-wide shared institution, a focus of mass gambling, social bonding, and politics, and it is striking enough that it gets its full description with the rest of the Mississippian world (Chapter 6).

**A shared art-religion system.** A common vocabulary of sacred images recurring across the whole region: a winged human warrior in falcon costume (the central figure, the one later chapters will call the Birdman), a cross set inside a circle (almost certainly a sun-and-fire and four-directions symbol), a "forked eye" marking copied from the facial pattern of a falcon, a horned serpent monster, and a stylized human hand. These were engraved on shell, hammered into copper, and painted on pottery from Oklahoma to Georgia. Scholars call this shared image-system the **Southeastern Ceremonial Complex**, sometimes the "Southern Cult." It is the closest thing the Mississippian world had to a shared scripture, except that it was pictures, not words, and we are still learning to read it; it becomes the dominant religious-art thread of the next several chapters (Chapters 5 and 6).

---

Now the load-bearing term: **chiefdom**. The book will use it constantly and it is precise, not vague.

Anthropologists arrange human societies along a rough scale of political complexity, and three rungs of it matter here. A **tribe** (or "band," for the smallest) is a society of relatively equal people organized by **kinship**, meaning that the bonds holding the group together are family ties, real and reckoned: descent, marriage, and clan membership do the work that laws and offices do in a state. Leadership in a tribe is informal, situational, and not inherited (a respected elder, a war leader for one campaign) with no permanent ruling office. A **state** is at the other end: a society with a permanent government, a bureaucracy of administrators, written records or some equivalent, full-time specialists, taxation, standing armed force, and rulers whose authority is institutional rather than personal. Between them sits the **chiefdom**: a society with permanent, *hereditary*, ranked leadership, a chief whose authority is inherited within a particular lineage and who outranks ordinary people by birth, but *without* the apparatus of a state. No bureaucracy. No writing. No professional standing army. No taxation in the formal sense. Instead the chief gathers and redistributes surplus and labor through a web of kinship obligation, gift, and **tribute**. Tribute differs from a tax: a tax is a compulsory, accounted payment owed to an institution and enforced by the state's machinery, while tribute is goods and labor rendered up to a superior chief as an act of submission and relationship, owed to a person and his sacred role, not to an office, and backed by obligation and prestige rather than a salaried enforcement apparatus. His power is bound up with that sacred and ceremonial role rather than with administration. A chiefdom can be enormous and impressive and still have no government in the sense a modern reader assumes.

This is exactly the category the Mississippian polities fall into, and getting it right matters because the reader's instinct, trained on the Old World, is to reach for "kingdom." Cross-civ context, and it cuts in two directions. The contemporaneous Mesoamerican civilizations were past this rung: the Maya city-states (Maya Ch 3) and especially the later Aztec Empire (Aztec Empire Ch 4) were full states with kings, scribes, tribute bureaucracies, standing warriors, and writing. The Inca ran a continent-spanning bureaucratic empire (Inca Empire Ch 5) with no writing in the usual sense, but with a state apparatus of every other kind: a professional administrative class, a census, mandatory labor levies, state warehouses, an army, and a knotted-cord accounting system (the *quipu*) doing the record-keeping that script does elsewhere. So "no writing" did not by itself keep a society a chiefdom; the Inca proved a stateless-of-script empire was possible. But the comparison also runs the friendly way. Before the Inca were an empire, the Inca were a chiefdom: the small Cuzco-basin polity of the generations before the conqueror Pachacuti was a hereditary-ranked, surplus-redistributing chiefdom of exactly the kind defined here (Inca Empire Ch 1). Two societies that both started as chiefdoms; one crossed into a continental state and one did not, which is one of the genuinely interesting questions to hold while reading. The Mississippian chiefdoms reached the threshold of monumental, hierarchical, surplus-funded society and *stopped there*, never crossing into statehood, and the most spectacular of them, the one in the next chapter, may have pushed further than any other. That is exactly the case scholars argue about: some read the biggest Mississippian polity as a complex paramount chiefdom, others as something approaching an early state, and the disagreement turns on how much centralized political control its scale actually implies. The honest position is that "chiefdom" is the consensus label and a useful one, but how complex the biggest Mississippian polity got is genuinely debated, and this book will flag the debate rather than settle it.

---

The engine under all of it was the maize surplus, and the causal chain is worth stating once, cleanly, because it recurs.

Improved flint maize, grown intensively on rich floodplain, produces far more storable calories per acre than the old foraged-and-gardened diet. More storable calories mean more people can live in one place and survive the winter, so population rises and concentrates. A storable surplus is also a controllable surplus: whoever organizes its collection, storage, and redistribution (for feasts, for lean years, for paying labor) accumulates influence, and if that role is inherited within a lineage, you have hereditary rank. A ranked society with a surplus can mobilize mass labor for projects that produce no food at all: piling up a platform mound is, in pure caloric terms, a society announcing that it can afford to spend tens of thousands of person-days moving dirt in baskets. The mounds are not just monuments. They are a visible accounting statement of agricultural surplus and the social power to direct it. Wherever in the eastern river valleys the maize-surplus switch got thrown (the American Bottom, the Black Warrior valley in Alabama, the Etowah valley in Georgia, the Arkansas valley) some version of this chain ran, and a Mississippian chiefdom appeared.

That chain ran in dozens of river valleys, and the result by 1100–1200 CE was a Mississippian world recognizable from the Arkansas River in Oklahoma to the Atlantic side of Georgia, from the Gulf coast up the Mississippi nearly to Wisconsin and up the Ohio and Tennessee deep into the interior, an area larger than Western Europe, occupied not by one polity but by dozens of independent, often hostile chiefdoms sharing a way of life and no government. But one instance of the chain ran hotter and faster than all the others. In one place the surplus switch did not just produce a chiefdom; it produced, almost overnight, the largest city anyone would build in what is now the United States for the next seven hundred years. It was on the American Bottom, and the builders never wrote down what they called it, so we have to call it something.

# Chapter 4 — Cahokia: The Big Bang

The name is **Cahokia** (pronounced "kuh-HOH-kee-uh"), and it is not the original name. It is the name of a group of historic-period people, the Cahokia, one nation within the **Illinois Confederacy** (a loose alliance of related Algonquian-speaking nations of the upper Mississippi and Illinois country, the people the French dealt with in the region), who were living in the area when the French arrived in the late 1600s, long after the city was abandoned, and who did not build it. We do not know what its builders called it, because they did not write, and the descendant nations who carry related traditions do not, in general, claim it by a single name. So we call the largest city in the ancient United States by the name of people who arrived after it was already ruins. This happens constantly in this story; it is the historiographical equivalent of the buildings being named by men who had no idea what they were looking at.

Cahokia sits on the **American Bottom** (Chapter 2), the wide, exceptionally fertile Mississippi floodplain directly across the river from modern St. Louis, in what is now southern Illinois. People had farmed there for centuries in scattered villages. And then, around the year 1050 CE, something happened that archaeologists call, with a borrowed-from-cosmology wink, the **"Big Bang."** The joke is in the borrowing: the term is lifted from the origin of the universe, deliberately oversized for a city, and the wink acknowledges that nobody can be quite that precise about an event with no written date. It is half a joke and half a serious claim, and the serious half is the part that matters.

The Big Bang is a real and important interpretive claim, so it is worth being precise about what it asserts and that it is the leading reading rather than an uncontested one. The claim is that Cahokia did not grow slowly, the way most cities do, accreting over centuries. Instead, within a span of perhaps one to two decades around 1050 CE, the area was deliberately and rapidly reorganized: existing villages were cleared, the ground was leveled and re-planned on a new layout, population surged in from the surrounding region, and monumental construction began on a scale with no local precedent. The evidence for a sudden, planned reorganization rather than gradual growth is now broadly accepted among Cahokia specialists (the construction dates cluster tightly, and neighborhoods were demonstrably leveled and rebuilt on a new plan) though the word "Big Bang" is a vivid label for a process whose exact tempo and degree of central planning are still argued. What is not seriously argued is that something fast and intentional happened around 1050. Cahokia was, to a meaningful degree, *founded*, the way a planned capital is founded, not grown the way a market town grows.

It is worth a glance at what else was happening in the Americas at that exact moment, because none of these societies knew the others existed. Around 1050 CE the Ancestral Puebloans' Chaco Canyon system in the Southwest was at or near its great-house peak (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 4), and on the desert coast of Peru the Chimor kingdom was on its way up toward building Chan Chan (Inca Empire Ch 1). Three very different large-scale organized societies, on the same continents, building or peaking within the same few generations, in total mutual ignorance of one another.

---

The centerpiece is **Monks Mound**, and the numbers on it are the kind that this book is obligated to anchor for you because they are otherwise just digits.

Monks Mound is a flat-topped earthen pyramid that rises in four terraces to about 100 feet high (30 m), roughly a ten-story building, with a base covering about 14 acres (5.7 hectares). That footprint is larger than the base of the Great Pyramid of Giza. Read that again: the largest earthen mound in the Americas covers more ground than Egypt's largest stone pyramid (Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 4). It is not the tallest pyramid in the Americas; the Maya outbuilt it for sheer height more than a thousand years earlier, with structures like La Danta at El Mirador rising over 200 feet (Maya Ch 1). But those are stone-faced rubble mountains; Monks Mound is the largest single earthen construction in the hemisphere, and it represents the same logic, a society converting an agricultural surplus into a communal pyramid by sheer mass labor. It contains on the order of 22 million cubic feet of earth (a modeled estimate; sources differ, hence the "on the order of"). Every cubic foot of it was dug with stone, wood, and shell tools, loaded into woven baskets, and carried on human backs and shoulders to the growing mound, one basket-load (roughly the weight of a full grocery bag) at a time. There were no wheelbarrows, because there was no wheel in use here. There were no oxen or horses, because there were no draft animals in North America. There were no metal tools. The labor estimate runs to millions of basket-loads and a sustained workforce over decades. The name, incidentally, has nothing to do with the builders: a community of **Trappist monks** (a strict, contemplative Catholic monastic order, French in origin) briefly settled near the mound and gardened on its terraces in the early 1800s, and the throwaway label of those transient newcomers is what stuck to the largest monument in ancient North America. The Mound Builder Myth, again, in miniature.

On the flattened summit of Monks Mound stood a very large building (its postholes survive) almost certainly the residence and ceremonial seat of Cahokia's paramount ruler, lifted a hundred feet above everyone else, visible for miles across the flat bottomland, the human apex of the city placed physically at its highest point. Architecture as a statement of where power sat: that is not a modern projection onto the site; it is the most parsimonious reading of why you put the biggest building on top of the biggest mound.

Spread at the foot of Monks Mound was the **Grand Plaza**, and it is an engineering artifact in its own right, not just empty ground. It is roughly 50 acres of open public space, and excavation has shown it was deliberately *engineered flat*: the natural undulating floodplain surface was cut down in the high spots and filled in the low spots to produce a vast, level ceremonial field. Leveling 50 acres by basket is itself a monumental act that leaves no monument; you only notice it if you check, and archaeologists checked. Around and beyond the Grand Plaza stood roughly 120 more mounds in the greater Cahokia area, platform mounds and conical burial mounds and ridge-top mounds, the whole arrangement organized on planned alignments rather than scattered at random.

To the west of Monks Mound stood **Woodhenge**: a series of large circles of upright **red cedar** posts (a wood that resists rot, important for posts set in the ground, and one widely treated as sacred and purifying in later Eastern Woodlands religious practice, so the choice was probably practical and ceremonial at once), rebuilt several times at increasing diameter, the largest reconstruction estimated at roughly 48 posts in a ring nearly 410 feet across. The instrument worked by sightline. An observer standing at a fixed post near the center watched the sun come up over the horizon; on the morning of a key date, the sun rose directly behind one specific perimeter post as seen from that center point. Because the sunrise point on the horizon swings north and south through the year, different perimeter posts caught the sunrise at the spring and fall equinoxes and at the summer and winter solstices, so the ring functioned as a fixed calendar for timing the agricultural and ceremonial year, built of wood on the flat. It is the eastern woodlands' answer to the solar architecture the Chacoans built into stone and butte (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 3): two societies on opposite sides of the continent, neither with writing, both engineering the sky into the ground to keep a calendar.

---

Now the hardest number in the book, and the rule about contested claims (it is the number-one accuracy risk here) applies at full strength: **how many people lived at Cahokia?**

This is genuinely, sharply debated, and you should distrust anyone who gives you a single confident figure. The honest statement is a range and a disagreement. Estimates for the urban core of Cahokia itself cluster somewhere around 10,000 to 15,000 people at its peak, roughly 1100 CE, with some estimates lower and a few older ones much higher. Estimates for *greater Cahokia*, meaning the central city plus the dense ring of associated towns and farmsteads across the American Bottom, run higher still, to perhaps 30,000 to 40,000. That greater figure folds in two nearby major mound centers, the **East St. Louis** group (a large mound complex just across Cahokia Creek, the second-biggest in the region) and the **St. Louis** group (the "Mound City" mounds of Chapter 1, on the far bank of the Mississippi), which were part of the same metropolitan cluster. The estimates disagree because the methods disagree, and they disagree in a consistent direction: counting excavated house floors and projecting outward tends to yield the lower numbers, while modeling how many people the American Bottom's farmland could carry tends to yield the higher ones, and the carrying-capacity approach assumes the land was farmed near its limit while the house-count approach assumes the excavated samples are typical. The spread between them is the truthful answer. What is *not* in dispute is the comparative claim, and it is the one worth carrying: by any of these estimates, Cahokia around 1100 CE was the largest settlement north of the great cities of Mexico. It would not be matched or surpassed by any settlement within the present-day United States until a city like **Philadelphia** around the year 1800 (Philadelphia held roughly 40,000 to 70,000 people in that era, depending on the exact year and whether you count the adjacent districts; the comparison is meant as an order-of-magnitude landmark, not a precise tie). For something like seven hundred years, the biggest city ever built in what is now the U.S. was a Native city of earthen pyramids on the Mississippi.

Cross-civ context, set against the contemporaneous world of about 1100 CE to make the scale legible: this is the era of Song-dynasty China, whose capital **Kaifeng** held perhaps half a million or more people (Tang–Song China Ch 6), a different order of magnitude entirely, and a useful reminder that "largest in North America" and "large by world standards" are not the same claim. **Constantinople** and Islamic **Córdoba** were among the great cities of the medieval world. The anchor closest to home is the English one: **London** in 1100 CE, the largest city in England, is usually estimated at only around 15,000 to 20,000 people (medieval urban populations are themselves reconstructed estimates, and some historians put early-medieval London somewhat higher, but it was unmistakably small by later standards). Cahokia was, very plausibly, in London's league or larger at the same moment, a city on the Mississippi the size of the capital of England, built by people the Mound Builder Myth insisted could not build a city at all. It is worth placing it among its actual Western Hemisphere contemporaries, too. The southern Maya cities had already been through their Classic-period collapse by 1100 (Maya Ch 5), and the surviving Postclassic Maya centers were generally smaller than Classic Tikal had been. The Aztec capital Tenochtitlan, eventually the largest city in the Americas, would not even be founded for another two centuries. The contemporaneous Chimor kingdom on the Peruvian coast was building Chan Chan, which by around 1300 CE held perhaps 60,000 people and would surpass Cahokia (Inca Empire Ch 1). At its 1100 CE peak, in other words, Cahokia was one of the larger cities anywhere in the hemisphere, in the same range as the Postclassic Maya centers, and would only be exceeded later, far to the south, by Chan Chan.

---

# Chapter 5 — Inside the City of the Sun

We have built the city from the outside. Now go in, with the standing reminder that "in" is reconstructed from postholes, trash pits, burials, and art, never from a Cahokian telling us anything in words.

At the top, literally, on Monks Mound, sat the **paramount chief**: the supreme hereditary ruler, the apex of a ranked society, his house and the principal temple raised a hundred feet above the city. Mississippian paramount chiefs across the Southeast, where we can see them later through Spanish and French eyes (Chapters 7 and 9), were treated as sacred persons, carried on **litters** (a litter is a platform or seat borne on the shoulders of attendants, so a high-status person travels without their feet touching the ground, a piece of portable status the Spanish would later describe in detail), addressed through intermediaries, and often associated with the sun. We cannot quote Cahokia's ruler or even name his office in his own language. But the architecture (supreme building on the supreme mound over the engineered plaza) is a hierarchy frozen in earth, and reading it as such is not a modern imposition; it is what the dirt shows.

The single most discussed feature of Cahokia, and the one where the contested-claims rule has to be applied hardest, is a relatively modest-looking burial mound on the city's edge called **Mound 72**.

Here is what excavation found, stated as findings before interpretation. Inside Mound 72 was a sequence of burials including a central pair of bodies, one laid upon a dense bed of roughly 20,000 small **marine-shell disc beads**. Each bead is an individually shaped, drilled disc of seashell about the size of a lentil; twenty thousand of them, laid out together, formed a single huge shape against which a body was placed, and the shape read as a bird. This is the burial known as the **"Birdman" burial**, named for that bird-shaped bead bed and for the falcon-warrior figure of Mississippian sacred art (the Birdman, fully explained with the Southeastern Ceremonial Complex in Chapter 6) that the form evokes. Near and around this central feature were additional mass burials: groups of people, including pits containing rows of young women (the analyzed individuals are largely young adults, roughly in their late teens to mid-twenties) laid out in orderly fashion, and a separate group of men, some lacking heads and hands.

Now the interpretation, and the discipline the writing rules demand: the *leading* scholarly reading of the mass burials is that some of these people were sacrificed (retainers or offerings killed and interred as part of the funerary ritual for an elite person) a practice documented for sacred kingship in a number of other ancient societies. That reading is plausible and is held by serious researchers. But it is explicitly *contested and partly reinterpreted*. Recent re-analysis of the Mound 72 skeletons has revised earlier accounts substantially: in particular, the long-retold claim that the central "Birdman" burial was two men turned out to be wrong (the central pair includes a female), which dismantled some of the most dramatic earlier storytelling about the site. The age and sex composition, the cause of death, the relationships among the burial groups, and the very question of whether this was human sacrifice at all rather than reburial of people who died otherwise are all actively argued in the literature. This book follows the rule deliberately: the vivid version, a king's mass human sacrifice, is the kind of colorful claim that is most tempting and least safe to assert as fact. The truthful statement is that Mound 72 contains elite burials and mass burials, that ritual killing is the leading interpretation of at least some of the mass component, and that the specifics have been significantly revised and remain debated. The reader can hold that uncertainty; it is more honest than the dramatic certainty.

---

What the Cahokians believed has to be reconstructed sideways: from their art, from the Southeastern Ceremonial Complex imagery (Chapter 6), and, with explicit caution, from the later religious traditions of descendant and related peoples. The most-used later sources are the recorded cosmologies of **Dhegihan-Siouan**-speaking nations (the Dhegihan languages are one branch of the large Siouan language family; the Osage and Omaha are the best-documented examples, and yes, the Siouan family is the same one the Plains Sioux belong to, a different branch of it) and of the historic Southeastern peoples, whose religious traditions plausibly preserve old structures. The flag matters: what follows is a careful inference from imagery plus later tradition, not a transcript.

The easiest way into this is not the abstract scheme but a single object you could put a hand on. Picture one of the **embossed copper plates** from the Etowah site in Georgia (Chapter 6), a sheet of hammered native copper roughly the length of a forearm, worked in low relief. The figure on it is a man in profile, in mid-stride as if dancing, wearing a falcon's wings spread from his arms and a forked marking under the eye that copies the facial pattern of a peregrine falcon. In one hand he holds a war club; in the other, a severed human head. That single image is the doorway: a human who is also a raptor, a warrior, a dancer, and a being of the sky, all at once. Hold that plate in mind, because the cosmology is essentially the world that figure lives in.

That world has three tiers. There is an **Above World**, orderly and associated with the sun, the sky, birds of prey, and a celestial hero. There is **This World**, the flat human earth in the middle, where people and most animals live. And there is a **Beneath World**, watery, chaotic, and powerful, associated with serpents and a monstrous creature of the deep. The man on the copper plate is the central figure of the Above World, the **Birdman**: a human with the wings, talons, and weapon-bearing posture of a falcon, a warrior-hero of the sky, the same identity laid into the 20,000-bead bird shape in Mound 72. In later Siouan oral tradition a hero with strikingly similar attributes is remembered under names like **Red Horn**, and the figure is widely associated with the **Morning Star** (the planet Venus seen as a brilliant point of light just before dawn, a recurring war-and-hero symbol across the region). The great power of the opposing Beneath World is the **Underwater Panther**, sometimes called the **Great Serpent**: in the surviving art it is depicted as a composite monster, with the body and snarling face of a great cat fused to the long scaled tail and sometimes the horns of a serpent, a creature of deep water that is dangerous but not exactly evil, the necessary chaotic counterweight to the sky's order. A third widespread figure is a maternal, regenerative power tied to corn, the earth, and life renewing itself; she appears in related traditions under names like the **Old-Woman-Who-Never-Dies** and the **Corn Mother** (these are regional names for the same kind of figure, an agricultural earth-mother, not a single fixed deity with one biography). This corn-and-earth-mother is one of the most widespread religious figures in the Americas: the Maya Maize God, the Aztec maize goddess, and the Andean earth-mother Pachamama are the same broad type of agricultural-cycle deity in other regions (Maya Ch 1; Aztec Empire Ch 5). The three-tier scheme itself is also a pan-American pattern: the Maya cosmos (sky, this world, and the watery underworld Xibalba) and the Aztec cosmos (stacked heavens, earth, and an underworld of the dead) are structural near-matches for the Mississippian Above World, This World, and Beneath World, each with a watery, dangerous lower realm and a hero who contends with it (Maya Ch 1; Aztec Empire Ch 5). North America's other great cosmological architecture works differently: set against the Ancestral Puebloan emergence cosmos with its **sipapu**, the small symbolic hole in the floor of every **kiva** (a kiva is a round, usually semi-subterranean Puebloan ceremonial chamber) marking the spot where the people climbed up out of the worlds below (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 2), the Mississippian structure is a different design from the same continent: stacked sky-earth-water layers rather than an ascent through nested underworlds. Whether the Mississippian Birdman and the raptor-warrior imagery of Mesoamerica (the Aztec eagle warriors, the Maya raptor-costumed war lords) are connected by the same Mexican diffusion that carried maize north, or are independent inventions of a symbol that recurs in agricultural societies, is genuinely debated and not settled (Aztec Empire Ch 3; Maya Ch 3).

---

That sacred architecture, the Birdman world made physical in mounds and plaza, did not hold together peacefully forever, and the clearest evidence is a single structure that contradicts the serene-theocracy picture entirely: the **palisade**. At some point after the city's peak, the Cahokians built a massive defensive wall of close-set timber posts, studded at intervals with **bastions** (projecting platforms or towers built out from a wall so that defenders standing on them could shoot arrows along the face of the wall at attackers trying to approach it), enclosing the central precinct: Monks Mound, the Grand Plaza, the sacred core. It cost an estimated 20,000 large logs and was *rebuilt at least three times*. You do not spend that much labor walling off the heart of your own city, repeatedly, against nobody. The palisade is read as evidence of real conflict and real social stress: defense against outside enemies, internal factional violence, the deliberate sealing-off of the sacred core from the commoner population, or some combination. Like the Ancestral Puebloans' shift into defensible cliff alcoves (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 6), a society's investment in walls is one of the loudest signals in the archaeological record that something was going wrong, and Cahokia rebuilding its palisade three times is a stress reading that becomes important when the city comes apart (Chapter 8).

That same sacred order also organized the ordinary working life of the city; the cosmos was not separate from the daily grind, it was its layout and its calendar. The **Rattlesnake Causeway** was a raised earthen avenue running south from the Grand Plaza, an axis that put the city's sacred geography into a deliberate line on the ground rather than leaving it scattered. Cahokia had specialist crafts: workshops producing finely drilled shell beads using **microliths** (very small chipped-stone tools, here tiny drill bits and blades; the term covers a broader class of miniature stone implements, not drills alone), and a distinctive fine pottery, **Ramey Incised**, whose cosmological motifs were cut into the surface of the wet clay with a tool before firing, which is what "incised" means (lines carved or scratched in, as opposed to painted on). That branded ceremonial ware was distributed outward from Cahokia, sacred objects moving through the same hierarchy the mounds expressed. A few miles to the east, the **Emerald site** was an acropolis of mounds that appears to have functioned as a pilgrimage and ceremonial destination, drawing people in from the countryside on a ritual schedule, so Cahokia's hinterland was tied to it ceremonially as well as economically. The enormous pits dug to get the dirt for the mounds, called **borrow pits** (the dirt was "borrowed" from the ground to build the mound, a piece of excavator's slang that stuck), were reused as the trash middens of mass events, and the food remains in them (quantities of deer and the unmistakable debris of very large gatherings) are direct physical evidence of **mass feasting** on the Grand Plaza: the chief's surplus, converted into the political glue of a giant communal meal, the redistributive engine of the chiefdom made literal. Around all of it spread the farming hinterland: thousands of dispersed farmsteads working the American Bottom's corn, the agricultural base that the whole top-heavy sacred structure stood on.

# Chapter 6 — The Mississippian World

Cahokia was the biggest, the first, and the most spectacular, but it was never the whole of the Mississippian world, and to think of it as a capital that ruled the rest is to make exactly the Old World mistake the chiefdom definition (Chapter 3) was meant to prevent. The Mississippian world was dozens of independent centers, contemporaneous and successive, often at war with each other, sharing a way of life and a sacred art but no government. Walk the map.

**Moundville**, on the Black Warrior River in central Alabama, was a planned ceremonial city flourishing roughly 1120 to 1450 CE, its great period running on while Cahokia was already declining, which is itself the point of Chapter 8. At its height it had more than 29 mounds arranged around a large rectangular plaza, the whole site enclosed by a palisade, with the layout apparently planned around social rank. Moundville produced some of the finest Mississippian art, including a famous engraved **stone palette** (a palette here is a flat, disc-shaped stone tablet, probably used as a surface for grinding and mixing the mineral pigments used in body and object painting, with its rim and face carved in relief). Its best-known example carries a central hand-and-eye motif (an open hand with an eye marked in the palm) ringed by a border of intertwined rattlesnakes; the hand-and-eye and the rattlesnake band are both standard Southeastern Ceremonial Complex images, and this palette is one of the signature objects of the whole tradition (the exact reading of the design is interpreted from the carving itself, and the descriptive details here follow the standard published accounts of the Moundville palettes).

**Etowah**, on the Etowah River in northern Georgia, is dominated by a platform mound over 60 feet tall and is famous for the contents of its **Mound C**: elite burials accompanied by extraordinary grave goods, including the embossed copper plates known as the **Rogan plates** (named for John Rogan, the Smithsonian Bureau of Ethnology field assistant who excavated them at Etowah in the early 1880s during the very mound survey Cyrus Thomas was running; Chapter 1), each hammered into the image of a winged, falcon-costumed dancer, the Birdman, in beaten copper. These are the same kind of copper Birdman plate described in the previous chapter (Chapter 5). Etowah was still a powerful chiefdom when the Spanish came through (Chapter 9).

**Spiro**, on the Arkansas River in eastern Oklahoma, holds one of the most important and most heartbreaking sites in North American archaeology. Inside its **Craig Mound** was a hollow chamber archaeologists call the **Great Mortuary**: a dry, sealed space that had preserved, for centuries, an unparalleled trove of Mississippian art, including engraved marine-shell cups and **gorgets** (a gorget is a flat ornamental pendant of shell or copper, often elaborately engraved, worn on the chest on a cord around the neck), embossed copper, basketry, textiles, carved wood, and pearls. It is the single richest assemblage of perishable Mississippian art ever found, and most of it was destroyed in the act of discovery. The mound stood on privately owned farmland, and in the legal vacuum of the time there was no federal antiquities law protecting archaeological sites on private property; in the early 1930s the landowners leased the digging rights to a group of commercial relic diggers, the **Pocola Mining Company**, who tunneled into the Great Mortuary and sold its contents to relic dealers, scattering and breaking irreplaceable objects. When the lease was running out they used explosives on the mound (accounts agree dynamite was used at Spiro to break into and through the chamber; the exact sequence is reconstructed from witnesses and the wreckage). The scientific knowledge in that chamber (the relationships among the objects, the context that gives artifacts their meaning) was annihilated for cash. The Spiro catastrophe is the Mississippian equivalent of the looting that destroyed so much of the Puebloan record (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 8). The public outrage over Spiro is part of why Oklahoma passed an antiquities act in the 1930s and why the United States eventually built stronger federal protection for archaeological sites, culminating decades later in the laws discussed in Chapter 10. Spiro is the western edge of a continent of centers, and only a few of the others can be named here: **Ocmulgee** in central Georgia and **Town Creek** in North Carolina anchored the Atlantic side, while **Angel** and **Kincaid** held the lower Ohio. Out west on the Red and Arkansas drainages were the **Caddo**, a distinct but related tradition: Caddoan-speaking (the **Caddo** are the people; **Caddoan** is the language family they belong to, named after them; Chapter 3), with their own mound-and-temple complexes and a famous fine pottery, a western variant working the Mississippian package on its own terms.

---

If those scattered centers shared no government, what held the Mississippian world together as a *world* rather than a set of unrelated towns? Two things did: a system of exchange and a system of sacred images. Take the exchange first, because the *mechanism* matters, and the mechanism was not a market.

The list of goods moving long distances is staggering: **marine shell** (whelk and conch) from the Gulf and Atlantic coasts, worked into beads, cups, and gorgets; **copper** from the Great Lakes, worked cold by hammering rather than **smelted** (smelting is heating ore hot enough to chemically free the metal and then casting or alloying it; the Mississippians did none of that and had no metallurgy in that sense, only cold-hammered native copper); **chert hoe blades** from the **Mill Creek** quarries in southern Illinois, the era's premier farming tool, traded out across hundreds of miles; **galena** (a heavy, shiny lead-gray mineral, lead ore, ground into a sparkling silvery-gray powder used as pigment on bodies and objects), **mica**, and Cahokia's branded **Ramey Incised** pottery. None of it moved through anything like a market, because there were no markets, no money, and no merchants in the Mississippian world. The mechanism was **gift, prestige, and tribute exchange**: rare and sacred materials moved between elites as diplomatic gifts, as bride-wealth and alliance-sealing presents, as tribute flowing toward paramount chiefs and prestige goods flowing back out, and as offerings to ceremonial centers. The value was social and sacred, not commercial; a copper falcon plate was not a commodity with a price but a charged object whose movement between chiefs created and maintained relationships. This is the same kind of system that moved obsidian across the Hopewell Interaction Sphere a thousand years earlier (Chapter 2), and it is worth flagging that it is not a primitive version of a market; it is a different institution that does work markets do not, binding distant elites into a web of obligation. The cold-hammered-copper detail is sharper when placed against the rest of the hemisphere: by the same centuries, Andean smiths had been smelting and alloying bronze for generations and Mesoamerican smiths were casting gold and copper (Early Andean Civilizations Ch 4; Aztec Empire Ch 4), so North America north of Mexico stands out as a region that did fine metalwork while deliberately never crossing into smelting. Old World comparison: the prestige-gift economies of, for instance, the early Germanic and Viking worlds (Viking Age Ch 3), where a lord's **arm-rings** (heavy precious-metal bands worn on the arm, given by a lord to bind warriors to him, both ornament and political currency) and a gifted sword were instruments of politics, not items for sale, ran on the same logic. So did the long-distance shell trade elsewhere in the Americas: the Maya moved Caribbean and Gulf marine shell deep inland, and the Andean civilizations carried the tropical *spondylus* shell from the warm Ecuadorian coast up into the highlands as a sacred material (Maya Ch 2; Early Andean Civilizations Ch 4; Inca Empire Ch 4). Three American civilizations, moving sea shell hundreds of miles through non-market sacred exchange at the same time, none aware of the others.

The other binding system was the shared art that traveled those same routes, the **Southeastern Ceremonial Complex**, the "Southern Cult": a recurring set of sacred images executed in shell, copper, and pottery from Oklahoma to Georgia. Its core motifs are remarkably consistent: the **falcon-warrior** (the Birdman of Chapter 5); the **forked-eye** marking (a forked or pointed line painted or engraved running down and back from the eye, copying the dark teardrop streak below a peregrine falcon's eye, which marks a human figure as falcon-like); the **cross-in-circle**, almost certainly a sun-and-fire and four-directions symbol; the **bilobed arrow** (a stylized motif shaped like an arrow shaft flanked by two rounded lobes, a recurring elite emblem whose precise meaning is uncertain, possibly a ceremonial weapon or a hairpiece worn by the Birdman); the **chunkey player**; and raptors, serpents, and the regalia of elite ritual combat. Because it appears across unrelated language groups, the Complex is the best evidence we have that the Mississippian peoples, divided into warring chiefdoms, nonetheless shared a religious vocabulary, a pictorial scripture for a world with no writing.

One open question hangs over both systems. Mesoamerican long-distance trade, run by the Aztec *pochteca* merchant guild, is documented as reaching at least as far north as the Pueblo Southwest (Aztec Empire Ch 6; Ancestral Puebloans Ch 4). Whether Mexican goods or influence also reached the Mississippian world to the east, and whether the Mississippian exchange network ever functioned as a relay between Mesoamerica and the Southwest, is an actively argued and unsettled question rather than a demonstrated fact. Some maize-route and iconographic links are real; a regular Mexico-to-Mississippi trade artery is not established. It is flagged here as a live question, not asserted as an answer.

That world was not peaceful. The archaeological signature of **endemic warfare** is everywhere: palisaded and ditched towns, fortified centers, weapon trauma on skeletons, trophy-taking, and (read across whole regions) **depopulated buffer zones**, empty no-man's-lands of contested ground between rival chiefdoms where it was too dangerous to live. The palisade at Cahokia (Chapter 5) was not an anomaly; walls were a Mississippian norm. Chiefdoms raided each other for prestige, captives, and dominance as a structural feature of the system, not an occasional breakdown of it.

And running straight through all of this, binding rivals and uniting the social order, was a game. **Chunkey** was played on a long, carefully maintained court of packed earth. A player rolled a small, beautifully ground and polished stone disk (a **chunkey stone**, often a treasured heirloom passed down for generations) down the court, and as it rolled, players hurled long wooden poles, trying to land a pole where the stone would stop. It was watched by crowds, played for very high stakes, and the subject of *enormous gambling*. The gambling is not a guess from the archaeology; it is described in the historic-period eyewitness sources, the eighteenth- and nineteenth-century European and American observers of the Southeastern nations (and consistent with the earlier de Soto-era accounts), who recorded players betting possessions and even their own freedom and reputations, sometimes catastrophically, on a single throw. Chunkey was social and political glue: a shared institution that crossed chiefdom and language lines, a focus of communal gathering, status competition, and alliance, played from Cahokia outward across the whole Mississippian world. A small Cahokian sculpture called the **Birger figurine** (a carved red stone figure only a few inches tall, found at a Cahokia-area site, depicting a kneeling woman with serpent and gourd-vine imagery, now in the collections at Cahokia), together with other small figurines depicting chunkey players, shows how deep the game and its imagery ran into the sacred. It is one of the rare cases where we can name, specifically, a thing Mississippian people *did* for fun and for keeps.

# Chapter 7 — The Great Sun

Everything so far has been reconstructed from things: postholes, pots, beads, bones, images. This chapter is different. This chapter is the one place where Mississippian-style society was watched, up close, by literate outsiders who wrote down what they saw while it was still happening. The window is small, late, distorted, and irreplaceable, and the discipline required to use it correctly is the whole point of the chapter.

The window is the **Natchez** (pronounced "NATCH-ez"), a people of the lower Mississippi in what is now southwestern Mississippi, whom French colonists lived alongside and traded with and ultimately destroyed in the early eighteenth century. By 1700 the great Mississippian centers were five centuries past their peak and the Spanish catastrophe (Chapter 9) had already swept through, but the Natchez were still living in a way (sacred paramount chief, temple, mounds, ranked classes, sun symbolism) that scholars recognize as a late survival of the Mississippian pattern. They are, in a real sense, the only living Mississippian-style society anyone with a notebook ever saw.

The notebooks need introducing, by the rule that you should know who a source is before you trust them. The fullest is **Antoine-Simon Le Page du Pratz**, a French planter and naturalist who lived in the Natchez region from about 1718 to 1734, knew Natchez people personally, learned a good deal of the language and customs, and published a detailed *History of Louisiana* describing them in the 1750s, the single richest eyewitness ethnographic account of any Mississippian-tradition society. Alongside him are accounts by French missionaries and colonial officers, including Jesuit priests and military men who recorded Natchez religion, government, and the war that ended their independence. These men were not neutral: they were agents of a colonizing power that wanted Natchez land, and the Jesuits in particular were hostile to the religion they were describing. We use them because they are all we have for an inside view, and we read them knowing the hand that held the pen.

---

What they describe is a sacred kingship. The Natchez paramount ruler was the **Great Sun**: a hereditary monarch regarded as a living descendant and embodiment of the sun, who lived on a mound, was carried everywhere on a litter so his feet need not touch the ground, was served and approached with ritual deference, and who each morning emerged to face the rising sun and salute it, greeting his celestial relative at dawn. The closest parallel anywhere in the Americas is the Inca **Sapa Inca**, far to the south in the Andes: a hereditary monarch held to be the son of the sun god Inti, borne on a litter so his feet did not touch the ground, whose royal ancestors' mummified bodies were preserved and consulted, and at whose sacred center a perpetual relationship with the sun was maintained (Inca Empire Ch 2, 5). The match is close enough to be worth staring at, two sun-descended litter-borne sacred kings with preserved ancestors, on the same hemisphere, with no contact between them. Near the Great Sun's mound stood the **temple**, on its own mound, and inside it burned a **perpetual sacred fire** that specially charged guardians were required never to let go out, tended beside the preserved bones of past Great Suns. The dread of a sacred fire going out is itself a recurring American idea: the Aztec New Fire ceremony, in which all fires were extinguished and a single new one rekindled to guarantee the world would continue, is a more dramatized version of the same logic that the world's continuation is bound to a fire that must not die (Aztec Empire Ch 5). A sacred ruler associated with the sun, on a mound, beside a temple holding a perpetual fire and the ancestral dead: this is the Mississippian package described from life, by a man who ate with the people who lived it.

The Natchez also had a rigid class system, and it contains one of the genuine intellectual puzzles of North American anthropology. Society was divided into the **Suns** (the ruling lineage, kin of the Great Sun), the **Nobles**, the **Honoreds**, and the commoners (whom the sources, carrying the contempt of the Noble informants who described them and the French who wrote it down, call the **"Stinkards"**). Broadly this is the same shape as the Aztec split between a hereditary noble class and the commoners (the *pipiltin* and *macehualtin* of Aztec Empire Ch 6), a ranked society with a hereditary top, which makes the Natchez wrinkle stand out by contrast. The wrinkle is the marriage rule. Members of the three upper classes were *required* to marry a Stinkard, a rule of class **exogamy** (exogamy means marrying *outside* your own group; here you were forbidden to marry within your own class), and class membership descended through the *mother*, a **matrilineal** system (kinship and rank pass down the female line, not the father's). Walk one case through it. A Sun woman must marry a Stinkard man; because rank follows the mother, her children are Suns. But a Sun man also must marry a Stinkard woman, and because rank follows the mother, *his* children are not Suns at all; they drop a rank. Run that forward over generations, with every elite marrying a commoner and rank always flowing from the mother, and the arithmetic says the elite lines fed by elite men keep shedding rank while the whole population keeps mixing, so the classes ought to wash out into one undifferentiated people within a handful of generations. They demonstrably had not; the classes were intact when the French watched them. Anthropologists have argued for a century over how the system actually held together. The leading proposals are roughly these: that the French and their Noble informants simply misreported the rules and the real system had exceptions or escape valves the observers missed; that there were unrecorded mechanisms (Stinkard war honors raising individuals, a steady supply of captured outsiders being fed into the bottom of the system to replenish it); or that the system was genuinely unstable and was already visibly breaking down under colonial pressure at the very moment it was being recorded. There is no settled answer. It is offered here not as a solved feature but as a flagged genuine open problem, exactly the kind of thing the contested-claims rule exists to keep honest. The Natchez principal town, the **Grand Village** on St. Catherine Creek, has been excavated, and the dig confirmed the broad picture in the French accounts: mounds in the described arrangement, including a temple mound and the Great Sun's residence mound flanking the plaza, in the right places and of the right kind, one of the rare cases where the notebook and the dirt confirm each other directly.

The independence ended in violence. In **1729**, after years of escalating French land seizure around the Grand Village (the local French commander had finally demanded the very ground a principal Natchez town stood on), the Natchez rose and overran the French settlement at Fort Rosalie in a single coordinated attack, killing more than two hundred colonists. This is the **Natchez Revolt**. The French response, mounted with allied Native nations, especially Choctaw forces, was annihilating and deliberate. Over 1730 and 1731 the French and their allies besieged the Natchez in their fortified refuges, took them by siege and starvation, and broke them as a people: many were killed in the fighting, the survivors were hunted in scattered bands, and several hundred captives, including, the sources indicate, a Great Sun and members of the ruling lineage, were shipped to the French Caribbean sugar colony of Saint-Domingue and sold into plantation slavery, the deliberate erasure of the leadership by deportation. What had been a functioning sacred-sun chiefdom in 1728 was, by about 1731, a refugee population with no homeland and no independent existence. The deportees largely vanished into the lethal world of Caribbean slavery, leaving almost no individual record; the destruction of the Natchez polity was as total as the French could make it. But the people did not all die. Survivors who escaped the deportations scattered eastward and were taken in by the Chickasaw, the Creek, and the Cherokee, who absorbed them, and their descendants live within those nations today, a thread that ties directly into the final chapter.

---

The caveat now, heavily, because misusing this chapter is the single biggest interpretive trap in the whole subject. The Natchez were one society, observed in the 1720s, on the lower Mississippi, after five centuries of change and after the demographic catastrophe of Chapter 9 had already hit the Southeast. They are *not* a transparent template for Cahokia in 1100, or Moundville in 1300, or any other Mississippian polity in any other place or century. Reasoning "the Natchez had a Great Sun, therefore Cahokia's ruler was exactly a Great Sun" is precisely the backward-projection error the writing rules warn against, and serious scholars do not do it. What the Natchez legitimately provide is the best surviving *analogy*: a demonstration that a Mississippian-tradition society *could* be organized around sacred sun-kingship, temple fire, ranked matrilineal classes, and mound-top residence, which makes those reconstructions for earlier sites more plausible without making them proven. Use the window for light, not as a blueprint.

Set the Natchez themselves aside now, with the window closed, and step back from ethnography to structure. Two points generalize safely beyond that one society and carry forward into the rest of the book. First, **matrilineal clan organization** (descent and clan membership reckoned through the mother's line) was widespread across the historic Southeastern nations and is a reasonable inference for much of the Mississippian world, which matters for understanding how authority and land passed without writing or bureaucracy. Second, and this is the hinge into the next chapter: paramount chiefdoms were inherently unstable. The recurring pattern scholars call **chiefly cycling** runs like this. A powerful chief pulls subordinate towns into a paramount chiefdom; the structure overreaches the thin web of kinship and ceremony holding it together; a bad succession or bad harvest or military defeat cracks it; it fragments back into its component towns; and within a generation or two some other ambitious center pulls a new paramount chiefdom together from the pieces. Mississippian political history is not a line of empires. It is this churn (rise, overreach, fragment, reform) and it is the key to understanding what happened to the greatest Mississippian center of all.

# Chapter 8 — The Vacant Quarter

Cahokia came apart, and the way it came apart is one of the places where the contested-claims rule has to govern the prose, because the popular version (the great city "mysteriously vanished") is exactly the kind of dramatic certainty the evidence does not support. There is no single cause anyone can prove and no mystery in the sense the roadside signs imply. There is a set of pressures, each independently supported and each genuinely debated in degree, that converged on a structure built to be fragile.

The unraveling was gradual and ran roughly from 1200 to 1400 CE. "Gone" here does not mean the mounds were torn down or the city burned; the earthworks still stood, and stand today. It means the people left and did not come back: the dense residential neighborhoods were abandoned and not rebuilt, new mound construction stopped, the population of the urban core fell away, and by around 1350–1400 the American Bottom that had held tens of thousands was largely empty of settled population, the houses rotted, the fields gone to brush. No siege, no eruption, no single dated catastrophe. A drain, over a century and a half. The candidate pressures, each flagged as debated:

The **palisade**, rebuilt three times around the core (Chapter 5), is read as direct evidence of escalating internal conflict or external threat; a society spending enormous labor walling its own heart is a society under stress, though whether the threat was outside enemies, factional civil violence, or social control of the commoners is argued.

**Resource depletion.** Concentrating tens of thousands of people on the American Bottom for generations strained the local environment: the surrounding bottomland forest was cut for the staggering quantities of construction timber and firewood (the palisades alone consumed tens of thousands of large logs, repeatedly), and intensive farming pressed the floodplain soils. The exact severity of a Cahokian "wood crisis" is debated, but the direction is not; a very large population in one place consumes its hinterland, the same dynamic that helped end the Ancestral Puebloan great-house center at Chaco Canyon in the Southwest (Chapter 1; Ancestral Puebloans Ch 5).

**Flooding.** A city on a floodplain lives by the flood and dies by it. The evidence comes from **sediment records**: scientists core down through the layered mud of old lake and floodplain bottoms, where each major flood drops a distinct band of coarser silt, so a column of those bands reads like tree rings, a datable stack of past flood events. Such cores indicate episodes of major Mississippi flooding during Cahokia's later period, big enough to have inundated the very farmland the population depended on. The role and timing of flooding in the decline is actively researched and debated, but a floodplain city is structurally exposed to its river turning against it.

**Climate.** Cahokia's rise coincided with the **Medieval Warm Period**, a span of roughly favorable climate across the Northern Hemisphere from about 950 to 1250 CE. The name is borrowed, slightly awkwardly, from European chronology: "medieval" means the European Middle Ages, and the warm interval was first studied there, but the climate pattern itself was hemisphere-wide and had nothing to do with Europe specifically, so the label is a convenience, not a statement that anything in North America was "medieval." Cahokia's decline runs into the onset of the **Little Ice Age**, a subsequent multi-century cooling that, in mid-continental North America, is associated with drought episodes and shorter, less reliable growing seasons. The American Bottom evidence is real if coarse: tree-ring and related paleoclimate work in the mid-continent points to a turn toward more frequent and more severe drought after roughly 1200 CE, exactly the window in which a maize economy feeding tens of thousands could least afford a run of short harvests. Marginal climate against a population already at the edge of its food supply is a plausible stressor, with the magnitude and timing (as always with paleoclimate causation) debated rather than settled. And this was not a local accident. The same Little Ice Age onset that pressured the American Bottom is associated with the drought stress that helped break the Ancestral Puebloan Chaco system in the Southwest (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 5) and, somewhat earlier, with the climate-linked fragmentation of the Tiwanaku and Wari states in the Andes (Inca Empire Ch 1). Across the Atlantic the same cooling is associated with the slow strangulation of the Norse farming colony in Greenland (a small medieval Scandinavian settlement that survived on marginal pasture and died out as the climate turned against it; Viking Age Ch 7). A single climate pulse leaned on very different societies on three continents within roughly the same span; what differed was how much structure each had to absorb the shock.

And underneath all of these, the structural point from Chapter 7: a paramount chiefdom is *inherently fragile*. It has no bureaucratic state to outlive a weak ruler, a bad decade, or a lost war: no salaried administration, no institutionalized succession, no standing apparatus that persists when the personal and sacred authority at the center falters. A state can survive a bad emperor. A chiefdom built on the sacred prestige of a ruling line and the voluntary-ish flow of tribute and labor can be cracked by a single failed generation, because there is nothing institutional underneath to catch it. Cahokia was not destroyed. It was a structure that, once the favorable conditions and the central authority's legitimacy both faltered, had nothing holding it up.

---

The emptying was not confined to Cahokia. By around 1450 a large region of the central Mississippi and lower Ohio valley (the very heartland, around the confluence of the two rivers) had been substantially depopulated. Archaeologists call this striking emptiness the **"Vacant Quarter."** It is one of the genuine puzzles of late prehistory: a previously dense region going quiet, its people moving out to the edges. Whether they moved chiefly because of climate, warfare, the collapse of the centers that organized their world, or all three together is debated; that the heartland emptied is not.

Here is the reframing that this whole book has been building toward, and it is the thesis applied to the collapse. The end of Cahokia and the emptying of the Vacant Quarter were *not the end of the Mississippian world.* This is the single most important correction to the popular story. While the central valley emptied, Mississippian chiefdoms elsewhere were thriving: **Moundville** in Alabama did its greatest work *after* Cahokia's decline (Chapter 6); the late prehistoric Southeast from roughly 1400 to 1500 was full of vigorous paramount chiefdoms (Coosa, Cofitachequi, and dozens more) that the Spanish would walk straight into a few decades later (Chapter 9). Chiefdoms were *cyclical by nature* (Chapter 7): they rise, overreach, fragment, and reform somewhere else with new leadership. The closest parallel in the Americas is the Classic Maya collapse, which is the textbook case of exactly this kind of event: a monument-building, city-centered, non-imperial system undone by a tangle of drought, resource depletion, and political fragmentation, leaving a depopulated core (the southern Maya lowlands) and a popular myth of a people who "mysteriously vanished" when in fact they reorganized and their descendants are still here (Maya Ch 5). Cahokia's fall was the largest, most dramatic instance of a churn that was a permanent feature of this kind of society, not a one-time fall of a civilization, and the misreading of it as a vanishing is the same misreading later applied to the Maya. The Mississippian world did not collapse in 1400. It reorganized.

---

# Chapter 9 — The Spanish Storm

In the spring of 1539, an army landed on the Gulf coast of Florida. It was led by **Hernando de Soto**, a Spanish **conquistador** (the Spanish word for the soldier-adventurers, part military officer and part private contractor, who led the armed conquest of the Americas for crown, God, and personal fortune) who had grown rich and famous as a senior commander in the conquest of the Inca Empire in Peru (Inca Empire Ch 7) and had come to North America expecting to find another Peru, another wealthy, gold-laden empire to plunder. He brought roughly six hundred soldiers, horses, herds of pigs chained along as walking rations, and **war dogs**: large mastiff- and greyhound-type dogs trained and used as weapons, set on people to terrorize, maim, and kill, a deliberate instrument of conquest the Spanish had already used across the Americas. For four years, until de Soto's death on the Mississippi in 1542 and the survivors' escape to Mexico in 1543, this expedition (the **de Soto entrada**, Spanish for an armed expedition of invasion and exploration into the interior) marched a looping, brutal course through the interior Southeast, through the heart of the still-functioning Mississippian world. It is the only time anyone described that world while it was alive, and it described it in the act of destroying it.

A warning about this chapter's shape before the sources: the battles and chiefs about to be described are *setup*, not the point. The point arrives at the very end, silent and without a chronicle, and the dramatic expedition exists in this chapter mainly to date it, locate it, and prove the world it destroyed was alive and full when it arrived. Read the de Soto narrative as the fuse, not the explosion. Four written accounts of that expedition survive, and the introduce-your-sources rule applies with force, because their reliability varies enormously. The **Gentleman of Elvas** was an anonymous Portuguese member of the expedition whose account, published in 1557, is generally treated as a sober and relatively reliable narrative. **Rodrigo Rangel** was de Soto's personal secretary; his account survives because the official Spanish court historian **Gonzalo Fernández de Oviedo** (who compiled a massive history of the Indies in the mid-1500s and never went on the entrada himself) copied and summarized it into his chronicle, so we read Rangel through Oviedo's transcription rather than from an original. It is a near-contemporary day-by-day record and is considered one of the most reliable. **Luys Hernández de Biedma** was the royal factor (the crown's financial officer on the expedition) whose short official report to the king in 1544 is terse, early, and valuable. And **Garcilaso de la Vega**, called **"El Inca"** (the son of a Spanish conquistador and an Inca noblewoman, and one of the most celebrated authors of the Spanish Golden Age, best known for his *Royal Commentaries of the Incas*, a foundational history of Inca Peru), wrote the most vivid and most quoted account of the entrada, *La Florida del Inca*, decades later, finished around 1605, and he was never there. He compiled it from the secondhand testimony of aged survivors, and it is the most literary, the most embellished, and by scholarly consensus the *least reliable* of the four. This matters because the most dramatic stories about de Soto's march tend to trace back to Garcilaso, and the writing rules require saying so out loud rather than retelling his best scenes as fact.

---

What the more reliable chronicles describe is a landscape of working paramount chiefdoms, ruled by sacred chiefs carried on litters, centered on mound-and-plaza towns, with temples and ancestor charnel houses on the mounds, stored maize surpluses, and ranked societies: the Mississippian world of the preceding chapters, alive, recorded from the outside for the only time in history.

They met the **Lady of Cofitachequi**, the female paramount ruler of a powerful chiefdom in what is now South Carolina, who came out to meet the Spanish carried on a litter and received de Soto with diplomatic gifts including freshwater pearls (pearls grown by the Southeast's abundant river mussels, harvested in quantity and worn and exchanged as elite wealth; to the Spanish they were the one thing in the interior that looked like portable treasure, which is exactly why the gift mattered and was noticed). De Soto repaid her by taking her hostage as a guide and a guarantee of safe passage. This was not improvised cruelty but a standard, repeatable conquistador playbook: seize the supreme ruler and you control the followers, the same tactic by which a tiny Spanish force had captured the Inca emperor Atahualpa at Cajamarca a few years earlier, an operation de Soto had personally taken part in (Inca Empire Ch 8). He applied the Cajamarca method again and again here. They passed through the great chiefdom of **Coosa**, a powerful paramount polity spanning parts of present-day Georgia, Alabama, and Tennessee, described by the chroniclers as populous and well-ordered; its ruler, too, was seized and dragged along as a hostage. They encountered the paramount chief **Tascalusa** (pronounced "tas-kuh-LOO-suh"), a famously tall and imposing ruler in what is now Alabama, who outwardly submitted, was taken hostage in the usual way, and led the Spanish toward his fortified town of **Mabila**. There, in October 1540, the trap closed: warriors concealed in Mabila attacked the army inside the palisade. The **Battle of Mabila** was one of the bloodiest engagements between Europeans and Native Americans ever recorded; the chroniclers describe the town burned and a very large number of its defenders killed. This is the chapter's hinge. The Spanish nominally "won," but they came out of Mabila gutted (wounded men, dead horses, baggage and supplies burned with the town), and the entrada never recovered from that day; everything after Mabila is a damaged army limping, no longer conquering. Pushing on, the Spanish reached the rival chiefs **Casqui** and **Pacaha** in the Arkansas region, two neighboring paramount chiefs who were enemies of each other, and de Soto did what conquistadors did with local rivalries: he allied with one against the other, joining Casqui's people in a raid on Pacaha to buy guides, food, and goodwill, the divide-and-use logic of the whole conquest in miniature. With Casqui he had a large wooden Christian cross erected on a mound, a scene that was Mississippian ceremony and Catholic ritual at once, the chroniclers reporting that the Casqui asked the foreign god for rain. And on the Mississippi, as the broken expedition retreated, the paramount chief **Quigualtam** is said to have sent it a message of flat defiance, refusing to come to the Spanish and telling them to come to him instead. The most colorful, much-quoted version of this exchange comes from Garcilaso, the least reliable of the four chronicles, so the speech should be read as a likely embellishment of a real refusal rather than a verbatim transcript; that a powerful river chief declined to submit to the failing army is consistent across the sources, even if the ringing words are Garcilaso's.

---

Then comes the part with no chronicle, because no one was there to write it, and it is the most consequential event in this entire history.

De Soto's army did not just fight Mississippian people. It carried, in its own bodies and in its herd of pigs, the diseases of the Old World (smallpox above all, but also measles, influenza, and others) to populations that had never encountered them and possessed no acquired immunity. A population with no prior exposure to a given pathogen is called, in **epidemiology** (the science of how diseases spread through and affect populations), a **virgin-soil population**, and the death rate when an Old World crowd disease first sweeps such a population can be catastrophic. The reason it is so much worse than a normal epidemic is the age distribution. A disease that a society has lived with for generations mostly strikes children, because surviving adults carry immunity from a childhood infection; the farmers, leaders, parents, and warriors keep functioning, and the society absorbs the loss. A virgin-soil disease has no immune adults to spare. It strikes every age at once, so it kills the people who plant the fields, lead the chiefdom, fight the wars, and care for the sick, all in the same season, and then it returns in waves before the population can rebuild. A society can survive losing its children. It cannot easily survive losing its adults, its leadership, and its food producers simultaneously. In the roughly century and a half between de Soto's march and the next sustained European presence in the interior Southeast (French and English, late 1600s), the Mississippian world *collapsed*: the paramount chiefdoms the chronicles describe were gone, the great towns abandoned, the dense populations replaced by far smaller, reorganized societies. When Europeans returned to the interior, they found not the world de Soto saw but its wreckage, and they assumed it had always been thin and empty. It had not. The same Old World disease pool did this across the hemisphere, but the timing differed in a way worth noticing. In the Andes, smallpox ran *ahead* of the Spanish, killing the Inca emperor and throwing the empire into a succession war years before Pizarro's army physically arrived (Inca Empire Ch 7). In the Mississippian Southeast the disease traveled *with* and *behind* a marching army rather than racing in front of one. Same pathogen, same virgin-soil catastrophe, different delivery: the Inca were destabilized by an invisible vanguard, the Mississippians by the wake of an invasion.

The contested-claims rule now applies as hard as anywhere in the book. The *magnitude* of the post-contact depopulation is one of the most fiercely argued questions in American archaeology: what fraction of the Southeastern population died, how fast, and how directly de Soto's specific expedition (as opposed to later, separate disease introductions) caused it. One school argues for a rapid, massive sixteenth-century die-off on the order of most of the population, triggered early. Another argues the collapse was later, more uneven, more drawn out, and driven by a longer cascade of disease, slaving raids, and disruption rather than a single sixteenth-century pandemic. The numbers proposed differ by enormous factors and the debate is unresolved; this book will not hand you a percentage and call it fact. What is *not* in serious dispute is the qualitative outcome: the functioning paramount chiefdoms the de Soto chronicles describe did not survive into the era of sustained European settlement, and Old World epidemic disease in virgin-soil populations was the principal engine of their destruction. Scholars call the resulting devastated, depopulated, violently reorganizing region the **"shatter zone."** It was gripped not only by disease but by **slave raiding**: as English and other colonists established Atlantic and Gulf footholds in the later 1600s, they armed favored Native nations and created a market for captives, and those nations raided weaker, disease-thinned groups to sell people into colonial slavery (worked on Caribbean and southern plantations or exported), so demographic collapse, gun-fueled raiding, and forced population movement compounded one another. The Mississippian world's last act was an apocalypse that left no written record, because the literate people had left and the people it happened to did not write. There is no need to re-derive the structural logic from scratch: the Aztec narrative lays out the same recurring American pattern (Old World disease arriving with or ahead of conquest, political crisis under demographic stress, a small invading force exploiting the wreckage) and the Mississippian Southeast is a third instance of it, alongside the Aztec and Inca cases (Aztec Empire Ch 8).

# Chapter 10 — The Mound Builders' Heirs

Out of the shatter zone, the survivors built something new, and the *mechanism* by which they did it is the answer to the question the Mound Builder Myth was invented to avoid: where did the mound builders go?

They did not go anywhere. They **coalesced**. When epidemic disease, warfare, and the collapse of the paramount chiefdoms shattered the old polities, the survivors did not simply die out and they did not stay in fragments. Remnant communities (refugees, the scattered survivors of broken chiefdoms, fractions of towns that had lost their centers and their ruling lines) merged with one another, often across old language and ethnic lines, and reorganized into new, larger, deliberately multi-ethnic confederacies. This process, **coalescence**, is the bridge between the Mississippian world and the historic Native nations of the Southeast. The confederacies were not the old chiefdoms renamed; they were new political forms built by survivors out of the pieces, designed for a more dangerous world. From this process came the **Muscogee (Creek) Confederacy**, an alliance of dozens of towns of varied origin; the **Chickasaw** and the **Choctaw** of Mississippi; the **Cherokee** of the southern Appalachians; the **Caddo** confederacies of the Red River country; the **Catawba** of the Carolina **piedmont** (the rolling foothill country between the Atlantic coastal plain and the Appalachian Mountains; "piedmont" means foot-of-the-mountain); and many more. The **Natchez** survivors (Chapter 7), their nation destroyed by the French in 1731, were absorbed into the Creek and Cherokee, a thread running directly from a sacred-sun chiefdom observed in the 1720s into nations that exist today. These nations are the mound builders' heirs, not metaphorically but by direct descent through the people who lived through the catastrophe. Their organized resistance was broken militarily before removal, in Tecumseh's shattered confederacy and the Creek War's defeat at Horseshoe Bend (Early American Republic Ch 15 and Ch 16). The same pattern played out elsewhere in the Americas: after the Classic Maya collapse, Maya survivors in the Yucatán reorganized into new Postclassic political forms built by remnants of the old order across former boundaries (Maya Ch 7), the same kind of survivor-built coalescence across old lines that produced the Creek and the Choctaw. Collapse-then-coalescence, not collapse-then-vanish, is the recurring American story.

---

So return, finally, to the lie the book opened with, and finish it off. The **Mound Builder Myth** (Chapter 1), the claim that a vanished non-Indian race built the mounds, was demolished scientifically by **Cyrus Thomas** in his 1894 Bureau of Ethnology report (Chapter 1), and everything in the eight chapters between then and now is the elaboration of why he was right: the mounds are the work of the documented ancestors of documented Native nations, traceable in an unbroken material and human line from Poverty Point through Cahokia through the de Soto chronicles to the Natchez to the Creek and Cherokee and Chickasaw and Caddo who are sovereign nations in the United States today.

The sites themselves barely survived to be vindicated, and that survival belongs here, before the moral close, not after it. Most of the mounds of "Mound City" St. Louis were destroyed in the nineteenth century (Chapter 1); the Spiro Great Mortuary was looted and blasted for the relic trade in the 1930s (Chapter 6). Cahokia itself was nearly lost in the twentieth century: Monks Mound and the central precinct survived only narrowly amid postwar suburban subdivisions, a drive-in theater, and a highway cut across the site. That it survived at all is the result of a long preservation fight; **Cahokia Mounds** is now a state historic site and was named a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1982**, placed on the same global list as the Pyramids and the Parthenon. **Poverty Point**, the 3,000-year-old forager-built earthworks (Chapter 2), became a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2014. The destruction of sites like Spiro is also part of why the United States eventually passed the **Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA)** in 1990, the federal law requiring museums and federal agencies to return Native human remains and sacred objects to the descendant nations they belong to, the same law that reshaped Puebloan archaeology (Ancestral Puebloans Ch 8). In Mississippian archaeology specifically, NAGPRA has meant that skeletal collections from major sites, Cahokia and Moundville among them, have been inventoried for return and in cases reburied in consultation with affiliated nations, and that the descendant nations now have a legal standing in what happens to their ancestors and their objects. Modern Mississippian archaeology is increasingly done *with* the descendant nations rather than merely *about* their ancestors, and new tools keep rewriting the story. Airborne **LIDAR** works by sweeping a laser down from an aircraft and timing millions of returning pulses to build a precise three-dimensional map of the ground surface; software can then mathematically remove the points that hit the forest canopy and keep only the ones that reached the actual earth, revealing the shape of the land under the trees. It does not find the mounds the bulldozer destroyed; it finds ones that were never destroyed, only hidden, intact mounds and whole site layouts standing under forest where no one had looked, plus the faint plowed-down ghost outlines of others. Re-analysis of old finds, as at Mound 72 (Chapter 5), keeps overturning the confident older readings, which is exactly how the discipline is supposed to work.

Now the moral close, uninterrupted. It is worth saying once, plainly, *why the myth mattered*, because the harm was not academic. The myth did ideological work. By inserting a fictitious vanished civilized race and casting Native Americans as its savage destroyers, the myth converted dispossession into restitution: removing Indians from the eastern woodlands could be told as a story not of theft but of a civilized nation reclaiming a land from people who had themselves stolen it from the noble Mound Builders. The myth was a moral laundering operation for Indian Removal, running in the same decades, in the same country, as the Trail of Tears (Antebellum America Ch 4, which carries the Southeastern nations' story forward into dispossession, the Indian Removal Act, and the Trail itself; it is the southeastern counterpart to where the Puebloan peoples went after the Four Corners emptied, Ancestral Puebloans Ch 8). To finally credit the mounds to Native people was not merely to correct an archaeological error. It was to withdraw the alibi.

The thesis, restated, to close. In the river valleys of eastern North America, Native peoples built a civilization of cities, monumental earthen pyramids, sacred kingship, continent-spanning sacred trade, and a shared religious art, without metal, draft animals, the wheel, or writing. It was devastated by an invisible epidemic apocalypse in the century after a Spanish army marched through it. It did not vanish. Its survivors coalesced into the nations the United States then tried, and failed, to erase a second time, first by removal and then by a myth that wrote them out of their own ancestors' achievement. The descendants are citizens of **sovereign Native nations** now (a sovereign Native nation is a federally recognized tribal nation with its own government, its own laws and courts, and a government-to-government relationship with the United States, not a club or an ethnic label but a self-governing political body): the Muscogee (Creek) Nation, the Cherokee Nation, the Chickasaw Nation, the Choctaw Nation, the Caddo Nation, and others. They did not lose the thread of who built the mounds. The mounds still stand. Two corners of the same continent, the same shape of story: the monuments were abandoned; the people were not lost; they are still here, and they will tell you, correctly, that the hills you are standing on were built by their ancestors' hands.

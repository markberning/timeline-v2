# Chapter 1 — The New City

Around 814 BCE, according to a tradition that ancient writers considered reliable and modern archaeologists find roughly plausible, a Tyrian princess named **Elissa** (eh-LISS-ah), later called **Dido** (DY-doh) by Latin writers, sailed west from the great Phoenician port city of **Tyre** (rhymes with "fire"; on the coast of modern Lebanon) and founded a new city on the coast of North Africa. She called it **Qart Hadasht** (kart hah-DASHT), which means, straightforwardly enough, "New City." The Greeks rendered this as *Karchedon*. The Romans rendered it as *Carthago*. We call it **Carthage**.

The founding story, as told by Greek and Roman writers (most elaborately by the Roman poet **Virgil** in his *Aeneid*, though Virgil was writing literature, not history, some seven centuries after the fact), goes like this. Elissa was the daughter of the king of Tyre and the wife of **Acerbas** (ah-SER-bass; also called Sychaeus in Latin sources), who was the high priest of **Melqart** (MEL-kart, the patron god of Tyre) and reputedly enormously wealthy. When Elissa's brother **Pygmalion** (pig-MAY-lee-on) inherited the throne, he murdered Acerbas to seize his fortune. Elissa, warned in a dream, secretly loaded the treasure onto ships, gathered a group of loyal followers and Tyrian nobles, and fled westward across the Mediterranean.

The refugees landed on the coast of what is now **Tunisia** (too-NEE-zhah), in North Africa, on a triangular peninsula jutting into the **Gulf of Tunis**. Local Berber inhabitants were already there. The famous founding legend says that the local king, **Iarbas** (ee-AR-bass), agreed to sell Elissa as much land as could be enclosed by a single oxhide. Elissa cut the hide into the thinnest possible strips, laid them end to end, and enclosed enough ground to build a citadel on the hill called **Byrsa** (BEER-sah). The story is almost certainly legendary (the oxhide trick appears in foundation myths from several Mediterranean cultures, and the word *byrsa* is conveniently similar to the Greek word for "hide," which may have generated the whole tale). But the broad outline, a Tyrian princess founding a colony on the Tunisian coast in the late ninth century BCE, fits what we know about Phoenician colonization in this period (Phoenicia Ch 5).

---

A word about sources, because it shapes everything in this book. Almost nothing you will read about Carthage comes from the Carthaginians themselves. The Carthaginians wrote extensively. They had a literature, a historical tradition, a body of scientific and agricultural writing, and religious texts. They wrote on papyrus and parchment, materials that rot in Mediterranean climates. When Rome destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE (Chapter 8), it destroyed or dispersed virtually the entire written record. The Roman Senate distributed Carthage's libraries to Numidian princes, allies who had helped Rome win the final war. Those libraries vanished. One single Carthaginian book was deliberately preserved: an agricultural treatise by a writer named Mago (Chapter 3). Everything else is gone.

What we have instead are Greek and Roman sources. **Polybius** (po-LIB-ee-us; a Greek historian who lived roughly 200-118 BCE and personally witnessed the destruction of Carthage) is the most reliable. He was methodical, skeptical, and had access to Carthaginian informants and documents. **Diodorus Siculus** (die-oh-DOR-us SIK-yoo-lus; a Greek historian writing in the first century BCE) drew on earlier Greek sources, including writers who lived during the Sicilian wars. **Appian** (APP-ee-an; a Roman historian of the second century CE) wrote a history of the Punic Wars that preserves details found nowhere else. **Livy** (LIV-ee; a Roman historian of the first century BCE) wrote the most dramatic accounts of Hannibal's campaigns but was openly patriotic and pro-Roman. And Virgil's *Aeneid* (written around 20 BCE) turned Dido into one of the great tragic heroines of Western literature, abandoned by the Trojan hero Aeneas and killing herself on a pyre, but the poem tells us more about Roman literary sensibilities than about Carthaginian history.

The problem with all these sources is obvious. They were written by people who either fought Carthage or inherited their information from people who did. The Greeks fought Carthage in Sicily for centuries. The Romans fought three existential wars against Carthage and ended by erasing the city from the earth. Reading Carthaginian history through Greek and Roman sources is like reading the history of a defeated nation through the archives of its conquerors. The conquerors are not lying, exactly, but they are choosing what to record, how to frame it, and what to emphasize. When Roman sources describe Carthaginian cruelty, treachery, or religious barbarity, the reader should ask: would the Carthaginians have described themselves this way? We will never know, because the Carthaginians' own account has been silenced.

Keep this in mind throughout this narrative. When the evidence is strong, I will say so. When we are relying on hostile witnesses, I will say that too.

---

The location Elissa chose was superb. The peninsula of Carthage commanded the **Gulf of Tunis** on the North African coast, roughly 10 miles (16 km) east of modern Tunis. More critically, Carthage sat near the narrowest point of the central Mediterranean, where the Tunisian coast and the island of **Sicily** (roughly 100 miles or 160 km to the northeast) almost pinch the sea shut. Any ship traveling between the eastern and western Mediterranean had to pass through this bottleneck. Controlling the bottleneck meant controlling the flow of trade. It was the ancient equivalent of sitting astride a highway toll booth, except the highway was the entire western Mediterranean.

Byrsa Hill, the citadel where the founding legend places Elissa's original settlement, rose about 200 feet (60 meters) above the coast, offering commanding views of the harbor approaches. The hill would eventually be crowned by the great **Temple of Eshmun** (esh-MOON; the Phoenician god of healing, equivalent to the Greek Asclepius), which became the most sacred building in Carthage. But in the early decades, Byrsa was simply a defensible hill with a small colony clinging to it, dependent on its mother city Tyre for trade goods, cultural identity, and religious legitimacy.

Carthage was not the only Phoenician colony in the western Mediterranean. **Utica** (YOU-tih-kah), about 20 miles (32 km) to the northwest on the Tunisian coast, may have been founded even earlier (Phoenicia Ch 5). **Gadir** (GAH-deer; modern Cádiz, on the Atlantic coast of Spain) was older still by some accounts. Phoenician trading posts dotted the coasts of North Africa, Sardinia, and Sicily. But Carthage had the best location of any of them: the central Mediterranean chokepoint, good harbors, a defensible peninsula, and a fertile agricultural hinterland (the **Cap Bon** peninsula and the **Medjerda River** valley) that could feed a growing city.

---

The young colony grew quickly, and by the eighth century BCE it had begun developing trade networks independent of Tyre. The mother city was increasingly distracted. Tyre was under mounting pressure from the **Neo-Assyrian Empire** (Assyrian Empire Ch 4-6), paying heavy tribute and losing its freedom of action. As Tyre's independence contracted, Carthage's expanded. Carthaginian merchants developed their own commercial routes, exchanging manufactured goods (pottery, metalwork, dyed textiles) for raw materials from the peoples of the North African interior and from Phoenician colonies across the western Mediterranean. The transition from dependent colony to independent power was gradual, probably spanning a century or more, but by roughly 700 BCE, Carthage was the dominant Phoenician city in the west.

The colony did not forget its roots entirely. Carthage maintained the cult of **Melqart**, Tyre's patron god, and according to multiple ancient sources, sent annual tribute to the Temple of Melqart at Tyre for centuries after achieving political independence. This was a religious obligation, not a political one. The bond was sacred, maintained through priestly delegations who sailed east with offerings even as the colony outstripped its mother city in wealth and power. It was, in a way, the Phoenician equivalent of a grown child still visiting a parent's church.

But Carthage was developing its own religious identity. The chief deities of Carthage became **Baal Hammon** (BAH-ahl HAM-on; depicted as a bearded elder with curling ram's horns, a weather and fertility god whose name may mean "lord of the incense altar" or "lord of Mount Amanus") and **Tanit** (TAH-nit; a lunar and fertility goddess whose distinctive symbol, a triangle topped by a circle with a horizontal bar between them, is found on thousands of Carthaginian artifacts). These gods had Phoenician roots but evolved into something distinctly Carthaginian. Baal Hammon gradually eclipsed Melqart as the supreme god of the city, marking Carthage's theological independence from Tyre just as its political independence solidified. Tanit would eventually eclipse Baal Hammon himself, her name appearing first on inscriptions from the fifth century BCE onward, becoming the most ubiquitous divine presence in the Punic world (Chapter 7).

---

The most controversial site in early Carthage, and in all of Punic archaeology, is the **Tophet** (TOH-fet). Established in the city's oldest quarter, probably around 750 BCE, the Tophet was a sacred precinct dedicated to Tanit and Baal Hammon. Over the next six centuries, thousands of urns containing cremated remains were deposited there, accumulating layer upon layer in a site that eventually covered several acres. The remains in the urns are those of young children and, in some urns, lambs or other animals.

What the Tophet represents is fiercely debated, and we will return to the question in detail in Chapter 7. For now, note two things. First, Greek and Roman authors unanimously described the Carthaginians as practicing **child sacrifice** (the ritual killing of children as an offering to the gods, called the *molk* offering in Punic inscriptions). These are hostile sources with obvious reasons to demonize Carthage. Second, the archaeological evidence is real: there are thousands of urns with infant remains, and the site was clearly a place of significant ritual activity over many centuries. Modern scholars are genuinely divided. Some believe the ancient literary testimony and the archaeological evidence together point to actual sacrifice. Others argue that the Tophet was a sacred burial ground for children who died of natural causes (infant mortality in the ancient world ran between 30 and 50 percent) and were given a special ritual cremation. The honest answer is that we do not know for certain, and anyone who tells you the debate is settled is overstating their case.

Carthaginian scribes developed their own version of the Phoenician alphabet into what linguists call the **Punic script**, a cursive variant that was written more quickly and fluidly than the angular Phoenician original. The Phoenician language and its Punic dialect are closely related (think the difference between British and American English, not the difference between English and French). Punic inscriptions, mostly votive dedications and funerary texts, are found across the western Mediterranean from Sardinia to Morocco. The script would survive the destruction of Carthage itself: **Neo-Punic** inscriptions (the latest stage of the language) appear in North Africa as late as the third or fourth century CE, over three hundred years after Rome burned the city.

Carthage eventually ringed its peninsula with **triple fortification walls** stretching over 20 miles (32 km). The Roman historian Appian, describing the walls at the time of the final siege in 146 BCE, reported the landward wall as 45 feet (14 m) high and 30 feet (9 m) thick, incorporating stables for 300 war elephants and 4,000 horses within the wall structure itself. Whether these dimensions describe the original sixth-century BCE fortifications or later expansions is unclear (Appian's numbers almost certainly reflect the walls at their final, most developed stage), but by 600 BCE Carthage was already one of the most heavily fortified cities in the ancient Mediterranean. Only Tyre's island fortress was comparably defensible, and Tyre achieved its defense through geography rather than engineering.

The New City had outgrown its name. By the late seventh century BCE, Carthage was the largest and wealthiest Phoenician city in the world, surpassing the homeland cities that had given it birth. The colony had become the capital. What it needed now was a military to match.

---

# Chapter 2 — Masters of the Western Sea

Around 580 BCE, a general named **Mago I** (MAH-goh) seized power in Carthage and established what historians call the **Magonid dynasty**, the first ruling family to dominate Carthaginian politics for multiple generations. The details of Mago's rise are murky (our sources are late and contradictory), but the effect was clear: Carthage transformed from a wealthy trading city into an aggressive military power that would contest Greek dominance across the central and western Mediterranean.

The transformation was driven by competition. Greek colonists had been pouring into the western Mediterranean since the eighth century BCE, founding powerful city-states in Sicily (especially **Syracuse**, SIR-ah-kyooz, on the island's eastern coast, which would become the largest Greek city in the west) and southern Italy. The Phoenicians and Greeks had coexisted for centuries, with the Phoenicians generally occupying the western end of islands and the African coast while the Greeks took the eastern end. But by the sixth century, the coexistence was breaking down. Both sides wanted the same harbors, the same trade routes, the same agricultural hinterlands. Commercial competition was becoming military confrontation.

Before Mago, Carthage had relied on a citizen militia for defense, supplemented by mercenaries when needed. Mago professionalized the army, built up the navy, and forged a critical alliance with the **Etruscans** (eh-TRUSS-kans; a powerful non-Greek civilization in central Italy, roughly modern Tuscany, which was itself being squeezed by Greek expansion in southern Italy). The Carthaginian-Etruscan alliance reflected a shared interest: both powers wanted to stop the Greeks from dominating the western Mediterranean.

The alliance was tested at the **Battle of Alalia** (ah-LAH-lee-ah) around 535 BCE, fought off the coast of **Corsica** (the island northwest of Sardinia). Greek colonists from **Phocaea** (foh-SEE-ah; an Ionian Greek city in Asia Minor whose people had been displaced by the Persian conquest) had established a settlement on Corsica and were raiding Carthaginian and Etruscan shipping. A combined Carthaginian-Etruscan fleet, reportedly 120 ships (a conventional round figure from Herodotus), clashed with the Phocaean Greeks. The Greeks claimed a tactical victory (they held the water after the battle), but their losses were so severe, roughly two-thirds of their ships, that they abandoned Corsica entirely. The aftermath divided the western Mediterranean into spheres of influence: Carthage took Sardinia, the Etruscans took Corsica, and Greek colonization west of Sicily was effectively blocked. Alalia established the geopolitical framework that would hold for nearly three centuries.

---

An earlier military figure named **Malchus** (MAL-kuss; the name may actually be a title meaning "king," transliterated from Punic sources) appears in the fragmentary record before Mago, leading campaigns to secure Carthaginian positions in western Sicily and Sardinia. The details are garbled. What survives suggests a pattern familiar from Phoenician homeland politics (Phoenicia Ch 1-4): a general who was successful abroad, was exiled after a failure, and then marched on Carthage to seize power by force before being executed. Whether Malchus was a real individual, a composite, or a title applied to multiple leaders is debated. The episode, if it happened, demonstrates that Carthage's political system was already struggling with the tension between military commanders and civilian authority, a tension that would define Carthaginian politics for the rest of its history.

Mago's real legacy was strategic vision. Under the Magonids, Carthage stopped thinking of itself as a trading city that occasionally had to fight and started thinking of itself as a military power that traded. The navy was expanded and upgraded with triremes and pentaconters, the standard warships of the sixth century. (The **quinquereme**, KWIN-kwee-reem, the massive five-banked warship that would become Carthage's signature vessel, was a later development, probably adopted in the early fourth century BCE.) Punic naval construction was remarkably advanced: ships were built with prefabricated, numbered components that could be assembled rapidly in dockyards, a technique so efficient that later Roman sources claimed the Romans reverse-engineered a captured Carthaginian ship to build their own fleet (Chapter 4). Carthaginian shipbuilding was, in effect, modular manufacturing applied to warships, over two thousand years before Henry Ford applied the principle to automobiles.

The Magonids also expanded Carthaginian territory beyond the sea. Carthage took control of existing Phoenician colonies in **Sardinia**, exploiting the island's metal ores (copper, lead, silver) and strategic central Mediterranean position. Sardinia would remain one of Carthage's most important overseas possessions for three centuries, held until Rome seized it after the First Punic War (Chapter 4). In **Iberia** (the ancient name for the peninsula containing modern Spain and Portugal), Carthage gradually absorbed older Phoenician coastal settlements and pushed into the interior, drawn by the peninsula's vast silver, copper, and tin deposits. The Iberian silver trade would eventually become the economic engine that powered Carthage's military machine in its final wars against Rome (Chapter 5).

---

The most remarkable Carthaginian achievements of the Magonid era were not military but exploratory. Around 500 BCE (the exact date is uncertain), two extraordinary voyages departed from Carthage in opposite directions.

**Hanno** (HAH-noh), later called "the Navigator," led a fleet of sixty ships carrying, according to his own account, 30,000 men and women (a number almost certainly exaggerated; many modern scholars think the original figure was closer to 3,000 or 5,000, and the larger number may be a scribal corruption in the Greek text) down the West African coast. His mission was to found colonies along the Atlantic coast of Africa and to explore as far south as possible. Hanno's account, known as the **Periplus of Hanno** (a *periplus* is a Greek word for a written sailing guide), survives in a Greek translation inscribed at the Temple of Baal Hammon in Carthage. It describes a voyage that may have reached as far south as modern Cameroon or Gabon, roughly 3,000 miles (4,800 km) from Carthage. Along the way, Hanno describes founding colonies along the Moroccan coast, encountering a volcanic eruption that lit up the night sky (possibly **Mount Cameroon**, one of the few active volcanoes in West Africa), and observing a group of hairy, aggressive creatures that his local interpreters called **"Gorillae."** These were probably chimpanzees or gorillas. When Western scientists formally described the gorilla in 1847, they named it after Hanno's creatures, making the Carthaginian explorer the unwitting godfather of a zoological classification twenty-three centuries later.

Simultaneously, or perhaps slightly before, another Carthaginian navigator named **Himilco** (hih-MIL-koh) sailed north from Carthage, through the Strait of Gibraltar, and up the Atlantic coast of Europe. His voyage reached the tin-producing regions of **Brittany** or possibly **Britain**, establishing the northern leg of Carthage's Atlantic trade network. Tin was essential for making bronze (an alloy of copper and tin), and the Atlantic tin routes were so commercially valuable that Carthage guarded them as state secrets. One ancient source claims a Carthaginian captain deliberately ran his ship aground rather than let a trailing Roman vessel discover the route to the tin sources. If true, the captain was reimbursed by the Carthaginian state for his sacrifice. Trade secrets were literally worth dying for.

These two voyages, Hanno's south into tropical Africa and Himilco's north into the cold Atlantic, represent the most ambitious exploration program of the ancient world. No other Mediterranean civilization was sending ships into the open Atlantic at this date. The Greeks, for all their intellectual curiosity, were coastal sailors who rarely ventured beyond the Mediterranean basin. The Egyptians had retreated from maritime activity centuries earlier. The Persians were a land empire. In 500 BCE, the Carthaginians were the only people in the Western world exploring waters that no one else would revisit for a thousand years.

---

Back in the Mediterranean, Carthage was building the diplomatic infrastructure of a regional power. In 509 BCE, Carthage and the young **Roman Republic** (which had just overthrown its last king and established republican government) signed their **first commercial treaty**. The text, preserved by Polybius, is revealing. It defines spheres of influence: Rome acknowledged Carthaginian dominance over the western Mediterranean sea lanes and much of North Africa, while Carthage recognized Roman authority in Latium (the small region around Rome in central Italy). The treaty reveals how early and how clearly Carthage saw Rome: as a minor regional power, worth dealing with but not worth worrying about. Rome, in 509 BCE, controlled roughly 300 square miles (780 sq km) of central Italian farmland. Carthage controlled the western Mediterranean. The power imbalance was so extreme that the treaty was less an agreement between equals and more an acknowledgment that Rome existed. It would take 250 years for the balance to shift, and when it did, the result would be catastrophic.

The last great Magonid ruler, **Hamilcar I** (HAM-il-kar; not the later Hamilcar Barca of the Second Punic War, an important distinction since the name recycled through Carthaginian aristocratic families like a family heirloom), spent his reign expanding Carthaginian territory in Sicily and Sardinia, building the military machine that his dynasty had begun to assemble. Hamilcar was preparing for a confrontation with the Greeks of eastern Sicily, a confrontation that both sides knew was coming. When it came, in 480 BCE, it would end in disaster for Carthage. But the catastrophe would also force Carthage to reinvent itself, and the civilization that emerged from the wreckage would be more powerful, more sophisticated, and more dangerous than the one that went in.

---

# Chapter 3 — The African Empire

In 480 BCE, the same year that the combined Greek fleet destroyed the Persian navy at **Salamis** (SAL-ah-miss) and saved Greece from Persian conquest (Persian Empire Ch 4), the Carthaginians suffered their own catastrophe on the other end of the Mediterranean.

Hamilcar I led a massive invasion force across the sea to **Himera** (hih-MAIR-ah), a Greek city on the northern coast of Sicily. The exact size of his army is debated (ancient sources give absurd numbers like 300,000, which is logistically impossible for a premodern overseas expedition), but it was clearly the largest force Carthage had ever assembled. The Greek tyrant **Gelon of Syracuse** (JEL-on), who had unified much of eastern Sicily under his rule, met the Carthaginians with a combined Sicilian Greek army.

The **Battle of Himera** was a catastrophe for Carthage. Hamilcar's army was routed. Hamilcar himself was killed. Greek sources disagree on how: some say he fell in the fighting, others that he threw himself into the sacrificial fire he had been tending throughout the battle when he saw the day was lost. The detail about the fire is colorful enough to be suspicious (it conveniently matches Greek stereotypes about Carthaginian religious fanaticism), but the outcome is not disputed. Carthage lost its army, its king, and its ambitions in eastern Sicily in a single afternoon.

Ancient Greek writers drew an explicit parallel between Himera and Salamis, both fought in 480 BCE. Some even placed both battles on the same day, though this is almost certainly a literary device rather than historical fact. The parallel served Greek propaganda: the message was that "civilization" (Greek, naturally) had defeated "barbarism" (Persian in the east, Carthaginian in the west) simultaneously, as if by divine coordination. The Carthaginians, who would have told the story rather differently, left no surviving version.

The defeat at Himera knocked Carthage out of Sicilian military politics for seventy years. But unlike most ancient powers that suffered catastrophic military defeats, Carthage did not collapse. It pivoted. And the pivot produced the most remarkable century in Carthaginian history.

---

After Himera, Carthage reformed its government. The Magonid dynasty did not survive the disaster. In its place, the Carthaginians developed a mixed constitutional system centered on two annually elected chief magistrates called **suffetes** (soo-FEET-eez; from the Punic word *shofet*, meaning "judge," the same word used for the biblical Judges of Israel). The suffetes served as the senior civil officials, roughly equivalent to the two consuls of the Roman Republic. Below them sat a **Council of Elders** (probably called the *adirim*, similar to a senate), and below that, a **popular assembly** of citizens that had the power to ratify decisions and, in some circumstances, override the council.

This system impressed the Greek philosopher **Aristotle** (AIR-ih-STAH-tul; 384-322 BCE, the Athenian philosopher whose *Politics* is the most systematic analysis of government in ancient literature). In his *Politics*, Aristotle ranked Carthage's constitution alongside Sparta and Crete as one of the best-governed states in the Mediterranean world. He noted that Carthage had avoided both tyranny (one-man rule) and popular revolution despite its enormous wealth, a remarkable achievement for any ancient city. The compliment is doubly striking because Aristotle was Greek, and Greeks did not normally praise "barbarian" (non-Greek) governments. For Aristotle to rank Carthage alongside Sparta was the equivalent of a Cold War American professor praising the Soviet economic system: a grudging acknowledgment of competence across an ideological divide.

The constitutional system was not democratic in the modern sense. Real power rested with wealthy aristocratic families who controlled the Council of Elders and used their wealth to influence the popular assembly. Suffetes were drawn from the same elite families. Military commands went to aristocrats. But the system did include genuine checks on power. When a general named **Bomilcar** (BOM-il-kar) attempted to seize power as tyrant during a military crisis in 308 BCE, marching troops through the streets of Carthage, the coup failed and Bomilcar was crucified in the public marketplace. Carthage's oligarchic republic violently resisted one-man rule. The generals served the state; the state did not serve the generals. This principle held for centuries, right up until the Barcid family (Chapter 5) began to strain it.

---

Blocked from eastward expansion in Sicily, post-Himera Carthage turned its energy inward and southward, building what historians call the **Carthaginian African empire**. Over the course of the fifth and fourth centuries BCE, Carthage conquered vast stretches of the **Libyan** interior (in ancient usage, "Libya" meant most of North Africa west of Egypt, not just the modern country). Carthaginian agricultural estates spread across the fertile **Cap Bon** peninsula and the **Bagradas Valley** (bah-GRAH-dahs; modern Medjerda River valley), which became the breadbasket of the Punic world. These estates, worked by Libyan tenant farmers and, increasingly, by slave labor, produced enormous grain surpluses that made Carthage one of the ancient world's great agricultural exporters alongside Egypt and Sicily.

A Carthaginian writer named **Mago** (MAH-goh; not the dynasty founder from Chapter 2, a different Mago entirely since the name was as common in Carthage as "John" is in English) produced a monumental **28-volume treatise on agriculture** around 450 BCE. Mago's work covered viticulture (grape growing), olive cultivation, livestock management, estate organization, and what we would now call agricultural science. The treatise was so respected across the Mediterranean that when Rome finally destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE (Chapter 8), the Roman Senate ordered Mago's work translated into Latin. It was the only Carthaginian book Rome deliberately saved. Think about that: an entire civilization's written heritage was destroyed or dispersed, and the one thing Rome preserved was a farming manual. Mago's treatise influenced Roman agricultural writers for centuries. Fragments survive in quotations by later Latin authors, and they reveal a level of agricultural sophistication (soil analysis, grafting techniques, irrigation management, livestock breeding) that was far more systematic than anything in the contemporary Greek literature. The Carthaginians were better farmers than the Greeks, and Rome knew it.

The African empire also reached southward, into the Saharan trade routes. Carthaginian merchants conducted what Greek writers called **"silent trade"** with peoples beyond the Sahara: goods were laid out on a beach or riverside, one side withdrew, the other approached and laid out their counter-offering, and the process repeated until both sides were satisfied. Neither party spoke the other's language. Neither party trusted the other enough to meet face-to-face. But the trade worked, because both sides wanted what the other had. The Carthaginians offered Mediterranean manufactured goods (metalwork, glass, textiles, salt). In return, they received **gold dust** from the auriferous regions of West Africa. This trans-Saharan gold trade predated the famous medieval trans-Saharan caravan routes by nearly a millennium and made Carthage one of the ancient Mediterranean's primary gold markets.

---

The Sicilian wars did not end permanently after Himera. They simmered. In 409 BCE, Hamilcar I's grandson **Hannibal Mago** (not the famous Hannibal of the Alps, but an earlier bearer of the name) launched a revenge expedition that sacked and razed the city of Himera itself. It was a vendetta seventy years in the making. Ancient sources report that 3,000 Greek prisoners were dragged to the spot where Hamilcar had died and executed as a sacrificial offering. Whether this actually happened or whether the Greek sources are projecting their own assumptions about Carthaginian religious brutality onto a military atrocity is impossible to say. Either way, Himera was destroyed, and Carthage was back in Sicily.

The next century saw intermittent warfare between Carthage and the Greek city-states of Sicily, a grinding cycle of invasion, siege, plague, retreat, and uneasy truce. In 397 BCE, a Carthaginian general named **Himilco** (hih-MIL-koh; not the Atlantic explorer from Chapter 2) besieged Syracuse itself, the greatest Greek city in the western Mediterranean, but plague devastated his army and he retreated in disgrace and committed suicide. Meanwhile, the general **Hanno the Great** (a different Hanno from the Atlantic explorer) expanded Carthaginian control deep into the Libyan interior, consolidating the African territorial empire that would sustain Carthage's wealth and manpower for the next century and a half.

In 348 BCE, Carthage and Rome renewed their commercial treaty, tightening Carthaginian trade monopolies in the western Mediterranean. The **second treaty** banned Roman merchants from Africa and Sardinia entirely, while permitting limited trade in Sicily. Polybius preserved the text, noting that even Roman scholars of his own day could barely read the archaic Latin of the original inscription. The treaty's increasingly restrictive terms reveal Carthage's growing confidence: the commercial republic was drawing its sphere of influence tighter.

In 341 BCE, the Corinthian general **Timoleon** (tih-MOH-lee-on) crushed a Carthaginian army at the **Battle of the Crimissus** (krih-MISS-us) River, killing an estimated 10,000 members of Carthage's elite citizen infantry, the **Sacred Band** (the only military unit composed entirely of Carthaginian citizens from aristocratic families, roughly 2,500-3,000 strong; the rest of the army was mercenaries and allied contingents). It was the worst single-day loss of citizen soldiers in Carthaginian history.

In 310 BCE, the Syracusan tyrant **Agathocles** (ah-GATH-oh-kleez) did something that nobody had ever done before: he invaded Africa. Blockaded in Syracuse by a Carthaginian fleet, Agathocles loaded his army onto ships, slipped past the blockade, sailed to North Africa, and ravaged Carthaginian territory for three years. He ultimately failed to take Carthage itself and had to withdraw, but he proved a crucial point: Carthage's African heartland was not invulnerable. An enemy who was bold enough to take the war to Africa could threaten the city directly. It was a lesson that a Roman general named Scipio would remember a century later (Chapter 6).

---

Through all this fighting, Carthage was building the urban infrastructure that would make it one of the greatest cities in the ancient world. The crown jewel was the **Cothon** (KOH-thon), the great double harbor, probably completed in its final form around 400 BCE. The harbor complex was unlike anything else in the ancient Mediterranean. The outer harbor was rectangular, open to the sea, and large enough to accommodate hundreds of merchant ships loading and unloading cargo. Behind it, connected by a narrow defensible channel, was the circular **military harbor**, an engineering marvel. The military cothon was a perfect circle of water surrounded by a ring of individual covered ship sheds, each one sized to house a single warship, arranged around a central island from which the admiralty could command operations. The capacity was staggering: 220 warships could be sheltered simultaneously, each in its own berth, protected from the weather and hidden from the eyes of approaching ships. No ancient city had anything comparable. The closest parallel might be the arsenals of Venice two thousand years later.

**Kerkouane** (ker-koo-AHN; on the Cap Bon peninsula, about 40 miles or 65 km east of Carthage) is the only intact Punic city ever found. Every other Carthaginian urban site was either destroyed by Rome and rebuilt or has been continuously inhabited for two millennia, burying the Punic layers under centuries of later construction. Kerkouane was abandoned in the mid-third century BCE, probably during the First Punic War (Chapter 4), and was never reoccupied. When archaeologists excavated it in the 1950s, they found a remarkably complete snapshot of everyday Punic urban life: gridded streets, standardized house lots with interior courtyards, indoor bathing rooms with terracotta tubs, mosaic floors with the distinctive Tanit symbol, a dedicated industrial quarter for purple dye production (complete with heaps of crushed murex shells), and a sophisticated drainage system. The level of domestic comfort surprised archaeologists who had expected something cruder. Kerkouane is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and it remains our best window into how ordinary Carthaginians actually lived, as opposed to how Greek and Roman writers described them.

By the mid-third century BCE, Carthage's population may have reached 300,000 to 400,000 at its peak (estimates vary widely, but even conservative figures place it among the largest cities in the ancient world, rivaling Alexandria and approaching Rome). Byrsa Hill was packed with residential quarters where buildings rose six stories high, crammed together in a density that ancient visitors found astonishing. The city had water infrastructure (cisterns and channeled springs), massive granaries, the double harbor, the Tophet sanctuary, temples to Tanit and Baal Hammon and Eshmun, workshops producing metalwork, glass, textiles, and pottery, and the enormous triple walls that would, by the time of the final siege, incorporate stables for war elephants and horses.

It was, by any measure, a great city. And it was about to collide with the only power in the Mediterranean that could destroy it.

---

# Chapter 4 — Rome

In 264 BCE, a minor dispute over the Sicilian city of **Messana** (meh-SAH-nah; modern Messina, at the northeastern tip of Sicily, controlling the narrow strait between Sicily and the Italian mainland) escalated into a war that neither Carthage nor Rome had planned and that neither could have imagined would last 23 years, destroy hundreds of ships, kill hundreds of thousands of men, and permanently alter the balance of power in the Mediterranean world.

The **First Punic War** (264-241 BCE) was the longest continuous military conflict in ancient history to that point. The Romans called the Carthaginians *Poeni* (from the Greek *Phoinikes*, "Phoenicians"), which is why we call these conflicts the "Punic" Wars. The war began because a group of Italian mercenaries called the **Mamertines** (MAM-er-teenz), who had seized Messana and were being squeezed by Syracuse, appealed to both Carthage and Rome for help. Both intervened. Their forces collided. And a local Sicilian squabble became a Mediterranean war.

The fundamental problem for both sides was geographic. Sicily is an island. Carthage controlled the western half and its surrounding waters. Rome controlled most of the Italian peninsula. To fight in Sicily, Rome needed to cross the Strait of Messina and supply its armies by sea. To defend Sicily, Carthage needed to maintain naval superiority. The war was therefore, inevitably, a naval war. And in 264 BCE, Rome had no navy.

Carthage had the best navy in the western Mediterranean. Its quinqueremes (five-banked warships crewed by experienced Punic sailors) had dominated western Mediterranean waters for generations. By any rational calculation, a naval war between a landlocked Italian city-state and the greatest maritime power in the ancient world should have been over quickly. But the Romans were, as they would demonstrate repeatedly over the next century, constitutionally incapable of accepting defeat.

The early fighting was on land, in Sicily. In 262 BCE, Rome besieged Carthaginian-held **Agrigentum** (ah-GRIH-jen-tum; one of the wealthiest Greek cities in Sicily, on the island's southern coast) for seven months, finally storming the city and selling 25,000 of its inhabitants into slavery. The siege was brutal and expensive, but it taught Rome a critical lesson: controlling Sicilian cities was pointless as long as Carthage could resupply and reinforce them by sea. Rome needed a navy.

---

According to Polybius, the Romans captured a beached Carthaginian quinquereme, reverse-engineered its design, and built a fleet of over 100 warships in sixty days. The story may be embellished (Rome probably had some naval experience through its Greek-allied cities in southern Italy), but the core claim is plausible: Rome built a fleet from scratch using Carthaginian designs as a model. Then, with characteristic Roman practicality, they added an innovation that neutralized Carthaginian seamanship. The **corvus** (KOR-vuss; literally "crow") was a boarding bridge, perhaps 36 feet (11 m) long and 4 feet (1.2 m) wide (the relevant passage in Polybius is partially garbled, and reconstructions vary), with a heavy iron spike at the far end. The bridge was mounted on a pivot at the bow of a Roman ship. When a Roman vessel came alongside an enemy ship, the corvus was released. It swung down, the spike punched through the enemy's deck, locking the two ships together, and Roman infantry charged across the bridge to fight hand-to-hand on the enemy's deck.

The corvus turned naval battles into infantry battles fought at sea. Roman soldiers were the best infantry in the Mediterranean. Carthaginian sailors were the best sailors. By forcing every engagement into a boarding action, the corvus negated everything the Carthaginians were good at and leveraged everything the Romans were good at. It was crude, it made the ships top-heavy and dangerous in rough seas, and it was devastatingly effective.

Rome's first major naval victory came at the **Battle of Mylae** (MY-lay) in 260 BCE, where the corvus destroyed 50 Carthaginian ships. Then at the **Battle of Cape Ecnomus** (ek-NOH-mus) in 256 BCE, one of the largest naval engagements in ancient history (over 600 ships and perhaps 300,000 men on both sides), Rome's victory opened the sea route to Africa.

Rome attempted to end the war quickly by invading North Africa directly. The consul **Regulus** (REG-yoo-lus) landed with a Roman army and initially won victories on Carthaginian soil. But Carthage responded by hiring a **Spartan mercenary general** named **Xanthippus** (zan-THIP-us), who reorganized the Punic army, deployed war elephants and Numidian cavalry effectively, and routed the Roman invasion force at the **Battle of Tunis** (or Bagradas) in 255 BCE. Regulus was captured. The Roman survivors were evacuated by sea, and then a storm destroyed most of the Roman fleet on the way home. Rome may have lost 100,000 men or more to shipwrecks alone during the First Punic War (the figure is compiled from Polybius's accounts of multiple storm disasters and sits at the high end of modern estimates), possibly more than it lost in battle. The Mediterranean did not care about the corvus.

---

The war ground on for another fourteen years. Both sides built and lost fleets. Both sides won and lost battles. Sicily changed hands repeatedly. The decisive moment came from a single Carthaginian general.

**Hamilcar Barca** (HAM-il-kar BAR-kah; "Barca" meaning "lightning," a surname earned in battle) seized **Mount Eryx** (EH-riks) in western Sicily in 247 BCE and waged a brilliant six-year guerrilla campaign against Roman forces with minimal support from Carthage. Operating from mountaintop strongholds with a small, mobile force, Hamilcar raided Roman supply lines, ambushed Roman detachments, and made Roman control of western Sicily a continuous expensive headache. He was never defeated in the field. But he could not win the war alone.

The end came at sea. In 241 BCE, the Romans (who had by now rebuilt their fleet yet again, this time funded by voluntary loans from wealthy Roman citizens after the state treasury was exhausted) won a decisive naval victory at the **Battle of the Aegates Islands** (eh-GAH-tees), off the western tip of Sicily. The last Carthaginian fleet was destroyed. Carthage sued for peace.

The terms were harsh. Carthage surrendered Sicily entirely. It agreed to pay a war indemnity of **3,200 talents** (roughly 80 tons of silver) over ten years. It agreed not to attack Syracuse or its allies. The war that had begun over a squabble in Messana ended with Carthage losing its most important overseas possession and going bankrupt.

But the worst was yet to come. Immediately after the war, Carthage's unpaid mercenaries (the polyglot army of Libyans, Numidians, Iberians, Balearic slingers, and assorted adventurers who did the actual fighting since Carthaginian citizens rarely served in the infantry) revolted. The **Mercenary War** (241-237 BCE), which Polybius called the **"Truceless War"** for its extraordinary savagery, nearly destroyed the Carthaginian state. Atrocities were committed by both sides that shocked even ancient observers accustomed to brutal warfare. Hamilcar Barca crushed the revolt through a campaign of divide-and-conquer, exploiting ethnic divisions among the mercenaries and using the terrain of the North African hinterland to trap and destroy rebel forces piecemeal. His performance cemented his political dominance and his reputation as the most capable military leader in Carthage.

Then Rome struck again. While Carthage was consumed by the Mercenary War, Rome seized **Sardinia and Corsica** in 238 BCE, in what even the pro-Roman historian Polybius called an act of naked opportunism with no legitimate justification. Carthage, still fighting for survival against its own mercenaries, could not resist. Rome demanded an additional **1,200 talents** on top of the existing war indemnity. Carthage paid. The humiliation was complete.

But Hamilcar Barca was already thinking about the next war. He had a plan to rebuild Carthaginian power, and it began with Spain.

---

# Chapter 5 — The Barcid Project

In 237 BCE, Hamilcar Barca loaded his army onto ships and crossed to **Iberia** (the peninsula containing modern Spain and Portugal). He brought with him his nine-year-old son, **Hannibal**. Before they left, according to a story that multiple ancient sources repeat with minor variations, Hamilcar took the boy to the Temple of Baal Hammon and made him swear an oath at the altar: eternal enmity against Rome. Whether the oath actually happened or is a legend constructed after the fact to explain Hannibal's single-minded lifelong vendetta, the story captures something true about the Barcid family project. This was not standard imperial expansion. This was a revenge operation, designed from the beginning to create the military and financial resources needed for a second war against Rome.

Hamilcar spent nine years conquering much of the Iberian Peninsula's southern and eastern coast. He subdued local Iberian and Celtiberian tribes through a combination of military force, strategic marriage alliances, and the simple fact that Carthaginian silver bought a lot of loyalty. The key resource was silver. The Iberian Peninsula sat atop some of the richest silver deposits in the ancient world, and Hamilcar seized the mines with the systematic focus of a mining executive. The silver funded everything: the army's wages, the construction of new cities, and, crucially, the payoff of the remaining Roman war indemnities that were draining the Carthaginian treasury.

The strategy was clear, even if the Carthaginian Senate back home did not fully appreciate its implications. Hamilcar was building a private army, funded by Spanish silver, loyal to the Barca family rather than to the Carthaginian state, in a territory thousands of miles from the oligarchs who controlled Carthaginian domestic politics. It was, in effect, a state within a state: a Barcid military kingdom on the Iberian Peninsula, connected to Carthage by religion and language and nominal political allegiance, but operationally independent.

Hamilcar died in 228 BCE, either killed in battle or drowned crossing a river during a retreat (the sources disagree). Command passed to his son-in-law, **Hasdrubal the Fair** (has-DROO-bal; yet another recycled Carthaginian name, not to be confused with Hannibal's brother, also named Hasdrubal, who will appear in Chapter 6).

---

Hasdrubal the Fair governed Iberia through diplomacy more than warfare. His most significant achievement was founding **Carthago Nova** (kar-TAH-goh NOH-vah; "New New City," modern Cartagena on Spain's southeastern coast), which became the Barcid capital in Iberia. The location was chosen with the same strategic eye that had guided the founding of Carthage itself: Carthago Nova had an excellent harbor, a defensible position on a peninsula, and, most importantly, it sat beside the richest **silver mines** in the western Mediterranean.

The silver output was staggering. Ancient sources claim the mines near Carthago Nova yielded **300 pounds of silver daily** (a modern conversion from Polybius's figure of 25,000 drachmas, which may refer to the broader mining district rather than the Barcid operations alone), employing as many as 40,000 workers. These figures are almost certainly inflated (as ancient production statistics usually are), but the scale was enormous. The silver financed a professional army that grew steadily larger, better equipped, and more experienced. By the time Hannibal inherited command, the Barcid army in Spain was arguably the most formidable military force in the western Mediterranean: a polyglot professional army of Iberian heavy infantry, Numidian cavalry, Balearic slingers, Libyan spearmen, and Carthaginian officers, bound together by Barcid pay, Barcid charisma, and a decade of shared campaigning.

Hasdrubal also negotiated the **Ebro Treaty** with Rome, establishing the **Ebro River** (in northeastern Spain) as the boundary between Roman and Carthaginian spheres of influence. The treaty reflected Rome's growing nervousness about Barcid expansion. Rome was not yet ready for another war with Carthage, and the Ebro Treaty was designed to prevent one by drawing a clear line. Everything south of the Ebro was Carthaginian. Everything north was off-limits. The treaty worked as long as both sides respected it.

Hasdrubal was assassinated by a Celtic slave in 221 BCE (the motives are unclear; personal vengeance is the most common explanation in the sources). The Barcid army in Spain, acting with the speed and decisiveness of a military organization rather than a constitutional government, acclaimed the twenty-five-year-old **Hannibal Barca** as their commander. The Carthaginian Senate back home, presented with a fait accompli by an army that was effectively loyal to the Barcid family, reluctantly confirmed the appointment.

---

Hannibal Barca is one of the most famous military commanders in human history, ranked alongside Alexander the Great, Julius Caesar, and Napoleon by virtually every military historian who has attempted the exercise. Ancient sources are nearly unanimous: even Roman writers who hated everything Carthage stood for acknowledged Hannibal's genius. Livy, as patriotic a Roman as ever lived, described Hannibal as possessing "extreme daring in braving dangers" and "supreme skill in turning them to his account." Polybius, the most sober and analytical ancient historian, considered Hannibal the finest tactician the Mediterranean world had produced.

The young commander moved quickly. In 219 BCE, Hannibal besieged and destroyed **Saguntum** (sah-GOON-tum), a city on the eastern Spanish coast that was allied with Rome. Saguntum was south of the Ebro River, technically within the Carthaginian sphere, but its alliance with Rome created an ambiguity that Hannibal deliberately exploited. The siege lasted eight months. The Saguntines resisted with desperate courage (the city's population reportedly burned their valuables and many killed themselves rather than surrender), but Hannibal took the city and sacked it.

Rome demanded that Carthage hand Hannibal over for punishment. The Carthaginian Senate refused. According to Polybius, the Roman envoy held up the fold of his toga and told the Carthaginian senators that it contained either peace or war, and they could choose which fell out. The Carthaginians told him to let fall whichever he pleased. He let fall war.

Hannibal had planned for this. He had been planning for it since he was nine years old at his father's altar. What he did next is the reason military academies exist.

Rather than wait for Rome to bring the war to Spain or Africa, where Carthage had always fought its wars and always lost, Hannibal decided to do what nobody expected: take the war to Italy itself. He would march his army overland from Spain, across southern France, over the Alps, and into the heart of Roman territory. He would defeat Rome on its own soil, peel away Rome's Italian allies, and destroy the Roman confederation from the inside.

The plan was audacious to the point of insanity. The overland route was roughly 1,000 miles (1,600 km). It required crossing the Pyrenees, traversing hostile Gallic territory, fording the Rhône River, and then climbing over the highest mountain range in Western Europe in late autumn with an army that included 37 war elephants. No one had ever attempted anything remotely comparable. Hannibal did it.

---

# Chapter 6 — The War That Changed Everything

In late October 218 BCE, Hannibal Barca led approximately 50,000 infantry, 9,000 cavalry, and 37 war elephants over the **Alps**. The crossing took about fifteen days. It was a nightmare.

The specific pass Hannibal used has been debated for two thousand years and remains unresolved (the most commonly cited candidates are the **Col de Clapier**, the **Col du Mont Cenis**, and the **Col de la Traversette**, all in the western Alps between modern France and Italy). What is not debated is the cost. Rockslides smashed through the column. Hostile mountain tribes attacked from above, rolling boulders down onto the marching army and raiding the baggage train. Early snowfall made the trails treacherous. The elephants, African forest elephants that had never experienced cold weather, slipped on ice and fell into ravines. Men died of exposure, exhaustion, and the simple catastrophe of trying to move a Mediterranean army through alpine terrain in late autumn.

When Hannibal reached the Po Valley of northern Italy, he had roughly 26,000 infantry, 9,000 cavalry (the cavalry fared better than the infantry), and a handful of surviving elephants. He had lost nearly half his army without fighting a single battle. The Romans, who had expected to fight Hannibal in Spain or at worst in southern France, were stunned to discover a Carthaginian army in Italy.

Hannibal's first priority was to rebuild his forces by recruiting among the **Gauls** (Celtic peoples of northern Italy who had been recently conquered by Rome and were eager for revenge). Thousands of Gallic warriors joined him. Then he began dismantling Roman armies with a methodical brilliance that still astonishes military historians.

---

At the **Battle of the Trebia** (TREH-bee-ah) in December 218 BCE, Hannibal lured a Roman army across an icy river on a freezing morning, then hit them with a cavalry charge and a concealed force of 2,000 men under his brother **Mago** (placed behind Roman lines the night before in a hidden ravine). The Romans, cold, hungry, and outnumbered in cavalry, were destroyed. Two-thirds of the Roman army was killed or captured.

At the **Battle of Lake Trasimene** (trah-sih-MEE-nee) in June 217 BCE, Hannibal executed the largest successful ambush in military history. A Roman army under the consul **Flaminius** (flah-MIN-ee-us), marching along the northern shore of the lake through a morning fog, walked directly into a trap. Hannibal had concealed his entire army in the wooded hills above the road. When the Roman column was strung out along the lakeshore, Hannibal attacked from every direction simultaneously. The Romans were pushed into the lake or cut down on the road. Roughly 15,000 were killed, including Flaminius himself, and another 15,000 were captured. The survivors who tried to swim the lake drowned in their armor. It was not a battle. It was an annihilation.

Rome responded by appointing **Quintus Fabius Maximus** (FAB-ee-us MAX-ih-mus) as dictator (a constitutional emergency office in the Roman Republic, not the modern sense of the word). Fabius adopted a strategy that became known as the **Fabian strategy**: avoid pitched battles, shadow Hannibal's army, raid his supply lines, and wait for him to exhaust himself. The strategy was militarily sound but politically unpopular. Romans considered it cowardly. Fabius was mocked as "the Delayer" (*Cunctator*).

When Fabius's term expired, the Roman Senate, impatient for a decisive victory, raised the largest army Rome had ever assembled: roughly 80,000 infantry and 6,000 cavalry, commanded by two consuls, **Lucius Aemilius Paullus** (ay-MIL-ee-us PAWL-us) and **Gaius Terentius Varro** (VAIR-oh). The plan was simple: overwhelm Hannibal with numbers. The two consuls alternated command daily. On August 2, 216 BCE, on Varro's command day, the Roman army met Hannibal on a flat plain near the village of **Cannae** (KAN-eye), in southeastern Italy.

---

The **Battle of Cannae** is the most studied tactical engagement in military history. It is still taught at West Point, Sandhurst, Saint-Cyr, and every other military academy in the world. What Hannibal did on that August afternoon has been called the gold standard of tactical annihilation, and no one has ever improved on it.

Hannibal had roughly 50,000 men (some estimates say fewer). The Romans had roughly 80,000 (some estimates say more). The Romans massed their heavy infantry in a deep formation in the center, planning to punch through Hannibal's line by sheer weight of numbers. Hannibal's response was the **double envelopment**.

He placed his weakest infantry (Gauls and Iberians) in the center of his line, arranged in a convex bulge facing the Romans. On the flanks, he placed his strongest troops: veteran **Libyan heavy infantry**, equipped with Roman weapons and armor scavenged from previous victories. On both wings, he deployed his cavalry: Numidian light cavalry on one wing, Iberian and Gallic heavy cavalry on the other.

The battle began. The Roman infantry advanced into Hannibal's center. The Gallic and Iberian troops gave ground, slowly, deliberately, bending backward under the Roman pressure. The convex bulge flattened, then became concave, drawing the Roman infantry deeper and deeper into what felt like a collapsing enemy line. The Romans pressed forward eagerly. They were winning. The Carthaginian center was breaking.

It was not breaking. It was bending. And while the Roman infantry pushed deeper into the trap, the Carthaginian cavalry on both wings was winning its own battle. The Iberian and Gallic heavy cavalry routed the Roman cavalry on one wing. The Numidian cavalry drove off the Roman horsemen on the other. Then both cavalry forces swung around behind the Roman infantry and sealed the circle.

The Roman army, 80,000 strong, was now completely surrounded. The Libyan heavy infantry on the flanks wheeled inward, compressing the Romans from both sides. The cavalry closed the rear. The Romans were packed so tightly that they could not swing their swords. They could not maneuver, could not retreat, could not form new lines. Ancient sources describe men being crushed to death by the pressure of bodies around them. The killing went on for hours.

By the end of the afternoon, somewhere between 50,000 and 70,000 Roman soldiers were dead. The consul Aemilius Paullus was killed. Eighty senators, roughly a quarter of the Roman Senate, died on the field. It was the worst single-day military disaster in Roman history, and arguably the worst single-day battlefield loss of any Western power until the first day of the Somme in 1916 CE.

---

Cannae should have ended the war. In the normal pattern of ancient Mediterranean warfare, a defeat of this magnitude forced the loser to negotiate. Hannibal expected Rome's Italian allies, shocked by the annihilation of the largest army Rome had ever assembled, to defect. Some did. The important southern Italian city of **Capua** (KAH-poo-ah) switched to Hannibal's side. Several Greek cities in southern Italy followed. **Syracuse**, the great Greek city in Sicily, defected from Rome to Carthage.

But most of Rome's allies stayed loyal. And Rome, in the most psychologically remarkable response in ancient military history, simply refused to negotiate. The Roman Senate forbade even the discussion of peace terms. They raised new armies. They adopted the Fabian strategy that they had mocked before Cannae. They fought Hannibal's allies one by one, besieging defecting cities, punishing defectors with exemplary brutality, and systematically isolating Hannibal in southern Italy without giving him another pitched battle.

Hannibal's strategic problem became clear. He had won every battle. He had destroyed three Roman armies. He had killed perhaps 100,000 Roman soldiers in two years. And he had not won the war. Italy was too large, too full of fortified cities, and too politically complex for even the greatest field commander to conquer with a single expeditionary army. Hannibal needed reinforcements, either from Carthage (which the Senate was reluctant to send, partly from political jealousy of the Barcid family) or from his brother Hasdrubal in Spain. Neither came in time.

In 207 BCE, Hannibal's brother **Hasdrubal Barca** finally crossed the Alps with a relief army, following roughly the same route Hannibal had used eleven years earlier. But the Romans intercepted Hasdrubal before he could link up with Hannibal. At the **Battle of the Metaurus** (meh-TAW-rus) River, the Roman consul Gaius Claudius Nero (not the later emperor) destroyed Hasdrubal's army and killed Hasdrubal himself. The Romans catapulted Hasdrubal's severed head into Hannibal's camp. Ancient sources report that Hannibal, looking at his brother's face, said he now saw the fate of Carthage. It was reportedly the first time anyone had seen Hannibal weep.

---

The war turned decisively when a young Roman general named **Publius Cornelius Scipio** (SKIP-ee-oh; later called **Scipio Africanus**), who had studied Hannibal's tactics with the dedication of a doctoral student, took command of the Roman forces in Spain. Scipio was in his mid-twenties, had survived Cannae as a teenager, and possessed a combination of tactical brilliance and personal charisma that made him Hannibal's first genuine Roman equal. He conquered Carthaginian Spain in a series of campaigns between 210 and 206 BCE, cutting off Hannibal's reinforcements and his Spanish silver revenue.

Then, in 204 BCE, Scipio did what Agathocles had done a century earlier (Chapter 3) and what Hannibal's own strategy had always been vulnerable to: he invaded Africa. Landing in North Africa with a veteran army, Scipio threatened Carthage directly. The Carthaginian Senate, panicking, recalled Hannibal from Italy to defend the homeland. After fifteen years of undefeated campaigning on Italian soil, Hannibal sailed home.

The two greatest generals of the ancient world met at the **Battle of Zama** (ZAH-mah) in 202 BCE, fought roughly 75 miles (120 km) southwest of Carthage. Hannibal had a larger army but inferior cavalry, because the **Numidian** horsemen (North African riders who fought without saddles or bridles, the finest light cavalry in the ancient world; Chapter 7) had switched their allegiance from Carthage to Rome. Scipio used the Numidian cavalry exactly as Hannibal had used them at Cannae: to rout the enemy cavalry, swing around behind the enemy infantry, and complete the encirclement. The student used the master's own technique against him.

Hannibal was decisively defeated for the first and only time in his career. The peace terms ended Carthage as a great power. Carthage surrendered its entire fleet except ten warships. It gave up all overseas territories. It agreed to pay an indemnity of **10,000 talents of silver** over fifty years (roughly 260 tons, more than three times the First Punic War indemnity). And it accepted the provision that would ultimately seal its fate: Carthage could never again wage war without Roman permission.

Hannibal served briefly as a suffete in post-war Carthage, reforming the city's finances and rooting out corruption. But Roman pressure forced him into exile in 195 BCE. He spent his remaining years as a military adviser to various eastern Mediterranean kings threatened by Rome. When Roman agents finally cornered him in **Bithynia** (bih-THIN-ee-ah; a kingdom in northwestern Anatolia) in 183 BCE, he took poison rather than be captured. His reported last words: "Let us now put an end to the great anxiety of the Romans, who have thought it too long to wait for the death of a hated old man."

Scipio Africanus, ironically, died in the same year, also embittered and politically marginalized by the Roman system he had saved.

---

# Chapter 7 — How the Carthaginians Lived

The previous chapters told the story of Carthage's wars. This chapter tells the story of the civilization that fought them. Because Punic literature was destroyed, most of what we know about Carthaginian daily life comes from archaeology, from scattered references in Greek and Roman texts, and from comparative analysis with the Phoenician homeland cities that Carthage descended from (Phoenicia Ch 1-5). The picture is incomplete, but it is more detailed than you might expect from a civilization whose enemies burned its libraries.

**Carthaginian religion** was rooted in the Phoenician tradition but evolved into something distinctively Punic over the centuries. The chief deities were **Baal Hammon** and **Tanit** (Chapter 1). Baal Hammon was a sky and fertility god, associated with agriculture, weather, and prosperity. His depictions show a bearded elder with ram's horns, seated on a throne, sometimes flanked by sphinxes. Tanit was a lunar goddess, associated with fertility, war, and protection. Her symbol, found on thousands of Punic artifacts from Spain to Libya, consists of a triangle (body) topped by a circle (head) with a horizontal line between (arms). By the fifth century BCE, Tanit had eclipsed Baal Hammon as the most prominent deity in Carthaginian religious life. Inscriptions at the Tophet place her name first: "To the Lady Tanit, Face of Baal, and to the Lord Baal Hammon."

Carthaginian religion also absorbed influences from its neighbors. **Punic religious syncretism** blended Phoenician gods with Libyan, Egyptian, and Greek elements. Tanit absorbed aspects of the Greek goddess **Demeter** (goddess of grain and the harvest) and the Egyptian goddess **Isis** (goddess of magic and motherhood). After a plague struck the Carthaginian army besieging Syracuse in 396 BCE, the Carthaginian state officially adopted the worship of Demeter and **Kore** (Persephone), building Greek-style temples and appointing Greek residents of Carthage as priests. The adoption was pragmatic: the Carthaginians believed the plague was divine punishment for destroying Greek temples, and they hedged their theological bets by adding the offended Greek gods to their own pantheon. This willingness to absorb foreign religious practices, to treat religion as additive rather than exclusive, was characteristically Phoenician and sharply different from the exclusivist traditions of monotheistic religions like Judaism.

The cult of **Melqart**, the patron god of Tyre (Phoenicia Ch 4), remained important in Carthage as a symbol of the city's Phoenician heritage, but it was a secondary cult by the fourth century BCE, overshadowed by Baal Hammon and Tanit. The annual tribute that Carthage sent to the Temple of Melqart at Tyre was more a cultural gesture than a theological necessity, like an immigrant family sending flowers to the old family church.

---

The **Tophet debate** deserves a fuller treatment than Chapter 1 could provide, because it is the single most contested question in Punic studies.

The facts are not in dispute. The Tophet of Carthage (and similar precincts at Punic sites across the western Mediterranean, including in Sardinia and Sicily) contains thousands of urns with cremated remains. The remains are overwhelmingly those of very young children, many of them neonates (newborns). Some urns contain animal remains (usually lambs) instead of, or mixed with, human remains. The urns are accompanied by **votive stelae** (stone slabs carved with inscriptions and images), many bearing the sign of Tanit and dedicatory texts. The inscriptions use formulaic language, often including the phrase *mlk b'l* (rendered in various ways by scholars), which some interpret as "molk sacrifice to Baal" and others interpret differently.

The case for child sacrifice rests on three pillars. First, the ancient literary evidence: Greek and Roman authors, including **Diodorus Siculus**, **Plutarch**, and **Cleitarchus** (KLY-tar-kus; an early Hellenistic historian), describe the Carthaginians sacrificing children by placing them in the arms of a bronze statue of Baal Hammon, from which the children rolled into a fire below. The descriptions are vivid and consistent across multiple independent sources. Second, the archaeological evidence: the disproportionate number of very young infants (including prenatal remains) suggests these are not random natural deaths but selected offerings. Some studies of the skeletal remains have found evidence consistent with death before cremation. Third, the inscriptions: some scholars read the votive texts as recording the fulfillment of vows, where a parent promised Baal Hammon or Tanit a child in exchange for divine favor.

The case against child sacrifice also rests on three pillars. First, the literary sources are all from Carthage's enemies: Greeks and Romans who had every reason to demonize the Carthaginians, and whose descriptions of Punic religious practices may be propaganda designed to justify wars of aggression. Accusations of child sacrifice have been leveled at many peoples throughout history, often falsely. Second, infant mortality in the ancient world was appallingly high, running between 30 and 50 percent in the first year of life. A cemetery dedicated specifically to infant burials, with special ritual treatment, is not inherently sinister. The Tophet may be a sacred burial ground for children who died naturally and were consecrated to the gods in death. Third, the presence of animal remains in some urns suggests substitution (an animal offered instead of a child), which implies a system more nuanced than wholesale infant sacrifice.

The honest conclusion is that the evidence is genuinely ambiguous, the scholarly community is genuinely divided, and the question may never be definitively resolved because the people who could have told us what they were doing left no surviving account of their own religious practices. This is perhaps the cruelest consequence of the destruction of Punic literature: the one question that most fascinates and horrifies modern observers is the one question the Carthaginians could have answered, if anyone had preserved their books.

---

The Carthaginian economy was among the most diversified in the ancient world. The **purple dye industry** (inherited from Tyre; Phoenicia Ch 4) continued on an industrial scale, harvesting murex sea snails from North African waters and producing the Tyrian purple that was the ancient world's most expensive textile dye. Agricultural exports from the African estates (grain, olive oil, wine, fruit) were massive. The trans-Saharan **gold trade** and the Atlantic **tin trade** provided access to metals that the Mediterranean world desperately needed.

Carthage began minting its own **coinage** around 410 BCE, relatively late for a major Mediterranean power (Athens had been minting coins for over a century). The early Carthaginian coins featured the head of Tanit on one side and a horse on the other, images that became iconic of Punic culture. Coins were struck in electrum (a natural gold-silver alloy), gold, silver, and bronze, and their primary purpose was paying mercenary armies. The Barcid coins minted in Spain, with their portraits of Melqart-Hercules (probably modeled on Hamilcar or Hannibal himself, though this is debated), are among the finest coins of the ancient world.

**Carthaginian navigation** was the best in the Mediterranean. Punic sailors navigated by **Ursa Minor** (the Little Bear constellation, containing the star closest to true north), a technique so identified with the Phoenicians that the Greeks called the constellation "the Phoenician" (Phoenicia Ch 4). Carthaginian pilots maintained detailed **rutters** (written sailing directions) covering the entire western Mediterranean, the Atlantic coast, and the West African shore. This navigational knowledge was classified as a state secret. The story of the captain who wrecked his ship rather than reveal the tin route (Chapter 2) illustrates how seriously Carthage took commercial intelligence. Carthaginian coins from the third century BCE have been reported found in the **Azores**, 900 miles (1,450 km) into the open Atlantic. If the finds are genuine (some scholars question the provenance), Punic sailors ventured far beyond the Pillars of Hercules into waters that no European would explore again for over a thousand years.

---

The Carthaginian military system was unique in the ancient world. Unlike Rome, where citizen soldiers formed the backbone of the army, and unlike the Greek city-states, where citizen hoplites (heavy infantry) were the primary fighting force, Carthage relied overwhelmingly on **mercenaries and allied contingents**. Libyan infantry from the African territories, **Numidian cavalry** (lightly armed riders from the kingdoms west of Carthage who fought without saddles or bridles, controlling their horses with just a rope halter, the finest light cavalry in the ancient world), Iberian swordsmen, **Balearic slingers** (from the islands of Majorca and Minorca, so accurate they could reportedly hit a man's head at 100 yards or 90 meters), Gallic warriors, and assorted Greek and Italian adventurers made up the bulk of every Carthaginian army.

The only citizen military unit was the **Sacred Band of Carthage**, an elite heavy infantry corps of roughly 2,500-3,000 men drawn from the city's aristocratic families. The Sacred Band served as the backbone of the army, but it was a small core surrounded by a much larger mass of hired soldiers. The mercenary system had obvious advantages (professional soldiers who fought for pay were more available and arguably better trained than citizen militias; citizen casualties were minimal, which reduced domestic political pressure during long wars) and equally obvious disadvantages (mercenary loyalty was to the paymaster, not to the state; and when the pay stopped, as it did after the First Punic War, the mercenaries did not go home quietly; Chapter 4).

**War elephants** were Carthage's most iconic military asset. Carthage used **North African forest elephants** (a now-extinct subspecies, *Loxodonta africana pharaoensis*, smaller than the Indian elephants used by the Hellenistic successor kingdoms in the East). The elephants were captured in the forests of the Atlas Mountains and trained for battle, carrying towers with archers and javelin-throwers. Their primary value was psychological: the sight and smell of a charging elephant terrified soldiers and horses who had never encountered one. Their military effectiveness was inconsistent. Elephants were devastating when everything went right, but they were as dangerous to friendly troops as to the enemy when they panicked, and experienced opponents learned to open lanes in their formations and target the elephants with javelins and fire. Hannibal's Alpine elephants are the most famous war elephants in history, but most of them died in the crossing or shortly after, and they played almost no role in his Italian campaigns.

One Carthaginian who achieved lasting cultural impact was not a general but a playwright. **Terence** (TEHR-ens; Publius Terentius Afer, roughly 185-159 BCE), a Carthaginian-born slave who was educated and freed by a Roman senator, became one of Rome's greatest comic dramatists. His six surviving plays, refined adaptations of Greek New Comedy, influenced European literature from Shakespeare to Moliere. His famous line, "I am human, and I think nothing human is alien to me" (*Homo sum, humani nihil a me alienum puto*), is one of the most quoted sentences in Western literature. The most enduring cultural export of the Punic world was a freed slave who wrote in Latin.

---

# Chapter 8 — Carthago Delenda Est

After the Second Punic War, the peace terms (Chapter 6) were designed to keep Carthage permanently subordinate. The city could not wage war without Roman permission. Its fleet was reduced to ten warships. It owed 10,000 talents over fifty years. Rome installed its ally **Masinissa** (mah-sih-NISS-ah), king of the **Numidians**, as a watchdog on Carthage's western border, with the implicit understanding that Masinissa could encroach on Carthaginian territory and Carthage could not fight back without Roman approval, which Rome would never grant.

For fifty years, it worked. Carthage obeyed the treaty. It paid its indemnity. It did not wage war. And, infuriatingly for the hardliners in the Roman Senate, it prospered. Carthage's **economic recovery** was so rapid that by the 190s BCE the city offered to pay off its entire remaining fifty-year debt ahead of schedule. Rome, not wanting to lose the financial leverage, actually declined the early payment. The commercial machine that had made Carthage the richest city in the western Mediterranean before the wars was still running. African grain, olive oil, and trade goods flowed through the harbor. The Cothon, stripped of its warships, hummed with merchant traffic. Carthage without its military was still Carthage: a city built to make money.

This recovery terrified Rome. Or rather, it terrified a faction within Rome led by the senator **Cato the Elder** (KAY-toh; Marcus Porcius Cato, 234-149 BCE), an archconservative who had fought in the Second Punic War as a young soldier and never forgave Carthage for the trauma of Hannibal's invasion. Cato visited Carthage as part of a diplomatic delegation around 157 BCE and returned to Rome alarmed by the city's prosperity. The Carthaginian figs he brought back were so fresh and plump that he waved them before the Senate as evidence of how close and how dangerous the old enemy remained.

From that point until his death in 149 BCE, Cato ended every speech in the Senate, regardless of the topic under discussion, with the same phrase: **"Ceterum censeo Carthaginem esse delendam"** ("Furthermore, I am of the opinion that Carthage must be destroyed"), shortened in popular memory to **"Carthago delenda est."** Whether or not the anecdote is literally true in every instance (it is widely attested in ancient sources, though some scholars suspect embellishment), it captures the obsessive quality of Roman anxiety about Carthage. A city that had been stripped of its military, its fleet, its territory, and its sovereignty was still perceived as an existential threat fifty years after its defeat. The anxiety was not entirely irrational. Rome had nearly been destroyed by Hannibal. The institutional memory of Cannae and Trasimene and fifteen years of a Carthaginian army roaming freely through Italy was seared into the Roman political class. Cato's campaign was fear dressed up as policy.

---

In 149 BCE, Rome manufactured the pretext it needed. Masinissa, the Numidian king who had been steadily encroaching on Carthaginian territory for decades, finally provoked Carthage into a defensive war. Carthage fought back against Numidian aggression, which technically violated the treaty prohibition against waging war without Roman permission. Rome declared the **Third Punic War**.

But Rome did not simply declare war. It demanded that Carthage surrender all weapons and military equipment, which Carthage did, handing over what Appian claims was 200,000 sets of armor and 2,000 catapults (suspiciously round numbers, but the point is the same: everything). Then, with Carthage disarmed, Rome delivered its final demand: Carthage must abandon its coastal city entirely and resettle at least **10 miles (16 km) inland**. For a maritime civilization whose identity, economy, and physical infrastructure were built around the sea, this was a death sentence. Rome was not demanding submission. It was demanding suicide.

Carthage refused. The city that had surrendered its weapons decided to fight anyway. In a scene that even Rome's own historians described with grudging admiration, the entire population mobilized. Workshops were converted into weapons factories. Citizens melted down household metal to forge swords and spearheads. Women cut their hair to braid into bowstrings. The city's walls, which Rome had not demanded be torn down (perhaps an oversight, perhaps confidence that a disarmed city could not resist), became the defensive perimeter for a three-year siege.

The early stages of the siege went badly for Rome. Carthage's walls were formidable, the population was desperate, and the Roman commanders were initially incompetent. Two years of inconclusive fighting passed before Rome sent its best general: **Scipio Aemilianus** (SKIP-ee-oh ay-mil-ee-AH-nus), the adopted grandson of Scipio Africanus, the man who had defeated Hannibal at Zama. The younger Scipio was methodical, patient, and ruthless. He sealed off the harbor, cut the supply lines, and slowly tightened the noose.

---

In the spring of 146 BCE, Scipio Aemilianus breached the walls and the final assault began. What followed was six days of street-by-street, house-by-house urban combat, some of the most brutal fighting in ancient history. The Carthaginians fought from the rooftops, from barricades, from the upper stories of buildings that lined the streets leading up to Byrsa Hill. Roman soldiers advanced by breaking through the walls of buildings, fighting room to room, and then pulling down the structures behind them to clear paths for reinforcements. The three streets leading from the harbor to the Byrsa were lined with six-story apartment buildings, and the fighting raged through every floor.

The last defenders retreated to the **Temple of Eshmun** on the summit of Byrsa Hill. Ancient sources describe the scene: the temple surrounded, the remaining defenders crowded inside, the building set alight. The commander's wife, having refused to surrender, dressed in her finest clothes, cursed her husband (who had offered himself to Scipio's mercy), killed her children, and threw herself and the children into the flames. The last Carthaginians burned alive in their holiest temple rather than submit. Whether the details are accurate or literarily enhanced by Roman historians who wanted a dramatic ending, the temple's fall was the end.

The **destruction of Carthage** was systematic and deliberate. The city was razed. Buildings were demolished. Temples were looted and torn down. The harbor complex, the great Cothon with its 220 warship berths, was destroyed. An estimated 50,000 survivors were sold into slavery. According to later tradition, the Romans plowed the ruins and sowed the fields with salt to ensure nothing would ever grow there. The salt story is almost certainly apocryphal (no ancient source mentions it; the detail first appears in the nineteenth century CE), but the spirit of the legend is accurate. Rome intended to erase Carthage from the map and from memory. The city's territory became the Roman province of **Africa** (the origin of the continent's name).

The city burned for seventeen days.

---

The destruction extended to the mind as well as the city. Rome distributed Carthage's libraries to Numidian princes, allies who had helped win the final war. Those libraries vanished into oblivion. Every history the Carthaginians wrote about themselves, every epic poem, every religious text, every philosophical work, every play, every personal letter that had been preserved in the city's archives was gone. The sole exception was **Mago's agricultural treatise** (Chapter 3), which the Roman Senate, in an act of selective mercy that reveals Rome's priorities with painful clarity, ordered translated into Latin. Rome saved the farming manual and burned everything else.

The **loss of Punic literature** is one of the great intellectual catastrophes of the ancient world. We know Carthage almost entirely through the words of its enemies. The Greeks who fought Carthage in Sicily for two centuries. The Romans who fought three existential wars against Carthage and ended by obliterating the city. These are our sources. They are not inventing facts wholesale, but they are choosing which facts to record and how to interpret them. When Polybius describes Carthaginian "treachery" (*fides Punica*, "Punic faith," was a Roman proverb meaning dishonesty), he is using a moral framework supplied by Carthage's enemies. When Diodorus describes child sacrifice, he is drawing on a literary tradition that served Greek self-congratulation. We have no Carthaginian rebuttal. We have no Punic Herodotus, no Punic Thucydides, no Punic Livy. The silence is total.

In one of history's darkest coincidences, Rome also sacked and destroyed **Corinth**, the great Greek commercial city, in the same year, 146 BCE. The twin destructions of Carthage and Corinth are conventionally taken as the moment when Rome became the undisputed master of the entire Mediterranean world. Carthage's seven centuries of civilization ended in fire. Corinth's glory was ground under Roman boots. The message was clear: Rome would tolerate no rival, no matter how weakened, no matter how compliant.

---

Carthage would eventually be refounded, first by Julius Caesar as a Roman colony in 46 BCE (a century after the destruction) and later expanded under Augustus into one of the great cities of the Roman Empire. Roman Carthage became the capital of Roman Africa, a center of Latin literature, a hub of early Christianity (Saint Augustine was born in Punic North Africa and spent years in Carthage), and eventually one of the largest cities in the Roman world. But Roman Carthage was a Latin-speaking Roman city, not a Phoenician one. The Punic language survived in rural North Africa for centuries (Saint Augustine noted that his parishioners still spoke it in the early fifth century CE), and Punic religious practices lingered in folk tradition long after the old temples were gone. But the civilization that had built the Cothon and produced Hannibal and written twenty-eight volumes on agriculture was finished.

What remains? More than you might think. The **sign of Tanit** appears on thousands of artifacts in museums across Europe and North Africa. The excavated streets of **Kerkouane** (Chapter 3) preserve the grid plan and bathing rooms of ordinary Punic houses. The **Tophet** urns, whatever they represent, are archaeological evidence of sustained ritual practice spanning six centuries. Punic inscriptions, mostly votive and funerary, provide a corpus of the written language even if the literature is lost. And the military campaigns, especially Hannibal's, are documented in such exhaustive detail by Greek and Roman historians that we know more about the Second Punic War than about most wars in ancient history, albeit entirely from the Roman perspective.

The Carthaginian legacy is, above all, a lesson in what happens when the losers of history leave no books. Carthage was a great civilization by any reasonable standard: wealthy, sophisticated, well-governed, innovative in agriculture and navigation and urban planning, capable of producing military genius of the highest order. It was also, according to its enemies, cruel, treacherous, and religiously barbaric. We cannot weigh these claims because the defendants never got to speak. The Romans wrote the verdict, served as the prosecution, and burned the defense's archives.

Every letter in every alphabet you have ever read descends from the Phoenician script that Carthage carried across the western Mediterranean (Phoenicia Ch 3). The name of the entire continent of Africa derives from the Roman province that replaced Carthage. The gorilla is named after creatures spotted by a Carthaginian admiral. The word "Punic" survives as a synonym for treachery, which tells you who wrote the dictionary.

Carthage built something remarkable, and Rome destroyed it so thoroughly that we can only glimpse the outline through the rubble and the hostile testimony of the people who struck the match. Seven centuries of civilization, silenced in seventeen days of fire.

# Elam — The Other Half of Mesopotamia

**Goal:** Narrative for the first TL in the Iranian Plateau chain. Covers roughly 3100 to 500 BCE — Proto-Elamite script through the long rival-and-partner relationship with Mesopotamia, the Middle Elamite peak at Chogha Zanbil, the spectacular sack of Babylon in 1158 BCE, the Assyrian destruction in 646 BCE, and the Persian echo under Cyrus and Darius when the language of the destroyed kingdom became the administrative language of the biggest empire the world had yet seen. The long and the short of it is that Elam never actually ended. It just went invisible by becoming the spine of the Persian state.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — Two Elams

Here is a sentence worth starting with: **Elam was two places, not one.**

This is the single most important thing to know about the civilization this book is about, and it is the thing most books about the ancient Near East fail to make clear. When an Assyrian scribe wrote about "Elam," and when a Babylonian priest wrote about "Elam," and when a Persian king a thousand years later reminisced about "Anshan," they were all talking about the same political and cultural entity. But they were talking about *two connected halves* of it — a lowland half on the flat plain east of the Tigris River, and a highland half in the mountain valleys of southwestern Iran. Those two halves were different in climate, in agriculture, in dialect, in architectural style, and often in political loyalty. Elam worked when the lowland and the highland cooperated. It fell apart whenever they didn't.

The reader who is willing to hold two places in mind at once — Susa (SOO-sah) down on the plain, and Anshan (AHN-shahn) up in the mountains — is going to do very well with the next two and a half thousand years of this story. The reader who tries to map "Elam" onto a single city or a single region is going to be confused roughly every other chapter, because the political center of gravity keeps sliding between the two halves.

So let's start with the land.

---

Southwestern Iran is a geography of contrasts, and the contrasts happen fast. If you stand at the Persian Gulf coast and look north, the land rises in steps. First a narrow coastal strip, then the lowland plain of **Khuzestan** (KHOO-zeh-stan), then the foothills, then the Zagros Mountains rearing up in long parallel ridges, then the high Iranian plateau above 5,000 feet. From bathing in the Gulf to riding a donkey over a 7,000-foot mountain pass is about 150 miles (240 km) as the crow flies. That vertical rise in that short horizontal distance is what made Elam possible — and what made it permanently difficult to govern.

**Khuzestan**, the lowland half, is a hot flat alluvial plain irrigated by three rivers flowing out of the Zagros toward the Gulf. The biggest is the **Karun**, the longest river entirely inside Iran, and the second is the **Karkheh**, both rich with silt deposited by seasonal floods. The climate on the plain is close to the Mesopotamian climate we have been studying in the sister volume to this book (Mesopotamia Ch 1): hot summers, mild winters, low annual rainfall, farmland sustained by irrigation rather than rain. The crops are the same as Sumer's — emmer wheat, barley, date palms, garden vegetables, sheep and goats. If you were dropped blindfolded into a Khuzestan village in 2500 BCE, you would think you were in lower Mesopotamia until someone started speaking. The local language would not be Sumerian or Akkadian. It would be **Elamite**, and it would be a language unrelated to anything else.

**Fars** (FARS — sometimes rendered "Fārs" with the Persian vowel length, the source of the English word "Persia"), the highland half, is a completely different world. Fars is the great high plateau region of southwestern Iran, sitting at 5,000 to 6,000 feet elevation, cold in winter and dry in summer, with short growing seasons and a landscape of mountain basins fed by seasonal streams. The main city up here, throughout the Elamite period, was **Anshan** — a large walled town sitting in a broad highland valley about 160 miles (260 km) east of Susa over the Zagros ridges. Anshan is now known archaeologically as the site of **Tall-i Malyan**, which is a modern Persian name meaning "Mound of Malyan." The Elamite identity of the site was not confirmed until 1971, when archaeologists excavating the mound found tablets with Elamite text naming the city. Until that identification, modern scholars knew "Anshan" from Mesopotamian and Persian royal inscriptions without being sure where it was. The answer turned out to be: deep in the Zagros, a long walled city that Mesopotamian expeditions never reached.

And here is the thing that matters. Khuzestan and Fars were economically, politically, and culturally bound together across the Zagros for almost three thousand years. Caravans linked them. Royal inscriptions named kings "of Anshan and Susa" or "of Susa and Anshan" depending on which end of the country the king considered primary. The same gods were worshipped in both halves. The same language was spoken in both halves, though with regional variants. Elam was a single civilization whose center kept swinging between a hot lowland plain that looked like Mesopotamia and a cold highland plateau that looked like the Iranian interior. This is the peculiar structural feature of Elamite history. No other major ancient Near Eastern kingdom had the same shape.

It also meant that Elam could not be conquered simply. When an invader took the Khuzestan lowland, the Elamite kings retreated into the highland and waited. When an invader tried to push past Susa into the Zagros, the mountains and the narrow passes and the Elamite archers did most of the defensive work for them. Repeatedly across the next 2,500 years, Mesopotamian and later Assyrian armies would destroy Susa, would camp in Khuzestan, would proclaim Elam conquered — and then would leave, and the Elamite kings would come back down from Anshan and rebuild Susa and start over. The two-Elam geography was Elam's most reliable strategic asset.

---

Now, the city of **Susa**.

Susa is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities on Earth. The earliest occupation at the site — a small village — dates to around 4200 BCE, making Susa older than almost every Sumerian city across the Tigris. The one plausible exception is **Eridu** (EH-ree-doo), the southernmost Sumerian settlement, whose earliest village layers reach back further still (Mesopotamia Ch 2 calls Eridu the oldest city in the world). Susa is older than Egyptian Memphis, older than anything in the Indus Valley, older than Longshan China. By the standards of "cities" as the next chapter of this book will use the word, early Susa wasn't one yet; it was a village that happened to sit on a particularly good spot where three river systems intersected and where the foothills of the Zagros met the plain. But it grew. Fast. By 4000 BCE, Susa had a substantial population, monumental buildings, painted pottery that is now one of the signature crafts of the Near Eastern Chalcolithic (the "Copper Age," roughly 4500–3300 BCE, the transitional period between stone-tool Neolithic farming and the full Bronze Age when tin-bronze replaced pure copper), and — most interestingly — tablets.

The Susa tablets from the end of the fourth millennium BCE are the Elamite contribution to the deep prehistory of writing. They are written in a script that modern scholars call **Proto-Elamite** (not a name the Elamites would have recognized), and it is one of the oldest writing systems in the world. It is also, frustratingly, still undeciphered. The Proto-Elamite script first appears at Susa on tablets dating from roughly 3100 BCE, essentially contemporary with the earliest Sumerian "proto-cuneiform" tablets from Uruk a few hundred miles to the west (Mesopotamia Ch 3, ~3200 BCE) and not much behind the earliest Egyptian hieroglyphs (~3200 BCE) or the earliest Indus proto-signs at sites like Harappa (Indus Valley Ch 3, ~3300–2800 BCE). The late fourth millennium BCE was the moment four different civilizations around the ancient world all started writing at roughly the same time, and Proto-Elamite is the one that is still a mystery. Nobody in the last 120 years of effort has managed to convincingly read it. We can tell from context that the Proto-Elamite tablets are accounting documents — they record quantities of commodities, they use repeated sign patterns that look like numerals and grain measurements — but the actual words the tablets say, the actual language they encode, remain a mystery. It is one of the great unsolved problems of ancient linguistics.

This is Elam's first pattern. It will not be the last. **Elamite civilization developed its own writing system, repeatedly, from scratch**, and two of those systems are still undeciphered today. The Proto-Elamite script is the first one. The Linear Elamite script of the next chapter is the second. Both of them represent independent acts of writing invention — scripts that were not Sumerian, not Akkadian, not a borrowed copy of somebody else's cuneiform, but homegrown answers to the same accounting and administrative problems the Sumerians were solving with cuneiform next door. That tells you something important about Elam that the rest of this book will keep returning to: the Elamites were never junior partners to Mesopotamia. They were culturally peers, developing in parallel, repeatedly hitting the same civilizational milestones at about the same time in their own distinctive way.

---

Proto-Elamite writing coincides with the rise of **Anshan** as a major highland center. The archaeology at Tall-i Malyan shows the city growing rapidly in the early third millennium BCE, reaching something like 500 acres of walled urban area by 2700 BCE. That is enormous. For comparison, Uruk, the famous early Sumerian city, covered about 600 acres at its peak (Mesopotamia Ch 3). Anshan was in the same size class as the biggest Mesopotamian cities, and it was a thousand miles away from any other comparable urban center, up in a mountain basin nobody who didn't belong there could easily reach.

We also start hearing names. The earliest Elamite political unit we can name is the **Dynasty of Awan**, a line of kings whose name comes from Sumerian king lists and later Mesopotamian historical references. Awan (AH-wahn) was probably a specific city — the location is still debated, but the best guess is somewhere in the Zagros foothills between Khuzestan and Fars — and the Dynasty of Awan ruled some portion of Elamite territory starting around 2600 BCE. The Sumerian King List (a much later Babylonian-era document purporting to list the legitimate kings of Mesopotamia from the creation onward) gives the Awan dynasty as rulers of Sumer itself at one point, which is almost certainly propaganda reflecting the fact that Elamite armies sometimes occupied Mesopotamian cities. The historical reality was messier: Awan was an Elamite kingdom that occasionally projected force into lower Mesopotamia, and the Sumerians noticed.

The Awan kings worshipped, among others, a god who was about to become the characteristic deity of Susa and eventually of Elam as a whole: **Inshushinak** (in-SHOO-shee-nak). The name Inshushinak probably means "Lord of Susa" in Elamite, and the god was the patron deity of the city — the one whose temple sat at the heart of the urban complex, whose festivals structured the ritual year, whose name the kings invoked when they swore oaths. Inshushinak was a judge, a protector, and an underworld figure whose most important annual role was presiding over the ritual performed at the death of a king, where Elamite texts suggest the god received the departing soul and weighed the king's life. The cult of Inshushinak at Susa is one of the oldest continuous religious traditions in the region — documented from around 2500 BCE, still alive at the time of the Assyrian destruction of Susa in 646 BCE, and traceable into the Achaemenid period as late as 500 BCE. More than 2,000 years of worship of a single urban god in a single city. Inshushinak is the Elamite answer to the question of what made Susa, Susa.

---

Trade is the other signature of early Elam, and trade is where the highland half of the country starts to really matter.

The Iranian plateau is rich in specific commodities the lowland Mesopotamian economies did not have. There is **tin**, the rarer metal you combine with copper to make bronze, the material that defines the entire Bronze Age; the main ancient source was the Badakhshan region in northern Afghanistan, and it reached Mesopotamia either via an overland caravan route through Elam or via a maritime route through the Harappan outpost of Shortughai (the Indus Valley colony in northern Afghanistan — Indus Valley Ch 7). There is **copper**, mined at places like Tepe Sialk (another site in central Iran, not in Elam proper but on the trade route). There is **lapis lazuli** (LAP-iss LAH-zoo-lee), the deep blue stone, prized across the ancient world for elite jewelry, found only in a few specific sources in Afghanistan and Central Asia, which had to be carried west through Iran to reach Mesopotamia and further — the same Shortughai maritime network and the overland Elamite caravan route were the two pathways. There is **carnelian**, the orange-red semi-precious stone, from sources in eastern Iran and the Indus valley, most famously worked into long-drilled etched beads in the Harappan carnelian workshops at Chanhu-daro and Dholavira (Indus Valley Ch 7) and then shipped west through the same twin pathways. There is **turquoise**, from sources in northeastern Iran. There are mountain hardwoods that the timber-poor Mesopotamian plain could not grow. There are horses and donkeys bred in the cooler highlands. There are obsidian flakes traded down from sources much further north.

All of these goods moved through Elamite territory, and much of the wealth of Susa and Anshan for most of their history came from taxing the caravans and operating the trading stations. By 2500 BCE, Elamite merchants are documented in Mesopotamian records as suppliers of metals and stones the Sumerian temples urgently needed — the same metals and stones that show up at this moment in Queen Puabi's headdress and the other Royal Tombs of Ur (Mesopotamia Ch 4), assembled from Badakhshani lapis, Harappan carnelian, and Anatolian/Iranian gold all routed through the trade network Elam sat on top of. By 2100 BCE, the trade connection is dense enough that the language of commerce along the route includes bilingual Sumerian-Elamite glossaries — the earliest surviving evidence of professional translators working between two major ancient languages. The Iranian highland trade routes running through Elam were the economic lifeline that let the cities of southern Iraq do the things they are famous for doing: building ziggurats (stepped mud-brick temple towers — the signature monumental architecture of Mesopotamian religion, which we'll meet in detail in Chapter 5 when Elam builds the biggest one of all at Chogha Zanbil), writing literature in cuneiform (the wedge-shaped writing system pressed into wet clay tablets that the Sumerians invented around 3200 BCE and that became the standard Near Eastern script for the next three thousand years), waging wars over canal access, supporting priests who spent their days on astronomy. The bronze that those wars were fought with came from tin carried by caravan through Elamite territory.

This means that even in the periods when Elam was politically rivals with Mesopotamia, the two regions could never fully disengage. Elam needed Mesopotamian finished goods, grain in lean years, prestige imports, and the Mesopotamian appetite for Elamite wealth to power its own elites. Mesopotamia needed the tin, copper, and stones that only came through Elamite traders. When Mesopotamian armies did destroy Susa, as will happen repeatedly in this book, they would then immediately have to negotiate with Elamite successor states to keep the metal flowing. When Elamite kings raided lower Mesopotamia, they were partly punishing rivals and partly collecting tolls in advance. The relationship was never a clean enemy relationship. It was a complicated, permanently entangled, mutually dependent, mutually resentful intimacy that lasted for more than two thousand years.

---

The Elamite language itself deserves a paragraph, because it is one of the oddities of the whole ancient Near East.

Elamite belongs to no known language family. It is not Semitic — that is, it is not in the family that includes Akkadian (the ancient Mesopotamian language of Sargon, Hammurabi, and the Assyrian kings — Mesopotamia Ch 5+), Hebrew, Arabic, or Aramaic (a Semitic language of the Syrian desert that would later become the administrative lingua franca of the Persian Empire and the everyday spoken language of much of the Near East, including the dialect Jesus spoke a thousand years later). It is not **Indo-European**, the huge language family that branches out across Eurasia to include Persian, Sanskrit and the languages of India, Greek, Latin, the Germanic languages including English, and the Celtic and Slavic branches — an Indo-European speaker today in London is a distant linguistic cousin of an Indo-European speaker in Delhi, and both are descended from a proto-language that probably started somewhere in the Pontic-Caspian steppes north of the Black Sea around 4000 BCE. Elamite is not related to any of that. It is also not **Hurrian** (HOOR-ee-an), the language of the later Mitanni kingdom of northern Syria (which we'll meet in Ch 5). It is not **Kassite**, the language of the dynasty that would rule Babylon in the Middle Bronze Age (which we'll meet in Ch 6). And it is not Sumerian, which is itself in no known family. Strikingly, three of the four major non-Semitic ancient Near Eastern languages — Sumerian, Elamite, and Kassite — are all language isolates with no demonstrated living relatives, a cluster of linguistic loners that modern linguists still cannot explain. Elamite is what linguists call a **language isolate**, a language with no demonstrated living or dead relatives, standing alone, probably the last surviving member of a language family that had already lost its other members before writing was invented. The closest proposal for a relative is a theory called "Elamo-Dravidian," which would link Elamite to the Dravidian language family of southern India (modern Tamil, Malayalam, Kannada). The theory is based on thin structural parallels and has never been established. It may be true. It may not. The professional consensus is that Elamite stands alone.

What we can say is that Elamite was a real, full-featured, agglutinative language — meaning that grammatical information is conveyed by stacking suffixes on nouns and verbs, the way Turkish and Finnish do today — and that it was the language of ordinary conversation across most of southwestern Iran for thousands of years. When the Persian empire wrote its administrative records in Elamite in the fifth century BCE, it was using a language that had already been spoken in that region for at least 2,600 years before Cyrus the Great was born. How much longer before that, we don't know, because the script to write it only got invented around 3100 BCE.

Two Elams. One old lowland city at Susa. One big highland city at Anshan. One trade-based economy moving Iranian metals west to Sumer. One undeciphered script. One god named Inshushinak whose cult lasted 2,000 years. One language with no relatives.

By 2500 BCE, the kingdom is ready to meet its neighbor. And its neighbor is in the middle of inventing the world's first empire.

# Chapter 2 — Naram-Sin's Stone

The Akkadian Empire is one of the great turning points in world history, and it is mostly told from the Akkadian side — from the side of the conquerors. This chapter tells the other side. The Elamite side. The side of the people who were on the receiving end of Sargon and Naram-Sin's new idea of imperial rule, who signed the first surviving political treaty we have in any language, who developed their own script in response, and whose reaction to the Akkadians is the thing that finally makes Elam visible in written history as a specific kingdom with specific kings.

Let me set the Akkadian moment briefly, for readers who have not read the Mesopotamia book alongside this one. Around 2334 BCE, a man we now call **Sargon of Akkad** seized the throne of the Sumerian city of Kish, declared himself king, and over the next five and a half decades of his reign conquered virtually the entire alluvial plain of Sumer and pushed his armies outward in every direction. Mesopotamia Chapter 5 tells this story in detail. The short version is that Sargon created what most historians consider the first true empire in human history — a multi-ethnic, multi-city, centrally administered territory where conquered local rulers were replaced with appointed governors loyal to the Akkadian king, and where a new written language (Akkadian, the Semitic tongue of the conquerors, written in cuneiform) became the administrative standard alongside the older Sumerian. It was a revolution in how political power worked at scale, and it created the basic template that every later Mesopotamian empire — Babylonian, Assyrian, Neo-Babylonian — would inherit.

From the Elamite side, the first question was: would the new imperial machinery reach across the Zagros?

The first answer was yes.

---

Sargon himself campaigned east into Elamite territory. His royal inscriptions (cut into stone monuments intended to record his conquests for posterity, and preserved in Babylonian copies made centuries later) boast of capturing Awan, sacking Susa, and taking Elamite captives back to Akkad as slaves. The details are thin — the inscriptions are short and the archaeology has only been excavated in patches — but the basic pattern is not in doubt. Sargon's campaigns reached Susa, probably around 2300 BCE, and for at least a short period the Akkadian Empire considered Elam a subject province and collected tribute from it. The specific Elamite kings who ruled during Sargon's reign are not named in surviving Elamite sources — the only contemporary royal names we have for Elam at this date come from Mesopotamian records, which used Akkadian transcriptions of Elamite names and which were written by the winners.

Then, at some point between Sargon and his grandson, somebody on one side or the other decided to write a treaty.

---

The **Elamite-Akkadian Treaty**, preserved on a clay tablet now in the Louvre and dated to roughly 2250 BCE, is one of the most remarkable documents in the entire ancient Near Eastern archive. It is a treaty text. It is bilingual. It has specific named parties on each side. It invokes specific gods as witnesses. It is the oldest surviving text of a full political treaty in any language from any civilization, and the best-known later parallel — the famous Egypt–Hittite treaty between Ramesses II and the Hittite king Hattusili III, signed around 1259 BCE — is nearly a thousand years younger.

The treaty binds the Akkadian king (probably **Naram-Sin**, Sargon's grandson — we'll get to him in a moment) and an Elamite king named Khita. It establishes a defensive alliance — the two parties promise not to attack each other and to come to each other's aid if a third party invades — and it invokes as witnesses more than thirty gods, including both the Akkadian pantheon (Enlil, Shamash, Ishtar) and the Elamite pantheon (Inshushinak, Humban, a goddess called Pinikir who we will meet again, and a group of Elamite deities whose names are otherwise unattested). It is written in Akkadian, not Elamite — the Akkadians were the more prestigious diplomatic language at that moment, and Elamite royal scribes evidently knew how to write in it — and the treaty text specifies that the Elamite king swears his oath "in the name of" both the Akkadian and the Elamite gods, binding himself on both sets of divine authority.

Two things are worth noticing about this document.

The first is that it treats Elam as a sovereign equal of Akkad, not as a subject province. The treaty is not a tribute imposition; it is a negotiated bilateral agreement between two kings who consider themselves peers. This is strikingly different from the tone of Sargon's earlier conquest inscriptions, which describe Elam as smashed and subjugated. In just one generation, the relationship had evolved from "Sargon sacks Susa" to "Naram-Sin and the king of Elam sign a mutual defense pact as equals." Either the Elamites had reasserted themselves quickly after Sargon's death, or Sargon's original claims had been exaggerated, or — most likely — both.

The second is that the treaty's invocation of the Elamite pantheon is our earliest real list of Elamite gods. We already knew about Inshushinak. Now we also have Humban (King of the Elamite gods, whom we'll meet properly in Chapter 3 along with the rest of the pantheon), the goddess Pinikir (an early sky goddess associated with Venus and later partly assimilated to the Mesopotamian Ishtar), and a handful of more obscure deities whose mythology is lost. The treaty is not just a political document. It is a window into Elamite religion, preserved because the Elamite king swore on his own gods to validate the agreement.

That a bilateral treaty exists at all, in 2250 BCE, between two kingdoms writing in a language borrowed from one of them and invoking the gods of both, is one of the most sophisticated political moves of the entire third millennium BCE. Elam was playing on the same diplomatic field as Akkad, and playing well.

---

Then Naram-Sin broke the treaty.

**Naram-Sin** (pronounced nah-rahm-SEEN, sometimes "Naram-Suen" in older transliterations) was Sargon's grandson and the most famous of the Akkadian kings after Sargon himself. His reign (roughly 2254–2218 BCE) was the high point of Akkadian imperial power. Naram-Sin expanded the empire aggressively, campaigned in every direction, and is the first ruler in Mesopotamian history whose name is written with the divine determinative — the cuneiform sign prefix used to mark the names of gods. Naram-Sin had himself deified while still alive, claiming to be a god rather than a mere king. It was an unprecedented theological move, and it is the single most famous propaganda gesture of his reign. (The famous Victory Stele of Naram-Sin, now in the Louvre, shows him wearing the horned crown of a god and standing above his defeated enemies — the first surviving artistic depiction of a living monarch as divine, and a source we will be returning to when it turns up in an unexpected place in Chapter 6.)

Somewhere during this reign, Naram-Sin's armies invaded Elam again. The new campaign broke the treaty Naram-Sin himself had signed — or broke one his father Manishtushu had signed, depending on whose chronology is right — and resulted in the sack of Susa a second time. Naram-Sin's royal inscriptions boast of capturing Elam and bringing back cedars, gold, and tribute. This invasion also produced one of the most spectacular surviving Akkadian artworks: the **Stele of Naram-Sin**, that same Victory Stele we just mentioned, which was carved in commemoration of the conquest of a people called the "Lullubi" (an obscure mountain group in the northern Zagros, but close enough to Elam to be part of the same campaign cycle). The stele itself is about six feet tall, cut from pink sandstone, carved with a scene showing Naram-Sin climbing a mountain at the head of his army while his enemies collapse and die. It is a propaganda masterpiece, and the kind of object that an Akkadian king would erect in his capital to celebrate an imperial victory.

Where did the stele end up?

We will get there in Chapter 6. But remember this: **the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin** was carved in the Akkadian capital around 2250 BCE, stood there for more than a thousand years as a trophy of Akkadian military supremacy, and is today in the Louvre in Paris. The route from the Akkadian capital to the Louvre runs through Elam. Through Susa. Through a specific act of revenge that an Elamite king would commit in 1158 BCE, more than a thousand years after Naram-Sin's death, which would bring this stele — and Hammurabi's famous law code, and half a dozen other Mesopotamian royal monuments — to Susa to be stored as war trophies. When European archaeologists started digging at Susa in the nineteenth century, they found those trophies still where the Elamite king had put them, and they packed them onto ships and sent them to Paris.

The Stele of Naram-Sin will travel a long way in this book. But in Chapter 2, at the moment of its making, it is Naram-Sin's proclamation of Akkadian supremacy over a defeated Elam.

---

The Elamite response to Naram-Sin was one of the most characteristic moves in their whole history: they invented their own writing system.

We have already met the **Proto-Elamite script** in Chapter 1 — the undeciphered tablet accountancy system from around 3100 BCE. By the time of Naram-Sin, Proto-Elamite had fallen out of use. Susa and Anshan were using Mesopotamian cuneiform for accounting purposes, borrowed from their Sumerian and Akkadian trading partners. But a new system was about to appear.

Around 2200 BCE, during or immediately after the Akkadian period, someone at Susa developed a new Elamite script known today as **Linear Elamite**. It is a much more clearly "writing" system than Proto-Elamite was. Linear Elamite has a distinct set of characters (about 80 signs, including both phonograms — signs representing sounds — and logograms — signs representing whole words), a clear left-to-right reading order, and a grammatical structure that looks like it encodes actual Elamite language rather than just commodity tallies. It appears on a small number of inscriptions, almost all of them short royal dedication texts carved into stone statues or seals, and it has been one of the great unsolved problems of ancient linguistics for over a century.

Linear Elamite was almost entirely undeciphered until 2020, when a research team led by François Desset in France used a combination of proper-name analysis and cross-comparison with bilingual Akkadian-Linear Elamite inscriptions to produce a probable partial decipherment. The Desset proposal is still being evaluated by other scholars, and it is not yet settled consensus, but the partial decipherment suggests that Linear Elamite was indeed encoding the Elamite language phonetically, with specific signs representing specific syllables. If the decipherment holds, Linear Elamite is one of the most significant "new old texts" to be unlocked in the twenty-first century.

The man most closely associated with Linear Elamite is one of the most interesting figures in all of early Elamite history: **Puzur-Inshushinak** (POO-zer in-SHOO-shee-nak, a name that means "Refuge is Inshushinak"), the last great king of the Awan dynasty. Puzur-Inshushinak ruled around 2100 BCE, several generations after Naram-Sin's death and during the collapse of the Akkadian Empire. He saw his opportunity and took it — campaigning westward into a fragmented Mesopotamia, proclaiming himself "king of the four quarters of the world" (a title borrowed from Naram-Sin's own propaganda), and for a brief moment making Elam an imperial rival to the dying Akkadian state. The surviving royal statues of Puzur-Inshushinak carry bilingual inscriptions in both Akkadian (the diplomatic prestige language) and Linear Elamite (the brand-new Elamite-specific script), and they are the single richest surviving corpus of Linear Elamite text.

Puzur-Inshushinak's brief imperial moment did not last. By the time of his death or shortly after, the Akkadian Empire was done — destroyed by drought, internal revolts, and the invasions of the **Gutians** (a mountain people from the Zagros whose own origins are obscure, who briefly occupied Sumer around 2150 BCE and whom the Sumerians hated enough that the later Mesopotamian literary tradition made them into a byword for uncivilized barbarism). In the power vacuum, several new dynasties emerged in Mesopotamia — including the famous Third Dynasty of Ur, which we will meet in a moment — and in Elam, the Awan dynasty was replaced by a new royal line called Shimashki.

Elam had survived the first Mesopotamian empire. It had signed the first surviving treaty in world history. It had invented a new script of its own in response. And it had learned something permanent about the shape of the two-kingdom relationship: when Mesopotamia was strong, Elam signed treaties and paid tribute. When Mesopotamia was weak, Elam invaded. This pattern was going to repeat, in both directions, for the next two thousand years.

In the next chapter, Elam invades. Hard.

---

# Chapter 3 — Ur Falls

Around 2004 BCE, give or take a year, an Elamite army walked into the city of Ur and burned it to the ground.

This matters a great deal. Ur was at that moment the capital of the most sophisticated state Mesopotamia had yet produced — the **Third Dynasty of Ur** (conventionally abbreviated "Ur III" by modern scholars, pronounced "Ur the Third"), a kingdom that had emerged after the collapse of the Akkadian Empire to reunify Sumer under native Sumerian rule. Ur III at its peak ruled the entire alluvial plain of southern Mesopotamia from roughly 2112 to 2004 BCE, with a bureaucracy so elaborate it produced tens of thousands of surviving clay tablets recording the daily movements of sheep, barley, oil, and labor across the kingdom. Ur III is the period when Sumerian civilization reaches one of its cultural high points — the great ziggurat of Ur is built under Ur III's founding king Ur-Nammu, the famous Code of Ur-Nammu (the oldest surviving written legal code, three centuries older than Hammurabi's) is promulgated, and Sumerian as a literary and administrative language reaches something close to its classic form. It is also, critically, the last moment in history when Sumerian was a major living language. After Ur III falls, Sumerian becomes a dead scholarly language used only by priests and scribes, the way Latin became in medieval Europe. The whole spoken Sumerian world ended with the fall of Ur in 2004 BCE.

And the people who ended it were Elamites.

---

The Ur III kingdom had been battling Elam on and off for its entire existence. The founder, **Ur-Nammu** (ur-NAH-moo, ruled 2112–2094 BCE), waged war against Elamite territory and was killed in battle — not against Elam directly but against another enemy. His son, **Shulgi** (SHOOL-gee, ruled 2094–2047 BCE, one of the great administrative geniuses of Sumerian history), spent substantial effort on the eastern frontier. Shulgi built defensive walls at a site called **Der** (in modern eastern Iraq, near the Iranian border) to block the passes into Iran, and his daughters were married off to Elamite princes as part of a sustained diplomatic effort to keep Elam quiet. For a while, it worked. During Shulgi's long reign and his son's, Elam was contained and partly integrated — Elamite soldiers even served in Ur III's army as specialized archer units, and the Elamite kings of the **Shimashki** dynasty (the royal house that had replaced Awan after Puzur-Inshushinak's reign) sent tribute and princesses to the Sumerian court.

The Shimashki dynasty was a highland-centered royal line — the name probably comes from a region in the southern Zagros mountains, though the exact location is still debated. They ruled Elam from roughly 2100 BCE until 1900 BCE, and they present the first clear example of the pattern we will see repeatedly throughout Elamite history: **fraternal and avuncular succession**. The Shimashki kings did not simply pass power from father to son. Instead, succession moved through a complex set of ranked offices — "grand regent," "viceroy of Elam," "viceroy of Susa" — with brothers, half-brothers, uncles, and nephews rotating through the offices in a pattern that kept the royal family tightly bound together as a collective. We will see this pattern formalized two centuries later in the Sukkalmah period (Chapter 4), where it becomes the single most distinctive feature of Elamite government. But it starts here, under the Shimashki, as an informal way of keeping multiple lines of the royal family invested in the kingdom's survival.

It is worth sitting with how unusual this is. Most ancient kingdoms used some version of primogeniture or father-to-son succession — Egypt, Sumer, Babylon, the Hittites, most of Mesopotamia, the later dynasties of China, India, Persia after Cyrus. The Elamite royal system is an outlier, but not a complete one. The closest structural parallel in the ancient world is actually **Shang dynasty China** (Ancient China Ch 3, roughly 1600–1046 BCE), where the early Shang oracle bones record repeated brother-to-brother throne transfers rather than father-to-son inheritance. The Shang and Elamite systems almost certainly developed independently — they were on opposite sides of Asia, with no documented contact — but both arrived at the same broad solution to the problem of what to do when a king dies and his son is a child. Elsewhere, brothers and uncles rotated through top offices while a "grand king" sat at the top, and when a king died, his younger brother or his uncle's son might succeed him rather than his own son. The advantages of the system were that it kept the royal clan unified and reduced the frequency of succession crises — no weak boy-kings, since there was always an adult male with experience ready to step up. The disadvantage was that it prevented any single king from building a dynastic faction of his own, and it meant Elamite rulers were almost never able to pursue long-term personal projects the way a Ramesses II or a Shulgi could. Elamite kingship was collective in a way that set it apart from the neighbors.

---

By the end of the third millennium, the Shimashki kings were done pretending to be tributary allies of Ur III.

The underlying cause of the break was probably the slow disintegration of Ur III itself. The kingdom was running into trouble by around 2020 BCE — crops failing, population movements bringing new Semitic-speaking groups (the Amorites, Mesopotamia Ch 6) into the plain from the western desert, and the Ur III administrative system becoming brittle under the strain. The last Ur III king, **Ibbi-Sin** (ruled 2028–2004 BCE), inherited a kingdom that was already unraveling, and his reign is recorded in the Ur III economic tablets as a period of rising grain prices, famine in the provinces, and lost cities. Ibbi-Sin tried to hold the state together by concentrating power at Ur and firing distant governors, but the moves alienated his own bureaucracy and the provincial capitals started defecting.

The Elamite king **Kindattu** (of the Shimashki dynasty) saw the opportunity. In 2004 BCE, he led an Elamite army west into Sumer, combined with allied troops from Susa and the highland regions, and attacked Ur directly. Ibbi-Sin's defense collapsed. The city fell, was looted, was burned, and Ibbi-Sin himself was captured and carried off to Anshan in chains. He died there in exile. We do not know what happened to him afterward, or where his body ended up.

The fall of Ur is one of the best-documented disasters in early Mesopotamian history, because within a few decades Sumerian priests wrote a long literary poem called the **Lament for Ur** (Mesopotamia Ch 6) commemorating it. The Lament for Ur is the oldest surviving example of a specific literary genre called the "city lament" — a ritual poem mourning the destruction of a Mesopotamian city and blaming the gods for abandoning it to its enemies. The Lament for Ur specifically blames the Elamites and another group called the Sua people (probably a Zagros mountain tribe allied with the Elamites) for the destruction, and the text is one of the great grief documents of the ancient Near East: the city is described as empty, the temples as desecrated, the people as slaughtered or enslaved, the streets as filled with unburied corpses, and the goddess Ningal (the tutelary deity of Ur) as weeping over the ruins of her house. It is a deeply personal, deeply emotional piece of literature, and it ensures that the Elamite sack of Ur was remembered in Sumerian and Akkadian tradition as one of the defining disasters of the early second millennium BCE.

From the Elamite side, we have almost nothing. No Elamite chronicle survives from the Shimashki period. We do not know what Kindattu thought he was doing beyond the basic political move (strike a weakening rival, collect loot, eliminate a long-standing enemy), and we do not know how the Elamite court commemorated the victory. The Elamite sack of Ur is known to us almost entirely through the eyes and grief of the losers.

---

But it mattered. And it mattered in a particular way that would shape the next thousand years.

After Ur fell, Elam occupied Ur and the surrounding region for a short period — probably only a few years, certainly less than a generation — and then pulled back to Susa. The Amorites, the Semitic-speaking newcomers from the western desert, flooded into the vacated Sumerian plain and established their own series of small kingdoms, one of which would eventually become Babylon. Sumer as a political entity was over. The cities remained, but they were now Amorite cities, Akkadian-speaking, slowly becoming Babylonian. The Elamites had not intended to wipe Sumer off the map — they had intended to loot a rival and go home — but the accidental consequence of the raid was the end of Sumerian political history.

Inside Elam, the sack of Ur was the prestige event that cemented the Shimashki dynasty's claim to greatness. The Elamite treasury was enriched. The temple of Inshushinak at Susa received votive offerings marked as "spoils of Ur." And the practice of carrying Mesopotamian religious objects back to Susa as war trophies — taking the statues of the gods of the conquered city and installing them in Elamite temples — began as a policy here. The Elamites also took, from the Sumerian city of Uruk during these same wars, the cult statue of the goddess **Nanaya** (nah-NAH-ya — an early Mesopotamian goddess of love and war, closely associated with Inanna/Ishtar), which would be installed in a Susa temple and would stay there for more than 1,600 years, long enough that an Assyrian king in the seventh century BCE would eventually fight a war to get it back (Chapter 7). This practice of trophy-hostaging foreign gods will matter enormously in Chapter 6, when Shutruk-Nahhunte does the same thing to Babylon on a much larger scale, and again in Chapter 7 when Ashurbanipal reverses the whole accumulated game.

The Elamites also came home with something else: a deeper familiarity with Mesopotamian administrative culture, and in particular with cuneiform writing.

---

Before the Ur III wars, Elamite scribes at Susa had been using a mix of local traditions (Linear Elamite) and imported Mesopotamian cuneiform for specific purposes. After the sack of Ur, the Elamite court made a decision: from this point forward, royal inscriptions, temple accounting, and official correspondence would use **cuneiform** as the standard script, adapted to write the Elamite language phonetically. The adaptation is awkward — cuneiform is a script designed for Sumerian and Akkadian, two languages with very different sound systems from Elamite, and it never fits Elamite comfortably the way it fit Akkadian — but it is functional, and within a generation Elamite cuneiform becomes the dominant writing system in the kingdom. Linear Elamite falls out of use entirely by around 1950 BCE and is not revived.

The decision to adopt Mesopotamian cuneiform for the Elamite language is one of the most consequential cultural moves in Elamite history. It means the Elamite royal scribes were now trained in the same broad scribal tradition as their Mesopotamian counterparts — using the same basic clay tablet medium, the same wedge-shaped signs, the same literary references. Elamite priests and diplomats could read Akkadian texts (which were everywhere in the Near East by 1800 BCE) without needing a separate linguistic training. Elamite kings could send letters in Akkadian to other Near Eastern courts and receive replies. The price of adoption was that Elam became more culturally porous to Mesopotamian influence. The benefit was that Elam plugged into the broader cuneiform world of diplomacy, literature, and bureaucracy that was about to become the standard of the Near East for the next 1,500 years.

From this point forward, most of what we know about Elamite history comes from Elamite-language cuneiform inscriptions alongside the Mesopotamian sources. We can finally read what Elamite kings said about themselves, in their own words — or at least, in their own language written in somebody else's script.

---

One thing that cuneiform Elamite lets us see clearly is the **Elamite pantheon**.

We have met Inshushinak already — the city-god of Susa, the judge of the dead. He is not going anywhere. But around the same time the Shimashki dynasty was adopting cuneiform, the surviving written sources start to fill in the rest of the Elamite divine family, and the Elamite pantheon turns out to be large, distinctive, and organized around a divine family structure that does not look like the Mesopotamian model it sits next to.

**Humban** (HOOM-bahn) was the king of the Elamite gods. His name appears in royal inscriptions as the first deity invoked before any others, he is addressed as "the king" and "the great one," and his sanctuary at the city of Liyan (on the Persian Gulf coast, probably at modern Bushehr) was one of the major pilgrimage destinations in Elamite territory. Humban's role is roughly equivalent to the Mesopotamian Enlil — the head-of-pantheon sky god who legitimizes human kingship. His name became a theophoric element (a name-component invoking a god) in royal names for more than 1,500 years: Humban-Numena, Humban-Haltash, Humban-menanu, Humban-Nikash — all names in the form "Humban-does-X," meaning something like "Humban has given" or "Humban has established." The last Elamite king ever to bear a Humban-name appears in Achaemenid-era texts from the fifth century BCE, long after Elam itself was politically absorbed. The cult of Humban survived the death of the kingdom that worshipped him.

**Kiririsha** (kee-ree-REE-shah, meaning "Great Lady") was the mother goddess of the Elamite pantheon and the consort of Humban. Her cult center was also at Liyan, which suggests that Humban and Kiririsha were a paired divine couple whose worship came from the southern highland and Persian Gulf region rather than from Susa itself. Kiririsha's role in royal inscriptions is to legitimize queens, to protect childbirth, and to receive offerings alongside her husband in the standard divine triad formulas that opened official Elamite texts. Like most ancient mother goddesses, her iconography often blurred with fertility, agriculture, and the general feminine principle, but her specific Elamite role — co-ruler of the divine family alongside Humban — is distinctive.

**Nahhunte** (NAH-hoon-tay, meaning "creator of the day") was the sun god — the Elamite equivalent of Mesopotamian Shamash (Mesopotamia Ch 4). Nahhunte was the divine judge, presiding over oaths and legal disputes, and his name became the second great theophoric element in royal names: Shutruk-Nahhunte, Kutir-Nahhunte, Hallutash-Inshushinak (whose name combines Inshushinak with a different element). The Elamite sun god and the Mesopotamian sun god probably had some cultural cross-pollination — both are judges, both are associated with the rising sun and with royal legitimacy — but they had distinct names, distinct myths, and distinct priesthoods. The Elamite kings who took Nahhunte into their royal names were declaring themselves servants of the judge, protectors of oaths and law.

**Inshushinak**, we already know. Lord of Susa, judge of the dead.

**Pinikir** (PIN-ee-kir) is a more puzzling figure — the great sky goddess who appears in the Elamite-Akkadian treaty of Chapter 2 and whose worship is documented from as early as 2100 BCE. Pinikir was associated with the planet Venus and with the fertility functions that Mesopotamian Inanna/Ishtar also covered, and at some point during the second millennium BCE Pinikir's cult was partly absorbed into or merged with the Mesopotamian Ishtar cult, so that later Elamite texts use the two names interchangeably in some contexts. Pinikir is probably the older layer — a pre-Mesopotamian Elamite goddess whose identity got complicated by the cultural traffic between Susa and Sumer.

That is the core of the Elamite divine family: Humban at the top, Kiririsha his consort, Nahhunte the sun-judge, Inshushinak the patron of Susa, Pinikir the sky-goddess. Dozens of lesser deities fill out the pantheon, many of them local city-gods whose worship was confined to specific towns. The whole system is organized around a family structure (royal father, royal mother, children, specialized functional gods) that has loose parallels to Mesopotamian pantheons but is clearly its own distinct thing. The gods have Elamite names. Their stories, to the extent that we can reconstruct them from scraps of ritual text, are Elamite stories. Whatever the Elamites were, they were not a cultural satellite of Mesopotamia. They had their own theology, and it was old.

---

By 1900 BCE or so, the Shimashki dynasty was gone, replaced by a new royal line whose distinctive government we are about to meet. The Elamite sack of Ur was a hundred years in the past. Susa had been rebuilt bigger. The cuneiform adoption was complete. The pantheon was crystallizing. The long imperial struggle with Mesopotamia had entered its classical middle period.

And a family called the Epartids was about to invent a political system unlike anything else in the ancient world.

---

# Chapter 4 — Grand Regents

Around 1850 BCE, a man named **Eparti I** (eh-PAHR-tee) founded the royal dynasty that would rule Elam for the next 350 years. His title, and the title of all his successors, was one of the strangest in all of ancient Near Eastern diplomacy: **sukkalmah** (SOOK-kahl-mah), meaning "grand regent" or literally "great vizier."

The word "regent" is usually a red flag in historical writing. A regent is, in most political systems, a placeholder — someone who holds power on behalf of a real king who is too young or too distant to rule personally. The sukkalmah was different. The Elamite sukkalmah *was* the real king. He ruled in his own name, made treaties in his own name, waged wars in his own name, and commissioned royal inscriptions in his own name. The title sukkalmah did not mean he was a stand-in for anyone. It meant that the Elamite royal ideology refused to use the ordinary Mesopotamian vocabulary for kingship (*lugal*, *sharrum*) and instead used a political vocabulary inherited from the Mesopotamian bureaucratic hierarchy, where "sukkal" was a specific high office meaning something like "vizier" or "minister." The Elamite move was to take the idea of a minister — a trusted servant of the real king — and elevate it to the highest royal office, so that the Elamite ruler was ceremonially a "servant" of the gods rather than a "king."

This is a genuinely interesting political-theology move, and it is worth spending a paragraph on. In most ancient Near Eastern monarchies, the king claimed to be the direct earthly representative of the chief god (or, as with Naram-Sin, actually a god himself). The king was above all mortals in rank, and below only the heavenly powers. The Elamite sukkalmah presented himself as a servant — the chief servant — of the divine family headed by Humban, Kiririsha, and Inshushinak. His power was delegated downward from the gods, not assumed directly. This created a subtle but important rhetorical gap between the Elamite monarch and the Mesopotamian one: an Elamite sukkalmah could legitimately be constrained by the priesthood, by tradition, and by the expectations of the divine patrons in ways that a Mesopotamian *lugal* (who was closer to a god-king) could not. The Elamite monarchy was, in its self-presentation, more humble than the Mesopotamian alternative, and the monarchy's authority flowed from the gods *through* the king to the people, with room for priests and family members to mediate at every step.

Along with the humble title came the most distinctive feature of Elamite government in the Sukkalmah period: the **three-office ruling structure**.

The Sukkalmah dynasty ran Elam through three formal offices, ranked vertically:

1. **Sukkalmah** — the grand regent, at the top. Ruled the whole kingdom. Sat at Susa or sometimes at Anshan.
2. **Sukkal of Elam and Simashki** — the second-ranking official, responsible for the mountain regions, usually a brother or uncle of the grand regent.
3. **Sukkal of Susa** — the third-ranking official, responsible for the lowland city of Susa itself and the surrounding Khuzestan plain, usually a nephew or younger brother.

The three offices were held simultaneously by three different members of the same royal family. When the sukkalmah died, the sukkal of Elam and Simashki was promoted to sukkalmah, the sukkal of Susa was promoted to sukkal of Elam and Simashki, and the next available adult male (usually a younger brother or a nephew) was installed in the vacant sukkal of Susa office. Succession was horizontal before it was vertical. A single generation of brothers would rotate through the three offices — each one holding each office in sequence — before the next generation took over.

The practical effect of this system was that Elamite royal authority was collective rather than individual. No single sukkalmah could rule as a personal autocrat, because he had two close relatives also holding formal authority and also members of the royal family with legitimate succession claims. Major policy decisions required consensus among the three offices. Regional administration in Elam and Susa was handled by the sukkals of each region, who operated with substantial local autonomy. The sukkalmah at the top was a coordinator of the royal family rather than an all-powerful king.

This is the opposite of how Mesopotamian kingship worked. It is also, as far as I can tell, unlike any other political system in the ancient Near East. Egypt had one pharaoh at a time. The Hittites (in Anatolia, around 1600 BCE onward, the first great Indo-European-speaking power in the ancient Near East) had one Great King, though with considerable powers delegated to subordinate rulers of the empire's subject kingdoms. The Assyrians had one *sharru*, always. Babylonia had one king. The Persians, later, would have one king of kings. Elam had three sukkals at once, rotating, none of them claiming ultimate authority individually.

Why it worked is a good question. Part of the answer is that the collective structure prevented the dynastic civil wars that destroyed many ancient monarchies — there was always an adult male ready to step up, and there was no single successor to be assassinated. Part of the answer is that the structure gave the two major regions of the kingdom (the highland Elam/Simashki and the lowland Susa) their own direct royal representation at the top, which kept both halves of the two-Elam country politically engaged. Part of the answer is just that it fit Elamite royal culture, which seems to have valued kin solidarity over individual glory more strongly than most of its neighbors did.

The practical result was that Sukkalmah Elam was, for most of the three hundred years of its duration, one of the most politically stable kingdoms in the Near East. While Mesopotamia was going through a dozen dynastic crises, Amorite invasions, and the rise and fall of Isin, Larsa, and early Babylon (Mesopotamia Chapters 6–7), Elam under the Sukkalmah just kept running the way it had always run. The same royal family, the same three-office structure, the same pantheon, the same scribal traditions, the same economy based on highland-lowland trade. Elam in the Sukkalmah period is a quiet kingdom, and the quiet is the result of the government working.

---

The Sukkalmah period is also the moment when Elam starts showing up as a peer power in the diplomatic correspondence of the Mesopotamian great kings.

The richest surviving archive of early second-millennium BCE Near Eastern diplomacy is the **Mari letters** — a hoard of roughly 25,000 clay tablets excavated from the royal palace of the city of **Mari** in modern Syria, spanning roughly 1810–1760 BCE. Mari was a middle-rank kingdom on the middle Euphrates, and its last king, **Zimri-Lim**, kept careful archives of the correspondence between his court and the other Near Eastern powers. When Hammurabi of Babylon conquered Mari in 1759 BCE, the palace was burned and the tablets were baked hard by the fire, accidentally preserving them for three thousand seven hundred years until French archaeologists excavated them in the 1930s. The Mari letters are the best single window into how the Near Eastern great powers of the early second millennium BCE actually talked to each other.

In the Mari letters, Elam — that is, the Sukkalmah Elam of the early eighteenth century BCE — appears as one of the top-ranked international powers. Zimri-Lim of Mari writes to the Elamite sukkalmah addressing him in formal diplomatic language, using the respect-ranked kinship terms that Near Eastern diplomats used to signal relative power. In these terms, a king whom you called "father" was your superior (not in actual kinship but in the diplomatic ranking), a king whom you called "brother" was your equal, and a king whom you called "son" was your inferior. Zimri-Lim addressed the Elamite sukkalmah as "father," and the correspondence from Babylon during the same period shows a similar deference — Hammurabi's own officials writing to the Elamite court in the register of diplomatic subordination. Both Mari and early Hammurabi-era Babylon considered the Elamite ruler their diplomatic *superior*, not merely an equal but an elder statesman at the top of the regional hierarchy. (The exact wording Hammurabi personally used in direct correspondence with the sukkalmah is harder to pin down from the surviving tablets — most of what we have is Mari's archive, not Babylon's — but the overall power relationship is unmistakable: Hammurabi's court bowed, in formal diplomatic terms, to Susa.)

This is astonishing, and it goes against every later historical tradition about this period. The later Babylonian and Assyrian literature always presents Elam as a lesser power, a frontier kingdom, an occasional nuisance that Mesopotamian armies dealt with when necessary. The Mari letters, written at the time rather than after the fact, show something very different. In 1770 BCE, the Elamite sukkalmah was *the* most senior Near Eastern monarch, acknowledged as such by Hammurabi himself before Hammurabi became the Hammurabi we remember. The Sukkalmah dynasty was at that moment the diplomatic center of the ancient Near East, and everyone from the Zagros to the Mediterranean coast was looking to Susa for leadership.

The specific Elamite sukkalmah whose correspondence dominates the Mari letters is a man named **Siwe-palar-hupak** (SEE-way pah-lar HOO-pak — the name is an Elamite sentence that probably means "Servant of Hupak has extended power," but the exact etymology is debated). Siwe-palar-hupak ruled as sukkalmah from roughly 1778 to 1745 BCE, and in that time he ran an aggressive Elamite foreign policy: he campaigned into Mesopotamia repeatedly, pressured the smaller kingdoms of the middle Euphrates, arranged political marriages to strengthen his influence, and at one point came very close to unifying the entire Near East under Elamite hegemony. His coalition partners included Mari's Zimri-Lim, several smaller Assyrian and Syrian kingdoms, and for a while even Babylon — until Hammurabi switched sides.

Hammurabi's switch is one of the decisive moments of early second-millennium BCE history, and it is told in exquisite detail in Mesopotamia Chapter 7 from the Babylonian side. The short version: Hammurabi had been an Elamite client for the first fifteen years of his reign, acknowledging Siwe-palar-hupak as his diplomatic "father." Around 1764 BCE, Hammurabi decided to break free, switched his alliances, and began waging the wars that would eventually conquer Mari, Elam, Assur, and most of Mesopotamia — the wars that created the Old Babylonian Empire we remember him for. In the process, Hammurabi personally defeated Siwe-palar-hupak in battle, sacked one of the Elamite frontier fortresses, and forced the Sukkalmah dynasty to pull back behind Susa. The Elamite supremacy was over. A new Babylonian supremacy replaced it.

But — and this is important — Hammurabi did not destroy Elam. He did not sack Susa. He did not break the Sukkalmah government. He pushed Elam out of Mesopotamia and back to its two-kingdom highlands-and-lowlands base, and then he accepted Elam as a subordinate but still-sovereign neighbor on his eastern frontier. The Sukkalmah dynasty continued to rule Elam for another two hundred years after Siwe-palar-hupak's death. Hammurabi's concern had been consolidating Babylon's position in Mesopotamia, not eliminating Elam as a civilization. The two kingdoms reverted to the old mutually-suspicious mutual-dependency relationship, with Elam slightly diminished and Babylon slightly enlarged but both still recognizable as peer Near Eastern great powers.

---

One more thing worth noting about Sukkalmah Elam before we move on: it is the first kingdom in Elamite history from which we have substantial quantities of ordinary people's documents.

Up until the Sukkalmah period, most of what we know about Elam comes from royal inscriptions — the kind of boastful, formulaic texts that kings carved into stone to celebrate their conquests and building projects. Royal inscriptions are useful but heavily biased; they tell you what the king wanted posterity to know, not what ordinary life was like. Under the Sukkalmah, something changes: the archaeological record at Susa starts producing large quantities of **legal contracts**, drawn up in cuneiform on clay tablets, recording sales of slaves, marriages, inheritances, loans, and lawsuits between ordinary middle-class residents of the city.

These contracts are the best window we will ever have into Elamite daily life. They show that Susa in 1700 BCE was a cosmopolitan urban center with a population of several tens of thousands, a substantial middle class of merchants and craftsmen, a functional court system that heard disputes between private parties, a legal culture in which witnesses and seals and formal contracts mattered, and a level of literacy high enough that ordinary people were regularly generating and preserving written legal documents. The contracts are in a specific Elamite legal tradition that has some parallels to contemporary Babylonian law (the Code of Hammurabi era) but also its own distinctive features — particularly around marriage and inheritance, where Elamite women seem to have had more direct legal agency than their Babylonian counterparts. Elamite contracts show wives owning property in their own names, initiating legal disputes, inheriting from parents on equal terms with brothers, and occasionally serving as witnesses for other people's contracts. This is not a modern gender-equal society by any stretch — slavery exists, patriarchal structures exist, and most political offices are male-only — but it is a society in which women have more legal visibility than most of their contemporaries.

The Sukkalmah period, in short, is Elam as a functioning civilization. Stable government. Peer diplomacy. Rich middle class. Legal culture. Distinct pantheon. Functional writing system. The whole package. It is, in a lot of ways, the moment when Elam is most fully itself — not at war, not collapsing, not being destroyed by an invader, just quietly running the way it had designed itself to run.

By 1500 BCE the Sukkalmah dynasty was coming to an end, replaced by a new royal line called the Kidinuid and then by the Igihalkid dynasty. The quiet Elam of the Sukkalmah was about to become the aggressive building-boom Elam of the Middle Elamite period. And the most visible legacy of that new era is still standing in the desert of Khuzestan — a brick ziggurat the size of a small mountain.

---

# Chapter 5 — The Ziggurat Generation

Around 1250 BCE, on a dry plain in southwestern Iran about 25 miles (40 km) southeast of Susa, an Elamite king ordered the construction of a religious complex so ambitious that it required the founding of an entirely new city around it. The city was called **Dur-Untash** — "Fortress of Untash," named for the king who built it — and at the heart of the city was a stepped mud-brick tower called a **ziggurat**, a specifically Mesopotamian architectural form that was by 1250 BCE already fifteen centuries old as a construction tradition. The ziggurat at Dur-Untash was not a new invention. It was a statement.

The statement was: **Elam can do Mesopotamian architecture too, and bigger**.

The ziggurat at Dur-Untash is today called the **Ziggurat of Chogha Zanbil** (CHOH-gah ZAHN-beel — the modern Persian name for the site, meaning "basket-shaped mound"), and it is one of the most remarkable preserved monuments in the whole ancient Near East. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It is the largest ziggurat standing anywhere in the world today, larger than any ziggurat still preserved in Mesopotamia itself. It is also effectively the last great ziggurat of the ancient world — a handful of later, smaller Mesopotamian ziggurats exist, but Chogha Zanbil is generally considered the final flowering of the tradition on a monumental scale. Nobody after the twelfth century BCE bothered to build one this big again. And it is worth noting the global context: while Untash-Napirisha was finishing Chogha Zanbil around 1250 BCE, Ramesses II was carving the colossal rock-cut temples at Abu Simbel into the cliffs of Nubia (Ancient Nubia Ch 7), New Kingdom Egypt and Middle Elamite Elam staging parallel monument-building competitions at opposite ends of the Bronze Age Near East in almost exactly the same generation.

And the king who built it is the figure who dominates the entire Middle Elamite period: **Untash-Napirisha** (oon-TAHSH nah-pee-REE-shah), ruled roughly 1275–1240 BCE.

Before we walk into his ziggurat, a quick note on what had changed politically. The quiet stability of the Sukkalmah period we just left ended around 1500 BCE. The Sukkalmah dynasty fell. A transitional period called the **Kidinuid** dynasty ruled briefly in the fifteenth century BCE (named for a king called Kidin-Khutran — the records are thin), followed by a long period of ambiguous rule and diminished international visibility while the rest of the Near East was absorbed in the great power politics of Egypt's New Kingdom, the Hittite empire, and the rising Middle Assyrian state (Mesopotamia Ch 8). From roughly 1500 to 1400 BCE, Elam mostly disappears from the diplomatic record. The internal archaeology shows the kingdom kept functioning — Susa kept being rebuilt, the pantheon kept being worshipped, the Elamite cuneiform scribal tradition kept producing contracts — but the external projection of power dropped to near zero.

Then, around 1400 BCE, a new royal line called the **Igihalkid dynasty** (named for its founder, **Ige-halki** — the name may mean something like "he for whom Igi is a blessing") emerged from the highland regions of Elam and began rebuilding the kingdom's external ambitions. The Igihalkid kings of the fourteenth century BCE restored the Elamite military, refortified Susa, rebuilt temples across the kingdom, and started cultivating the sort of aggressive foreign-policy posture that would culminate in the great sack of Babylon in Chapter 6. The Igihalkid period is the Middle Elamite period proper, and it is the era when Elam goes from being a recovering provincial kingdom to being one of the three or four great powers of the Late Bronze Age Near East — the others being New Kingdom Egypt, the **Hittite Empire** (the Indo-European-speaking kingdom of central Anatolia, modern Turkey, whose armies fought Ramesses II at Kadesh), **Kassite Babylonia** (the dynasty that had taken over Babylon after Mursili I's 1595 BCE sack, ruling southern Mesopotamia for nearly four centuries using a non-Semitic language whose affinities we still can't pin down — the third big language isolate in the Bronze Age Near East, Mesopotamia Ch 8), and the **Middle Assyrian state** (a predecessor of the famous Neo-Assyrian empire of Chapter 7, already aggressive but not yet the terrifying regional dominator it would become). Elam of the Igihalkid period is playing in the same league as the kingdoms Ramesses II and the later Assyrian royal founders would rule.

Untash-Napirisha was the greatest of the Igihalkid kings. His name means roughly "Untash is the Great God" — "Untash" being a personal name element and "Napirisha" being the name of a specific Elamite god, one of the highland deities who would later be absorbed into the broader pantheon. Untash-Napirisha came to the throne around 1275 BCE as the grandson of earlier Igihalkid kings, inherited a relatively stable kingdom, and used the stability to launch the most ambitious building program in Elamite history.

---

At the center of that building program was Dur-Untash and its ziggurat.

A **ziggurat** (ZIG-uh-rat) is a stepped tower made of mud brick, usually with a small temple at the top and ritual platforms on the lower levels. The form was invented in Sumer in the late fourth millennium BCE and evolved over the next two thousand years into one of the signature monumental architectures of ancient Mesopotamia. The great ziggurat at Ur, built by Ur-Nammu around 2100 BCE, is the most famous surviving example; others were built at Babylon (the "Tower of Babel" of the Hebrew Bible), at Nippur, at Eridu, and at dozens of other Mesopotamian cities. By the Middle Elamite period, ziggurats had been a standard feature of Mesopotamian religious architecture for more than 1,500 years, and Elamite religious sites had been importing the form for almost as long.

What made Chogha Zanbil different was the scale and the ambition of Untash-Napirisha's conception. The ziggurat rises in five stepped levels — though today only the first two or three levels survive, weathered down from the original height — to an estimated original height of about 174 feet (53 meters), or roughly a fifteen-story building. The base is a square approximately 345 feet (105 meters) on each side. The total volume of baked and sun-dried brick used in construction has been estimated at roughly 300 million bricks, each one stamped with Untash-Napirisha's name. That figure may be an overestimate, but the brick stamps are genuine: archaeologists working at the site from 1951 onward have recovered hundreds of individual bricks carrying the same royal inscription, identifying the builder and dedicating the building to the god Inshushinak. The dedication is important. The ziggurat was not dedicated to Humban, the king of the gods; it was dedicated to Inshushinak, the lord of Susa, despite being built at Dur-Untash rather than at Susa itself. Untash-Napirisha was trying to create a new cult center that would be specifically for the Elamite highland-lowland synthesis, with Inshushinak (the lowland patron) at the top and Humban (the highland patron) honored in the surrounding temple precincts.

Surrounding the ziggurat was the rest of the religious complex: eleven subsidiary temples dedicated to other gods of the Elamite pantheon, a royal palace, a sacred enclosure marked off from the rest of the city by three concentric walls, water-supply channels bringing fresh water from the Karkheh River seven miles away, and a small residential quarter for priests and palace staff. Dur-Untash was conceived as a pilgrimage city rather than a commercial center, and at its founding it probably had a resident population of only a few thousand people — administrators, priests, temple servants, and royal retinue — with the main population only arriving for major religious festivals.

The most striking thing about Chogha Zanbil for a modern visitor is not the ziggurat itself, impressive as it is. It is the quality of the brickwork. The walls of the ziggurat are faced with fired (rather than sun-dried) brick on the outer surfaces, with the internal core built of sun-dried brick, and the face bricks are inscribed with the royal name in repeated rows. This is extraordinarily expensive construction for the era — fired brick requires fuel and kilns and skilled labor on a much larger scale than sun-dried brick — and it means the ziggurat's outer surfaces were designed to last. They have. Where the weathered Mesopotamian ziggurats are now mostly unrecognizable mounds of eroded mud, Chogha Zanbil still has substantial sections of its original fired-brick face intact, preserving the royal inscriptions and letting modern archaeologists read them directly off the monument itself.

The site was rediscovered in 1935 by an oil surveyor named Browne, who was flying over southwestern Iran looking for petroleum prospects and noticed an unnatural square mound on the horizon. Full excavation began in 1951 under the French archaeologist Roman Ghirshman and continued through the 1970s, interrupted by the Iran-Iraq War, and has resumed periodically since. The site has survived the vagaries of twentieth-century politics remarkably well — it is protected today as a national monument — and its designation as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1979 was the first Iranian site to receive UNESCO status. For a brief moment in the Middle Elamite period, a king of Elam had commissioned the biggest religious monument anywhere in the ancient Near East outside Egypt. Three thousand years later, his ziggurat is still standing, still recognizable, still bearing his name on every brick of its outer face.

---

One more object from Untash-Napirisha's reign deserves its own section, because it is arguably the single most impressive artifact that has ever come out of Elamite territory: the **Bronze Statue of Queen Napir-Asu**.

Queen **Napir-Asu** (nah-PEER AH-soo) was the wife of Untash-Napirisha. The bronze statue, excavated at Susa in 1903 by French archaeologists, depicts her standing formally in court dress, with her hands folded in front of her body and her skirt rendered in intricate incised patterns imitating textile weave. The figure is life-sized — about four and a half feet tall (1.4 meters) — and the head has been broken off and lost at some point in antiquity, so we are looking at a headless statue today. Everything surviving below the neckline is intact and in remarkable condition.

What makes the Napir-Asu statue extraordinary is not the artistic quality, though the artistic quality is very high. It is the weight. The statue is solid bronze. Not hollow, not cored, not a shell with internal supports. Solid all the way through. It weighs **3,760 pounds (1,750 kg)** — nearly two tons of bronze cast in a single piece. To appreciate what this means, you need to understand that casting solid bronze in a single piece on that scale is an extremely difficult metallurgical achievement that most ancient civilizations never attempted. The largest surviving Egyptian bronze statues are smaller and were cast in multiple pieces welded together afterward. The famous Greek bronze sculptures of the Classical period (the Charioteer of Delphi, the Riace Warriors) were all hollow-cast over internal cores. Hollow casting saves enormous amounts of metal and is structurally much easier. Solid casting on the Napir-Asu scale is a display of raw metallurgical dominance — the kind of thing you do specifically to impress visitors with how much bronze you can afford to waste on a single object.

The direct contemporary counterpart in the ancient world is across all of Asia, in the Shang dynasty bronze tradition of central China (Ancient China Ch 3). Within a century of Napir-Asu's casting at Susa, Shang bronze workers at Anyang were producing the enormous ritual bronze vessels known as **ding** (DING), including the famous **Houmuwu Ding** cast around 1200 BCE for the tomb of the consort of a Shang king. The Houmuwu Ding is a rectangular four-legged cauldron weighing 1,832 pounds (832 kg) — about half the weight of the Napir-Asu statue, but cast using a completely different "piece-mold" technique in which the mold itself was assembled from multiple interlocking clay sections before the pour. The Shang bronze workers and the Elamite bronze workers had almost certainly no idea the other tradition existed, and they arrived at the same problem (how do you cast huge amounts of bronze in a single piece?) and solved it in different ways, at opposite ends of Asia, in the same century. The mid-second millennium BCE is the first moment in history when two completely unrelated civilizations are casting massive solid-cast bronze at the same time, and the Napir-Asu and Houmuwu Ding are the two objects that prove it.

Why was it cast solid? The most plausible explanation is religious. The statue's inscription, carved in Elamite cuneiform around the lower part of the skirt, calls down curses on anyone who attempts to destroy or damage the figure. Solid casting would make the statue effectively impossible to melt down and reuse — no thief with a hammer and a crucible could extract the metal easily. The solidity was a defense against desecration. The queen wanted her image in the temple of Inshushinak to outlast any political misfortune that might tempt later kings to recycle the bronze for their own projects.

She almost succeeded. The statue did survive the sack of Susa in the eighth century BCE, the destruction by Ashurbanipal in 646 BCE, the Achaemenid absorption, the Alexandrine conquest, the Parthian and Sasanian periods, the Arab conquest, and the medieval Islamic period, lying buried in the rubble of the temple it had been installed in. Only the head was lost at some point along the way, probably broken off by a looter who could not carry the whole figure. French archaeologists found it and moved it to the Louvre, where it has been on display ever since as one of the signature pieces of the museum's Near Eastern collection. If you visit the Louvre, you can stand next to the headless queen of Elam and look at her at close range. The inscription is still legible around the base of her skirt. The bronze has weathered to a dark green patina. Her folded hands are still visible. The statue is one of the very few surviving Late Bronze Age artworks that gives a modern viewer a direct sense of what elite Near Eastern kingship actually looked like — not as a ruined mound or a broken fragment, but as a nearly intact representation of a specific named queen standing at the center of her temple, solid as metal.

---

Untash-Napirisha died around 1240 BCE, having spent his reign on Chogha Zanbil, the Napir-Asu statue, and the restoration of major temples at Susa and Anshan. He was succeeded by a series of Igihalkid kings whose specific contributions are less visible archaeologically — the building boom slowed, Dur-Untash was already complete, and the royal focus shifted to foreign policy rather than monument construction.

What the building boom did leave behind, and what matters for the next chapter, is the visible evidence of Elam as a Late Bronze Age superpower. The scale of Chogha Zanbil, the solid-bronze casting of the Napir-Asu statue, the fortification of Susa under Untash-Napirisha, and the building programs at Anshan and the smaller Elamite cities together represent a kingdom at its peak of wealth, confidence, and organizational capacity. The Igihalkid dynasty of the thirteenth century BCE was running an Elam that was architecturally, economically, and militarily capable of anything its Mesopotamian neighbors could do.

And by the twelfth century BCE, the Elamite kings decided to prove it.

---

# Chapter 6 — Sack of Babylon

In 1158 BCE, the Elamite king **Shutruk-Nahhunte** (shoo-trook nah-HOON-tay) led an army west across the Zagros passes, crossed the Tigris River, and marched into Babylonia. The Babylonian king at the time was **Zababa-shuma-iddin**, a late Kassite ruler whose dynasty had been weakening for a generation. The Babylonian army met Shutruk-Nahhunte in the field, was defeated, and Babylon itself fell to Elamite assault. The city was looted in a systematic, methodical way that made clear the Elamites had been planning this for years. Temples were opened. Royal palaces were stripped. The famous cedar-wood processional barge of the god Marduk was seized. Statues, stelae, and monuments were pulled down and loaded onto carts. Then the whole convoy of war trophies — one of the greatest hauls of looted art in the entire ancient world — was dragged east over the Zagros passes back to Susa, where the Elamite king installed the trophies in his own temples as votive offerings to Inshushinak and as permanent displays of Elamite supremacy.

Among the trophies were two objects we have already met in this book. The first was the **Code of Hammurabi** — the tall black diorite stele carved around 1754 BCE with the famous legal code and Hammurabi's portrait at the top receiving the laws from the sun god Shamash, one of the most famous artifacts in all of Mesopotamian archaeology. The second was the **Victory Stele of Naram-Sin**, Sargon's grandson's victory monument from Chapter 2, carved around 2250 BCE showing the Akkadian king in divine regalia climbing a mountain at the head of his army. Both objects had been sitting in the religious and palace districts of lower Mesopotamia for centuries when Shutruk-Nahhunte's army arrived, and both were among the most important pieces he took. Both traveled to Susa on Elamite carts and were erected in the Elamite temple precinct as displays of the king's new prestige.

They would stay there for the next three thousand years.

---

Shutruk-Nahhunte is the most famous Elamite king, and the reason he is famous is the sack of Babylon. But he was also a genuinely capable ruler who came to the throne around 1185 BCE at the head of a new royal line called the **Shutrukid dynasty**, which had displaced the older Igihalkid line through a palace coup or an inheritance crisis we can only partly reconstruct. Shutruk-Nahhunte's reign represents Elam's last great imperial moment before the long collapse that will dominate the next several chapters, and it is the single clearest example in all of Elamite history of the kingdom projecting power outward across its entire neighborhood on the scale of a first-tier Near Eastern empire.

A note on the Shutrukid royal names is worth pausing on, because the reader is about to meet four kings whose names start to blur together. The dynasty reads, in sequence: **Shutruk-Nahhunte** (the father, the sacker of Babylon, the subject of this chapter's middle section), then his son **Kutir-Nahhunte III** (KOO-teer, who continued the Babylonian wars), then Kutir-Nahhunte's brother **Shilhak-Inshushinak** (SHEEL-hak, the great builder and restorer whose Sit Shamshi bronze survives), then Shilhak-Inshushinak's son **Hutelutush-Inshushinak** (hoo-teh-LOO-tush, who lost the kingdom to Babylon). The trick to keeping them straight: the first three are a father-and-two-brothers generation of peak imperial Elam, and the fourth is the son who inherited the collapse. The two "-Nahhunte" names (invoking the Elamite sun god) belong to father and son; the two "-Inshushinak" names (invoking the Elamite city god of Susa) belong to uncle and nephew.

His reign started with the usual building projects — temples restored, royal inscriptions issued, frontier fortifications repaired — and for most of the first decade nothing particularly dramatic happened. The shift came around 1175 BCE when the Kassite dynasty of Babylon, which had been a relatively quiet neighbor for nearly four centuries (Mesopotamia Chapter 8), entered a period of rapid political decline. The late Kassite kings were weak, contested internally, and could not maintain their grip on the western and eastern frontiers. Shutruk-Nahhunte watched the decline and began preparing his response. The response took another decade to organize — military logistics at this scale did not happen overnight — and then in 1158 BCE the army marched.

Why did he do it? The short answer is that he wanted the wealth and the prestige, and the opportunity was there. The long answer is more interesting, and it involves a specifically Elamite claim to Mesopotamian legitimacy.

The Shutrukid kings asserted a blood relationship with the Kassite royal line. Several Shutrukid royal inscriptions claim that the Elamite royal house was descended from a Kassite princess who had married an Elamite king several generations earlier. The claim is probably true in some form — dynastic marriages between Elam and Babylon were routine in the Middle Elamite period, and a Kassite princess turning up at the Elamite court and producing heirs would be entirely consistent with what we know about Late Bronze Age diplomatic practice. Shutruk-Nahhunte used the ancestry claim to assert that the Elamite royal house had a legitimate hereditary claim to the Babylonian throne, and that when the Kassite dynasty died out, the rightful successor was the Elamite king. It is exactly the kind of legal fiction that ancient kingdoms used to dress up aggressive wars of conquest as legitimate succession disputes. Whether Shutruk-Nahhunte actually believed it or was cynically manipulating the diplomatic language is impossible to say now.

What is clear is that the claim gave him a rhetorical framework for the invasion. He could present the 1158 BCE campaign not as an Elamite attack on Babylon but as an Elamite king enforcing his family's hereditary right against a usurping Kassite dynasty. In the royal inscriptions Shutruk-Nahhunte left behind at Susa describing the campaign, he uses language that frames the Kassite king as a rebel and himself as the legitimate restorer of order. This is propaganda, but it is politically sophisticated propaganda, and it places the sack of Babylon within a broader Mesopotamian legal tradition of dynastic inheritance rather than treating it as a simple foreign invasion.

---

The actual sack of Babylon deserves to be described carefully, because it is one of the richest looting events of the ancient world.

The Elamite army besieged Babylon for a short period — probably only a few weeks — before the walls were breached. The Kassite king Zababa-shuma-iddin was captured and deposed; what happened to him afterward is not recorded in surviving texts, though later Babylonian tradition holds that he was taken to Susa in chains and died in Elamite captivity. Once inside the city, Shutruk-Nahhunte's forces conducted a systematic sweep of the major temples, palaces, and monuments. They did not burn the city wholesale — Babylon continued to be occupied and was rebuilt within a generation — but they stripped every valuable object of religious or political significance they could find and loaded it onto carts for the journey home.

The loot inventory is partly reconstructible from a combination of sources: Shutruk-Nahhunte's own surviving inscriptions at Susa boasting about the campaign, later Babylonian historical texts lamenting the losses, and above all the objects themselves that nineteenth and twentieth century archaeologists excavated at Susa and identified as Mesopotamian imports. The inventory includes:

- The **Code of Hammurabi**, the diorite stele we already mentioned. Shutruk-Nahhunte took it from its temple at Sippar (a major religious city in northern Babylonia), carried it to Susa, and erected it in the temple of Inshushinak. It stayed at Susa until 1901 CE, when the French archaeologist Jacques de Morgan excavated it, identified it, and shipped it to Paris. It is today on permanent display at the Louvre, where it remains one of the most visited ancient artifacts in the museum. The Code of Hammurabi is the most famous Mesopotamian artwork in the world, and it would not be intact today if Shutruk-Nahhunte had not carried it to Susa. Left in Sippar, it would almost certainly have been destroyed by later wars or recycled for its expensive black stone.

- The **Victory Stele of Naram-Sin**, same story. Taken from the Akkadian cultural heritage sites of lower Mesopotamia, carried to Susa, installed in an Elamite temple, excavated in 1898 by the same French team, and sent to the Louvre where it is currently displayed.

- A statue of **Manishtushu** (Sargon of Akkad's son, the brother and predecessor of Naram-Sin). The statue, known today as the "Statue of Manishtushu" or the "Obelisk of Manishtushu," is a carved stone figure of the king seated on a throne, also excavated at Susa by French archaeologists.

- The cuneiform "**Obelisk of Manishtushu**" — a separate four-sided pillar carved with cuneiform text recording land sales made during Manishtushu's reign, from the twenty-third century BCE. Also at Susa, now at the Louvre.

- A stone **statue of Shulgi**, the famous Ur III king from Chapter 3.

- Dozens of smaller objects: bronze weapons, cylinder seals, royal portraits, votive statues, inscribed stones, foundation deposits, and what appear to be private temple treasures from Babylonian shrines.

- The **cedar-wood processional barge of Marduk** — the Babylonian chief god's ceremonial boat, used in the annual New Year festival for solemn processions down the Euphrates and back. The barge was the focal point of Babylon's most important religious ceremony, and its seizure was a gesture of extraordinary ritual aggression. Shutruk-Nahhunte brought it back to Susa and installed it in an Elamite temple.

- The **statue of Marduk himself** — the cult statue of the Babylonian chief god, which had been the focal point of Babylonian religion for centuries. To capture and remove the statue of a city's chief god was the ultimate act of religious and political domination. It meant the god had abandoned Babylon and accepted a new residence at Susa. Shutruk-Nahhunte brought Marduk back to Elam and installed him in a temple there. This is the object that will matter most in the counterattack in a few years. (Worth knowing: Shutruk-Nahhunte was not the *first* foreign king to kidnap Marduk from Babylon. The Hittite king Mursili I had carried the statue off in his famous 1595 BCE sack of Babylon — the event that ended the Old Babylonian dynasty, Mesopotamia Ch 8 — and the Assyrian king Tukulti-Ninurta I had done it again around 1225 BCE, Mesopotamia Ch 9. Marduk had been through two prior captivities before Shutruk-Nahhunte's men loaded him onto an Elamite cart. Each time, a later Babylonian campaign had eventually brought the statue home. The 1158 BCE seizure was the third round of the same kidnapping game, and the Babylonians expected it would end the same way — which, eventually, it did.)

What the Elamites did with the loot is characteristic of how Near Eastern victors used captured religious objects. They did not melt down the metal or burn the wood. They installed the trophies in their own temples, where the physical presence of captured Mesopotamian art served both as a trophy and as a demonstration of Elamite supremacy over the rival pantheon. To walk into the temple of Inshushinak at Susa in 1150 BCE and see the Code of Hammurabi and the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin and the Marduk statue all lined up along the walls was to see three thousand years of Mesopotamian imperial self-presentation reduced to an Elamite backdrop. The Elamite priests had been busy.

---

Shutruk-Nahhunte died around 1155 BCE, only a few years after his great campaign, and was succeeded by his son **Kutir-Nahhunte III** (KOO-teer nah-HOON-tay — the "III" is a modern scholarly numbering to distinguish him from earlier Kutir-Nahhuntes; the Elamites themselves did not number their kings). Kutir-Nahhunte III continued his father's wars against a weakened Babylon, leading additional campaigns west into Mesopotamia and briefly installing himself as overlord of the former Kassite territory. His reign represents the absolute high-water mark of Elamite political reach — the closest Elam ever came to actually conquering and ruling Mesopotamia as a subject territory rather than just raiding it.

The closest Kutir-Nahhunte came was installing Elamite governors over several Babylonian cities and demanding tribute from the post-Kassite local rulers for a period of approximately a decade. This is not quite full conquest — the Elamite imperial infrastructure never reached the depth that the Akkadian or Neo-Assyrian imperial systems did — but it is substantially further than any earlier Elamite king had pushed. For about ten years, roughly 1155 to 1145 BCE, the Elamite kings ruled a two-region empire spanning both sides of the Zagros, with western and eastern halves connected by the same imperial bureaucracy.

Kutir-Nahhunte was succeeded by his brother **Shilhak-Inshushinak** (SHEEL-hak in-SHOO-shee-nak — "Shilhak" meaning "he who spreads" or "he who multiplies," and "Inshushinak" being the god's name appended as a theophoric element), whose reign from roughly 1150 to 1120 BCE represents the last moment of Elam as a great power. Shilhak-Inshushinak was an ambitious and energetic ruler who continued the imperial wars against Babylon, expanded the Elamite temple-building program at Susa, and commissioned one of the most remarkable surviving Elamite artworks: the **Sit Shamshi Bronze Tableau**.

---

The **Sit Shamshi** (meaning "Sunrise") is a bronze sculpture group approximately 23 inches long (60 cm) showing a miniature scene of two nude male figures performing what appears to be a sunrise ritual at a miniature temple complex. One figure is kneeling, pouring water from a vessel; the other is standing with hands raised in a priestly gesture. Between them are tiny bronze representations of a ziggurat, several subsidiary temples, a sacred grove of bronze trees, and various ritual objects including miniature altars and offering stands. The whole thing is cast from solid bronze and was designed to sit on an altar as a permanent miniature of the morning ritual it depicts.

The Sit Shamshi is one of the most detailed surviving representations of Elamite religious practice at its operational level. It shows us what the morning ritual at an Elamite temple actually looked like: two naked priests, a pouring-of-water ceremony at dawn, ritual space marked by architectural miniatures, and a complete built environment for the ceremony. The surviving inscription on the base identifies Shilhak-Inshushinak as the dedicator and calls the tableau "my bronze sunrise" — explicitly framing the object as the king's personal ritual offering to the god Inshushinak. The Sit Shamshi is the clearest window we have into the daily religious operations of an Elamite temple, and it survives today in the Louvre along with most of the other great Elamite bronzes.

Shilhak-Inshushinak's reign was also the moment when Elamite kings starting commissioning **restoration inscriptions** for temples and monuments across the kingdom. These are texts in which a king records his restoration of a shrine originally built by an earlier ruler, often citing the names and dates of his predecessors. The restoration inscriptions are historiographically invaluable because they give us an Elamite view of Elamite history — kings commemorating earlier kings, citing chronological predecessors, establishing a dynastic framework that ties the current reign to the deep past of the kingdom. Shilhak-Inshushinak's restoration inscriptions are some of the richest sources for reconstructing the sequence of earlier Elamite dynasties, because his scribes carefully listed the names of predecessors whose original monuments were being restored. The texts are also a window into how the Elamite royal court imagined its own history: as an unbroken chain of rulers stretching back to the Proto-Elamite period, each one accountable to his predecessors and to the patron gods who had legitimized the whole sequence.

---

And then it collapsed.

The Elamite imperial moment ended around 1120 BCE with the reign of **Hutelutush-Inshushinak** (hoo-teh-LOO-tush), Shilhak-Inshushinak's son and successor. Hutelutush-Inshushinak inherited a kingdom that had already overextended itself — the Elamite military was stretched thin garrisoning Mesopotamia, the treasury was depleted by the ongoing building programs, and the Babylonian resistance under a new dynasty was starting to organize.

The Babylonian king who organized the resistance was **Nebuchadnezzar I** (NEB-yoo-kad-NEZZ-ar — the first king of that name, not to be confused with the much more famous Nebuchadnezzar II of the Neo-Babylonian Empire six centuries later; Mesopotamia Chapter 12). Nebuchadnezzar I ruled Babylon from roughly 1125 to 1104 BCE, at the head of the Second Dynasty of Isin — a new native Babylonian royal line that had replaced the collapsed Kassites. His primary political goal was reversing the humiliation of Shutruk-Nahhunte's sack of Babylon and recovering the Marduk statue that still sat in an Elamite temple at Susa.

Nebuchadnezzar I's war against Elam is told from the Babylonian side in Mesopotamia Chapter 10. The Elamite side is briefer: Hutelutush-Inshushinak's army met the Babylonian forces in the field, was defeated, and retreated back to Susa. Nebuchadnezzar I pursued, besieged Susa, sacked the city, recovered the Marduk statue, and returned to Babylon in triumph. Hutelutush-Inshushinak himself fled into the highlands and apparently lived out the rest of his reign in reduced circumstances at Anshan, ruling a contracted kingdom that no longer included the lowland plain.

The Marduk statue was recovered. The other trophies — the Code of Hammurabi, the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin, the Manishtushu obelisk, the Statue of Shulgi — were not. Either Nebuchadnezzar I did not know they were at Susa, or he could not find them in the sacked city, or he was focused specifically on the Marduk statue as the religiously critical object and left the older trophies behind. Whatever the reason, the Hammurabi and Naram-Sin stelae stayed at Susa. They would still be there, buried under rubble, when French archaeologists arrived nearly three thousand years later.

After Hutelutush-Inshushinak's death around 1110 BCE, Elam enters a period of historical silence that lasts almost three centuries. The kingdom does not disappear — Susa is still inhabited, Anshan is still a highland center, the cuneiform tradition continues, and Elamite kings are still ruling somebody somewhere in southwestern Iran — but the external records go nearly dark. Mesopotamian chronicles mention Elam only in passing. Surviving Elamite inscriptions are few. The archaeological strata at Susa and Anshan show continuous occupation but without the monumental building programs and trophy imports of the Middle Elamite period. Between roughly 1100 and 750 BCE, Elam is going through what later historians call the **Elamite Dark Age** — a 350-year quiet period about which we know very little.

When Elam re-emerges into the historical record in the eighth century BCE, it is a very different kingdom. Smaller, more decentralized, ruled by a network of highland kings rather than a single imperial sukkalmah. And facing a new enemy who will prove to be the most destructive force Elam has ever met: the Neo-Assyrian Empire.

That is Chapter 7.

---

# Chapter 7 — Til-Tuba

The Neo-Elamite period (roughly 1100–539 BCE) is a very different world from the imperial Elam of Chogha Zanbil and Shutruk-Nahhunte. It is fragmented, regional, shifting, and difficult to narrate as a unified political history. For most of the early Neo-Elamite centuries, "Elam" is best understood as a loose confederation of highland and lowland city-states, each with its own royal line, sometimes cooperating under a paramount king and sometimes fighting each other, all of them loosely identified as Elamite and all of them sharing the old pantheon, the old scribal tradition, and the old two-region geography. The Sukkalmah-style centralized state is gone. A decentralized Elam takes its place, and for three hundred years or so it manages to keep running in the background of regional politics while the big story of the Near East moves elsewhere.

The big story of the Near East in those centuries is the rise of **Assyria**.

Mesopotamia Chapter 11 tells the Assyrian story in detail. The short version is that the Assyrian heartland in northern Iraq — centered on the cities of Assur, Nineveh, and Kalhu — had been a middle-rank Near Eastern power since the early second millennium BCE, but starting in the ninth century BCE under a series of aggressive and militarily innovative kings (Ashurnasirpal II, Shalmaneser III, Adad-nirari III), Assyria began expanding outward from its heartland at a pace no earlier Mesopotamian empire had matched. By the mid-eighth century BCE, the Assyrian military machine had perfected mass cavalry warfare, siege engineering, forced-population transfer as a strategic tool, iron weaponry, and a professional standing army organized into specialized units with a reliable logistical support system. The Neo-Assyrian Empire of the seventh century BCE was the first military-industrial complex in world history, and it was terrifying.

Between 745 BCE and 612 BCE, Neo-Assyria conquered almost the entire ancient Near East. The **Arameans** (Semitic-speaking tribes from the Syrian desert, whose language Aramaic would later become the everyday lingua franca of the whole Near East) were pacified. Syria and the Levant (including Israel and Judah) were absorbed. Phoenicia (the coastal trading cities of modern Lebanon, famous as the inventors of the alphabet that became Greek and Latin and most modern Western scripts) was subjected. The Kingdom of Urartu in the Armenian highlands was beaten back. Egypt was invaded twice, briefly conquered, and made to pay tribute. The **Medes** (an Indo-European people of western Iran, cousins of the Persians, who would later help destroy Assyria and then briefly run their own empire before the Persian takeover) were pressured into a client relationship. Babylon was repeatedly occupied — sometimes ruled as a direct Assyrian province, sometimes left as a subject kingdom, sometimes destroyed and rebuilt. By the mid-seventh century BCE, the Assyrian Empire was the largest state that had ever existed in human history, and its armies could be anywhere in the Near East within a month.

And Elam was next door.

---

The Assyrian-Elamite wars of the seventh century BCE are the long, bloody endgame of the story this book has been telling. They span roughly sixty years — from the 680s BCE to the 640s BCE — and they involve at least seven separate Assyrian invasions of Elam, countless skirmishes, multiple Elamite alliances with Mesopotamian rebels, and a slowly tightening Assyrian pressure that eventually destroyed Elam as a functioning kingdom. The wars are well-documented on the Assyrian side because the Neo-Assyrian kings commissioned extensive narrative reliefs in their palaces at Nineveh showing scenes from the campaigns — reliefs that survive in substantial quantities and are now in the British Museum, the Louvre, and other major European collections. They are less well-documented on the Elamite side because the Elamite kingdom was too fragmented during this period to produce the kind of royal inscriptions we have from earlier centuries. What we know about Elamite resistance to Assyria comes overwhelmingly from Assyrian sources — the winners telling the story of the losers, with all the usual biases.

The central figures on the two sides are worth introducing.

On the Assyrian side, the war eventually comes to be dominated by **Ashurbanipal** (ah-shur-BAH-nee-pahl, ruled 668–631 BCE), the last great king of the Neo-Assyrian Empire and one of the most complicated rulers in all of ancient history. Ashurbanipal was a warrior — he personally led campaigns across half the Near East — but he was also a scholar who assembled the famous Royal Library at Nineveh containing over 30,000 cuneiform tablets of Mesopotamian literature, religion, medicine, and history, preserving the corpus that most of what we know about Sumerian and Akkadian civilization has come down to us. He was also brutal even by Neo-Assyrian standards. His royal inscriptions boast of torturing captives, flaying enemies alive, impaling resisters on stakes outside the walls of conquered cities, and — a specifically Ashurbanipal touch — commissioning artworks depicting the gory details of his victories in the throne rooms of his own palace. The Nineveh palace reliefs of Ashurbanipal's reign are some of the most horrifying pieces of ancient propaganda that survive, and they were carved on the orders of a man who also built the biggest library in the ancient world.

On the Elamite side, during the decisive campaigns of the 650s BCE, the central figure is a king named **Te-Umman** — short for Tempti-Humban-Inshushinak, a throne name combining "tempti" (meaning "lord") with the names of the two Elamite divine patrons Humban and Inshushinak, which together meant something like "Lord of Humban and Inshushinak." Te-Umman was one of the few surviving rulers of the post-Sukkalmah Elamite kingdoms and seems to have controlled a substantial territory from a base at Susa. Assyrian inscriptions describe him as a tyrant who had murdered rival claimants to consolidate his power, which may be propaganda or may be accurate; the pattern of Neo-Elamite succession crises would certainly have made palace coups common. What is clear from the surviving sources is that Te-Umman was an aggressive and militarily capable ruler who, in 653 BCE, decided that the growing Assyrian pressure on Elam had to be met head-on rather than through diplomacy.

Te-Umman raised an army, marched west across the Tigris with Elamite cavalry and archers, and attacked Assyrian-controlled territory in southern Mesopotamia. Ashurbanipal responded by leading a major Assyrian army east to meet him. The two forces collided at a place the Assyrian sources call **Til-Tuba** (TIL-TOO-bah) — probably a hill or ridge along the east bank of a river in southern Mesopotamia, though the exact location has never been securely identified — on a day in 653 BCE that would produce one of the most famous battles in the entire ancient world.

---

The Battle of Til-Tuba is uniquely well-documented because Ashurbanipal commissioned a series of narrative relief panels carved into the walls of his palace at Nineveh that depict the battle in detail — frame by frame, almost like a comic book laid across a wall. The reliefs survive and are today mounted in a single large gallery in the British Museum, where you can walk along the wall and follow the sequence of events as Ashurbanipal's sculptors chose to tell them. Most ancient battles are known only from a single royal inscription boasting about victory. Til-Tuba is known from a graphic multi-panel visual narrative that names specific soldiers, depicts specific moments, and tells the story with a theatrical intensity that has no real parallel in earlier Near Eastern art.

The reliefs show the battle unfolding across several connected scenes, reading from left to right:

First: the armies approach each other. Te-Umman in his chariot is shown at the front of the Elamite line, wearing his royal headdress and flanked by his closest warriors. The Elamite soldiers are identifiable by their distinctive equipment — headbands, long hair, short spears and composite bows — which is different from the Assyrian style of armor and weaponry.

Second: the battle itself. The Assyrian army crushes the Elamite formation. Bodies pile up. Horses fall. Chariots overturn. Soldiers drown trying to cross a river behind the Elamite line. The reliefs show dozens of specific individual deaths with a level of grisly detail that is unprecedented in earlier Near Eastern battle art — a soldier being speared through the back as he tries to flee, another caught by the hair and decapitated, a third being trampled by a charging horse. The Assyrian sculptors were showing off both their technical skill and their king's military mercilessness.

Third: Te-Umman is killed. This is the central narrative moment of the reliefs, and it is depicted in a sequence of panels that reads like a cinematic chase. First Te-Umman's chariot is seen with an arrow protruding from the king's shoulder. The chariot begins to lose control. Te-Umman falls out, landing on the ground. He tries to rise. His son (shown in a separate panel, identified by an inscription) tries to protect him. Both are struck down. An Assyrian soldier named in the inscription — a specific man identified by name in the Assyrian texts, though the name is now lost in the deterioration of the reliefs — steps forward with a sword. Te-Umman's head is severed from his body on the spot. The head is lifted up. The body remains on the battlefield. The whole sequence takes perhaps eight or ten individual panels across a stretch of wall, and it is the most detailed narrative depiction of a single battlefield death in all of surviving ancient art.

Fourth: the head travels. Ashurbanipal's sculptors show the head of Te-Umman being carried back from the battlefield to Nineveh, where it is delivered to the Assyrian king. A specific panel in the sequence shows the head being presented to Ashurbanipal himself. And then — and this is the most famous single moment in the reliefs — the head is hung from a tree in the royal garden at Nineveh, where Ashurbanipal and his queen are shown reclining on couches, drinking wine, being served food by attendants, while the severed head of the Elamite king dangles from the branch directly above them.

The garden scene at Nineveh is one of the most famous images to survive from the ancient Near East. It is now known as the "Garden Party Relief" or the "Banquet of Ashurbanipal," and it has been reproduced in virtually every general history of the ancient world written in the last century. The relief shows the king and queen at ease in their palace garden, with music playing and servants fanning them, while the trophy head of their defeated enemy rotates gently in the breeze among the leaves. It is a specific choice. Ashurbanipal could have commissioned a simpler victory image. He chose instead to have himself depicted drinking wine in front of the hanging head of an enemy king. The relief is a statement about what kind of king Ashurbanipal understood himself to be — and it is a statement about what the Neo-Assyrian Empire was willing to do to the people who fought it.

---

Te-Umman's death did not end the Elamite resistance. It only intensified the Assyrian pressure.

Within a year of Til-Tuba, the new Elamite king — a man named **Humban-Nikash II**, one of several claimants to the throne in the political chaos following Te-Umman's defeat — made a fatal decision. He allied with a rebel movement against Ashurbanipal that was unfolding inside the Assyrian empire itself.

The rebel was Ashurbanipal's own older brother, **Shamash-shum-ukin** (SHAH-mash-shoom-YOO-keen). Their father, the previous Assyrian king Esarhaddon, had divided his empire between his two sons: Ashurbanipal would rule Assyria proper from Nineveh, and Shamash-shum-ukin would rule Babylonia from Babylon as a subordinate king. The arrangement had been intended to prevent the civil war that usually followed an Assyrian succession, and for almost seventeen years it worked. Shamash-shum-ukin ruled Babylon loyally, deferring to his younger brother in the larger empire's affairs, while building up his own court and administrative network.

In 652 BCE, Shamash-shum-ukin rebelled. The causes are complex and covered from the Mesopotamian side in Mesopotamia Chapter 11, but the short version is that the older brother grew increasingly resentful of being in the junior position, the Babylonian elite grew increasingly tired of Assyrian domination, and Shamash-shum-ukin saw an opportunity to lead a general Babylonian revolt against his brother. He declared Babylon independent of Assyria, began raising troops, and began sending diplomatic messages to the powers along Assyria's frontiers — including Elam — offering alliances to anyone willing to help bring Ashurbanipal down.

Humban-Nikash II, the Elamite king, said yes.

This was probably the worst political decision any Elamite king had ever made. The reasoning was sound in the moment: Ashurbanipal was weaker than he looked because his empire was now fighting itself, and Elam had plenty of legitimate grievances after the Til-Tuba disaster and the ongoing Assyrian pressure. Allying with Shamash-shum-ukin seemed like a way to deflect Assyrian attention west and secure the Elamite frontier through collective action with other enemies of Assyria. It was the standard move for a small power faced with a bigger enemy: find a distraction, back it, and hope the big enemy gets destroyed by someone else while you recover your strength.

It did not work. Ashurbanipal was unshakable. The rebellion dragged on for four years (652–648 BCE), during which Ashurbanipal besieged Babylon, starved the city, and eventually captured it. Shamash-shum-ukin committed suicide in the burning royal palace rather than be captured by his brother's forces. The rebellion collapsed. Babylon was brutally punished, and Ashurbanipal turned his full attention to Elam — the power that had backed the wrong brother.

---

The Assyrian response to Elamite treason was the **destruction of Elam**, carried out across a series of campaigns between 647 and 645 BCE. These campaigns are not a single event. They are a sustained, methodical, multi-year operation in which Assyrian armies repeatedly invaded Elamite territory, captured and destroyed Elamite cities, executed captured nobles, seized treasure, relocated populations, and systematically dismantled the physical infrastructure of the kingdom. Ashurbanipal's royal inscriptions describe the operation in exulting detail, because he was proud of it and wanted posterity to know exactly what he had done.

The highlights — if that is the right word — include:

- The **Sack of Susa** in 646 BCE, which is the symbolic end of Elamite political history. Susa had been continuously inhabited for more than three thousand years at this point, through every earlier political upheaval. The Assyrian campaign of 646 BCE was the first event that actually destroyed it in a meaningful way. Ashurbanipal's army entered the city, stripped every temple of its treasures, carried off or broke every royal monument, looted the palace, and burned the central ceremonial district. Ashurbanipal's own inscription, in the standard translation of his royal annals, describes the sack with the triumphalism of a king who has finally achieved what his ancestors only dreamed of — roughly: he destroyed the ziggurat of Susa, tore down its temples, burned the shrines of the gods of Elam, and carried off the gods and goddesses of the kingdom to Assyria.

- The **carrying off of the Elamite royal dead**. This is perhaps the most striking specific detail of the sack. Ashurbanipal's forces, upon capturing Susa, opened the royal tombs of the Elamite kings. The royal bodies — the preserved or partially decayed remains of Elamite kings from earlier centuries, buried with their regalia in the royal necropolis — were disinterred, wrapped in sacks, and carried back to Nineveh as trophies. Ashurbanipal's inscriptions describe this in language whose standard translation runs roughly: he carried off the bones of the kings of Elam to the land of Assyria so that their ghosts should find no rest and their spirits should have no peace. The purpose was religious as well as political. In the Mesopotamian and Elamite understanding of the afterlife, the dead depended on their living descendants to make regular offerings at their tombs. If the tomb was empty and the bones were somewhere far away, the dead would be cut off from the sustaining prayers and offerings of their community. Ashurbanipal was not just humiliating the Elamite royal family. He was consigning their dead to eternal suffering by breaking the ritual connection between living descendants and ancestral tombs.

- The **salting of the fields around Susa**. Ashurbanipal's inscriptions describe his army spreading salt over the agricultural fields surrounding the city to render them sterile. The "salting the earth" detail is often repeated as the most memorable part of the destruction, though some modern scholars question whether it was actually done at that scale or whether it is a rhetorical flourish describing a more limited and symbolic action. (Modern readers sometimes encounter the same trope attached to the Roman destruction of Carthage in 146 BCE — the story that the Romans salted the fields of the defeated city — and that Roman version is also probably more rhetoric than reality. The trope may actually originate with Ashurbanipal and have been imitated by later ancient propagandists.) Either way, the intention is clear: the king wanted to ensure that no one would be able to rebuild Susa as an agricultural center in the following generations. He was not merely conquering the city; he was trying to render it uninhabitable.

- The **recovery of the statue of Nanaya**. This was the Uruk cult image we met back in Chapter 3 — the goddess statue that Elamite forces had looted from Uruk during the Shimashki-era wars of the early second millennium BCE, held at Susa as a trophy ever since (in the same way Shutruk-Nahhunte later held the Code of Hammurabi). Ashurbanipal found the statue in a Susa temple, reclaimed it as Mesopotamian property, and returned it in triumph to Uruk, where it was reinstalled in its original temple after more than sixteen hundred years of Elamite captivity. Nanaya came home.

After the sack of Susa, Ashurbanipal continued his campaigns through the rest of Elamite territory, destroying Dur-Untash and Chogha Zanbil (though by this point Chogha Zanbil was already largely abandoned as a functioning religious center), capturing the highland cities, and systematically eliminating Elamite royal claimants. By 645 BCE, the Elamite state — already fractured in the post-Sukkalmah period — was effectively destroyed. No unified Elamite kingdom survived. A few local rulers continued to claim the title "king of Elam" over small highland territories for several more decades, but the political and military capacity of Elam as a Near Eastern great power was finished.

---

And then, within fifty years, Assyria itself collapsed.

The Neo-Assyrian Empire, which had seemed invincible in Ashurbanipal's lifetime, went through a series of internal crises after his death in 631 BCE. Succession disputes weakened the central government. The Medes in western Iran, previously an Assyrian client, rebelled. The Babylonians rebelled again, this time successfully, under a new dynasty that would become the Neo-Babylonian Empire. In 612 BCE, a coalition of Medes and Babylonians besieged Nineveh, captured the city, and burned it to the ground. Ashurbanipal had been dead for nineteen years. His royal library survived by accident, baked hard in the fire. His palace reliefs — including the Til-Tuba narrative panels and the Garden Party scene — were buried in the rubble and preserved for modern archaeologists to excavate. The empire he had ruled at its peak was gone.

The Elamite response to Assyria's collapse is one of the saddest footnotes in this book. Several Elamite local rulers in the mid-seventh century BCE tried to take advantage of Assyria's distraction to rebuild some portion of the old kingdom. Their efforts produced a few decades of renewed Elamite political activity, during which small kingdoms called "Elam" continued to exist in the highlands around Anshan and in the lowlands around Susa, but they never regained the scale or coherence of the pre-Ashurbanipal kingdom. The sack of Susa had done too much damage. The kingdom was broken, and the rebuilding efforts could not restore what had been lost.

Elam as an independent state was over.

But — and this is where the story gets unexpected — the Elamite civilization was not quite finished. The kingdom was dead, but the language, the scribal tradition, the administrative know-how, and the royal title "king of Anshan" all survived. They survived by being picked up and used by a new people who were about to become the greatest power the ancient world had ever seen.

Chapter 8 is about how Elam became Persia.

---

# Chapter 8 — The Persian Echo

Here is a fact that most general histories of the ancient world do not emphasize as much as they should: **the founder of the Persian Empire was, in his own self-presentation, a king of Anshan**.

Anshan is the Elamite city we have been carrying through this book — the highland capital in Fars, the half of the Elamite two-region geography that sat up on the Iranian plateau, the place excavated at Tall-i Malyan. When the Elamite kingdom was at its height, Anshan was a major political center equal in rank to Susa. When the Elamite kingdom was declining in the Neo-Elamite period, Anshan was one of the last highland centers that retained something like royal status. And when the Elamite kingdom was destroyed by Ashurbanipal in the 640s BCE, Anshan was one of the few places in the former kingdom that kept on functioning as a local capital under the control of new rulers who styled themselves "king of Anshan" in continuation of the old Elamite royal titles.

The specific new rulers who inherited the Anshan throne in the seventh century BCE came from a different ethnic and linguistic background from the old Elamites. They were **Persians** — that is, speakers of an early form of Persian, which is an Indo-European language (the family we met briefly in Chapter 1 — the huge language family branching from an ancestral Proto-Indo-European tongue spoken somewhere on the Pontic-Caspian steppes around 4000 BCE, eventually including Greek, Latin, Sanskrit, Persian, the Germanic languages, and the Celtic and Slavic branches). Linguistically, the Persians were related to the later Greeks and Romans, not to the Elamites, whose language was an isolate with no relatives. The Persians had migrated into the Iranian plateau from the north over several centuries in the late second and early first millennium BCE — a long, slow population movement that brought Iranian-speaking pastoralists down into the highland valleys of Fars and Media from an ancestral homeland somewhere in Central Asia. The same broader migration that split off the Persians also, around the same time, brought their linguistic cousins the **Indo-Aryans** southeast through the Hindu Kush into the Indus Valley and the Gangetic plain, where they would become the ancestral speakers of Sanskrit and the founders of the Vedic tradition (Indus Valley Ch 10). Persian and Sanskrit are sister languages descended from the same Indo-Iranian branch, and the two populations are effectively the eastern and western halves of the same Bronze Age population movement splitting around the Hindu Kush.

When the Persians arrived in Fars, they found Anshan already there — a long-established Elamite highland capital with a functioning court, a bureaucratic tradition, a cuneiform scribal culture, and a royal legitimacy that traced back two thousand years. Rather than destroying what they found, the incoming Persian leaders absorbed it. They married into the existing Elamite royal families. They took Elamite administrative personnel into their own service. They adopted the Elamite royal title "king of Anshan" as their own. They used the Elamite language for official correspondence, the Elamite cuneiform scribal tradition for royal records, and the Elamite bureaucratic system for administering the region. For several generations — through the late seventh and early sixth centuries BCE — the "Persian" kings of Anshan were functionally indistinguishable from the Elamite kings of Anshan they had replaced. They wore Elamite regalia. They worshipped Elamite gods alongside their own Iranian deities. They spoke Persian at home but wrote in Elamite at court. They were a hybrid culture, and the Elamite side of the hybrid was the more visible public face.

One of these hybrid Persian-Elamite kings of Anshan was named **Cyrus I**. He ruled in the mid-seventh century BCE, around the time of Ashurbanipal's destruction of Susa, and his surviving seal impression — a clay sealing stamped with his name and royal title — identifies him as "Cyrus, king of Anshan, son of Teispes." The seal itself is in Elamite artistic style and the title is an old Elamite royal title. Cyrus I's grandson would be the most famous king in ancient history.

---

**Cyrus the Great** (Cyrus II, ruled 559–530 BCE) is one of the handful of ancient rulers whose name is still widely known to modern Westerners who have never studied the ancient Near East. He is the founder of the Achaemenid Persian Empire. He is the king who conquered the Neo-Babylonian Empire and released the Jewish exiles held there, allowing them to return to Jerusalem and rebuild the Temple — an act that earned him a famous blessing in the Hebrew Bible, where the book of Isaiah calls him God's "anointed" (the original Hebrew word is *mashiach*, from which English derives "Messiah"). He is credited in some traditions as the inventor of human rights for his policy of respecting conquered peoples' religious customs. The Cyrus Cylinder — a small clay cylinder with a cuneiform inscription recording his conquest of Babylon in 539 BCE — is sometimes described, with some historical simplification, as the first human rights charter. Cyrus is a founder-figure in Persian national memory in exactly the way that Romulus is a founder-figure in Roman memory or Shi Huangdi is in Chinese memory.

What is less often emphasized in popular accounts of Cyrus is that the same Cyrus Cylinder which describes his conquest of Babylon also gives a genealogical titulature in which Cyrus calls himself king of Anshan, son of Cambyses (who was also king of Anshan), grandson of Cyrus I (king of Anshan), great-grandson of Teispes (king of Anshan), of "an enduring royal line" — the standard translation, by Irving Finkel and others, chains all four generations together through the Anshan title. The title "king of Anshan" is the one that anchors Cyrus's genealogy. His father, grandfather, and great-grandfather were all kings of Anshan before him. The Achaemenid royal line, in Cyrus's own official self-presentation on the Cylinder he commissioned to celebrate his greatest conquest, is first and foremost the royal line of Elamite Anshan. The later titles ("king of Sumer and Akkad," "king of the four quarters") are Mesopotamian imperial traditions that Cyrus adopted upon conquering Babylon, but the base-level title — the title that establishes who Cyrus was before he became a world-conqueror — is an Elamite one.

This means something important. When Cyrus the Great conquered the Neo-Babylonian Empire in 539 BCE and established the Persian Empire as the largest political unit the ancient world had ever seen, he was not simply a Persian outsider coming in to replace a Semitic Mesopotamian dynasty with an Indo-European Iranian one. He was the heir, in his own self-understanding, of a specifically Elamite royal tradition that went back through Anshan to the third millennium BCE. Cyrus presented himself as the inheritor of the Elamite kingdom that Ashurbanipal had destroyed a century earlier, and he used the Elamite royal title to legitimize his claim to the wider Mesopotamian world. From the Elamite perspective — if there still was an Elamite perspective — the conquests of Cyrus were not a foreign takeover but a restoration. An Anshan-royal line had returned.

---

The Persian Empire that Cyrus and his successors built is conventionally narrated in the context of Greek history — the Persian Empire as the great enemy that the Greek city-states fought at Marathon, Thermopylae, and Salamis in the early fifth century BCE (the Persian Wars, Greece Chs 5–6 in future). That's a real and important story, but it is not the only story. The Persian Empire also ran a massive internal bureaucratic apparatus that administered everything from Egypt to the Indus River — a population of perhaps 50 million people, an area of roughly 2.9 million square miles (7.5 million km²), thousands of cities and towns, thousands of miles of royal roads, a royal postal system, a uniform coinage, a tax structure, a legal framework, a religious policy, and a multi-ethnic military. The administrative apparatus of the Persian Empire was the most elaborate government that had ever existed up to that point, and it was designed to function for centuries regardless of which particular king sat on the throne at any given time.

And the bureaucratic language of that apparatus — the everyday working language of the Persian Empire's administration — was **Elamite**.

This is the most remarkable fact about the relationship between Elam and Persia, and it is the fact that most general histories of the ancient world completely miss. The Persians who conquered Babylonia, Egypt, Lydia, Phoenicia, and half the Greek world did not, for most of their history, run their bureaucracy in their own Persian language. They ran it in Elamite. The Elamite-speaking scribes of the old Susa tradition, whose training and methods went back to the cuneiform adoption of the Shimashki dynasty nearly 1,500 years earlier, were employed by the new Persian emperors to manage the paperwork of the largest empire in the ancient world.

The evidence for this is overwhelming. It comes from two major archives, both of them among the most important documentary finds of the twentieth century.

---

The first archive is the **Persepolis Fortification Archive**.

**Persepolis** (per-SEH-po-lis, meaning "City of the Persians" in Greek — the actual name was Parsa) was the ceremonial capital of the Persian Empire from the time of Darius I onward, built around 515 BCE in the highland valleys of Fars about forty miles north of Shiraz. It was a spectacular complex of palaces and audience halls designed to impress foreign ambassadors and emphasize the grandeur of the Persian kingship. The site was visible from every direction, dominated by the massive fortification walls that surrounded it, and for nearly two hundred years it functioned as the administrative and ceremonial center of the empire. When Alexander the Great conquered the Persian Empire in 330 BCE, his army burned Persepolis in what may have been an accident or may have been a calculated act of revenge for the Persian burning of Athens 150 years earlier. The fire preserved a great deal of the site by carbonizing and hardening its archaeological remains. When modern archaeologists excavated the fortification walls in the 1930s under the leadership of Ernst Herzfeld and later Erich Schmidt of the University of Chicago's Oriental Institute, they made a discovery that transformed our understanding of Persian administration.

Inside the fortification walls, buried under rubble from the Alexandrine fire, were approximately **30,000 clay tablets and fragments** recording the daily economic activities of the Persian royal court over a 15-year period from 509 to 494 BCE — that is, the last decades of Darius I's reign. The tablets recorded things like grain rations issued to workers, traveling expenses of court officials, wages paid to craftsmen, food supplies sent between royal estates, and the daily logistics of running the Persian royal household. They were the archaeological equivalent of finding a file cabinet of expense reports and payroll records from the court of a medieval European monarch.

The critical fact about the Persepolis Fortification Archive is that **the tablets are in Elamite**. Not in Old Persian (the language Cyrus and Darius spoke at home). Not in Aramaic (the Semitic lingua franca that was becoming the imperial chancellery language). In Elamite, written in the same cuneiform scribal tradition that had been the Elamite royal standard since the Shimashki dynasty of 2000 BCE. The scribes who wrote the Fortification tablets were trained Elamite-cuneiform specialists, and the tablets use vocabulary, grammatical forms, and scribal conventions that are direct continuations of the old Elamite royal chancery.

Here is what this means, concretely. In 509 BCE, roughly 140 years after Ashurbanipal had sacked Susa and carried off the bones of the Elamite kings, a royal scribe at Persepolis sat down with a clay tablet and wrote, in Elamite cuneiform, a ration receipt for so many liters of barley issued to so many workers on the royal estate. That scribe was working for the Persian Empire, under the authority of the Persian king Darius I. He was writing in the language of the supposedly-destroyed kingdom, using the writing system of the supposedly-destroyed kingdom, following the administrative conventions of the supposedly-destroyed kingdom. Elam had been politically dead for almost a century and a half. Elam was still running the paperwork.

How was this possible? The answer is that when the Persians took over the old Elamite administrative region, they inherited an existing scribal training system that was producing new Elamite-cuneiform scribes every generation. The scribal schools at Susa and Anshan had not stopped operating just because the old Elamite royal family had been destroyed. They kept training new scribes, and those scribes kept being employed — first by the late Neo-Elamite local kings, then by the incoming Persian nobility who found them useful and hired them. By the time Cyrus conquered Babylon, the Persian royal court at Anshan had been staffed by Elamite-cuneiform scribes for several generations already. When Cyrus and his successors expanded the Persian Empire to cover the whole Near East, they took their existing staff of Elamite scribes with them and put them to work managing the new territories. The Persian Empire used Elamite as its bureaucratic language because that was what its bureaucrats already spoke and wrote.

Over time, the balance shifted. During the reign of Darius's successor Xerxes I (486–465 BCE) and especially under later Achaemenid kings, **Aramaic** became more common as the everyday administrative language of the empire, because Aramaic was the dominant spoken language of the Semitic-speaking populations of Babylonia and Syria who made up the single largest subject group inside the empire. By the middle of the fifth century BCE, most routine correspondence was being conducted in Aramaic rather than Elamite. But the Elamite scribal tradition continued alongside the Aramaic one, handling high-prestige documents and specific court functions, for another century or more after that. Elamite-cuneiform tablets are still being produced in Persia as late as the middle of the fourth century BCE, when Alexander the Great arrived and the whole apparatus finally broke down. That is a language tradition that stretches almost 2,800 years continuously from its first Proto-Elamite tablets around 3100 BCE to its final Achaemenid chancery use around 330 BCE — among the longest continuous scribal traditions in the ancient world. (For context, Egyptian hieroglyphics ran slightly longer, from ~3200 BCE to the fifth century CE, and Chinese script, running from the Shang oracle bones ~1200 BCE to the present day, is the longest of all. Elamite belongs in the same rare club: scribal traditions that survived across multiple political collapses and linguistic transitions because they had become embedded in the deep training pipelines of their region's institutions.)

---

The second archive is the **Behistun Inscription**.

**Behistun** (bee-HIS-toon — the Persian name is Bisotun, bee-soh-TOON) is a site in western Iran, along the old royal road connecting Babylon to the Iranian plateau, about 60 miles (100 km) east of modern Kermanshah. At Behistun, a tall cliff rises dramatically above the road, and on the face of the cliff — about 200 feet (60 meters) above the ground, visible to every traveler passing below — the Persian king Darius I carved an enormous trilingual inscription commemorating his rise to power, his defeat of internal rivals, and the establishment of his authority over the empire. The inscription was carved around 520 BCE, a few years into Darius's reign, and it is one of the most important single documents of the whole ancient Near East.

The inscription is trilingual: it exists in three versions, carved side by side on the cliff face, in three different languages: **Old Persian** (the everyday spoken language of Darius and his immediate court, which at that point had only recently developed its own cuneiform-based writing system specifically for this kind of monumental inscription), **Babylonian** (the form of Akkadian used in first-millennium Mesopotamia, the most prestigious imperial-literary language of the Near East), and **Elamite**.

The fact that Elamite is one of the three languages carved on the cliff tells us a great deal about its status in the Persian Empire around 520 BCE. Elamite was not a dead language of a destroyed kingdom. It was one of the three politically necessary languages for a Persian king addressing his subjects — alongside his own Old Persian and the culturally dominant Babylonian. All three languages had to be there for the inscription to do its political work, and the Elamite version was written first — it was the first of the three versions to be carved on the cliff, and the other two were added afterward. That chronological priority is telling. When Darius commissioned the inscription, he commissioned the Elamite version first, because Elamite was the working language of his imperial bureaucracy and the natural default for expressing the royal message to the kingdom's scribal-administrative class.

The Behistun inscription is also the most important single document in the history of deciphering cuneiform. When European scholars in the early nineteenth century began trying to decipher the cuneiform writing systems of the ancient Near East, they had three unknowns — three mostly-unknown languages written in related but distinct cuneiform scripts — and almost no bilingual texts to work from. Behistun provided the first major bilingual anchor. The Old Persian version of the inscription was simpler than the other two (the Persian cuneiform system was a mostly alphabetic script rather than a fully logographic one) and could be worked out first through careful linguistic analysis. The breakthrough came in 1837 when the British army officer Henry Rawlinson, climbing up the cliff face on ropes to make paper impressions of the inscriptions, produced the first reliable copies of the Old Persian text. Working from those copies, and building on earlier decipherment efforts by the German scholar Georg Friedrich Grotefend, Rawlinson and his colleagues were able to decipher Old Persian cuneiform by the 1840s. Once Old Persian was readable, the Babylonian cuneiform version of Behistun became effectively a Rosetta Stone for Babylonian Akkadian — which was then used as a Rosetta Stone for Sumerian and eventually for all the other cuneiform scripts of the ancient Near East.

The Elamite version of Behistun was deciphered along with the other two, though with more difficulty because Elamite has no living relatives to cross-reference. Modern readings of the Elamite text are still being refined, and a few phrases remain uncertain. But the broad content is clear: the text tells the same story as the Old Persian and Babylonian versions, describes Darius's victory over his rivals and his establishment of Persian rule, and uses the phrase "king of Anshan" (in the Elamite version, the Elamite phrase is written directly rather than being a Persian loan) as part of Darius's royal titulature.

Darius, like Cyrus before him, was a king of Anshan. The Elamite royal tradition ran through the whole Achaemenid period as a live, active, formally recognized element of Persian imperial self-presentation. The Persians never pretended that the old Elamite kingdom was an alien thing they had replaced. They presented themselves, officially, as its heirs.

---

Here is the final, underappreciated fact of this whole book:

**Elam never actually died.**

What died in 646 BCE, when Ashurbanipal sacked Susa and carried off the bones of the Elamite kings, was a specific political entity — the late Neo-Elamite kingdom that had descended from Shutruk-Nahhunte's imperial state through three and a half centuries of decline and fragmentation. That kingdom was finished. No new Elamite royal line would ever again rule an independent Elamite state after Ashurbanipal's destruction.

But the civilization continued. The language kept being spoken across southwestern Iran for several more centuries, probably as late as the middle of the first millennium CE in some form. The scribal tradition kept producing cuneiform tablets in Elamite for the Persian Empire's administration until the fourth century BCE. The royal title "king of Anshan" kept being used by the Achaemenid dynasty as one of their core legitimizing titles all the way down to Alexander the Great's conquest. The pantheon — Humban, Kiririsha, Nahhunte, Inshushinak, Pinikir — kept being worshipped in local Elamite temples for centuries after the fall of Susa, and the god Humban's name kept appearing in personal names (as a theophoric element) into the Parthian period of the second century BCE. The city of Susa itself was rebuilt after Ashurbanipal's destruction, became an Achaemenid administrative center, was visited regularly by the Persian kings, was later occupied by Alexander the Great's successors, survived into the Parthian and Sasanian periods, and was still a functioning city when the Muslim conquest of Iran began in the seventh century CE. Susa was continuously inhabited for over six thousand years — from the earliest fourth-millennium BCE village to the medieval Islamic period — one of the longest-running urban continuities in human history.

The Persian Empire we remember from Herodotus and the Hebrew Bible, the empire that fought the Greeks at Marathon and the empire that Alexander the Great conquered, was built on an Elamite foundation. It used Elamite language, Elamite scribal methods, Elamite administrative vocabulary, Elamite royal legitimacy, and Elamite religious continuity. It treated the Elamite heritage not as a conquered enemy's history but as its own deep ancestral past. Cyrus and Darius and Xerxes and all the later Achaemenid kings were, in their own official self-presentation, the legitimate heirs of the Elamite kings whose sequence reached all the way back to the Proto-Elamite period.

And when Alexander the Great, at the head of his Macedonian army, burned Persepolis in 330 BCE, he was unknowingly destroying an imperial capital that was the direct administrative descendant of the scribal tradition that had begun at Susa in 2000 BCE. The cuneiform tablets that burned in the Persepolis fire were Elamite in language, in script, in scribal convention, and in bureaucratic lineage. The Elamite civilization had outlasted the Elamite kingdom by nearly three centuries, embedded inside the Persian imperial state as its working bureaucratic backbone. Only with Alexander's arrival did the chain finally break.

Even then, something survived. The Elamite language is last attested in documentary form in a few late Parthian-era tablets from the first century BCE, by which point it was a regional Iranian language on the edge of extinction. Somewhere around the turn of the first millennium CE, the last native speakers stopped speaking it, and Elamite became a dead language for the first time in its roughly three-thousand-year history as a spoken tongue. It left behind no living descendants — it is a language isolate even in death — but it also left behind one of the most thoroughly documented ancient civilizations in the Near East, because the Persian Empire's use of Elamite as its administrative language produced such an enormous documentary archive that modern scholarship has been able to reconstruct the language in unusual detail.

The bones Ashurbanipal carried off from Susa have never been recovered. They were presumably interred or destroyed somewhere in Assyria after the sack of Nineveh in 612 BCE, and the ritual harm Ashurbanipal had intended to inflict on the ghosts of the Elamite kings was indeed accomplished, as much as such things can be accomplished. But the civilization whose kings those bones had been finished the job of outlasting the kingdom that destroyed them. When a Persian scribe in 509 BCE wrote "give thirty liters of barley to the workers at the royal estate" in Elamite cuneiform, he was continuing a scribal tradition that his teacher had learned from his teacher back through an unbroken chain of Elamite scribal instruction. The ghosts of the Elamite kings did not find rest, but their language and their scribes and their administrative system lived on as the skeleton of the largest empire the ancient world had ever built.

That is the actual ending of the Elamite story. Not "destroyed by Ashurbanipal in 646 BCE." Destroyed and re-absorbed and re-emerged and kept running for three more centuries as the imperial bureaucracy of Persia, visible in thirty thousand clay tablets at Persepolis and in the middle carved version of a trilingual royal inscription on a cliff at Behistun.

# Ancient China — The Dawn of Chinese Civilization

**Goal:** Narrative for the first TL in the Chinese Dynasties chain. Covers 7000–1600 BCE — the Neolithic river valley cultures, jade ritual, walled towns, and the legendary Xia Dynasty that gave way to the Shang. Like Indus Valley, this is a pre-textual era: almost everything comes from archaeology, supplemented by much-later Chinese traditions about legendary sage-kings. Every event from the TL data woven into the prose, plus the broader context needed for a reader with zero prior knowledge of Chinese geography, language, or mythology.

# Chapter 1 — Before China

Here is a sentence that sounds simple and isn't: **China has always been China.**

It sounds simple because China — the place on the map, the culture on TV, the country of a billion-plus people — seems like one of the oldest, most continuous civilizations on earth. You can point to the Great Wall and the Forbidden City and a written language that first-graders in Beijing learn today using characters whose ancestors were carved on cattle bones three and a half thousand years ago. Chinese civilization feels like a single long river, flowing unbroken from the beginning of recorded time to the present.

It isn't simple because, if you rewind far enough, there is no China. There is no country, no emperor, no writing, no rice paddies, no Great Wall, no silk. There are only river valleys, mountains, forests, and bands of people hunting deer and picking wild grasses. This is the story of how those river valleys became the country we now call China — not in the age of Confucius or the First Emperor, but thousands of years earlier, in the muddy villages where the recognizable pieces of Chinese civilization were assembled for the first time: rice, millet, jade, dragons, ancestors, walls, kings, and the idea that Heaven chooses the ruler.

It is, in many ways, a stranger story than Mesopotamia's or Egypt's. Those civilizations left cuneiform tablets and hieroglyphic walls — the people who built them wrote down their own names. The people who built Neolithic China left no writing we can confidently read, only a handful of scratched symbols that may or may not be words. Almost everything we know about them, we know from the dirt they left behind: their pots, their burials, their tools, the post-holes of their houses, and — more than anything else — their jade. This is a civilization rediscovered from the ground up, thousands of years after its oldest layer was laid down, by archaeologists who are still arguing about what the symbols mean and where the boundary sits between myth and history.

Before we meet the farmers and the jade workers, though, we have to meet the land. Chinese civilization is a story about rivers, and to understand what happened along them you need to know why the rivers are there, why they matter, and why two of them — not one — became the cradles of everything that followed.

---

If you stand on a very tall mountain in western China and look east, the land falls away in three giant steps, like a staircase built for a giant.

The top step is the **Tibetan Plateau** — the highest and largest plateau on Earth, averaging about 14,800 feet (4,500 meters) above sea level, roughly the altitude of the base camp at Everest. Most of it is cold, thin-aired grassland, too high and dry for farming. The plateau is hemmed in by the **Himalayas** to the south (the world's tallest mountain range, which blocks the Indian monsoon from pushing further north) and the **Kunlun Mountains** to the north (a huge range stretching into the deserts of modern Xinjiang, a province in China's far west). Almost nothing grows on the plateau itself, but it is the water tower of Asia: the snowmelt from its ice fields feeds nearly every great river in eastern and southeastern Asia, including the Mekong, the Brahmaputra, the Salween — and, most importantly for this story, the two rivers that would give birth to Chinese civilization.

The second step is a mid-altitude zone of mountains, basins, and plateaus running through central China — cold, rugged, and in places thickly forested. This is where the water from the plateau picks up speed, cuts through canyons, and begins to do the work of shaping the landscape.

The third and lowest step is the great plains of eastern China, stretching from the foot of the mountains out to the Pacific Ocean. This is where the two rivers finish their journey, where they deposit enormous quantities of silt, and where — once farming arrives — some of the most fertile land on the planet supports dense human populations. Everything in this book happens on the second and third steps. The top step belongs to the sky.

The two rivers are the **Yellow River** (in Chinese, **Huang He** — HWONG HUH, though the English name is what the rest of the world uses) and the **Yangtze** (YANG-tsee — in Chinese, **Chang Jiang**, meaning "Long River"). They are the reason Chinese civilization exists, and they are as different from each other as two great rivers can possibly be.

The Yellow River is the northern river. It is about 3,400 miles (5,500 km) long, making it the second-longest river in China. Its source is on the Tibetan Plateau; it curves north, makes a huge horseshoe bend around the **Ordos Desert** (an arid steppe region in what is now the Inner Mongolia region), then swings east and south through the middle of northern China before emptying into the Bohai Sea. Along most of its middle and lower course, the Yellow River runs through a region called the **Loess Plateau** (LESS — "loess" is a type of very fine, wind-blown silt), and the river gets its color, and its name, from this soil.

Here's where it gets interesting. Loess is an extraordinary material. It was deposited across northern China over hundreds of thousands of years by wind blowing fine dust off the Central Asian deserts during the ice ages. In some parts of Shanxi and Shaanxi provinces (in north-central China, west of the modern capital Beijing), the loess is 300 feet (90 meters) deep — the thickest loess deposits anywhere on Earth. It is fine, soft, and fertile enough that a person with a wooden digging stick can plant a crop in it. You do not need a plow, oxen, or iron tools. A community with sharp sticks and strong backs can farm loess, which turns out to matter enormously for who got to start farming first.

But loess also has a dark side. It is loose and easily eroded, which is why the Yellow River looks yellow: it is carrying a vast load of silt washed out of the plateau upstream. In some places, the river carries more sediment per gallon than any other major river in the world. When it reaches the flat plains of eastern China, the water slows down and the silt drops out. Over centuries, the river raises its own bed, piling up silt higher and higher between its banks until it is actually flowing above the surrounding countryside. Then the banks give way, and the river jumps out of its channel and finds a new path to the sea — sometimes hundreds of miles from the old one, drowning cities and villages in the process. The Yellow River has changed its course at least twenty-six times in recorded history, and several of those changes killed millions of people. The traditional Chinese name for the Yellow River is not a term of affection: it is **"China's Sorrow."**

This is the river that would support millet farmers, walled towns, the Xia and Shang dynasties, and the idea that the fundamental job of a legitimate ruler is flood control.

The Yangtze is a different animal entirely. It is longer (about 3,900 miles / 6,300 km — the longest river in Asia and the third-longest in the world), wetter (fed by both snowmelt and the much heavier rains of subtropical southern China), and slower-paced in its lower course. It begins on the Tibetan Plateau, flows east through deep canyons in Sichuan province (the **Three Gorges** — spectacular and unforgiving gorges now flooded by a massive modern dam), widens out across the central Chinese plains, and empties into the East China Sea near modern Shanghai. Along most of its course through central and eastern China, it runs through a warmer, wetter world than the Yellow River — a world of lakes, marshes, floodplains, bamboo, and dense subtropical forest.

The Yangtze does not carry the apocalyptic silt loads of the Yellow River. It does not routinely change course and erase cities. It is a more forgiving river. And critically for this story, the climate along its lower course is warm enough, and wet enough, to grow rice in standing water. The Yangtze delta and lower basin became the cradle of the other great Chinese agricultural revolution: rice paddies, wet farming, fish and waterfowl, stilt houses on marshes.

Two rivers. Two climates. Two soils. Two sets of wild plants. Two utterly different ways of being human on the same ancient landscape. If you remember only one fact about Chinese prehistory, make it this one: **ancient China had two cradles, not one.** The north and the south were doing different things at the same time, and when they eventually blended, the combination was what gave Chinese civilization its distinctive shape.

Between the two river basins sat a world of mountains, hills, and smaller river valleys — the central Chinese plains and the uplands of Henan, Shanxi, and Shaanxi provinces. **Henan** (HEH-nahn, meaning "south of the river" — specifically south of the Yellow River) in particular will come up constantly in this story: more major ancient Chinese sites have been dug up in Henan than in any other province on earth. The site of the first Chinese oracle bones, the earliest known Chinese palace complex, the oldest flutes in the world, and several of the founding villages of Chinese civilization all lie within an area smaller than the US state of Ohio. Henan is the elbow where the Yellow River turns east on its final run to the sea, and it is where the northern, southern, and western cultural worlds rubbed against each other and exchanged ideas, tools, and — eventually — wives, warriors, and gods.

Around the edges of this core zone were things to keep an eye on. To the north, the **Mongolian steppe** — vast grasslands inhabited by mobile hunting and (later) herding peoples who would loom larger and larger in Chinese history as time went on. To the northeast, the forested lowlands of modern Liaoning province and Inner Mongolia, where a remarkable culture called the Hongshan would make the world's first dragon images out of jade (Chapter 4). To the west, the deserts of Xinjiang and the passes leading into Central Asia, from which would eventually come tin, horses, chariots, and Buddhism. To the east and southeast, the Pacific coast and the offshore islands — Taiwan, the Ryukyus, and eventually Japan — which in deep prehistory received migrations of rice farmers heading out to sea. And to the south, the subtropical forests of what is now Fujian, Guangdong, and Vietnam, which in this chapter are still mostly outside the story but will slowly get pulled into it over the millennia that follow.

This is the stage. Now for the actors.

---

For most of the period covered by this book — from about 7000 BCE to 1600 BCE — we are looking at a thin crust of settled villagers farming small plots on the edges of rivers and marshes, surrounded by a vaster, older world of hunter-gatherers. The villagers are going to get all of our attention because they are the ones who invented jade ritual and dragon kings and flood-controlling sage emperors. But it is worth a moment to say who was here first, and who was here alongside them.

The **last glacial period** — the most recent ice age, which locked up enormous quantities of fresh water in polar ice caps and dropped global sea levels by hundreds of feet — began to retreat around 20,000 years ago, and by 10,000 BCE the world was warming rapidly. In East Asia, this meant milder winters, heavier summer rains, longer growing seasons, and the return of forests to regions that had been cold steppe during the depths of the ice age. Sea levels rose and cut off the land bridges that had once connected the Chinese mainland to Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. Coastlines moved hundreds of miles inland in some places — much of the modern **North China Plain** (the low, flat region around Beijing and Tianjin) was under shallow sea during the warmest period of the postglacial climate, around 6000 BCE, and only reappeared as the oceans stabilized and silt piled up.

The people who were alive in these changing landscapes were, as far as anyone can tell, anatomically modern humans — our species — who had arrived in East Asia at least 40,000 years earlier, long before the ice age ended. They were hunter-gatherers, living in small mobile bands, making their tools from stone, bone, and wood. Their sites tend to be rock shelters and open camps rather than permanent villages, and they left few burials, so they are harder to study than the farmers who come later. But they were here, they were skilled at exploiting a wide range of environments, and — crucially — they were the people who first noticed that certain wild grasses in certain river valleys produced unusually large and unusually tasty seeds. The farmers did not drop in from the sky. They grew, over thousands of years, out of hunter-gatherer communities whose ancestors had been watching and eating wild millet and wild rice for dozens of generations before anyone ever thought to deliberately plant them.

We do not know most of their names for themselves, because they did not write. We know a little about what they looked like (genetically, they were closely related to the modern populations of East and Southeast Asia), a little about what they ate (wild deer, wild pig, fish, tubers, nuts, and the aforementioned wild grains), and very little about what they thought. But we know the moment their world changed, because the archaeology of the changed world is unmistakable. Around 7000 BCE, in two very different river valleys separated by almost 600 miles (1,000 km), human beings stopped just gathering wild grain and started planting it. And from the moment that happened, China was no longer just a landscape. It was the beginning of a story.

That story starts in the next chapter, with two grains — one in the north, one in the south — and a village called **Jiahu** (JEE-ah-HOO), where a farmer nine thousand years ago carved a tortoise shell with marks that some scholars think might be the world's first writing, and where, in the same burial ground, archaeologists found a bone flute, crafted from the wing of a red-crowned crane, that still plays a recognizable scale when a modern musician puts it to their lips.

Nine thousand years later, we are still listening.

# Chapter 2 — Two Grains, Two Worlds

Around 7000 BCE, something happened in East Asia that had only happened a handful of times before in the whole history of our species: people stopped being hunter-gatherers and started being farmers. It didn't happen in one place. It didn't even happen in one river valley. It happened, independently and almost simultaneously, in two river valleys, on two different grains, under two different climates — and the long echo of that split would shape Chinese civilization all the way to the present day.

We need to start with the word **Neolithic**. It comes from Greek and means "New Stone Age" — a label invented by 19th-century European archaeologists for the era when people were still making their tools out of stone (because metal hadn't been invented yet) but had started farming, settling in villages, making pottery, and generally behaving in ways that would look recognizable to modern eyes. The key thing that defines the Neolithic isn't the stone tools; it's the farming. A Neolithic community grows its own food, stores it for later, and stays in one place long enough to build a house that isn't a tent. Almost everything else that we think of as human civilization — property, inheritance, organized religion, cities, kings, taxes — grew out of that single change.

Around 7000 BCE in China, that change hit two places at once.

---

The northern cradle was the **Yellow River valley** — specifically the loess plateau and the middle reaches of the river, in what is now Henan and Hebei provinces (the two provinces whose names mean "south of the river" and "north of the river" respectively, in both cases referring to the Yellow River). The people here, the earliest farming communities we can identify from the ground, are called the **Peiligang culture** (pay-lee-GAHNG). We do not know what the Peiligang people called themselves. "Peiligang" is simply the name of the modern village near the first site where archaeologists dug up one of their settlements, and the label got attached to everything that looked similar — their pottery, their tools, their burials, their pit houses — all across the region.

The Peiligang were farmers of **millet**, not rice. Millet is a catch-all English name for a group of small-seeded grasses that were domesticated in various parts of the world for their hardy, drought-resistant grain. Two species matter for Chinese history: **foxtail millet** (*Setaria italica*) and **broomcorn millet** (*Panicum miliaceum*). Both grow wild in the Yellow River loess plains; both have small, starchy seeds that can be ground into flour, boiled into porridge, or fermented into beer; both will grow in soil too dry and too cool for rice. For the next four or five thousand years, millet — not rice, not wheat — would be the staple grain of northern Chinese civilization. Shang dynasty nobles drank millet wine. Han dynasty soldiers were paid in millet. Tang dynasty granaries were full of millet. Its dominance begins here, on the loess plateau, with Peiligang farmers working the soil with stone sickles and grinding slabs.

What a Peiligang village looked like: several dozen semi-subterranean pit houses (essentially round or rectangular pits dug a few feet into the loess and roofed over with thatched poles, a form of construction that was warm in winter and easy to build in the soft fine soil), a scattering of storage pits for grain, a separate area for burials, a kiln for firing simple pottery, and a hundred or two hundred people. No walls. No temples. No rulers we can identify. But enough food stored in the pits to last the winter — and enough surplus, by the time the culture was a few centuries old, to support craftsmanship and trade.

A few hundred miles to the north, in the foothills of the **Taihang Mountains** (tie-hahng, a major north-south range in modern Hebei province), was a closely related culture called the **Cishan** (tsuh-SHAHN). The Cishan people farmed the same millets, lived in the same kinds of pit houses, and used the same kinds of tools as the Peiligang. The reason to mention them separately is an archaeological discovery that still startles scholars: at the Cishan type site, archaeologists found **88 underground grain storage pits** containing the carbonized remains of over **50,000 kilograms (roughly 110,000 pounds) of millet**. That is, to put it mildly, a *lot* of grain. It is the earliest evidence of large-scale grain storage anywhere in the world. Whatever Cishan was — a single extended family community, a village of a few hundred, a communal feasting center — it was producing surpluses many, many times larger than the immediate daily needs of the people who lived there. Somebody at Cishan was planning ahead, organizing labor, and probably redistributing food. That is the economic signature of emerging social complexity: not kings yet, but something in motion.

---

About 370 miles (600 km) south of Peiligang, in the warm, wet marshes of the **Yangtze River valley**, a different set of people were domesticating a different crop. This is the southern cradle, and the crop is **rice**.

**Rice** is a cultivated descendant of a wild grass called *Oryza rufipogon* that still grows in the swamps and shallow lakes of the Yangtze basin. The wild grasses drop their seeds when the plants mature — a perfectly sensible behavior from the plant's point of view, since it's how the species reproduces, but a nightmare for anyone trying to harvest the grain, because by the time you get to a stand of wild rice, most of the seeds are already on the ground. Domesticated rice, by contrast, holds its seeds on the stalk until a human comes along and cuts them off. That single genetic change — a mutation that happens occasionally in any wild population — is the difference between a grass you can gather and a grass you can farm. Sometime around 7000 BCE, probably over the course of many generations of selective harvesting and replanting, Yangtze valley communities pushed wild rice through that change and turned it into the crop we know today.

The earliest rice paddies yet found in the world are in China. Sites like **Kuahuqiao** (KWAH-hoo-chow) and **Shangshan** (shahng-SHAHN) in the lower Yangtze preserve storage pits full of carbonized rice, stone tools used for harvesting and processing grain, and in some places the distinct earthen banks and flooded fields of actual paddy agriculture. By 5000 BCE, the Yangtze delta was dotted with rice-farming communities using wooden tools and living in thatched houses built on stilts to keep them above the marsh. The crop that would one day feed more than half of humanity began as an experimental harvest in these Chinese swamps.

So by about 6500 BCE, you have a remarkable situation. In the north, farmers are growing dry-field millet on loess plains, storing it in underground pits, and living in warm winters-and-cool summers pit houses. In the south, farmers are growing wet-field rice in flooded paddies, eating fish and waterfowl from the surrounding marshes, and living on stilts above the standing water. The climates are different, the soils are different, the tools are different, the diets are different. The people are not yet one culture, not even close. But both sets are doing something that almost no one else in the world is doing yet: they are Neolithic farmers in the full modern sense, producing their own food on their own land and settling down to live with the consequences.

---

Between the two cradles, a third site deserves a chapter of its own, because what was found there is out of proportion to almost anything else from this era. The site is **Jiahu** (jee-ah-HOO), in central Henan province, excavated in the 1980s and 1990s and gradually recognized as one of the most extraordinary Neolithic sites on Earth.

Jiahu was a rice-farming village — interesting in itself, because it is about halfway up the Yellow River basin, well north of what we usually think of as the rice-growing zone, and suggests that the north-south grain line was fuzzier than the map makes it look. The Jiahu people raised rice, kept pigs and dogs, fished in a nearby lake, and buried their dead in a cemetery on the edge of the settlement. The cemetery turned out to be full of surprises.

The first surprise was the **Jiahu bone flutes**. In 1986, archaeologists excavating Jiahu burials began finding long, slender bone objects with rows of small holes drilled into them — five holes, six holes, seven holes, drilled with a precision that was clearly not accidental. Chemical analysis identified the material as the wing bones of the **red-crowned crane**, a large migratory bird that still nests in eastern Chinese wetlands. Careful examination showed that the holes were spaced in ways that produce intervals our ears would recognize as a musical scale. And then someone did the obvious experiment: they made a modern copy of one of the flutes, brought in a musician, and asked the musician to play.

The Jiahu flutes are **the oldest playable musical instruments ever found** — and playable is the key word. Several of them, when reproduced using the same materials and techniques, still produce clear, controlled tones. One intact original, approximately **nine thousand years old**, produces a recognizable pentatonic or diatonic scale depending on how you finger it. A modern musician can pick up a replica of a Jiahu flute and, within minutes, be playing a tune that fits comfortably into what a present-day Chinese listener would recognize as Chinese musical tradition. The implications are staggering. It means that music, as a formal structured art — with intervals, scales, and instruments designed for precise tuning — was part of village life in China from the very beginning of farming. These were not primitive bone whistles. They were instruments.

And then there were the symbols.

In the same Jiahu cemetery, archaeologists found tortoise shells and bones bearing small, incised marks — not accidental scratches, but deliberately cut symbols, 11 or more of them identifiable as distinct recurring forms. The shells were mostly in elite burials: richer graves with more offerings, suggesting that whoever carried tortoise shells in their grave was someone important. A handful of the Jiahu symbols, when you squint at them, look a bit like characters from the **oracle bone script** — the earliest undisputed form of Chinese writing, used by the Shang dynasty about 5,000 years later to record divinations on cattle bones and turtle shells. It is the same material (turtle shell), the same kind of context (ritual, elite, associated with spiritual practice), and at least some of the same symbolic forms.

This is where the scholars start arguing. One school of thought — dominant among some Chinese archaeologists — holds that the Jiahu symbols represent the earliest form of Chinese writing, predating Mesopotamian cuneiform (the wedge-shaped writing developed in what is now Iraq) by several thousand years. A more cautious school, common among Western scholars, argues that 11 symbols used in ritual contexts do not yet constitute a writing system — they might be clan marks, ownership marks, counting tallies, or purely symbolic gestures with no linguistic content. Nobody can prove it either way, because there simply isn't enough material to say whether the Jiahu marks encode spoken language. What is clear is that 9,000 years ago, somebody in central China was already treating incised symbols on turtle shells as meaningful enough to put them in burials — and that the same material was going to be used for the same kind of ritual purpose by the Shang, on the same kind of shells, thousands of years later. Whether it's "writing" or not, it is the earliest ghost of a thread that runs straight through Chinese history.

The Jiahu site gives us a glimpse of what the Neolithic was actually like when you get past the potsherds and dig down to the people. These were not grunting proto-humans eking out a bare existence. They were farmers who played music, buried their dead with care, carved symbols on sacred objects, and had already started using the materials and gestures that would define Chinese civilization for another five thousand years. They had rice paddies and flutes and turtle-shell divinations in the same village, and they were doing all of this while the Egyptian pyramids were still four and a half thousand years in the future.

By around 5000 BCE, the Peiligang and Cishan cultures in the north had spread and diversified into a larger, brighter cultural complex that would take over the entire middle Yellow River valley. The Yangtze farmers expanded their paddies and built more sophisticated villages. Jiahu itself was abandoned — the reasons are unclear, possibly local climate shift — but its traditions of music, ritual, and symbol-making didn't die. They moved on. And the next wave of villages, bigger and more ambitious, was about to start painting pottery in colors so vivid that modern museums still line them up in glass cases and turn down the lights.

# Chapter 3 — The Painted Pottery World

By 5000 BCE, the scattered farming communities of the early Neolithic had been replaced by something much more impressive. The villages were bigger. The pottery was better. The communities traded with each other across longer distances. Craft specialization — people spending most of their time making pots, or stone tools, or jade ornaments, instead of farming — was becoming possible because some villages could now produce enough surplus food to feed a few full-time craftspeople. And over most of the northern Chinese agricultural zone, a single cultural tradition emerged that would dominate the region for the next two thousand years. It is called the **Yangshao culture** (yahng-SHAO), and it is the first phase of Chinese prehistory where you can start pointing to things and saying: *that is going to be Chinese forever*.

Yangshao takes its name from the village near which its defining pottery was first found in 1921 — an important date, because the discovery of Yangshao was the moment modern archaeology realized that China had its own Neolithic, independent of Mesopotamia and Egypt. The great prehistorian **Johan Gunnar Andersson**, a Swede working for the Chinese government, excavated a site in western Henan province and turned up a cache of beautifully painted red pottery that turned out to be more than six thousand years old. Before Andersson, it was quite possible to believe — as many early 20th-century European scholars did — that Chinese civilization had been imported from the Near East. Yangshao killed that idea. It was indisputably local, indisputably ancient, and beautiful enough that it made the front pages of Chinese newspapers.

The hallmark of Yangshao culture is its **painted pottery**. Before firing, Yangshao potters coated their vessels with a reddish-brown slip and then painted bold geometric designs — spirals, triangles, zigzags, net patterns — along with stylized fish, frogs, deer, and, most strikingly, human faces with fish attached to them. The "human face and fish" motif, which shows up on dozens of Yangshao bowls, is one of the iconic images of Chinese prehistory: a round, flat face with curved fishes where the ears should be, framed by geometric borders. Scholars argue about what it means. A totem? A shamanic image? A representation of a water god? A child's toy? Nobody knows, but the design is so specific and so repeatable that it must have meant something important to the people who painted it, over and over, for a thousand years.

What makes Yangshao pottery historically significant isn't just that it's pretty. It's that the specific artistic vocabulary — the spirals, the animal faces, the geometric borders, the rhythm of decoration in bands around a vessel — would resurface, almost unchanged, on Shang dynasty bronze vessels two thousand years later. Chinese art has a very long memory, and its deep grammar starts in Yangshao.

---

The best-known Yangshao village is **Banpo** (BAHN-poh), on the outskirts of modern Xi'an in Shaanxi province. Banpo was occupied from roughly 4800 to 3750 BCE — over a thousand years of continuous village life — and when Chinese archaeologists excavated it in the 1950s, they found one of the most completely preserved Neolithic villages anywhere on earth.

Banpo is laid out in a way that looks almost planned. A central area held a large communal building, probably used for village meetings or shared storage. Surrounding it were smaller pit dwellings, each with its own hearth and storage pits. A dedicated kiln area on the edge of the village produced the painted pottery. A cemetery — with adults buried in one section and infants buried in pottery jars in another — sat outside the living area. And around the whole thing, the villagers dug a defensive **moat** — a trench several meters wide and deep. Whatever the Banpo people were worried about, it was organized enough that they invested in earthworks to keep it out.

Banpo is roughly the size of a modern small village — five hectares (about 12 acres), housing a few hundred people. But everything about it suggests a level of social organization beyond the bare Neolithic minimum. There are specialized craft zones. There is a communal building. There is a burial ground with differentiated treatment for different kinds of dead (adults outside, infants in jars near the houses, suggesting different beliefs about their spirits). There is a moat, which implies either defense against enemies or containment against predators, neither of which comes free. And — in one of the details that will echo through the rest of this story — there are incised symbols on some of the pottery.

The **Banpo pottery marks** are a puzzle. Over a hundred distinct geometric symbols have been catalogued on Yangshao vessel rims, mostly at Banpo and related Wei River valley sites. Some are simple — a vertical line, a plus sign, a circle. Some are more elaborate — linked diamonds, branching lines, curved hooks. They are small, consistent, and clearly made on purpose. But they are not yet a writing system. Nobody has been able to extract from them a grammar, a set of words, or a reading. The best guesses are that they were **ownership marks** (this pot belongs to family X), **clan symbols** (our lineage uses this sign), or **counting tallies** (this vessel holds five units). A few of the Banpo symbols look vaguely like later Chinese characters, but the resemblance is suggestive rather than conclusive.

What the Banpo marks *do* tell us is that the habit of marking meaningful things with symbols was already deeply embedded in Yangshao culture. Whatever these marks meant, they were part of a world in which people had started using visual signs to stand for things — a habit that, a few thousand years later, would crystallize into the Chinese writing system. The marks are not the beginning of Chinese writing. But they are part of the long prehistory of it. Combined with the Jiahu symbols (Chapter 2), they make it clear that Chinese civilization was groping toward writing for thousands of years before it finally arrived.

---

While Yangshao was dominating the Yellow River valley, a very different kind of culture was flourishing in the Yangtze delta — a culture that has to be explained separately because its world didn't look anything like Banpo's. This is the **Hemudu culture** (HUH-moo-doo), centered in modern Zhejiang province around the lower Yangtze marshes.

If you imagine Banpo — dusty loess soil, dry-farmed millet, pit houses, moats — then Hemudu is its opposite. Hemudu sat on a tidal flat near the mouth of the Yangtze. The ground was wet most of the year, the climate was subtropical, the local environment was a patchwork of rivers, lakes, and marshes. You couldn't dig pit houses here; the water table was too high. So the Hemudu people built their houses **on stilts**, above the water level, using wooden piles driven into the mud and a superstructure of thatched beams and mat walls. The style was essentially a longhouse raised on a wooden platform — the same basic architecture that villagers still use today in parts of Southeast Asia and Indonesia. The Hemudu site preserves the wooden joinery remarkably well because the waterlogged soil prevented it from rotting. Archaeologists have recovered **mortise-and-tenon joints** (a woodworking technique in which a protruding "tenon" fits into a matching hole, a "mortise," holding two timbers together without nails) that are as sophisticated as anything made two thousand years later.

Hemudu farmed rice, of course — vast fields of it, flooded paddies covering the tidal flats around the villages. They also kept domestic pigs, harvested fish, hunted deer, and maintained large groves of a native Chinese nut tree called the **water chestnut**. Their tools included plentiful bone and wooden implements — picks, spades, weavers' shuttles — that show a craft tradition as refined as the Yangshao pottery tradition but focused on completely different materials and needs. And, in one of the remarkable survivals at the site, Hemudu preserves the **earliest known Chinese lacquerwork**: wooden bowls painted with red lacquer (a hard, glossy coating made from the sap of the lacquer tree, *Toxicodendron vernicifluum*, which is native to central and southern China). Lacquerwork would become one of the great decorative arts of Chinese civilization, and its ancestry traces back to these Yangtze stilt-house villages seven thousand years ago.

Hemudu matters because it is the first clear sign that the Yangtze world was not just lagging behind the Yellow River world — it was doing its own thing, at its own pace, with its own materials and its own traditions. The north and the south of China, from almost the beginning of the Neolithic, were building two parallel versions of settled life. They would stay parallel for thousands of years. When they finally started to merge, the combination produced a richer and more varied civilization than either alone could have.

---

At some point in this era — impossible to date precisely, because it is technically not an event so much as a slow emergence — the Chinese started making **silk**.

The silkworm, *Bombyx mori*, is native to China and feeds exclusively on the leaves of the white mulberry tree. Its larvae spin cocoons of a single continuous filament of silk protein, which hardens into the threads we associate with luxury clothing. Wild silk moths have existed for millions of years in East Asia. Domesticating them — selecting for larger cocoons, feeding them on cultivated mulberry, unwinding the filaments to make thread and cloth — is a specifically Chinese achievement that was not matched anywhere else in the world for thousands of years.

Chinese legend dates the invention of silk to **Leizu** (LAY-dzoo), the wife of the Yellow Emperor (Chapter 6), who supposedly discovered silk when a cocoon fell into her tea cup and unraveled. Archaeology dates it earlier. Traces of silk fiber have been found at several late Yangshao and Liangzhu sites — a cocoon cut in half with a stone tool, recovered from Shanxi province, dates to around 3600 BCE, and actual woven silk fragments from the Liangzhu culture (Chapter 5) are almost as old. By about 3500 BCE, silk was already being produced and worked somewhere in Neolithic China. A technology that would eventually create the Silk Road and transform the global economy was already present, quietly, in these late Stone Age river valleys.

---

One more piece of this chapter is hard to pin down in time but impossible to leave out: **the legendary sage-kings**.

Much later Chinese texts — particularly the *Records of the Grand Historian* by **Sima Qian** (suh-MAH chee-EN), written around 100 BCE, and the *Book of Documents* (*Shujing*), parts of which are older — describe a series of semi-divine rulers who supposedly governed China before the first dynasties. These are the **Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors**, a rotating cast of figures whose names and exploits vary between sources but whose role in Chinese tradition is the same: they are the culture heroes who invented the arts of civilization. They are not history in any modern sense. No archaeological evidence connects them to actual individuals. But they are deeply embedded in Chinese self-understanding, and they represent how classical Chinese writers imagined the era we are living through in this chapter.

**Fuxi** (FOO-shee) is usually listed as the first of the Three Sovereigns. He is depicted as a half-human, half-serpent figure, sometimes shown intertwined with his sister-wife **Nüwa** (NOO-wah), and he is credited with inventing fishing nets, teaching animal domestication, and — most importantly — creating the **bagua** (BAH-gwah), the Eight Trigrams. The Eight Trigrams are an ancient divination system in which combinations of broken and unbroken lines encode the fundamental forces of nature (heaven, earth, fire, water, mountain, thunder, wind, lake). They became the foundation of the *I Ching* (the *Book of Changes*, an ancient Chinese philosophical and divinatory text that Confucius reportedly studied obsessively), and they still appear today in martial arts schools, feng shui practice, and the South Korean flag. Whether or not Fuxi was real, the idea he represents — the sage who imposes cosmic order on chaos through pattern recognition — is one of the oldest recurring themes in Chinese thought.

**Shennong** (shen-NONG — "the Divine Farmer") is usually listed next. He is depicted with a transparent belly that let him watch the effect of each plant he ate, which he did in the name of discovering which species were edible, which were medicinal, and which were poisonous. He supposedly invented the plow, taught crop cultivation, and catalogued hundreds of herbs before dying from a toxic plant he ate while testing it. The text associated with his name, the *Shennong Bencao Jing* (Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica), is the foundational text of traditional Chinese medicine. Shennong's story, like Fuxi's, is mythological, but it encodes a real truth: Chinese agricultural and medical knowledge accumulated over thousands of years of observation, trial, error, and — sometimes — lethal experimentation. Somebody had to figure out which plants were safe. Somebody did, over generations. Shennong is the mythological personification of that accumulated wisdom.

Chinese tradition places both Fuxi and Shennong somewhere in the fifth or sixth millennium BCE — in the middle of the Yangshao and Hemudu era we've been describing. Obviously they were not real people. But the Chinese placing of them in this specific era reflects an ancient Chinese intuition that something culturally important was happening right around then: villages were getting bigger, agriculture was getting better, medicine and ritual were becoming more elaborate, and the basic pattern of settled Chinese life was taking shape. Later generations needed names and stories to attach to that transformation. They invented them.

As the fourth millennium BCE gave way to the third, this world of painted pottery villages was about to get a jolt from an unexpected direction: a culture far to the northeast, in the forests and grasslands of what is now Liaoning and Inner Mongolia, was already carving the most extraordinary objects anyone in the Chinese Neolithic had yet produced, out of the most sacred material in Chinese history: **jade**.

---

# Chapter 4 — Jade and the Pig-Dragon

If you are reading a book about ancient China and you want to understand the culture in a single object, don't look at a bronze or a scroll. Look at a piece of jade.

**Jade** is not a single mineral. It's a catch-all word for two very different tough, green stones: **nephrite**, which is a variety of a calcium-magnesium-iron silicate mineral called tremolite-actinolite, and **jadeite**, a harder sodium-aluminum silicate. The Chinese word **yu** (pronounced "yoo") covers both and a handful of other similar green stones as well. In the period we're talking about in this book, the only jade the Chinese had access to was nephrite, sourced from deposits in the Kunlun Mountains of northwestern China (Chapter 1) and from lesser deposits in the northeast, near modern Liaoning province. Jadeite, the flashier Burmese green stone most Western museum-goers think of when they hear "jade," wouldn't arrive in China until almost 4,000 years later, during the Qing dynasty.

Nephrite jade has some peculiar physical properties that explain its cultural weight. It is **extraordinarily tough** — resistant to shattering in a way that even diamond isn't, because of the way its fibers interlock. It is hard enough that until the invention of metal tools, the only way to shape it was by slowly grinding it down with abrasive sand — a process that could take weeks or months per object. When polished, it takes on a deep greasy luster that is unlike any other stone. And when struck gently, it resonates with a musical tone that classical Chinese writers called **"the voice of virtue."** Jade is hard, enduring, pure, and beautiful — all the qualities that Chinese thought came to associate with the ideal human being. Later generations would explicitly identify jade with moral virtue. Zhou dynasty nobles wore jade pendants to signal their character. Han emperors were buried in suits stitched together from thousands of jade plaques, because the stone was believed to preserve the body and soul from decay.

All of that cultural weight traces back, ultimately, to the Neolithic. And the most important Neolithic jade-working culture was not in the Yellow River or the Yangtze valleys, but in a corner of northeastern China that doesn't usually get much billing: the grasslands and river valleys of **Liaoning** and **Inner Mongolia**. The culture is called **Hongshan** (hong-SHAHN, "Red Mountain"), and it changed the rules.

---

The Hongshan culture flourished from about 4700 to 2900 BCE — almost two thousand years — in a region that is today cold grassland and scrubby forest but was, in the warm postglacial climate, a more hospitable mix of marshes, lakes, and open woodland. The Hongshan people were farmers (millet again — they were part of the northern dry-farming tradition) but they seem to have been equally invested in hunting, gathering, and ritual. Their most famous site is **Niuheliang** (NYOO-huh-lee-ahng), in what is now western Liaoning, where archaeologists uncovered the earliest known Chinese **ritual landscape**: a series of platforms, stone-circle altars, and a structure now called the **Goddess Temple** — a ruined building that contained fragments of life-sized clay statues, including a famous fragmentary head with inset green jade eyes. The Niuheliang complex is not a village. It is a sacred precinct — the earliest one yet found in China — built on the tops of low hills, aligned with each other and with celestial features, and surrounded by elite burial grounds. It is evidence that by around 3500 BCE, the Hongshan people had specialists whose entire job was maintaining a ritual landscape: a priestly class, or at least a category of people whose work was sacred rather than agricultural.

And the burials at Niuheliang and related Hongshan sites contain the **jade**.

The Hongshan jade workers, using nothing but abrasive sand, wooden drills, and cords strung with grit, carved shapes that no one in the world was carving at the time. There are jade turtles, jade birds, jade fish, jade cloud-shaped ornaments, and small jade plaques pierced for suspension as pendants. There are **anthropomorphic figures** — carved humans with stylized features, some seated cross-legged, some with their arms folded across their chests. But the most famous Hongshan jades are the ones that aren't quite anything: C-shaped curled creatures with pig-like snouts, slit eyes, and coiled serpentine bodies. They are called **zhulong** (JOO-long), which translates literally as **"pig-dragon,"** and they are the earliest dragon images yet found anywhere in Chinese art.

---

The pig-dragon needs a moment of its own, because of what it eventually becomes.

The object itself is small — typically 2 to 10 inches long (5 to 25 cm) — and simple in outline: a curled body forming most of a circle, a pig-like head at one end, a tail at the other, and sometimes a hole drilled through the center for suspension. They were found in elite Hongshan burials, laid on the chests of the deceased or around their heads. What they were *for* is a matter of educated guessing. The pig and the dragon were two of the most important mythological creatures in later Chinese thought — the pig as the embodiment of domestic wealth, fertility, and the earth; the dragon as the embodiment of water, rain, and cosmic power. Combining them in a single coiled object may have been the Hongshan way of invoking both at once: fertility, rain, and the promise of a continuing cycle of rebirth for the buried elite.

What is historically important about the pig-dragons is that the shapes don't go away. The same curled-serpent motif appears, slightly modified, on Shang bronzes two thousand years later. It appears again on Han dynasty jade ornaments. It becomes, over the millennia, the recognizable **Chinese dragon** — the long, serpentine, clawed and horned creature that adorned imperial robes, imperial thrones, imperial temples, and the imperial self-conception of every Chinese dynasty from the Qin to the Qing. When a Chinese emperor of the Ming dynasty embroidered a dragon on his yellow robe, he was participating in an unbroken visual tradition that started with a Hongshan jade worker grinding a curve into a lump of nephrite in a grassland village five thousand years earlier.

This kind of deep continuity is one of the strangest and most distinctive features of Chinese civilization. It is entirely possible, in fact common, for a symbol or a motif to start in the Neolithic, evolve through every subsequent era without ever really dying, and still be meaningful to a modern Chinese person today. Dragons are the most famous example. Jade ritual objects are another. Ancestor worship (which we'll come back to in Chapter 6) is a third. The cultural DNA of what will become imperial China is being laid down, piece by piece, by people who left no writing we can read and whose names no one alive knows.

---

Across the Hongshan world and its neighbors, you can watch another critical thing take shape in the burials: **ancestor worship**.

The basic archaeological signal is a change in how the dead are treated. In the earlier Neolithic — in Peiligang, Cishan, early Yangshao, Hemudu — burials are usually fairly egalitarian. Everyone gets buried in a simple grave with a modest set of offerings: a few pots, a handful of beads, maybe a tool. By the Hongshan period, and even more strongly by the Longshan period that follows (Chapter 6), something has changed. Some burials are much richer than others. A handful of elites — almost always adults, usually men, sometimes women — are interred with hundreds of jade objects, enormous quantities of pottery, and other prestige items, while their neighbors are still being buried with almost nothing. Status is becoming hereditary, and death is becoming a place where that status is publicly displayed.

Beyond the simple wealth difference, a subtler change matters even more. The rich burials contain ritual vessels — specific kinds of bowls, goblets, and tripod pots that were used to offer food and drink. The strong implication is that the living were *feeding the dead*, continuing a relationship with the deceased after death, treating dead ancestors as entities who remained present and powerful in the world of the living. This is the archaeological signature of **ancestor worship**, and it is the defining feature of Chinese religious life for the next five thousand years. Shang kings would communicate with dead ancestors through oracle bones. Zhou nobles would maintain elaborate ancestral temples. Confucian scholars would codify the ritual obligations of descendants to their forebears. Chinese families today still sweep graves at the Qingming festival every spring, bring food to the deceased, and burn paper money so their ancestors can spend it in the afterlife. This is a religious tradition with deep, deep roots — and those roots are visible in the changing burial customs of the Hongshan and late Yangshao worlds.

---

One more thing is worth mentioning about this era, because it sets up the chapter that follows.

All across Neolithic China — Yangshao, Hemudu, Hongshan, and the smaller cultures in between — trade in **prestige goods** was starting to create long-distance connections between communities that had no direct political relationship with each other. Jade traveled. Shells from the coast ended up in inland burials. Painted pottery styles spread across vast regions. Obsidian, marble, and mica moved hundreds of miles from their source quarries to the villages of elites who could afford to have exotic materials in their graves. Nobody was mapping roads or organizing caravans, but materials were moving — carried from hand to hand, village to village, along chains of exchange that were starting to tie the whole of eastern China together into a loose cultural network.

This is the world that was about to be shaken, around 3300 BCE, by something no one had seen before: a walled city of 300 hectares, with a massive hydraulic infrastructure, hundreds of jade-rich burials, a ritual precinct holding the largest jade objects anyone had ever seen, and proto-written symbols on pottery that scholars are still arguing about. The place is called **Liangzhu**, and when Chinese archaeologists finally understood what they had found there, it rewrote the entire map of where Chinese civilization started.

---

# Chapter 5 — The Liangzhu Miracle

Here is an archaeological puzzle: for almost a century, the standard story of Chinese civilization said that it began on the Yellow River. The Yellow River, the millet farmers, the walled towns of the Longshan horizon, the Xia and Shang dynasties — that was the core narrative. Anything that happened in the Yangtze delta was considered peripheral: rice farming, yes, stilt houses, yes, some interesting pottery, but nothing that had really *mattered* in the way the northern sites had.

Then archaeologists kept digging around a place called **Liangzhu** (lee-AHNG-joo), a few miles northwest of the modern city of Hangzhou in Zhejiang province, near the southern shore of Lake Tai. They had known Liangzhu was a Neolithic site since 1936. What they didn't know, for most of the 20th century, was how big it was — or what was underneath it. As the excavations widened through the 1980s, 1990s, and especially the 2000s, the picture kept getting larger. And larger. And larger. By the time they were done mapping it, the consensus was that the standard story was wrong, and that Chinese civilization had a second origin point — a very early one — that might actually have been the first.

Liangzhu is now a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**, and the phrase "first Chinese civilization" is increasingly being applied to it. To understand why, you have to imagine what the place looked like at its peak, around 3000 BCE.

---

The Liangzhu city core covered about **300 hectares** (roughly 740 acres, or 1.2 square miles — about the area of Central Park in New York City). In the center was a roughly rectangular walled compound of about **30 hectares**, likely the palace or ceremonial precinct — larger than Beijing's modern Forbidden City. Around that, inside a second, much longer outer wall, lay residential districts, workshops, craft zones, and storage areas. The whole thing was built on an artificial earthen platform raised several meters above the surrounding marshland, which would have made it visually striking to anyone approaching on foot: a vast flat mound rising out of the lake country, topped with wooden palaces and ceremonial terraces.

Walls this big were almost unknown anywhere in the world at this date. A few isolated settlements in the Near East and Europe had enclosures at roughly similar scale, but nothing quite like the integrated urban-and-ceremonial complex at Liangzhu. And the city wasn't even the most impressive part.

The most impressive part was the **water system**.

Surrounding Liangzhu, and extending for **more than 100 square kilometers** (roughly 40 square miles), was an enormous network of **dams, dikes, canals, and reservoirs** — an integrated hydraulic system designed to manage the runoff from the surrounding hills, prevent the rice paddies from flooding during storms, maintain a steady water supply during dry periods, and enable boat transport across the whole region. The outer dams are several miles long, built up from layers of rammed earth held together with clay cores and stone armor. At least eleven major dams have been mapped. They appear to have been built as a coordinated project — not accumulated over centuries of piecemeal construction, but engineered as a single system, with a common design and a common purpose.

This is the **largest and most sophisticated water control project anywhere in the world** for another two thousand years. The next thing comparable in scale is the Han dynasty's irrigation works in the 2nd century BCE. That Liangzhu — a Neolithic culture with stone tools and no writing we can read — was running hydraulic engineering on this scale around 3000 BCE is, to put it mildly, a shock.

A water system of this size implies several things at once. It implies **centralized authority** — someone, or some small group, had the power to mobilize thousands of workers over years, to feed them while they dug, and to direct their labor toward a single coordinated plan. It implies **agricultural surplus** large enough to support that labor without starving everyone. It implies **engineering knowledge** — not just about how to pile up earth, but about hydrology, about flow rates, about seasonal runoff, about soil mechanics. And it implies **political continuity**, because a project of this scale can't be finished by a single leader in a single lifetime. It has to be inherited, maintained, expanded. The Liangzhu people were running, in effect, a small early state.

---

Once you see the water system, the rest of Liangzhu starts to make sense.

The walled city at the center was the political and ceremonial heart of a region that may have covered **35,000 square kilometers** (about 14,000 square miles, roughly the size of Maryland). Hundreds of smaller Liangzhu sites — villages, towns, ceremonial platforms, craft workshops, rice fields — have been identified within that radius, and all of them trace culturally back to the same core. This is a regional polity, not just a single city. The people in outlying settlements made pottery in the Liangzhu style, buried their dead with Liangzhu-style jade, and presumably paid tribute, in rice or labor, to the elite at the center. What to call such a polity is a matter of semantics. Some archaeologists flatly call it a state. Others prefer "chiefdom" or "proto-state" because there is no writing to tell us what the Liangzhu people called themselves. For our purposes, the question is mostly academic. Whatever you call it, it was something that had never been seen in China before, and something that the rest of East Asia wouldn't match for another fifteen hundred years.

And then there were the jades.

---

The jade treasures of Liangzhu are what distinguish it from every previous and contemporary culture in China. The Hongshan jade workers had been extraordinary, but Hongshan worked mostly in smallish pendants and figurines. The Liangzhu jade workers worked on a completely different scale, with a completely different set of forms, for a completely different kind of ritual purpose.

The two defining Liangzhu jade forms are the **bi** (BEE) and the **cong** (TSONG), and they are the things you should remember from this chapter if you remember nothing else.

The **bi** is a flat circular disc with a hole in the middle — a large, polished, green disc the size of a dinner plate, or sometimes smaller, shaped like a donut. Chinese tradition, preserved in much later texts, associates the bi with **heaven**. The round shape, the hollow center, the way the disc catches light when held up to the sun — all of these are read as symbols of the sky, the cosmos, the realm of the spirits. Thousands of bi discs have been excavated from Liangzhu burials. In the richer graves, they are stacked on the chest and abdomen of the deceased, one on top of another, sometimes a dozen or more, forming a column of jade from throat to pelvis. The deceased was being wrapped, posthumously, in a column of sky.

The **cong** is its counterpart. It is a **square-sectioned tube** with a circular bore running through it — a hollow rectangular prism, carved from a single block of jade, often decorated on its outer faces with stylized animal-mask designs. Chinese tradition associates the cong with **earth**. The square outer shape represents the four corners of the world, while the round inner bore represents heaven passing through earth — the two realms locked together in a single object. Cong come in all sizes. A small one might be a few inches tall; a large ceremonial one can be a foot and a half. They are harder to carve than bi, because cutting a square-sectioned hollow tube out of a single jade boulder is an extraordinarily demanding piece of stone work, and Liangzhu cong sometimes have as many as **fifteen rings of carved decoration** stacked down their length, each with precision-cut animal-mask designs.

The bi and the cong, together, encoded a cosmology. Round sky over square earth, heaven and earth linked by the ritual objects of the king — that is a **Chinese cosmological model** that would be stated explicitly in much later texts, and here it is at Liangzhu, already fully formed, thousands of years before anyone was writing it down.

---

Alongside the bi and cong, Liangzhu workshops produced elaborate **jade axes**, **jade headdresses**, **jade pendants**, and small **jade figurines**. The most spectacular single object is called the **"King's Yue Axe"** — a jade ceremonial axe carved from a single block, bearing a delicate **taotie-like animal mask** on its blade. The **taotie** (tow-tee-YEH) is a stylized animal face — usually with bulging eyes, a wide gaping mouth, a pair of horns or a crest, and stylized body elements radiating out symmetrically. It is one of the most iconic motifs in Chinese art, appearing on Shang bronzes, Zhou jade, and beyond. And it is present, in proto-form, on Liangzhu jades — another of those unbroken visual threads from the Neolithic to the historical era.

The amount of jade found at Liangzhu is staggering. The richest burials contain **hundreds of jade objects per person**. A single elite grave from the Fanshan burial ground, excavated in 1986, contained **over 600 pieces** of jade, including nearly 100 bi discs, more than 20 cong, axes, beads, pendants, and ornaments for a headdress. The quantity alone is evidence of both wealth and labor organization: each piece took days or weeks to carve, and some pieces may have taken months. A single burial could have consumed the labor of dozens of specialist jade workers for a year.

The source of the jade itself is another mystery that deepens the picture. Nephrite doesn't naturally occur near Liangzhu. The raw material must have been imported — possibly from distant deposits in Liaoning, possibly from even farther afield. Nobody has found a single Liangzhu jade quarry on site. The entire enormous jade production tradition was being fed by long-distance trade in raw stone, which means the Liangzhu elites were plugged into exchange networks that reached across much of eastern China. They had the political power to bring precious materials hundreds of miles to their workshops, and they had the craft capacity to turn those materials into the most refined ritual objects anyone in East Asia had yet produced.

---

One more thing about Liangzhu, because it will come up again: the **proto-symbols**.

Hundreds of artifacts at Liangzhu bear incised symbols — not as individual marks on individual pots, the way Banpo symbols had been, but sometimes **in sequences**, two or three or even four symbols strung together on a single jade or ceramic object. Over **600 artifacts** with such marks have been catalogued. The most tantalizing examples are symbols that appear in clusters on cong tubes or on pottery jars — clusters that look less like decorative patterns and more like **short phrases**, as if somebody were marking these objects with a formula or label.

Are the Liangzhu symbols writing? The same scholarly fight as with Jiahu and Banpo plays out here, but with slightly more material to chew on. Chinese archaeologists increasingly think so — the sequence patterns, the ritual context, the consistency across the region, all suggest a system more developed than any earlier one in China. Western scholars tend to be more cautious, pointing out that without a known language behind the symbols, it's impossible to prove they encode speech rather than serving as elaborate ritual notation. Nobody has read the Liangzhu symbols. Nobody can. But they are the most sophisticated Neolithic symbol system China has yet produced, and whatever they encode, they were important enough for the Liangzhu elite to inscribe on their most sacred objects.

---

And then, between about 2400 and 2300 BCE, **Liangzhu collapsed**.

The collapse was relatively sudden and relatively complete. The central city was abandoned. The water system fell into disuse. The jade workshops stopped producing. Population in the region dropped dramatically. The distinctive Liangzhu burial style and pottery style disappeared from the archaeological record. Within a few generations, the entire civilization that had taken almost a thousand years to build was simply gone.

The reasons are disputed. The leading theory is **catastrophic flooding**. Core samples from the region show evidence of massive water events around the end of the Liangzhu period — either a sustained rise in sea level that inundated the lowland rice paddies, or a single enormous storm surge, or a combination of a failing hydraulic system and extreme weather that the Liangzhu engineers could no longer control. The very water management that had made the civilization possible may have failed at precisely the wrong moment. Other theories point to disease, climate shift, political collapse, or some combination. What is clear is that by 2300 BCE, Liangzhu was finished.

There is a haunting possibility embedded in this collapse. The Chinese tradition that would crystallize thousands of years later includes a foundational story about a **Great Flood** — a catastrophe that drowned the world for generations until the legendary hero **Yu the Great** tamed the waters and founded the first dynasty (Chapter 7). For most of the 20th century, scholars treated the Great Flood myth as pure folklore, a mythological explanation borrowed from older traditions or invented out of ritual significance. But the Liangzhu collapse is the kind of event that could plausibly have been remembered, distorted, and mythologized over centuries of oral tradition. A massive flood that destroyed the greatest civilization anyone had seen, leaving a scar on the cultural memory of the Yangtze delta, is exactly the kind of event that mythology is made of. The connection is speculative — the dates don't perfectly align, and the geography is not the same as the later legend's — but the possibility that the Chinese Great Flood tradition has a Liangzhu memory at its root is one of the more evocative hypotheses in modern Chinese archaeology.

Whatever the cause, Liangzhu's disappearance did not erase its influence. The jade traditions — the bi and the cong, the animal masks, the prestige burial with jade stacked on the body — were inherited by later cultures, including the Longshan horizon (Chapter 6) and eventually the Shang. Many of the specific forms the Liangzhu jade workers had perfected kept being made, almost unchanged, for another fifteen hundred years. The city was gone. The cosmology was forever.

And while Liangzhu was at its height and then collapsing in the south, something very different was happening in the north. The Yellow River valley was producing a new kind of society — a society of walled towns, warlords, and legendary god-kings. It is this world, not Liangzhu, that Chinese tradition would eventually remember as the seedbed of the first dynasties.

---

# Chapter 6 — Longshan: Walls, Warlords, and the Yellow Emperor

If you had been a farmer living in the Yellow River valley around 3000 BCE, you would have noticed, over the course of your life and the lives of your parents and grandparents, that the world was getting harder and stranger.

The old Yangshao villages, with their painted pottery and their communal houses and their modest moats, had become something more serious. Walls were getting bigger — in some places, much bigger. Villages were piling up in clusters. The people who lived in the biggest houses were taking more and more of the food. People were starting to keep track of fights — old weapons, armor, burned foundations, bodies hastily buried in walls. There was a new kind of pottery coming out of certain workshops — black, wheel-thrown, so thin you could see sunlight through it, meant for ritual use and nothing else. And there were stories about great men: a tribal chief who commanded the spirits of the rivers, a warrior with a head of iron, a battle that had decided who got to rule everything.

This is the **Longshan horizon** — not a single culture, but a broad stretch of related cultures across the North China Plain between about 3000 and 1900 BCE. It is the phase where the egalitarian village world of the early Neolithic breaks down for good, and something that looks unmistakably like early states takes its place. By the end of it, the gap between the living conditions of the elite and the ordinary farmer was vast. Ritual was being monopolized by specialist priests. Warfare had become a normal feature of life. And a particular kind of elite power — the power to command labor, monopolize sacred knowledge, and dictate what happened in the afterlife — had crystallized in ways that anticipate the first dynasties.

It is also the phase where Chinese tradition places its first named rulers. Archaeology cannot confirm that any of them were real. But the stories about them are older, in some cases, than the Shang dynasty itself, and they encode something about how the Longshan-era transformation was remembered.

---

Start with the pots. **Longshan black pottery** is one of the great technical achievements of the Neolithic. Yangshao potters had made their vessels by hand, coiling or paddling the clay into shape. Longshan potters used the **fast kick-wheel** — a spinning turntable powered by a foot pedal, which allowed a skilled craftsperson to throw a symmetrical vessel in minutes. The fast wheel wasn't invented in China (it appears earlier in Mesopotamia), but Longshan potters took it to an extreme no one else ever matched: they produced cups and goblets with walls **0.5 millimeters thick** — literally eggshell-thin, so fragile they could not have been used for anything practical. Some of the stem cups have stems so slender they couldn't support their own weight if you filled them with liquid.

These were not dinnerware. They were ritual objects, made exclusively for the dead and for ceremonies — objects whose entire purpose was to display the technical mastery and economic surplus of the community that could produce them. Black Longshan pottery is also notable for its firing technique: the potters used **reduction firing**, deliberately limiting oxygen in the kiln so that the clay vitrified into a glossy, jet-black surface rather than the oxidized red of earlier Chinese pottery. The result is striking in a way that the museum photographs don't quite capture — a surface so dark and so polished that the vessels look almost like they are made of black glass.

The significance of Longshan pottery isn't just aesthetic. The existence of eggshell-thin ritual cups means there were **full-time specialist craftspeople** — not farmers who made pots in their spare time, but people whose entire working life revolved around producing objects for elites who paid them (in grain, shelter, or prestige) to do nothing else. Craft specialization at that level is one of the hallmarks of an emerging state. It means an economy complex enough to support non-producing specialists, an elite wealthy enough to patronize them, and a distribution system that gets their products from the workshop to the burial. The same fast-wheel technology, incidentally, would later be applied by Shang dynasty craftsmen to make the ceramic molds used for **bronze casting** — so the technique itself is a direct ancestor of one of the defining crafts of the Chinese Bronze Age.

---

And then there are the walls.

Across the Yellow River plain, from Shandong in the east to Shaanxi in the west and down into central Henan, Longshan-era communities began building **rammed-earth walls** around their settlements on a scale that dwarfed anything in the Yangshao period. The technique is called **hangtu** (HAHNG-too): workers piled layers of loess, added a little water, and pounded each layer with wooden rammers until the soil compacted into a surface as hard as soft concrete. Layer after layer was built up until the wall reached its intended height, sometimes 10 meters (33 feet) or more. Longshan hangtu walls can be 5 to 15 meters (16 to 50 feet) thick at the base. They enclose areas ranging from a few hectares to, at the largest known sites, **over 400 hectares** — close to four square kilometers of walled town.

The rammed-earth technique would become the standard for Chinese monumental construction for the rest of the pre-modern era. The first Great Wall (not the stone one tourists see today, but earlier earth versions) was hangtu. The Han, Tang, and Ming capitals were surrounded by hangtu walls. The Forbidden City sits on a hangtu platform. The construction method, perfected during the Longshan horizon, was still in use thousands of years later. And it comes out of this specific period, out of this specific reason to build walls: people were **fighting each other**, at a scale and a frequency that made simple palisades inadequate. Chiefs needed real defenses.

The most famous Longshan walled sites are **Taosi** (TAO-suh, in Shanxi province), **Liangchengzhen** (lee-AHNG-chung-JEN, in Shandong province), and **Shimao** (SHEE-mao, in Shaanxi province). Shimao is particularly startling. It covers about 400 hectares, with an outer wall made of stone rather than rammed earth — unusual for China — and in its lower walls, archaeologists have found **decapitated human heads, deliberately buried in the foundations**. Six pits containing about 80 skulls in total, mostly young women, placed in a clear ritual arrangement. Someone was sacrificing people to consecrate the walls of Shimao around 2000 BCE. The practice of foundation sacrifice — killing people or animals and burying them in the footings of important buildings to bless the structure — would continue in China through the Shang and Zhou dynasties, but Shimao is one of its earliest and most graphic examples.

The picture you should be holding in your head is this: fortified towns within sight of other fortified towns, built with enormous investments of labor, defended with warnings carved out of human remains. Warfare is not occasional. It is structural. There are winners and losers. Some towns grow. Others get burned and abandoned. The elites inside the big walls are accumulating more and more wealth, more and more political power, more and more sacred authority over ritual and calendar. The ingredients of the first Chinese dynasty are being assembled, in place, in the Longshan horizon. The Xia Dynasty, when it finally appears in Chinese tradition, comes out of this world. So does the Shang. So does everything that follows.

---

The legendary stories about this era are, of course, more colorful than the archaeology. According to much later Chinese tradition, the Longshan horizon was dominated by a series of great rulers — the **Five Emperors**, a list of half-divine culture heroes who ruled in sequence during this era of walled towns and battles. The most famous of them, by a long margin, is the **Yellow Emperor**.

The **Yellow Emperor** — **Huangdi** in Chinese (HWAHNG-dee), which literally means "Yellow Thearch" — is the ancestor whom modern Han Chinese, which is to say over a billion people, still trace their mythological descent from. His traditional dates are about **2698 to 2598 BCE**, placing him squarely in the middle of the Longshan horizon. He is credited with a staggering list of inventions and achievements: unifying the tribes of the Yellow River plain, inventing the calendar, developing Chinese medicine, introducing silk cultivation (through his wife **Leizu**, Chapter 3), creating the first writing system (through his minister **Cangjie**), codifying music, establishing the ritual of government, and laying down the basic pattern of legitimate rule for all subsequent Chinese dynasties.

How much of this is real? Almost certainly none of it in the literal sense — the Yellow Emperor is no more a historical figure than King Arthur or Romulus. But the idea of him is historical in the sense that it tells us what classical Chinese tradition thought was happening in this era. The tradition remembered the Longshan horizon as the moment when a scatter of competing chiefdoms were forged into a coherent cultural entity under a single paramount figure, through war, wisdom, and divine legitimacy. The archaeology shows us the walls, the weapons, the craft specialization, and the emerging elites. The tradition adds the myth that gives this transformation a name and a face.

The Yellow Emperor's signature exploit in the legends is the **Battle of Zhuolu** (JWOH-loo), in which he defeats a rival chief named **Chi You** (CHEE YOH). Chi You is depicted as a fearsome warrior with an **iron head and a bronze forehead** — an image that would make no sense in 2700 BCE (neither iron nor bronze was yet in use in China) but makes plenty of sense as a mythological symbol of elemental, almost monstrous power. Chi You leads a band of 81 "brothers" with animal bodies, summons fog to confuse the Yellow Emperor's army, and is defeated only when the Yellow Emperor himself invokes **dragons, wind spirits, and rain spirits** to disperse the fog and break the enemy lines. The battle ends with Chi You's capture and execution, and with the Yellow Emperor installed as the paramount ruler of the Yellow River world.

Behind the mythology, there is almost certainly a kernel of memory: the Yellow River plain in the Longshan era saw real, large-scale conflicts between coalitions of chiefdoms. Who fought whom, who won, and whose name got attached to the victory — all of that is lost, and maybe was lost within a few generations of the actual events. But the story as Chinese tradition preserved it encodes the *idea* that the unified north of China was born out of a decisive war, and that the winning side's leader became the progenitor of Chinese civilization. This is not a story about evolution. It is a story about a single founding moment, won in a single battle, by a single sage-warrior. That founding-moment frame would become the template for every subsequent dynastic change.

---

The Yellow Emperor's reign, according to the traditions, also gets credit for the invention of **Chinese writing**. The specific inventor is named: **Cangjie** (TSAHNG-jee-YEH), a court historian who supposedly had **four eyes** — a visual exaggeration symbolizing his supernatural ability to perceive patterns invisible to ordinary mortals.

The legend is worth telling in full because it is beautiful. Before Cangjie, the story goes, the Yellow Emperor's court kept records with knotted cords and carved wooden tallies — the same primitive recording methods used by many pre-literate societies, including the Inca with their quipus. But the empire was growing too large and too complex for knots and tallies. Cangjie was tasked with finding a better way. He walked out of the palace and into the world, studying the patterns of everything he saw: the tracks of animals in mud, the flight paths of birds, the cracks in shell, the branching of rivers, the shapes of leaves. From these observations, he abstracted visual signs — pictures simplified into symbols — and built up a system where each symbol stood for a word or an idea. When he finished, the legend says, **heaven rained down millet in celebration, and the ghosts of the dead wept through the night in fear** — because now their secrets could be written down and could never be fully hidden again.

The charming thing about the Cangjie legend is that it is, in one respect, technically accurate. Chinese writing really *did* evolve from pictographic observation of the natural world. The oldest Chinese characters — those on Shang oracle bones — are recognizably stylized drawings of things: a hand, a foot, a horse, a moon, a mountain. Over thousands of years these pictures were abstracted, simplified, and combined into the modern Chinese character set, but the pictographic origin is still visible if you know what to look for. The legend preserves a real memory of how the system worked, even if the "single inventor" framing is mythological.

As for when real Chinese writing actually appeared: the Banpo marks, the Liangzhu symbols, and various other proto-writing traditions were accumulating across the Neolithic, but the first undisputed Chinese script — the **oracle bone script** — doesn't appear for another thousand years, during the Shang dynasty. It's not in Ancient China as we're defining it in this book. But the Longshan-era Yellow Emperor tradition tied the invention of writing to this era anyway, because the tradition wanted to credit the founding of Chinese civilization with all the arts of civilization at once.

---

The Longshan horizon is also when the **religious practices** that would define Chinese civilization for the next four thousand years crystallized.

The first is the full flowering of **ancestor worship**. We saw its roots in the Hongshan and late Yangshao burial practices (Chapter 4), but in the Longshan period, elite tombs become astonishingly elaborate. Rich graves at Taosi and elsewhere contain hundreds of pottery vessels, jade objects, animal sacrifices, and occasionally human sacrifices of retainers who were killed to accompany their lord into the afterlife. The scale of the grave offerings signals that the Longshan elite believed their ancestors maintained real power after death — power that could be tapped by the living through proper ritual, or could be turned against them if they were neglected. Feeding the dead, honoring the dead, consulting the dead: these are not folk customs in Longshan China. They are state religion, or something very close to it.

The second is **scapulimancy** — divination by reading the cracks in heated animal bones. The word comes from the Latin *scapula* (shoulder blade), because the technique typically used the shoulder bone of a cow, ox, or sheep, or sometimes a turtle shell. A diviner would pose a question to the ancestors or spirits, then heat the bone or shell until it cracked in a characteristic pattern. The pattern of the cracks was read as the response — yes or no, good or bad, auspicious or inauspicious. Longshan-era scapulimancy is still **unwritten**: the diviners did not inscribe their questions or answers on the bones, they just read the cracks and remembered the results. But the practice was enormously influential. A thousand years later, the Shang dynasty would take this exact technique, add inscriptions to it, and produce the **oracle bones** that constitute the earliest known Chinese writing. Every oracle bone ever inscribed traces its ancestry to these Longshan-period diviners, cracking shoulder blades in the courtyards of walled towns.

The third is the development of long-distance **jade exchange networks**. By the late Longshan period, high-quality nephrite was moving across China on a scale that would not be matched until the Han dynasty and the Silk Road. Jade from the Kunlun Mountains in Xinjiang — over 2,000 miles (more than 3,000 km) from the Yangtze delta — was appearing in Liangzhu burials. Hongshan-sourced jade from the northeast was appearing in Yellow River burials. And so on. These were not casual trade goods. Jade was the substance of cosmic power, and its movement across the landscape traced the political and religious relationships between elite houses that, a few centuries later, would emerge as the first named dynasties.

---

The most haunting Longshan-era site is **Taosi** in Shanxi province. Taosi covered about 280 hectares at its peak — a major walled town with a palace zone, craft workshops, elite burials, and a strange semicircular structure on one edge of the town that has puzzled archaeologists since it was identified.

The structure is a platform of rammed earth, roughly 20 meters across, with 13 pillars of rammed earth arranged in an arc along the outer edge. From the central point of the platform, an observer looking out through the gaps between the pillars can watch the sun rise over the eastern horizon. When archaeologists and astronomers reconstructed the sight lines, they found that the pillars align with the points on the horizon where the sun rises on the **summer solstice, winter solstice, spring equinox, and autumn equinox**. In other words, the Taosi platform is a **solar observatory** — probably the oldest one in China, and arguably the oldest purpose-built solar observatory yet identified anywhere in the world.

Some Chinese scholars have gone further and suggested that Taosi may be the **capital of the legendary Emperor Yao** — one of the sage-kings who preceded the Xia Dynasty in Chinese tradition. The identification is speculative. But the site's scale, its observatory, its elite burials with calendar-related grave goods, and its dates all fit the traditional chronology of the late Five Emperors period. Whether or not Taosi was Yao's capital, it is exactly the kind of place Chinese tradition had in mind when it described the era: a powerful walled town ruled by a king who controlled the calendar and thus, through the calendar, the timing of planting, harvest, and ritual.

The political logic of an observatory at this date is worth pausing on. Agriculture depends on knowing when to plant — when the last frost will pass, when the spring rains will come, when the summer heat will arrive. A community that can predict these things accurately has a decisive advantage over communities that can't. In Longshan China, the people who controlled astronomical observation could tell the farmers when to plant, and that gave them a kind of authority that was simultaneously practical, religious, and political. The **ruler as calendar-maker** is one of the oldest motifs in Chinese political thought: the emperor as the **"Son of Heaven"** whose job includes regulating the cycle of time itself, publishing the official calendar, and ensuring that earthly events stay synchronized with heavenly ones. That idea, which would persist unchanged through every Chinese dynasty until 1912, is visible at Taosi in rammed-earth pillars pointing at the horizon, 4,300 years ago.

The Longshan horizon, with its walled towns and its sage-kings and its ritual calendar, was the world in which the first Chinese dynasty was born. The Chinese tradition remembered the transition precisely: the sage-kings ruled for generations, each choosing his successor based on virtue rather than birth, until the day the rule passed to a man named **Yu the Great**, who tamed a great flood, saved the world, and passed his throne to his own son — the first time power had moved by heredity rather than by moral selection. That is the story we turn to in the next chapter. It takes us to the edge of the line between legend and history, and when we cross that line, we find something nobody expected: in 2016, geologists found physical evidence of a real, catastrophic flood, right on schedule.

---

# Chapter 7 — The Flood and the First Dynasty

The Chinese tradition about the origin of the first dynasty is one of the great foundation myths in world history, and it is worth telling as the tradition tells it before we start picking at what might be true.

In the late Longshan era, according to the *Records of the Grand Historian* and other classical Chinese texts, a terrible disaster struck the Yellow River world: a **great flood**. Not a season of bad rain, not a single burst of a dam — something much larger. The waters rose, the rivers left their banks, and for years the flood persisted. Crops failed. Villages were swept away. Whole populations had to move to higher ground to survive. The disaster was so complete that it threatened the continued existence of settled life in the Yellow River valley.

The ruler at the time was **Emperor Yao** (YAO) — one of the last sage-kings of the Five Emperors era. Yao commanded a minister named **Gun** (GOON) to control the flood. Gun tried, for **nine years**, using the obvious technique: building dams to block the water and channel it away from inhabited land. It didn't work. The dams failed, the waters kept rising, and the disaster continued. Gun was executed for his failure. Leadership of the flood control effort passed to Gun's son, a man named **Yu**.

Yu took an entirely different approach. Instead of building walls to stop the water, he led the work of **dredging the rivers** — digging out the silted channels, straightening curves, opening up new outlets to the sea, allowing the excess water to flow away through the land rather than be held back by it. The work took **thirteen years**. During those thirteen years, Yu was so devoted to his task that he reportedly passed by the gate of his own house three times without stopping to see his wife and children. He worked alongside the common laborers, his arms calloused from digging, his legs swollen from wading in mud. He slept in the field. He ate what the workers ate. And at the end of thirteen years, the waters receded. The land was reclaimed. The people returned to their villages. And Yu — by this point universally admired for his virtue, his skill, and his willingness to sacrifice everything for the common good — became the obvious choice to succeed Emperor Shun, who had succeeded Yao, as the paramount ruler of the Yellow River world.

Up to this point, succession in the legends had worked by merit. Each sage-king chose his successor from among the most virtuous men of the next generation, passing over his own sons in favor of whichever outsider was worthiest. Yao passed over his son Danzhu to choose Shun. Shun passed over his own sons to choose Yu. The principle was clear: virtue, not bloodline, legitimizes rule.

And then Yu died, and the principle broke.

The traditional account says that Yu had designated a man named **Yi** as his successor, continuing the merit-based system. But after Yu's death, Yu's son **Qi** (CHEE) seized power, backed by a coalition of nobles who preferred a hereditary king to a chosen one. Yi resisted. There was a brief conflict. Qi won. And in winning, he established the principle that **power passes from father to son** — the principle of **hereditary dynasty**, which would govern Chinese politics for the next four thousand years, until the abdication of the last Qing emperor in 1912.

The dynasty that Qi founded was called the **Xia** (SHYAH), and it is traditionally considered China's first dynasty. Its dates, according to the classical chronologies, run from about **2070 to 1600 BCE** — just under five hundred years. The Xia dynasty had, by tradition, seventeen rulers, from Yu's son Qi at the beginning to the terrible **Jie** at the end (Chapter 8). It is, in Chinese self-understanding, the beginning of the historical era — the moment when legend gives way to recorded dynasty.

---

So: is any of this true?

For most of the 20th century, Western scholarship treated the Xia as pure legend. There was no contemporary writing to document its existence — Chinese writing doesn't begin until the Shang oracle bones, about 1250 BCE — and no archaeological site had been definitively identified as a Xia capital. The whole Yu-the-Great-and-the-flood story looked like a mythology of the ruling classes projected backward to legitimize hereditary kingship. Many Western sinologists considered the Xia to be no more historical than King Arthur.

Chinese scholarship has always been more generous toward the Xia — partly out of cultural pride, partly because the classical textual tradition was more systematic and internally consistent than the Western skeptics gave it credit for. The *Records of the Grand Historian*, written around 100 BCE by Sima Qian, provides a detailed Xia king list with specific dates and events. Later inscriptions refer to Xia descendants. The traditions about Yu, the flood, and the Xia were too deeply embedded in Chinese ritual and memory to be dismissed as pure invention.

The debate has shifted considerably in the 21st century, and the reason is twofold: archaeology and geology.

The archaeology we'll come to in the next chapter. The geology is worth handling now, because it is one of those moments in modern science when a legendary story gets a physical reality check — and actually gets confirmed, at least in part.

In **2016**, a team of Chinese and Western scientists published a paper in the journal *Science* reporting the discovery of physical evidence for a **massive catastrophic flood** in the upper Yellow River, around **1920 BCE**. The flood was caused by an **outburst** from a dammed lake in **Jishi Gorge** (on the border of modern Qinghai and Gansu provinces): a landslide had blocked the river, forming a lake that grew and grew until the earthen dam gave way. When it did, the release was apocalyptic — roughly **300 to 500 times the normal flow of the Yellow River**, a wall of water high enough to scour the river canyon clean and flood the valleys downstream for hundreds of miles.

The scientists identified signature sediments from this flood — distinctive layers of silt and gravel deposited by an unusually violent water event — in sites along the upper Yellow River. They dated the flood to within a generation of the traditional dates for Yu the Great's flood control work. And they argued that the flood would have been so disruptive to the Yellow River settlement pattern that it could plausibly lie behind the Chinese Great Flood tradition.

There are complications. The 2016 date of 1920 BCE is roughly **150 years later** than the traditional date of 2070 BCE for Yu's flood, so the fit is not perfect. Some scholars have pushed back on the identification, arguing that the evidence is not as clear-cut as the *Science* paper claimed, or that the flood would have been a local catastrophe rather than the civilization-threatening disaster the legend describes. But the basic finding is striking: there **was** a catastrophic flood on the Yellow River, at roughly the right time to lie behind the legend, and its effects would have been severe enough to mobilize a major flood-control response. Whether or not Yu the Great was a real person, the flood that the legend remembers appears to have been a real event.

---

Beyond the geology, there is another kind of evidence for the historical reality of the era: the rise of a specific kind of **archaeological culture** in the Yellow River valley during the exact period when the Xia Dynasty is supposed to have ruled. This culture is centered on a site called **Erlitou** (ar-lee-TOE), in central Henan province, and it is the subject of the next chapter. For now, the key fact is this: the Erlitou culture dates to roughly **1900 to 1500 BCE**, which overlaps neatly with the traditional Xia dates. Its material culture — palaces, bronze casting, jade ritual, elite burials, long-distance trade — looks like exactly what a small early state would produce. Most Chinese archaeologists, and a growing minority of Western ones, now identify Erlitou as either the capital of the Xia Dynasty, or as an early-Shang polity that was the direct successor to the Xia, or as something in between. The dates line up. The features line up. And the alternative — that Erlitou is a completely separate Bronze Age civilization with no connection to the Xia tradition — requires believing that the classical Chinese account is pure fabrication, which is an increasingly uncomfortable position when the material evidence keeps confirming parts of it.

So: was Yu the Great real? Probably not as a specific individual. Was there a catastrophic flood that reshaped the Yellow River world around the end of the third millennium BCE? Yes. Was the memory of that flood preserved in the Chinese tradition and eventually attached to a legendary sage-engineer whose descendants founded the first dynasty? Very probably. Was there a first dynasty around that time? Almost certainly, yes — there was a polity of some kind, centered somewhere around Erlitou, that later Chinese tradition remembered as the Xia. Whether the kings' names and the specific stories are accurate, nobody can say. But the broad outline — flood, flood control, transition to hereditary rule, first dynasty — has enough supporting evidence that it cannot be dismissed as pure myth.

---

Two more things from the Xia tradition are worth mentioning before we move to Erlitou.

The first is the **Xia calendar**. Chinese tradition credits the Xia Dynasty with developing the **Xia Zheng** (SHYAH-jung), the "Xia Month System," which set the first month of the year at the new moon after the winter solstice and established the basic structure of the **Chinese lunisolar calendar** — a calendar that tracks both the moon's phases and the sun's position, so it stays synchronized with the seasons. This calendar, refined and formalized by later dynasties, is still the basis for Chinese New Year, the Mid-Autumn Festival, and every other traditional Chinese holiday observed today. Over a billion people still plan their lives around this calendar, and its root, according to tradition, is in the Xia.

Whether the Xia actually developed a formal calendar system is impossible to confirm. But the Taosi observatory (Chapter 6) shows that by the late Longshan period, Chinese communities had the astronomical knowledge to track solstices and equinoxes, which is the foundation of any lunisolar calendar. A Xia-era codification of an already-existing knowledge tradition is entirely plausible. The attribution to the Xia is traditional, but the underlying reality — that Chinese calendar-making was maturing during this era — is archaeologically defensible.

The second is the **Mandate of Heaven**.

The **Mandate of Heaven** is one of the central concepts in Chinese political thought, and it was, according to tradition, formally crystallized during the Zhou dynasty a thousand years later. But the *idea* behind it — that heaven watches over human affairs, withdraws its approval from corrupt rulers, and grants the right to rule to those who deserve it — was already implicit in the Xia tradition. The Xia dynasty was understood to have begun when Yu's virtue earned him the right to pass his throne to his son, and it was understood to have ended when the last Xia king, **Jie** (JYEH), proved so tyrannical that heaven withdrew its approval and allowed the Shang to overthrow him. The pattern — **virtuous founder, long stable middle, corrupt and tyrannical last ruler, righteous overthrow** — would repeat, with different casts but the same structural beats, for every Chinese dynasty for the next four thousand years. It is the **dynastic cycle**, and its template is laid down in the Xia tradition.

Whether or not Yu was real, and whether or not Jie was real, the idea that rulers must earn their right to rule and can be legitimately overthrown when they lose it is the oldest and deepest principle in Chinese political philosophy. It does not come out of nowhere. It comes out of the tradition's memory of how the first dynasty began and ended. And that memory — the Xia tradition — is rooted in a real historical era that archaeology is slowly starting to illuminate.

For the specific shape of that illumination, we turn to Erlitou.

---

# Chapter 8 — Erlitou: Legend Becomes Archaeology

The story of **Erlitou** began as an accident.

In 1959, a team of Chinese archaeologists led by **Xu Xusheng** (SHOO SHOO-shung) was surveying the area around Luoyang in central Henan province, looking for possible Shang-period sites. The Shang dynasty's capitals and ritual centers were already well known from earlier excavations at Anyang (the late-Shang capital, Yinxu, where the oracle bones had been found), but the **early** Shang — and anything earlier than that — was still mostly a gap in the archaeological record. Xu's team was hoping to find a site that might push the known Shang timeline back, or perhaps turn up physical evidence for the legendary Xia Dynasty.

What they found, near a small village called **Erlitou** in Yanshi County, about 10 kilometers east of Luoyang, was unexpected: a massive stratified occupation site covering roughly **3 square kilometers**, with deep cultural layers stretching back centuries. Over the next sixty years of continuous excavation — still ongoing today — Erlitou has become one of the most important archaeological sites in all of China, and the single most important site for understanding how the transition from Neolithic chiefdoms to the first Chinese dynasties actually happened on the ground.

Erlitou is big, important, and chronologically exactly where you would want an early Xia or proto-Shang capital to be. Its dates, established through decades of careful stratigraphy and radiocarbon analysis, run from about **1900 to 1500 BCE** — overlapping the end of the traditional Xia Dynasty and the beginning of the traditional Shang. It sits in the right region: the central Henan plain, the heartland of every ancient Chinese dynasty. And its material culture shows, on an unprecedented scale for China, the features that archaeologists associate with early states: palatial architecture, bronze casting, jade ritual, craft specialization, long-distance trade, and elite burials separated from common burials by a gulf of wealth and labor.

---

The heart of Erlitou is its **palace zone**, a walled precinct of about 11 hectares near the center of the site. Within this zone, archaeologists have uncovered the foundations of several large building complexes, the most famous of which is called simply **Palace No. 1**.

Palace No. 1 is the earliest known example of Chinese **palace architecture**, and it establishes the template for nearly everything that follows. The building sat on a rammed-earth platform about **108 by 100 meters** (roughly 10,000 square meters, about 2.5 acres) — a vast raised foundation made by the same hangtu technique used for Longshan walls. On top of the platform, post-holes indicate a main central hall surrounded by a **colonnade** — a row of columns forming a gallery around the central space. Running along the outside of the palace platform was another row of posts, probably supporting a covered walkway or outer gallery. The whole structure was oriented roughly **north-south**, with the main entrance on the south side, opening onto a large courtyard.

If you stretch your imagination and picture this, you can see the bones of something familiar. A **raised platform**, a **central hall**, a **surrounding gallery**, a **south-facing entrance**, a **courtyard**. This is the basic template of Chinese imperial architecture for the next three and a half thousand years. The Shang palaces at Anyang followed it. Zhou palaces followed it. The Qin imperial complexes followed it. The Han dynasty's Weiyang Palace in Chang'an followed it. The Forbidden City in Beijing — built by the Ming dynasty and still standing today — is a vastly elaborated version of the same layout: raised platform, central hall on a north-south axis, surrounded by galleries and courtyards, south-facing entrance. The **template** originates at Erlitou in the early second millennium BCE, and it never really changes.

This is one of those moments where the continuity of Chinese civilization stops being a general claim and becomes a specific, testable statement. A Chinese imperial architect of the Ming dynasty, building in the 15th century CE, was following a blueprint whose oldest known example was laid down 3,300 years earlier at Erlitou, before there was any writing to record the design. Over thirty centuries, dozens of dynasties, the arrival and departure of Buddhism, the rise and fall of regional powers, the layout of the ruler's house stayed recognizably the same. There is nothing quite like this in the architectural history of any other ancient civilization.

---

Around the palace zone at Erlitou, archaeologists have identified several other functional areas that together paint a picture of an early state economy.

There is a **bronze-casting workshop** — one of the earliest in China — where Erlitou craftsmen produced the first significant bronze ritual vessels in Chinese history. The vessels are small by Shang standards — mostly **jue** (JWEH), tripod wine goblets about 20 centimeters tall, and simple dagger-shaped weapons — but they are technically sophisticated. The craftsmen used the **piece-mold casting** technique, in which a clay model of the final vessel was made, then wrapped in clay sections that were baked into molds, then assembled around a clay core, with molten bronze poured in to fill the space between the core and the outer molds. This technique — which is **uniquely Chinese**; nobody else in the ancient world used piece-mold casting at this complexity — allowed for vessels with elaborate surface decoration, including early versions of the **taotie** animal mask that would become the dominant decorative motif on Shang bronzes a few hundred years later.

The Erlitou bronzes are significant for several reasons. They are the first **true bronzes** in Chinese history — copper-tin alloys, not pure copper — which means somebody at Erlitou had access to both **copper ore** and **tin ore**, the latter of which was particularly rare in the Yellow River region and had to be imported from distant mountain sources. The presence of tin at Erlitou is evidence that the site was plugged into long-distance metal trade networks that extended across much of northern and western China. They are the ancestors of the Shang bronze tradition — the direct precursors, in form and technique, to the great Shang ritual vessels that would be produced a few centuries later. And they mark the beginning of the **Chinese Bronze Age**, the era during which bronze was the most prestigious and politically charged material in Chinese civilization. For the next thousand years, Chinese power would be measured in the quality and quantity of bronze ritual vessels a ruler could command. It starts at Erlitou.

There is also a **turquoise workshop** producing what may be the most exquisite craft objects of this era. Erlitou craftsmen took bronze plaques and inlaid them with thousands of tiny individually shaped turquoise pieces, creating **mosaic animal-face designs** in blue-green stone on bronze background. A single plaque might contain **300 to 500 turquoise tesserae**, each shaped by hand, each set with precision into its position. The resulting images are early versions of the **taotie** — the stylized animal mask with bulging eyes, a wide mouth, and horns or crests — that would become the defining decorative motif on Shang bronzes. The turquoise came from distant sources in Qinghai province or possibly Xinjiang, over **1,000 kilometers (620 miles)** away. The existence of an Erlitou workshop capable of this work speaks to both the political reach of the early state (commanding materials from such distances) and its craft sophistication (employing full-time specialists whose only job was assembling turquoise mosaics for elite ritual objects).

And there is a **jade workshop**, continuing the Chinese jade tradition that had started at Hongshan and peaked at Liangzhu. The Erlitou jade objects include ceremonial blades, plaques, and small animal figurines, all carved in the Longshan and Liangzhu-influenced style that would be inherited wholesale by the Shang dynasty. The Erlitou craftsmen were not innovating new jade forms; they were maintaining a tradition that had been running for almost 2,000 years, and they were doing it well enough to supply an elite burial economy.

---

Beyond these workshops, the distribution of **exotic materials** at Erlitou is probably the single strongest piece of evidence that the site controlled a real early state economy. Erlitou elite graves contain:

- **Turquoise** from Qinghai or Xinjiang, over 1,000 km away
- **Marine shells** from the east coast, over 500 km away
- **Tin** for bronze casting, probably from mountain sources in the south or southwest
- **Copper** from various sources, including some probably from the Lower Yangtze
- **Nephrite jade** from the northwest or northeast, over 1,500 km away in some cases
- **Cowrie shells** from tropical coasts, possibly from as far as the South China Sea

No single region produced all of these materials. Their simultaneous presence at Erlitou means that Erlitou was the **center of a trade network** that reached across much of eastern China, from the steppe borders to the tropical coast, from Central Asia to the Pacific. The Erlitou elite were not just receiving these goods as gifts; the sheer volume and the concentration of craft specialists who worked with them at Erlitou suggest they were **organizing the flow**. Trade goods were coming in, being worked into ritual objects, and then being redistributed to local elites, either as exchange or as tribute. This is how early states actually function, in practice: they convert their political centrality into control over the flow of prestige goods, and they use that control to reinforce their own authority over outlying regions.

The **Shang Dynasty** would inherit all of this at a much larger scale, refining it into one of the most impressive Bronze Age state economies in world history. But the prototype was running, at perhaps a third the scale, at Erlitou, four or five hundred years earlier.

---

Who were the Erlitou people, and what was their relationship to the legendary Xia Dynasty?

There are three broad positions in the current scholarly debate. The **first position**, widely held by Chinese archaeologists, identifies Erlitou as the **capital of the Xia Dynasty**. The dates line up. The region lines up. The scale of political organization lines up. Classical Chinese tradition places the Xia capital in the general area near Luoyang, and Erlitou is exactly there. In this view, Palace No. 1 is the palace of a Xia king, and the whole Erlitou culture is the material culture of the Xia people.

The **second position**, more common among Western scholars, treats Erlitou as a **real early state** but is agnostic about whether it should be called "Xia." The reason for caution is simple: there is no writing from Erlitou that identifies the rulers, names the dynasty, or mentions Yu the Great. Without text, any identification with a legendary dynasty is necessarily speculative. In this view, Erlitou is important because it's real and early — it proves that something state-like was happening in central Henan at the right time — but attaching the Xia label to it is an interpretive choice, not a hard fact.

The **third position** is a compromise: Erlitou's earlier phases are Xia, its later phases are early Shang, and the transition from one to the other corresponds to the legendary overthrow of the Xia by the Shang that classical tradition describes. Some versions of this view see Erlitou as a long-term political center that was inherited by successive dynasties.

All three positions are defensible with the current evidence. What is not defensible is the **old dismissive view** that the Xia never existed at all and the whole tradition was fabricated. Whatever you choose to call it, something was happening at Erlitou that matches the broad Chinese tradition of the first dynasty remarkably well: a central palace, elite ritual, bronze casting, long-distance trade, and a chronology that fits. The burden of proof has shifted. It is no longer ridiculous to say "the Xia were real." It is just not yet provable in the specific way that would satisfy the strictest historians.

---

According to Chinese tradition, the Xia Dynasty ended in tyranny.

The last Xia king was named **Jie** (JYEH), and he is the archetypal **"bad last emperor"** in Chinese historical memory — a figure whose name would echo through every subsequent dynastic collapse as a warning to rulers about what happens when you lose the Mandate of Heaven. Jie was, by tradition, a man of great personal strength and intelligence who squandered his gifts on cruelty and indulgence. He had a favorite consort named **Mo Xi** (MOH-SHEE) who encouraged his worst impulses. He built lavish pleasure palaces, ignored the advice of his ministers, and — in the most lurid image preserved in later texts — he reportedly constructed a **"pool of wine"** large enough to row a small boat across, and a **"forest of meat"** where slabs of roasted food hung from trees, so that his court could drink and eat in perpetual luxury while the peasants outside starved.

These stories are, of course, not to be taken literally. "Pool of wine, forest of meat" is a standard Chinese literary trope, used for the last ruler of almost every collapsing dynasty. The same accusation would later be leveled at the last Shang king, **Di Xin**, at the Qin Emperor **Huhai**, and at various "bad last emperors" through the Han and Tang. The trope encodes a moral message — that dynastic collapse is always caused by the ruler's moral failure, not by structural or environmental factors — and it tells us more about Chinese political philosophy than about the actual behavior of any specific ruler. But the fact that Jie is the **original** bad-last-emperor — the prototype for every subsequent one — gives him a particular significance in the tradition. Whatever he actually did, his fall became the template for how Chinese civilization would understand the transfer of power between dynasties.

Against Jie rose **Tang of Shang**, a chief of a vassal clan in the eastern part of the Xia territory. Tang was presented in the tradition as exactly the opposite of Jie: virtuous, humble, concerned with the welfare of the common people, respected by his peers, guided by wise advisors (most famously his minister **Yi Yin** [YEE-yin], who is sometimes credited with actually running the government). Tang spent years building alliances among disaffected Xia vassals, presenting himself as the legitimate alternative to a corrupt ruler. When Jie finally executed one of his own loyal nobles in a fit of paranoia, Tang declared that heaven had withdrawn the Mandate from the Xia and marched against him.

---

The climactic event of the transition is called the **Battle of Mingtiao** (MING-tyao), and it is, by tradition, the first **decisive battle** in Chinese history — the battle that ended the Xia and founded the Shang. Its location is not certainly known, but classical texts place it near **Fengqiu** (FUNG-chyoh) in eastern Henan province. The date, according to traditional chronologies, is around **1600 BCE**, which matches the end of the Erlitou culture in the archaeological record almost exactly.

At Mingtiao, according to the *Book of Documents* (*Shujing*), one of the oldest Chinese historical texts, Tang's army met Jie's army in open battle. Jie's forces were, the tradition says, **unwilling to fight for him** — his own soldiers had heard the stories of the pool of wine and the forest of meat, had seen their own villages neglected while the king indulged himself, and refused to die for a ruler who had clearly lost heaven's favor. Tang's forces, by contrast, were fighting for a ruler who had already been acclaimed as virtuous and legitimate. The outcome was never in doubt. Jie fled the battlefield, was pursued, and died in exile. Tang became the first **Shang** king, and the dynasty he founded would rule northern China for the next five centuries — until it, too, would be overthrown by a virtuous rebel when its own last king proved tyrannical, in an almost exact repetition of the Xia-Shang transition.

That pattern — **righteous founder defeats corrupt tyrant in decisive battle, founds new dynasty, inherits the Mandate of Heaven** — is the **dynastic cycle**, and its original template is the Battle of Mingtiao. The Zhou would use the exact same narrative framework when they overthrew the Shang at the Battle of Muye in 1046 BCE. The Han would use it when they overthrew the Qin. The Tang would use it when they overthrew the Sui. The Ming would use it when they overthrew the Yuan. Every time a Chinese dynasty fell to a new one, the transition was narrated in terms of the template that Tang and Jie established at Mingtiao. The specific details changed. The structure never did.

---

And so, at about 1600 BCE, the world we have been following in this book ends. The Neolithic cradle, the painted pottery villages, the jade workshops, the walled towns, the legendary sage-kings, the flood, the first dynasty, the first palace, the first bronzes, the first decisive battle — all of it is complete. The **Shang Dynasty** takes over from the Xia, inherits the entire cultural toolkit we have watched being built over five thousand years, and adds two things that will change everything: **writing** (the oracle bone script, inscribed on turtle shells and cattle bones to record royal divinations, the oldest undisputed form of Chinese writing) and **monumental bronze** (the massive ritual vessels that would become the defining art of the Chinese Bronze Age). Those are the next TL's story.

What matters for this book is that nothing the Shang would add was fundamentally new. The Shang didn't invent Chinese civilization. They **inherited** it — from the Liangzhu jade workers, from the Longshan potters, from the Taosi astronomers, from the Erlitou bronze casters, from the Hongshan dragon carvers, from the Banpo villagers, from the Yangshao painters, from the Jiahu flute players, from the Peiligang millet farmers, from the Yangtze rice farmers. Every single thing that later Chinese civilization would consider essential to its own self-understanding — rice and millet agriculture, jade ritual, dragon imagery, ancestor worship, the dynastic cycle, the Mandate of Heaven, the palace architecture, the bronze tradition, the calendar, the cosmological model of round sky and square earth, even the basic layout of a proper capital city — all of it was already in place, in rudimentary or advanced form, by 1600 BCE. The Shang added writing, scale, and military power. They added nothing essential that wasn't already there.

This is the deepest point about ancient China, and it is worth stating one more time. Chinese civilization was not founded by a single dynasty or a single emperor. It was not imported from the Near East. It did not spring fully formed from the forehead of the First Emperor. It was **assembled**, piece by piece, over five thousand years, by generations of anonymous farmers, potters, jade workers, chiefs, warriors, priests, and engineers, in river valleys most of us have never heard of, working with materials most of us have never touched, producing objects we can still hold in our hands today. By the time written history begins, the civilization is already old. By the time the First Emperor unifies China, the civilization is already four thousand years old. By the time Confucius is born, it is already three and a half thousand years old.

We think of ancient civilizations as something that starts — Mesopotamia starts with Sumer, Egypt starts with Narmer, Rome starts with Romulus. China starts earlier than any of them, and it starts so slowly and so continuously that there is no moment when it began. There was no China, and then there was China, and the transition was nine thousand years of accumulated pottery sherds and jade carvings and buried ancestors and incised symbols. Somebody — no, everybody — built this. And then it just kept going.

The story we have been telling ends here, with Tang of Shang riding toward Mingtiao and Jie's army refusing to draw their swords. But the civilization we have been watching being born doesn't end here. It continues, in the next book, with oracle bones and monumental bronzes and the first writings we can read. It continues after that, in book after book, for another three thousand five hundred years, all the way to the present day.

Listen carefully enough, and you can still hear the Jiahu flutes in it.

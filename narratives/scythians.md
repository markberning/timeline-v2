# Chapter 1 — The Steppe and Its People

Here is a civilization that built nothing.

No temples. No palaces. No city walls, no ziggurats, no pyramids, no aqueducts. No writing system, no law codes, no tax records. No permanent settlement of any kind that you could point to on a map and say: this is where the Scythians lived. They didn't live in places. They lived in motion — on horseback, in felt-covered wagons, following their herds across a landscape so vast that it made the empires of the settled world look like garden plots.

And yet the Scythians terrified every civilization they touched. The Assyrians, who bragged about skinning their enemies alive, made alliances with them. The Persians, who ruled the largest empire on earth, sent an army of 700,000 men (or so they claimed) into Scythian territory and came back with nothing but exhausted horses and wounded pride. The Greeks, who thought of themselves as the pinnacle of human culture, were so fascinated by Scythian women warriors that they invented an entire mythology around them. And when the Scythians died, they were buried in graves so lavish, so filled with gold and sacrifice, that the tombs have been drawing grave robbers and archaeologists for three centuries and are still producing masterpieces that fill the great museums of Europe.

This is the story of the civilization that didn't build cities. It is also the story of one of the most dramatic problems in ancient history: how do you write the history of a people who didn't write? The Scythians left no texts. Not one. Everything we know about them comes from three sources: the writings of their neighbors (especially the Greeks, who were fascinated by them), the archaeology of their graves (which are spectacularly rich), and the frozen tombs of the Altai Mountains (which preserved organic material that normally rots away in centuries). These three windows — literary, archaeological, and frozen — give us a picture of the Scythians that is vivid but uneven. We know what their enemies thought of them. We know what they buried with their dead. We know what their tattoos looked like. But we do not know what they called themselves, what stories they told around their campfires, or what songs they sang while riding.

A note on "firsts" before we go further. The Scythians are credited with perfecting mounted archery, pioneering the military tactics that would define Eurasian steppe warfare for two millennia, and creating trade networks that prefigured the Silk Road. Some of these claims are solid. Some are contested. And all of them carry the caveat that "first" in ancient history means "first surviving evidence of." Nomadic peoples by definition leave fewer traces than settled ones. The Scythians may have inherited some of these innovations from earlier steppe peoples whose names we will never know. Keep that in mind.

---

The stage is the **Eurasian steppe** — and you need to understand its scale to understand why the Scythians were what they were.

The steppe is a belt of grassland stretching roughly 5,000 miles (8,000 km) from the plains of Hungary in central Europe to the mountains of Mongolia and Manchuria in East Asia. It is the largest grassland on earth, wider than the continental United States, and for most of human history it was one continuous ecological zone: flat or gently rolling terrain, too dry for forest, too wet for desert, covered in grasses that grow waist-high in summer and die back to frozen stubble in winter. The climate is continental in the extreme — baking in July, frozen solid in January, with winds that blow unobstructed across thousands of miles of open terrain. There are no natural barriers. No mountain ranges cross the steppe east to west (though the Ural Mountains divide it roughly in half, they are low enough and broken enough that they have never been a serious obstacle to movement). A person on horseback can ride from the Danube River to the Altai Mountains without encountering a single physical feature that requires them to stop.

This geography created a specific kind of human society. You could not farm the steppe in any sustainable way with ancient technology (the rainfall is too low and too unpredictable, the growing season too short). But you could herd animals on it — sheep, goats, cattle, and above all horses, which could survive the winter by pawing through snow to reach the frozen grass beneath. The people who lived on the steppe were **pastoral nomads**: they didn't grow food, they grew herds, and they moved with those herds in seasonal circuits, following the grass. Their homes were portable (felt tents, which the later Mongols called **yurts**; the Scythians used both tents and felt-covered wagons drawn by oxen). Their wealth was measured in livestock. Their political structures were tribal — loose confederacies of extended families and clans, united by kinship, marriage alliances, and the personal authority of a chief or king who held power as long as he could hold the loyalty of his warriors.

This was not primitive. It was a sophisticated adaptation to one of the harshest environments on the planet. But it produced a civilization that was invisible by the standards of the settled world. No walls to dig up. No foundations to excavate. No pottery sequences to calibrate. A nomadic camp that was occupied for a season and abandoned leaves almost no archaeological trace — a few postholes, some animal bones, a scatter of broken artifacts in the grass. The only places where the Scythians left substantial, permanent, archaeologically recoverable evidence of their existence were their graves.

---

The **Scythians** (SITH-ee-uns, from the Greek *Skythai*; we don't know what they called themselves) were **Iranian-speaking** pastoral nomads who dominated the western and central Eurasian steppe from roughly the ninth to the third century BCE. "Iranian-speaking" does not mean they came from modern Iran — it means they spoke a language belonging to the **Iranian branch** of the Indo-European language family, the same family that includes Persian, Kurdish, and the ancient Avestan language of the Zoroastrian scriptures. Scythian was as different from Greek as Persian is from German — same distant family tree, completely different languages. The Scythians were linguistically related to the Persians, but culturally they were worlds apart: the Persians built Persepolis; the Scythians built nothing.

Where exactly they came from is debated. The traditional view, based on later Greek and Persian sources, is that the Scythians migrated from **Central Asia** — from the grasslands east of the Caspian Sea, in the region of modern Kazakhstan and western Siberia — and moved west into the **Pontic steppe** (the grasslands north of the Black Sea, in modern Ukraine and southern Russia) sometime around 900–800 BCE. The archaeological evidence broadly supports this: Scythian-type artifacts and burial practices appear in the eastern steppe earlier than in the west, suggesting a migration direction from east to west. But "migration" is a misleading word for what nomads do. They don't pack up and move to a new homeland. They shift their seasonal routes, push into new pastures, absorb or displace the peoples already there, and gradually extend the zone they control. The Scythian "migration" was probably a gradual westward expansion over generations, not a single dramatic event.

The people they displaced were the **Cimmerians** (sih-MEER-ee-uns), another group of Iranian-speaking steppe nomads who had previously dominated the Pontic steppe. Around 750 BCE, the Scythians drove the Cimmerians out of their grasslands and south through the Caucasus Mountains into Anatolia (modern Turkey) and the Near East, where the displaced Cimmerians proceeded to cause havoc among the settled kingdoms of the region. The Cimmerians raided Phrygia, attacked Lydia, and menaced the Assyrian frontier before eventually being absorbed into the populations of Anatolia. The Greek poet Homer, writing roughly around this time, placed the Cimmerians at the edge of the known world, near the entrance to the land of the dead. By the time the real Cimmerians showed up at the gates of civilized cities, they had already become mythological.

---

The single most important thing about the Scythians was the horse.

The **horse nomad culture** that the Scythians perfected around 800 BCE was one of the most revolutionary military and social innovations in human history. Horses had been domesticated on the steppe by roughly 3500 BCE (the exact location is debated; the site of Botai in Kazakhstan was long the leading candidate, but recent DNA analysis suggests Botai horses are ancestors of Przewalski's horse, not modern domestics, and the Pontic-Caspian steppe is now favored). People had been riding them for nearly two thousand years before the Scythians. But the Scythians developed something qualitatively new: a complete way of life built around mounted combat.

The key innovation was **mounted archery** — the ability to shoot a bow accurately while riding a horse at full gallop. This sounds simple. It is extraordinarily difficult. You need to control a horse with your legs alone (because both hands are occupied with the bow), you need to aim at a target while bouncing on a moving animal, you need to fire in the brief moment when all four of the horse's hooves are off the ground (the only instant when the rider is not being jolted), and you need a bow short enough to use on horseback but powerful enough to punch through armor at distance. The **Scythian composite bow** — a compact, recurved weapon made of layers of wood, horn, and sinew glued together and cured for months — was the technological solution. It was about 3 feet (90 cm) long (short enough to clear the horse's neck), but its composite construction gave it a draw weight and range comparable to bows twice its size. A skilled Scythian horse archer could hit a man-sized target at 50 to 75 yards while riding at a gallop, with maximum range well beyond that from a stationary position.

This combination of mobility and firepower made Scythian warriors effectively impossible for infantry-based armies to defeat in open terrain. A Scythian raiding force could appear, loose a storm of arrows, and vanish before the enemy could close to sword range. If pursued, the Scythians could retreat while firing backward over their horses' rumps — a technique that would later be called the **Parthian shot** (from the Parthians, who inherited it from the steppe tradition, and from which the English expression "parting shot" may derive, though linguists debate the connection). If cornered, they could scatter in every direction, regroup at a prearranged point, and attack again. They carried everything they needed on horseback: food (dried meat and mare's milk), shelter (portable tents), water (leather skins), and a seemingly inexhaustible supply of arrows. The logistics of steppe warfare were built into the Scythian way of life. Every man was a soldier. Every meal was a ration. Every horse was a mount, a source of milk, and, in extremis, a source of meat.

The Scythians also bred their horses carefully. **Scythian horse breeding** produced sturdy steppe horses prized for endurance and speed, capable of sustained long-distance riding that would have exhausted the larger but less hardy breeds of the settled world. Scythian horses were typically smaller than the warhorses of later cavalry armies (about 13–14 hands, roughly the size of a large modern pony), but they were tough, sure-footed, and capable of surviving on grass alone — no grain feeding required. A Scythian warrior might bring three or four remounts on a long campaign, switching horses throughout the day to keep them fresh while covering 50 or 60 miles (80–100 km) per day, a pace that no infantry army could match.

Hippocrates (the Greek physician, writing around 400 BCE) described the Scythian way of life in his medical treatise *On Airs, Waters, and Places*, noting that the Scythians spent so much time on horseback that their legs became bowed and their bodies adapted to a life in the saddle. He described their **wagon dwellings** — felt-covered wagons drawn by oxen in which entire families traveled with their herds, the wagons serving as mobile homes on the grasslands. The wagon was the basic unit of Scythian domestic life: a household on wheels, following the grass.

The Scythians also developed sophisticated military intelligence and communication systems suited to the steppe. Mounted relay riders could carry messages across the Pontic steppe in days — a distance that would take a walking messenger weeks. Signal fires on hilltops could alert tribes across hundreds of miles within hours. And the decentralized tribal structure, which looked like a weakness compared to the Persian Empire's chain of command, was actually a strength in wartime: each tribal unit could fight independently, make local tactical decisions, and coordinate through pre-arranged meeting points rather than waiting for orders from a central authority. The Scythian military system was not an army in the settled-world sense. It was a distributed network of warrior bands that could mass, disperse, and re-mass faster than any infantry force could react.

The settled civilizations of the Near East, the Mediterranean, and China would spend the next two thousand years trying to figure out how to fight horse nomads. The Scythians were the first to demonstrate the problem. The Huns, the Turks, the Mongols, and the Comanche all practiced variations of the same mounted archery that the Scythians pioneered. Every time a steppe army met a civilized army in open terrain, the result was the same, from the Pontic steppe in 513 BCE to the plains of Hungary in 1241 CE: the horse archers won.

---

Cross-civ check: the Scythians' entry into the Pontic steppe (~900–750 BCE) coincides with the **Assyrian Empire** at its height in Mesopotamia, the **Zhou dynasty** in China (where the feudal Western Zhou system was about to collapse into the Spring and Autumn period), the **early Greek Dark Ages** transitioning into the Archaic period, and the **Nubian kingdom of Kush** establishing itself at Napata. In the **Andes**, the Chavín culture was just beginning its rise. In **Mesoamerica**, the Olmec civilization at San Lorenzo was at its peak. The Scythians were not an isolated phenomenon — they were the western end of a steppe world that stretched all the way to China's northern frontier, where the Zhou Chinese were already dealing with their own nomadic raiders. The steppe connected civilizations that didn't know each other existed, and the Scythians sat at the western end of that connection.

# Chapter 2 — Raiders of Empires

Around 680 BCE, the Scythians came south.

Not to settle. Not to build. To raid, to plunder, and to insert themselves into the power politics of the ancient Near East with the confidence of people who knew they could ride away from any army that tried to catch them. For the next eighty years, the Scythians would be a major player in the most politically complex region on earth, allying with and fighting against the great empires of Mesopotamia and Iran, reaching as far as the borders of Egypt, and then vanishing back into the grasslands as abruptly as they had appeared.

The timing was not accidental. The early seventh century BCE was a period of extraordinary political upheaval in the Near East. The **Assyrian Empire** (the world's first superpower, ruling from the Tigris River across Mesopotamia, the Levant, and Egypt) was at the peak of its territorial extent under kings like Esarhaddon and Ashurbanipal, but it was also under constant pressure from rebellions, frontier wars, and the rising power of two peoples on its borders: the **Medes** (an Iranian-speaking people building a kingdom in western Iran, in the Zagros Mountains) and the **Babylonians** (who would eventually destroy Assyria in 612 BCE). Into this three-way struggle between Assyria, Media, and Babylon rode the Scythians, a wild card that none of the settled powers fully understood and none could fully control.

---

The first documented Scythian leader in the Near East was **Bartatua** (bar-tah-TOO-ah, also spelled Protothyes in Greek sources), who appears in Assyrian records around 670 BCE. Bartatua did something that reveals the Scythians' political sophistication: he allied with the Assyrian Empire. According to an Assyrian text, Bartatua asked to marry an Assyrian princess — a request that the Assyrian king Esarhaddon apparently granted, after consulting the oracle of the god Shamash about whether the Scythian chief could be trusted. (The oracle's answer, if it was recorded, has not survived. The marriage apparently happened anyway.) The alliance gave Bartatua access to Assyrian diplomacy, Assyrian intelligence, and Assyrian prestige, and it gave Assyria a mobile strike force on its northern frontier that could be aimed at the Medes and Cimmerians.

The deal was pragmatic on both sides. The Assyrians had been dealing with the Cimmerians — the same people the Scythians had driven out of the Pontic steppe (Chapter 1) — for decades, and the Cimmerians were causing serious problems in Anatolia, raiding **Phrygia** (FRIJ-ee-uh, a kingdom in central Anatolia whose king, the legendary **Midas**, was said to have turned everything he touched to gold — the real Midas was a historical figure who committed suicide when the Cimmerians overran his capital) and threatening **Lydia** (the wealthy kingdom in western Anatolia whose king Croesus would later be conquered by Cyrus the Great, Persian Empire Ch 1). The Scythians, who had displaced the Cimmerians in the first place, were natural allies against them. And the Scythians got what nomadic peoples always wanted from settled empires: access to manufactured goods (metalwork, textiles, luxury items), diplomatic recognition, and the right to raid someone else's territory with at least tacit permission.

The Assyrian sources are frustratingly brief about the Scythians — the Assyrians were prolific record-keepers but they cared mostly about their own victories, and alliance partners got less ink than enemies — but the records confirm that the Scythians were a recognized political entity by the 670s BCE, with a king who could negotiate treaties, marry royalty, and command a fighting force significant enough to interest the most powerful empire on earth.

---

Bartatua's son **Madyes** (MAH-dee-eez) took the Scythian presence in the Near East to a different level entirely.

Around 650 BCE, Madyes led the Scythians to a position of dominance that Herodotus (the Greek historian, writing about two centuries later) described as a 28-year period during which the Scythians "ruled all of Asia." This is almost certainly an exaggeration — Herodotus was writing long after the fact, using oral sources, about events in a region he had never visited — but the core claim is taken seriously by most scholars: the Scythians, under Madyes, defeated the Medes and established a period of military dominance over parts of western Asia that lasted for a generation.

Herodotus says the Scythians overthrew Median rule outright. The Medes under their king **Cyaxares** (sye-AX-ar-eez) had been building a powerful kingdom in western Iran, and they had already begun the campaigns against Assyria that would eventually destroy the Assyrian Empire. But the Scythians intervened, defeating Cyaxares in battle and imposing themselves as the dominant military power in the region. For roughly a generation — the "28 years" of Herodotus, though the exact chronology is uncertain — the Scythians collected tribute, raided at will, and behaved as overlords of a territory they had no interest in governing.

The Scythian raids reached astonishing distances. Around 625 BCE, Scythian raiding parties struck deep into the Levant (the coastal region of modern Syria, Lebanon, and Israel), reaching as far south as the Philistine city of **Ascalon** (modern Ashkelon in Israel), where they sacked the city and plundered the temple of Aphrodite Urania (or rather, whatever local goddess the Greeks later identified with Aphrodite). Herodotus says the raiders reached the borders of Egypt itself, and that the Egyptian pharaoh **Psammetichus I** (psam-MEH-tih-kus) bought them off with gifts to prevent an invasion. The image is striking: Scythian horse archers, born on the grasslands of Central Asia, standing on the Mediterranean coast and staring at the pyramids. Whether they actually saw Egypt is uncertain, but they were close enough to scare the pharaoh.

The Near Eastern adventure ended as abruptly as it had begun. Herodotus tells a story — probably legendary, definitely dramatic — about how the Medes finally expelled the Scythians. Cyaxares, who had been biding his time under Scythian domination, invited the Scythian leaders to a feast. The Scythians came, drank themselves insensible (a weakness that Herodotus and other Greek writers attributed to them consistently), and were massacred. The surviving Scythians withdrew north through the Caucasus and back to the Pontic steppe.

The feast-and-massacre story is a literary trope — similar tales appear in many ancient cultures — and may well be fiction. What is not fiction is that the Scythians were expelled from the Near East sometime around 625–600 BCE and did not return. The Medes, freed from Scythian pressure, immediately turned on Assyria. In 612 BCE, a combined Median and Babylonian army sacked the Assyrian capital of **Nineveh**, and the Assyrian Empire — the most feared military power the ancient world had ever known — collapsed within three years. The Scythians had been a critical factor in delaying that collapse: their alliance with Assyria and their domination of the Medes had given the Assyrian Empire an extra generation of life. When the Scythians left, the balance of power shifted, and Assyria died.

---

What did the Scythians get out of their Near Eastern adventure?

First, they got rich. The plunder from decades of raiding in Mesopotamia, the Levant, and Iran — gold, silver, metalwork, textiles, weapons — poured back into the Pontic steppe and fueled the explosion of luxury goods that characterizes Scythian culture from the seventh century onward. The earliest royal kurgans (burial mounds) with spectacular gold artifacts date to precisely this period, and the connection is probably not coincidental: the Scythian aristocracy's wealth was built, at least in part, on Near Eastern plunder.

Second, they got technology. Scythian metalwork after the Near Eastern period shows clear Mesopotamian and Iranian influences — techniques of gold granulation, filigree, and cloisonné that the steppe peoples had not previously used. The **akinakes** (ah-kih-NAH-keez), the characteristic Scythian short sword or dagger, was carried by all free men as both weapon and status symbol. Its design shows Iranian influence, and it may have been adopted or adapted during the period of contact with the Near East.

Third, they got a reputation. After the seventh century BCE, every literate civilization in the ancient world knew who the Scythians were. The Assyrians recorded them. The Babylonians mentioned them. The Persians remembered them. The Greeks wrote about them at length. The Hebrew Bible may reference them — the people called **Ashkenaz** in Genesis 10 and Jeremiah 51 are often identified by scholars as the Scythians, and the later use of "Ashkenaz" as a designation for European Jews derives from this biblical name. The Scythians had announced themselves on the stage of world history, and they would not leave it for another four hundred years.

Fourth, they got connections. The decades of contact with the Near Eastern world — with Assyrian courts, Median warriors, Levantine cities — gave the Scythian elite a familiarity with the settled world's political structures, religious practices, and luxury goods that would shape Scythian culture for centuries. The Scythians who returned to the steppe after the Near Eastern adventure were not the same people who had left it. They had seen cities, temples, palaces, and writing. They chose not to adopt any of these things permanently. But they had seen what the settled world had to offer, and they used that knowledge to position themselves as intermediaries between the steppe and the civilized world — a role they would play for the next three centuries.

Cross-civ check: the Scythian Near Eastern raids (~680–625 BCE) overlap with the final century of the **Assyrian Empire**, the rise of the **Median kingdom** in Iran, the **26th Dynasty** in Egypt (the Saite period, when pharaohs like Psammetichus I were reasserting Egyptian independence), and the early **Archaic period** in Greece. In **China**, this corresponds to the late Western Zhou and early Spring and Autumn period. The Scythians were raiding the same Assyrian Empire that was about to destroy the **Elamite civilization** (Elamite Civilization Ch 7) — Ashurbanipal's sack of Susa in 646 BCE happened during the Scythian period of Near Eastern dominance, and it is entirely possible that Scythian warriors and Elamite refugees were in the same region at the same time.

# Chapter 3 — The Kingdom of Grass

After their expulsion from the Near East around 600 BCE, the Scythians turned their attention to the territory that would define them for the next three centuries: the **Pontic steppe**, the enormous grassland stretching north of the Black Sea from the Danube River in the west to the Don River (and beyond) in the east, covering most of modern Ukraine and southern Russia.

This was the Scythian heartland. It was not an empire in any sense that the settled world would recognize — no provinces, no tax collectors, no centralized bureaucracy, no capital city. It was a territory controlled by a loose confederation of tribes, united by a shared language, shared religious practices, shared artistic traditions, and the authority of a royal clan whose power rested on military prestige, the redistribution of wealth (especially plunder), and the elaborate ritual of royal burial. The Scythians governed their world not with institutions but with relationships: kinship ties, marriage alliances, gift exchanges, and the personal loyalty of warriors to their chief.

Herodotus described the Scythian social structure as divided into groups that loosely corresponded to castes: the **Royal Scythians** (the dominant tribe, from whose ruling clan the king was chosen), the **nomadic Scythians** (the pastoral majority), and the **agricultural Scythians** (subject peoples in the forest-steppe zone to the north who farmed grain for the Scythian elite and for export through the Black Sea ports). Whether this three-part structure was as rigid as Herodotus implies is debated — nomadic societies tend to be more fluid than settled hierarchies — but the basic distinction between a mounted warrior aristocracy and a subject agricultural population is confirmed by the archaeological contrast between rich kurgan burials (the elite) and simpler graves (the commoners).

The king's power was personal, not institutional. He held authority through military success, the redistribution of plunder and trade wealth, and the ritual prestige of the royal funeral tradition (which transformed dead kings into ancestors whose tombs anchored the kingdom to the landscape). There was no bureaucracy, no written law, no formal tax system. The king collected tribute from subject peoples and tolls from traders, distributed wealth to his warriors in feasts and gift-giving, and maintained loyalty by being generous, victorious, and terrifying. When a king stopped being any of those things, he could be replaced — by assassination, by the defection of subordinate tribes, or by simple flight. The system was effective but fragile: it depended entirely on the personality of the ruler.

And yet this "kingdom of grass" controlled a stretch of territory larger than the Persian Empire. From the Danube to Central Asia, the **Scythian triad** — the three-part combination of distinctive weapons (especially the composite bow and the akinakes short sword), horse gear (bridles, bits, and saddle equipment with specific decorative styles), and **animal-style art** (the dynamic, stylized depictions of deer, panthers, eagles, and mythological creatures that are the Scythians' most recognizable artistic trademark) — has been found from Hungary to Mongolia. Wherever the triad appears, Scythian cultural influence was present. Whether every site with Scythian-style artifacts represents actual Scythian political control or merely cultural imitation is debated, but the geographic range is extraordinary: the Scythian cultural zone was the largest in the ancient world.

---

The center of this world was the burial mound.

The **kurgan** (kur-GAHN, a Turkic word meaning "fortress" or "mound," adopted into archaeological terminology) was the defining institution of Scythian culture, and the royal kurgans of the Pontic steppe are among the most spectacular archaeological monuments in the world. A kurgan is, at its simplest, a mound of earth heaped over a burial pit. But a royal Scythian kurgan was anything but simple. The largest ones rise 60 to 70 feet (18–20 meters) above the steppe — visible for miles across the flat grassland — and cover underground chambers that were essentially furnished houses for the dead.

Herodotus (writing around 440 BCE) described the Scythian royal funeral in detail that has been substantially confirmed by archaeology. When a Scythian king died, his body was embalmed — the internal organs removed, the cavity filled with aromatic herbs, the body coated with wax — and then carried on a wagon through every tribe in the kingdom for a forty-day procession. At each stop, the mourners cut off pieces of their own ears, slashed their arms and foreheads, and pierced their left hands with arrows. After forty days, the body was brought to the royal cemetery (the most important of which, according to Herodotus, was in a region called **Gerrhi** (GEHR-eye), probably in the lower Dnieper area) and placed in a large pit-chamber along with one of his wives (strangled), his cupbearer, his cook, his groom, his squire, and his messenger (all strangled), plus horses (strangled, arranged around the chamber in a circle), gold cups, weapons, and personal ornaments. A mound of earth was then heaped over the chamber, and the Scythians competed to make it as tall as possible.

This is not speculation. This is what the kurgans contain. The **Royal Kurgan Burials** of the Pontic steppe, excavated over the past two centuries, have yielded exactly what Herodotus described: human sacrificial victims, horse burials arranged in rings, gold vessels and ornaments, weapons, and personal effects. The correlation between Herodotus's text and the archaeological record is one of the most remarkable confirmations of an ancient literary source by modern science.

One year after the burial, Herodotus adds, the Scythians performed an even more gruesome rite: fifty of the dead king's young attendants and fifty of his finest horses were strangled, gutted, stuffed with chaff, mounted on stakes in a circle around the kurgan, and left as a ghostly mounted guard for the dead king. Whether this specific detail is confirmed by archaeology is less certain — some kurgans show evidence of post-funeral horse and human deposits around the mound, but the precise staging Herodotus describes has not been conclusively demonstrated. It may be embellished. The core of the description is reliable.

---

The wealth inside the kurgans is staggering.

Gold — worked into every conceivable form: plaques, pectorals, necklaces, bracelets, earrings, sword scabbards, quiver covers, cups, belt buckles, horse bridles, and clothing appliqués. The Scythian aristocracy wore gold on their bodies, strapped gold onto their horses, drank from gold cups, and were buried in gold from head to toe. The sheer quantity of gold in the major kurgans suggests that the Scythian elite controlled a significant fraction of the world's gold supply in the fifth and fourth centuries BCE — some of it looted from the Near East, some traded through the Black Sea ports, some mined from gold deposits in the Ural Mountains and the Altai.

The **Scythian Animal Style** — the artistic tradition that decorated all this gold — is one of the most distinctive art forms in the ancient world. Animals are depicted in dynamic, often violent poses: a stag curled into a circle with its legs tucked under its body and its antlers stretched along its back. A panther coiled to spring. An eagle tearing at prey. Two griffins fighting over a fallen deer. A fish twisting in water. The animals are stylized — they don't look like naturalistic portraits — but they are rendered with extraordinary energy and attention to physical detail. Muscles bunch. Tendons strain. Antlers branch into elaborate scrollwork. Ears prick forward. Eyes are alert. The overall effect is of nature in constant motion, captured in gold.

The animal style was not purely decorative. Scholars believe the animals represented cosmological concepts — the deer associated with the upper world (sky, gods), the panther or wolf with the middle world (earth, warriors), and the fish or serpent with the lower world (water, the dead). The repeated scenes of animal combat — eagle vs. snake, panther vs. deer — may represent the cosmic struggle between these realms. But this interpretation is necessarily speculative, since the Scythians left no texts explaining their art. The images are eloquent. Their meaning is uncertain.

**Scythian bronze mirrors** (handled mirrors decorated with animal motifs) were another signature product, found across the steppe and traded widely as prestige goods. The combination of weapons, horse gear, and animal-style art formed the **Scythian triad**, the material-culture package that defines "Scythian" for archaeologists: wherever you find all three together, you are looking at the Scythian cultural world.

---

Not all interactions with outsiders were violent.

Around 590 BCE, a man named **Anacharsis** (an-uh-KAR-sis) traveled from Scythia to Athens — and was remembered by the Greeks for centuries afterward as one of the wisest men they had ever encountered. Anacharsis was a Scythian prince (Herodotus says he was the brother of a Scythian king) who traveled widely in the Greek world, studying Greek customs, laws, and philosophy. Some ancient authors counted him among the **Seven Sages** of Greece — the legendary roster of the wisest men of the age — placing a Scythian nomad in the same category as Solon of Athens and Thales of Miletus. Anacharsis reportedly said that "the vine bears three grapes: the first of pleasure, the second of intoxication, the third of disgust." Greek writers loved the quote, and they loved the idea of a "noble barbarian" who cut through Greek pretension with steppe common sense.

The story of Anacharsis had a dark ending. According to Herodotus, Anacharsis returned to Scythia and was seen performing Greek religious rituals (specifically, worshipping the Mother of the Gods in the Greek manner, with cymbals and images). His own brother, the Scythian king, shot him dead with an arrow for abandoning Scythian customs. The message was clear: you could visit the Greeks, you could admire their philosophy, but you could not bring their religion home. This hostility to foreign religious practices would surface again a century later with King Scyles (Chapter 5), with the same lethal result.

---

The other great development of this period was trade.

The **Greek colonies** on the northern Black Sea coast (planted by Greek city-states starting in the seventh century BCE) became the interface between the Scythian steppe and the Mediterranean world. The most important of these colonies was **Olbia** (OL-bee-ah), founded around 647 BCE at the mouth of the Bug River (in modern southern Ukraine) by colonists from the Greek city of Miletus. Olbia was a typical Greek colonial city (agora, temples, fortification walls, Greek-speaking citizens) but its economy depended entirely on trade with the Scythian hinterland. The Greeks brought wine, olive oil, painted pottery, metalwork, and luxury goods. The Scythians brought grain, cattle hides, furs, honey, wax, and slaves. The exchange enriched both sides and created a zone of cultural contact that would shape both Greek and Scythian civilization for the next three centuries.

The **amber trade route** also passed through Scythian territory, linking the **Baltic amber** sources in northern Europe to the Black Sea and Mediterranean markets. Amber artifacts (the fossilized tree resin prized across the ancient world for its color and workability) have been found in kurgans across the steppe, confirming that the Scythians were not just consumers of trade goods but active participants in long-distance trade networks spanning from the Baltic to the Mediterranean.

Cross-civ check: the sixth century BCE (~600–500 BCE) is one of the most crowded moments in world history. While the Scythians were consolidating their steppe kingdom and trading with Greek colonies, **Cyrus the Great** was building the Persian Empire (Persian Empire Ch 1), **Confucius** was teaching in China, the **Buddha** was preaching in India, and the Greek city-states were developing democracy. The world that Karl Jaspers called the **Axial Age** — the era when the foundational ideas of major civilizations were all being formulated simultaneously — was playing out in the settled world, while the Scythians were inventing a different kind of civilization on the grasslands. They had no philosophers, no prophets, and no written laws. They had horses, gold, and a way of life that would outlast most of the Axial Age's empires.

# Chapter 4 — Darius's Humiliation

In 513 BCE, **Darius I** of Persia (ruler of the largest empire the world had ever seen, master of 23 satrapies from Egypt to India, builder of Persepolis, carver of the Behistun inscription) assembled a massive army and marched north across the Danube River into Scythian territory.

He came back with nothing.

The Persian invasion of Scythia is one of the great military debacles of the ancient world, and it contains a lesson that would be repeated, with remarkable consistency, every time a settled empire tried to conquer a nomadic people: you cannot conquer people who do not hold territory. Territory is what settled empires understand — they capture cities, they occupy provinces, they draw borders on maps. Nomads don't have cities. They don't hold provinces. They don't care about maps. And when you march an army of infantry and supply wagons into the steppe, you are fighting the steppe itself — its distances, its emptiness, its refusal to provide the fixed points that make conventional warfare possible.

---

Darius's reasons for invading are debated. Herodotus (our main source, and a detailed one for this campaign) says Darius wanted revenge for the Scythians' earlier Near Eastern raids (Chapter 2). Modern scholars have proposed more strategic motivations: securing the northern frontier of the empire, controlling the gold and grain resources of the Pontic steppe, intimidating the Greek colonies of the Black Sea coast, or simply demonstrating Persian military supremacy to the northern barbarians. Whatever the reason, Darius committed serious resources. The army he led across the Bosporus and through Thrace (modern Bulgaria and eastern Greece) was enormous — Herodotus claims 700,000 troops, a number that is wildly inflated but probably reflects a real force of 70,000 to 100,000, plus a fleet of 600 ships to secure the Danube crossing.

The campaign began impressively. Darius crossed the **Bosporus** (the strait connecting the Mediterranean to the Black Sea, at modern Istanbul) on a **bridge of boats** engineered by the Greek architect Mandrocles of Samos — one of the great engineering feats of the ancient world, linking Europe and Asia across roughly half a mile (about 700 meters) of open water. He marched through Thrace, subduing local tribes, and then crossed the **Danube** on another boat bridge, leaving a garrison of Greek Ionians (subject peoples from the western coast of Anatolia, conscripted into Persian service) to guard the bridge and wait for his return.

Then he marched into the steppe. And the Scythians did exactly what every competent nomadic force does when confronted by a larger, slower, supply-dependent army: they refused to fight.

---

The Scythian strategy was **scorched earth** (though the term is anachronistic — it was the Scythians who invented the tactic, not the other way around). As the Persian army advanced into the Pontic steppe, the Scythians retreated before them. They burned the grass so the Persian horses and pack animals had nothing to eat. They fouled the wells and water sources. They drove their herds away from the Persian line of march. They kept a day's ride ahead of the Persian advance guard, close enough to be seen, far enough to be unreachable. And whenever small Persian foraging parties or scout detachments ventured away from the main army, Scythian horse archers appeared, riddled them with arrows, and vanished.

Herodotus describes the Persian army trudging across an empty landscape for weeks — hungry, thirsty, losing horses to exhaustion and men to Scythian harassment, never finding a target to close with, never capturing a settlement (there were no settlements), never seizing a granary (there were no granaries). The steppe was doing what the steppe always does to invaders: absorbing them. The Persians were advancing into an emptiness that grew larger with every mile.

At some point, Darius sent a message to the Scythian king **Idanthyrsus** (eye-DAN-thir-sus), demanding that he either stand and fight or submit. Herodotus records Idanthyrsus's reply, which is one of the great lines in ancient literature:

"I have never fled from any man in fear, and I do not flee from you now. I am not doing anything different from what I do in peacetime. We Scythians have no cities and no cultivated land for which we might be willing to fight you. But if you wish to fight, come to the tombs of our fathers, and you will learn what kind of men we are."

In other words: we have nothing you can threaten, nothing you can take, and nothing that would make us stand still long enough for your infantry to reach us. Our only permanent possessions are the graves of our ancestors, and if you desecrate those, we will kill you. Otherwise, keep walking. We have all the time in the world.

---

Then came the riddle.

The Scythians sent Darius a set of gifts: a bird, a mouse, a frog, and five arrows. No letter. No explanation. Darius's advisors debated the meaning. The optimistic interpretation: the Scythians are surrendering earth (the mouse), water (the frog), the sky (the bird), and their weapons (the arrows). The realistic interpretation, offered by Darius's advisor Gobryas: unless you become birds and fly through the air, or mice and burrow into the earth, or frogs and dive into the swamps, you will never escape this land — and these arrows will be the death of you.

Darius, according to Herodotus, chose the optimistic interpretation. He was wrong.

The campaign continued for several more weeks, with the Scythians maintaining their strategy of retreat, harassment, and environmental denial. The Persians never caught the Scythian main force. They never found a target to besiege. They never won a battle, because no battle was offered. Eventually, Darius turned around and marched back to the Danube. He left a force in Thrace (which remained a Persian satrapy for the next two decades), but the Scythian campaign was abandoned. The Persian army crossed back over the Danube bridge — which the Ionian Greek guards had been tempted to destroy, trapping Darius in Scythia, but ultimately kept in place — and Darius returned to Persia.

The invasion had achieved nothing. No territory was gained. No tribute was imposed. No cities were captured (there were none to capture). The Persian army lost unknown thousands of men to starvation, disease, dehydration, and Scythian archery, and it returned home having demonstrated to the entire ancient world that the largest empire on earth could be humiliated by people who lived in wagons.

The comparison with other famous failed invasions is instructive. When Darius invaded Scythia, he was arguably the most powerful ruler on earth, commanding an empire that stretched from Egypt to the Indus, governing perhaps 50 million people. The Scythians had no cities, no bureaucracy, no standing army in the Persian sense, and a total population of perhaps 1 to 2 million — a fraction of a single Persian satrapy. And they won, not by fighting but by refusing to fight. Two thousand years later, the Russians would use the same strategy against Napoleon — retreating, burning crops, denying supplies, letting the vast landscape devour the invading army. The Russians had a name for it: they called it "trading space for time." The Scythians, who had more space and more time than anyone, invented the concept.

---

The significance of Darius's Scythian campaign extends well beyond the immediate military result.

It was the first well-documented case of a settled empire learning the hard way that conventional military power is useless against mobile nomads in their own territory. The lesson would be repeated endlessly: by the Romans against the Parthians (who inherited Scythian-style cavalry tactics), by the Chinese against the Xiongnu (steppe nomads on China's northern frontier), by the Crusaders against the Seljuk Turks, and by Napoleon against the Russians (who used the same scorched-earth strategy in 1812 that the Scythians had pioneered two millennia earlier). The Persian Empire narrative (Persian Empire Ch 2) notes that Darius died in 486 BCE after a reign of thirty-six years, during which he built the most sophisticated administrative state the ancient world had produced. But on the steppe, none of that sophistication mattered. You cannot administrate an empty grassland.

Darius's failed invasion also had consequences for the Scythians' western neighbors. The Persian army's march through **Thrace** (modern Bulgaria and northern Greece) on its way to and from Scythia established Persian control over the region and brought the Persian frontier to the edge of mainland Greece. This proximity was one of the factors that led to the Ionian Revolt of 499 BCE, the punitive expedition to Marathon in 490 BCE, and the full-scale invasion under Xerxes in 480 BCE (Persian Empire Ch 3). In a sense, Darius's inability to conquer the Scythians redirected his military ambitions westward, toward Greece — with consequences that shaped the entire subsequent history of Western civilization.

For the Scythians, the repulse of Darius was a source of immense prestige. They had faced the King of Kings, refused to kneel, and watched him walk away. No other people north of the civilized world had accomplished anything comparable. The Scythian kingdom entered the fifth century BCE with its reputation enhanced, its territorial control unchallenged, and its access to Black Sea trade expanding. The next century would be their golden age.

Cross-civ check: Darius's Scythian campaign (513 BCE) falls between the founding of the **Roman Republic** (traditionally 509 BCE) and the **Battle of Marathon** (490 BCE). In **China**, Confucius was alive and teaching. In **India**, the Buddha had recently died (traditionally 483 BCE). The Persian Empire at this moment was the largest political unit on earth, stretching from Libya to the Indus — and the Scythians, who had no political unit at all, had just made it look foolish.

# Chapter 5 — The Golden Age

In the century after Darius's retreat, the Scythians faced a problem that no army had been able to force on them: Greek civilization was seeping in, and it was making them rich.

Black Sea trade flooded the steppe aristocracy with Greek wine, Greek metalwork, and Greek ideas. Scythian kings married Greek women. Greek goldsmiths worked on Scythian commission. Greek merchants lived in port cities just miles from Scythian pastures. And one Greek historian decided to devote an entire book to describing a people that most of his readers had never seen. The wealth was intoxicating. The cultural entanglement was dangerous. And the central question of the Scythian golden age was not whether the Greeks and Scythians could do business together — they could, spectacularly — but how much Greek influence the steppe could absorb before the thing that made it Scythian dissolved.

The historian's name was **Herodotus** (her-OD-oh-tus), and without him, we would know almost nothing about what the Scythians believed, how they organized their society, or what their daily customs looked like.

---

Herodotus of Halicarnassus (a Greek city on the Anatolian coast, in modern Turkey) is often called "the father of history" — he was the first Greek writer to produce a systematic prose account of past events rather than mythologizing them. His *Histories*, written around 440 BCE, are a sprawling, digressive, frequently fantastic account of the Greek-Persian Wars and the peoples of the known world. **Book IV** is devoted almost entirely to the Scythians, and it remains the single most important ancient text about them.

Herodotus probably visited the Greek colony of Olbia on the northern Black Sea coast (he describes conversations with local informants that have the ring of genuine field research), and he had access to Scythian oral traditions transmitted through Greek-speaking intermediaries. His account covers Scythian origins, geography, customs, religion, warfare, and the Darius campaign (Chapter 4). It is invaluable. It is also problematic. Herodotus was a Greek writing for a Greek audience, and his account oscillates between genuine ethnographic curiosity (he describes Scythian burial customs, religious practices, and daily life with real attention to detail) and Greek cultural bias (he treats some Scythian practices as exotic curiosities and others as barbaric). He also includes material that is clearly legendary — **Scythian origin myths** involving golden objects falling from the sky, magical ancestors, and cosmic trials — alongside material that archaeology has confirmed in remarkable detail.

The most famous of the origin myths Herodotus recorded involves **Targitaus** (tar-GIT-ow-us), the legendary first Scythian, and his three sons. Golden objects fell from the sky — a plow, a yoke, an axe, and a drinking cup — and when the two older sons tried to pick them up, the gold burst into flame and drove them away. Only the youngest son, **Colaxais** (ko-LAX-ays), could grasp the golden objects without being burned. From Colaxais descended the royal Scythians; from his older brothers descended the warrior and pastoral classes. The myth is a charter story: it explains and legitimizes the social hierarchy of Scythian society (royals, warriors, commoners) by grounding it in divine selection. Similar myths of the youngest son inheriting divine favor appear in many Indo-European traditions, including the Hebrew Bible (Jacob, David).

Herodotus also records a second version of the origin story in which **Heracles** (the Greek hero) fathers the Scythian nation — a version clearly influenced by Greek mythological conventions. And he mentions a third version, from a Greek poet named **Aristeas of Proconnesus** (ah-ris-TEE-us), who claimed to have traveled deep into Scythian territory around 650 BCE and written a lost poem called the *Arimaspea*, describing one-eyed people called the **Arimaspi** and gold-guarding griffins in the far north. The Arimaspi story is almost certainly pure myth, but it reflects genuine Greek awareness that gold came from the northern steppe and that the people there were different from anyone the Greeks had previously encountered.

---

Herodotus wrote the Scythians into history. But the force that actually transformed them was not literature. It was commerce.

The economic engine of the Scythian golden age was the **Greek-Scythian Black Sea trade**.

Greek colonies along the Black Sea coast — Olbia on the Bug River, **Panticapaeum** (pan-tih-kuh-PEE-um, modern Kerch in Crimea, capital of the **Bosporan Kingdom**), and dozens of smaller settlements — served as the commercial interface between two utterly different economic systems. The Greeks had surplus manufactured goods: wine (which the Scythians loved), olive oil, painted pottery, metalwork, jewelry, and textiles. The Scythians had surplus raw materials: grain from the agricultural peoples they dominated in the forest-steppe zone north of the Pontic grasslands, cattle hides, furs, honey, wax, fish, and slaves captured in raids.

The grain trade was particularly important. By the mid-fifth century BCE, the Scythian-controlled steppe had become a major grain supplier to Athens and other Greek cities. **Scythian grain exports** through Bosporan Kingdom ports — especially Panticapaeum on the Kerch Strait, which controlled access to the Sea of Azov and the fertile river valleys of the Don and Kuban — provided enormous quantities of wheat to the Mediterranean world. Athens, whose own farmland could not feed its growing population, depended on Black Sea grain imports to the point where disruption of the supply was treated as a strategic crisis. The Bosporan Kingdom, a Greek state on both sides of the Kerch Strait that maintained careful diplomatic relations with the Scythians, grew wealthy as the middleman in this trade, and Panticapaeum became one of the richest Greek cities outside of Greece itself.

The Scythians also operated as middlemen in longer-distance trade networks. **Steppe trade routes** connected China, Central Asia, and the Mediterranean, with goods moving through Scythian-controlled territory along paths that prefigured the later Silk Road. Chinese silk, Central Asian jade and turquoise, Iranian metalwork, and Mediterranean wine and oil all flowed through the steppe corridor, and the Scythians profited from the transit. The **slave trade** was another major commerce: the Scythians were significant suppliers of slaves to the Greek world, capturing prisoners in warfare and raids and selling them through Black Sea ports like **Tanais** (on the Don River) and Olbia. The scale of the trade is hard to estimate, but Greek sources consistently list Scythian-supplied slaves among the most common in Athenian slave markets. The word "slave" in Greek (*doulos*) does not derive from "Scythian," but the association between Scythian captives and slave markets was strong enough that "Scythian" became a common slave name in Athens — an ethnic label repurposed as a mark of ownership. The economics of the relationship were blunt: the Greeks wanted labor, the Scythians had captives, and the Black Sea ports facilitated the exchange. It was one of the darker engines of the Scythian golden age.

But the most revealing product of the Greek-Scythian entanglement was not grain or slaves. It was the gold — and what it showed about how deeply two civilizations could interpenetrate without either one surrendering.

The great kurgans of the Scythian golden age — roughly 450 to 350 BCE — contained goldwork that ranks among the finest metalwork of the ancient world. Some of it was made by Scythian craftsmen in the traditional animal style. Some of it was made by Greek goldsmiths working to Scythian commissions, blending Greek technical mastery with Scythian artistic subjects. The combination produced masterpieces that are neither purely Greek nor purely Scythian but something new: a hybrid art born from the collision of two civilizations.

The **Kul-Oba kurgan** (kool OH-bah), near Kerch in Crimea, was excavated in 1830 and was the first major Scythian archaeological find. It yielded spectacular gold artifacts including a famous **electrum vessel** (electrum is a natural gold-silver alloy) showing scenes of Scythian daily life: warriors bandaging wounds, pulling teeth, stringing bows, braiding hair. The vessel is remarkable because it depicts the Scythians as they saw themselves (or at least as they wanted to be depicted), not as the Greeks saw them. The figures are bearded, wearing trousers and tunics (not Greek togas), with bows and quivers at their sides. It is one of the very few ancient artifacts that shows Scythians through Scythian eyes rather than Greek or Persian ones.

The **Solokha kurgan** (so-LOH-kah), near the Dnieper River, contained a famous **gold comb** depicting a battle scene of armored Scythian warriors — one of the finest examples of Greco-Scythian metalwork. The warriors wear Greek-influenced armor and fight in a style that blends Greek and Scythian military equipment. The comb was almost certainly made by a Greek goldsmith who had studied Scythian subjects closely, and it demonstrates the depth of cultural exchange between the two worlds.

**Scythian goldsmithing** at its peak — whether produced by Scythian or Greek artisans — combined extraordinary technical skill with a distinctive aesthetic sensibility. The animal-style pieces, with their dynamic curving forms and overlapping bodies, are unlike anything produced in the contemporary Greek, Persian, or Egyptian worlds. They belong to a visual tradition rooted in the steppe, in a culture that lived among animals, watched them constantly, and understood their movements with the intimacy of people whose survival depended on their herds.

---

The gold was Greek hands working Scythian subjects. The grain trade was Scythian labor feeding Greek cities. The cultural exchange was deep, profitable, and apparently stable. So where was the breaking point?

It was in the palace. The Scythian royal succession during the golden age tells the story of an elite that kept marrying deeper into the Greek world — and a warrior class that kept killing kings for going too far.

Around 470 BCE, **Spargapithes** (spar-GAH-pih-theez) was king of the Scythians. Herodotus records him killing an Agathyrsian king (the Agathyrsi were a neighboring people living west of the Scythians, in the Carpathian region) in a dynastic dispute — standard steppe politics. His son **Ariapeithes** (ah-ree-uh-PYE-theez), who ruled around 460 BCE, illustrates how far the entanglement had gone: he married a Thracian woman, a Greek woman from the city of Istria, and a Scythian noblewoman. Three wives from three worlds. The multi-ethnic marriage network gave Ariapeithes diplomatic reach across the Black Sea region, but it also meant his heirs were pulled in different cultural directions. Ariapeithes was assassinated by a rival king (also named Spargapithes — Scythian royal names recycled), and his son **Scyles** inherited the throne and the complications.

Around 450 BCE, **Scyles** (SKY-leez) crossed the line that Anacharsis had been killed for crossing a century earlier (Chapter 3). Scyles' mother was the Greek wife from Istria, and he had been raised partly in the Greek world. He kept a secret house in Olbia where he changed out of his Scythian clothes into Greek garments, performed Greek religious rituals, and was even initiated into the **Dionysian mysteries** (the ecstatic rites of the wine god Dionysus, involving dancing, trance, and symbolic death-and-rebirth). When his double life was discovered by his own warriors — they reportedly peered over the walls of Olbia and saw their king dancing in Greek robes with ivy wreaths — Scyles was overthrown and killed.

The pattern was now unmistakable. Anacharsis, a prince who adopted Greek religious practices, was shot dead by his own brother (Chapter 3). Scyles, a king who secretly lived as a Greek, was overthrown and executed by his own warriors. The steppe tolerated Greek goods, Greek gold, and Greek wine — Herodotus says the Scythians drank their wine unmixed with water, unlike the Greeks, who diluted theirs, and that getting drunk "Scythian-style" was a byword in Athens for going too far. But Greek religion was the line. You could trade with the Greeks. You could commission goldwork from Greek craftsmen. You could drink yourself insensible on their wine. But you could not worship their gods and remain Scythian. The boundary was religious, not economic or artistic — and it was enforced with arrows.

Meanwhile, in Athens itself, the Scythians had a peculiar presence that captured the whole relationship in miniature. Around 500 BCE, Athens employed a corps of roughly 300 **Scythian archers** — purchased as slaves — as a public police force. These **Scythian archers in Athens** patrolled the agora (marketplace), kept order at public assemblies, and functioned as the closest thing ancient Athens had to a standing police department. They appear in the comedies of Aristophanes (the Athenian comic playwright) as comic figures, speaking broken Greek with heavy accents. The irony is rich: the world's most famous democracy was policed by foreign slaves from a non-literate nomadic culture. Athenian politics was apparently too important to leave to Athenian citizens.

The golden age, then, was not simply a period of wealth. It was the period when the Scythians discovered the terms on which they could engage with the settled world without being absorbed by it. Greek grain ships, Greek goldsmiths, Greek wine — all welcome. Greek gods — lethal. The answer to how much Greek was too much Greek turned out to be precise: everything up to the altar, and nothing beyond it. It was a boundary the steppe would enforce for as long as the Scythians had the power to enforce anything at all.

Cross-civ check: the Scythian golden age (~500–350 BCE) overlaps with the **classical period** in Athens (Pericles, the Parthenon, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle), the height of the **Achaemenid Persian Empire** (which the Scythians had already humiliated), the **Warring States period** in China, and the period just before the **Maurya Empire** in India. The Greek writer **Ephorus** (EF-oh-rus), writing around 340 BCE, idealized the Scythians as "the most just of all peoples," portraying their simple nomadic life as morally superior to the corruption of settled Greek civilization. The "noble barbarian" trope — the idea that people without cities and laws are morally purer than people with them — begins here, with the Greeks projecting their own anxieties about civilization onto the Scythians. Ephorus had never met a Scythian. His idealization tells us more about Greek self-doubt than about Scythian justice.

# Chapter 6 — Warriors, Women, and the Sword of Ares

This chapter breaks from chronology to look at the Scythians as they lived — their warfare, their religion, their gender roles, and the astonishing discovery that the Greek myth of the Amazons may not have been a myth at all.

---

Scythian warfare was built around a single tactical principle: never let the enemy choose the terms of engagement.

We covered the basics in Chapter 1 — the composite bow, mounted archery, the Parthian shot, the speed advantage over infantry. But the Scythian military system was more sophisticated than simple hit-and-run. Scythian warriors fought in coordinated formations, used feigned retreats to lure enemies into ambushes, employed light cavalry screens to gather intelligence and harass supply lines, and, when necessary, could mass for a set-piece charge using heavier cavalry armed with lances and protected by **Scythian scale armor** — hundreds of small overlapping iron or bronze plates sewn onto a leather backing, creating flexible body protection suited to mounted combat. The scale armor was another Scythian innovation (or at least, they were among the earliest peoples to use it systematically), and it was good enough to stop arrows and deflect glancing sword blows while remaining light and flexible enough for horseback use.

Every free Scythian male was a warrior, trained to ride and shoot from childhood. The distinction between "army" and "population" that exists in settled societies did not apply: when the Scythians went to war, every man who could ride was a soldier. This gave them a theoretical mobilization rate far higher than any settled state — a Scythian tribe of 10,000 people might field 2,000 to 3,000 warriors, while a settled kingdom of 100,000 people might field only 5,000 to 10,000 professional soldiers. The Scythians were always outnumbered on paper but rarely outmatched in the field.

Herodotus describes several customs that shocked his Greek audience. Scythian warriors drank the blood of the first enemy they killed in battle. They scalped their enemies and hung the scalps from their horse bridles as trophies. They made drinking cups from the skulls of their most important enemies, lining them with gold. They sewed the skins of their dead enemies into cloaks. These practices may be exaggerated — Herodotus was not above repeating colorful stories uncritically — but some of them are confirmed by archaeological evidence: skull cups and scalp-taking implements have been found in Scythian contexts. The violence was real, and it was ritualized.

---

The religious world of the Scythians was a shamanic cosmos centered on the sky and the sacred fire.

The supreme deity of the Scythian pantheon was **Tabiti** (tah-BEE-tee), a goddess of fire and the hearth, whom Herodotus equated with the Greek goddess **Hestia**. Oaths sworn by Tabiti were the most sacred in Scythian law — to swear falsely by the royal hearth was punishable by death. Below Tabiti in the divine hierarchy were **Papaeus** (the sky father, equated by Herodotus with Zeus) and **Api** (the earth goddess), along with several other deities whose Scythian names and functions are only partially understood.

The most dramatic Scythian religious practice was the **Sword of Ares cult**. Herodotus describes it in vivid detail: in every Scythian district, a large brushwood platform was built, roughly three stadia (about 1,800 feet or 550 meters) square, and on top of this platform was planted an ancient iron sword. This sword was the cult object of the Scythian war god (whom Herodotus called "Ares" using the Greek name). Annual sacrifices were made at the sword: cattle and horses were slaughtered, and their blood was poured over the blade. Every hundredth prisoner of war taken in battle was also sacrificed to the sword, with blood poured over the blade and the right arm of the victim cut off and thrown into the air so that it fell on the ground separately from the body. The sword-platform was rebuilt annually with fresh brushwood, while the iron sword — the actual divine object — was maintained from year to year.

This is not a temple religion. There are no buildings, no priests in the Mesopotamian or Egyptian sense, no written rituals. It is a religion of the open steppe: sky worship, fire worship, and blood sacrifice, conducted under the open sky, around portable sacred objects. The iron sword planted in the brushwood is a god — not a representation of a god, not a symbol of a god, but the god himself, embodied in metal.

The other defining religious practice was **Scythian shamanism**. The Scythians practiced a shamanistic religion in which specialists (sometimes called **Enarees**, eh-NAR-eez) served as diviners and spiritual intermediaries. The Enarees were described by Herodotus as biological males who dressed and lived as women — he calls their condition a "female disease" inflicted by the goddess **Artimpasa** (ar-TIM-pah-sah, equated with Aphrodite Urania). Modern scholars interpret the Enarees as occupying a recognized transgender or gender-variant social role that carried religious authority — they practiced divination using strips of linden bark and were consulted on matters of spiritual importance. This is not a modern projection: Herodotus describes them as an established, recognized social category with specific religious functions, and similar gender-variant shaman roles are documented in many Siberian and Central Asian cultures down to the ethnographic present.

The most archaeologically visible religious practice was the **cannabis purification ritual**. After a funeral, Herodotus says, the Scythians constructed small tent-like structures from felt, placed red-hot stones inside, and threw hemp seeds (cannabis) onto the stones. The resulting vapor bath filled the tent with intoxicating smoke, and the participants "howled with pleasure." Herodotus presents this as a form of ritual cleansing or purification after contact with death. Archaeological finds have spectacularly confirmed the account: bronze censers, charred cannabis seeds, and the remains of felt tent-structures have been found in multiple Scythian burial contexts. The Scythians were not just casual cannabis users — they incorporated the plant into their most important religious ceremonies.

---

And then there are the women.

The question of **Scythian women warriors** is one of the most dramatic intersections of myth, archaeology, and gender in the ancient world.

The Greeks had a well-developed mythology of the **Amazons** — a nation of warrior women who lived somewhere beyond the edges of the civilized world, fought on horseback, and either killed or exiled their male children. The Amazons appear in Greek art, literature, and mythology from at least the eighth century BCE onward: Heracles fights them, Theseus abducts their queen, they besiege Athens, and Achilles kills (and may have loved) the Amazon queen Penthesilea at Troy. For centuries, scholars treated the Amazons as pure myth — a Greek male fantasy about gender inversion, a way of defining "civilization" by imagining its opposite.

Then the graves started talking.

Beginning in the 1950s and accelerating dramatically in the 1990s and 2000s, excavations of Scythian and Scythian-related burials across the Pontic steppe and Central Asia revealed something unexpected: a significant percentage of female burials contained weapons. Swords, arrowheads, spears, and in some cases full panoply (scale armor, quivers, whetstones for sharpening blades) were found in graves of biological females, often in the same martial arrangement as male warrior burials. DNA analysis and bone examination confirmed the sex of the skeletons. Some of the female skeletons showed combat injuries — arrowheads embedded in bone, healed fractures consistent with close-quarters fighting, bowed legs from a lifetime of horseback riding.

The current scholarly consensus, based on a growing body of physical evidence, is that at least some Scythian women trained for and participated in warfare. This is not surprising in a nomadic society where every able-bodied person might need to defend the camp while the men were away raiding, but the evidence goes further than occasional self-defense: some female burials are indistinguishable from male warrior burials in terms of weapons quality and quantity, suggesting that these women were recognized warriors with the same social status as their male counterparts.

The Greek Amazon myth, it appears, was not invented from nothing. It was an exaggeration and a mythologization of a real phenomenon: Scythian and related steppe cultures in which women could and did fight. The Greeks, encountering armed women on the steppe frontier, processed what they saw through their own cultural framework (in which respectable women stayed home and wove) and produced the Amazon legend. Like most myths, it contains a core of truth wrapped in layers of cultural anxiety.

How widespread female warriorship was among the Scythians is still debated. The armed female burials represent a minority of all female burials — broad regional surveys find roughly 15% to 20%, though individual cemeteries can run much higher — so it was clearly not universal, and most Scythian women were not warriors. But it was common enough to be a recognized social category, and common enough to produce the most durable warrior-woman myth in Western literature.

The most famous warrior woman in the Scythian sphere was not technically a Scythian but a member of a closely related people. Around 530 BCE, **Tomyris** (TOM-ih-ris), warrior queen of the **Massagetae** (mass-uh-JEE-tee, a nomadic confederation in Central Asia, east of the Caspian Sea), led the force that defeated and killed **Cyrus the Great** of Persia — the founder of the Achaemenid Empire, the conqueror of Babylon, the most successful military commander of his era (Persian Empire Ch 1). Herodotus tells the story with characteristic detail: Cyrus invaded Massagetae territory, used wine (which the Massagetae had never tasted) to lure and capture Tomyris's son in an ambush. The son killed himself in captivity. Tomyris, furious, gathered her army and defeated the Persian force in battle, killing Cyrus himself. She reportedly had his body decapitated and his severed head plunged into a wineskin full of blood, telling the dead king: "I warned you that I would quench your thirst for blood, and so I shall."

The story may be embellished — Cyrus's death is reported differently by different sources — but the core fact that he died fighting steppe nomads on the northeastern frontier is not seriously disputed. The greatest empire-builder of the sixth century BCE was killed by a queen of the steppe. Tomyris has been a symbol of female military leadership ever since, depicted by Renaissance painters, cited by feminist historians, and claimed as a national heroine by modern Kazakhstan.

Cross-civ check: the concept of women participating in warfare is unusual but not unique in the ancient world. The Egyptian pharaoh **Hatshepsut** ruled and may have led military campaigns around 1470 BCE. Tomyris of the Massagetae defeated and killed Cyrus the Great (see above). In **China**, the Shang dynasty queen **Fu Hao** commanded armies around 1200 BCE. But in none of these cases was female military participation a widespread cultural practice in the way it appears to have been among the Scythians. The steppe, where survival required every hand that could hold a weapon, seems to have produced a more egalitarian approach to warfare than the settled world.

# Chapter 7 — The Pazyryk Tombs

Everything we have discussed so far — the warfare, the gold, the religion, the social structure — comes either from Greek literary sources or from kurgans on the western steppe, where the climate is warm enough that organic materials (wood, leather, felt, textiles, flesh) rotted away over the centuries, leaving only metal, bone, and stone. The gold survived. The people who wore it did not. We know what Scythian jewelry looked like, but not Scythian clothing. We know their weapons, but not their saddles. We know their bones, but not their skin.

Until the Altai.

---

The **Pazyryk** (pah-ZIH-rik) frozen tombs are one of the most extraordinary archaeological discoveries of the twentieth century, and they changed everything scholars thought they knew about the Scythians.

The Pazyryk valley lies in the **Altai Mountains** (al-TYE), a rugged range in southern Siberia near the borders of Russia, China, Mongolia, and Kazakhstan — roughly 3,000 miles (4,800 km) east of the main Scythian heartland on the Pontic steppe. The tombs, dated to roughly the fifth and fourth centuries BCE, were built by a people closely related to the western Scythians: they shared the same animal-style art, the same burial customs, the same horse-centered lifestyle, and the same basic cultural package. Whether they called themselves Scythians or by some other name is unknown. Archaeologists classify them as part of the broader Scythian cultural world.

What makes Pazyryk special is ice. The tombs were dug into permanently frozen ground at high altitude. After burial, water seeped into the tomb chambers and froze, creating a sealed ice lens that preserved everything inside — including organic materials that would have rotted in any warmer climate. When Soviet archaeologist **Sergei Rudenko** (roo-DEN-ko) excavated the Pazyryk burials in 1947–1949, he found not just gold and bronze (the standard kurgan contents) but also wood, leather, felt, textiles, food, fur, and human bodies with preserved skin.

The skin changed everything.

---

The bodies in the Pazyryk tombs bore elaborate **tattoos** — and because the frozen conditions preserved the skin, the tattoos are still visible 2,500 years later. The most famous is the body from **Pazyryk 2** (a male, probably a chief), whose arms, shoulders, chest, and legs are covered with images of fantastical animals: deer with elaborately branching antlers, twisted felines, rams, birds, and mythical creatures that combine features of multiple animals. The tattoos are executed in the same animal style as the goldwork — the same dynamic, curving, interlocking animal forms that characterize Scythian art across the entire steppe. The implication is clear: the animal style was not just a decorative convention applied to metalwork. It was a total aesthetic system, applied to the human body as well as to objects, and it carried the same cosmological meanings on skin that it carried on gold.

In 1993, a Russian archaeologist named **Natalia Polosmak** (po-los-MAK) excavated a later Pazyryk-related burial on the Ukok plateau (also in the Altai) and found the preserved body of a young woman, dubbed the "**Ice Maiden**" or "Princess of the Altai," with similarly elaborate tattoos of deer and griffins on her arms and shoulders. The Ice Maiden was buried with six sacrificed horses, a meal of mutton and horse meat, and a small container of cannabis seeds — connecting her to the same ritual complex that Herodotus described for the western Scythians, 3,000 miles away.

The tattoos were not just decoration. They were markers of status and identity — the complexity and extent of a person's tattoos apparently correlated with their social rank. They may also have served as spiritual protection, marking the body for passage through the afterlife, or as cosmological maps aligning the bearer with the animal powers of the Scythian universe. But without texts explaining them, this remains interpretation, not fact.

---

Beyond the tattoos, the Pazyryk tombs preserved an astonishing range of material culture that would have been completely lost in warmer climates.

**Textiles.** The Pazyryk burials contained the oldest known **pile carpet** in the world, a knotted wool carpet roughly 6 by 6.5 feet (1.8 by 2 meters) with an elaborate design of horsemen, deer, and geometric borders, knotted at a density of about 360,000 knots per square meter. The carpet is now in the Hermitage Museum in Saint Petersburg, and its quality and complexity demonstrate that whoever made it (debate rages over whether it was produced by the Scythians themselves, by Persians, or by some intermediary culture) had access to a highly developed textile tradition. Other textiles found at Pazyryk include felt wall hangings decorated with appliqué figures, silk fragments (imported from China — some of the earliest evidence of Silk Road trade reaching the steppe), and elaborately decorated saddle covers.

**Felt.** The Scythians were masters of felt-making — compressing wool fibers into dense, warm, waterproof fabric without weaving. Felt was the material of the steppe: used for tents, clothing, saddle pads, wall hangings, and burial wrappings. The Pazyryk felt works are decorated with dyed designs showing mythological scenes, animal combat, and geometric patterns. Felt-making was probably the most important textile craft of steppe civilization, and the Pazyryk examples are its oldest surviving masterworks.

**Horse equipment.** The Pazyryk horses were buried with complete tack: bridles, bits, saddle frames, and elaborate decorative pieces including felt and leather masks that transformed the horses into mythological creatures, with antlers, beaked bird heads, and wings attached to their headgear. The horses were clearly dressed for a ceremonial or supernatural journey, not for practical use. The horse masks are among the most surreal objects in all of ancient art: real horses wearing costumes that turn them into griffins and stags, buried in frozen earth for the afterlife of a chief.

**Food.** Meals placed in the tombs — frozen and preserved — tell us what the Scythians ate: mutton, horse meat, and a fermented mare's milk drink (kumiss, still consumed across Central Asia today). Cheese remains have also been found. The diet was protein-heavy and dairy-dependent, consistent with a pastoral nomadic lifestyle.

The Pazyryk discoveries, combined with the western steppe kurgan excavations and Herodotus's literary account, give us a remarkably complete picture of Scythian material culture. We know what they wore (trousers, tunics, soft leather boots — very different from Greek or Persian dress), what they ate (meat, dairy, grain obtained through trade), what they drank (kumiss, imported Greek wine), how they decorated themselves (tattoos, gold jewelry), how they treated their horses (as partners, as sacrificial victims, as dressed-up supernatural companions), and how they went to their graves (in furnished chambers under enormous mounds, surrounded by sacrificed humans and animals, equipped for an afterlife that looked very much like this one, only more golden).

What we still don't know — and may never know — is what songs they sang, what stories they told, what they called the constellations, and what name they gave themselves. The Scythians' deliberate choice to maintain their history, genealogies, and laws through **oral tradition** rather than writing was not ignorance — they were surrounded by literate civilizations and could have adopted writing at any time. The Greeks at Olbia would have been happy to teach them. The Persians used three different scripts. The Scythians chose none of them. It was a cultural decision, possibly rooted in the belief that sacred knowledge should be memorized, not written down (a pattern seen in other Indo-European cultures — the Vedic Hindus memorized their hymns for over a millennium before committing them to writing, and the Druids of Celtic Europe reportedly refused writing on religious grounds). Whatever the reason, it means that the Scythian voice, in the most literal sense, is lost. We have their bodies, their gold, their horses, and their tattoos. We do not have a single word in their own language that they themselves chose to preserve.

The Pazyryk tombs also tell us something about the sheer geographic scale of Scythian-related culture. The same animal-style art, the same burial customs, the same horse-centered lifestyle, and the same cannabis ritual described by Herodotus for the western Scythians on the Pontic steppe (3,000 miles to the west) appear in nearly identical form in the Altai Mountains. This cultural uniformity across a distance roughly equal to the width of the continental United States is remarkable. It was not maintained by a state, by bureaucrats, by roads, or by any administrative structure at all. It was maintained by movement — by people on horses, carrying goods, ideas, and traditions along the steppe corridor that connected the Black Sea to Siberia. The steppe was its own highway, and the Scythian cultural world was its traffic.

Cross-civ check: the Pazyryk tombs (~400 BCE) are contemporary with the **classical golden age** of Athens, the height of the **Achaemenid Persian Empire**, and the **Warring States period** in China. The Chinese silk found at Pazyryk is evidence that steppe trade networks connected the Mediterranean world to China centuries before the formal "Silk Road" was established in the Han dynasty (roughly 130 BCE). The Pazyryk people were intermediaries in a trade system that linked the two ends of Eurasia — carrying Chinese silk west toward Greek colonies and Greek wine east toward Siberia — a role that the Scythian world had been playing since at least the sixth century BCE.

# Chapter 8 — Decline and Legacy

The Scythian kingdom began to crack in the middle of the fourth century BCE, and by 200 BCE it had been reduced to a Crimean rump state clinging to the last corners of a former dominion. The decline was not a single event but a compression from three directions simultaneously: Macedonian armies from the west, Sarmatian pressure from the east, and the slow erosion of the economic advantages that had sustained Scythian power for three centuries.

---

The Scythians' last great king was **Ateas** (ah-TEE-as), who ruled from roughly 350 to 339 BCE and made the most ambitious attempt in Scythian history to transform the nomadic confederation into something resembling a territorial state. Ateas unified the Scythian tribes (or at least the western Pontic tribes) under a single command, extended Scythian control to the Danube River in the west, and minted coins — the only Scythian king known to have done so, a clear sign of engagement with the Greek economic world. His realm stretched from the Don to the Danube, a distance of roughly 750 miles (1,200 km), and for a brief moment the Scythians controlled a compact, powerful state on the northwestern shore of the Black Sea.

Ateas was also, by all accounts, incredibly old. Greek sources say he was past ninety when he fought his last battle, which, even allowing for ancient exaggeration, means he was an elderly man commanding cavalry charges — a feat that says something about Scythian horsemanship, if nothing else.

His opponent was **Philip II of Macedon** — the father of Alexander the Great, the man who unified Greece under Macedonian hegemony and who was, by the standards of his era, the most dangerous military commander in Europe. In 339 BCE, Philip marched north with his Macedonian army and met Ateas somewhere near the lower Danube. The details of the battle are poorly recorded, but the outcome is clear: Ateas was killed, the Scythian army was broken, and Philip captured 20,000 women and children and vast herds of livestock. The **defeat of Ateas by Philip II** was the first major military disaster in Scythian history — the first time a settled army had beaten the Scythians in a pitched battle rather than simply failing to catch them.

The defeat mattered because it was different from the Darius campaign. Darius had invaded the steppe and been defeated by the steppe itself — the distances, the emptiness, the scorched-earth tactics. Philip defeated the Scythians in a conventional battle near the Danube, in territory where the Scythians were trying to hold ground rather than trading space for time. The lesson: when nomads try to become a territorial state — when they start holding fixed positions, minting coins, and defending borders — they become vulnerable to the same military strategies that work against any settled kingdom. The Scythians' strength was their mobility. Ateas sacrificed that mobility for territorial ambition, and Philip punished the trade.

---

Eight years later, the Scythians got a chance to remind the world that steppe warfare still worked.

In 331 BCE, **Zopyrion** (zo-PIR-ee-on), a general left in charge of Thrace by **Alexander the Great** (who was busy conquering the Persian Empire), decided to win glory by invading Scythian territory with an army of 30,000 troops. The expedition was a catastrophe. The Scythians used the same tactics they had used against Darius two centuries earlier: retreat, scorched earth, and relentless archery harassment. Zopyrion's entire army was annihilated and the general himself was killed. The disaster was so complete that it barely registers in the Greek historical sources — it was an embarrassment, and embarrassments get underreported. But it demonstrated that Scythian steppe tactics remained lethal against any army foolish enough to pursue mounted nomads into the grasslands.

---

The real threat to the Scythians came not from the west but from the east.

The **Sarmatians** (sar-MAY-shuns) were a related group of Iranian-speaking nomadic peoples from the eastern steppe — the region between the Don River and the Ural Mountains, east of the main Scythian territory. The Sarmatians spoke languages closely related to Scythian, practiced similar burial customs, fought with similar cavalry tactics, and shared the same animal-style artistic tradition. They were, in effect, the Scythians' eastern cousins — and starting around 300 BCE, they began pushing westward.

The Sarmatian displacement of the Scythians was gradual — not a single battle but a slow, steady migration pressure over the course of a century. The Sarmatians moved into the eastern Pontic steppe, took control of the pastures and trade routes that had sustained Scythian power, and pushed the Scythians westward and southward into a shrinking territory. The Sarmatians may have had a military edge, too: they placed greater emphasis on heavy armored cavalry (cataphracts) than the Scythians, who relied primarily on light horse archers. In the arms race of the steppe, heavier cavalry with longer lances could break a formation of lighter horse archers in close combat. The Scythians, who had spent centuries defeating infantry armies by staying out of reach, found themselves outmaneuvered by cavalry that could close the distance and fight them on equal terms.

By around 200 BCE, the Scythians had been compressed into **Crimea** and the lower Dnieper region — a fraction of their former range.

The pattern was familiar. It was exactly what the Scythians had done to the Cimmerians five centuries earlier: one steppe people displacing another, the newcomers taking the grasslands and the old power being pushed to the margins. The steppe is a conveyor belt, and the Scythians had ridden it west in 900 BCE. Now the Sarmatians were riding it west in their turn. The Scythians went from being the conveyor belt's riders to being its casualties.

The economic foundations of Scythian power also shifted. The Black Sea grain trade was increasingly controlled by the Bosporan Kingdom, which maintained its own relations with the Sarmatians and no longer needed Scythian middlemen. The Greek colonies had matured into independent city-states with their own military forces, and they were less willing to pay tribute to a weakened Scythian elite. The gold supply from the Ural mines was now in Sarmatian territory. The wealth that had sustained the kurgan-building aristocracy dried up, and without wealth to redistribute, the kings lost the loyalty of their warriors. It was the classic nomadic death spiral: territorial loss leads to economic decline, which leads to political fragmentation, which leads to further territorial loss.

---

The remnant Scythian kingdom centered itself on **Scythian Neapolis** (neh-AH-po-lis, meaning "New City" in Greek), a fortified settlement in central Crimea (near modern Simferopol) that became the last capital of the Scythians. Scythian Neapolis was something new in Scythian history: a permanent, fortified, stone-walled settlement — exactly the kind of structure that the steppe-era Scythians would never have built. The nomads had finally settled down, not by choice but because they had nowhere left to roam.

**King Skilurus** (SKIL-oo-rus) ruled the Late Scythian kingdom from Neapolis around 250–225 BCE and briefly restored something of Scythian power, building fortifications, minting coins, and competing with the Greek cities of the Crimean coast for control of trade and territory. His son **Palakus** (PAH-lah-kus) continued the struggle, fighting a desperate war against the expanding power of **Mithridates VI of Pontus** (mith-rih-DAY-teez), the ambitious king of a Black Sea state in northern Anatolia who was building his own empire across the region.

The end came around 110 BCE, when Mithridates' general **Diophantus** (die-oh-FAN-tus) invaded Crimea with a professional army and crushed the Late Scythian kingdom. Palakus rallied Sarmatian allies in a final attempt to resist, but the coalition was defeated and the Late Scythian state was effectively finished as an independent political entity. The Scythians did not vanish entirely — communities identifying as Scythian persisted in Crimea for several more centuries, and the geographer **Strabo** (writing around 25 BCE) still described the remnant kingdom at Neapolis — but they were a shadow of the power that had humiliated Darius and traded gold with Athens.

---

Much later, writers who had never seen a living Scythian were still thinking about them.

The Roman poet **Ovid** (OV-id), exiled to the Black Sea port of **Tomis** (modern Constanta in Romania) in 8 CE, spent his final years among the Getae and other Scythian-influenced peoples, writing bitter elegies describing the frozen landscape, the raids, and the customs of the steppe peoples surrounding him. Ovid's descriptions are poetic rather than ethnographic, but they show that the Scythian cultural imprint on the Black Sea region was still visible a century after the Scythian state had collapsed.

Even later, in the second century CE, the satirist **Lucian** (a Syrian Greek writing in the Roman Empire) composed *Toxaris*, a dialogue in which a Scythian and a Greek debate the nature of friendship. The Scythian wins the argument. Lucian was playing with the old "noble barbarian" trope that Ephorus had established four centuries earlier (Chapter 5), but the fact that a writer in Roman-era Syria could still use "Scythian" as a recognizable cultural category — one that his audience would understand and respect — shows how deeply the Scythians had embedded themselves in the Mediterranean literary imagination.

---

What the Scythians left behind is less a civilization than a blueprint.

They pioneered the mounted nomadic warfare that the Huns, Turks, Mongols, and Comanche would later use to reshape Eurasia and the Americas. They created trade routes connecting China to Greece that prefigured the Silk Road by centuries. They produced an art tradition — the animal style — that spread from Hungary to Mongolia and influenced the decorative arts of every culture it touched. They demonstrated that a civilization could be powerful, wealthy, and culturally sophisticated without building a single city, writing a single word, or planting a single field. And they left behind graves so richly furnished that 2,500 years later, the gold still glows in the display cases of the Hermitage, the British Museum, and the Museum of Historical Treasures of Ukraine.

The **Gold Pectoral of Tovsta Mohyla** (TOV-stah mo-HEE-lah, a kurgan in Ukraine excavated in 1971) may be the single most stunning artifact of the Scythian world. It is a massive crescent-shaped gold breast ornament weighing over two pounds, divided into three registers: the bottom register shows lions and griffins attacking horses and cattle (the cosmic hunt, violence in nature); the middle register is a delicate gold filigree of scrolling vines (the vegetable world, the steppe in flower); and the top register shows scenes of Scythian daily life — two men sewing a shirt from a sheepskin, a man milking a mare, a youth holding an amphora, a foal nursing. The pectoral is simultaneously a work of art, a cosmological diagram, and a snapshot of daily life. It is the Scythian civilization compressed into a single object: beauty, violence, gold, horses, grass, and the open sky.

The **Chertomlyk kurgan** (cher-TOM-lik), near the Dnieper River, yielded a famous silver amphora depicting Scythians taming wild horses — a scene of such naturalistic detail that you can see the strain in the horses' necks and the concentration on the riders' faces. The **Alexandropol kurgan** (al-ex-AN-dro-pol), one of the largest Scythian burial mounds in Ukraine at over 65 feet (20 meters) high, contained multiple burials with horse sacrifices and elaborate gold ornamentation. These kurgans, and dozens more like them, are the Scythian equivalent of the pyramids or Persepolis — monumental expressions of power and belief, except that the Scythians built theirs underground and covered them with the grass of the steppe.

The tragedy of Scythian archaeology is that most of the major kurgans were looted long before modern archaeologists reached them. From the medieval period onward, steppe peoples and treasure hunters dug into the mounds, ripping out gold and scattering bones. The tsars of Russia organized systematic kurgan excavation in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries — Tsar Peter the Great's collection of Scythian gold, begun in 1715, became the core of the Hermitage Museum's famous Siberian Collection. Much of what was found went into private collections and royal treasuries rather than into scientific documentation. The kurgans that survived unlooted into the twentieth century — Pazyryk, Tovsta Mohyla, a handful of others — are miracles of archaeological luck, windows into a world that was systematically dismantled by centuries of grave robbery.

---

The deepest irony of Scythian history is this: the civilization that left no written records has become one of the best-documented cultures of the ancient world.

Herodotus wrote them. The Persians failed to conquer them. The Greeks traded with them, hired them as police, and built myths around them. Their kurgans preserved their gold, their art, and (in the frozen Altai) their very bodies. And the questions they raise — about the relationship between civilization and cities, between literacy and culture, between mobility and power — are questions that still cut against the assumptions of the settled world.

The Scythians proved that you don't need cities to be civilized. You don't need writing to have history. You don't need walls to have power. What you need is horses, gold, a good bow, and the willingness to ride forever across a landscape that has no end.

They rode until the grass ran out.

Cross-civ final check: the decline of the Scythians (~350–110 BCE) overlaps with the **conquests of Alexander the Great** (334–323 BCE), the **Qin unification of China** (221 BCE), the **Maurya Empire** in India under Ashoka (268–232 BCE), and the **rise of Rome** as a Mediterranean power. The Sarmatians who displaced the Scythians would themselves be displaced by the Huns around 370 CE, who would be displaced by the Turks, who would be displaced by the Mongols. The steppe conveyor belt kept running for another 1,500 years after the Scythians. Every new wave of steppe nomads used the same tactics the Scythians had pioneered: mounted archery, strategic retreat, and the absolute refusal to fight on the enemy's terms. Genghis Khan, riding across the same grasslands in 1220 CE, was the Scythians' direct tactical heir. The civilization that built nothing built the template for the most effective military system in pre-modern history.

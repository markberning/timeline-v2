# Khmer Empire — The City That the Forest Kept

**Goal:** Narrative for the Khmer Empire (802–1431 CE and after), the Hindu-then-Buddhist state in mainland Southeast Asia that built Angkor — the largest religious monument on Earth and, it turns out, the largest pre-industrial city ever found. This is a story with three big arcs and one big argument. The arcs: how a scatter of rice kingdoms on the lower Mekong became an empire ruled by god-kings; how those kings spent six centuries pouring a civilization's entire surplus into stone temples and the water that fed the people who built them; and how the whole thing wound down — not in a single dramatic fall, but in a long, contested, still-argued retreat. The argument running underneath is about what "collapse" even means, because Angkor is the case study every textbook reaches for, and almost everything the textbooks used to say about it has been rewritten in the last fifteen years by tree-rings and lasers. Along the way: a temple shaped like the universe, two hundred giant stone faces nobody can firmly identify, a Chinese diplomat's once-in-history eyewitness report, and a "lost city" that was never actually lost.

**Status:** First draft, 10 chapters.

**A note on names and pronunciation.** The Khmer wrote their kings' names in Sanskrit, the prestige language of the court, and their administration in Old Khmer, the everyday language. Royal names almost all end in *-varman* (Sanskrit for "protected by," "shield of"), so the kings run together fast: Jayavarman, Suryavarman, Indravarman, Yasovarman. There were *eight* kings named Jayavarman alone. This narrative gives a pronunciation guide in parentheses the first time a hard name appears, spells the regnal numbers out, and reintroduces each major king with a one-line tag. When the kings start to blur, that is not you failing to keep up — it is a genuine feature of a dynasty that recycled about six names for six centuries. Dates are CE unless marked BCE.

**A note on "first," on numbers, and on what we don't know.** Angkor is the most-studied archaeological landscape in Southeast Asia and *still* one of the most argued-over. A surprising amount of what follows is contested, and this narrative will say so out loud, repeatedly, because the contest is half the story. Specifically: the exact meaning and date of Jayavarman II's 802 ceremony, the population of greater Angkor, how intensively its farmland was worked, what the giant Bayon faces represent, whether Jayavarman VII's building spree wrecked the economy, and above all *why* Angkor stopped being a capital — these are live scholarly fights, not settled facts, and you should distrust any source (including this one) that sounds too certain about them. As with every history in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of." Tropical soil destroys wood, cloth, and bone; almost everything non-stone that the Khmer made is gone. We are reconstructing a civilization from its temples, its reservoirs, about 1,200 stone inscriptions, one short foreign travelogue, and — only since about 2007 — what lasers can see under the trees. Absence of evidence, in a rainforest, is very much not evidence of absence.

# Chapter 1 — Before Angkor: Funan, Chenla, and Indianization

To understand Angkor you have to start with water, because the whole civilization is, in the end, an argument about water — how to catch it, store it, move it, worship it, and what happens when it stops behaving.

Picture mainland Southeast Asia: a fist of land hanging below China, drained by a handful of huge rivers running roughly north to south. The biggest is the **Mekong** (MAY-kong), which falls out of the Tibetan plateau and runs about 2,700 miles (4,350 km) — roughly the distance from New York to Las Vegas — before it spreads into a delta and empties into the South China Sea. Near the bottom of its run, in what is now Cambodia, the Mekong does something almost no other river on Earth does. A tributary called the **Tonlé Sap** (TON-lay SAHP) connects it to a large shallow lake of the same name. For most of the year the lake drains *into* the Mekong, the normal direction, downhill to the sea. But when the summer monsoon dumps its rain and the Mekong swells, the main river backs up so hard that it shoves water the wrong way up the tributary, and the Tonlé Sap River **reverses and flows backward**, pouring into the lake instead of out of it. The lake balloons from roughly 1,000 square miles to as much as 6,000 — it roughly quadruples — drowning the surrounding forest. Then, as the dry season comes, it drains back down and leaves behind two things every civilization wants: a thick layer of fresh silt, and an almost unbelievable quantity of fish. The flooded forest is a fish nursery. When the water retreats, the fish are concentrated into the shrinking lake and the draining channels in numbers that, even today, make the Tonlé Sap one of the most productive inland fisheries in the world.

That reversing lake is the reason a great civilization grew up *here*, inland, rather than only on the coast. It guaranteed protein and it guaranteed fertile, rebuilt soil, year after year, for free. Everything else in this book — the god-kings, the hundred-square-mile city, the temples the size of small mountains — is built on the surplus that the lake and the monsoon handed to whoever could organize the people around them.

---

People had been organizing around it for a long time before there was anything you could call Cambodia. By the last few centuries BCE, Mon-Khmer-speaking farming communities along the lower Mekong were growing wet rice, smelting iron, and trading along the coast. A word on who these people were, because it matters later: the Khmer language belongs to the **Austroasiatic** family (also called Mon-Khmer), which is its own deep linguistic root, unrelated to Chinese, unrelated to the Indian languages, and — this will matter enormously in Chapter 6 — completely unrelated to the Austronesian languages spoken by the seafaring peoples of the islands and the central Vietnamese coast. Austroasiatic and Austronesian are as far apart as English and Chinese; a Khmer speaker and a Cham speaker could no more understand each other than an Englishman and a Cantonese speaker. The Khmer were, and are, the indigenous people of the lower Mekong. They did not arrive from somewhere else within the span of this story; they were already there when the written record begins, which in this region is frustratingly late and almost entirely in stone.

The first organized state we can name appears not in local records but in Chinese ones. Around the first century CE, Chinese histories describe a wealthy kingdom in the Mekong delta they called **Funan** (foo-NAHN). Funan's great asset was a port — the archaeological site is called **Oc Eo** (ock-AY-oh), in the delta of modern southern Vietnam — and the excavations there are startling. Roman coins from the second century. Indian carved gemstones and gold jewelry. Han Chinese bronze mirrors. A canal system linking the port to inland settlements. Funan was a hub on a trade route that already ran, in relay, from the Mediterranean to China, four hundred years before Angkor existed. (The same monsoon-driven, layover-port trade world produced Srivijaya out on the islands a few centuries later; Funan is the mainland prologue to that maritime story, Srivijaya Chapter 1.)

Funan matters less for what it was than for what it imported, because the single most important thing that came up the Mekong was not Roman silver. It was a package of ideas from India.

---

Beginning around the early centuries CE and accelerating for the next half millennium, the elites of Southeast Asia adopted an enormous bundle of Indian culture: the **Sanskrit** language (India's ancient sacred and literary language, the Latin of the Hindu-Buddhist world) for religion and royal display, Indian-derived scripts, the Hindu gods (especially **Shiva**, the great god of destruction, transformation, and ascetic power, and **Vishnu**, the god who preserves the cosmic order and descends into the world to save it), Buddhism, Indian temple architecture, Indian-style coronation rites, Indian law codes and statecraft manuals, the Indian calendar, and the deep Indian idea that a king is the earthly pivot of a divinely ordered cosmos. Historians call this **Indianization**, and it is one of the largest cultural transfers in human history, comparable in scale to the spread of Greek culture across the Near East after Alexander, or the spread of Chinese writing and Confucian statecraft into Korea (Ancient Korea Chapter 4) and Japan.

Here is the crucial thing about Indianization, because the reader will reasonably suspect this is a story of conquest: it wasn't. India never invaded Southeast Asia. There was no Indian army, no Indian colonial administration, no Indian settlement. So how does an entire region come to write in your script, pray to your gods, and crown its kings with your rituals without you sending a single soldier? The mechanism — and the writing rule here is that you have to explain the mechanism, not just announce the result — was a three-way pull. Trade brought Indian merchants and, with them, Indian holy men. Local rulers found Indian religion and Indian statecraft *useful*: a Sanskrit-literate Brahmin priest (a member of the hereditary Hindu priestly class, the keepers of ritual and sacred text) could give a jungle chief a genealogy descending from the gods, a coronation that made him a *king* rather than a strongman, a writing system for taxes and a cosmology that explained why everyone should obey him. And Indian prestige was simply attractive — adopting it was how you signaled you were a serious civilization. So local elites pulled Indian culture toward themselves, selectively, and ran it through their own institutions. The result looked Indian on the surface and stayed thoroughly Khmer underneath. The Funanese founding legend captures the whole process in one image: a Brahmin from across the sea named Kaundinya marries a local **naga** (NAH-gah) princess — a serpent-spirit of the water and the soil — named Soma, and their line rules. The foreign sacred man weds the indigenous water-goddess. That is Indianization as the Khmer themselves told it, and the multi-headed naga serpent will be carved onto every balustrade at Angkor seven centuries later.

---

Funan faded in the sixth century and was absorbed by an inland Khmer power the Chinese called **Chenla** (CHEN-lah). Chenla is the direct political ancestor of the Khmer Empire — same people, same river, more inland, more agricultural. Its kings left the first substantial body of local evidence: stone temples and inscriptions. The single most important is unglamorous and precious. The inscription known to scholars as **K.557**, dated to the equivalent of 611–612 CE, is the oldest securely dated text in the Khmer language anywhere — and one of the oldest dated texts in *any* Southeast Asian vernacular. From the very start, the Khmer wrote in two registers at once: ornate Sanskrit verse for the gods and the king's glory, and plain Old Khmer prose for the things that actually ran the kingdom — who owned which rice field, who owed how many days of labor, how many cattle and slaves were given to which temple. The poetry is in Sanskrit; the accounting is in Khmer. Historians have been quietly grateful for the accounting ever since.

Chenla also built. The brick temple-city of **Isanapura** (ee-shah-nah-POO-rah), modern Sambor Prei Kuk, raised by King Isanavarman I around 616, is the largest pre-Angkorian monument complex and now a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Its octagonal brick towers, its "flying palace" reliefs, and above all its *idea* — a built mountain of shrines as the seat of a sacred king — are the prototype. Angkor did not invent the Khmer temple. It inherited a tradition already two centuries deep and rebuilt it, bigger, in sandstone.

But Chenla in the eighth century was not an empire. Chinese sources describe it splitting into a "Land Chenla" up the river and a "Water Chenla" down by the coast, and the southern, coastal half seems to have fallen under the influence of a sea power the inscriptions call simply "Java" — most likely the Sailendra dynasty associated with the Srivijayan world (Srivijaya Chapter 3). So at the close of the eighth century the lower Mekong is what it had been at the start of this chapter: fertile, populous, Indianized, literate, temple-building — and politically scattered, with a chip on its shoulder about foreign overlordship. It is waiting for someone to walk up a mountain and declare it whole. A man named Jayavarman is about to.

# Chapter 2 — Jayavarman II and the God-King

Every empire has a founding scene. The Khmer founding scene takes place on top of a mountain, in the year 802, and almost everything about it is reported to us by a single inscription carved 250 years after it supposedly happened. Hold both of those facts in your head at once, because the gap between them is where the historians live.

The mountain is **Phnom Kulen** (puh-NOM koo-LEN), a forested sandstone plateau about 30 miles (48 km) northeast of where Angkor would later rise. The man is **Jayavarman II** (JAI-ah-var-man), and according to the tradition he had returned from "Java" — the same foreign overlord power Chenla had been chafing under — to reunify the Khmer lands. In 802, on Phnom Kulen, a Brahmin priest performed a ritual that, the texts say, established Jayavarman II as **chakravartin**, a "universal monarch," and instituted the cult of the **devaraja** (DAY-vah-RAH-jah). The word literally means "god-king," and the ceremony's stated political purpose was blunt: to make Cambodia (in the inscription's phrasing) "no longer dependent on Java," ruled by a single sovereign who answered to no outside power.

That is the founding myth of the Khmer Empire, and Cambodian tradition has dated the nation to that moment for a thousand years. Now the honest part. The source for nearly all of it is the **Sdok Kok Thom inscription** (suh-DOK kawk TOM), commissioned in 1052 by a family of priests to glorify their own hereditary office — the office of administering the devaraja cult. It is a magnificent document and a self-interested one, written a quarter of a millennium after the events it describes by people whose status depended on those events having happened a particular way. Modern historians therefore treat the 802 scene less as a transcript and more as a *charter myth*: a later dynasty's authorized origin story, probably built around a real and important king, but smoothed into legend. Even what the devaraja *was* is contested. The romantic reading — a literal cult of the king's divine essence, housed in a portable golden image — comes mostly from Sdok Kok Thom's own framing. Other scholars argue the term refers more modestly to a state deity, a "king of the gods" (an aspect of Shiva), whose worship the monarch sponsored, rather than the worship of the monarch himself. The distinction sounds academic; it is actually the whole question of what kind of thing a Khmer king was, and the texts genuinely do not settle it.

What is *not* in serious doubt is the structural fact, because the structure is carved across five centuries of stone. Khmer kingship fused the ruler and the divine. The king built, during his lifetime, a state temple at the symbolic center of his capital, usually a stepped pyramid representing the cosmic mountain, housing a god (most often a **linga**, the abstract pillar-form of Shiva) that was bound up with the king's own sacred identity and frequently named for him. When he died, he merged with that god. This is divine kingship, and the reader should know it is not unique to Cambodia — Egypt's pharaohs were living gods (Old Kingdom Egypt Chapter 2), the Maya lords were *k'uhul ajaw*, "holy lords," who performed as gods on pyramid stages (Maya Civilization Chapter 4), and Mesopotamian kings occasionally claimed divinity (Mesopotamia Chapter 5). The Khmer version's distinctive feature is the sheer physical scale of the building it justified, and the fact that the justification held for six hundred years.

---

After the mountaintop ceremony, Jayavarman II came down and ruled mostly from the lowlands, finally settling at a capital called **Hariharalaya** (huh-ree-huh-RAH-luh-yah) at modern Roluos, southeast of the future Angkor. The name honors **Harihara**, a single deity who is Shiva and Vishnu fused into one body, left side and right side — a very Khmer solution to the question of which great Hindu god to back: both, in one statue. Jayavarman II reigned, by the traditional dating, an implausibly long time and died around 835. His son Jayavarman III is remembered mainly as a legendary elephant-hunter, which is roughly all the early inscriptions bother to tell us, and is a fair warning about how thin the ninth-century record is. We are reconstructing the founding dynasty of a six-century empire from a handful of temple-dedication texts and one self-serving inscription from 1052. The honest scholarly position is that Jayavarman II was real, was important, and probably did unify and assert independence — but that the tidy story of a single 802 founding moment is the kind of clean origin every dynasty wants and few actually have.

The reign that really set the template was the next strong one: **Indravarman I** (in-druh-VAR-man), who ruled from about 877. He did three things, and every later Khmer king did the same three things in the same order, so it is worth stating them as a formula. First, he built a **baray** (bah-RYE), a vast rectangular reservoir — his was called the Indratataka, an embankment-walled lake of standing water. Second, he built a temple to his ancestors: **Preah Ko** (preeah KOH), six brick towers whose dedicatory inscription is one of our best sources for the murky royal genealogy. Third, he built his own state temple-mountain: **Bakong** (bah-KONG), the first major sandstone temple-mountain at Angkor, a stepped pyramid for Shiva. Water, ancestors, mountain. A king proved he was a king by giving his people a reservoir, his dynasty a shrine, and his god a mountain. Hold onto that triad. For the next five hundred years, almost every chapter of this book is a variation on those three construction projects, getting bigger.

# Chapter 3 — The Hydraulic City: Yasodharapura and the Barays

In 889 a king named **Yasovarman I** (yah-show-VAR-man) moved the capital a few miles northwest, onto the plain between the Tonlé Sap lake and the Kulen hills, and founded a city he named **Yasodharapura** (yah-show-dah-rah-POO-rah) after himself. That plain is the place we call **Angkor**. The word "Angkor" is just the Khmer pronunciation of the Sanskrit *nagara*, "city" — it is not a name, it is the noun "the City," the way Romans said *urbs*. Yasodharapura was rebuilt, expanded, and re-centered many times over the next five centuries, which is why "when was Angkor founded" has no clean answer: there was no single Angkor, but a moving, growing palimpsest of royal cities on one plain. 889 is when the plain became the center, and it stayed the center, with interruptions, until 1431.

Yasovarman I followed the formula and supersized it. His state temple, **Phnom Bakheng** (puh-NOM bah-KENG), sits on a real hill in the middle of the plain — a natural Mount Meru, improved with a stone pyramid on top. **Mount Meru**, in Hindu and Buddhist cosmology, is the axis of the universe: a colossal mountain at the world's center, around which the continents and oceans are arranged in concentric rings, and on whose summit the gods live. This is the single most important idea in Khmer architecture, so it is worth saying plainly why every big temple is a stepped pyramid: it is not decoration and it is not just grandeur. The temple *is* Mount Meru, modeled at buildable scale. Its tower or towers are the peak where the gods live; its enclosure walls are the mountain ranges that ring the world; its moat is the cosmic ocean. When a king built a temple-mountain, he was building a working scale model of the universe with himself, and his god, installed at the center of it. Phnom Bakheng's design encodes this with a near-obsessive numerology: counts of towers that map onto the days of the lunar month and the cosmic cycles. This is documented in the structure itself, not a modern over-reading — the Khmer told us in stone what the numbers meant.

And the reservoir scaled up to match. Yasovarman built the **East Baray**, an artificial rectangular lake about 4.5 miles long and 1.1 miles wide (7.5 by 1.8 km) — you could not see across it. Later kings added the even larger **West Baray**, which still holds water today. These were not natural lakes; they were embanked, engineered sheets of standing water, among the largest hand-built structures of the pre-modern world, and what they were *for* is the central technical debate of Angkorian studies. Park that question for a moment; it gets its own argument at the end of this chapter.

---

The tenth century is the Khmer equivalent of a turbulent succession-soap, and it is worth one paragraph because it shows the empire's permanent structural weakness. There was no firm rule of succession — not strict primogeniture, not anything reliable — so the throne was contestable, and it was contested, repeatedly, by brothers, uncles, nephews, and usurpers, often by war. Around 928 a usurping king, **Jayavarman IV**, abandoned Angkor entirely and built a brand-new capital 75 miles (120 km) to the northeast at **Koh Ker** (koh KAIR), crowned by Prasat Thom, a steep seven-tiered sandstone pyramid that looks more like a Mesoamerican structure than a classic Angkor temple. It is a strange, brutal, magnificent place, and it lasted barely twenty years. In 944 **Rajendravarman II** (rah-jen-druh-VAR-man) brought the court back to Angkor and rebuilt it, ending the experiment. The pattern — a strong king builds, a disputed succession, sometimes a capital moves and comes back — repeats so often that you should treat it as the empire's resting heartbeat rather than as crisis. Empires with no succession rule pay for it forever; the Khmer paid in periodic civil wars and the occasional abandoned city for six straight centuries, and the surprising thing is not that it eventually failed but that it took that long.

The tenth-century revival under Rajendravarman and his successors produced the temple that, of all of them, people tend to fall in love with. **Banteay Srei** (ban-tay SRAY), consecrated in 967, breaks every rule in this book and is better for it. It is tiny — you could walk its whole interior in a few minutes. It was built not by a king but by a Brahmin royal teacher named **Yajnavaraha** (yahj-nah-VAH-rah-hah), which is itself remarkable: a courtier, not a monarch, endowing a major temple. And it is carved from a deep rose-pink sandstone in relief so fine, so deep, so crisp that nineteenth-century French visitors refused to believe it was a thousand years old. (One of them, the writer André Malraux, was so taken with it that in 1923 he tried to *steal* a cache of its carved panels, prised four blocks loose, and was arrested — a story we will return to in Chapter 10, because the looting of Angkor is its own long chapter.) Banteay Srei is the proof that Khmer building was never only about size. The biggest temple in the world is in this story, and so is one of the smallest, and the small one is arguably the more perfect.

---

Now the argument this chapter has been deferring: what were the barays *for*?

The romantic and long-dominant answer comes from the French archaeologist **Bernard-Philippe Groslier**, who in 1979 proposed that Angkor was a "hydraulic city" (*cité hydraulique*): a vast engineered landscape in which the giant reservoirs fed a network of canals that irrigated enough rice to support an enormous population, possibly producing several harvests a year. In this reading the waterworks are the whole secret of Angkor's power, and — crucially — their eventual silting-up and breakdown is the secret of its fall. It is a beautiful, total theory, and you will meet it again in Chapter 10 as a defendant.

The skeptics' answer, dominant for a while after Groslier, was nearly the opposite: that the barays were primarily *cosmological and symbolic* — the cosmic ocean around Mount Meru, made real — plus practical urban water supply for drinking, bathing, and ritual, with little or no large-scale field irrigation. Their evidence: temples built on islands in the middle of the barays, reachable only by boat, like the East Mebon and the West Mebon (the latter once held a colossal reclining bronze Vishnu, fragments of which survive). You do not put a god on an island in the middle of an irrigation tank. The water clearly *meant* something.

The current position, built from decades of survey and now from the laser mapping of Chapter 10, is the unsatisfying-but-honest one: both, and bigger than either side guessed. The barays were genuinely sacred *and* part of a genuinely massive water-management system — not a tidy irrigation grid feeding triple rice crops, as the strongest Groslier version held, but an enormous, sprawling, sometimes ad hoc network for storing monsoon water, buffering flood and drought, and supplying a low-density city spread over hundreds of square miles. The reason this matters is that the same disagreement, scaled up, is the disagreement about why Angkor ended. Keep the baray debate in your pocket. We are going to need it.

# Chapter 4 — The Eleventh Century: the Khleang Kings, the Baphuon, and Suryavarman I

The eleventh century opens with a civil war, which by now should not surprise you. Around 1000–1010 the throne was fought over by a king called Jayaviravarman and a rival, **Suryavarman I** (soor-yah-VAR-man), who won after roughly eight years of fighting and went on to a long, consequential reign. Suryavarman I is the kind of king who matters more than he is remembered, because he did the unglamorous work: he expanded the state, and he tried to fix the thing that kept breaking it.

The expansion went west. Suryavarman I pushed Khmer power into the Mon kingdom of **Lavo** (LAH-voh) — modern Lopburi, in central Thailand — and across much of what is now Thailand's central plain. This is worth flagging now because of how the story ends: the western frontier Suryavarman I planted Khmer governors and temples across is the exact ground from which the Thai kingdoms will later rise and, in Chapter 9, destroy Angkor. Empires often plant the seed of their successor while expanding; the Khmer did it in the eleventh century and reaped it in the fifteenth.

The attempt to fix the broken thing is more interesting. The "broken thing" was the lack of a reliable bureaucracy: a king with no fixed succession and no standing professional administration ruled through personal loyalty, which evaporated the moment he died or weakened. Suryavarman I's response is one of the most remarkable documents in the whole Khmer record. He had an **oath of allegiance** sworn by some 4,000 officials and carved into the stone of the gateway of the royal palace at Angkor. The text has the officials swear, by elaborate and gruesome self-curse, absolute devotion to the king and his lineage. It is a king without a bureaucratic machine trying to *manufacture* one out of ritual, stone, and the fear of the gods — government by mass loyalty oath, literally set in the palace wall so no one could claim not to have sworn it. Compare it to the way the contemporary Song dynasty in China was running a vast professional civil service recruited by written examination (Tang & Song China Chapter 6): Angkor and the Northern Song were near-contemporaries, comparably sophisticated as builders and engineers, and ran on completely different operating systems — China on a meritocratic paper bureaucracy, Angkor on sacred personal oaths chiseled into a doorway. Neither was primitive. They were different solutions to the same problem, and the Khmer solution was structurally fragile in exactly the way the Chinese one was not.

---

The mid-eleventh century king **Udayadityavarman II** (oo-dye-AH-dit-yah-VAR-man) built the period's signature monuments and also got the period's signature trouble. His state temple, the **Baphuon** (bah-poo-AWN), is a huge sandstone temple-mountain that the Chinese envoy Zhou Daguan, visiting two and a half centuries later (Chapter 8), would call a "tower of bronze," a "truly astonishing spectacle." It was so structurally ambitious — built fast, on sand, too steep — that it has been collapsing and being rebuilt almost ever since; its twentieth-century restoration, interrupted by the Khmer Rouge era, became the largest archaeological jigsaw puzzle in the world (Chapter 10). Udayadityavarman II also built the **West Baray**, the largest reservoir at Angkor, with the West Mebon temple and its giant bronze Vishnu on an island in the middle of it.

And he faced repeated revolts. An inscription records in unusual detail the rebellion of a general named **Kamvau** and its suppression by a loyal officer, one of the few accounts we have of internal Khmer warfare from the inside. Stack the eleventh-century facts together and a pattern jumps out: grand temple, giant reservoir, expensive western wars, *and* armed rebellion at home. The Khmer state was a machine for converting an agricultural surplus into stone and conquest, and the more aggressively a king ran the machine, the more it shook. We will see the maximum version of this — maximum building, maximum strain — under Jayavarman VII in Chapter 7, and the argument about whether the machine finally shook itself apart is the subject of Chapter 10.

One more eleventh-century monument deserves its own line, because it is the most spectacularly sited building the Khmer ever made and because it is still, today, a place people are willing to shoot each other over. **Preah Vihear** (preeah vee-HEER) is a temple strung out along an 800-foot (240 m) cliff on the **Dangrek** escarpment, the natural mountain wall between the Cambodian plain and the Thai plateau. Built and extended across the eleventh and twelfth centuries, it is a sequence of sanctuaries climbing a processional stairway to a shrine at the cliff's edge with the whole plain falling away below. It is also, as Chapter 10 will return to, the subject of a modern armed border dispute between Cambodia and Thailand that the World Court has had to rule on twice, in 1962 and 2013. An eleventh-century Shiva temple is still live ammunition in twenty-first-century politics. That is the kind of afterlife Angkorian stone has.

# Chapter 5 — Suryavarman II and Angkor Wat

This is the chapter with the building on the flag. Everything so far — the water, the god-kings, the temple-mountain as a model of the cosmos, the formula of baray-ancestors-mountain getting bigger every century — has been building toward one monument, and in the early twelfth century the Khmer built it.

First the king, because the building cannot be separated from the man, and the man took the throne by murder. **Suryavarman II** came to power around 1113, and his own inscription describes how: as a young prince he attacked the reigning king — a great-uncle, Dharanindravarman I — and killed him in battle, leaping onto the enemy's war-elephant and ending him, in the inscription's image, "as Garuda kills a serpent." Garuda is the eagle-mount of Vishnu, the eternal enemy of the naga serpents. The line is royal propaganda, of course, but notice what propaganda chooses to brag about: not a peaceful inheritance, but a spectacular act of dynastic violence reframed as a god's eagle destroying a snake. Khmer kingship did not pretend succession was orderly. It made the seizure itself sacred.

Suryavarman II was an aggressive, ambitious, outward-facing king. He sent embassies to the **Song** court of China in 1116 and 1120, and Chinese records (which still call the kingdom Zhenla, their old name for Chenla from Chapter 1, even though the Khmer Empire had existed for three centuries by then) describe its wealth and its war-elephants. He launched repeated invasions of his neighbors: **Dai Viet** (the emerging Vietnamese state to the northeast, Dai Viet's own chapter is forthcoming in this chain) and **Champa**, the Hindu kingdom down the central Vietnamese coast, whose capital Vijaya his armies sacked in 1145. Hold the Champa wars in mind. Suryavarman II's aggression against Champa is the wind-up; the pitch comes back at Angkor's own throat in Chapter 6, and it nearly ends everything.

---

But the reign is remembered for one thing. South of the city, Suryavarman II built **Angkor Wat** — *vat* is the Khmer word for a temple, so the name just means "the City Temple," the building that eventually gave its informal name to the whole site.

Start with the records that get broken, because the cumulative-caveat rule applies and these "firsts" need their definitions. Angkor Wat is, by floor area and by total enclosed footprint, the **largest religious monument ever built**, and that claim survives scrutiny: its moated enclosure is about 1.5 by 1.3 km, roughly 200 hectares (about 500 acres), and there is no serious rival of comparable age and intactness. To anchor what that means, the great Gothic cathedrals of Europe were going up at almost the same moment — Chartres, Notre-Dame de Paris, begun in the 1160s — and any one of them would fit, with room to spare, inside Angkor Wat's outer enclosure several times over. The contemporary Song Chinese cities were larger as *cities*, and Constantinople's Hagia Sophia (Byzantine Empire Chapter 3) is a more daring single roofed space, but as a single integrated religious complex, moat and causeway and galleries and central towers together, Angkor Wat has no equal in the medieval world. It was built in roughly the working lifetime of one king, perhaps thirty-odd years, from sandstone quarried at Phnom Kulen 30 miles away and floated down purpose-cut canals — a logistical feat that the laser surveys of Chapter 10 are still uncovering the infrastructure for.

Two things about Angkor Wat are strange and both point the same way. First, almost every Khmer state temple before it was dedicated to **Shiva**; Angkor Wat is dedicated to **Vishnu**. Second, almost every Khmer temple faces east, toward the rising sun; Angkor Wat faces **west**, the direction of the setting sun and, in Hindu symbolism, of death. The most widely held explanation ties the two anomalies together: Angkor Wat was conceived as Suryavarman II's **funerary temple**, the place where, in death, he would merge with Vishnu — its westward orientation and its god both chosen for the king's passage out of the world rather than the sun's daily return. This is the standard reading and it is well argued, though, in keeping with this book's contested-claims rule, it is an interpretation, not a settled certainty; some scholars stress the temple's ordinary functions in the king's lifetime too. What is not in doubt is that the building is a deliberate, exact model of the Hindu cosmos at colossal scale. The five central towers are the five peaks of Mount Meru. The concentric galleries are the mountain ranges ringing the world. The vast moat is the cosmic ocean. The long western causeway is the bridge between the human world and the divine, and you cross it, deliberately, slowly, on foot. The whole structure is meant to be *walked through* as a passage from the everyday into the center of the universe.

And then there are the walls. Running around the temple's third level is roughly **half a mile of continuous bas-relief**, carved galleries depicting Hindu epic and Khmer history at a scale no other ancient monument attempts. One panel shows the **Churning of the Sea of Milk**: gods and demons, ranged in a cosmic tug-of-war, using the giant serpent Vasuki as a rope wrapped around Mount Mandara to churn the ocean of milk and extract the elixir of immortality, with Vishnu presiding at the center. Another shows the battle of Kurukshetra from the *Mahabharata*. And one gallery — this is the historian's treasure — shows **Suryavarman II himself**, seated in state, reviewing his army on the march, his name carved beside him. It is one of the only securely identified contemporary portraits of any Khmer king, a near-photograph from the twelfth century of the man who built the largest temple on Earth, looking out at his troops, on the wall of his own tomb. He died around 1150, probably on a failed campaign, and the throne fell into exactly the kind of contested, weak-handed succession this book has taught you to expect. This time the empire would pay for it almost everything.

# Chapter 6 — The Cham Catastrophe: the 1177 Sack and the Interregnum

For five chapters this book has shown you the Khmer building. This is the chapter where the Khmer almost stop existing.

After Suryavarman II died around 1150, the throne went through the usual instability — a weak king, a usurper named **Tribhuvanadityavarman** (tree-BHOO-vah-nah-DIT-yah-VAR-man) who overthrew and killed his predecessor around 1165 — and the kingdom that had been launching invasions a generation earlier was now divided, distracted, and badly led. That mattered, because the neighbor Suryavarman II had spent his reign attacking had not forgotten.

That neighbor was **Champa**. The reader needs to be told clearly who the Cham were, because the language-family rule applies here with full force and the difference is the whole point of this chapter. Champa was a string of Hindu (and Buddhist) kingdoms along the central coast of modern Vietnam. The Cham people spoke — and speak — **Cham**, a language of the **Austronesian** family. That is the seafaring island family from the Srivijaya story (Srivijaya Chapter 1): the family of Malay, Javanese, Polynesian, the language stock that spread by boat from Madagascar to Easter Island. The Khmer, recall from Chapter 1, spoke **Austroasiatic** Khmer, a completely separate linguistic root. These two peoples were neighbors and rivals for centuries, they shared Indianized Hindu-Buddhist culture, their kings used similar Sanskrit titles and built similar brick temples — and they were, in language and origin, as far apart as an Englishman and a Malay sailor. The Cham were a *maritime* people on the coast; the Khmer were a *riverine-agrarian* people of the lake and the plain. That maritime character is the hinge of the catastrophe, because it meant the Cham could do something no land enemy could.

---

In 1177, a Cham fleet under their king **Jaya Indravarman IV** did the unthinkable. Instead of marching overland, they came by water: up the Mekong, into the Tonlé Sap river, across the great lake — guided, the chronicles say, by a defector — and fell on Yasodharapura from the lake side, by surprise. They took the city. They killed the Khmer king, Tribhuvanadityavarman. They looted and burned the capital of the largest empire in Southeast Asia. And then they did not leave. For roughly four years, from 1177 to about 1181, Champa effectively occupied the heartland of Angkor.

Sit with that, because it is the single most important "almost" in this book. The civilization that built the largest religious monument on Earth was, for four years, a defeated and occupied territory of a maritime rival it had spent decades bullying. There is no Angkor Wat on a national flag, no Bayon, no hospitals, no Zhou Daguan travelogue, in the timeline where 1177 is permanent. Everything in the next two chapters — the most famous king, the most enigmatic temple, the empire's absolute peak — happens *only because* the occupation was reversed. This is the hinge.

It was reversed by a man who had been, as far as the record shows, nowhere. A royal prince, already in his late fifties or older, who had spent years out of power — possibly in Champa itself, possibly in internal exile — emerged during the occupation to organize Khmer resistance. Over several years of fighting he won a decisive battle, and it was a *naval* battle, fought on the water of the Tonlé Sap itself: to beat a sea power that had come by river, the Khmer had to win on the river. He drove the Cham out, and around 1181 he was crowned king. His name was Jayavarman VII, and he is the subject of the next chapter and arguably the most important individual in this entire history. We even have what is close to a documentary record of the war that made him, because — in one of the great strokes of luck in the whole Angkor story — he later had it carved, in vivid, brutal, ground-level detail, onto the outer walls of the strangest temple he built. Capsizing war-canoes. Hand-to-hand fighting on the decks. Crocodiles in the lake eating the men who fell in. The Bayon's reliefs are, among other things, the war diary of the catastrophe this chapter just described, told by the man who reversed it. We turn to him now.

---

# Chapter 7 — Jayavarman VII: the Bodhisattva King and the Bayon

If this book has a protagonist, it is the old man crowned around 1181 over a burned and recently occupied capital. **Jayavarman VII** was already roughly sixty years old when he became king. In something like the next forty years he built more of Angkor than every king before him combined, reorganized the empire's official religion, projected power further than any Khmer ruler ever had, and left behind the most haunting and most argued-over monument in Southeast Asia. He is also the king the modern Cambodian state has chosen, above all others, as its symbol of resilience — the ruler who rebuilt the nation after it was broken. You can see why the choice was attractive after the twentieth century Cambodia had (Chapter 10).

The deepest thing he changed was the idea of the king. For nearly four centuries Khmer monarchs had been Hindu **devaraja** god-kings, fused with Shiva or Vishnu (Chapter 2). Jayavarman VII was a devout **Mahayana Buddhist**, and he recast kingship in its terms. Buddhism, founded in India around the fifth century BCE, teaches that ordinary life is bound to suffering by craving, and that the goal is release (an escape from the endless cycle of death and rebirth) reached by extinguishing that craving. Mahayana ("the Great Vehicle") Buddhism centers on the **bodhisattva**: a being who has earned release from the cycle of rebirth but *postpones* it, voluntarily, to stay in the suffering world and help everyone else get free. (This is the major split in Buddhism; the other great branch, **Theravada**, "the Teaching of the Elders," is the more austere, monastic, original-scripture tradition — remember the name, because Theravada quietly ends this whole civilization in Chapters 8 and 9.) Jayavarman VII presented himself as a bodhisattva-king: a ruler whose sacred role was not to *be* a god but to *relieve the suffering* of his people. It is a profound reframing, and — crucially — it is not just theology. It is a building permit. A god-king builds a temple to house his divine essence. A bodhisattva-king builds *hospitals, roads, rest-houses, reservoirs, and a city for his people*, and calls all of it an act of compassion. The single most ambitious public-works program in Khmer history flows directly out of this one religious idea.

---

The works themselves are almost a list of superlatives, so here is the list, because the scale is the point.

He built **Angkor Thom** — "the Great City" — a new walled royal capital about 2.2 miles (3.5 km) on each side, surrounded by a wall and a moat, entered through five monumental gates each topped with a tower of huge serene faces and approached by a causeway lined with stone gods and demons hauling on a giant naga, the Churning of the Sea of Milk rendered as a bridge. Inside it, at the dead center, he built the **Bayon** (bye-OHN).

The Bayon is the strangest major temple in Asia and the honest thing to say about it is that we do not fully understand it. It is a mountain of stone faces: somewhere around 200 huge, calm, faintly smiling faces, four to a tower, on roughly fifty towers, gazing out in every direction so that from nowhere in the temple are you unobserved. Who are they? Here the contested-claims rule is not a hedge, it is the actual state of knowledge. The leading interpretation is that they represent **Avalokiteshvara**, the bodhisattva of compassion — the same compassion-figure Jayavarman VII modeled his kingship on, his gaze falling protectively in all directions over the realm. A second, very old interpretation is that the faces are the **king himself**, his serene features multiplied across the empire as an inescapable divine-royal presence. A third, increasingly favored reading is that the ambiguity is *deliberate* — that the faces are meant to be the bodhisattva and the king at once, the whole point of a bodhisattva-king being that you cannot separate the two. Scholars genuinely disagree. The smile you are looking at is, on purpose or by accident, an unsolved problem, and the people who study it for a living will tell you so.

He built temple-monasteries for his family on a scale that beggars belief, and we know the scale because the Khmer, gloriously, wrote it on the wall. **Ta Prohm** (tah PROHM), dedicated in 1186 to the king's mother, has a foundation inscription that functions as a census: it records something on the order of 12,640 people resident at the temple and tens of thousands more in attendant villages supplying it — a single temple as a town. (Ta Prohm is also, today, the one strangled by enormous fig and silk-cotton tree roots, the most photographed ruin on Earth and a deliberate conservation choice we will come back to in Chapter 10.) **Preah Khan** (preeah KAHN), for his father, was a Buddhist temple-university whose inscription records nearly a thousand teachers. He dug a new northern reservoir, the **Jayatataka**, and put in the middle of it **Neak Pean** (nee-ak PEE-an), a small, perfect island shrine conceived as Anavatapta, the mythical Himalayan lake whose four waters cured all disease — a sculpture, in water and stone, of his entire healing ideology.

Because the healing was literal, too. Identical stone **hospital edicts** have been found across the empire, from Angkor into modern Thailand and Laos, announcing royally endowed *arogyasala*, "houses for the sick," each staffed and stocked with rice and medicine at the king's expense. The inscriptions are the documentation behind the famous claim that Jayavarman VII founded **102 hospitals**. And he laid out a network of stone-paved **royal roads** radiating from Angkor toward the provinces and Champa, lined with **121 rest-houses** — the inscriptions call them "houses with fire" — spaced roughly a day's travel apart, an empire-scale system of state infrastructure for the movement of people, troops, and goods. He even, finally, finished the old business: between about 1203 and 1220 Khmer armies under his command conquered and outright annexed **Champa** — the very kingdom that had sacked Angkor in 1177. The hinge swung all the way back.

---

Now the question that has shadowed this chapter, and the whole book, since the eleventh-century pattern in Chapter 4. Was it too much?

The thesis — associated with Groslier and others, and influential for decades — is that Jayavarman VII's building program was so colossal that it broke the empire. The argument: converting nearly the entire agricultural surplus, and an enormous corvée labor force (unpaid work the population owed the state by obligation, the way it owed taxes), into stone for forty straight years drained the treasury, exhausted the population, and starved the productive base (the canals, the rice land, the maintenance) that everything depended on. In this reading the empire's greatest king is also, unintentionally, its undertaker — the peak and the beginning of the end are the same reign.

It is a powerful story and you should be suspicious of how powerful it is, because vividness, this book keeps warning, is often the tell of a contested claim dressed as a fact. The exhaustion thesis is a *hypothesis*, and a debated one. Critics point out that the empire visibly persisted for two centuries after Jayavarman VII, that the most dramatic evidence for decline (the climate data of Chapter 10) postdates him by generations, and that "they built too much and collapsed" is a suspiciously moralized shape for a story to have. What is solidly established is narrower and still significant: that after Jayavarman VII died, around 1218, no Khmer king ever again built on anything like that scale, and the empire's outer territories began to contract almost immediately. Whether that is because he spent the future, or merely because he was the last king with the religious mandate and the centralized control to do such things before the ground shifted under all of it, is exactly the argument of Chapter 10. He died as the most accomplished builder in the history of mainland Southeast Asia, and the very next thing the empire did was start arguing with itself about his religion.

# Chapter 8 — The Empire at Its Height: Society, Trade, and Zhou Daguan

Here is a useful corrective before this chapter does anything else: the thirteenth century, *after* the supposedly ruinous Jayavarman VII, is the period from which we have the single best description of Angkor as a living, functioning, prosperous city. The empire did not fall over when its greatest king died. It kept going, populous and rich, for a long time. This chapter is about that city at its zenith, and about the slow, invisible change that was already dissolving it from inside.

First, a religious whiplash. Jayavarman VII had made Mahayana Buddhism the royal religion. Within a couple of generations a king named **Jayavarman VIII** (reigning roughly 1243–1295) presided over a furious **Hindu reaction**. This was not a quiet doctrinal shift; it was iconoclasm. Across Angkor, including all over the Bayon and the great Buddhist temples, thousands of carved Buddha images were systematically smashed, pried out, or recut into Shiva lingas. You can still see it: the empty niches, the chiseled-out Buddhas, the violence of it, on the walls today. It is one of the clearest pieces of evidence that the fusion of religion and Khmer kingship cut both ways — when the god at the top of the model of the universe changed, the universe got re-carved with hammers. Jayavarman VIII's reign also brought Angkor its one brush with the Mongols: around 1283 the armies of Kublai Khan's Yuan, having already hammered Champa and Dai Viet, demanded Khmer submission, and Angkor avoided invasion by sending tribute and acknowledging Mongol overlordship — a quieter outcome than its neighbors got, and a sign Angkor still mattered enough on the Asian stage to be worth a threat rather than a sack.

And then, around 1295, a king associated with the name **Srindravarman** took the throne, and the religion changed one last time, and this time it stuck, and this time it was fatal to the whole system. The court turned to **Theravada Buddhism**. Hold that thought through the description of the glittering city that follows, because it is the worm in the apple, and we will pull it out at the end of the chapter.

---

The reason we can describe thirteenth-century Angkor *as a place*, with markets and gossip and law and food, rather than only as a set of inscribed temple-dedications, comes down to one document by one foreign tourist, and it is one of the great pieces of luck in world history.

In 1296 a Chinese envoy named **Zhou Daguan** (joh dah-GWAN) arrived at Angkor as part of a diplomatic mission from the **Yuan** — the Mongol dynasty then ruling China under Kublai Khan's heirs (Yuan Dynasty Chapter 4; this is the same Mongol world, and almost the same decade, that produced Marco Polo's account of China). Zhou Daguan stayed about eleven months, paid attention, and after returning home wrote a short book, *The Customs of Cambodia*. It is the **only** detailed eyewitness account of daily life inside the Khmer Empire that exists. Not the only good one — the only one, period. Everything textured we can say about how Angkorians lived, dressed, ate, married, traded, judged crime, and treated each other comes substantially from this one observant Chinese diplomat, the way almost everything textured about the medieval Mongol world comes from Marco Polo and Ibn Battuta (Yuan Dynasty Chapter 6). When a civilization leaves almost nothing perishable behind, one good travelogue is worth a library.

What did he see? A wealthy, hierarchical, surprisingly woman-run commercial city. He describes the king's processions in gold and on elephants; the multiple tiers of society from princes down through officials and Brahmins and monks to a large population of bonded laborers and slaves drawn largely from upland peoples; a marketplace economy in which, he notes with some surprise, the traders were overwhelmingly **women**. He describes houses (the king's tiled, commoners' thatched, sumptuary law deciding who could roof with what), the trade goods the Chinese wanted (kingfisher feathers, elephant ivory, aromatic woods, cardamom) and the Chinese goods the Khmer wanted (silk, porcelain, ironware, paper). He describes a brutal justice system, including trial by ordeal. He describes the festivals, the heat, the fish, and the rice. And he confirms the great engine of it all from the ground: the **Tonlé Sap** fishery and the rice land, the reversing lake of Chapter 1 still doing in 1296 exactly what it had done for the Funanese — feeding, almost effortlessly, one of the largest concentrations of people in the pre-industrial world.

Two hard debates run through any honest reading of Zhou Daguan. One is *how many* people: estimates for greater Angkor at its height have ranged from a few hundred thousand to, in the boldest Groslier-style versions, nearly a million, and the number depends entirely on the rice-cropping question of Chapter 3 — how many harvests a year that landscape really produced. The high numbers have been cut by later soil and survey work; the laser-mapping of Chapter 10 has reopened the whole question by showing the city was far more *extensive*, if lower-density, than anyone thought. The other debate is how much to trust one foreigner who did not speak Khmer, working through interpreters, for eleven months. The answer scholars have reached is: more than you would expect, because where he can be checked against inscriptions and archaeology he is strikingly often right — but with the permanent caveat that the entire daily texture of a six-century civilization is reconstructed from a single outsider's notebook.

---

Now pull the worm out. While the city Zhou Daguan describes still glittered, the religious ground had shifted under it, and the shift was quiet, popular, and total. **Theravada Buddhism** — spreading from Sri Lanka through the Mon and the rising Tai world, in the Pali language, carried by ordinary monks rather than royal decree — was becoming the religion of the Khmer people. And Theravada has no use for a god-king. It needs no devaraja, no Mount Meru in sandstone, no colossal state temple binding a divine ruler to the cosmos, no army of corvée laborers hauling stone to earn a king his merger with Vishnu. It needs monks, the scriptures, and a modest wooden monastery in every village. The last great royal **Sanskrit** inscription dates to 1327; after it, the brahmin-Sanskrit court culture that had run the empire for five centuries simply goes quiet. Nothing was conquered. No temple was stormed. The entire religious justification for the Angkorian way of being a state — the reason to mobilize a whole society's surplus into divine architecture — just evaporated from below, one village monastery at a time. That, more than any army, is what ended Angkor. Chapter 9 is what the ending looked like from the outside, and Chapter 10 is the long, unfinished argument about what really caused it.

# Chapter 9 — The Long Retreat and the 1431 Abandonment

The most important sentence in this chapter is a sentence about something that *stopped*. After Jayavarman VII, no Khmer king ever built another great stone temple. Not a smaller one, not a cheaper one — the tradition that had defined the civilization for five centuries, the baray-ancestors-mountain formula from Chapter 2 that every chapter since has been a variation on, simply ends. When you visit Angkor you are visiting a place where monumental construction stopped around 1220 and never restarted, and the silence in the stone after that date is the single clearest evidence that the civilization had changed into something else. Chapter 8 explained *why* it stopped: Theravada Buddhism removed the religious reason to build. This chapter is about everything else that went wrong on top of that, over the two slow centuries it took for "the capital is here" to become "the capital is somewhere else."

The external threat had a name, and the Khmer had planted it themselves. Recall from Chapter 4 that Suryavarman I pushed Khmer power deep into what is now Thailand. Over the following centuries, **Tai**-speaking peoples (the ancestors of the modern Thai and Lao, speaking yet another distinct language family, unrelated to Khmer or Cham or Vietnamese) became dominant across that western frontier and founded their own kingdoms on top of the Khmer template they had absorbed. First **Sukhothai**, around 1238. Then, far more dangerously, **Ayutthaya** (ah-YOO-tah-yah), founded in 1351 on the lower Chao Phraya river. Ayutthaya is the crucial one. It took Khmer statecraft, Khmer court ritual, Khmer art, even much Khmer vocabulary — Indianized kingship learned, in large part, *from* Angkor — and then turned the whole apparatus against its teacher. The western frontier Suryavarman I had conquered in the eleventh century produced, in the fourteenth, the power that would finish Angkor as a capital. Empires sometimes raise their own undertakers; the Khmer raised this one over four hundred years.

---

Ayutthayan and Cambodian chronicles record repeated Siamese campaigns against Angkor across the late fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries — sieges, captures, the carrying-off of people and treasure and sacred images, in 1352, 1369, 1389, and finally the big one. In **1431** an Ayutthayan army took Angkor, and this time the Khmer court did not move back. The capital relocated south, to the area of **Chaktomuk** and then **Phnom Penh**, near the confluence of the great rivers, and later to Lovek and Oudong. 1431 is the date in every textbook for "the fall of Angkor," and it is a real and important date, but the textbook framing is wrong in two ways that this book has been preparing you to see.

First, 1431 was not a bolt from the blue; it was the loudest event in a two-century retreat already underway for reasons (Theravada, the end of the temple economy, the decay of the waterworks, the pull of maritime trade toward the coast) that had nothing to do with Ayutthaya. The Siamese did not topple a thriving Angkor. They knocked over a capital that had already, in every way that made it *Angkor*, been winding down for two hundred years, and whose center of gravity was already sliding toward the river and the sea and the new world of maritime commerce that a southern capital was better placed to exploit. The move south was a strategic relocation as much as a flight.

Second — and this is the correction the whole next chapter is built on — **Angkor was never abandoned**. The *capital* moved. The city's population thinned. But Angkor Wat itself, the great temple, was never lost, never swallowed, never forgotten. It was converted into a **Theravada Buddhist** monastery and pilgrimage site and continuously maintained by monks. Cambodian kings still came. Pilgrims still came; there is sixteenth- and seventeenth-century graffiti on its walls from Japanese, and later Khmer royal inscriptions added by King Ang Chan in the 1500s. The "lost city reclaimed by the jungle, unknown until a European stumbled on it" — the single most famous thing most people think they know about Angkor — is a myth, and a revealing one. We turn now to where that myth came from, and to the much harder, still-unsettled question the myth was always covering up: not *when* Angkor fell, but *whether "fell" was ever the right word at all*.

# Chapter 10 — The Collapse Debate and Afterlife: the Jungle, Mouhot, LiDAR, and Angkor as Symbol

This final chapter has two jobs. The first is to walk through the argument — still live, still unsettled — about what actually happened to Angkor, because Angkor is the textbook case of "civilizational collapse" and the textbook has been substantially rewritten in the last fifteen years. The second is to follow the temples themselves from 1431 to now: the European visitors, the famous "rediscovery" that wasn't one, the French archaeologists, the looters, the Khmer Rouge, the lasers, and the flag. Angkor's afterlife is, in some ways, the most eventful part of its history.

---

Start with the debate, and meet the defendants. For most of the twentieth century the dominant explanation was **Bernard-Philippe Groslier's** "hydraulic city" thesis, laid out in 1979 (you met it in Chapter 3). Angkor, in this reading, was a single vast machine for managing water and growing rice; its enormous, aging waterworks silted up, broke down, and could no longer feed the population, and the civilization collapsed under the failure of its own infrastructure. It is a clean, total, almost tragic explanation, and it dominated for a generation.

It has not survived intact, and the things that broke it are worth knowing because they are good history. The first is **climate data**. In 2010 a team led by the dendrochronologist Brendan Buckley published tree-ring evidence — from rare long-lived cypress in the region — showing that fourteenth- and fifteenth-century mainland Southeast Asia was hit by extreme climate swings: prolonged, severe **multi-decade droughts** punctuated by years of unusually violent monsoon. For a civilization whose entire existence was a bet on managing water, this is close to a worst case: not too little water or too much, but wild, unpredictable alternation between both, exactly the regime a centuries-old reservoir-and-canal network is least able to absorb. The climate evidence did not replace Groslier so much as supply the trigger his mechanism was missing.

The second challenger is the **trade and religion** argument, which says the "collapse" framing was wrong from the start. The court did not die; it *moved*, deliberately, toward the southern rivers and the booming maritime trade of the era (the same trade world that powered Srivijaya and the port-empires; Srivijaya Chapter 7). And the engine of the old order, as Chapter 8 argued, had already been switched off from below by the spread of **Theravada Buddhism**, which made the entire god-king temple economy religiously pointless. In this reading there was no collapse to explain — there was a relocation and a religious transformation, and "the fall of Angkor" is mostly a story Europeans found romantic.

The current scholarly consensus is multi-causal and deliberately un-dramatic, and it is the position this book endorses: there was no single "fall." There was a convergence — climate shocks straining a decaying water system, the religious shift dissolving the temple-state's reason to exist, the productive pull of maritime trade toward the coast, and Ayutthayan war finishing the job on a capital already hollowed of its old purpose. The reason this matters beyond Cambodia is that Angkor, alongside the Classic Maya (Maya Civilization Chapter 5), is one of the two great test cases in the global debate about why complex societies unwind — and in both cases the lesson of the last few decades has been the same: "collapse" is almost always too simple a word, the dramatic monocausal story is almost always the contested one, and the honest answer is a slow convergence of several pressures, no one of which was destiny.

---

Now the temples. After 1431 Angkor Wat went on as a working Buddhist shrine (Chapter 9), but the wider city thinned, and the forest did move into the abandoned districts — not the total burial of legend, but enough that the place acquired an air of overgrown grandeur. European visitors trickled through and were stunned. A Portuguese friar, **António da Madalena**, described Angkor Wat around 1586; the chronicler Diogo do Couto preserved his account of a temple "so extraordinary that it is not possible to describe it with a pen." But the visit that made Angkor a global icon came in 1860, when the French naturalist **Henri Mouhot** spent time at the ruins and wrote about them in a journal published, after his death from fever in 1861, to a sensational European reception. Mouhot is universally credited with the "discovery" of Angkor, and this book has spent a chapter and a half preparing you to reject that word. He discovered nothing. Angkor Wat was a functioning, monk-tended pilgrimage temple that the Khmer had never lost; local people guided him to it. What Mouhot did was *publicize* it — turn a place Cambodians knew perfectly well into a Western sensation and a colonial-era cause. The "rediscovery" myth says more about who gets to count as having "known" a thing than it does about Angkor.

What followed Mouhot was a century of French archaeology, and it cut both ways. From 1907 the **École française d'Extrême-Orient** (the EFEO, the French research institute for the Far East) ran the long campaigns of clearing, study, and reconstruction that made the park visitable. Their signature technique, **anastylosis**, means reassembling a collapsed building from its own original fallen stones, adding as little new material as possible — the method that put the Baphuon's million-piece puzzle (Chapter 4) and much of Angkor back upright. In the same era, a 1907 treaty returned the Angkor region from Siamese to Cambodian (then French-protectorate) control, putting the monuments back inside the country that built them. But the colonial period also industrialized the **looting**. The young André Malraux's 1923 attempt to cart off Banteay Srei's carvings (Chapter 3) was only the famous case; the trafficking of Khmer sculpture became, and remained, an enormous and destructive trade.

Then the twentieth century did to Cambodia what no medieval enemy had. From the 1970s into the 1990s Angkor sat inside a war zone: civil war, the **Khmer Rouge** regime and its genocide, Vietnamese occupation, Khmer Rouge insurgency in the forests around the temples, landmines in the fields, and a wave of statue looting and trafficking that, in two decades, did more damage to Angkor's sculpture than the previous five centuries of jungle had. It is against that background — a nation that had just been through the killing fields — that the choice of national symbol becomes legible. **Angkor Wat has appeared on every Cambodian flag since independence in 1953**, through monarchy, republic, the Khmer Rouge's own banner, and the present state. It is the only national flag on Earth that depicts a building. For a country that the twentieth century tried very hard to erase, a thousand-year-old temple that proves the Khmer once built the greatest monument in the world is not decoration. It is the spine of national identity, which is also why an eleventh-century cliff temple, **Preah Vihear** (Chapter 4), could trigger real, fatal Cambodia–Thailand border combat in 2008–2011, with the World Court reaffirming Cambodia's title in 2013. Angkorian stone is not inert. People still die over it.

---

The last act is the one that rewrote everything, and fittingly it is about being able to see. Angkor became a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** in 1992 and was placed immediately on the danger list, triggering a large international rescue effort coordinated through Cambodia's **APSARA Authority** and bringing, eventually, the mass tourism — millions of visitors a year — that is now both the funding and the central management problem of the site.

But the genuine revolution was technological. Beginning around 2007 and most dramatically in 2012 and 2015, the **Greater Angkor Project** flew **LiDAR** over the region — airborne lasers that pulse the ground millions of times and, by timing the returns that slip between the leaves, reconstruct the bare earth *under the forest canopy*, digitally stripping away the trees that had hidden the city for centuries. What it revealed reframed the entire civilization. Angkor was not a cluster of grand temples in the jungle with villages somewhere nearby. It was a single, continuous, **low-density urban sprawl** — temples, palaces, occupation mounds, ponds, roads, rice fields, and a water network of staggering extent — covering on the order of **1,000 square kilometers**, roughly the footprint of a modern metropolitan area, making it one of the **largest pre-industrial settlement complexes ever identified anywhere on Earth**. Set that against the cities this book has measured it by along the way: Tang Chang'an with its million people behind walls (Tang & Song China Chapter 3), Abbasid Baghdad, imperial Rome — Angkor was not in a different league as a *building*; it was, by sheer inhabited extent, plausibly the most spread-out great city of the pre-modern world, and almost all of it was invisible until the lasers looked.

That is the right note to end a Khmer history on, because it is the same note it began on. We started this book by admitting how little survives in tropical soil and how much of Angkor is contested, argued, unsettled — the 802 ceremony, the meaning of the devaraja, the purpose of the barays, the faces on the Bayon, whether the greatest king broke the empire, why it really ended. The lasers did not end those arguments. They made several of them bigger. Angkor is not a solved ancient mystery with a moral; it is a city the forest kept, that a defeated people rebuilt after occupation, that ran for six centuries on water and a religious idea, that did not so much fall as transform, and that we are — genuinely, right now, with new tools — still learning to see. The temple on the flag is not a monument to a dead civilization. It is an unfinished argument that the people who live there have decided to put at the center of who they are.

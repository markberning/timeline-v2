# Asuka & Nara Japan — The Borrowed Civilization

**Goal:** Narrative for the second TL in the Japanese Civilization chain, covering 538–794 CE — the 256 years between the prehistoric/protohistoric Yamato of pottery and tomb-mounds (Prehistoric Japan) and the Heian court of Murasaki Shikibu and Lady Sei (Heian Japan). In these two and a half centuries, Japan was remade. A golden Buddha statue arrived from Korea in 538 and fractured the Yamato court into a generation-long religious civil war. A teenage prince named Shotoku Taishi served as regent under Japan's first reigning empress and tried to make Japan into a small-scale Tang China. A 645 palace coup destroyed the dominant Soga clan and triggered the Taika "great change" Reform — a Tang-modeled program of land redistribution, centralized taxation, and bureaucracy. A catastrophic naval defeat at Baekgang in 663 ended Japanese ambitions on the Korean continent for nearly a thousand years. Two new permanent capitals were built — Fujiwara-kyo, then the spectacular Heijo-kyo (modern Nara) — both copying the grid plan of Tang Chang'an. The Daibutsu (Great Buddha) at Todai-ji was cast in 752 from copper, tin, mercury, and gold drawn from across the country, in the largest wooden building in the world. A monk named Dokyo nearly seized the throne through his influence over a reigning empress in 769. And in 794, Emperor Kammu finally moved the capital again, away from the suffocating political power of Nara's Buddhist temples, to a city he named Heian-kyo — modern Kyoto — where it would stay for the next 1,074 years. Everything later about Japan — the samurai, the shogunate, the imperial line, the Buddhist establishment, the deep Tang-Korean cultural roots — is laid down in these two and a half centuries.

A note on names and pronunciation: Japanese personal names use modern Hepburn romanization, with phonetic pronunciation guides on first use. Long vowels (the "ou" in Shotoku, the "oo" in Tenmu) are pronounced as straight long vowels in English, not as diphthongs. Where a name appears in two common forms in the literature (Shotoku Taishi vs. Prince Shotoku, Heijo-kyo vs. Nara), both forms appear here. Dates are CE unless noted otherwise.

A note on "first" claims: the Asuka and Nara periods are studded with them — first reigning empress, first written constitution, first permanent capital, first national history, first official coinage, first surviving Japanese poetry collection. Each is qualified in context. As with every TL in this project, "first" means "first surviving evidence of," and what survives depends on archaeological and documentary luck. Japan's pre-eighth-century record is unusually thin because writing only just arrived in this period, and almost nothing was written down before then.

# Chapter 1 — The Buddhist Crisis

In the year 538 CE — or possibly 552, the early Japanese chronicles disagree and the discrepancy itself is a clue to how confused the surviving record is — King Seong of **Baekje** (BEHK-jeh, the southwestern of the three Korean kingdoms then dividing the peninsula) sent a diplomatic gift to the **Yamato** (YAH-mah-toh) court in central Japan. The gift was a small gilded bronze statue of the Buddha, a set of Buddhist sutras in Chinese, and a polite letter recommending the new religion to the Japanese court. The court was at the time housed in a series of impermanent palaces in the **Asuka** (AHS-koo-kah) basin, a small valley in the hills south of modern Nara that gives this entire era its name.

What followed inside the Yamato court was one of the most consequential factional wars in Japanese history. It lasted roughly fifty years, killed several court nobles, destroyed one of the great founding clans of the Japanese aristocracy, and decided whether Japan would become a Buddhist civilization. Japan did. But it was close.

The fight is worth dwelling on, because everything that happened afterward — the Tang-modeled bureaucracy, the great Nara temples, the Buddhist grip on imperial politics that would eventually drive the capital to Kyoto — flows directly from the Soga clan's victory at the **Battle of Shigisen** (shih-gee-SEN) in 587. If the Mononobe had won that battle, the Yamato state would still have absorbed continental civilization (the pressure was enormous, the Korean immigrants were already arriving in waves, the writing system was unstoppable), but it would have done so on a different theological basis, with a different priesthood at the center, and very probably without the great state-Buddhist building program of the next two centuries. This chapter is the story of how Japan became Buddhist.

---

To understand the dispute, you have to understand the state of things at the Yamato court in the early sixth century.

Japan in 538 was still a network of clan-based aristocratic households, knit loosely together by their common acknowledgment of the **tenno** (TEN-noh — the Yamato sovereign, "heavenly sovereign," though the title itself was almost certainly not yet in use and was applied retroactively by later chroniclers) and his ritual authority. The previous era is called the **Kofun period** (KOH-fun, "old mounds") after the keyhole-shaped burial mounds the great clans built for their dead, some larger by surface area than the Great Pyramid at Giza (Prehistoric Japan Ch 3). Power inside the Yamato federation was divided among a handful of **uji** (OO-jee) — extended aristocratic clans, each with its own hereditary specialization, its own protected lands, and its own claim on a slice of court ritual.

Three uji mattered most for the religious crisis:

The **Soga** (SOH-gah) were administrators and finance specialists. Their territory lay in the Asuka basin near the seat of the court itself. Crucially, the Soga had married into and absorbed several immigrant lineages of **toraijin** (toh-RYE-jin, "people who came across") — Korean and Chinese specialists who had migrated into Japan over the previous centuries with skills the native Japanese wanted: writing, accountancy, metalwork, sericulture, and increasingly, Buddhism. The Soga's connections to the continental immigrant communities meant they were the one great clan with a working knowledge of Buddhist practice before it arrived as a state question.

The **Mononobe** (mon-oh-NOH-beh) were the warrior clan. Their hereditary specialization was military command and weapons production. They were the closest thing the Yamato court had to a national army. They were also, by tradition, deeply tied to the cult of certain Yamato war-gods whose shrines and rites they administered.

The **Nakatomi** (nah-kah-TOH-mee) were the priest clan. Their hereditary specialization was the ritual service of the **kami** (KAH-mee — the Japanese word for the local spirit-deities of mountains, rivers, forests, ancestors, and natural forces, in what would later be systematized as Shinto, "the way of the gods"). The Nakatomi conducted the seasonal court rites, divined imperial questions, and performed the purification ceremonies that kept the divine balance between court and gods stable. If you accepted Yamato religious authority, you implicitly accepted Nakatomi ritual expertise.

The Mononobe and the Nakatomi together represented the indigenous religious establishment. The Soga represented the immigrant-influenced reform faction. The Buddhist gift from Baekje became the issue around which the long-running tension among these three clans crystallized into open war.

---

The first Soga leader to take Buddhism seriously was **Soga no Iname** (SOH-gah noh ee-NAH-meh, lived c. 506–570).

When the Buddha image arrived from Baekje, the Yamato sovereign — Emperor Kinmei, by the traditional reckoning — convened his court to ask whether to accept the foreign deity. Iname was enthusiastic. He pointed out that Buddhism was the religion of the great civilized states (Baekje, the Korean kingdoms, the Chinese dynasties to the west) and that adopting it would put Japan on the same cultural footing. The Mononobe leader and the Nakatomi leader both opposed accepting it. Their argument was simple: the kami of Japan were the gods of this land and had always given victory to the Yamato court. To worship a foreign god would be to insult the kami of Japan, who would punish such betrayal with disasters.

The compromise the court reached was characteristic and revealing. Iname was permitted to enshrine the Buddha image as his own private devotion, in his own household, on his own land. The court itself would not adopt the new religion. The other clans would not be required to acknowledge it. If something terrible happened, it would be Iname's fault and Iname's problem.

A plague broke out shortly afterward. The Mononobe and Nakatomi went straight to the sovereign and announced that this was exactly what they had warned about — the kami were taking revenge for the foreign image. With the court's grudging permission, they raided Iname's compound, threw the Buddha statue into the **Naniwa** (nah-NEE-wah — modern Osaka) canal, and burned down the small temple that Iname had built around it. The plague did not stop. The argument did not stop either.

The Buddha-into-the-canal episode happened more than once over the following decades. Each time a plague or a famine or a bad harvest struck, the Mononobe-Nakatomi faction blamed the Buddhist deviation, raided the latest Soga shrine, and disposed of the latest Buddha image. Each time the disasters continued anyway, the Soga retrieved a fresh image from Korea and started over. By the time Iname died in 570, the Soga had built up a small Buddhist establishment in the Asuka basin (involving Korean monks, Korean-style architecture, and steadily growing influence) that they were prepared to defend by force.

---

Iname's son **Soga no Umako** (SOH-gah noh OO-mah-koh, c. 551–626) took up the religious cause and turned it into a military project.

Umako was, by the surviving evidence, one of the most politically capable figures in early Japanese history. He spent his career consolidating Soga influence at court — installing Soga-friendly princes as crown prince, marrying Soga women into the imperial line, and building the institutional base that would eventually make him the de facto ruler of Japan for the four decades from 587 to his death in 626. The Buddhist question and the political question were the same question. Whoever won the religious war would dominate the court. Umako intended to be that person.

In 585, another plague broke out. The Mononobe leader **Mononobe no Moriya** (mon-oh-NOH-beh noh MOH-ree-yah) and the Nakatomi leader Nakatomi no Katsumi went to the sovereign yet again with the same complaint. The sovereign yet again gave permission for an anti-Buddhist raid. The Buddha images were destroyed, the temples burned, the nuns Umako had ordained as Japan's first Buddhist clergy were stripped naked and publicly flogged. The plague did not stop.

This was the breaking point. When the sovereign died in 587 (suspiciously soon, some chroniclers thought, after the latest cycle of religious violence), the question of who would succeed him became inseparable from the question of which clan would dominate the court. Umako backed one prince. Moriya backed another. The two factions raised armies. In the autumn of 587, Soga and Mononobe forces met at **Shigisen** in modern Nara Prefecture, near the Mononobe's home estates.

The battle was a rout. Mononobe no Moriya was killed in the fighting, reportedly shot out of a tree where he had taken refuge as a vantage point. The entire Mononobe leadership was destroyed. The clan was broken up, its lands distributed among the victors, and its hereditary military role transferred to Soga clients. The Nakatomi, who had been the Mononobe's allies in the religious war but had not committed military forces to the actual battle, survived as a clan but lost their political influence at court for the next sixty years. Buddhism became, by default, the official religion of the Yamato court.

A famous scene from the Soga side of the battle: the young **Prince Umayado** (OO-mah-yah-doh, the future Prince Shotoku Taishi and the subject of Chapter 2), then about fourteen years old and serving in Umako's army as a junior commander, is said to have personally carved small wooden images of the four Buddhist guardian kings before the battle, vowing to build them a temple if the Soga won. The temple — **Shitenno-ji** (shih-TEN-noh-JEE, "Temple of the Four Heavenly Kings") at modern Osaka — was duly founded after the victory and is still in operation today, though the original buildings were destroyed and rebuilt many times over the centuries. The story may be partly legendary, but it captures the kind of vow-and-fulfillment cycle that early Japanese Buddhism ran on: pray for victory, win the battle, build the temple. The Mononobe had bet on the kami. The Soga had bet on the Buddha. The Buddha won.

---

The aftermath of Shigisen reshaped Japanese religion permanently, but not in the way either side had expected.

The pattern that emerged after 587 was not the replacement of Shinto by Buddhism. The kami did not lose their shrines. The Nakatomi did not lose their priesthood (though they lost their political clout). What happened instead was a uniquely Japanese accommodation that scholars later called **shinbutsu-shugo** (shin-BOO-tsoo SHOO-goh, "the merging of kami and buddhas"). Buddhist temples were built next to Shinto shrines, sometimes literally on the same compound. The native kami were reinterpreted as Japanese local manifestations of Buddhist deities. Buddhist monks performed rituals at Shinto shrines, and Shinto priests participated in Buddhist temple ceremonies. The two traditions interpenetrated until they became, for ordinary Japanese, almost indistinguishable in practice.

This accommodation is genuinely unusual. In most religious histories, when an organized world religion arrives in a region with a strong indigenous tradition, one of two things happens: either the new religion suppresses the old, or the old digests the new and produces a syncretic hybrid that the new religion's foreign authorities consider heretical. Christianity in late-Roman Europe drove out the old gods (mostly) and demonized them. Islam in conquered Iran tolerated Zoroastrians for a generation and then squeezed them down to a marginal minority over the following centuries (Persian Empire Ch 10). The Buddhist arrival in Tibet in the seventh and eighth centuries fused with the local Bon religion in a contested process that took five hundred years and produced multiple incompatible Buddhist schools to unsort it.

Japan did something different. Buddhism did not destroy the kami; it absorbed them and was absorbed by them. The two traditions coexisted for the next thirteen centuries, until the early Meiji government in the 1870s forcibly separated them as part of its modernization program — itself a remarkable piece of religious engineering in the opposite direction. From 587 to 1868, Japan was a country in which most people did not consider a Buddhist temple visit and a Shinto shrine visit to be acts of two different religions. They were two complementary aspects of the same religious life.

This pattern starts here, in the immediate Soga aftermath. Umako, who had just won a war over whether Buddhism would be permitted at all, did not ban the kami cults. He installed Buddhist statues but did not demolish kami shrines. The reason was probably political — the surviving anti-Buddhist factions were too important to alienate, and the kami-worshipping rural population was the entire labor base — but the political pragmatism produced a religious settlement that lasted longer than most pure religious settlements ever do.

---

Soga no Umako spent the years immediately after the Shigisen victory consolidating his political position. In 587 he installed his nephew Hatsusebe on the throne as Emperor Sushun. Sushun lasted five years before Umako had him assassinated in 592 (the chronicles say Umako found Sushun was no longer pliable enough). In 593 Umako installed his own niece — Sushun's half-sister, who had been an empress consort to a previous emperor — as the new sovereign. Her reign name was **Suiko** (soo-EE-koh), and she was the first reigning empress in Japanese history.

Suiko was the first of a remarkable series of Asuka-Nara empresses regnant. Of the eight monarchs who reigned between 593 and 770, six were women, and a seventh (Junnin) was deposed by a woman. This concentration of female rule has no equivalent in any other major monarchy of the period — Tang China would have only one female emperor in three centuries, and her reign was treated as an aberration by every later Chinese historian (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 2). The Asuka-Nara pattern was not aberrational: it was simply how the Yamato succession worked when a strong adult male candidate was unavailable, when the powerful clans needed a temporarily neutral figure on the throne, or when a regent (like Umako) needed a sovereign whose loyalty he could rely on. Whether Suiko herself was the captive of her uncle or his active partner is a matter of debate — the chronicles, written later under Suiko's family, present her as an active and learned monarch, while modern historians have variously read her as a Soga puppet or as a strong figure in her own right. The truth was probably both at different moments.

What is not in dispute is that Suiko's reign produced one of the most consequential political appointments in early Japanese history. In 593 she made her nephew Prince Umayado — the same teenager who had vowed to build Shitenno-ji at Shigisen six years earlier, now twenty years old — the **regent** of her court. He took up his post at the Asuka palace, opposite his great-uncle Soga no Umako. The two of them, with Suiko presiding from the throne, would govern Japan for the next thirty years.

History remembers Prince Umayado by a posthumous title: **Shotoku Taishi** (SHOH-toh-koo TYE-shee, "Prince of Sagely Virtue"). His regency is the subject of Chapter 2.

---

A pause to anchor the international moment.

The arrival of Buddhism in Japan in 538 happened in a world that had been in motion for a long time. The Buddha himself had lived around 480 BCE in northern India (Vedic Period Ch 7, Maurya Empire Ch 1) — the religion was already over a thousand years old when it reached the Yamato court. The great propagator-king **Ashoka** of the Maurya dynasty (Maurya Empire Ch 5) had used Indian state machinery to spread Buddhism across south Asia in the third century BCE. By the first century CE, missionary networks had carried Buddhism north through the Kushan Empire (Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 5) and along the Silk Road to **Han Dynasty** China (Han Dynasty Ch 6). By the fourth century CE, it had reached the **Three Kingdoms of Korea** — Goguryeo accepted Buddhism in 372, Baekje in 384, Silla last and most reluctantly around 528 (Ancient Korea Ch 4–5). And then, in 538, the Baekje statue arrived at the Yamato court. The journey from the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gaya in northern India to a small valley in central Japan had taken roughly a thousand years and covered well over 3,000 miles (4,800 km). Buddhism was the most successful intellectual export in Asian history.

Cross-civ check: while the Soga and Mononobe were fighting over Buddha statues in the Asuka basin, Justinian I was rebuilding the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople (537), the **Sassanid Empire** was at the height of its final century under Khosrow I (Persian Empire Ch 10), the **Gupta Empire** in India was already past its peak and the Gupta synthesis of mathematics-and-Sanskrit-and-Hindu-revival was diffusing across south Asia (Gupta Empire Ch 5–7), and on the Korean peninsula, **Baekje, Silla, and Goguryeo** were the three competing kingdoms whose shifting alliances shaped Japanese policy for the next century and a half (Ancient Korea Ch 4–7). The **Six Dynasties** chaos in China was just then in its closing decades, with the brief Sui reunification in 581 about to remake East Asia (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 1). Buddhism was diffusing from India to Japan along a route that ran through every one of these civilizations. The fact that Japan got it last, after Korea and China and Central Asia had each had centuries to digest and reshape it, meant Japan got a particular form: a fully developed, monastically organized, image-and-sutra-and-temple religion that came pre-packaged with Korean monks, Chinese architectural plans, and a millennium of accumulated theological argument. Japan would spend the next two centuries figuring out which parts of the package it actually wanted.

# Chapter 2 — Shotoku Taishi and Empress Suiko

The figure who dominates the early Asuka period is a young prince named Umayado — known to history as **Shotoku Taishi** (SHOH-toh-koo TYE-shee, "Prince of Sagely Virtue"). He was twenty years old when his aunt **Empress Suiko** (soo-EE-koh) made him regent of her court in 593. He held that post for twenty-nine years, until his death in 622 at age forty-eight. In those twenty-nine years he attempted, with mixed success, to remake the Yamato state into a centralized monarchy on the Chinese model — and along the way he founded a temple that still contains the oldest standing wooden buildings anywhere on Earth.

Shotoku is the closest thing Japan has to a founding father. The cult that grew up around him after his death portrayed him as a saint, a Buddhist sage, a political prophet, a mystical figure who could read ten petitions simultaneously, who heard the cries of dying ascetics from miles away, and who composed his own funeral elegy at age six. Modern historians spend much of their time peeling back this cult to find the historical man underneath, and the picture that emerges is more interesting than the legend. The historical Shotoku was an ambitious, intellectually voracious, politically savvy regent who used his position as the favored nephew of both Empress Suiko and Soga no Umako to push reforms that neither his patroness nor his great-uncle could have pushed alone. He failed, in important ways. The Tang-style centralized state he envisioned was not built in his lifetime, and his own line was wiped out by the Soga within a generation of his death. But the direction he set held. Every major Japanese reform of the next century was built on Shotoku's program.

---

The court Shotoku inherited in 593 was small, rural, and impermanent. The Yamato sovereigns moved their palace with each new reign — partly for ritual reasons (Shinto beliefs about death pollution made it taboo to remain in a building where the previous monarch had died) and partly because the court was small enough to relocate without enormous expense. The new palace under Suiko was at **Toyura** (TOH-yoo-rah) and then **Oharida** (oh-hah-REE-dah) in the Asuka basin, a few miles from the previous court site. The basin itself was a small valley running roughly north-south, bordered by low hills, with the Asuka River winding through it. The total population of the Asuka court complex was probably in the low thousands. By Tang Chinese standards, this was barely a provincial market town. Shotoku had been to Korea (probably as a child or young man, accompanying diplomatic missions) and would have known how primitive the Yamato setup looked compared to a real continental capital.

His response was a sustained campaign of institutional borrowing.

In 603, Shotoku promulgated the **Twelve Level Cap and Rank System** (often shortened to **Twelve Cap Ranks**), Japan's first formal court rank system. Twelve grades of rank, marked by colored caps that officials wore at court, replaced the older system in which rank attached to the clan rather than the person. The colors were graded — purple at the top, then blue, red, yellow, white, and black, each in two shades — and were modeled directly on the **Sui** (SWAY) Chinese rank system that had been promulgated in the 580s (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 1). The point was to give the throne a tool for elevating talented officials regardless of their clan birth. In practice, the great clans continued to dominate the high ranks for generations, but the principle had been established: rank was a personal possession, not a clan inheritance. The seed planted in 603 would eventually grow, by the eighth century, into a court bureaucracy in which appointment depended on rank and rank depended (at least nominally) on individual qualification.

The next year, 604, Shotoku promulgated his most famous document: the **Seventeen-Article Constitution**. The name is misleading. It is not a constitution in the modern sense — it establishes no branches of government, defines no procedures, grants no individual rights, sets no limits on royal power. What it is, instead, is a moral handbook for court officials, a hortatory document blending Buddhist ethics, Confucian hierarchy, and a pragmatic Japanese insistence on group harmony. Article One reads: "Harmony is to be valued, and an avoidance of wanton opposition is to be honored." Article Two: "Sincerely revere the Three Treasures of Buddhism — the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha." Article Three: "When the imperial commands are received, they shall be scrupulously obeyed." Article Twelve: "Provincial authorities and district lords shall not levy exactions on the people." The document reads, in places, like a memo from a thoughtful CEO to a fractious management team — it earnestly tries to impose civil-service norms on a court that was used to clan feuding.

Whether Shotoku himself wrote the Constitution is debated. The document survives only as quoted in the Nihon Shoki of 720 (Chapter 7), more than a century later. Some modern scholars argue that the Constitution as we have it is in part an eighth-century composition projected backward onto Shotoku's era to give later reforms a venerable founding document. The current scholarly reading is somewhere in between: Shotoku probably promulgated some version of a moral-political directive, and the eighth-century text we have is an elaboration and stylization of his original. Either way, the Seventeen-Article Constitution is one of the foundational texts of East Asian political thought, and it has been the subject of continuous Japanese commentary for fourteen centuries.

The same year, Shotoku adopted the **Chinese lunisolar calendar** (the Yuanjia calendar that the Sui used), enabling for the first time a standardized system of timekeeping across the Yamato court — useful for agricultural planning, taxation, and bureaucratic record-keeping, all of which were starting to matter more than they had a generation earlier.

---

Shotoku's foreign policy was as ambitious as his domestic reforms.

In 607, he sent a diplomatic embassy to the Sui Chinese court under the diplomat **Ono no Imoko** (OH-noh noh ee-MOH-koh). The embassy was the first formal Japanese mission to China in over a century, and it carried a letter from the Yamato sovereign to the Sui emperor that opened with a famous and politically explosive greeting: *"From the Son of Heaven in the land where the sun rises, to the Son of Heaven in the land where the sun sets. We hope you are well."*

The phrase translated as "Son of Heaven" is the Chinese term *tianzi*, the formal title of the Chinese emperor — the unique earthly representative of Heaven. By Chinese diplomatic convention, no other ruler in the world was a Son of Heaven. Korean kings, Central Asian khaganates, Indian rajas — all were vassals or barbarians, addressed in correspondence as inferiors. To address the Chinese emperor as one Son of Heaven to another was a frontal challenge to the entire Sinocentric world order. To call China "the land where the sun sets" while calling Japan "the land where the sun rises" added insult to the challenge. Sui Emperor Yangdi (the second and last Sui emperor, the disastrous overreacher whose Korean wars would shortly destroy his dynasty — Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 1) was reportedly furious. The chronicle says he ordered his foreign-relations officials never to bring such a letter to him again.

But he did not refuse the embassy. He accepted Ono no Imoko's gifts, sent a Chinese diplomat back with him, and the channel was opened. The Yamato court's claim to be a peer of the Chinese empire was, of course, ludicrous on a power-arithmetic level — Sui had a population of around 50 million people and an army that had just (605–611) built the Grand Canal with five million conscripts, while Yamato had a population of perhaps 5 million and a court that lived in wooden palaces it relocated every reign. But the diplomatic gesture mattered. It established the principle that would define Japanese foreign relations forever after: Japan would borrow Chinese culture intensively but would not formally subordinate itself to Chinese suzerainty. Korean kingdoms, which were physically connected to China and could be invaded overland, accepted vassal status and tribute relations. Japan, sitting on islands a hundred miles offshore, could afford to be impudent.

Ono no Imoko's 607 mission was followed by a continuous stream of **kentoshi** ("envoys to Tang") — though when the Sui collapsed in 618 and was replaced by the Tang, the Japanese embassies simply switched targets. Over the next two and a half centuries, Japan would send roughly twenty official missions to the Chinese court, some carrying hundreds of monks, scholars, and officials on dangerous voyages across the East China Sea. The embassies were the central conduit by which Tang Chinese institutional models, Buddhist learning, art, technology, and material culture flowed into Japan. They were also enormously expensive and lethally risky: ships sank, embassies were lost, returning students arrived decades later having forgotten which emperor was on the Yamato throne. But the program continued for as long as the Tang was visibly a model worth copying. (We will meet the embassies several more times — Chapters 6 and 8 in particular.)

---

Shotoku's most enduring physical legacy is a temple complex called **Horyu-ji** (hor-YOO-jee, "Temple of the Flourishing Dharma"), founded in 607 in the village of Ikaruga, a few miles north of the Asuka basin. Horyu-ji is a remarkable place for several reasons.

First, it contains the oldest surviving wooden buildings anywhere on Earth. The main hall (the Kondo) and the five-story pagoda at Horyu-ji have been standing in their current form since the late seventh century — making them roughly 1,300 years old. (There is a small archaeological complication here: the original 607 buildings burned down in a fire in 670, and the surviving structures are a careful reconstruction completed by around 711, using the same Korean-derived techniques. Some of the timbers in the central pillar of the pagoda, however, have been radiocarbon-dated to around 594 — meaning at least some original wood was reused in the reconstruction, and parts of the pagoda may genuinely date to Shotoku's era.) Either way, no other wooden buildings in human history have stood for so long. The cypress timbers used in the construction have proven remarkably resistant to rot, fire, and insect damage; the joinery techniques have absorbed earthquake stress that would shatter rigid stone construction; and continuous Buddhist patronage has paid for the periodic refurbishment that any wooden building requires.

Second, Horyu-ji's architectural style is a frozen snapshot of late Sui–early Tang Chinese building practice, transmitted via Baekje. The deep eaves, the brackets that fan out from the column tops to support the roof load (called **tokyo** in Japanese), the use of stone podiums and wooden columns, and the layout of the temple compound all derive directly from continental models that have not survived in their original Chinese or Korean forms. To see what a major early Chinese Buddhist temple looked like, you go to Horyu-ji. The Chinese originals burned down centuries ago.

Third, the pagoda contains a structural innovation that Japanese carpenters would refine over the next millennium: a central pillar (the **shinbashira**, shin-BAH-shih-rah) that runs the full height of the structure but is not load-bearing. Rather than supporting the floors and roof above it, the central pillar hangs from the top of the pagoda, swinging slightly as the building moves, while the actual structural load is carried by the surrounding columns. The pillar acts as a kind of pendulum that absorbs earthquake stress by oscillating out of phase with the floors. Whether the original seventh-century carpenters fully understood the engineering is unclear — some modern scholars argue the design was originally just symbolic, with the structural benefits noticed and refined later — but the result is real: Japanese pagodas almost never fall in earthquakes, even at scales (60-meter pagodas) that would seem catastrophically vulnerable. Modern engineers have compared the design to a passive tuned mass damper, the kind of pendulum suspended in skyscrapers like Taipei 101 to absorb wind and seismic loads. The comparison is retrospective, not a deliberate engineering choice, but striking.

Horyu-ji is not the only temple Shotoku founded. He is also credited with **Shitenno-ji** in modern Osaka (the temple he vowed at Shigisen, Chapter 1, much rebuilt and now in a 1960s reinforced-concrete reconstruction), and his patronage helped the Soga clan complete **Asuka-dera** (also called Hoko-ji), Japan's first true Buddhist temple complex, which was finished in 596 with Korean architects from Baekje. The bronze Asuka Daibutsu inside Asuka-dera, cast around 609 by the sculptor **Tori Busshi** (TOH-ree BOO-shee — grandson of a Korean immigrant), is the oldest surviving Japanese Buddhist statue.

---

Shotoku died in 622 at age forty-eight. His mother and his consort died within months of him; the cluster of deaths in the same family at the same time was probably an epidemic but produced lasting suspicions of poisoning. Whatever the cause, Shotoku's death cut short his reform program before it could institutionalize itself. His son Prince Yamashiro no Oe inherited his father's temples and his father's faction at court but did not have his father's combination of imperial blood, political favor, and personal capability. Within twenty years, the Yamashiro line would be wiped out by the Soga (Chapter 3).

The cult of Shotoku as a near-Buddhist saint began almost immediately after his death. By the 660s, only forty years on, the chroniclers were attributing supernatural deeds to him. By the eighth century he had become a quasi-bodhisattva, the embodiment of Japanese Buddhist learning, the first translator of the great Mahayana sutras into Japanese commentary. The Hokke-ji nuns in Nara claimed the prince had personally written commentaries on the Lotus Sutra, the Vimalakirti Sutra, and the Shrimala Sutra — the so-called Three Sutra Commentaries, traditionally attributed to Shotoku himself. Modern scholars are skeptical of the attribution (the linguistic evidence in the surviving texts is mixed, and the standard scholarly reading is that they were composed in the late seventh century by court Buddhist scholars in Shotoku's name), but the tradition itself testifies to the cultural weight Shotoku came to carry.

His face appeared on the 10,000-yen note from 1958 to 1986 — for a generation the most-reproduced image in Japanese history. He has been depicted in countless paintings, sculptures, anime, manga, dramas, and elementary-school textbooks. The Japanese imagine him roughly the way Americans imagine the founding fathers, with the same mixture of reverence, partisan reinterpretation, and selective memory.

The historical Shotoku was a real person, ambitious and intellectually serious, who pushed the Yamato court several decades further down the road toward bureaucratic centralization than it would have gone without him. He did not finish the project — that would take another century — but he set the direction so unmistakably that everything that followed could be presented as completing his work.

---

Cross-civ check: while Shotoku was promulgating the Seventeen-Article Constitution and sending letters to Sui Yangdi addressing him as an equal, **Justinian** had been dead for forty years and the Byzantine Empire was sliding into the catastrophic Persian and then Arab wars that would shortly halve its territory (Byzantine Empire Ch 5). The Sassanid Empire under Khosrow II was at the height of its power, about to conquer Egypt and besiege Constantinople in the 610s — and about to be destroyed by the Arab armies of the new Islamic faith in the 630s (Persian Empire Ch 10, Umayyad Caliphate Ch 1). The **prophet Muhammad** was a young man in Mecca during Shotoku's regency, beginning his career as a merchant and preparing the religious career that would begin in 610 — within a few years of Horyu-ji's founding. The world that Shotoku addressed as "the land where the sun sets" was about to be remade in ways that no one in 607 could have anticipated. By the time Shotoku's grandnephew presided over the Taika Reform forty years later (Chapter 3), the Sassanid Empire would no longer exist.

A structural parallel worth pausing on: Shotoku's reforms — a young royal-family member promulgating a moral-political code, building monumental religious architecture, claiming a kind of equality with the dominant continental empire of the day — bear a remarkable resemblance to the program of **Emperor Justinian** the previous century. Both were trying to import the institutional model of a more sophisticated neighbor (Roman tradition for Justinian, Tang China for Shotoku) into a culturally distinct local setting. Both built a great religious building that was meant to demonstrate the new order in stone (Hagia Sophia for Justinian, Horyu-ji for Shotoku — though Horyu-ji is wood, with a different relationship to permanence). Both worked through a strong female imperial figure (Empress Theodora for Justinian, Empress Suiko for Shotoku) whose actual role in policy is debated by modern historians. And both ran out of time before their reform projects could be fully institutionalized — Justinian's reconquests collapsed within a century, Shotoku's centralizing program had to be re-launched in 645 (Chapter 3). The comparison is not exact (Shotoku was a regent, not a sovereign; Justinian inherited a more developed state). But the resemblance suggests something general about how middle-rank powers absorb the institutional templates of their dominant neighbors. Japan and Byzantium were doing structurally similar things at the same historical moment, on opposite ends of Eurasia, in mutual ignorance.

# Chapter 3 — The Coup of 645 and the Taika Reform

For sixty years after the Battle of Shigisen, the Soga clan had run the Yamato court. Soga no Umako had been the de facto regent under Empress Suiko (Chapters 1 and 2). After his death in 626, his son **Soga no Emishi** (eh-MEE-shee) inherited the position, and after Emishi, his grandson **Soga no Iruka** (ee-ROO-kah). By 645, the Soga were not just dominant at court — they had become arrogant in a way that earlier Japanese aristocrats had been careful to avoid.

The signature of Soga overreach was Iruka's destruction of the Shotoku family. In 643, Iruka sent troops to the Ikaruga estate of Prince **Yamashiro no Oe** (yah-mah-SHEE-roh noh OH-eh), Shotoku Taishi's son and a possible candidate for the throne. Yamashiro and his entire household — wives, children, retainers — chose suicide over capture, hanging themselves inside the Horyu-ji compound. The chronicles describe the scene in mournful detail: a great prince of Buddhist learning, the son of a saint, exterminated with his whole line, in the temple his father had built. Even allowing for the chronicles' partisan editing (they were written under the regime that had just overthrown the Soga, with every motive to blacken Iruka's reputation), the basic facts are not seriously disputed. Iruka had decided that no rival imperial line could be allowed to survive. The Soga grandchildren of Empress Suiko's line were the only acceptable claimants.

This was a miscalculation. Even the Yamato aristocracy, which had a high tolerance for political violence, considered the destruction of Shotoku's family an outrage. The temples Shotoku had founded were national institutions. The Buddhist establishment owed the prince's memory a debt that no Soga purge could erase. And other princes of the imperial line — including some who were themselves part Soga — began to fear that Iruka would come for them next.

The leading edge of the resistance was a young prince named **Naka no Oe** (NAH-kah noh OH-eh), grandson of Empress Suiko's predecessor Bidatsu and a candidate for the throne in his own right. He was around twenty years old, ambitious, and dangerous in the way Iruka had failed to appreciate. He found his political partner in a court noble of the **Nakatomi** clan — the same clan that had backed the Mononobe in the 587 religious war and had been politically marginal ever since. The Nakatomi heir was named **Nakatomi no Kamatari** (nah-kah-TOH-mee noh kah-mah-TAH-ree), about ten years older than Naka no Oe, brilliant, embittered by his clan's exclusion from real power, and reportedly the man with the actual coup plan in his head.

The two princes met, the chronicles say, at a kemari game (a kind of ceremonial kickball played at court — the standard upper-class social activity of the period) in 644, where Kamatari fetched Naka no Oe's lost shoe and the two of them began their careful conversations about how to remove Iruka. Whether the kemari-game origin story is true or romanticized, the conspiracy was real. By the spring of 645, they had a plan.

---

The opportunity came on July 10, 645 (in the modern Western calendar; the chronicles give the date in Japanese ritual reckoning). A formal court ceremony was scheduled at Empress Kogyoku's palace at Asuka, at which envoys from the three Korean kingdoms — Baekje, Silla, and Goguryeo — would present tribute to the Yamato sovereign. Soga no Iruka, as the dominant figure at court, was expected to attend in his full regalia. The ceremony would draw all the senior nobles into a single hall on a single afternoon. Naka no Oe and Kamatari intended to make sure Iruka did not leave it alive.

The attack went badly at first. The two assassins Naka no Oe had stationed in the wings froze at the sight of Iruka and the assembled nobility. Naka no Oe himself had to leap forward and personally cut Iruka down with a sword, in front of his stunned mother the empress, while the Korean envoys watched. Iruka, badly wounded, reportedly crawled toward Empress Kogyoku and asked what crime he had committed; she fled the hall without answering. He died on the floor of the audience chamber.

The next day, Iruka's father Soga no Emishi, who had retreated to the family compound at Asuka with his retainers, set fire to his own mansion and committed suicide rather than face the loyal Naka no Oe troops marching on his estate. The compound burned, and a number of irreplaceable historical records — the chronicles say the Tenno-ki ("Records of the Sovereigns") and the Kokki ("National Records") that Shotoku Taishi and Soga no Umako had compiled together in the 620s — were destroyed in the flames. (The episode is one reason eighth-century historians had to reconstruct so much: the records the seventh-century court had assembled were ash.) The remaining Soga were rounded up and either executed or stripped of their offices over the following weeks. The clan that had dominated Yamato politics for sixty years was wiped out in a long weekend.

This event is called the **Isshi Incident** (ISH-shee), after the name of the year in the traditional Chinese sexagenary cycle. It is the most famous palace coup in early Japanese history, and it is the hinge that turns the Asuka period from its first half — the Soga era — into its second half, the era of Tang-modeled reform.

---

Empress Kogyoku, who had presided in shock over the assassination of her chief minister, abdicated within days. Her brother took the throne as **Emperor Kotoku** (KOH-toh-koo). The real power in the new regime was Naka no Oe, who declined the throne for himself (he was technically still a prince, not directly the heir, and taking the throne immediately after a violent coup would have looked too much like a Soga-style usurpation) but became Crown Prince and effective ruler. Nakatomi no Kamatari was made the new Inner Minister, the senior advisor at court. The two of them — the warrior prince and the bureaucratic minister — began work on the reform program that would define the next decade.

The first symbolic act was a calendar change. The new regime adopted Japan's first reign-name (a Chinese-style era designation that subsequent emperors would use to mark major regnal moments) and called it **Taika** (TYE-kah, "Great Change"). The era name announced the political program in two syllables: this was not a continuation of the old order, this was a great change, on the Tang Chinese model. Every reform that followed in the next four years was promulgated under the banner of "Taika," and the whole program is collectively known as the **Taika Reform**.

The second symbolic act was a capital move. Naka no Oe and Kotoku abandoned the Asuka basin — too closely associated with the Soga, too rural, too cramped — and moved the court to **Naniwa-kyo** (modern Osaka), on the coast where the Yodo River meets Osaka Bay. The new capital was laid out for the first time in Japanese history on a Tang-style north-south grid, with a great palace called the **Naniwa Nagara-Toyosaki Palace** at the head of a central avenue. Nothing of the architectural scale survived (the palace burned down later in the century and modern Osaka has been built and rebuilt over the site many times), but the gridded urban plan was the first signal that the new regime intended to copy continental city planning along with continental institutions.

---

In the first month of the Taika era — January 646 — Emperor Kotoku promulgated the **Taika Edicts**. The edicts are the most ambitious single piece of legislation in early Japanese history. They proposed, in four articles, a wholesale restructuring of the Japanese state on the Tang model.

Article One abolished private ownership of land and private subjects (the **be**, BAY — hereditary servant-lineages attached to particular clans). All land was to revert to the throne. All cultivators were to become subjects of the central state. The economic basis of clan power was to be liquidated.

Article Two reorganized the country into a system of provinces (**kuni**, KOO-nee), districts (**gun**, GOON), and townships (**ri**, REE), each governed by an officer appointed from the center. The chaotic patchwork of clan territories was to be replaced with a Tang-style provincial bureaucracy. Provincial governors and district magistrates would report through a chain of command running back to the imperial palace.

Article Three ordered the compilation of population registers (**koseki**, KOH-seh-kee) and tax registers, on which the new central tax system would be based. Each adult in the realm was to be enrolled, and taxes would be levied on the basis of the registers, not on the basis of clan obligations.

Article Four replaced the older clan-based labor and tribute system with a uniform tax in rice (a fixed percentage of the harvest from each adult cultivator's allocated land), a tax in cloth and other products, and a labor obligation (so many days per year of corvée work for the central government). The system was modeled on the Tang **equal-field** (juntian) system that Wen and Taizong of Sui-Tang had perfected to support their massive bureaucracies and armies (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 1–2).

This was, on paper, a revolution. It proposed to turn Japan from a clan-federation patched together by ritual obligations into a centralized empire on the Chinese model — with all land state-owned, all cultivators state-registered, all officials state-appointed, all taxes state-collected.

In practice, the reforms were only partially implemented. The powerful clans — even after the Soga purge, there were several other great clans whose cooperation the throne could not do without — resisted the abolition of private land in their own territories. The bureaucratic apparatus needed to actually run a Tang-style state did not yet exist; it had to be built up over the following half-century, official by official, register by register, province by province. The 646 Edicts were less an instruction set that could be executed in the short term and more a manifesto: a statement of what the reformers thought Japan ought to look like in fifty or a hundred years. Some of the proposed reforms (universal tax registration, the provincial-district system) eventually happened, by the early eighth century. Others (true central ownership of all land) never fully happened — the Tang system itself broke down in China within a couple of centuries (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 4), and Japan never built the institutional muscle to keep its own version intact.

But the direction had been set. From 645 onward, the Yamato court was committed to becoming a Tang-style state. Every subsequent law code (the Omi Code, the Asuka Kiyomihara Code, the Taiho Code — Chapters 4 and 5) pushed further down the road. Every subsequent capital was built more aggressively on the Tang model. Every subsequent generation of officials was trained on Confucian classics and Chinese legal precedent. The 645 coup turned the country onto a track that it would stay on for a century and a half.

---

A crucial side-effect of the 645 coup was the elevation of Nakatomi no Kamatari, who had been a minor noble of a marginalized clan and who in the next twenty years became the most powerful courtier in Japan.

Kamatari served as Inner Minister to three emperors. He drafted laws, advised on Korean policy, ran the censure courts, and built up a clientele of officials who owed their careers to him personally. On his deathbed in 669, he was visited by Naka no Oe (by then on the throne as Emperor Tenji), who granted him a new family name as the highest possible reward for a lifetime of service: **Fujiwara** (foo-jee-WAH-rah), after the wisteria-flowered estates where Kamatari had lived. The new clan was Kamatari's own line and his son's line; the rest of the Nakatomi remained Nakatomi.

This is the founding of the **Fujiwara clan**, which would dominate Japanese court politics for the next four centuries. Every regent of the Heian period (Heian Japan Ch 1–3), every great Heian poet, every emperor's mother for most of the next four hundred years, would be a Fujiwara. Kamatari's son **Fujiwara no Fuhito** (FOO-hee-toh, 659–720) would architect the Nara-period legal codes (Chapter 5), marry his daughters into the imperial line in three different generations, and build the political machine his descendants would inherit. The Fujiwara were what the Soga had failed to be: a clan that controlled the throne not by murdering imperial princes but by marrying them. The Fujiwara strategy was elegant, durable, and almost impossible to dislodge from below.

---

In 660, in the middle of the post-Taika reform period, an event on the Korean peninsula changed everything.

The kingdom of **Baekje**, Japan's closest ally on the continent and the cultural conduit through which Buddhism, writing, and most of the toraijin specialists had flowed for the previous century, was destroyed by a coalition of Tang Chinese armies and Korean **Silla** (SIL-la) forces. The Tang general Su Dingfang led a 130,000-man invasion fleet across the Yellow Sea; the Silla king committed his own armies overland from the south; Baekje's smaller forces could not hold against a two-front war. The Baekje capital Sabi fell within months. The Baekje king was taken to the Tang court as a captive. The kingdom was carved up between Tang occupation forces and Silla allies (Ancient Korea Ch 6).

For the Yamato court, Baekje's destruction was a strategic catastrophe and an emotional crisis. Baekje was the kingdom that had sent the Buddha statue, whose royal family had intermarried with Yamato princes for generations, whose specialists had been arriving in Japan in steady waves for nearly two centuries. To lose Baekje was to lose the closest thing the Yamato court had to a continental cousin. And it raised the immediate prospect that Tang and Silla, now allied and victorious, would turn next to Japan itself.

The Baekje resistance held out in the southwest of the peninsula for three years after the fall of Sabi, led by Baekje generals who escaped the initial Tang sweep, and a Baekje prince fled to the Yamato court asking for help in reclaiming his throne. Naka no Oe — by 661 effectively running the Yamato state on behalf of the now-elderly Empress Saimei (who was Empress Kogyoku reigning under a new name after her abdicated brother Kotoku had died) — decided to intervene.

The decision was momentous and, in retrospect, mistaken. The Yamato court committed an enormous expedition: an estimated 800 ships and 40,000 men, the largest military force Japan had ever assembled, sent across the Korea Strait to reinforce the Baekje resistance and contest the Tang-Silla occupation.

In August 663, the Yamato fleet met the Tang and Silla naval forces at the mouth of the Geum River, on the Korean west coast. The battle is called **Baekgang** (BEHK-gahng — "White River," the Korean name for the Geum estuary). It was a disaster.

Tang naval doctrine was vastly more sophisticated than anything Japan had experience with. Tang ships were larger, more heavily built, and crewed with professional naval troops. Tang fire-arrow tactics could ignite an opposing fleet at standoff range. Tang formation discipline allowed concentrated boarding actions where Yamato ships were forced into single combats. Over two days of fighting, the Tang and Silla forces destroyed roughly 400 Japanese ships (half of the entire fleet) by ramming, burning, or boarding. Yamato casualties were enormous — possibly 10,000 dead, most of them drowned. The surviving Yamato ships fled south down the coast and back to Japan with the remnants of the Baekje resistance. The Baekje resistance collapsed within months.

Baekgang is one of the most consequential battles in Japanese history. Its three major effects:

First, Japanese military ambitions on the continent ended for nearly a thousand years. From 663 until **Toyotomi Hideyoshi's** invasions of Korea in the 1590s (Edo Japan Ch 1), no Japanese government attempted to project military power onto the Korean peninsula. The geographic logic — Japan could be defended by the sea, but offensive operations into Korea required a fleet that could decisively beat whatever continental power was paying attention — held throughout the medieval and early-modern periods. The Yamato lesson was learned: stay home, build the islands, defend the coasts, do not try to fight Tang armies on Tang terms.

Second, Naka no Oe ordered the construction of a chain of defensive fortifications across northern Kyushu against a possible Tang counter-invasion. The most famous was the **Mizuki** (MEE-zoo-kee, "water castle"), a long earthwork dike with a moat that ran across the narrow neck of land protecting the inland Dazaifu administrative center. A network of mountain fortresses (**yamashiro**, yah-mah-SHEE-roh) was thrown up on key passes, with Korean Baekje refugee engineers — fleeing into Japan in the wake of their kingdom's destruction — providing the construction expertise. Tang never invaded. By the 670s the threat had dissipated, as Tang and Silla turned on each other in the post-conquest carve-up of Korea (Ancient Korea Ch 7) and as Tang's attention pivoted to the Tibetan Empire on its western frontier. But the fortresses had been built, the construction labor had been mobilized, and the Yamato court had its first taste of what large-scale military mobilization actually cost.

Third, the destruction of Baekje produced a refugee wave that profoundly accelerated Japan's cultural development. Tens of thousands of Baekje aristocrats, scholars, monks, architects, metalworkers, and military officers fled the conquered kingdom and were welcomed in Japan. Many were given lands in central Honshu, granted court ranks, and absorbed into the Yamato aristocracy. Their descendants would shape Japanese cultural production for generations. The kingdom that had been Japan's mentor was gone; many of its best people now lived in Japan.

---

A pause to reflect on Baekgang's broader significance.

The defeat at Baekgang was the moment Japanese sovereignty became, by default, an island sovereignty. Korea, China, and the steppe powers fought their wars over the same Eurasian landmass, with the same rules: armies marched across borders, sieges took down capitals, dynasties replaced each other through conquest. Japan, after 663, was defended by its sea moat. No foreign army would land on the Japanese islands again until the Mongol invasions of 1274 and 1281 (Mongol Empire Ch 8) — over six centuries of continental immunity. This is one of the foundational facts of Japanese history. Japan had the option, in a way that Korea or Persia or Hungary did not, to choose what it borrowed from abroad and at what pace, because the choice was never enforced by an invading army.

Naka no Oe finally took the throne himself in 661 after Empress Saimei died, and reigned as **Emperor Tenji** (TEN-jee) until 672. His reign concentrated on the post-Baekgang reconstruction: defensive fortifications, the **Omi Code** of 668 (Japan's first comprehensive legal code, intermediate between the Taika Edicts and the later Asuka Kiyomihara Code — no copy survives, but later sources reference it heavily), the first comprehensive household and population register of central Japan (the **Kogo-nenjaku** of 670, taking over a year to compile and registering perhaps 5 million subjects), and the relocation of the capital from Naniwa to a new palace at **Otsu** on Lake Biwa, near modern Kyoto, in 667.

Tenji died in 672 after a reign of eleven years. The succession dispute that followed was the largest civil war of the entire Asuka period: the Jinshin War. That, and the late-Asuka regime that came out of it, are the subject of Chapter 4.

---

Cross-civ check: the 645 Isshi Incident was contemporary with the early **Rashidun Caliphate** in the Arab world — Caliph Umar had just been assassinated in 644, Uthman was in the early years of his reign, the explosive Arab conquests of Persia and Byzantine Egypt and the Levant were under way (Persian Empire Ch 10, Byzantine Empire Ch 5, Umayyad Caliphate Ch 1). The Sassanid Empire of Persia, the dominant power of western Asia for four centuries, was destroyed between 636 and 651 — exactly the years Naka no Oe was building his Tang-modeled state in the Yamato court. The world was being remade in two directions at once. In the Tang Chinese capital of **Chang'an**, Emperor Taizong was at the height of the Zhenguan era (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 2), governing the largest, wealthiest, most cosmopolitan empire on Earth and providing the model the Yamato reformers were trying to copy. The contrast Naka no Oe could draw on was vivid: Tang power was real, Sassanid power was suddenly nothing, and the difference was institutional. Centralized states with bureaucracies and standing armies could mobilize resources and absorb shocks; clan federations and nomadic confederations could not. The Taika Edicts were partly a response to this geopolitical lesson.

A direct comparison: the Taika Reform (646) and the **Tang Code of 624** were almost exactly contemporary attempts to codify Sui-derived bureaucratic models into operational law. **Tang Taizong's** Zhenguan reforms in China — equal-field land system, civil service examinations, the cap-and-rank bureaucracy — are the direct model for what Naka no Oe and Kamatari were attempting to build in Japan two decades later. Tang Taizong was twenty years older than Naka no Oe and had taken power through a similarly bloody palace coup (the Xuanwu Gate Incident of 626 — Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 2). Both rulers used a coup-and-reform sequence to reshape their states: a dramatic seizure of power followed by an aggressive program of institutional modernization. Taizong's reforms succeeded more thoroughly, partly because Tang inherited a far more developed bureaucratic substrate to build on. Naka no Oe's reforms were more aspirational, partly because Japan had no comparable substrate. But the model was the same, and the trajectory both regimes set their countries on lasted for centuries.

# Chapter 4 — The Hakuho Era and the Jinshin War

The thirty years between Baekgang in 663 and the move to Fujiwara-kyo in 694 are a transitional moment in Japanese history. Politically, they cover the late reign of Emperor Tenji, the largest civil war in early Japanese history, and the long consolidating reign of Tenji's brother Tenmu and Tenmu's widow Empress Jito. Culturally, they are called the **Hakuho period** (hah-koo-HOH, "white phoenix"), a name applied retrospectively by twentieth-century art historians to distinguish the late-Asuka decades from the earlier Soga-era Asuka and the later Nara periods. The Hakuho is best known for a particular style of Buddhist sculpture — fuller, more lifelike, more naturalistic figures than the angular early-Asuka work — but it is also the moment Japan stopped imitating Korean Buddhist art and started absorbing direct Tang influences brought back by Yamato monks who had spent years studying in Chang'an.

The political story of the Hakuho is the story of how the imperial line consolidated its authority after Baekgang, weathered a violent succession crisis, and emerged with the centralized power it had been trying to build since the Taika Edicts twenty years earlier.

---

Emperor Tenji, the former Naka no Oe and the architect of the 645 coup, finally died in late 671 after a reign that had been spent reconstructing from the Baekgang disaster. His death immediately produced a succession crisis.

Tenji had a younger brother named Prince **Oama** (OH-ah-mah), who had served as Crown Prince during much of his brother's reign and was the conventionally expected heir. He had also fathered, late in life, a son named Prince **Otomo** (OH-toh-moh) by a relatively low-ranking consort. As Tenji aged, he had become increasingly favorable to making his own son Otomo the successor, even though Otomo was younger, had less experience, and lacked the network of allies that Oama had built up over decades. Tenji formally elevated Otomo to a position of high authority in late 671, signaling — without actually changing the nominal succession — that he wanted his son rather than his brother to inherit.

Oama, sensing what was coming, did something dramatic. He renounced all claims on the throne, shaved his head as a Buddhist monk, and retired to **Yoshino** (yoh-SHEE-noh), a remote mountainous region south of Asuka, where he announced his intention to spend the rest of his life in religious meditation. The chronicles describe this as a strategic move: by making himself look completely uninterested in politics, Oama bought time and reduced the suspicion that Tenji's deathbed faction had been gathering against him. As soon as Tenji died, however, Oama began secretly assembling allies among the eastern aristocracy — especially the warrior families of the Mino-Owari region (modern central Honshu, between Nagoya and Lake Biwa), who had grievances against the Otomo faction and who controlled key passes leading from the eastern provinces toward the capital.

In the summer of 672, Oama left Yoshino with a small party of loyal retainers and rode east into the mountains. Within weeks, he had assembled an army of perhaps 30,000 men, marched on the Otsu capital where the Otomo faction was concentrated, and defeated his nephew's larger but less coordinated forces in a series of battles around Lake Biwa. The decisive engagement came at the **Battle of Seta**, where Oama's eastern coalition broke through Otomo's defensive line on the Seta Bridge and routed his army. Otomo, surrounded and defeated, hanged himself rather than surrender. Tenji's son was dead, and Oama took the throne as **Emperor Tenmu** (TEN-moo).

This was the **Jinshin War** (JIN-shin, again named after the year in the sexagenary cycle). It lasted only about a month of active fighting but mobilized larger armies and produced more casualties than any other event of the Asuka period. Several thousand men died in the battles. Many of Otomo's senior supporters were executed in the aftermath. The Yamato court, which prided itself on its civilized refinement, had just gone through a violent and bloody civil war between an uncle and a nephew, both of whom were direct grandchildren of imperial mothers.

The war's lasting effect was the consolidation of imperial authority. Tenmu — who was already an experienced political operator with a clear reform program — used his victory to break the political power of any clan that had supported Otomo, redistribute their lands and offices among his own loyalists, and centralize court rituals and decision-making in the imperial palace to a degree that no previous Yamato sovereign had attempted. The Soga had fallen in 645. The pro-Otomo faction fell in 672. The two great post-Shotoku reform moments produced the same result: a court whose dominant figure was the emperor, surrounded by reform-minded officials who owed their positions directly to the throne, with the great hereditary clans permanently weakened relative to imperial authority.

---

Emperor Tenmu's fourteen-year reign (672–686) is when many of the institutions of the next two centuries were either invented or formalized.

He formalized the title **tenno** (TEN-noh — "heavenly sovereign"). Earlier sovereigns had been called by various titles, including **okimi** (great king) and *sumera-mikoto* (a Japanese sacred title roughly meaning "divine sovereign"). Tenmu adopted *tenno* as the official designation, with explicit reference to the Daoist concept of the heavenly emperor and the Tang Chinese imperial title huangdi. The name change was a theological claim: the Yamato sovereign was not just the head of the senior aristocratic clan, he was a heavenly figure with cosmic significance. From Tenmu onward, Japanese monarchs are conventionally called *tenno*, and the Western convention of translating this as "emperor" persists today (despite the technical implications — there was never an actual Japanese empire in the sense of a multi-ethnic conquered territory governed by the tenno).

He commissioned the writing of national histories. Tenmu, troubled by the burning of the Tenno-ki and Kokki at the Soga compound in 645 (Chapter 3) and by the absence of any organized Yamato historical record, summoned the court reciter **Hieda no Are** (hee-EH-dah noh ah-REH) — said by the chronicles to have had a perfect memory and the ability to recite long oral compositions verbatim — and instructed Are to memorize a complete narrative of Yamato dynastic history from the age of the gods down to Tenmu's own day, integrating the surviving fragments of older records, oral traditions, court genealogies, and ritual chants. The recitation, when finally written down decades later, became the **Kojiki** (Chapter 7). Tenmu also commissioned a parallel historical project, written in classical Chinese for foreign-policy use, which would eventually become the **Nihon Shoki** (Chapter 7). Both works were national histories on the Chinese model — official documents written by court committees under imperial direction — designed to legitimize the Yamato dynasty by tracing it back to the gods themselves. The Kojiki begins with the creation of the world; the Nihon Shoki begins with the same creation. Both then trace the imperial line through gods, demigods, semi-mythical heroes, and finally historically attested rulers, until the line reaches Tenmu's own court. The traditional date of the founding of the imperial line — 660 BCE under the legendary Emperor Jimmu — was almost certainly calculated by the Tenmu-era chroniclers to align with Chinese astrological cycles, projecting an imperial pedigree backward by more than a thousand years to make the Yamato line look at least as old as China's.

He decreed a new legal code, the **Asuka Kiyomihara Code** (AHS-koo-kah kee-yoh-mee-HAH-rah). The code was drafted under Tenmu but promulgated under his successor Empress Jito in 689. It was the first Japanese legal code with the structural division — borrowed from Tang practice — between **ritsu** (criminal law, with prescribed penalties for specific offenses) and **ryo** (administrative regulations, governing the operation of the bureaucracy). This ritsu/ryo split would become the structural template for the great Taiho Code of 701 (Chapter 5) and the Yoro Code of 718 — the operational legal framework of Japan for the next thousand years. No copy of the Asuka Kiyomihara Code survives, but its provisions are reconstructable from later citation.

He elevated state Buddhism while strengthening the **Shinto** (SHIN-toh) cult of the imperial line. Tenmu was an enthusiastic Buddhist patron — he founded the **Yakushi-ji** temple ("Medicine Buddha temple") in 680, vowing to build it if his wife (the future Empress Jito) recovered from illness. She did, the temple was built, and Yakushi-ji became one of the great Asuka-Hakuho-era temples (it was relocated to Nara in 718; only its East Pagoda survives in original form). Simultaneously, Tenmu massively expanded imperial patronage of the **Ise Shrine** (the great Shinto shrine on the eastern coast of Honshu, sacred to the sun goddess **Amaterasu** — ah-mah-teh-RAH-soo — whom the Yamato sovereigns claimed as their divine ancestor). He instituted a regular cycle of imperial offerings at Ise, codified the rituals of imperial succession (which involved, and still involve, ceremonial visits to Amaterasu's shrine), and established the principle that one of the Yamato princesses would be sent to Ise as the **Saio** (sah-EE-oh), a vestal-like figure who served as the high priestess of Amaterasu's cult. The Saio system would continue for over six centuries. Tenmu's religious project was both/and, not either/or: state Buddhism and state Shinto, expanded simultaneously, both subordinated to imperial authority. This is a continuation of the shinbutsu-shugo accommodation that had emerged after Shigisen (Chapter 1), now operationalized at the highest level of state policy.

---

Tenmu died in 686. The succession then passed, after a brief interregnum during which a designated crown prince was forced to commit suicide on plausible-but-trumped-up charges of conspiracy, to Tenmu's widow, **Empress Jito** (jee-TOH).

Jito's reign (formally 690–697) finished her late husband's reform program. She promulgated the Asuka Kiyomihara Code in 689. She maintained the Tenmu-era policy of imperial centralization. She held the dynasty steady until her grandson — the future Emperor Mommu, Tenmu and Jito's grandson by their daughter — was old enough to inherit. And she made the most consequential single decision of the late Asuka period: she moved the capital to a new, permanent, Tang-modeled grid city.

The new capital was called **Fujiwara-kyo** (foo-jee-WAH-rah KYOH), built on the southern edge of the Asuka basin starting around 690 and occupied from 694 onward. It is named after the Fujiwara clan estates the city was built near, not after the Fujiwara as occupants — the clan would not move its main house to the new capital until the Heijo-kyo era (Chapter 5). Fujiwara-kyo was the first Japanese city laid out as a permanent capital with a comprehensive grid plan, on a scale that committed the state to lasting more than a single reign. It covered roughly 4 square miles (10 km²), with the imperial palace at the geometric center (an unusual placement — Tang Chang'an had its palace at the north end), wide north-south avenues, walled residential wards, and two great markets to the east and west. Three monarchs ruled from Fujiwara-kyo — Jito, her grandson Mommu, and Mommu's mother Empress Genmei — over roughly sixteen years.

Fujiwara-kyo did not last. By the early eighth century, the imperial court had decided that the city's design was flawed (the central-palace placement created drainage and traffic problems), that the site was too far from the Yodo River trade arteries, and that a fresh start was needed. In 710, Empress Genmei moved the capital again, this time to **Heijo-kyo** (hay-JOH-kyoh — modern Nara), in a basin a few miles north (Chapter 5). Fujiwara-kyo was abandoned within a year of the move. Today the site is a quiet rice-field landscape with archaeological reconstructions of the palace foundation marked out for visitors.

But Fujiwara-kyo had set the principle. From 694 onward, the Japanese capital would be a permanent planned city, not a relocatable palace, and the design would be based on Tang grid models. This is the moment Japan stopped being a country of itinerant courts and became a country of fixed imperial cities.

---

The Hakuho-era cultural production deserves a brief note before the chapter closes.

The **Hakuho style** of Buddhist sculpture, named after the late-Asuka decades from 645 to 710, shows the absorption of direct Tang and Korean Unified Silla artistic influences. Earlier Asuka sculpture (Tori Busshi's work for Asuka-dera and Horyu-ji, Chapter 2) had been visibly Korean Baekje in style — angular faces, archaic smiles, frontal pose, long thin bodies. Hakuho figures are fuller, with more naturalistic body proportions, gentler facial expressions, and more relaxed postures. The bronze Yakushi Triad at Yakushi-ji, completed in the late seventh century, is the most famous example: three figures (the Medicine Buddha flanked by two attending bodhisattvas) in lifelike poses, with detailed jewelry and flowing robes that suggest fabric weight rather than abstract decoration. The shift parallels what had happened in Tang China between the early and high Tang: a movement away from the abstract spiritual style of the immediately post-Buddhist-arrival centuries toward a more naturalistic aesthetic that would peak in the eighth century. Japan, importing the new style from China within a generation of its development there, was for the first time in artistic synch with the continental mainstream rather than a generation behind.

Hakuho-era scholars and monks also became central to Japanese intellectual life. **Kakinomoto no Hitomaro** (kah-kee-NOH-moh-toh noh hee-toh-MAH-roh, active around 680–710), the greatest Japanese poet of the early period and the central figure of the Manyoshu poetry anthology (Chapter 7), wrote his elegies on dead empresses and his court occasion poems during the late Hakuho. The poet **Yamabe no Akahito** (yah-mah-BEH noh ah-kah-HEE-toh) was a contemporary. The court was now actively producing literature in Japanese (using the awkward Chinese-character-as-phonetic system that would later be called man'yogana, Chapter 7), not just consuming Korean and Chinese imports. The Hakuho is the moment Japanese culture starts producing its own art, in its own language, while still drawing intensively on continental models.

---

By the end of Empress Jito's reign in 697, the Yamato state had come a long way from the Asuka basin clan-federation of a century earlier. It had a centralized bureaucracy on the Tang model (still being elaborated, but functional). It had a permanent grid capital. It had a written legal code (the Asuka Kiyomihara Code), and a more comprehensive code in preparation (the Taiho Code of 701, Chapter 5). It had a state Buddhist establishment with a network of temples scattered across central Honshu. It had a state Shinto establishment with the Ise Shrine as its imperial center. It had two national histories in compilation. It had defensive fortifications across northern Kyushu. It had a strengthened sense, after Baekgang, that its job was to consolidate the islands rather than project power onto the continent.

What it was missing, still, was scale. The capital was small. The bureaucracy was small. The Buddhist establishment was small. The next phase of Asuka-Nara development would be the scaling-up. The Taiho Code of 701 codified the Tang-modeled state at full operational detail. The move to Heijo-kyo in 710 built a city large enough to house a real metropolitan court. And the great Nara-period building program, culminating in the casting of the Daibutsu in 752, would put Japan's state Buddhism on the same monumental scale as anything in Tang China. That story is the next two chapters.

---

Cross-civ check: the Jinshin War of 672 happened in a remarkable global moment. The **Umayyad Caliphate** was just then beginning its rapid eastward and westward expansion — by 711, the Umayyads would conquer Spain, and within Tenmu's lifetime they would push Arab armies as far east as the Hindu Kush (Umayyad Caliphate Ch 1–4). The **Tang dynasty** was midway between Taizong's reforms and Wu Zetian's reign as Empress (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 2), still expanding, still the cultural and military hegemon of East Asia. **Unified Silla** in Korea, having expelled Tang armies from the peninsula in 676, was beginning the golden age that would last for the next two centuries (Ancient Korea Ch 7). The **Byzantine Empire** under the Heraclian dynasty had survived the catastrophic Arab and Persian wars of the early seventh century and was stabilizing on a smaller territorial base centered on Anatolia (Byzantine Empire Ch 6). And in the Americas, **Teotihuacan** had collapsed around 600 (Teotihuacan Ch 8) but its successor cultures were thriving, and the **Maya** Classic Period was at full flower (Maya Civilization Ch 5–6). The world Tenmu and Jito built their reformed state in was a connected one — at least in fits and starts, through the Silk Road, through Korean intermediaries, through the periodic kentoshi missions to Tang. Japan's centralizing reforms were one piece of a larger pattern of state-formation across Eurasia in the seventh and eighth centuries, in which a number of polities were simultaneously consolidating under Tang-, Arab-, or Byzantine-influenced bureaucratic models.

# Chapter 5 — Building the Capital

By the year 700, the Yamato court had been talking about being a Tang-style state for two generations. The Taika Edicts of 646 had laid out the program (Chapter 3). Tenmu and Jito had elaborated and partly implemented it (Chapter 4). The Asuka Kiyomihara Code of 689 had codified the bureaucratic structure. The Fujiwara-kyo capital had built the first permanent grid city. What was still needed was the operational machinery: a comprehensive legal code that would make the system work day-to-day, a coinage that would let the state collect taxes in money rather than only in rice and cloth, and — most consequentially — a capital city large and durable enough to project the image of a real continental empire. The first decade of the eighth century delivered all three.

This chapter covers the short window from 694 to roughly 720 — the Fujiwara-kyo experiment, the great Taiho Code of 701, the first official Japanese coinage in 708, and the founding of Heijo-kyo (Nara) in 710 that gives the next eighty years their name.

---

The **Taiho Code** of 701 (TYE-hoh, named after the Taiho era, "Great Treasure," in which it was promulgated) was the first comprehensive Japanese legal code on the full Tang model. It superseded the Asuka Kiyomihara Code of 689 (Chapter 4) by elaborating it in operational detail across both halves of the ritsu/ryo split — the criminal code (ritsu, eleven volumes prescribing penalties for specific offenses) and the administrative regulations (ryo, eleven volumes governing every aspect of how the bureaucracy was supposed to function).

The drafting committee was led by **Fujiwara no Fuhito** (FOO-hee-toh, 659–720) — Kamatari's son, the second-generation Fujiwara, the architect of Nara-era institutional design. Fuhito had spent his career studying Tang law and Tang governance, and the Taiho Code reflects his depth of understanding. The administrative ryo sections cover the full Tang-modeled bureaucracy: a council of state at the top (the Daijokan, dye-JOH-kahn — Council of State) with the Daijo-daijin (Grand Minister) at its head; eight ministries below it (Personnel, Treasury, Civil Affairs, Imperial Household, Ceremonies, Justice, Military, and Popular Affairs), each with subordinate bureaus; a parallel Department of Worship (the Jingikan, jin-GEE-kahn) responsible for state Shinto rites; an investigation-and-censorship bureau; a national university; and a province-district-township structure extending the central bureaucracy out to the rural population.

The Taiho Code's land system was the **handen-shuju** (hahn-DEN SHOO-joo, "field allocation and revocation") system, adapted from the Tang equal-field system (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 2). Every six years, the population would be re-registered and farmland would be allocated: each adult male received about 2 tan (roughly 0.6 acres) of irrigated rice paddy, each adult female received 2/3 of that, and slaves received fractional allotments depending on category. In return, each adult male owed a fixed grain tax (the **so**, SOH — typically about 3% of the rice harvest from his allocated land), a tax in textiles or local products (the **cho**, CHOH), labor service (the **yo**, YOH — typically ten days per year, often commuted to extra cloth payments), and military service in a part-time conscript army (the **gundan** system). Land reverted to the state on the holder's death and was reallocated. The system kept the peasantry tied to the land, kept the tax base broad, and (in theory) kept the army supplied with men.

The Taiho Code is the document that turned Japan into what historians call a **ritsuryo state** (rits-RYOH, "codes-and-regulations state") — a polity organized around a comprehensive written legal code on the Tang model, with a centralized bureaucracy, a registered taxpaying population, and an explicit hierarchy of court ranks. Every later piece of pre-modern Japanese law — the Yoro Code of 718 (a polished revision of Taiho, formally promulgated in 757), the various supplementary regulations issued through the Heian period, even fragments cited in court rulings as late as the Edo period — derived from the structural framework set down in 701.

In practice, the ritsuryo system never worked as fully as its drafters had envisioned. The handen-shuju land registration was supposed to be redone every six years; in reality, the great bureaucratic effort required to do it right meant that re-registrations slipped to once a decade, then once a generation, and by the late Heian era had effectively stopped. The aristocratic estates and the Buddhist temple landholdings quietly carved off chunks of the registered tax base into legal exemptions called **shoen** (SHOH-en — manorial estates outside the regular tax system), which by the Heian period would consume most of Japan's productive farmland (Heian Japan Ch 2). The conscript army system burned out within fifty years against the Emishi frontier (Chapter 8) and was replaced by warrior aristocracies that the central court could not control. The Taiho Code was a Tang-on-paper system that Japan's actual social structure could not fully sustain. But the framework remained the official law of the land for over a thousand years, and the language and concepts of ritsuryo governance shaped Japanese political thought down to the Meiji Restoration.

---

The other major operational reform of the early eighth century was the introduction of official coinage.

In 708, copper deposits were discovered in **Musashi Province** (modern Saitama Prefecture, near Tokyo), and the court immediately struck Japan's first official coins: the **Wado Kaichin** (wah-DOH KYE-chin), modeled directly on the Tang **Kaiyuan Tongbao** copper cash. The coins were circular with a square hole in the center (so they could be strung on cords for transport — the standard East Asian coinage form for the next twelve centuries), inscribed with four characters reading "Wado Kaichin" ("Inaugural Treasure of the Wado [Soft Copper] Era"). Twelve more official coin issues would follow over the next two and a half centuries, mostly minted in Yamashiro and Musashi.

The coinage program was, in part, a propaganda exercise: a respectable state in the Tang model needed to issue its own currency, and Japan now issued one. It was also a serious attempt to monetize the rural economy and ease tax collection. The state pushed hard on the program — it required certain ranks of officials to hold portions of their salary in coin, attempted to require coin payments for certain transactions, and offered tax discounts for taxpayers who paid in coin instead of in kind. None of this worked very well outside the capital. Rural Japan stuck with rice-and-cloth payments for centuries. By the late ninth century, the state had effectively stopped minting coins, and Japan would not have a functioning monetary economy again until imported Chinese cash flooded in during the Heian period (Heian Japan Ch 2). The Wado Kaichin program is one of the clearer examples of a Tang-modeled reform that Japanese society was not yet ready to absorb at scale. It worked for the bureaucracy in Heijo-kyo. It did not work for the village economy.

---

The big political fact of the early eighth century was the move to **Heijo-kyo** (hay-JOH-kyoh) in 710. This was the city later known as **Nara** (NAH-rah), and it gives the next eighty years of Japanese history their name: the **Nara period**.

The decision to move was made by **Empress Genmei** (gen-MAY), Mommu's mother, who had taken the throne in 707 after her son's early death. Fujiwara-kyo had been functioning as the capital for sixteen years but had drainage problems, traffic problems, and (the chroniclers noted) a series of inauspicious portents. The court accepted the proposal of an imperial commission that a fresh start was needed at a new site a few miles north of Asuka, in a flat basin at the southern edge of the Yamato plain. Construction of the new capital began in 708 and was advanced enough by spring 710 to allow the imperial court to relocate. The remaining government offices and aristocratic families followed over the next year.

Heijo-kyo was Japan's first major capital built at the scale of a Tang Chinese imperial city. It covered roughly 9 square miles (24 km²), with a north-south axis running about 2.9 miles (4.7 km) and an east-west axis of about 2.7 miles (4.3 km). The layout was a careful copy of Tang **Chang'an** — at the time the largest city in the world (population around 1 million), and explicitly the model the Japanese planners were following. The imperial palace sat at the north end of the city (correctly placed this time, unlike at Fujiwara-kyo). A great central avenue, the **Suzaku Oji** ("Red Phoenix Avenue") — about 280 feet wide, running directly south from the palace gate — bisected the city. Walled residential wards lined either side of Suzaku Oji, organized in a grid pattern of major streets. Two enormous markets, the East Market and the West Market, were located in the southern half of the city. The whole thing was enclosed (in plan, though never fully in earthworks) by a roughly rectangular boundary, with a great southern gate (the **Rajomon**) facing south.

There was one significant difference from Chang'an. Chang'an was a fully walled city, with massive earth ramparts protecting it against the steppe armies that periodically threatened China's northern frontier. Heijo-kyo was not walled. Japan's island geography meant the city did not face the threat of mounted nomad invasion — no Japanese city would be walled until the castle-building era of the late medieval period nearly nine hundred years later. The Nara court could afford to build for prestige and beauty rather than defense. The central avenue, the orderly grid, the great markets, the temples scattered across the city, the imperial palace at the head — all were architectural statements about the kind of state Japan now intended to be: not a fortress but a civilization.

At its peak (around 740), Heijo-kyo housed perhaps 100,000 to 200,000 residents. By Tang Chang'an standards, this was small — Chang'an had perhaps ten times the population. By the standards of any other city in eighth-century East Asia outside China itself, Nara was substantial. By the standards of the previous Yamato capitals (Asuka, with its low-thousands court population; Fujiwara-kyo, with maybe 30,000), it was an order of magnitude larger than anything Japan had ever built before.

The aristocratic and government population was also vastly larger. The Taiho Code's bureaucracy required thousands of officials — drafters, archivists, treasurers, censors, ritualists, military planners, judges, provincial supervisors, and the clerks who kept the registers and the tax records flowing. Nara housed all of them. The court itself, with all its concubines and nurses and household staff and attendants, was a small city by itself. The Buddhist establishment grew correspondingly: the great temples that would dominate Nara's southern and eastern districts (Kofuku-ji, Yakushi-ji after relocation, Todai-ji built later, Saidai-ji) all had monastic populations in the hundreds, plus their lay supporters and craftspeople.

---

A crucial early move at Heijo-kyo was **Fujiwara no Fuhito's** decision to relocate the Fujiwara clan temple to the new capital and rebuild it as **Kofuku-ji** (KOH-foo-koo-JEE — "Temple of Flourishing Fortune"). The Fujiwara had a small clan temple in Asuka called Yamashina-dera; Fuhito moved its institutional identity, its monks, and its religious functions to a new and much larger temple compound on prime land in the eastern half of Heijo-kyo, dedicated in 710. Kofuku-ji would become the religious headquarters of the Fujiwara clan and one of the great power centers of the next several centuries — its monk-soldiers (Heian Japan Ch 4) would be a major political force from the late Heian period onward. The placement of Kofuku-ji in the new capital was a Fujiwara claim on the religious life of the new city, a counterweight to the imperial monasteries the throne would soon be founding.

Yakushi-ji (Tenmu's vow temple, Chapter 4) was also relocated to Heijo-kyo in 718, rebuilt on a new site west of the imperial palace. Its East Pagoda from this rebuilding still stands today — a three-story pagoda that, because of an architectural device of three small pent roofs called **mokoshi** between the main roof levels, is sometimes mistakenly described as a six-story pagoda. It is one of the most beautiful pagodas in Japan and one of only a handful of Nara-era structures to have survived continuously in its original form.

A number of other temples were founded or relocated to Nara in the first two decades of the new capital, gradually building up the institutional Buddhist apparatus that would be elaborated in the great Shomu-era projects of the 740s and 750s (Chapter 6).

---

Empress Genmei reigned until 715, when she abdicated in favor of her unmarried daughter, who took the throne as **Empress Gensho** (gen-SHOH). This is the only consecutive mother-to-daughter imperial succession in Japanese history. Gensho reigned until 724, when she abdicated in favor of her nephew (Genmei's grandson), who took the throne as **Emperor Shomu**. The Genmei-Gensho-Shomu sequence is a single political continuity, all of them working under the umbrella of Fujiwara no Fuhito's reforms and (after Fuhito's death in 720) under the political guidance of Fuhito's four sons.

Shomu's reign would dominate the central decades of the Nara period, and his program would culminate in the casting of the Daibutsu — the next chapter.

But before turning to Shomu, one more item from the Genmei era deserves mention: in 712, Empress Genmei accepted the long-promised completion of Hieda no Are's recitation, in the form of a written manuscript prepared by the court attendant **O no Yasumaro** (OH noh yah-soo-MAH-roh). This was the **Kojiki** (koh-JEE-kee, "Record of Ancient Matters") — Japan's oldest surviving written work and the text that would eventually be elevated, by nineteenth- and twentieth-century nationalist scholars, into the founding scripture of Japanese identity. Eight years later, in 720, the parallel state-history project would also be completed: the **Nihon Shoki** (nee-HON SHOH-kee, "Chronicles of Japan"), in classical Chinese, written for foreign-policy use. Both works are central to the literary history of the Nara period, and both deserve a chapter of their own; we will come back to them in Chapter 7.

---

The early Nara court was also when state-sponsored ethnographic and geographical compilation got under way. In 713, Empress Genmei issued the **Fudoki edict** ordering every province to compile a local **Fudoki** (FOO-doh-kee — "records of wind and earth") documenting the geography, climate, agricultural products, place-name etymologies, and folk legends of the area. The orders went out to all sixty-odd provinces; only five provincial Fudoki survive intact today (Izumo, Hitachi, Harima, Hizen, and Bungo), with fragments from a number of others. The Fudoki are an irreplaceable source for early Japanese rural culture, preserving traditions that would otherwise have vanished into the imperial cult — local creation myths, lists of indigenous deities not absorbed into the Yamato pantheon, agricultural rituals, etymological speculations about why this hill is called what it's called and that river is named for what god.

The Fudoki edict was a small bureaucratic moment — a single court directive to provincial governors. But it shows the eighth-century court trying to systematize knowledge of its own territory in a Tang-bureaucratic way. The Tang state had its own gazetteer tradition (the Tang Liudian, a comprehensive encyclopedic compilation of administrative geography). Japan was now compiling its own version. The country was being mapped, registered, and inventoried in the categories of the new ritsuryo state.

---

Cross-civ check: the founding of Heijo-kyo in 710 was contemporary with one of the most consequential single decades in Eurasian history. **Umayyad** Arab armies had reached the Indus River and conquered al-Andalus (modern Spain) by 711 — within a year of Empress Genmei's capital move (Umayyad Caliphate Ch 4). The **Battle of Tours** in 732 (about a generation later) would mark the Umayyad expansion's western limit at the Pyrenees. In **Tang China**, Emperor Xuanzong had just taken the throne in 712 and was beginning the High Tang reign that would peak in the great cultural moment of Li Bai and Du Fu (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 3). The **Tang Chang'an** that Heijo-kyo was modeling itself on was at the height of its cosmopolitan power: a million-person multi-ethnic capital where Sogdian merchants, Persian refugees from the destroyed Sassanid Empire, Indian Buddhist monks, Korean students, and now Japanese kentoshi embassies all moved through the same wards. **Unified Silla** in Korea was at the start of its golden age (Ancient Korea Ch 7). The **Byzantine Empire** under the Isaurian dynasty was about to enter the Iconoclasm crisis (Byzantine Empire Ch 7). The early eighth century is one of those moments when several civilizations were simultaneously building on the institutional models inherited from a previous generation — Tang on Sui, Umayyad on early-Caliphate, Japan on its own Asuka-era reforms — and producing the operational metropolitan capitals that would define the high medieval period.

# Chapter 6 — Nara Buddhism and the Great Buddha

The dominant figure of the central Nara period is **Emperor Shomu** (SHOH-moo, reigned 724–749), and his obsession was Buddhism.

Shomu was the great-grandson of Tenmu and Jito, the grandson of Empress Genmei, the nephew and heir of Empress Gensho. Through his maternal grandfather Fujiwara no Fuhito, he was also the first emperor in whose veins ran a substantial dose of Fujiwara blood — his mother had been Fuhito's daughter Miyako, and his empress consort would also be a Fuhito daughter (his maternal aunt by half-blood, technically), the formidable **Empress Komyo**. The intermarriage of imperial and Fujiwara lines was the deliberate Fujiwara strategy that would dominate Japanese politics for centuries (Chapter 3 noted Fuhito as the political architect; Heian Japan Ch 1 picks up the strategy at maturity), and Shomu's reign is the moment the strategy first paid off at full strength.

But Shomu's defining personal characteristic was not his Fujiwara connections. It was his religious intensity. He was a fervent Buddhist, increasingly convinced over the course of his reign that the spiritual power of organized Buddhism could protect Japan from the disasters that kept hitting it — and there were many disasters. His twenty-five-year reign saw plague (the catastrophic 735–737 smallpox epidemic), famine, drought, an attempted Fujiwara coup that ended in 740 with the murder of one rebel and the suicide of three others, and the political collapse of the great Fujiwara generation his uncle Fuhito had led. Shomu's response to each disaster was to invest more deeply in state Buddhism. By the end of his reign, he had committed an enormous fraction of Japan's national resources to a Buddhist building program of unprecedented scale, culminating in the Daibutsu of Todai-ji.

---

The crisis that reshaped Shomu's reign was the **735–737 smallpox epidemic**. The disease had spread along the Eurasian trade networks for centuries; it appears to have entered Japan through the Korean trade in 735, from a Tang ship that reached Dazaifu in northern Kyushu carrying infected sailors. From there it moved up through western Honshu, reached the capital Heijo-kyo by early 737, and ravaged the densely populated central provinces through the summer.

The mortality was catastrophic. Modern epidemiological estimates put the death toll at roughly one-third of the Japanese population — somewhere between 1.5 and 2.5 million people, in a country of about 5 million. Whole communities lost half their adults. Tax registers from the period show massive defaults — large numbers of registered taxpayers simply gone. The court itself was decimated: in the spring of 737, all four Fujiwara brothers — sons of Fuhito and the dominant figures in court politics for two decades — died of smallpox within months of each other. Their deaths broke the senior Fujiwara line's grip on policy and triggered a generation of factional instability that would not really stabilize until the late eighth century.

Shomu's response to the catastrophe was not retreat but expansion of the state-Buddhist program. He had already been a heavy temple patron. After 737, he became something more aggressive: a sovereign convinced that only a national network of Buddhist prayer could save Japan from further calamities. In 741, four years after the worst of the epidemic, he issued the **Provincial Temple Edict**: every province in Japan was to build a **kokubunji** (KOH-koo-boon-JEE — "provincial monastery"), with a seven-story pagoda; and a parallel **kokubunniji** (KOH-koo-boon-NEE-JEE — "provincial nunnery"), housing the female counterpart Buddhist establishment. Together the kokubunji network was meant to garrison the whole country with prayer — a national infrastructure project on a religious basis, coordinated from the central court but funded substantially by provincial revenues.

The kokubunji program was a real burden on provincial finances. It was also a massive employment program for craftsmen, sculptors, painters, carpenters, bronze-casters, and the bureaucratic clerks who supervised the construction. Across the provinces, the project transformed the countryside. By the late eighth century, every Japanese province had a Buddhist cathedral on the Tang temple model, with a pagoda visible from miles away, a great Buddha image, a community of resident monks, and a dedicated tax-exempt land allotment to support the operation. Many of these provincial temples have either survived in some form (sometimes only a foundation outline, sometimes a partly reconstructed building) or are mentioned as ruins in medieval sources. The kokubunji network is one of the most visible Nara-era impositions on the landscape of pre-modern Japan.

---

The crown jewel of Shomu's program was at Heijo-kyo itself: **Todai-ji** (toh-DYE-jee — "Great Eastern Temple"), founded in 743, and its centerpiece, the **Daibutsu** (dye-BOO-tsoo — "Great Buddha").

The Daibutsu was a colossal bronze seated Buddha statue, approximately 49 feet (15 meters) tall, depicting the cosmic Buddha **Vairocana** (vy-roh-CHA-nah — the central deity of the **Kegon school** of Buddhism, the meditation sutra-based tradition that Shomu favored above the other schools). The statue was cast in eight sequential stages over multiple years, using copper, tin, mercury, and gold drawn from across the country. The bronze itself weighed over 500 tons. The gilding was applied as a mercury amalgam — gold dissolved in liquid mercury, painted onto the surface, then heated to drive off the mercury and leave the pure gold bonded to the bronze. The mercury fumes from the gilding process are thought to have killed or seriously poisoned a significant number of the workmen — there is no precise contemporary tally, but the casting process was understood at the time to be hazardous, and the chronicle accounts of post-casting mortality among the bronzeworkers are not seriously disputed.

The casting consumed most of Japan's known copper reserves and a large portion of its known gold. New gold deposits discovered in Mutsu (the northeastern frontier province) in 749 — by Korean Baekje refugee descendants who had settled the region after the seventh-century refugee waves — were specifically dedicated to the Daibutsu project; their discovery is recorded in the chronicles as a divine sign that the Buddha approved the work. The state issued offerings asking aristocratic families to contribute personal jewelry, household bronze, and other metallic possessions to the casting; the contributions came in. The Daibutsu casting was, in resource terms, the largest single material project the Yamato state had ever undertaken.

The official figure given by the chronicles for the labor force is 2.6 million workers — almost certainly a stylized number rather than a precise count (the figure is suspiciously close to half the country's population), but indicative of the scale at which the project was being remembered. Whatever the actual labor numbers, the casting drew on conscript labor, hereditary craftsman lineages (many of Korean immigrant descent), corvée tax obligations, and voluntary religious donations.

The dedication ceremony in **752** is one of the great set-piece moments in Japanese cultural history. It was held in the open-air pavilion at Todai-ji while the Daibutsuden ("Great Buddha Hall") was still under construction around the statue. Over 10,000 monks attended. Musicians, dancers, and ritual specialists came from Tang China, from Korea, from India, and from Central Asia — making the event the most cosmopolitan religious gathering in Japanese history to that point. The ceremonial "eye-opening" of the statue (the painting of pupils onto the carved eye-sockets, ritually animating the figure) was performed by an Indian monk named **Bodhisena** (boh-dih-SEE-nah), who had traveled from India via Vietnam and China to attend, brushing the giant pupils onto the bronze face with a brush whose handle was attached to long silken cords; the cords ran out into the audience, and dozens of the assembled dignitaries (including the retired Empress Gensho and the now-retired Shomu himself) held the cords as Bodhisena did the actual brushwork — sharing in the merit of the eye-opening.

The Daibutsu sat inside the **Daibutsuden** ("Great Buddha Hall"), at the time among the largest wooden buildings in the world by floor area. The original Daibutsuden was approximately 280 feet wide and 165 feet deep — wider and possibly taller than the current rebuilt version, which is itself among the largest traditional wooden structures still standing. The original building was destroyed in fires in 1180 (during the Genpei War, Heian Japan Ch 6) and again in 1567 (during the late medieval Sengoku-era civil wars); the current Daibutsuden is an early-eighteenth-century reconstruction that is only about two-thirds the width of the Nara-era original. The Daibutsu itself was also damaged in those fires and repaired multiple times — the head of the current statue dates to the seventeenth century, though much of the body and the lotus-petal pedestal preserve original eighth-century bronze. The Vairocana visible in Nara today is a partial Heian-, Kamakura-, and Edo-era reconstruction wrapped around a Nara-era core.

---

After Shomu died in 756, his widow **Empress Komyo** (KOH-myoh) made one of the most generous and culturally consequential donations in world history. She gave Shomu's entire personal collection — over 9,000 objects, including textiles, musical instruments, glasswork, calligraphy, paintings, ritual implements, weapons, mirrors, masks, and writing brushes — to Todai-ji. The collection was housed in a raised-floor log-cabin storehouse called the **Shoso-in** (SHOH-soh-in), built in the **azekura** (ah-zeh-KOO-rah) style — interlocking triangular cypress logs that flex and breathe with seasonal humidity changes, providing exactly the right preservation environment.

The Shoso-in collection has survived intact for over 1,250 years, in extraordinary condition. It is the most important single time capsule of the eighth-century world. Its objects came from across Eurasia: Persian glass, Chinese Tang Dynasty mirrors, Central Asian textiles, Indian-influenced musical instruments (including a five-stringed lute called a biwa, the only surviving example of its type from the Tang era), Korean metalwork, and Japanese lacquerware. The presence of these foreign objects in a Japanese temple storehouse demonstrates concretely what the historical record tells us in summary: that Heijo-kyo, sitting at the far eastern end of the Silk Road, was connected to a trade network stretching from the Mediterranean to the Pacific. Some of the objects in the Shoso-in had been gifts to Shomu from Tang Chinese embassies; others were luxuries acquired through merchants traveling the kentoshi routes; still others were Japanese-made imitations of Tang and Korean originals.

The collection is opened to the public for one month each autumn, in a carefully staged annual exhibition at the Nara National Museum. A different selection of objects is shown each year. Visitors who attend the Shoso-in exhibition see things — eighth-century Persian rhinoceros-horn cups, eighth-century Chinese-language documents written by Korean scribes, eighth-century lutes still strung with their original silk strings — that have not significantly aged in 1,250 years. The Shoso-in is, by any measure, one of the most remarkable surviving early-medieval collections anywhere on Earth.

---

The institutional structure of Nara Buddhism deserves a brief explanation because it is unusual.

By the mid-eighth century, the Nara temple complex had organized itself into the **Six Schools of Nara Buddhism** (the **Nanto Rokushu**, NAHN-toh roh-KOO-shoo): six distinct scholarly traditions, each with its own canon of sutras, its own training program, its own residential monks, and its own home temple. The schools were:

- **Hosso** (HOHS-soh) — the "Consciousness-Only" school based on the philosophy of the Indian masters Asanga and Vasubandhu, headquartered at Kofuku-ji and Yakushi-ji.
- **Kusha** (KOO-shah) — the school based on the Abhidharma (Buddhist scholastic) literature, primarily a sub-school of Hosso.
- **Sanron** (SAHN-rohn) — the "Three Treatises" school, based on the Madhyamaka philosophy of the Indian master Nagarjuna.
- **Jojitsu** (JOH-jeet-soo) — based on the *Satyasiddhi Shastra*, primarily a sub-school of Sanron.
- **Kegon** (KEH-gohn) — based on the *Avatamsaka Sutra*, the cosmic-Buddha tradition that produced the Vairocana focus of Todai-ji.
- **Ritsu** (RIT-soo) — the discipline-based school, focused on the precise rules of Buddhist monastic ordination, headquartered at Toshodai-ji.

These were not popular sects competing for lay followers in the way that later Japanese Buddhism would (the Pure Land and Nichiren sects of the Heian and Kamakura periods, see Heian Japan Ch 2). They were scholarly traditions, each one essentially an academic department within the larger Nara temple complex. A serious Nara monk would study at multiple temples and engage with multiple schools rather than belonging exclusively to one. The Six Schools specialized the intellectual labor of Buddhist analysis, with each school maintaining the textual lineage and exegetical tradition for a different segment of the Mahayana canon.

This is one reason Nara Buddhism functioned as state Buddhism. The organization was not designed to evangelize the rural population (which mostly continued to practice a syncretic mix of kami worship, ancestor rites, and folk Buddhism). It was designed to maintain a sophisticated scholarly Buddhist establishment in the capital, capable of staffing the kokubunji network, conducting state rituals, advising the emperor, and producing the prayers that were supposed to protect the country from disasters. Nara Buddhism was, fundamentally, an academic-and-bureaucratic institution rather than a missionary one.

The exception that proves the rule is **Gyoki** (GYOH-kee, 668–749), a charismatic monk who defied the official restrictions on independent Buddhist activity and went out into the countryside to preach Buddhism to commoners, organize voluntary labor for public works projects (bridges, irrigation channels, harbors), and run charitable services for the destitute. The state initially persecuted him — Gyoki was, by Taiho Code definitions, an unauthorized religious teacher operating outside the licensed system. But he proved enormously popular and had assembled a following so large that the court could not safely suppress him. By the 740s, Emperor Shomu had reversed the relationship: he formally honored Gyoki, made him Japan's first **Daisojo** ("Grand Priest"), and recruited him to coordinate fundraising and labor for the Todai-ji Daibutsu project. Gyoki's popular religious networks proved essential to the project's success. He died in 749, three years before the Daibutsu dedication, but his organizational work made the casting possible.

---

The most consequential foreign Buddhist arrival of the entire Nara period was **Ganjin** (GAHN-jin) — the Tang Chinese name was **Jianzhen** (jyen-JEN, 688–763), a senior monk of the Vinaya (monastic discipline) tradition, recruited by Japanese embassies to come to Japan and properly transmit the Buddhist ordination lineage that the Japanese clergy had been doing without.

The story is one of the great narratives of Buddhist missionary history. Ganjin was approached by Japanese envoys around 742 and agreed to come. He attempted the voyage from Tang China to Japan five times over the next eleven years. The first attempt was sabotaged by a jealous disciple. The second was wrecked by a storm. The third hit pirates. The fourth was blown so far off course that Ganjin's party landed on Hainan Island in the South China Sea, several thousand miles away from their destination. The fifth was abandoned partway. By the time of the sixth and successful voyage in 753, Ganjin was sixty-five years old and had gone completely blind from exposure during one of the failed attempts. He arrived in Kyushu in late 753 and was carried to Heijo-kyo in early 754.

In Nara, Ganjin formally ordained the leading Japanese clergy in the proper Buddhist ordination lineage. (Until his arrival, Japanese ordinations had been performed by the local clergy without the formal transmission line back to the Buddha that the Vinaya tradition required. Strictly speaking, no Japanese monk had been a fully proper Buddhist monk before Ganjin formally ordained them.) He founded **Toshodai-ji** (toh-SHOH-dye-jee — "Temple of the Tang Dynasty Founder") as the new headquarters of the Ritsu school and the institutional home of proper ordination. He spent his last decade in Japan teaching, writing, and passing on his disciplinary tradition. He died at Toshodai-ji in 763.

The Toshodai-ji main hall (the **Kondo**) is the finest surviving example of Tang-influenced architecture in Japan. Built in the late eighth century, after Ganjin's death, it shows the deep eaves, the wide proportions, the simple bracketing, and the unpainted natural-wood finish characteristic of mid-Tang temple construction in China. (Almost no original Tang wooden buildings survive in China; the few that do are in remote western provinces. To see a real Tang temple, you go to Toshodai-ji in Nara.) A lacquer portrait sculpture of Ganjin himself, made by his disciples in his lifetime or shortly after his death, is preserved at Toshodai-ji and is shown to the public for a few days each year. It is one of the most striking pieces of early Japanese sculpture: a portrait of a blind, elderly, deeply weathered monk who had survived five shipwrecks and crossed the East China Sea to bring Buddhism to a country he could not see.

---

By the 760s, the Nara state-Buddhist program had reached an apex from which only a few directions of motion were possible. The Daibutsu had been built. The kokubunji network was established. The Six Schools were institutionalized. The Shoso-in had been founded. The Manyoshu was being compiled (Chapter 7). And the Buddhist establishment was, by sheer institutional weight, becoming the most powerful non-imperial force in Japan — with implications for the throne that will be explored in the next chapter.

---

Cross-civ check: the Daibutsu dedication of 752 happened in a remarkable synchronous moment of religious-political monumentalism across Eurasia. The **Umayyad Mosque of Damascus** had been completed in 715 — a massive imperial mosque sponsored by the Umayyad caliphs (Umayyad Caliphate Ch 5). The **Abbey of Saint-Denis** north of Paris, the burial church of the Frankish kings, was being built and rebuilt across the eighth century. In **Tang China**, Emperor Xuanzong had spent the 730s and 740s expanding the imperial palaces at Chang'an and Luoyang on the largest scale of the entire Tang dynasty (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 3). The **Borobudur** monumental Buddhist temple in Java (one of the largest Buddhist monuments ever built, see Srivijaya Ch 1) would begin construction within a generation after the Daibutsu was cast. The **Great Mosque of Samarra** in Iraq (the largest mosque ever built, Islamic Golden Age Ch 3) would be built by the Abbasids in the next century. Every major civilization of the Eurasian middle was investing enormous resources in monumental religious architecture in the same period. The Daibutsu is Japan's contribution to this Eurasian moment of imperial religion-as-stone, and it stands well in the company.

A particular comparison is striking: the Daibutsu was dedicated in 752, two years after the **Abbasid Revolution** of 750 that overthrew the Umayyad Caliphate and established the Abbasid dynasty in Baghdad (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). The same year the world's largest Buddhist statue was being unveiled in Nara, a new Islamic dynasty was being founded that would shortly embark on its own great urban project — the building of Baghdad in 762 as the new caliphal capital, on a Tang-influenced grid plan eerily similar to Heijo-kyo's. The two great planned capitals of the late eighth century — Heijo-kyo in Japan and Baghdad in Iraq — were being built within a decade of each other on opposite ends of Eurasia, both consciously borrowing the Chang'an model. And one year before the Daibutsu was dedicated, in 751, a Tang army was defeated by Arab forces at the **Battle of Talas** in Central Asia — a battle whose practical effect was to fix the boundary between the Tang and Abbasid spheres of influence, and whose cultural effect was to transmit Chinese papermaking technology to the Arab world. The world that the Daibutsu sat at the center of was a connected one, in which institutional templates and technologies and religious practices were diffusing rapidly across vast distances.

# Chapter 7 — Literature, Law, and the Dokyo Affair

The Nara period's most enduring legacy is not its temples or its bronze. It is its books.

In a single seventy-year window — roughly 700 to 770 — Japan produced its first surviving written history, its first comprehensive legal code, its first poetry anthology, its first national geography, and its first attempts at vernacular Japanese prose. The volume of writing the Nara court generated, after centuries of Japanese illiteracy, is one of the most striking transitions in the literary history of any premodern civilization. By the end of the Nara period, Japan had a written canon. Almost everything Japan had not written down before was now being written down, in a sustained burst of bureaucratic-and-literary energy that would not be equaled again until the Heian period.

This chapter covers the Nara textual moment — the great chronicles of 712 and 720, the Manyoshu poetry anthology, the Yoro Code of 718 — and then turns to the most peculiar episode of the late Nara period: the Dokyo Affair, in which a Buddhist monk nearly seized the imperial throne through his influence over a reigning empress.

---

The two great Nara chronicles are the **Kojiki** of 712 and the **Nihon Shoki** of 720.

The **Kojiki** ("Record of Ancient Matters") was completed by **O no Yasumaro** (OH noh yah-soo-MAH-roh) in 712, on the basis of recitations from the court reciter Hieda no Are that had been commissioned by Emperor Tenmu decades earlier (Chapter 4). It is Japan's oldest surviving written work. It is also, despite being shorter and less polished than the parallel Nihon Shoki, the more spiritually loaded of the two Nara histories, the one that nineteenth- and twentieth-century nationalist scholars elevated into the founding scripture of Japanese identity.

The Kojiki opens with the creation of the world: the formless cosmic chaos, the emergence of the first divine couple **Izanagi** (ee-zah-NAH-gee) and **Izanami** (ee-zah-NAH-mee), the divine couple's creation of the Japanese islands by churning the ocean with a heavenly spear, the birth of the first generation of kami including the sun goddess **Amaterasu**, the great cosmological cycle of Amaterasu's withdrawal into a cave (a partial solar eclipse interpreted mythologically), the descent of Amaterasu's grandson Ninigi to Earth carrying the three sacred imperial regalia (the mirror, the sword, and the curved jewel), and the founding of the imperial line by Ninigi's great-grandson Emperor Jimmu, traditionally dated to 660 BCE. The narrative then continues with a semi-mythological chronicle of the early emperors, blending genealogy, legend, ritual, and poetry, before reaching more historically attested figures and ending around the death of Empress Suiko in 628.

The Nihon Shoki ("Chronicles of Japan") was completed eight years later, in 720, by a committee that included **Prince Toneri** (Tenmu's son, the senior imperial figure on the project) and the same O no Yasumaro who had finished the Kojiki. It is longer (thirty volumes versus the Kojiki's three), more comprehensively documented, written in classical Chinese (whereas the Kojiki uses a hybrid system in which Chinese characters were sometimes used for their meanings, sometimes for their phonetic values to represent Japanese syllables — see the discussion of man'yogana later in this chapter), and includes alternative versions of many myths, with the text noting "another tradition says..." or "the records of such-and-such province say..." It carries the historical narrative through 697 CE, ending with the abdication of Empress Jito to her grandson Mommu.

Why were these works compiled, and why two of them so close together?

The political motivation is reasonably clear. The Yamato court needed a written foundation myth to legitimize its rule. Every civilized state in East Asia — China, Korea, even the smaller polities along the steppe frontier — had official histories tracing the ruling dynasty back to divine or cosmic origins. Japan, which had only recently acquired writing, had none. The Kojiki and Nihon Shoki filled the gap. They established that the emperor descended from Amaterasu, that the imperial line was unbroken since the age of the gods, that the calendrical-traditional founding date of 660 BCE was older than any rival East Asian state's foundational date, and that Japan's political order was ordained by heaven. These claims were not neutral historical observations — they were state propaganda, composed by court officials under imperial patronage to serve imperial interests.

This is not a modern cynical reading. It is exactly what the texts present themselves as. The Nihon Shoki opens with explicit acknowledgment that it was commissioned to be a state history. The Kojiki preface by O no Yasumaro discusses, with some pride, the political motivation: a written record of the ages was needed to "rectify the falsehoods" that were circulating in oral tradition and to give the imperial line a definitive lineage. The chroniclers were not pretending to be neutral. They were doing a state-sponsored job of legitimization.

The two-version production reflects different audiences. The Kojiki was for internal Japanese consumption — written in the hybrid script, focused on mythological detail and ritual genealogy, composed in a style that was sometimes archaic and rhythmic. The Nihon Shoki was for external (especially Chinese and Korean) consumption — written in classical Chinese, presented as a proper East Asian dynastic history, more conscious of comparative chronology, attempting to demonstrate that Japan was a respectable civilized state with a documented past. Both versions were doing political work, but for different audiences.

The texts preserve enormous amounts of cultural information that would otherwise have been lost. Mythological cycles, regional traditions, royal genealogies, ceremonial practices, place-name etymologies, fragments of pre-Buddhist religious thought — all of it preserved, even if filtered through the eighth-century court's editorial purposes. They are propaganda and treasure at the same time, just like every other ancient national origin text from the Hebrew Bible to Virgil's Aeneid. Modern Japanese historiography spends a great deal of effort trying to disentangle the historical from the mythological in the early sections, with mixed results — most scholars accept the chronicle accounts of late-sixth-century and seventh-century events as substantially reliable, treat the early-emperors sections as legendary, and dispute everything in between.

---

The Nara period's other great literary achievement was the **Manyoshu** (MAHN-yoh-shoo, "Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves"), Japan's oldest poetry anthology, compiled around 759.

The Manyoshu contains 4,516 poems by over 500 named authors plus many anonymous compositions, organized into twenty volumes. The poems range across the entire seventh and early-eighth centuries, with the bulk of the material from the late seventh and early eighth centuries — making the Manyoshu a generation-by-generation snapshot of the Asuka and Nara court's literary production.

What makes the Manyoshu unusual in the world's anthologies of this period is its social range. Most ancient and early-medieval poetry collections preserve aristocratic or scholarly poetry. The Manyoshu does that — emperors and high nobles are well-represented — but it also includes poems by frontier guards posted to the eastern Emishi border, by anonymous farmers and fishermen, by roadside laborers, by women separated from their husbands, by mothers grieving for dead children, and by men whose names did not survive but whose verses did. No other surviving poetry collection of comparable size, anywhere in the eighth-century world, includes commoner poets alongside emperors. The Manyoshu's anonymous frontier-guard poems (the **sakimori-uta**) are among the most affecting compositions in the collection: short laments by men leaving home for years of guard duty in distant Kyushu, addressed to the wives and parents and children they were leaving behind.

The greatest poet of the Manyoshu is **Kakinomoto no Hitomaro** (kah-kee-NOH-moh-toh noh hee-toh-MAH-roh), a court poet active around 680–710 (Chapter 4 introduced him as a Hakuho-era figure). Hitomaro composed elegies for dead empresses, court occasion poems for imperial banquets, travel poems written on the road between Asuka and the various provinces he visited on official duty, and personal lyrics about love, separation, and grief. His formal sophistication and emotional intensity have caused him to be recognized for thirteen centuries as one of the great poets in the Japanese tradition. A famous Hitomaro poem on the death of his wife — addressed to her ashes after her cremation, lamenting that he can no longer find her form in the smoke rising from the funeral pyre — is one of the foundational texts of the Japanese elegiac tradition.

The Manyoshu's principal compiler was probably **Otomo no Yakamochi** (OH-toh-moh noh yah-kah-MOH-chee, 718–785), an aristocrat-poet of the Otomo clan who held provincial governorships and military posts across the Nara period and who wrote 473 of the anthology's poems himself — the largest single contribution by any author. His selections preserved the Manyoshu's social breadth: it was Yakamochi who included the frontier-guard poems, the work-songs, the regional dialects, the unpolished but heartfelt voices of non-court Japanese alongside the formal compositions of his own aristocratic class. The result is one of the most democratic poetry anthologies in any pre-modern literary tradition.

The Manyoshu is also the greatest surviving monument of an unusual writing system called **man'yogana** (MAHN-yoh-gah-nah, named after the anthology itself). Man'yogana uses Chinese characters for their phonetic values rather than for their meanings — each character represents one Japanese syllable. Since the two languages are completely unrelated (Japanese is an agglutinative non-tonal language with completely different grammar from monosyllabic tonal Chinese), the only way to write Japanese poetry while preserving its actual sound was to strip Chinese characters of their meaning and use them purely as sound symbols. One character per syllable, hundreds of different characters in use, no standardization on which character to use for which sound — different scribes used different conventions, sometimes within the same poem.

The system was absurdly inefficient. Imagine writing English by repurposing Chinese numerals as sounds (一 = "wun," 二 = "too," 四 = "for") and then spelling out entire sentences that way, with each writer free to substitute different characters for the same sounds. That's man'yogana. It was begging to be simplified. Within a century, by the early Heian period, Japanese scribes had simplified the man'yogana characters into the two streamlined syllabaries — **hiragana** and **katakana** — that would let Japanese be written as Japanese rather than as a tortured phonetic adaptation of Chinese (Heian Japan Ch 1). But until the kana revolution, the Manyoshu represents the best the Japanese could do: the heroic, awkward, beautiful effort of writing native poetry in a borrowed script.

---

The Nara period also saw the polishing and re-promulgation of the great Tang-modeled legal code.

The **Yoro Code** (YOH-roh) was drafted by Fujiwara no Fuhito and his collaborators around 718, as a careful revision of the Taiho Code of 701. The two codes were structurally identical and operationally similar; the Yoro version made textual refinements, clarified ambiguous provisions, updated some of the bureaucratic ranking, and incorporated the experience of two decades of running the ritsuryo system. The Yoro Code was held in reserve for several decades after Fuhito's death in 720, then formally promulgated in 757 under Empress Koken — and once promulgated, it became the operational legal framework of Japan for the next millennium. Yoro provisions were still being cited in court rulings during the Edo period in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Few legal codes anywhere in the world have had a longer continuous official life.

The Yoro Code is also unusual in that nearly the entire text survives. The Taiho Code of 701, despite being the more historically important of the two, survives only in fragments cited in later sources. The Yoro Code survives almost intact, as a thirty-volume compilation that has been continuously preserved at imperial archives since the eighth century. Modern Japanese legal historians have an almost embarrassing wealth of primary material on Nara-period law — the Yoro text itself, plus the Yoro commentaries (Ryo no Gige of 833 and Ryo no Shuge of around 880), plus the fragments and citations from earlier codes. The full operational shape of the Nara state can be reconstructed in detail.

What the Yoro Code reveals, as a document, is just how far the Japanese state had pushed the Tang model into operational reality. The Code specifies the duties of every bureaucratic post, the schedule of ranks and salaries, the procedures for tax assessment and collection, the sentencing framework for criminal offenses, the rituals of ceremonial occasions, the regulations governing Buddhist clergy, the rules for provincial governance, and the protocols for diplomatic correspondence. It is a Tang state on paper, adapted at almost every detail to Japanese institutional realities, fully functional as a governing document. The fact that the actual operational reach of the central state never matched the Code's ambitions — that aristocratic estates and Buddhist temple lands carved themselves out of the registered tax base, that the conscript army system collapsed in fifty years, that the handen-shuju land registration broke down by the late ninth century — does not change the fact that the framework existed and shaped Japanese political thought for over a thousand years.

---

The most peculiar episode of the entire late Nara period is the **Dokyo Affair**.

The story begins with **Empress Koken** (KOH-ken), daughter of Shomu and Komyo, who took the throne in 749 after her father's abdication and reigned until 758. She was unmarried and had no children, both unusual for a Japanese empress. After 758 she retired and her cousin took the throne as Emperor Junnin, with the senior Fujiwara minister **Fujiwara no Nakamaro** (nah-kah-MAH-roh) as the dominant figure at court.

In the early 760s, the retired Koken became seriously ill. She was attended by a Buddhist monk of the Hosso school named **Dokyo** (DOH-kyoh — usually rendered without the long o-mark in plain ASCII). Dokyo was a learned monk, an effective spiritual healer, and (the chronicle accounts repeatedly emphasize) personally charismatic to a degree that captured the retired empress's deep loyalty. By the time Koken recovered from her illness in 762, Dokyo had become her most trusted personal counselor. She moved him into senior court positions — Master of Imperial Sutras, then State Minister, then increasingly extraordinary titles invented to justify the level of authority she was placing in him.

The senior Fujiwara minister Nakamaro, watching this with growing alarm, attempted a coup in 764 to remove Dokyo and reassert Fujiwara dominance. The **Fujiwara no Nakamaro Rebellion** lasted only a few weeks — Nakamaro was outmaneuvered, defeated militarily, and beheaded with most of his senior allies. The retired Empress Koken returned to active reign in 764, deposing her cousin Junnin (who was sent into exile and died there, possibly murdered), and taking the throne under a new reign name as **Empress Shotoku** (SHOH-toh-koo — confusingly the same name as the great Asuka prince of Chapter 2, but as a posthumous designation rather than an active personal name).

This was Empress Shotoku's second reign. It was completely dominated by Dokyo. He was given the title **Hoo** ("Dharma King") in 766 — an unprecedented designation that styled him as a kind of monastic-imperial figure parallel to the secular emperor. He moved his temple base into the Heijo-kyo palace complex itself. His Hosso-school faction took over the Buddhist establishment, displacing rival schools. Court appointments increasingly went to Dokyo's allies. The chroniclers — writing later under the post-Dokyo restoration — describe the period in tones of horror, suggesting (without quite stating it directly) that Dokyo's relationship with the empress was sexual, that he was using her infatuation with him to seize political control of Japan, and that the country was on the edge of being taken over by a Buddhist priest.

In 769, the affair came to its head. An oracle was reported from the Usa Hachiman shrine in Kyushu (one of the most powerful Shinto shrines in Japan, dedicated to the deified emperor Ojin, regarded as a war-god protector of the imperial line). The oracle, the chronicle account says, declared that Dokyo should be made emperor. The court was thrown into crisis. If the Usa oracle was authentic, the throne itself was about to pass from the imperial line — descended from Amaterasu through unbroken male succession (with female intervals) for the entire history of the country — to a Buddhist monk with no imperial blood.

Empress Shotoku sent an envoy to Usa to verify the oracle. The envoy chosen was **Wake no Kiyomaro** (WAH-keh noh KEE-yoh-mah-roh), a young court official who had not yet been compromised by the Dokyo faction. Kiyomaro went to Usa, performed the ritual consultation, and returned with the opposite oracle: the throne could only pass through the imperial line, and Dokyo could not become emperor. Whether the original "Dokyo oracle" had been a forgery, whether Kiyomaro's counter-oracle was genuine or invented, whether the Usa shrine priesthood was politically aligned with the Fujiwara faction — none of this can be resolved from the surviving sources, all of which were written by parties hostile to Dokyo. What is clear is that Kiyomaro's report blocked the succession plan. Dokyo had Kiyomaro exiled in disgrace.

Then, in 770, Empress Shotoku died. She left no clear successor. Her court faction, led by her dying preferences, had lost decisive momentum. The senior aristocrats — primarily the surviving Fujiwara — moved quickly to install a male emperor of impeccable imperial lineage (Konin, a sixty-two-year-old grandson of Tenji, chosen specifically because he was old, conservative, and far from the Dokyo faction). Dokyo himself was demoted, exiled to a remote temple in the eastern provinces, and died there a few years later. Wake no Kiyomaro was recalled from exile and rewarded.

The Dokyo affair had two enormous consequences. First, the political power of Nara Buddhism was now visible to every court faction. A monk had nearly taken the throne. The risk that Buddhist temples could collude with reigning empresses to overthrow the secular government had been demonstrated. Second, and as a direct result, female imperial succession was now politically suspect. The court that had installed Konin in 770 began an unwritten policy of avoiding reigning empresses unless absolutely necessary, on the theory that women were vulnerable to being manipulated by court Buddhist factions in the way Empress Shotoku had been manipulated by Dokyo. This policy would harden over time into a formal exclusion that lasted, with very minor exceptions, for over a thousand years. The next reigning Japanese empress would not appear until 1629, and the policy would not be formally reversed until the post-1868 Meiji Constitution. A single eccentric monk had warped the gender of Japanese imperial succession for the next millennium.

The Dokyo affair is also one of the events that drove the eventual decision to move the capital out of Nara entirely. If the Buddhist establishment in Heijo-kyo had grown so politically powerful that it could nearly seize the throne, then the obvious solution was to take the throne somewhere else. Emperor Konin's son and successor, **Emperor Kammu**, would do exactly that in 784 (Chapter 8).

---

Two final cultural products of the late Nara period deserve mention before we close.

The **Hyakumanto Darani** (hyah-koo-MAHN-toh dah-RAH-nee — "One Million Pagodas with Dharani") is one of the most extraordinary printing projects in world history. Empress Shotoku, in the wake of the suppression of the Nakamaro rebellion in 764, commissioned the production of one million miniature wooden pagodas, each containing a printed Buddhist dharani charm (a short ritual text). The project took six years and was completed around 770. Each of the million pagodas was a small hand-carved wooden object about three to four inches tall; inside each was a printed paper scroll bearing one of four short Sanskrit-derived dharani prayers in Chinese transliteration. The dharani prayers were printed using woodblock printing — wooden printing blocks carved with the text, inked, and pressed onto paper.

These printed dharani are among the earliest surviving examples of woodblock printing anywhere in the world. They predate the famous Diamond Sutra of 868 (long considered the earliest surviving printed book, found in the Dunhuang caves in western China — see Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 4) by nearly a century. There are competing claims — Korean printing, Chinese pre-Tang printing — but most are based on fragmentary or contested evidence; the Hyakumanto Darani are not contested. Roughly 50,000 of the original million pagodas survive in temple collections (the bulk at Horyu-ji, which received a tenth of the original distribution), and dharani prints from the project can be examined by visiting scholars. The fact that the world's earliest substantially surviving printed texts come from late-Nara Japan — produced as a state-Buddhist propitiatory project at the height of an empress's grief and political guilt — is one of the more unexpected facts in the technological history of the medium.

And finally, the late Nara period continued the kentoshi missions to Tang China that had been the central conduit of continental influence for over a century. The 717 embassy was particularly consequential: it included the young scholar **Kibi no Makibi** (KEE-bee noh MAH-kee-bee), who spent eighteen years in China (returning in 735), bringing back astronomy, military strategy, and a general intellectual sophistication that made him one of the highest-ranking non-Fujiwara officials of his generation. The same embassy included the priest **Genbo**, who returned with the largest single shipment of Buddhist sutras Japan had ever received (over 5,000 scrolls), establishing the Hosso-school textual library that Dokyo would later draw on. And the most famous Japanese figure of the entire kentoshi tradition was a contemporary: **Abe no Nakamaro** (AH-beh noh NAH-kah-mah-roh), sent to China at sixteen on the same 717 embassy. Nakamaro stayed. He passed the imperial examinations (an extraordinary achievement for a foreigner), became a high official under Tang Emperor Xuanzong, befriended the great poets Li Bai and Wang Wei, and spent his entire life in China, never managing to return home alive despite multiple attempts. He wrote a famous poem looking eastward across the sea toward Japan and watching the moon rise: "If I could see in my homeland the same moon that rises here over Mount Mikasa..." Li Bai wrote a poem mourning him after a false report of his death. Nakamaro died in Chang'an in 770 and was buried in China. He is the most poignant single figure in the long story of Japan's eighth-century relationship with Tang.

---

Cross-civ check: the 770s — when the Dokyo Affair was unfolding and the Hyakumanto Darani was being printed — were also when the **Abbasid Caliphate** under Caliph al-Mansur was completing the construction of Baghdad as the new caliphal capital (founded 762, walls complete by 766). The Abbasid revolution had ended the Umayyad dynasty in 750 (Islamic Golden Age Ch 1). In **Tang China**, Emperor Daizong was struggling to recover from the catastrophic **An Lushan Rebellion** of 755–763, which had killed perhaps 36 million people and broken the back of the Tang dynasty (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 4) — Tang would limp along for another 144 years but never recover its earlier vitality. In **Europe**, **Charlemagne** had just inherited the Frankish throne (from his father Pepin in 768) and was beginning the conquests that would unify most of western Europe under Carolingian rule by the end of the century. The Manyoshu, completed around 759, is roughly contemporary with the Old English **Beowulf** (composed in some form around 700–800), the great surviving Anglo-Saxon poem in alliterative meter. The two poetic monuments are interesting to compare: Beowulf preserves a single epic narrative in the warrior aristocracy's voice; the Manyoshu preserves 4,500 lyrics across the entire social spectrum from emperors to anonymous frontier guards. Different societies produced different kinds of monumental poetry. Both have lasted.

# Chapter 8 — The Move to Heian

By the early 780s, the Heijo-kyo capital had been the seat of the Japanese imperial court for over seventy years. Three generations of emperors and empresses had reigned there. The great Buddhist temples had grown into the most powerful religious institutions in the country — and after the Dokyo Affair of 769–770 (Chapter 7), into the most politically dangerous ones. The Fujiwara clan had consolidated its grip on senior court positions. The Tang-modeled bureaucracy had reached the limits of its actual operational reach into the rural provinces. Something had to change.

The change came from a determined emperor whose mother was a Korean royal descendant, whose political program was to break the temple-court symbiosis that had built up at Nara, and who was prepared to move the capital twice in a decade to do it. His name was **Emperor Kammu** (KAH-moo), and his decision in 794 to settle the imperial court at a new city he named Heian-kyo — modern Kyoto — closed the Asuka-Nara period and opened the Heian one.

This chapter covers Kammu's reign from 781 to his death in 806, the Emishi frontier wars that reshaped Japan's military system in the same period, and the legacy of the Asuka-Nara centuries.

---

**Emperor Kammu** (737–806) was an unlikely figure for the throne. He was the son of Emperor Konin (the elderly conservative who had been installed after Empress Shotoku's death in 770) and a consort named **Takano no Niigasa** (TAH-kah-noh noh NEE-gah-sah), who was descended from the Yamato no Fubito immigrant clan — a lineage tracing back to refugees from the Baekje royal family who had fled to Japan in the seventh century. Kammu was, in other words, partly Korean by ancestry on his mother's side. This fact was discreetly noted in the eighth-century chronicles and quietly minimized through the medieval period; in 2001, Emperor Akihito of Japan publicly acknowledged the Korean ancestry in his great-distant ancestor's line, in a remark that was a small but real diplomatic moment between modern Japan and Korea.

Kammu became Crown Prince in 773 and Emperor in 781, when his father Konin abdicated in his favor at age sixty-five. He was forty-four years old at his accession, a relatively mature emperor with clear political views and a willingness to push them through. His twenty-five-year reign is one of the most consequential of the entire pre-modern Japanese imperial line.

Kammu's central political conviction was that the institutional symbiosis between the throne and the Nara Buddhist temples had to be broken. The Dokyo Affair was still in living memory. The kokubunji network had become an enormous economic burden on provincial finances. The great temples in Heijo-kyo had become major landholders in their own right, with monastic populations of thousands, with tax-exempt estates spreading across central Honshu, and with the political reach to influence court appointments at the highest level. As long as the imperial court remained in Nara, the temples would always be on its doorstep, with the institutional ties between them only deepening over time.

Kammu's solution was radical: move the capital, and leave the temples behind. The aristocratic and bureaucratic court would go to a new site. The great Nara temples could keep their existing buildings, their existing monastic populations, and their existing landholdings — but the political court they had attached themselves to would no longer be there. The new capital would have its own temples, on the imperial court's terms, not on the temples' terms.

---

Kammu's first attempt was at **Nagaoka-kyo** (nah-gah-OH-kah-KYOH), in the Yamashiro region a few miles north of Heijo-kyo. Construction began in 784, and the imperial court moved within a year. Nagaoka-kyo was meant to be a permanent replacement: it was laid out on a Tang-style grid like Heijo-kyo but at a slightly larger scale, with better river access (the new capital sat at the confluence of the Yodo, Katsura, and Uji rivers), and at a sufficient distance from the great Nara temples to break their political grip on the court.

The Nagaoka project went badly almost from the start. The construction was supervised by Kammu's chief minister, **Fujiwara no Tanetsugu** (TAH-neh-tsoo-goo), an architect of the relocation strategy. In 785, just over a year after the move, Tanetsugu was assassinated at the Nagaoka construction site — shot with arrows in a coordinated attack while supervising work on the new palace. The assassins were tracked down and executed, but the conspiracy reached high into the imperial family itself. Kammu's brother, the Crown Prince **Sawara** (sah-WAH-rah), was implicated in the plot — possibly justly, possibly framed by Kammu's faction as a way of removing a rival.

Sawara was stripped of his Crown Prince title and exiled. Refusing to eat during the journey toward exile, he died en route — possibly by deliberate self-starvation, possibly from the stress of the political destruction, possibly through assassination disguised as natural death. The chronicle accounts are inconsistent and partisan. Whatever the exact cause, the brother of the reigning emperor was dead, by means that would not bear close examination.

Then a series of disasters hit Nagaoka. Plague broke out in the new capital. Floods damaged the construction sites. Kammu's mother died unexpectedly in 789. His wife died in 790. His son died in 792. By 793, the court was convinced that Sawara's angry spirit was taking revenge on the family that had destroyed him, and that the new capital was cursed. Kammu was prepared to move again.

The decision to abandon Nagaoka-kyo was formalized in 793, after only nine years of occupation. The court began the search for a new site, and a third capital was selected just north of Nagaoka, in a basin protected on three sides by mountains, with the Kamo and Katsura rivers running through it. The site was traditionally regarded as auspicious — in the Chinese geomantic system the Japanese used for capital site selection, it was considered a particularly favorable location, with mountains to the north (blocking malevolent influences), open ground to the south (allowing positive influences), and rivers framing the site (representing prosperity).

Construction began in 793. The court moved in 794. Kammu named the new capital **Heian-kyo** (hay-AHN-kyoh — "Capital of Peace and Tranquility").

The name worked. The new city would remain the imperial capital for the next 1,074 years — until 1868, when the Meiji Restoration would relocate the imperial court to Edo, renamed Tokyo. Heian-kyo, called Kyoto from the late medieval period onward, was the longest-serving imperial capital in Japanese history and the longest-serving continuously occupied imperial capital of any major civilization. It is still inhabited today, with significant portions of the original eighth-century street grid preserved in the modern city's layout.

The opening of the Heian period — the brilliant aesthetic civilization of Murasaki Shikibu and Lady Sei, of *The Tale of Genji* and *The Pillow Book*, of the Fujiwara regents and the cloistered emperors and the Genpei War (Heian Japan, all chapters) — is the next narrative. The closing of the Asuka-Nara period is the move to Heian-kyo in 794.

---

Kammu's reign also produced the major reorganization of the Japanese frontier military system, in the long-running campaigns against the **Emishi** (eh-MEE-shee) people of northeastern Honshu.

The Emishi were the indigenous population of what is now the Tohoku region — roughly the northeastern third of the main Japanese island. They were not Yamato Japanese in language, culture, or political organization. They had maintained a distinct culture (probably descended from Jomon-period populations who had not been fully absorbed by the Yayoi-period continental migration, see Prehistoric Japan Ch 2–3) into the historical period. They lived in scattered settlements, organized around chiefs and clan leaders rather than centralized states, with subsistence economies that combined hunting, fishing, and limited agriculture. They were not "barbarians" in any meaningful sense — they had complex social organization, distinctive material culture, and effective military traditions — but they were not the kind of agriculturally settled tax-paying subjects that the ritsuryo system was designed to register.

For most of the Asuka-Nara period, the Yamato court had pursued a slow expansion of the frontier into Emishi territory — establishing fortified administrative posts, settling immigrant farmers, gradually drawing border populations into the registered tax base. This gradual process accelerated under Kammu, who was determined to definitively bring the Emishi region under imperial control. The campaigns of the 780s and 790s were the most intensive military operations the Yamato state had ever mounted.

They went badly at first. The Emishi had a strong cavalry tradition, knew the difficult northern terrain, and could disperse and re-form against larger invading armies in ways that the Yamato conscript forces found difficult to counter. The campaign of 789 — sent under the general Ki no Kosami with an army of perhaps 50,000 — was decisively defeated at the Subuse River by Emishi forces under their leader **Aterui** (ah-teh-ROO-ee). Ki no Kosami's army lost over a thousand men dead and several thousand more wounded, a significant defeat for the Yamato side.

Kammu reorganized. He recruited a new general, **Sakanoue no Tamuramaro** (sah-kah-noh-OO-eh noh tah-moo-rah-MAH-roh, 758–811), and gave him a new title: **Sei-i Taishogun** (SAY-ee TYE-shoh-gun — "Barbarian-Subduing Great General"). The title is the origin of the later word **shogun** (which would, under different historical circumstances, become the title of the de facto rulers of Japan from the late twelfth century onward — Heian Japan Ch 6 onward). Tamuramaro would shape it into a permanent post.

Tamuramaro's campaigns of the 790s and early 800s gradually pushed the Yamato frontier northward. He fought the Emishi with mounted forces, professional soldiers paid out of imperial revenues, and a willingness to negotiate with Emishi leaders rather than insist on unconditional subjugation. Aterui himself eventually surrendered in 802 and was taken to Heijo-kyo (the old capital, now the religious center of the country though no longer the political one). The court refused Tamuramaro's request to spare him; Aterui was executed. The campaigns continued through the early 800s and gradually subdued the Emishi region down to roughly the modern boundary between Tohoku's central provinces and Hokkaido.

The Emishi campaigns had two enduring effects. First, they exhausted the Taiho-Code conscript army system. Mobilizing Japanese peasants under arms for years of frontier warfare in the cold northeast turned out to be politically and economically unsustainable. Kammu eventually abolished the conscript system in 792 and replaced it with a smaller force of professional soldiers recruited from local strongmen. This is the institutional foundation for the eventual rise of the warrior class — the **bushi** (BOO-shee) and eventually the **samurai** — that would dominate Japan from the late Heian period onward (Heian Japan Ch 4–6).

Second, the campaigns created the first regional warrior aristocracies. The military families that supplied generals like Tamuramaro, that provided the professional cavalry, that learned to fight Emishi-style wars in the northeastern terrain, were the first generation of what became hereditary warrior lineages. The northeastern frontier produced tough, mounted, militarily experienced families — the Abe, the Kiyowara, the eventual Northern Fujiwara — that would matter politically for centuries. The Emishi wars are the unromantic but real beginning of Japanese warrior culture.

---

The legacy of the Asuka-Nara period — the 256 years from 538 to 794 — is enormous and largely invisible to the casual modern eye, because so much of what was built in this period became simply the structure of Japan, taken for granted by everything that came after.

The imperial line that the Asuka and Nara historians traced back to the gods continued unbroken through the Heian, Kamakura, Ashikaga, Tokugawa, and Meiji periods, all the way to the present day — an imperial line of officially 126 monarchs, of which about 60 reigned after the Asuka-Nara period closed, all of them building their legitimacy on the foundation that the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki had laid. The Japanese imperial family today is the longest continuous reigning dynasty in the world, and that dynastic continuity was textually constructed in the Asuka and Nara periods.

The Buddhist establishment that Shomu and the kokubunji network created became the bedrock institution of pre-modern Japanese religion. Subsequent centuries layered new Buddhist schools on top of the Nara foundations — Tendai and Shingon in the early Heian, Pure Land and Zen and Nichiren in the Kamakura — but the great Nara temples never lost their importance, and many continued to operate without interruption from the Asuka-Nara period to the present. Todai-ji, Horyu-ji, Kofuku-ji, Yakushi-ji, Toshodai-ji, and dozens of others are still functioning Buddhist institutions today, with continuous monastic traditions reaching back twelve centuries.

The Tang-modeled state that the Taika Reform and the Taiho/Yoro Codes established became the conceptual framework of Japanese political thought. Even when the actual operational reach of the central state failed in subsequent centuries — when the Heian-period shoen estates carved up the registered tax base, when the Kamakura shogunate established a parallel warrior government, when the Ashikaga and Tokugawa periods further fragmented political authority — the language and concepts of ritsuryo governance remained the official terminology. The Meiji Restoration in 1868 explicitly invoked the Asuka-Nara model: it claimed to be returning Japan to the original imperial-bureaucratic state that the Taika Reform had attempted to build, before the warrior governments had usurped imperial authority.

The two great chronicles — the Kojiki and the Nihon Shoki — became the founding scriptures of Japanese national identity, especially after the late-Tokugawa and early-Meiji nativist scholars (the **kokugaku** movement) elevated them to a quasi-sacred status. The myths they contain were taught as literal national history in pre-1945 Japanese schools and were one of the textual foundations of the wartime emperor cult. Modern Japan has been more careful with these texts, but they remain canonical sources for understanding pre-modern Japanese self-conception.

The Manyoshu became the foundational anthology of the Japanese poetic tradition. Every later poetry anthology (the Kokin Wakashu of 905, the Shin Kokin Wakashu of 1205, the medieval imperial anthologies, the Edo-period commentaries) defined itself in relation to the Manyoshu. The vernacular Japanese literary tradition — including the Heian-period explosion of court literature in kana script — built on the Manyoshu's establishment of vernacular poetry as a respectable art form.

The capital city tradition — that the imperial court resided in a permanent grid-planned capital named with auspicious Chinese characters — held from Heijo-kyo onward through Heian-kyo, Edo, and Tokyo. Kyoto preserves substantial portions of its Asuka-Nara-derived street grid into the modern city. The basic civic geography of Japan was set in the Asuka and Nara periods.

And finally, the Asuka-Nara periods set the precedent for the deeper cultural pattern that would define Japan for the next millennium: a society that absorbed enormous amounts of foreign cultural material, especially from China and Korea, and transformed every borrowed element into something distinctively Japanese. Buddhism became Japanese Buddhism. Chinese writing became Japanese writing. Tang government became Japanese government. Korean architecture became Japanese architecture. The pattern was not unique to the Asuka-Nara period — Japan would later absorb European industrial science in the Meiji era, American consumer culture in the postwar era, with the same selective creativity — but the pattern was established here. The Yamato court of the Soga era had been a small, rural, clan-based polity. The Heian court of 794 was a sophisticated imperial court that ran a Tang-modeled state, patronized a Buddhist establishment of continental scale, produced national chronicles in two scripts and poetry in a third, and held a capital city of 100,000-plus residents at the eastern end of the Silk Road. The transformation took 256 years. It was the foundation of everything that came next.

---

Cross-civ check: the move to Heian-kyo in 794 happened in a remarkable European moment. Six years later, in 800, **Charlemagne** would be crowned Emperor of the Romans by Pope Leo III in Rome — the Carolingian moment that would reshape Western European political organization for centuries. The **Abbasid Caliphate** under Harun al-Rashid (786–809) was at the height of its golden age, with Baghdad as the largest city in the Islamic world and the cultural capital of the Eurasian middle (Islamic Golden Age Ch 2). In **Tang China**, the dynasty had survived the An Lushan Rebellion (Sui, Tang & Song China Ch 4) but was visibly weakening, with provincial governors increasingly autonomous and the central state's revenue base shrinking. In **Korea**, the Unified Silla golden age was approaching its last century, with Korean trade networks reaching as far as the Persian Gulf. The **Viking Age** had just begun — the raid on Lindisfarne in 793, one year before the Heian-kyo move, opened two and a half centuries of Scandinavian expansion across the North Atlantic and Europe (Viking Age Ch 1). And in the Americas, the **classic Maya** civilization in the Yucatan was approaching its mysterious collapse in the ninth and tenth centuries (Maya Civilization Ch 7), and the **middle-horizon empires** of the Andes (Wari and Tiwanaku) were at their peak (Middle Horizon Empires Ch 1). The world Kammu's new capital opened onto was a connected one — through the Silk Road, through the kentoshi missions, through Buddhist-monastic networks reaching across Eurasia — and Heian-kyo would spend its early centuries quietly producing one of the most distinctive aesthetic civilizations any of those connected regions ever generated.

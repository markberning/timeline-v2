# Meiji Japan — How a Sealed Country Built an Empire in Forty Years

**Goal:** Narrative for the Meiji era (1868–1912 CE), the chapter of Japanese history when a feudal isolated agrarian state — the Tokugawa Japan that ended in the previous TL — turned itself, in roughly forty years, into a constitutional monarchy with railways, factories, a Prussian-style army, a British-style navy, a French-style civil code, a German-style university system, a parliament, an empire, and a dead emperor whose funeral was front-page news in every Western capital. No country had ever modernized this fast. None has matched the speed since. The Meiji project succeeded so completely that within thirty-three years of the restoration, Japan was sinking Russian battleships at Tsushima; within forty-two years, it had annexed Korea. The same project also baked in the militarism and the ethno-religious nationalism that would carry Japan to total catastrophe in 1945. Both the success and the disaster ran on the same operating system. This book is about how that system was built.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — The Restoration

The previous TL ended on a beach. On July 8, 1853, four black ships of the United States Navy — two of them steam frigates, the largest things the lookout had ever seen — sailed into the entrance of Edo Bay, dropped anchor, and refused to leave (Edo Japan Ch 8). The man on the lead frigate was **Commodore Matthew Perry** (PEHR-ee), sixty years old, carrying a letter from the American president demanding that Japan open to trade. The Tokugawa shogunate, the warrior government that had ruled Japan for 253 years from the city of **Edo** (modern Tokyo), had no military force capable of resisting the steam-powered cannons in front of it. After fifteen years of escalating crisis, the shogunate would be gone. After fifteen years of escalating crisis, a coalition of samurai from two southwestern domains would have placed a fifteen-year-old emperor on a real throne for the first time in eight centuries, and Japan would have begun the most rapid national transformation any country has ever attempted.

This chapter covers the fifteen years between Perry's arrival and the proclamation of the **Meiji Restoration** in January 1868. The story is dense, the names are unfamiliar, and the chronology is messy. You don't need to remember all of it. What you need to come away with is the political logic: a sealed country was forced open by a foreign power it could not resist, the regime that had failed to resist lost legitimacy as a result, a new regime built itself out of the wreckage, and the new regime then spent the next forty years making sure no foreign power would ever dictate to Japan again.

---

To set the scene: Japan in 1853 had been governed for two and a half centuries by the **Tokugawa shogunate** (toh-koo-GAH-wah SHOH-guh-nayt), a hereditary military government founded by **Tokugawa Ieyasu** in 1603 (Edo Japan Ch 1). The shogun (literally "barbarian-subduing generalissimo") was the actual political ruler. The **emperor** in **Kyoto** (KYOH-toh) was the ceremonial sovereign, descended in unbroken line — by official Japanese tradition — from the sun goddess **Amaterasu**, but had not exercised real political power since the late twelfth century, when the warrior class first took over (Ancient Japan Ch 7–8). The country was divided into roughly 270 **domains** (han), each ruled by a hereditary lord (**daimyō**, DIE-myoh) who governed his fief under the shogunate's overall authority. Below the daimyō stood the warrior caste (**samurai**, SAH-moo-rye), legally about 6% of the population, who held a state monopoly on weapons and on government office. Below the samurai were the peasants (~80%), the artisans, and the merchants, in officially descending Confucian social rank.

The country had been formally **closed** since the 1630s under the **sakoku** (sah-KOH-koo, "closed country") policy: no Japanese could leave; no foreigners could enter except a handful of Dutch traders confined to a small artificial island called **Dejima** in **Nagasaki** harbor, plus limited Chinese and Korean trade through specific channels (Edo Japan Ch 3). For two centuries this worked. Japan was peaceful, prosperous, literate, urbanized — Edo had over a million people by 1700, the largest city in the world — and almost completely insulated from the rest of the planet. Western technological developments were absorbed through the Dutch trickle and by **rangaku** ("Dutch learning") scholars who taught themselves European languages from smuggled books (Edo Japan Ch 6). But the country had no modern military, no modern navy, and no modern industry.

Meanwhile the world outside had transformed. Britain had industrialized. The **Opium Wars** (1839–42 and 1856–60) had shown that European steam warships could dictate terms to the Chinese empire (Tang-Song China was nine centuries gone, but its Qing successor was still nominally the dominant Asian power). The **United States**, fresh from the Mexican-American War, had reached the Pacific coast in California in 1848 and wanted Pacific coaling stations for steamships heading to China. A door was about to be kicked in.

---

When Perry returned in February 1854 with a larger fleet, the shogunate signed the **Treaty of Kanagawa**: the United States got the right to enter the ports of **Shimoda** (on the Izu Peninsula, south of Edo) and **Hakodate** (in southern **Hokkaido**, the northern island), the right to station an American consul, and a most-favored-nation clause. It was a treaty of friendship, not of full commerce. Sakoku as a legal regime was over.

Four years later, in 1858, the American consul **Townsend Harris** negotiated the **Harris Treaty** (the Treaty of Amity and Commerce). The terms were what Britain had imposed on China sixteen years earlier: **extraterritoriality** (foreigners accused of crimes would be tried by their own consular courts, not Japanese ones); a 5% tariff cap (Japan could not protect its own industries); five additional open ports including **Yokohama**, near Edo. Within weeks, Britain, France, the Netherlands, and Russia signed near-identical **Ansei Treaties** (named for the imperial era then in effect). The unequal treaty regime that would humiliate Japan for the next four decades was now in place.

The shogunate had signed without imperial sanction — the reactionary nationalist **Emperor Kōmei** (KOH-mei) on the Kyoto throne had refused to ratify. The chief minister **Ii Naosuke** (EE nah-oh-SOO-keh) authorized the signing anyway. This had two consequences. First, the imperial court suddenly had political relevance for the first time in centuries, because the rapidly growing **sonnō jōi** (son-NOH JOH-ee, "Revere the Emperor, Expel the Barbarians") movement could rally around the emperor's refusal. Second, Ii was assassinated in March 1860 outside the Sakurada Gate of Edo Castle by a band of nationalist samurai. The **Sakuradamon Incident** demonstrated that the shogunate could no longer protect even its highest officials. The bakufu (the shogunal government) began visibly cracking.

---

The opposition coalesced around three southwestern domains:

**Satsuma** (SAHT-soo-mah), the Shimazu domain in southern Kyushu — the southernmost of the four main Japanese islands. Wealthy from sugar and unofficial trade with the Ryukyu Islands. Led by the reformist daimyō **Shimazu Hisamitsu**.

**Chōshū** (CHOH-shoo), the Mōri domain at the western end of Honshu, the main island. Poor, mountainous, but home to the most active rangaku-trained reform faction in the country.

**Tosa** (TOH-sah), the Yamauchi domain on the island of Shikoku.

All three were **tozama** ("outside") domains — descendants of clans that had lost the **Battle of Sekigahara** to Tokugawa Ieyasu in 1600 (Edo Japan Ch 1) and had been excluded from central power for two and a half centuries afterward. All three had spent the late Edo period quietly building modernized military forces using imported French and British weapons — much of it acquired through illegal channels routed through the **Glover Trading Company** in Nagasaki, run by the Scottish merchant **Thomas Glover**. All three had specific reasons to hate the shogunate.

Satsuma had been forced to pay a 100,000-pound indemnity to Britain after the **Namamugi Incident** of 1862, when Satsuma samurai cut down a British merchant on the Tōkaidō highway for failing to dismount before their lord's procession; the British navy then bombarded **Kagoshima**, Satsuma's capital, in 1863. Chōshū had fired on Western ships at the Shimonoseki Strait, leading to a four-power Western fleet bombardment in 1864 that destroyed Chōshū coastal defenses. The bakufu, blamed for both incidents, then mounted two punitive expeditions against Chōshū. The **first Chōshū Expedition** of 1864 forced the domain to back down. The **second Chōshū Expedition** of 1866 was a humiliating bakufu failure: the Chōshū riflemen, drilled in Western infantry tactics by the rangaku-trained military reformer **Ōmura Masujirō**, outclassed every bakufu force sent against them.

The decisive political move had taken place earlier in 1866, when a Tosa rōnin (masterless samurai) named **Sakamoto Ryōma** (sah-kah-MOH-toh ree-OH-mah) personally arranged a secret alliance between Satsuma and Chōshū. The two domains had been bitter enemies. Sakamoto's alliance brought them into coordinated opposition to the bakufu. The **Satsuma-Chōshū Alliance** (Satchō Dōmei) was the coalition that would, within two years, overthrow the Tokugawa shogunate.

---

The fifteenth and last shogun, **Tokugawa Yoshinobu** (toh-koo-GAH-wah yoh-shee-NOH-boo), came to office in late 1866. He was probably the most capable shogun the Tokugawa had produced in over a century — energetic, intelligent, conversant in Western affairs. His response to the situation was, in effect, a calculated surrender. In November 1867 he formally returned political authority to the emperor in the **Taisei Hōkan** ("Restoration of Imperial Rule"), proposing that he continue to head the new government as a constitutional minister under imperial sovereignty. He was betting that no other faction had the administrative experience to govern.

The bet failed. Sakamoto Ryōma was assassinated in Kyoto in December 1867, removing the most influential mediator. The Satsuma-Chōshū faction, dominated by hardliners, refused to keep Yoshinobu in any government. On January 3, 1868, they staged a palace coup at the Kyoto imperial court and proclaimed the **restoration of direct imperial rule** under the fifteen-year-old **Emperor Meiji**. The shogunate's military forces in and around Kyoto, refusing to accept the political settlement, marched on the imperial capital at the end of January 1868. They were intercepted at **Toba-Fushimi**, just south of Kyoto, by a smaller but better-armed Satsuma-Chōshū force flying the imperial banner. The bakufu forces broke and fled.

The **Boshin War** (Year of the Yang Earth Dragon War, January 1868 to June 1869) is the civil war that completed the overthrow. Imperial forces marched east through the central highway, took Edo without serious resistance in May 1868 — Yoshinobu, surrendering his sword, ordered no defense — pursued the shogunate diehards north into **Tōhoku** (the northeastern region of Honshu) and into Hokkaido. The last shogunate force, a naval contingent that had set up an independent **Republic of Ezo** at **Hakodate** under the navy admiral **Enomoto Takeaki** (eh-noh-MOH-toh tah-keh-AH-kee), surrendered in June 1869 after a six-month siege. The Boshin War's casualties — perhaps 10,000 dead total — were modest compared to the **Imjin War** of the 1590s (Joseon Korea Ch 4) or the Sengoku-era wars of the sixteenth century (Edo Japan Ch 1). But the political consequence was total. The Tokugawa shogunate, after 265 years of rule, was over.

---

The new government was nominally headed by the fifteen-year-old emperor. He had been born **Mutsuhito** in 1852, ascended the throne in early 1867 on the death of his father Kōmei (officially of smallpox, possibly of poison — the timing was suspiciously convenient), and would reign for forty-five years under the era name **Meiji** ("enlightened rule"). The actual administration was run by a coalition of senior **Satsuma**, **Chōshū**, **Tosa**, and **Hizen** officials — most of them under forty years old, most of them former samurai of relatively modest stipend rank, most of them rangaku-trained. Of the founding generation, a small group would do most of the governing for the next thirty years: the Satsuma giants **Saigō Takamori** (sigh-GO tah-kah-MOH-ree) and **Ōkubo Toshimichi** (OH-koo-boh toh-shee-MEE-chee); the Chōshū intellectuals **Kido Takayoshi** (KEE-doh tah-kah-YOH-shee), **Itō Hirobumi** (ee-TOH hee-roh-BOO-mee), and **Yamagata Aritomo** (yah-mah-GAH-tah ah-ree-TOH-moh); the Tosa political organizer **Itagaki Taisuke** (ee-tah-GAH-kee TIE-soo-keh); and the court noble **Iwakura Tomomi** (ee-wah-KOO-rah toh-MOH-mee), who acted as the bridge between the imperial palace and the samurai oligarchs.

In April 1868, the new government issued the **Charter Oath** — a five-article document that the boy emperor formally proclaimed before a ceremonial gathering of court nobles and daimyō. The articles promised public assembly and political consultation, abolition of feudal class restrictions, and "the seeking of knowledge throughout the world" so that Japan might secure "the foundations of imperial rule." It was the founding document of the new state — programmatically the diametric opposite of the late Tokugawa program, which had been about preserving the closed country at almost any cost. The Charter Oath was the program of the open country, and of catch-up modernization on whatever terms catch-up demanded.

In September 1868, **Edo** was renamed **Tokyo** ("Eastern Capital") and designated the new imperial capital. Kyoto, after a thousand years (Ancient Japan Ch 5–6), was demoted to a provincial city. The young emperor moved into the former shogun's castle in Edo. The symbolic transfer of sovereignty was complete.

---

The country the new government inherited was, by any external measure, a wreck.

Population around 33 million. Agrarian economy with negligible industry. Military — a few decentralized domain armies of varying modernization, no national navy beyond what the bakufu had begun to build, no railways, no telegraph, no modern banks, no national currency, no national tax system, no national legal code, no compulsory education, no centralized administration outside what the shogunate had loosely overseen. The country was bound by unequal treaties to four major Western powers, all of whom regarded it as a quaint object of curiosity, a possible coaling station, and a future colony. The Iwakura Mission of 1871–73, which we'll cover in Chapter 2, would discover that Western diplomats considered Japan roughly comparable to Persia or Egypt — interesting, exotic, fundamentally backward, and ripe for partition once the imperial powers got around to it.

The new government had to decide, very quickly, what it wanted Japan to become. The answer it gave shaped everything that followed. Japan would not be partitioned. Japan would not be a client state. Japan would build itself into an industrialized constitutional power on the European model so fast and so thoroughly that no foreign government would ever again dictate the terms of Japanese sovereignty. Whatever institutions were necessary to achieve that — feudal abolition, conscription, mass education, centralized taxation, parliament, written constitution, national religion, modern army, modern navy, overseas empire — would be built. Whatever institutions stood in the way — domains, samurai class, Buddhist temples, hereditary stipends, customary tariffs — would be dismantled. The institutional Tokugawa Japan, the country that had taken two and a half centuries to build, would be taken apart in roughly fifteen years.

That is what the next two chapters describe.

---

A pause, before the chronology takes over. The Meiji project is one of the most-studied transformations in modern world history, and the standard scholarly question about it is: why did Japan succeed where China and the **Ottoman Empire** failed? The Ottomans (Ottoman Empire Ch 9) faced essentially the same external pressure as Japan from roughly the same period (the **Tanzimat** reforms of 1839 onward), wanted essentially the same outcome (industrialized constitutional power on the European model), and had a head start in size, wealth, and institutional development. The Qing Chinese **Self-Strengthening Movement** of the 1860s–1890s had similar goals. Both projects produced significant achievements but neither produced the wholesale transformation that Japan accomplished. Why?

The standard scholarly readings emphasize several factors that the Meiji case had and the others didn't: a coherent reform faction that captured the central state intact (rather than reformers having to fight entrenched conservatives at every step); a relatively small and ethnically homogeneous population that could be administered uniformly; a literate population already accustomed to centralized authority; a commercial economy already in place from the Edo period (Edo Japan Ch 4); and — crucially — a foreign threat that was real but not immediate. The Western powers had imposed unequal treaties on Japan but were not actively partitioning it the way they were China. Japan had a window in which to reform without being invaded; China and the Ottomans, by the time they tried, no longer did. Some historians add that the rangaku tradition (Edo Japan Ch 6) had given Japan a small but capable cadre of intellectuals already familiar with Western knowledge. Others point to the Tokugawa-era development of a unified national consciousness through the **kokugaku** ("native learning") movement, which had argued for centuries that Japan was a unique sacred polity descended from the sun goddess — an ideological resource that Meiji nationalism could weaponize.

Whatever the right combination of factors, the result was that the Meiji oligarchs, between 1868 and 1900, accomplished something no other non-Western country has ever quite matched. The cost was real and will appear in later chapters: the same machinery that built the modern Japanese state also built the imperial expansion of 1894–1910 and the militarist catastrophe of the 1930s. The success and the disaster ran on the same operating system.

Cross-civ check: the Meiji Restoration of 1868 was almost exactly contemporary with the **Italian Risorgimento** (the unification of Italy under the House of Savoy, 1859–71); the **American Reconstruction** following the Civil War (1865–77); and the **German unification** under Prussia after the Franco-Prussian War (1870–71). The 1860s and 1870s were one of the densest decades of nation-state consolidation in modern history. Japan's project was a participant in, not an exception to, that broader pattern. The crucial difference was that Japan was building a nation-state on a non-Western cultural foundation while consciously importing Western institutions — what the historian Marius Jansen called "selective borrowing" — and managing to make the imported institutions feel sufficiently Japanese that the population accepted them. The same generation that watched the Charter Oath promulgated would, sixty years later, watch their grandsons march to war in Manchuria. The chain from one to the other is what this TL is about.

# Chapter 2 — Dismantling Feudalism

The new government came to power in 1868 with no actual government to govern with. The Tokugawa bakufu had been dismantled. The 270-odd domains still existed, each running its own administration, collecting its own taxes, maintaining its own samurai retainer corps, and answerable to its own daimyō. The new imperial regime in Tokyo had control of about a third of the country (the former shogunal lands, plus the territories of the loyalist domains) and influence over the rest. It had no national tax system, no national army, no national legal code, no national currency, and no national administration to speak of. Before it could industrialize, build a constitution, or do any of the things on its program, it had to construct the basic apparatus of a modern centralized state. That meant abolishing feudalism — the social and political system that had organized Japan for nearly seven centuries.

This chapter covers the first five years of the project (1868–1873), in which the new government dismantled the daimyō, dissolved the samurai class, established universal conscription, reformed the land tax, and dispatched half its senior leadership to spend twenty-two months in the West learning how the modernizing powers actually worked. By the end of it, Japan looked, on paper, almost nothing like the country Perry's ships had encountered twenty years earlier.

---

Step one was the daimyō.

In March 1869, four of the most powerful loyalist domains — Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa, and Hizen — voluntarily "returned" their fiefs to the emperor in a coordinated ceremonial gesture engineered by **Kido Takayoshi** and **Iwakura Tomomi**. The other daimyō, seeing the writing on the wall, almost all followed within months. The daimyō were then re-appointed as imperial governors of their old territories, drawing salaries from the central government rather than collecting their own taxes. This was a polite half-measure — the same families still ruled the same regions — but it established the principle that the territory was the emperor's, not the daimyō's.

Step two came two years later. On July 14, 1871, the government issued **Haihan Chiken** (HIGH-han CHEE-ken — "Abolition of the Han, Establishment of the Prefectures"). The 270 domains were dissolved outright. In their place, the country was divided into 72 prefectures (eventually consolidated to 47), each governed by an appointed prefectural official answerable directly to the central government. The daimyō were stripped of their administrative roles, given lifetime pensions in central-government bonds, and required to live in Tokyo. In a single edict, eight centuries of decentralized warrior rule ended.

This was an extraordinary thing to have done. The daimyō and their senior retainers controlled Japan's only effective military forces. They had every theoretical reason to refuse and to fight. That they didn't — that Haihan Chiken was implemented essentially without resistance — is one of the under-explained miracles of the Meiji period. The standard reading attributes it to a combination of factors: the daimyō's correct assessment that resistance against a unified Satsuma-Chōshū military would be suicidal; the bond pensions, which compensated them generously enough that most went along; the genuine prestige of the imperial restoration, which framed the abolition as the emperor's will rather than as an oligarchic power grab; and the fact that many daimyō were themselves bankrupt, glad to be relieved of administrative responsibilities they could no longer afford. Whatever the right combination, the central government had, in three years, gone from controlling a third of the country to controlling all of it.

---

Step three was the samurai. This took longer — about five years — and was harder.

The samurai were a hereditary warrior caste of about two million people, including dependents. They held a legal monopoly on bearing arms (specifically the daishō, the paired long and short swords that signaled samurai status). They were the only people legally eligible for government office. They received hereditary stipends from their domains, paid in rice, that constituted their entire household income. The dissolution of the domains in 1871 had left the central government on the hook for those stipends — which collectively absorbed about 30% of the new government's annual revenue. This was unsustainable.

Between 1871 and 1876, the government dismantled the samurai class in pieces. The 1871 imperial decree abolished the **eta** and **hinin** (the outcaste groups below the four-class system) and theoretically established equality of all subjects before the law. The 1872 decree abolished the legal distinction between samurai and commoner; the samurai retained the social title of **shizoku** (warrior household) but had no legal privileges over heimin (commoners). The 1873 **Conscription Law**, drafted by **Yamagata Aritomo** and modeled on the French and Prussian systems, required three years of military service from every able-bodied male over twenty regardless of class. This was the act, more than any other, that ended the samurai monopoly on arms. The samurai were no longer the country's warriors. The country's warriors were now the conscript peasants the samurai had spent centuries despising.

In 1876 came the final blow. The **haitōrei** edict (the "sword abolition order") prohibited the wearing of swords in public except by the active military and the police. The samurai social signature — the daishō at the belt — was banned. The same year, the government converted hereditary samurai stipends into one-time bonds, paying out roughly five to fourteen years of stipend value depending on rank. About 400,000 samurai households received bonds. The face value was real money, but inflation eroded it quickly, and the bonds proved an inadequate substitute for the inherited income they replaced. Two million people had effectively lost their hereditary livelihood within five years.

Some samurai prospered in the new system. The most adaptable became officials in the new prefectural governments, officers in the conscript army, schoolteachers, journalists, lawyers, businessmen. The founders of the **Mitsubishi** and **Sumitomo** zaibatsu (Chapter 4) were ex-samurai. The Meiji bureaucracy that ran the new state was, for at least a generation, almost entirely staffed by ex-samurai. The middle-class professional caste of late-nineteenth-century urban Japan was the destination most successful ex-samurai families ended up in. But the bottom half of the samurai class — the low-stipend warriors who had been functionally rural landlords or barely-paid retainers — slid into poverty. Some turned to small farming, some to small business, many to the traditional Japanese remedy for hereditary disappointment: drink. Many also turned to political resistance. The **Satsuma Rebellion** of 1877 (Chapter 3) was the largest of the samurai uprisings against the Meiji government, but smaller revolts erupted across western Japan from 1874 onward.

---

Step four was money.

The 1873 **Land Tax Reform** (Chisō Kaisei) replaced the variable rice-tax of the Edo period with a fixed cash tax of 3% of the assessed market value of agricultural land, payable in money rather than in rice. This had several enormous effects. It established private land titles for the first time in Japanese history — under the Edo system, the daimyō had been the legal owner and the peasants the tenants; now individual farmers held title to specific plots. It gave the state a stable predictable revenue base for industrialization. It collected the tax in cash, which forced peasants to sell some portion of their crop on the market — which in turn deepened the cash economy across rural Japan. And it meant that bad harvests no longer reduced the tax bill, which pushed marginal farmers into debt and eventually off the land in massive numbers. Tenancy rates rose steadily through the Meiji period. By 1900, roughly 40% of arable land was farmed by tenants paying rent to landlords. The land tax that funded industrialization also created the rural underclass that would supply the labor for the new factories.

The **New Currency Act** of 1871 introduced the **yen** (literally "round object," in reference to the shape of Western coins) as a decimal currency replacing the bewildering Edo-era system of ryō, mon, and bu. The yen was modeled on the Mexican silver dollar that was then the international trade currency in East Asia. Japan now had a single national money for the first time. The 1872 **National Bank Act**, modeled on the American National Banking Act of 1863, authorized the establishment of joint-stock banks. The 1882 **Bank of Japan** (Chapter 4) would eventually consolidate national monetary policy.

The 1871 introduction of the modern **postal service**, organized by **Maejima Hisoka** after his return from study in Britain, gave the new state a national communication network at peasant scale: stamps, post offices in every village, uniform rates regardless of distance. The 1869 **telegraph** line between Tokyo and Yokohama was the first link of what within a decade would be a national network reaching every prefectural capital. The 1872 opening of the **first railway**, eighteen miles of British-built track between Tokyo's Shimbashi station and Yokohama, was inaugurated by Emperor Meiji riding the first train. By 1890 Japan had roughly 1,400 miles of railway; by 1910, over 5,000 miles. The country was being wired together as fast as foreign engineers could build the infrastructure.

---

Step five was the Iwakura Mission. This deserves a longer description, because it was the diagnostic exercise on which the rest of the Meiji project depended.

In December 1871, half the senior leadership of the new government — the court noble **Iwakura Tomomi** as plenipotentiary, accompanied by **Ōkubo Toshimichi**, **Kido Takayoshi**, **Itō Hirobumi**, and roughly fifty subordinate officials and students — boarded the steamship **America** at Yokohama and sailed for San Francisco. They would spend the next twenty-two months touring the United States and twelve European countries: the US (San Francisco to Washington and back, four months); Britain (four months); France; Belgium; the Netherlands; Germany; Russia; Denmark; Sweden; Italy; Austria-Hungary; Switzerland. They visited factories, naval shipyards, parliaments, schools, prisons, hospitals, museums, banks. They met President Grant, Queen Victoria, Bismarck, the Tsar, the Pope. They came back to Japan in September 1873 with a 100-volume official report, written by the diplomat **Kume Kunitake** (KOO-meh koo-nee-TAH-keh), that became the essential reference document for Meiji policy.

The mission's official purpose was to negotiate revision of the unequal treaties. This failed completely — the Western powers told the Japanese delegation politely but firmly that no revision would be considered until Japan had a Western-style legal code, a Western-style constitution, and a Western-style army and navy. The unofficial purpose, which became the actual purpose, was to study Western institutions and decide which ones Japan should copy. On this front the mission was extraordinarily successful.

The lessons the delegates drew from the trip were specific and consequential:

Britain was the model for industry and naval power, but its political institutions (an unwritten constitution, a parliamentary system in which the cabinet was responsible to the legislature) were judged unsuitable for Japan — too anarchic, too dependent on local political culture.

The United States was admired for its technological dynamism but dismissed as politically inappropriate — a republic, no monarch, a constitutional structure that the delegates considered too radical.

France had had four constitutional regimes in eighty years and was undergoing the Paris Commune just before they arrived. Politically a cautionary example.

Germany under **Bismarck** was the revelation. Recently unified under Prussian leadership, with a written constitution that preserved monarchical sovereignty while granting limited parliamentary participation, with a powerful conscript army, with an ascendant industrial economy, with a strong civil service tradition — Germany looked like the country Japan wanted to become. Itō Hirobumi in particular returned from Berlin convinced that the Prussian constitutional model was the right template. He would later return to Germany in 1882 specifically to study constitutional law under the legal theorists **Lorenz von Stein** and **Rudolf von Gneist**, and the constitution he drafted in 1889 (Chapter 5) would be unmistakably Prussian.

The mission also taught the delegates that the gap between Japan and the Western powers was much larger than they had thought. The shipyards at Liverpool, the factories of Manchester, the chemical works of Leverkusen, the Krupp steel cannons at Essen — these were a different order of magnitude from anything Japan possessed. The mission's most influential consequence may have been the realization that aggressive expansion abroad was insane until aggressive industrialization at home was complete. Which brings us to the Korea debate.

---

While the mission was abroad, the caretaker government in Tokyo — dominated by **Saigō Takamori** and **Itagaki Taisuke** — had decided that Japan should invade Korea. The pretext was a diplomatic insult. The Korean **Joseon dynasty** (Joseon Korea Ch 8), still nominally subject to Qing China and pursuing its own version of sakoku-style isolation, had refused to recognize the new Meiji government in 1868 on the grounds that the new regime's diplomatic note used imperial titles inappropriate to a non-Chinese power. Saigō proposed, in summer 1873, that he personally go to Seoul as imperial envoy, deliberately get himself killed (he genuinely meant this), and thereby provide casus belli for an invasion. The plan would have several useful effects, in the proponents' view: vindicating Japanese honor; testing the new conscript army; providing employment for the increasingly restive ex-samurai class; and projecting Japanese power onto the Asian continent.

The Iwakura Mission returned in September 1873, fresh from observing actual European military and industrial power, and immediately vetoed the invasion as suicidal. Japan, **Ōkubo** argued, had no business projecting power abroad until it had built the industrial and financial base to sustain such projection. The country could not afford a war with a Korea backed (potentially) by China. The conscript army was untested. The treasury was thin. The unequal treaties had not yet been revised. Modernization first; expansion second.

The hawks were defeated. **Saigō**, **Itagaki**, and several other senior officials resigned from the government in October 1873 over what is called the **Seikanron** (sei-KAHN-ron, "the Korea Debate"). Saigō went home to Kagoshima in Satsuma. Itagaki organized the Liberal Party (Chapter 5) and became the leader of the parliamentary opposition. The government's center of gravity shifted decisively to the modernizers around Ōkubo Toshimichi, who served as the central architect of Meiji policy from 1873 until his assassination in 1878.

Korea would be invaded — but not until 1894, on the modernizers' schedule, when the conscript army was a fully-equipped European-style force trained in modern infantry tactics, the navy was a fleet of British-built cruisers, and the country had the industrial base to sustain a major war (Chapter 7). Then Korea would be annexed, in 1910, on the modernizers' schedule, when the country had defeated both China and Russia in successive wars and had British backing for the annexation (Chapter 8). The hawks of 1873 had wanted an invasion that would have failed and probably destroyed the regime. The modernizers waited two decades and got a successful annexation that built an empire.

The cost of the wait was Saigō Takamori, the most popular man in Japan, sitting in Kagoshima nursing his grievances and gradually becoming the focal point for every disaffected ex-samurai in the country. That is the next chapter.

---

Cross-civ check: the Meiji dismantling of feudalism in 1868–73 has obvious comparative interest, because the process happened — and largely failed — at roughly the same time in two other major Asian states. The Qing **Self-Strengthening Movement** (1861–95) tried to modernize Chinese arsenals, shipyards, and education without touching the social structure or political institutions of the Qing dynasty. It produced significant achievements — a modern Chinese navy, a few arsenals, the first Chinese railways, the first modern schools — but the underlying structure of imperial bureaucracy, hereditary banner privileges, and gentry-landlord rural domination was preserved. When the modernized Chinese navy met the modernized Japanese navy at the Yalu River in 1894 (Chapter 7), the Chinese navy was destroyed.

The Ottoman **Tanzimat** reforms (1839–76; Ottoman Empire Ch 9) were more ambitious — they aimed at legal equality of all Ottoman subjects regardless of religion, modernization of the army and the bureaucracy, and a written constitution. The Tanzimat produced the 1876 **Ottoman constitution**, which was suspended within two years by Sultan Abdülhamid II. The Ottoman Empire kept modernizing in fits and starts but never accomplished anything like the full institutional restructuring Japan undertook in the 1870s.

The closest comparison, in some ways, is **Peter the Great's Russia** of the early 1700s. Peter — like the Meiji oligarchs — visited the West, returned convinced that wholesale institutional borrowing was necessary, and dragged a reluctant aristocratic society into European-style absolutism, conscription, navy-building, and a new capital city. Peter and the Meiji oligarchs both faced the basic question of whether catch-up modernization was even possible for a non-Western state, and both answered yes, at the price of treating the existing institutional structure as raw material to be smashed and rebuilt. The differences are also instructive: Peter modernized to the level of Sweden and ended up the strongest power in eastern Europe; the Meiji oligarchs modernized to the level of Britain and ended up briefly the strongest power in the Pacific. Both projects used external pressure as both a justification and a deadline. Both worked.

What Japan accomplished in the years between Haihan Chiken in 1871 and the Iwakura Mission's return in 1873 was the demolition phase. Construction was about to begin.

# Chapter 3 — The Satsuma Rebellion

The most popular man in Japan in 1873 was **Saigō Takamori**, the Satsuma samurai who had led the imperial forces at Toba-Fushimi in 1868, accepted the bloodless surrender of Edo from Katsu Kaishū later that same year, and personified, more than any other Meiji founder, the moral seriousness of the Restoration. He was a large man — close to six feet tall and barrel-chested in a country whose average male height was below five feet five — with heavy eyebrows, a booming laugh, and a personal style of immense Confucian rectitude. He was the one Meiji oligarch ordinary Japanese felt they understood. He was also, by 1873, in profound disagreement with what the Restoration he had created was becoming.

In October 1873, after losing the Korea debate to Ōkubo Toshimichi and the Iwakura Mission returnees, Saigō resigned from the government and went home to **Kagoshima**, the Satsuma capital on the southern Kyushu coast. Six hundred Satsuma officers and soldiers in Tokyo resigned with him and followed him south. He was forty-six years old. He would be dead in four years. His death, and the destruction of the rebellion he led, would mark the end of the warrior class in Japanese politics — which was, paradoxically, the thing he was fighting to preserve.

This chapter is about the **Satsuma Rebellion** of 1877 and what it meant. It is the shortest chapter in this book, because the events are simple. But it deserves its own chapter because the political and symbolic stakes were so large. The conscript army of peasants would defeat the last samurai army at **Shiroyama**. After that, no one could pretend that the modernization of Japan was not also the dismantling of the seven-century-old warrior politics that had defined Japanese society since the late twelfth century.

---

Saigō, back in Kagoshima, did not initially intend to lead a rebellion. He spent the first two years (1874–76) running a network of private military academies — the **Shigakkō** ("Private Schools") — that taught Confucian ethics, traditional swordsmanship, and modern European infantry tactics to roughly 20,000 disaffected young samurai. Satsuma was the one prefecture where the Meiji reforms had only been imposed on paper; the local government, dominated by Saigō's old comrades and former retainers, had quietly maintained samurai stipends, kept domain weapons stockpiles intact, and refused to enforce the haitōrei sword-abolition edict. By 1876 Satsuma was effectively an autonomous samurai mini-state inside a country that had legally abolished samurai status.

Smaller samurai uprisings broke out elsewhere in western Japan over the same period. The **Saga Rebellion** of 1874 in Hizen, led by the disaffected ex-councilor **Etō Shinpei**, was put down within weeks; Etō was executed. The **Shinpūren Revolt** ("League of the Divine Wind") of 1876 in Kumamoto saw a small band of 170 ultra-traditionalist samurai attack the local conscript garrison, kill several officers, and be wiped out within hours. The **Akizuki Rebellion** and the **Hagi Rebellion**, both also in 1876, followed similar patterns: small groups of ex-samurai attacking modern garrisons, defeated quickly, leaders executed. The pattern was clear: localized samurai resistance was futile against the new conscript army. But the pattern would not stay localized if Saigō moved.

The Meiji government in Tokyo, watching Satsuma with mounting alarm, sent secret agents to assess the situation and (according to confessions later extracted from the agents) to assassinate Saigō if necessary. The Satsuma Shigakkō captured the agents in late January 1877. The agents confessed under torture. Whether the Tokyo government had actually authorized assassination is debated — Ōkubo Toshimichi, who almost certainly would have known, denied it for the rest of his life — but the perception of assassination plot was enough. Around the same time, the central government attempted to remove the weapons stockpiles from the Satsuma arsenals, presumably to prevent them being used in any future rising. A Shigakkō faction raided the arsenals first to secure the weapons. By early February 1877, both sides were committed.

Saigō, presented with the situation by his lieutenants, formally accepted leadership of the rebellion in mid-February 1877. The official pretext was the alleged assassination plot, on which he proposed to march to Tokyo to "ask questions of the government." His actual program, insofar as he had one, was probably a kind of restorationism within the Restoration — a return to a Japan of samurai virtue, Confucian ethics, and traditional military forms, modernized only enough to be defensible. He had no clear constitutional vision. He had a moral stance and an army.

---

The army was about 30,000 strong at peak, drawn from the Shigakkō networks and from samurai sympathizers across western Kyushu. They were armed with a mix of Western rifles (mostly older Enfield muskets, some Snider breechloaders), some artillery, and traditional swords. They were well-drilled, brave, and absolutely committed to the rebellion's success. They were also, at the strategic level, hopeless.

The first objective was the imperial garrison at **Kumamoto Castle**, the largest fortress in central Kyushu, defended by a conscript force of 4,000 under General **Tani Tateki**. Saigō moved north from Kagoshima in late February with 12,000 troops and reached Kumamoto on February 22. He laid siege. The defenders had been alerted and had fortified the castle thoroughly. The Satsuma siege lasted fifty-four days. The samurai army threw itself repeatedly at the castle walls and was repeatedly broken by defensive fire. The conscript garrison held.

While Kumamoto was being besieged, the central government mobilized roughly 60,000 conscript troops under **Yamagata Aritomo** and shipped them by rail and steamer to Kyushu. The relief force broke the Satsuma blockade in mid-April. Saigō's army was forced to retreat south, harried at every step by larger and better-equipped imperial columns. The retreat became a six-month running fight through the mountains and rice paddies of central and southern Kyushu. The Satsuma forces fought brilliantly at the tactical level — the samurai army in retreat could still inflict serious casualties on conscript units that closed too quickly — but they were progressively boxed in, cut off from reinforcement, starved of ammunition. By August 1877, Saigō's force had been reduced from 30,000 to about 3,000. By September it was down to 400.

On September 24, 1877, the last 400 Satsuma rebels were cornered on **Shiroyama** ("Castle Mountain"), a small hill in Kagoshima where Saigō had grown up. The imperial force surrounding them numbered roughly 30,000. The final Satsuma assault began at dawn. Saigō was hit in the hip and the thigh by a bullet around six in the morning. According to the standard version, he turned to one of his lieutenants, **Beppu Shinsuke**, and asked him to perform the role of second in **seppuku** — the ritual disembowelment that closed the warrior life of a defeated samurai. Beppu agreed. Saigō knelt facing east toward the Imperial Palace in Tokyo, drew his short sword, and committed seppuku. Beppu cut off his head with a single stroke, hid it in a hole in the hillside, and led the surviving rebels in a final suicidal charge into the imperial guns. By eight in the morning the rebellion was over.

Saigō's head was found later that day. The imperial commander, General **Yamagata Aritomo** — Saigō's old comrade from the Boshin War, the architect of the conscript army that had just destroyed him — washed the head personally and arranged its burial. The standard account says Yamagata wept.

---

The casualty totals from the rebellion were heavy: roughly 7,000 imperial dead and wounded, perhaps 20,000 Satsuma dead and wounded out of the original 30,000-strong force. The financial cost was enormous — the suppression had absorbed a large portion of the central government's annual revenue and pushed the early Meiji budget into emergency taxation. But the political result was decisive in two ways.

First, the conscript army had won. The peasants-turned-soldiers, drilled in European tactics, had defeated the most fanatical samurai force the country could field. The seven-century-old assumption that warriors fought wars and peasants tended fields had been overturned in the most public and bloody possible way. After Shiroyama, no one could pretend that the Meiji reforms were optional or reversible. The Satsuma Rebellion was the last samurai uprising. There would be smaller acts of resistance — the assassination of Ōkubo Toshimichi by six Satsuma sympathizers in May 1878, in particular — but no more armed revolts of significant scale.

Second, Saigō's death made him a permanent national hero, which the government immediately understood it had to manage. The official posthumous treatment of Saigō was a masterpiece of political symbolism: he was officially rehabilitated by imperial pardon in 1889, his statues erected in Tokyo (including the famous one in Ueno Park, dressed in commoner clothes walking his dog, that still stands), and his image absorbed into the official iconography of Meiji loyalty. The standard formula of the rehabilitation was that Saigō had not actually rebelled against the emperor — he had been "deceived" by his lieutenants into a misguided protest, and his fundamental loyalty to imperial Japan was beyond question. This was face-saving for the government and, from a certain angle, for Saigō. It also defanged the rebellion politically — once Saigō was a martyred imperial loyalist rather than a defeated samurai rebel, no future opposition movement could claim him as a precedent for resisting the Meiji state.

The legend that grew up around Saigō was complicated. He became, simultaneously, the symbol of the loyal subject who served the emperor unto death, and the symbol of the traditional Japanese values being lost to Westernization. His ghost would be invoked by every subsequent generation of conservative critics of Meiji modernization — including, eventually, the militarists of the 1930s, who claimed Saigō's example as justification for their own moral seriousness about overthrowing what they considered corrupt civilian governments. The 2003 Tom Cruise film *The Last Samurai* is a (heavily fictionalized) version of the Saigō story; the historical Saigō never met any American military advisors and was not seeking to preserve traditional samurai culture against Westernization so much as to preserve the political position of his class within a Japan that was westernizing too fast. But the broad outlines — the moral seriousness, the doomed last stand, the seppuku at the moment of defeat — are accurate.

---

The deeper meaning of the Satsuma Rebellion is captured in a single image: General Yamagata Aritomo, the Chōshū samurai who had spent his career building the conscript army, weeping over the head of Saigō Takamori, the Satsuma samurai who had spent his career trying to preserve the warrior tradition the conscript army was replacing. The two men had been comrades a decade earlier, fighting on the same side at Toba-Fushimi against the bakufu. They had taken opposite turns at the moment when the Meiji state had to decide whether it was building a modern conscript army or preserving the samurai class. That decision had, in 1877, taken the form of one of them killing the other.

Yamagata went on to dominate Japanese military politics for the next forty years. Saigō went into the national mythology. The conscript army Yamagata built would defeat China in 1894, defeat Russia in 1905, occupy Korea in 1910, invade Manchuria in 1931, and finally collapse in 1945. The samurai tradition Saigō embodied would persist as a romantic memory and as an ideological resource that the militarists of the twentieth century would draw on freely.

The Restoration had begun in the name of imperial loyalty and traditional Japanese values. By 1877 it had become clear that imperial loyalty and traditional Japanese values, in any honest reading, were incompatible with the institutional changes the Restoration required. The government chose the institutional changes and rebranded them as imperial loyalty. The samurai who refused the rebrand were defeated militarily and absorbed into the national myth. The Japan that emerged was something neither the Tokugawa nor Saigō Takamori would have recognized as Japan.

That Japan is the subject of the next four chapters.

Cross-civ check: the closest comparison to Saigō's last stand at Shiroyama is probably the **Boxer Rebellion** of 1900 in Qing China — another doomed traditional uprising against modernizing pressure, also rooted in profound disgust at foreign influence and the dissolution of a traditional warrior class, also defeated by modern military force (in that case, an Eight-Nation Alliance). The structural parallel is striking: in both cases, an indigenous traditional movement attempted to reverse the dissolution of the old order, was crushed militarily, and was then absorbed into national mythology by the very modernizing regime that had defeated it. The difference is that the Qing court tried both to use the Boxers and to suppress them, and ended up doing neither well; the Meiji government simply destroyed Saigō's movement without trying to use it, and rehabilitated him only after his physical defeat was complete. Cleaner politics, harsher methods, more enduring result.

# Chapter 4 — Industrial Revolution by Decree

By 1877, with the Satsuma Rebellion crushed and the central government's military monopoly secure, the Meiji oligarchs could turn to the deeper problem: building an industrial economy from approximately scratch.

The country they were starting from had no railways outside the eighteen miles between Tokyo and Yokohama, no telegraph network beyond a few major cities, no modern banks, no national currency older than six years, no joint-stock corporations to speak of, no integrated steel mill anywhere in Asia, no mechanical textile production beyond a handful of state pilot plants, no shipyard capable of building ironclad warships, no chemical industry, no machine-tool industry, and almost no engineers. By 1900, twenty-three years later, Japan had a national rail network, a telegraph system reaching every prefectural capital, a modern central bank, an integrated steel mill at **Yawata**, the world's largest silk-textile export sector, a domestic shipbuilding industry capable of producing modern warships, four major industrial conglomerates (the **zaibatsu**), a national gold standard, and a generation of engineers trained at home and abroad. The transformation was the most rapid industrialization any country had ever attempted, and it was managed almost entirely by a state that combined developmentalist planning with aggressive privatization once each industry was viable.

This chapter is about how it happened.

---

The basic Meiji approach to industrialization was a three-step pattern that economists now call the **developmentalist state model**: the government built pilot plants in every key sector, demonstrated that the technology could work in Japanese conditions, then sold the plants cheaply to politically connected merchant houses that operated them at scale. The pattern was applied across silk, cotton, mining, shipbuilding, glass, cement, sugar, and paper. The state took the financial and technical risk of the initial investment; the private sector took the operating profits once the technology was proven. The state then collected taxes on the resulting industrial growth and recycled the revenue into the next pilot plant. The cycle ran continuously from the early 1870s through the early 1900s.

The flagship pilot plant was the **Tomioka Silk Mill**, opened in June 1872 in Gunma Prefecture northwest of Tokyo. It was built with French machinery, designed and run by the French engineer **Paul Brunat** under contract with the Meiji finance ministry, and staffed by 400 young women recruited from samurai families across Japan. The mill's purpose was to demonstrate that Japanese workers could operate Western mechanical filatures (silk-reeling machines) at international quality standards, which they did. Within a decade, dozens of private silk mills had opened across Japan modeled on Tomioka. By 1900 Japan was the world's largest silk exporter; by 1914, raw silk accounted for roughly 30% of Japanese export earnings. Silk built the early Meiji economy.

Why silk? Because Japan had three structural advantages: a long indigenous tradition of sericulture (silkworm cultivation) dating back to the Asuka period (Ancient Japan Ch 4), a labor force of young rural women willing to work for very low wages, and an immediate global market created by the European silkworm-disease epidemic of the 1860s that had devastated French and Italian sericulture. Japanese silk filled the gap. The export earnings from silk were then recycled — through the central government's tax base and through the merchant networks that traded silk — into the next round of industrial investment.

Cotton textiles were the second wave. The first modern cotton mill was the **Kagoshima Spinning Mill**, opened in 1867 by the Satsuma domain (before the Restoration) using British machinery imported through the Glover Trading Company. The Meiji state established several pilot mills in the 1870s. By the 1880s, private cotton mills around **Osaka** were producing yarn at competitive international prices. The **Osaka Spinning Mill**, founded in 1882 by the future industrialist **Shibusawa Eiichi**, was the first Japanese cotton mill to operate profitably at scale. By 1900, Japanese cotton textiles dominated Asian markets and were beginning to compete in India and Manchuria with British exports. The cotton-textile boom of the 1880s and 1890s created the urban industrial working class that would shape Japanese politics for the next century.

---

Heavy industry came later and was harder. The Tokugawa-era domain arsenals at **Yokosuka** and **Nagasaki** had built small ironclad ships in the 1860s, but Japan had no integrated steel production at the scale needed for railways, naval construction, or modern weaponry until the **Yawata Steel Works** opened in northern Kyushu in 1901. Yawata was state-funded, German-equipped (much of the machinery came from Krupp), and designed by German engineers under contract to the Meiji finance ministry. Within a decade it produced 80% of Japan's steel — the strategic asset that finally made the navy and the railways independent of imports. The mill is still operating today. It is a UNESCO World Heritage site.

Shipbuilding ran in parallel. The **Mitsubishi Nagasaki Shipyard**, founded in 1884 when the Mitsubishi conglomerate purchased the former bakufu shipyard at Nagasaki cheap from the government, built the country's first modern steel warships in the 1890s. By 1905, Mitsubishi Nagasaki had built Japanese cruisers and battleships that fought at Tsushima (Chapter 7). The **Kobe Shipyard**, founded by Mitsubishi in 1905 to produce commercial vessels, became one of the largest shipyards in Asia within a decade. The state-private partnership ran in shipbuilding the same way it ran in steel: government contracts secured the financing, private operation handled the building.

Coal mining followed the same pattern. The **Takashima Coal Mine** in Nagasaki, taken over by Mitsubishi in 1881 from the bankrupt government-run operation, became the largest coal producer in Japan by 1900. The **Miike Coal Mine** in Fukuoka, sold to Mitsui in 1888, was for decades the most profitable single industrial operation in Japan. Both mines used convict labor and brutal contracting practices; both produced the coal that fueled the national rail network, the navy, and the steel mills.

---

The political face of all this was the **zaibatsu** ("financial clique"), the four great industrial conglomerates that emerged from the developmentalist state model. These were:

**Mitsui** (MIT-soo-ee), the oldest of the four, descended from a Kyoto kimono merchant house that had been one of the wealthiest commercial families of the Edo period. Mitsui survived the Restoration intact, financed the new government in 1868, and became the founding bank, mining, and trading conglomerate of Meiji Japan.

**Mitsubishi** (mit-soo-BEE-shee), founded by **Iwasaki Yatarō**, a low-rank Tosa samurai who had served as the Tosa domain's commercial agent in the late 1860s. Iwasaki founded Mitsubishi as a shipping company in 1873, contracted with the Meiji government to ship troops to Taiwan in 1874 and to Kyushu during the Satsuma Rebellion of 1877, and parlayed the government contracts into shipbuilding, mining, banking, and trading ventures. By the 1890s, Mitsubishi was the second-largest zaibatsu after Mitsui.

**Sumitomo** (soo-mee-TOH-moh), descended from the **Besshi Copper Mine** operation that the Sumitomo family had run in Shikoku since the 1690s. The Edo-era family business survived the Restoration, modernized its mining operations with European technology in the 1870s and 1880s, and expanded into banking, chemicals, and metals through the 1890s.

**Yasuda** (yah-SOO-dah), founded by **Yasuda Zenjirō**, an ex-samurai turned money-changer who built the largest banking network in Meiji Japan starting in 1879.

Below the four giant zaibatsu sat several smaller industrial-financial groupings: **Furukawa**, **Shibusawa Eiichi's** banking and textile network, the **Asano** cement-and-shipping group, the **Suzuki** trading house. By 1900, these conglomerates collectively controlled most of Japanese heavy industry, modern shipping, and commercial banking. By the 1930s they would control roughly 75% of the Japanese economy. The post-1945 American occupation would attempt to break them up; most of them would reorganize themselves as the **keiretsu** business groups that still dominate the Japanese economy today. The 1872 finance ministry decisions about how to structure the developmentalist state cast a long shadow.

---

The financial infrastructure that made all of this possible was built under the direction of one man: **Matsukata Masayoshi** (mat-soo-KAH-tah mah-sah-YOH-shee), a Satsuma samurai who served as finance minister from 1881 to 1898 with brief interruptions and as prime minister twice. Matsukata's accomplishments are technical but indispensable.

The early Meiji government had paid for the dismantling of feudalism (the samurai bond conversions of 1876) and the suppression of the Satsuma Rebellion (1877) by printing paper money. By 1880 the Japanese economy was in serious inflation, the yen had depreciated against silver, and the government's finances were precarious. Matsukata, on becoming finance minister in 1881, pursued a deliberate deflation: he raised taxes, cut government spending, and withdrew paper currency from circulation. The deflation was painful — agricultural prices crashed, rural debt soared, peasant land was expropriated by creditors at unprecedented rates — but it stabilized the yen by 1885.

In 1882, Matsukata founded the **Bank of Japan** on the Belgian central-bank model. The Bank of Japan became the country's note-issuing central bank, the lender of last resort, and the institutional foundation of modern Japanese finance. Matsukata then negotiated the conversion of the Japanese currency to a silver standard in 1886, then to the **gold standard** in 1897, using the indemnity payments from the Sino-Japanese War (Chapter 7) to fund the gold reserve. By 1900, Japan was on the international gold standard, integrated into the London-centered global financial system on the same terms as the European powers. Capital could flow into Japanese industry; Japanese government bonds could be sold in London and Paris. The Russo-Japanese War of 1904–05 (Chapter 7) would be financed partly by foreign loans floated in London and New York, made possible by Matsukata's monetary architecture.

The cost of the Matsukata deflation, in agricultural distress and rural land expropriation, was severe and is sometimes underplayed in popular accounts of the Meiji success story. Roughly 40% of Japanese farmland was tenanted by 1900, up from about 20% in 1872. The surplus rural population either migrated to the new urban industrial centers (where they fed the cotton mills and the coal mines) or emigrated overseas to Hawaii, California, Brazil, and Peru. Japanese emigration was officially licensed from 1885; roughly 200,000 Japanese left for the Americas during the Meiji period, founding the diasporic communities that exist today. The Meiji industrial revolution was real, but it was paid for in large part by the Japanese rural population, whose land tax funded the developmentalist state and whose displaced sons and daughters supplied the factory labor.

---

The infrastructure layer connected the industries.

Railways: the first 18-mile Tokyo-Yokohama line opened in 1872, the second line (Osaka-Kobe) in 1874, the third (Kyoto-Osaka) in 1877, the trunk line connecting Tokyo to Osaka via Nagoya completed in 1889. By 1890 the country had roughly 1,400 miles of track; by 1900, 3,600 miles; by 1910, over 5,000 miles. The 1906 **Railway Nationalization Law** consolidated the major private rail companies into the state-owned **Japanese Government Railways**, a network that, with successor reorganizations, still operates as **Japan Railways** today.

Telegraph: the first Tokyo-Yokohama line of 1869 expanded to a national network of 8,000 miles by 1880, 32,000 miles by 1900. Telephones followed: a Tokyo-Yokohama experimental line in 1877, public telephone service in Tokyo and Yokohama from 1890, a national network by the early 1900s.

Postal service: organized by **Maejima Hisoka** in 1871 on the British model, the modern postal service had 4,000 post offices by 1880 and 7,000 by 1900. Uniform rates, postage stamps, and door-to-door delivery extended state communication infrastructure to every village in the country. The postal savings system, founded 1875, channeled small-saver deposits into government bonds and industrial finance — by 1900 it held more deposits than all the private banks combined.

Roads, harbors, water systems, urban gas lighting, electrification (Tokyo's first electric utility opened in 1887), public sewers in the major cities — every category of infrastructure that defines a modern industrial economy was rolled out across Meiji Japan in the thirty years between 1872 and 1902. The combined investment was enormous; the state directly financed perhaps 40% of it, with the zaibatsu and provincial governments handling the rest.

---

By 1900, the gross numbers were beginning to look comparable to second-tier European economies. Japanese industrial output had roughly tripled since 1880. Cotton textile production exceeded that of British India. Silk exports were the largest in the world. Steel production was small but growing rapidly. The merchant marine had quadrupled in tonnage. The number of factory workers had grown from about 50,000 in 1880 to over 500,000 by 1900. Japanese industrial cities — Osaka, Nagoya, Kobe, Yokohama, the new industrial city of Yawata — were growing at rates comparable to American cities of the period.

Per-capita income was still well below Western European levels, but the rate of growth was the fastest in the world. The rate matters more than the level. By 1900, Japan was on a trajectory that would, within twenty more years, make it a fully industrialized economy capable of supporting a fleet that fought the Russians at Tsushima, an army that fought the Russians at Mukden, and a colonial empire stretching from Taiwan to Korea to Manchuria.

The Meiji oligarchs in 1900 were almost all dead or aging — Ōkubo had been assassinated in 1878, Kido had died of overwork the same year as Saigō, Iwakura was dead by 1883. The next generation of Japanese leaders was ready to take over. They had been trained in the new universities, served in the new bureaucracy, traveled to the new colonies. Some of them had been born after the Restoration. The Japan they would govern was the country the Meiji oligarchs had built.

That country needed a constitution. Which is the next chapter.

Cross-civ check: the speed of Meiji industrialization can really only be compared to a handful of other historical episodes. Britain's industrial revolution (roughly 1760–1840) took eighty years; Germany's catch-up (roughly 1840–1900) took sixty years; Japan's catch-up (roughly 1872–1910) took thirty-eight years. The Soviet Union's first Five-Year Plans (1928–37) and the Chinese Communist industrialization of the 1950s would later compress similar transformations into a decade through ruthless coercion. The South Korean and Taiwanese miracles of the 1960s–80s built explicitly on the Meiji model, including the developmentalist-state pattern of pilot plants and zaibatsu-style conglomerates. So did the **rise of post-Mao Chinese** industry from the 1980s onward. The Meiji template — state direction of investment, strategic protection of pilot industries, recycled export earnings, tight integration of finance and industry — is one of the most-copied institutional designs in twentieth-century economic history. Almost every major late industrializer studied it, and most of them adopted some version of it. What the Meiji oligarchs invented in the 1870s out of desperation became the standard operating manual for late industrialization for the next 150 years.

# Chapter 5 — The Constitution and the Diet

The Meiji oligarchs had a problem by the early 1880s: they had built a modern administrative state, a modern conscript army, a modern industrial economy, and a modern finance system. They had not built a modern constitution. The Western powers, who had told the Iwakura Mission in 1872 that no treaty revision was possible without Japan having a written constitution and a representative legislature, were still saying the same thing fifteen years later. Domestic political pressure for a parliament was rising — the **Freedom and People's Rights Movement** (Jiyū Minken Undō) was holding mass rallies and pamphleteering across the country. And the senior oligarchs themselves understood that some kind of representative institutional framework was eventually necessary, both for international legitimacy and for managing the long-term political stability of the modernized state. The question was what form the framework should take.

This chapter is about how the Meiji oligarchs designed a constitution that gave Japan the appearance of constitutional monarchy while keeping real political power in the hands of the founding oligarchs themselves. The 1889 **Meiji Constitution** was a remarkable document. It worked exactly as intended for forty-five years. It also created the structural cracks through which the militarism of the 1930s would eventually flood. Both effects deserve careful attention.

---

The Freedom and People's Rights Movement began in January 1874, when **Itagaki Taisuke** and seven other Tosa-faction officials — most of them recently resigned over the Korea debate (Chapter 2) — submitted a memorial to the government demanding the establishment of an elected national assembly. The memorial argued, in classic mid-nineteenth-century liberal terms, that taxation without representation was tyrannical, that the new state needed a popular legislative body to counterbalance the bureaucratic executive, and that without such a body the Meiji government was simply a more efficient version of the autocracy it had replaced.

The memorial was rejected, but the movement it launched grew steadily through the 1870s and 1880s. Itagaki founded the **Liberal Party** (Jiyūtō) in 1881, drawing support from rural landlords, ex-samurai, and the small but growing urban middle class. The agriculturalist intellectual **Ōkuma Shigenobu**, an ex-Hizen oligarch who had broken with the central government in 1881 over fiscal policy, founded the rival **Constitutional Progressive Party** (Rikken Kaishintō) in 1882, drawing support from urban commercial interests. Both parties demanded an elected parliament, a written constitution, civil liberties, and the gradual transition of Japan to a Western-style parliamentary government.

The movement was harassed by the government but never crushed. Press censorship laws (the 1875 Press Ordinance and the 1880 Public Meeting Ordinance) restricted what the parties could publish and how they could organize, but the parties survived. Itagaki himself was stabbed by a fanatic in 1882 and is supposed to have shouted, while bleeding on the ground, "Itagaki may die, but liberty never!" — a line probably embellished but indispensable to Japanese constitutional folklore.

The government's response was a calculated concession. In 1881, in what is called the **Imperial Decree of 1881**, Emperor Meiji formally promised that a constitution would be promulgated and a parliament convened by 1890. This bought the government nine years to design both institutions on its own terms. **Itō Hirobumi**, the Chōshū intellectual who had emerged as the Meiji oligarchy's senior political tactician, was put in charge of the design. He immediately departed for Europe.

---

Itō's eighteen-month constitutional study tour of 1882–83 was the diagnostic exercise on which the Meiji Constitution depended. Like the Iwakura Mission a decade earlier, it was a comparative survey of European institutional designs. Unlike the Iwakura Mission, it had a single specific goal: identify the constitutional model best suited to preserving oligarchic power in a country that needed the appearance of constitutional government.

Itō spent most of his time in Berlin and Vienna studying with two German constitutional theorists: **Lorenz von Stein** at the University of Vienna and **Rudolf von Gneist** at the University of Berlin. Both men taught a version of constitutional monarchy that had been worked out in the German states in the decades before German unification: a written constitution that preserved monarchical sovereignty as the constitutional foundation; an elected lower house with limited legislative powers; an appointed upper house; a cabinet appointed by and responsible to the monarch rather than to the legislature; an army and navy reporting directly to the monarch outside the cabinet's control; and a strong civil service operating under bureaucratic norms inherited from the absolutist period. This was Bismarck's Germany, basically. It was the constitutional model of a state that wanted to look modern to international observers while preserving the autocratic power of the existing ruling elite.

Itō came back to Tokyo in 1883 with the model fully formed in his head and spent the next six years drafting the constitution and the supporting institutional architecture. The major preparatory steps were:

**1884:** The **Peerage Act** created a new five-rank European-style nobility (prince, marquis, count, viscount, baron), to be drawn from the former daimyō, court nobles, and Meiji oligarchs. The new peerage would form the membership pool for the upper house.

**1885:** The **Cabinet System** replaced the older Council of State (Dajōkan) with a European-style cabinet of ministers, each running a department, headed by a prime minister. Itō Hirobumi became Japan's first prime minister on December 22, 1885.

**1886:** The **Imperial University Ordinance** established Tokyo Imperial University as the elite training institution for the new bureaucratic civil service. Subsequent imperial universities at Kyoto, Tōhoku, Kyushu, Hokkaido, Osaka, and Nagoya would form the educational backbone of the bureaucratic state.

**1888:** The **Privy Council** was created as a constitutional advisory body to the emperor, dominated by the senior oligarchs.

The constitution itself was drafted in secret by Itō and a small team — **Inoue Kowashi**, **Itō Miyoji**, **Kaneko Kentarō**, and the German legal advisor **Hermann Roesler** — between 1886 and 1888. The drafters consciously borrowed from the Prussian constitution of 1850, the Belgian constitution of 1831, and elements of the Japanese imperial tradition.

---

The **Meiji Constitution** was promulgated on **February 11, 1889** — the date chosen to coincide with **Kigensetsu**, the legendary anniversary of the founding of the Japanese imperial line by **Emperor Jimmu** in 660 BCE (a date that almost certainly never happened, but which had been canonized by the kokugaku tradition; Ancient Japan Ch 3). The constitution was presented to the public as a "gift from the emperor" to the Japanese people, given by the imperial sovereign of his own free will rather than negotiated with any representative body. The framing was important. The Japanese people had not granted themselves a constitution; they had received one from above, and the relationship of the constitution to imperial sovereignty was therefore the opposite of the American or French models.

The substance of the constitution had seven major features:

**Imperial sovereignty.** Article 1: "The Empire of Japan shall be reigned over and governed by a line of Emperors unbroken for ages eternal." Article 4: "The Emperor is the head of the Empire, combining in Himself the rights of sovereignty." The emperor was the source of all sovereignty; he held legislative, executive, and judicial power; the constitution itself derived from his grant. He could dissolve the Diet, command the military, declare war, sign treaties, and rule by imperial ordinance in emergencies.

**A bicameral Diet.** The lower house (House of Representatives) was elected; the upper house (House of Peers) was appointed from the new peerage. The Diet had legislative power but could be dissolved by the emperor. The cabinet was appointed by the emperor (in practice, by the genrō; see below) and was not responsible to the Diet — a parliamentary majority could not bring down a cabinet, and a Diet hostile to the cabinet could be dissolved and replaced by a new election.

**Limited suffrage.** The first electoral law restricted the vote to adult males paying at least 15 yen per year in direct national taxes — about 1% of the population, roughly 450,000 voters in a country of 40 million. This was, by design, a property-restricted franchise heavily weighted toward landlords and the urban commercial class. The franchise was gradually expanded — to about 2.2% in 1900, about 5.5% in 1919 — and finally made universal for adult males in 1925.

**A constitutional bill of rights.** Articles 18 through 32 enumerated rights of Japanese subjects: freedom of religion, freedom of speech, freedom of association, the right to property, the right of petition, due process. All were qualified by the formula "within limits not prejudicial to peace and order and not antagonistic to their duties as subjects." In practice, these qualifications gave the government wide latitude to suppress political activity it considered subversive.

**Independent military command.** The army and navy reported directly to the emperor through the chiefs of the General Staff and the Naval General Staff. The cabinet had no constitutional authority over military operations, military budgets (in detail), or military appointments. The minister of war and the minister of the navy were required to be active-duty generals or admirals respectively. This was the so-called "right of supreme command" (tōsuiken), and it created a structural separation between the civilian government and the military that would prove catastrophic in the 1930s, when the army could (and did) initiate military operations in Manchuria without civilian authorization.

**A strong civil service.** The bureaucracy was constitutionally insulated from political control. Senior civil servants were recruited from imperial-university graduates through competitive examination, served under bureaucratic norms inherited from the absolutist period, and were not subject to political appointment or removal. The Meiji bureaucracy was a permanent professional governing class largely independent of the political parties.

**The genrō.** This was not in the constitution at all, but it was the most important institutional fact about how the Meiji state actually worked. The **genrō** ("elder statesmen") were the unofficial council of senior Meiji founders — initially Itō Hirobumi, Yamagata Aritomo, Inoue Kaoru, Kuroda Kiyotaka, Matsukata Masayoshi, Saigō Tsugumichi (Saigō Takamori's younger brother), and Ōyama Iwao — who selected prime ministers, vetoed cabinet decisions, advised the emperor on major policy, and effectively governed Japan from outside the constitution they had written. The genrō institution had no legal standing. It depended on the personal authority of the founders. As they aged and died (Itō was assassinated in 1909, Yamagata died in 1922, Matsukata in 1924), the institution decayed, and no equivalent successor body emerged. The 1920s saw the genrō functions partially transferred to the **jūshin** (senior court advisors) and to elected party governments. The 1930s saw both decay further, until the field marshals and admirals could effectively bypass the civilian government and run national policy from inside the army and navy ministries.

The Meiji Constitution was, in short, a constitutional monarchy on the Prussian model — a written constitution that preserved monarchical sovereignty while granting limited parliamentary participation, with real political power concentrated in an oligarchic council operating outside the formal constitutional structure. It was the most successful instance of this design in nineteenth-century world history. It was also the most successful failure mode: when the founding oligarchs died and the genrō decayed, the constitutional structure became hollow, and the only remaining cohesive institution with the right to "advise" the emperor and the constitutional independence to act on its own initiative was the military. By the 1930s, the structural cracks the founders had built into the constitution had become the channels through which militarism flooded into politics.

---

The first **Imperial Diet** convened on **November 29, 1890**, with the boy-emperor (now thirty-eight, but still ceremonially "the young emperor") presiding over the opening session. The lower house contained 300 members elected by the propertied 1% of the population; the upper House of Peers contained 250 members appointed from the new peerage and the imperial appointments. The first session immediately produced a confrontation between the elected Liberal-Progressive opposition majority in the lower house and the Yamagata cabinet over the budget. The Diet refused to approve the military budget. Yamagata threatened dissolution. The crisis was resolved by emperor's intervention and a partial budget compromise. The pattern — Diet opposition, cabinet pressure, imperial mediation, eventual compromise — would repeat throughout the 1890s.

The early Diet was a frustrating institution for everyone involved. The opposition parties wanted real parliamentary government on the British model and were continually thwarted by the constitutional structure. The cabinet wanted a docile rubber-stamp legislature and was continually frustrated by opposition obstruction. The genrō wanted political stability and were continually called in to mediate. By 1900 a partial accommodation had emerged: Itō Hirobumi founded the **Rikken Seiyūkai** (Constitutional Government Party) in 1900 as a pro-government party that could deliver Diet majorities to support oligarchic policies, in exchange for shared patronage with the bureaucracy. The Seiyūkai dominated Japanese parliamentary politics for the next twenty-five years. The Liberal opposition collapsed into a series of short-lived parties.

By 1900, the Meiji Constitution was working as designed. The Diet existed and met. The cabinet ran the government. The bureaucracy ran the administration. The military reported to the emperor outside cabinet control. The genrō selected the prime minister. The voters voted. The emperor signed. Japan was, by every external measure, a constitutional monarchy on the European pattern. The international observers were satisfied. The treaty revisions began in earnest. In 1899 the unequal treaties' extraterritoriality clauses were finally abolished; in 1911 the last tariff restrictions were lifted. The thirty-year campaign that had been the original purpose of the Meiji project was finally complete.

What was not visible in 1900, but would become visible in 1931, was that the constitutional structure depended absolutely on the founders being alive to manage it. When they died, the structure would not survive them. The genrō would become a memory. The military would discover its constitutional independence and use it. The Diet would become a debating society powerless to restrain cabinets that had ceased to govern. The Meiji Constitution would not formally fall until 1947, when the American occupation imposed the postwar constitution. But the political system the Meiji oligarchs had built would functionally collapse by the early 1930s, and the country would slide into the disasters of 1931–45 along the constitutional fault lines the founders had built into the document in 1889.

The success and the disaster ran on the same operating system, as advertised.

Cross-civ check: the Meiji constitutional design has obvious comparative interest because so many other late-nineteenth-century non-Western states attempted similar constitutional projects with much less success. The **Ottoman Constitution of 1876** (Ottoman Empire Ch 9) was suspended within two years and not revived until 1908, by which time the empire was in terminal decline. The Qing Chinese **constitutional reforms** of 1906–11 produced the abortive 1911 constitution that fell with the Qing dynasty months later. The **Persian Constitutional Revolution** of 1905–11 produced a constitution that was repeatedly suspended by Qajar shahs and by the Russian and British occupations. None of these attempts produced a working constitutional state. The Meiji Constitution did. The reason was probably not that the Meiji document was better drafted (it wasn't, particularly) but that the Meiji oligarchs had successfully captured the central state in 1868 and had thirty years of institutional consolidation behind them by 1889. They were drafting from a position of strength; the Ottoman and Qing reformers were drafting from positions of dynastic weakness, and their constitutions could not survive the pressures they were trying to manage. The closest successful comparison is probably the **German Empire's Constitution of 1871**, which Itō had explicitly modeled the Meiji document on. Both produced functional constitutional monarchies that lasted about half a century, and both produced catastrophic militarist failures when the founding political class died and the institutional structure could not be self-sustaining without them. Japan's catastrophe came in the 1930s; Germany's came in the 1910s. The structural similarities are not coincidental.

# Chapter 6 — Education and Cultural Engineering

The Meiji oligarchs understood, more clearly than any other modernizing elite of the period, that institutional reform was not enough. To produce a modern industrial state on the schedule they were attempting, they needed to produce a population that thought in modern industrial categories. They needed soldiers willing to die in foreign wars for a state most of them had never thought of as a state. They needed factory workers willing to accept time-discipline, wage labor, and authority structures their grandparents had never encountered. They needed bureaucrats trained in technical disciplines that did not exist in Japan in 1868. They needed taxpayers who understood why they were paying. They needed, in short, a modern population — and they had to build one out of the agrarian society they had inherited.

This chapter is about the Meiji project of cultural engineering: the universal education system, the **Imperial Rescript on Education**, the elevation of **State Shinto**, the construction of **kokutai** (national essence) ideology, and the systematic destruction of Buddhist institutional infrastructure that did not fit the new model. The cultural engineering was extraordinarily successful. It produced, within one generation, a population that was literate, numerate, militarily disciplined, ideologically committed to the imperial state, and willing to die in extraordinary numbers for that state's foreign wars. It also produced the ideological substrate of the militarist catastrophe of the 1930s. As with the constitutional project of Chapter 5, the success and the disaster were the same project.

---

The educational project came first.

The 1872 **Education Order** (Gakusei) established a system of universal compulsory primary education for both sexes, organized into 53,760 elementary schools across 32,000 school districts. The structure was modeled partly on the French educational system (with its highly centralized administration), partly on the American (with its district-based local funding), and partly on the German (with its emphasis on technical training). The curriculum was a mix of traditional Japanese moral instruction and modern Western subjects: reading, writing, arithmetic, geography, history, science.

The first decade was hard. Building 53,760 elementary schools in five years required enormous local investment, much of it raised through new taxes that were unpopular with rural communities still recovering from the dismantling of feudalism. Teacher shortages were severe; the early Meiji school system relied heavily on ex-samurai and Buddhist monks recruited as teachers, neither group having any training in modern pedagogy. Enrollment was inconsistent — by 1880 only about 40% of school-age children attended classes regularly, and dropout rates were high. The 1879 **Education Order revision** loosened central control and gave more authority to local school districts, partly to reduce the cost burden on the central government.

But the project worked. By 1900, primary school enrollment had reached 80%. By 1910, 95%. By 1915, virtual universality. Japan had achieved what the United States, Britain, France, and Germany had each taken multiple generations to build: a near-universal literate population, accomplished within forty years of starting. The literacy rate by 1910 was probably the highest in the world, somewhat above contemporary Britain or France. This was the human-capital base on which the Meiji industrial revolution depended.

Above the primary system, secondary and higher education developed in parallel. The 1877 founding of **Tokyo Imperial University** by merging existing schools created the elite training institution for the new bureaucracy and professional class. Imperial universities were subsequently founded at Kyoto (1897), Tōhoku (1907), Kyushu (1911), Hokkaido (1918), and several more in the early twentieth century. These were modeled on the German university system — research-oriented, with prestigious chair-professorships, drawing on Western academic literature, hiring senior Western scholars on contract during the early years and gradually replacing them with Japanese scholars trained abroad. By 1900, the imperial universities were producing graduates competitive in international scientific research. **Kitasato Shibasaburō** (kee-tah-SAH-toh shee-bah-SAH-boo-roh), the bacteriologist who had trained under **Robert Koch** in Berlin, co-discovered the plague bacillus in Hong Kong in 1894 and developed antitoxin therapy for tetanus and diphtheria — landmark contributions to international medical science. **Nagaoka Hantarō** in physics, **Honda Kōtarō** in metallurgy, **Suzuki Umetarō** (the discoverer of vitamin B1 in 1910), and many others established Japanese science as an active contributor to international research by the early twentieth century.

Below the imperial universities sat a system of private universities — **Keio Gijuku** (founded by **Fukuzawa Yukichi** in 1858, formally chartered as Keio University in 1890); **Waseda** (founded by **Ōkuma Shigenobu** in 1882); **Meiji Gakuin**; **Dōshisha** in Kyoto, founded by **Niijima Jō** as a Christian institution; and others — that trained the journalistic, commercial, and professional middle class. The combined Meiji higher-education system, by 1900, was producing several thousand university graduates per year and was integrated into international academic networks. The traditional Confucian and Buddhist scholarly institutions of the Tokugawa period, by contrast, were marginalized within a generation. The intellectual center of gravity had shifted decisively to the new Western-style universities.

---

The man who shaped the educational system more than any other was **Mori Arinori** (MOH-ree ah-ree-NOH-ree), a Satsuma diplomat who had served as Japan's first ambassador to the United States in the 1870s and as the first Japanese diplomatic representative to Britain. As education minister from 1885 to 1889 under the first Itō cabinet, Mori designed the centralized administrative structure of the Meiji school system, the imperial-university hierarchy, and the teacher-training institutions that produced the professional teaching corps. His personal political views were liberal — he had advocated, in the 1870s, for replacing Japanese with English as the national language as a modernization measure (the proposal went nowhere) — and his administration emphasized scientific rigor and international standards in higher education.

Mori was assassinated on February 11, 1889, the day the Meiji Constitution was promulgated, by a Shinto fanatic named **Nishino Buntarō** who had decided that Mori had committed sacrilege at the **Ise Shrine** (the most sacred Shinto shrine, dedicated to Amaterasu) by lifting his cane to brush aside a sacred curtain during a tour of inspection. Whether Mori had actually done this is unclear; the story may have been entirely invented by his attacker. The assassination signaled a darker undercurrent in the cultural-engineering project that the Meiji oligarchs were building. The same population that was being educated to read, write, and contribute to a modern industrial state was also being educated to revere the imperial line, to venerate Shinto sacred objects, and to consider Western influence on traditional sacred sites a form of pollution. The two streams of Meiji ideological formation — modern technical education and traditional sacred nationalism — coexisted in tension throughout the period. The fanatic who killed Mori had absorbed both streams: he could read, having attended Meiji-era schools; he could write the manifesto explaining his action; he had also internalized the ideology of imperial-Shinto sacredness deeply enough to commit a political murder over a perceived insult to a curtain.

This combination of modern technical capability and traditional sacred nationalism is the cultural signature of Meiji-era Japan. It would prove to be the cultural signature of the militarist era as well.

---

The ideological framework that held the educational project together was given its canonical form in the **Imperial Rescript on Education** (**Kyōiku Chokugo**), issued over Emperor Meiji's signature on October 30, 1890. The Rescript was a 315-character document drafted primarily by the conservative Confucian scholar **Motoda Eifu** in collaboration with the legal theorist **Inoue Kowashi** (one of the constitutional drafters of the previous chapter). It was distributed to every elementary school in the country and was required reading at every school ceremony for the next fifty-five years.

The substance of the Rescript was a fusion of Confucian filial piety and imperial loyalty. The opening invoked the imperial line "established by Our Imperial Ancestors on a basis broad and everlasting" and called the people to "be filial to your parents, affectionate to your brothers and sisters; as husbands and wives be harmonious, as friends true; bear yourselves in modesty and moderation; extend your benevolence to all; pursue learning and cultivate arts; ... advance public good and promote common interests; always respect the Constitution and observe the laws; should emergency arise, offer yourselves courageously to the State; and thus guard and maintain the prosperity of Our Imperial Throne coeval with heaven and earth."

The key clause was "should emergency arise, offer yourselves courageously to the State." Schoolchildren were being instructed, in their formative years, to view the willing sacrifice of their lives for the imperial state as the highest moral duty. The Rescript framed this duty in Confucian language as continuous with traditional filial obligation — service to the emperor was the same obligation as service to one's parents, scaled up to the national level. The framing was effective. By the time the Sino-Japanese War broke out in 1894, Japan had a generation of soldiers who had been taught from primary school that dying for the emperor was the natural extension of normal moral conduct. By 1937, when Japan invaded China, the second generation of Imperial-Rescript graduates was running the army.

The Rescript was treated with formal religious reverence. Copies were stored in special wooden boxes at every school, with a portrait of the emperor and empress nearby. School ceremonies began with the principal opening the box, removing the Rescript, and reading it aloud while students bowed deeply. School fires that destroyed the Rescript box were treated as catastrophic events; multiple cases are recorded of principals and teachers running into burning school buildings to rescue the Rescript and dying in the attempt. The Rescript was the central liturgical text of the Meiji ideological project, and the practices around it were as ritually serious as anything in the old Buddhist or Shinto traditions.

---

The supporting religious framework was **State Shinto** (Kokka Shintō).

**Shinto** ("the way of the gods") is the indigenous religious tradition of Japan, a set of practices oriented around veneration of nature spirits (kami) and the imperial ancestors at local and national shrines (Ancient Japan Ch 3). Throughout the historical period, Shinto and Buddhism had been functionally fused in Japanese practice (a syncretism called **shinbutsu shūgō**), with most shrines and temples sharing personnel, ritual structures, and theological frameworks (Ancient Japan Ch 4–5). The two traditions were inseparable in popular practice.

The Meiji government, beginning immediately in 1868, separated them. The **shinbutsu bunri** ("separation of Shinto and Buddhism") edicts issued in March and April 1868 ordered the physical separation of Shinto shrines and Buddhist temples that had been combined institutions for centuries; the removal of Buddhist clergy from Shinto shrine administration; the removal of Buddhist statues, sutras, and ritual objects from Shinto shrine grounds; and the creation of a new state administration for Shinto under the central government. The purpose of the separation was to elevate Shinto to a state-managed national religion oriented around the imperial line, decoupled from the cosmopolitan Buddhist tradition that was deemed insufficiently nationalist.

The separation triggered an iconoclastic movement called **Haibutsu Kishaku** ("abolish the Buddha, destroy Shakyamuni") that destroyed thousands of Buddhist temples, statues, and texts across Japan in the late 1860s and 1870s. Roughly 40,000 Buddhist temples were closed, demolished, or amalgamated in the first decade of Meiji rule — out of perhaps 100,000 that had existed in the late Tokugawa period. Many of the great medieval temple complexes of Kyoto and Nara survived only because they had cultural value as historical monuments; smaller local temples were less fortunate. Centuries-old Buddhist statuary was burned, melted down, or sold to American and European collectors for export. The cultural loss was enormous and is largely uncatalogued.

The Buddhist response was fragmented. The major sects (**Tendai**, **Shingon**, **Pure Land**, **Zen**, **Nichiren**) eventually adapted to the new environment by emphasizing their cultural and historical importance, by reorganizing as private religious associations under new Meiji religious-association laws, and by carving out distinct institutional roles (funeral services, in particular, became a Buddhist monopoly that has persisted to the present day). By 1900, Japanese Buddhism had recovered organizationally and had begun a period of intellectual renewal under modernizing reformers like **Suzuki Daisetsu** (D.T. Suzuki, the figure who would later popularize Zen Buddhism in the West). But the cultural status of Buddhism in Meiji Japan was permanently reduced. Shinto was now the official religion of the imperial state, and Buddhism was a private cultural tradition tolerated within that framework.

State Shinto was institutionalized through several specific measures: the placement of Shinto shrines under government administration; the public funding of national-tier shrines (those associated with the imperial family or with major historical figures); the standardization of shrine ritual procedures; the requirement that government officials and military personnel attend shrine ceremonies on national holidays; and the elevation of certain shrines as central national institutions. The most important of these elevations was the founding of **Yasukuni Shrine** in Tokyo in 1869, dedicated to enshrining the spirits of those who had died fighting for the emperor — beginning with the Boshin War dead. Yasukuni became the central religious institution of the Meiji military project. Every soldier who died in the Sino-Japanese War, the Russo-Japanese War, the Pacific War, and every smaller military operation in between was enshrined at Yasukuni after death, and his family was assured that his spirit had been received into the company of all those who had previously died for the imperial state. The shrine remains one of the most politically contentious religious sites in East Asia today, primarily because the post-1945 enshrinement of Class A war criminals from the Pacific War has made it a focal point for arguments about Japanese responsibility for wartime atrocities.

---

The ideological superstructure that united the educational project, the Imperial Rescript, and State Shinto was the doctrine of **kokutai** (KOH-koo-tye, "national essence" or "national body" or "national polity").

Kokutai had earlier roots in the Tokugawa-era kokugaku tradition — the "national learning" school of scholars like **Motoori Norinaga** and **Hirata Atsutane** who had argued in the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries that Japan was a uniquely sacred polity, distinguished from China and the rest of the world by the unbroken descent of its imperial line from the sun goddess Amaterasu (Edo Japan Ch 6). The Meiji ideologists took this kokugaku argument, simplified it, and systematized it into a doctrine that could be taught in primary schools, recited in army training, and printed on imperial holiday posters.

The standard kokutai formula went something like this: Japan is unique among the nations because its imperial line is unbroken from the foundation of the country by the sun goddess, the people stand in a familial relationship to the emperor as children to parents, the duty of every subject is to serve the emperor and the state, the emperor's will is identical with the welfare of the nation, and the moral test of any individual action is whether it serves or harms the imperial polity. Western individualism, liberalism, and democracy were considered foreign imports incompatible with the kokutai. Service to the imperial state took precedence over individual conscience, family obligation, religious belief, or any other form of moral allegiance.

This doctrine was, in 1890, a relatively benign nationalist framework that emphasized social cohesion, civic duty, and national unity. By 1935 it had become the ideological license for arresting and torturing anyone who deviated from official orthodoxy on imperial matters. The 1925 **Peace Preservation Law** criminalized advocacy of "altering the kokutai" — language that allowed the police to detain Communists, socialists, liberals, and eventually liberal Christians and dissenting Buddhists for thoughts and statements considered hostile to the imperial polity. By the late 1930s, the kokutai concept had become so totalizing that the Education Ministry's 1937 publication *Kokutai no Hongi* ("Cardinal Principles of the Kokutai") asserted as official doctrine that the Japanese imperial line was literally divine, that the Japanese race was uniquely capable of moral perception because of its descent from the sun goddess, and that the spread of Japanese imperial rule to other peoples was a moral obligation deriving from the unique sacred character of the kokutai.

This is where the seeds the Meiji educational system planted in the 1890s came to fruit. The Imperial-Rescript children of 1900 became the kokutai-true-believer officers of the 1930s. The cultural engineering had worked, in the worst possible sense.

---

The cultural-engineering project also produced, simultaneously, the basis for an extraordinarily rich and creative modern Japanese culture that was anything but uniform.

The literary scene of late Meiji and early Taishō Japan included some of the greatest writers of any modern literary tradition: **Natsume Sōseki** (NAHT-soo-meh SOH-seh-kee), Japan's greatest modern novelist, who had studied in London 1900–02 (and hated it), taught at Tokyo Imperial University, and produced *I Am a Cat* (1905), *Botchan* (1906), *Kokoro* (1914), and a body of work exploring the psychological dislocation of Meiji modernization — the loneliness of being neither traditionally Japanese nor properly Western. **Mori Ōgai**, the army surgeon-turned-novelist who had studied medicine in Germany and produced *The Wild Goose* (1911) and *The Abe Family* (1913). **Higuchi Ichiyō**, the great female novelist of the early Meiji period, who died of tuberculosis at twenty-four in 1896, leaving behind a small body of work that established her as one of the most important women writers in Japanese literary history. **Shimazaki Tōson**, **Tayama Katai**, **Kunikida Doppo**, **Izumi Kyōka** — the late Meiji literary scene was bursting with experimentation, translation of European literature, and the development of a modern Japanese prose style that synthesized classical Japanese, modern Western, and colloquial speech registers.

In the arts, **Yokoyama Taikan** and **Hishida Shunsō** developed a modern Japanese-style painting (Nihonga) that integrated Western techniques while preserving Japanese subject matter. The Western-style oil painters **Kuroda Seiki** and **Asai Chū** trained in Paris and brought Impressionist-influenced styles back to Tokyo. The kabuki theater modernized under reformers like **Ichikawa Danjūrō IX**. The new musical theater forms — the **shimpa** modern drama, then the **shingeki** new theater of the 1900s — drew on Western dramatic conventions to address modern Japanese subjects. Western classical music was introduced through the **Tokyo Music School** (founded 1887). The Japanese cinema began with imported French and American films around 1898 and produced its first domestic narrative films by the early 1900s.

Architecturally, the Meiji period mixed Western brick and stone construction (the **Ginza Brick Town** of 1872, designed by Irish-British engineer **Thomas Waters**; the **Rokumeikan** ballroom of 1883, designed by British architect **Josiah Conder**; **Tokyo Station** in 1914, designed by Japanese architect Tatsuno Kingo who had trained under Conder) with traditional Japanese forms (the new **Meiji Shrine** in Yoyogi, planned 1912, completed 1920, in pure Japanese style with 100,000 trees donated from across the country and the colonies).

The Meiji oligarchs had built the institutional skeleton. The Meiji writers, artists, and intellectuals built the cultural musculature on top of it. The Japan that existed in 1912 was — as much as any other society in the world — a fully modern, fully literate, intellectually rich and creative society, with a deep traditional culture coexisting alongside the most up-to-date Western-influenced cultural production. It was also, simultaneously, a society in which the kokutai ideology was being absorbed by the next generation of military officers, in which State Shinto was being institutionalized at every shrine in the country, and in which the structural cracks in the Meiji Constitution were beginning to widen as the founding genrō aged toward death.

The cultural-engineering project had succeeded at producing both a modern population and a militarist substrate. Both products were now ready for use.

The next chapter is about the use.

Cross-civ check: the Meiji cultural-engineering project has obvious comparisons to other late-nineteenth-century nationalist state-building exercises. The unification of **Italy** (1860–71) included a parallel project to construct a national Italian identity out of regional populations who had not previously thought of themselves as Italians; the literacy rate in Italy at unification was roughly 25%, vs. Japan's somewhat higher figure. The unification of **Germany** under Bismarck (1871) included parallel projects of mass literacy, conscription-based national identity, and Lutheran-influenced nationalist religious framing. Both Italian and German nationalism in this period mixed a relatively benign civic patriotism with darker undercurrents of racial-religious sacralization that would become catastrophic in the twentieth century. The Meiji project followed the same pattern. The closest non-European parallel is probably the **Ottoman Tanzimat-era** attempt to construct an Ottoman national identity overlaying the multi-religious millet system (Ottoman Empire Ch 9–10), which never quite worked because the Ottoman population was too religiously and ethnically diverse to be integrated under a single national framework. Japan had the structural advantage of relative ethnic and linguistic homogeneity, which made the cultural-engineering project much easier than it would have been in any other late-nineteenth-century modernizing state. The disadvantage was that the homogeneity made the resulting nationalism unusually intense and unusually hard to moderate — the kokutai doctrine had no internal counterweights, no minority traditions that had to be respected, no religious pluralism that had to be accommodated. When the doctrine became militarist in the 1930s, there was nothing inside the cultural framework to resist it. That is one of the dark secondary consequences of the Meiji cultural-engineering project.

# Chapter 7 — The Sino-Japanese and Russo-Japanese Wars

By the early 1890s, the Meiji project had built — at extraordinary speed — the components of a modern industrial military power. The conscript army of 1873 had grown into a 220,000-man peacetime force trained in Prussian tactics under German military advisors. The navy of borrowed bakufu hulks had grown into a fleet of British-built modern cruisers and Japanese-built smaller warships. The merchant marine that supported both was the largest in Asia. The financial system could float foreign loans on the London market. The diplomatic establishment had built relationships with all the major Western powers. Japan had spent twenty-five years preparing for international competition. By 1894 it was ready to compete.

This chapter is about the two wars that announced Japan's arrival as a great power: the **First Sino-Japanese War** of 1894–95 against Qing China, and the **Russo-Japanese War** of 1904–05 against Imperial Russia. The Sino-Japanese war established Japan as the dominant power in East Asia and gave it the first colony of its growing empire (Taiwan). The Russo-Japanese War established Japan as a great power capable of defeating a major European empire and opened the path to the annexation of Korea (the next chapter). Both wars were short, decisive, and had effects on world politics that took the next forty years to play out.

---

The First Sino-Japanese War was fought, immediately, over Korea.

The Korean **Joseon dynasty** (Joseon Korea Ch 7–8) was, by the 1890s, in serious institutional decay. The Korean state's governing class — the **yangban** scholar-officials trained in Confucian classics — had spent most of the nineteenth century in factional warfare while the rural economy stagnated and Western and Japanese commercial pressure increased. Korea had been forced open by Japan in 1876 (the Treaty of Ganghwa) on terms similar to the unequal treaties Western powers had imposed on Japan two decades earlier. The Japanese government considered Korean territory of strategic importance — both for trade and as a buffer against Russian expansion from the north — and had spent the 1880s attempting to build pro-Japanese factions within the Korean court. The Qing Chinese government considered Korea a tributary state — Korea had been a Qing tributary since the seventeenth century after the Manchu Korean War of 1636 (Joseon Korea Ch 5) — and had spent the 1880s reasserting Chinese influence over the Korean court through the Chinese resident-general **Yuan Shikai**.

The proximate cause of the war was the **Donghak Rebellion** of 1894, a peasant uprising in southwestern Korea led by the Donghak ("Eastern Learning") religious-political movement against rural taxation, foreign encroachment, and yangban corruption. The Korean government, unable to suppress the rebellion, requested Chinese military assistance under the standard tributary protocol. Qing forces landed in Korea in early June 1894. Japan, citing its 1885 **Tianjin Convention** with China (which required mutual notification before either power sent troops to Korea), immediately landed its own forces. The two armies converged on Seoul. The Korean government, caught between them, attempted to negotiate the withdrawal of both. Negotiations failed. On July 25, 1894, the Japanese navy attacked a Chinese troopship in the Yellow Sea without a declaration of war. Formal war was declared on August 1, 1894.

The war lasted nine months. The Japanese expeditionary force overran Korea within the first six weeks, defeating the main Chinese army at **Pyongyang** in mid-September. The Japanese navy then destroyed the Chinese **Beiyang Fleet** at the **Battle of the Yalu River** on September 17, 1894 — sinking five Chinese warships to no Japanese losses, in what was the first major engagement of modern steam-powered ironclad fleets in Asian waters. The Japanese army crossed the Yalu into Manchuria, captured **Port Arthur** (the Chinese naval base on the Liaodong Peninsula) in November 1894 after a brief siege, and crossed the **Bohai Sea** to capture **Weihaiwei** (the Chinese naval base on the Shandong Peninsula) in February 1895. The remaining Chinese fleet surrendered. The Qing court sued for peace.

The **Treaty of Shimonoseki**, signed April 17, 1895, by Itō Hirobumi for Japan and **Li Hongzhang** for the Qing, was punitive: China ceded **Taiwan**, the **Pescadores Islands**, and the **Liaodong Peninsula** (including Port Arthur) to Japan; recognized the "complete independence" of Korea (i.e., no longer a Chinese tributary, with the implication that Korea was now within the Japanese sphere); paid an indemnity of 200 million silver taels (roughly 360 million yen, equivalent to about four years of Japanese government revenue); opened additional treaty ports in Chongqing, Suzhou, Hangzhou, and Sashi; and gave Japan most-favored-nation status. The indemnity was paid in installments and went directly into the Japanese treasury. It funded the gold standard adoption of 1897 and the next decade of military expansion. In effect, the Qing dynasty paid for the Japanese fleet that would defeat the Russians ten years later.

---

There was a postscript. Six days after the Treaty of Shimonoseki was signed, Russia, France, and Germany delivered a joint diplomatic note to the Japanese government demanding that Japan return the Liaodong Peninsula to China on the grounds that Japanese possession of Liaodong would threaten "the peace of the Far East." Japan, recognizing that the three powers could enforce the demand militarily and that Britain (Japan's only potential ally) would not support resistance, complied. In a public face-saving exchange, China paid Japan an additional 30 million taels in exchange for Liaodong's return, and Japan formally returned the peninsula to Chinese sovereignty. Three years later, in 1898, **Russia leased the Liaodong Peninsula from China** for itself, including Port Arthur, and began building it up as a Russian Pacific naval base.

This **Triple Intervention** of April 1895, and the subsequent Russian seizure of Liaodong in 1898, were the immediate provocations that produced the Russo-Japanese War nine years later. Japanese public opinion was permanently radicalized by the incident. The slogan **gashin shōtan** ("perseverance and self-discipline," literally "lying on firewood and tasting bile") became a national mantra in 1895, signaling the popular determination to build the military and naval capacity to make sure no foreign powers could dictate to Japan again. The 1896 Japanese military expansion programs — a 220,000-man peacetime army, an expanded navy of new battleships and cruisers ordered from British shipyards, the founding of the Yawata Steel Works (Chapter 4) to make Japanese arms production independent of imports — were directly products of the Triple Intervention humiliation. Japan spent the next decade preparing to fight Russia.

The diplomatic preparation was equally important. Britain, increasingly alarmed by Russian expansion in northern China and Manchuria, began signaling interest in an Asian alliance partner in the late 1890s. The Boer War (1899–1902) had stretched British military resources thin, and the British government wanted a regional power that could check Russian ambitions in East Asia at low cost to Britain. Japan, which had spent the 1890s carefully positioning itself as the most reliable Asian power, was the obvious candidate. Negotiations began in 1901. The **Anglo-Japanese Alliance** was signed January 30, 1902 — the first equal alliance between a European and an Asian power in modern history. Britain agreed to neutrality if Japan fought one power and intervention if Japan fought two. The alliance was the diplomatic insurance policy that made the Russo-Japanese War possible: Russia's potential allies (France, primarily) would now stay out of any Russo-Japanese conflict, because intervention would draw Britain in on Japan's side.

The war broke out two years later.

---

The Russo-Japanese War began on **February 8, 1904**, when the Japanese navy launched a surprise torpedo attack on the Russian Pacific squadron at Port Arthur — two days before the formal declaration of war on February 10. The pattern of attacking before declaring war (used at Pyongyang in 1894, at Port Arthur in 1904, and would be used again at Pearl Harbor in 1941) was a Japanese naval tradition that confirmed a pattern Japanese strategists considered legitimate and Western observers considered duplicitous; the historiographical disagreement on this point continues today.

The war lasted nineteen months. The major land campaigns were fought across Manchuria — the Japanese expeditionary force under **Field Marshal Ōyama Iwao** (oh-YAH-mah ee-WAH-oh) crossed the Yalu in May 1904, fought a series of major engagements at Liaoyang (August 1904), the Sha River (October 1904), and **Mukden** (February-March 1905). The Battle of Mukden involved roughly 600,000 troops on both sides and was the largest land battle in world history to that date — only the world wars of the twentieth century would exceed it. Japanese casualties were 75,000; Russian, 90,000. The Russian army withdrew north of Mukden in good order, but the Japanese had effectively pushed Russia out of southern Manchuria.

The siege of **Port Arthur**, conducted by **General Nogi Maresuke** (NOH-gee mah-reh-SOO-keh), was a separate operation. From August 1904 to January 1905, Nogi's Third Army besieged the Russian fortress, attempting repeated assaults on the perimeter that produced staggering casualties — roughly 60,000 Japanese killed or wounded over five months. Nogi himself lost both his sons in the siege, one at the Battle of Nanshan in May 1904 and the other at the assault on 203 Meter Hill in November 1904. Port Arthur fell on January 2, 1905, after 156 days. The fortress had cost more lives, on both sides, than any other single engagement of the war. Nogi survived; he would commit ritual suicide seven years later (Chapter 8) following the Meiji Emperor to the grave.

---

The decisive engagement of the war was at sea.

The Russian Baltic Fleet, under Admiral **Zinovy Rozhestvensky**, departed Kronstadt in October 1904, sailed around Africa, around India, through the Singapore Strait, and finally entered the Tsushima Strait between Japan and Korea on May 27, 1905, after seven months at sea. The fleet's mission was to reinforce the Russian Pacific squadron at Vladivostok; the loss of Port Arthur had eliminated the only other Russian port in the area. Japanese intelligence had tracked the fleet's progress closely, and Admiral **Tōgō Heihachirō** (TOH-go hay-ee-hah-CHEE-roh), commanding the Japanese **Combined Fleet**, was waiting in the Tsushima Strait when the Russians arrived.

The **Battle of Tsushima** of May 27–28, 1905, lasted roughly twenty-four hours and was the most decisive naval engagement since **Trafalgar** a century earlier. Tōgō, executing a tactical maneuver later studied at every naval academy in the world, "crossed the T" of the Russian formation — turning his fleet across the head of the advancing Russian column to bring his full broadside to bear on the leading Russian battleships. The Japanese gunnery was significantly more accurate than the Russian (the Russian crews had been at sea for seven months and were exhausted; the Japanese crews were fresh, drilled, and operating in home waters). Within two hours of the action's opening, the lead Russian battleships had been disabled. By nightfall, most of the Russian heavy units had been sunk or were sinking. By the next morning, the Japanese destroyer flotillas had finished off the remaining Russian survivors.

The final tally: 21 of the 38 Russian ships engaged were sunk; 7 were captured; 6 escaped to neutral ports; 4 reached Vladivostok. Russian casualties: roughly 4,400 killed, 5,900 captured, including Admiral Rozhestvensky himself, who was wounded and taken prisoner. Japanese losses: 3 torpedo boats sunk, 117 men killed. The disproportion was extraordinary — one of the most lopsided fleet actions in modern naval history.

Tsushima had several consequences. Strategically, it eliminated Russian naval power in the Pacific for a generation, made any Russian amphibious operation against Japan or Korea impossible, and forced the Russian government to accept American mediation of peace negotiations. Symbolically, it announced to the world that an Asian power had decisively defeated a European fleet in a fair pitched battle. Tōgō became a global celebrity: Western press dubbed him "the Nelson of the East," European naval establishments invited him for state visits, and the British Admiralty awarded him the Order of Merit. He outlived almost every other Meiji-era figure (he died in 1934 at age eighty-six), attended the 1922 **Washington Naval Conference**, and personally mentored a young naval officer named **Yamamoto Isoroku** who would, in 1941, plan the attack on Pearl Harbor.

---

Peace negotiations began at **Portsmouth, New Hampshire**, in August 1905, mediated by US President **Theodore Roosevelt**, who had personal interest in establishing the United States as a Pacific power capable of brokering East Asian disputes (and would receive the Nobel Peace Prize in 1906 for his role in the negotiations). The **Treaty of Portsmouth**, signed September 5, 1905, was less punitive than the Sino-Japanese settlement had been. Japan got: control of Korea (with Russian acknowledgment that Korea was within the Japanese sphere of influence); the southern half of **Sakhalin Island**; the Russian lease of the Liaodong Peninsula (Port Arthur and the South Manchuria Railway zone); fishing rights in Russian Pacific waters. Japan did not get: the indemnity it had demanded (Roosevelt pressured both sides to drop the indemnity claim to facilitate the peace settlement); the northern half of Sakhalin; Russian withdrawal from northern Manchuria.

The lack of an indemnity was politically costly in Tokyo. Japanese public opinion had expected, after the great victories at Mukden and Tsushima, a settlement comparable to or better than Shimonoseki. The **Hibiya Riots** of September 5–7, 1905, were the largest civil disturbances in Tokyo since the Restoration: angry crowds attacked police stations and government buildings, demanded the rejection of the treaty, and forced the imposition of martial law. Seventeen rioters were killed by police. The riots demonstrated that Meiji nationalism had developed a popular dynamic that the oligarchs no longer fully controlled. Future governments would have to deliver continuously expanding international successes or face the same kind of popular backlash. This pattern — the demand for ever-greater foreign-policy victories driven by mass nationalism — would shape Japanese politics for the next forty years.

---

The two wars together had transformed Japan's international position. In 1894, Japan had been a recently modernized regional power with no formal colonies and unequal treaties still partially in force. By 1905 it had a formal colonial empire (Taiwan since 1895, the Liaodong lease, southern Sakhalin), a protectorate over Korea (which would become formal annexation in 1910), an alliance with Britain, recognition as a great power in international diplomacy, and a navy that had defeated the third-largest navy in the world in a fleet action. The unequal treaties had been substantially revised — extraterritoriality was abolished in 1899, full tariff autonomy was achieved in 1911. The original Meiji project of equality with the Western powers had been accomplished.

But the wars had also institutionalized two structural patterns that would prove catastrophic.

First, the wars had demonstrated that aggressive foreign expansion produced political dividends: territory, indemnities, prestige, popular support. The civilian government's career incentives now favored expansion. The military's career incentives favored expansion even more strongly. The popular nationalism that the Imperial Rescript had cultivated demanded expansion. The structural pressure was constant and one-directional.

Second, the wars had given the Japanese army and navy enormous prestige and significant institutional autonomy. The army that had won at Mukden and the navy that had won at Tsushima could not be easily controlled by civilian politicians who had not led troops in those battles. The constitutional independence of military command (Chapter 5) became, after 1905, a political reality as well as a legal one. The Meiji oligarchs were aging, the genrō system was beginning to decay, and the institutional weight of the victorious military was beginning to fill the resulting political vacuum. By 1931, when the Kwantung Army would unilaterally seize Manchuria without civilian authorization, the institutional weight of the military would have become decisive. The Sino-Japanese and Russo-Japanese wars had built the army and navy that would, a generation later, take over Japanese foreign policy.

This was not visible at the time. In 1905, what was visible was that Japan was, for the first time in its history, a recognized great power. The sixty-year-old Iwakura Mission delegates — those still alive — could remember being told by Western diplomats in 1872 that Japan was politically comparable to Persia. They had spent thirty-three years building a country that was now politically comparable to Britain, France, Germany, and Russia. The achievement was extraordinary. The cost would not be apparent for another generation.

The next chapter is the closing of the era.

Cross-civ check: the Russo-Japanese War of 1904–05 was the first defeat of a European Great Power by an Asian power in the modern era, and its global political consequences were enormous. **Indian** nationalists like Mohandas Gandhi cited it as evidence that European racial superiority was a myth. The young **Sun Yat-sen**, future founder of the Chinese Republic, took it as confirmation that Asian modernization could match Western capability. The young **Vietnamese**, **Indonesian**, **Filipino**, and **Egyptian** nationalists made similar arguments. The **1905 Russian Revolution** was triggered partly by the Russian defeats in the war — the bloodthirsty mismanagement of the army by Tsar Nicholas II's incompetent generals discredited the Russian autocracy and produced the constitutional reforms of 1905–07 that briefly made Russia a quasi-constitutional state before being reversed by the Tsar. The **Persian Constitutional Revolution** of 1905–11 (Persian Empire's late successor states) was directly inspired by the Russo-Japanese War's demonstration that an Asian constitutional state could defeat a European autocratic empire. The **Young Turk Revolution** of 1908 in the Ottoman Empire (Ottoman Empire Ch 9) drew similar inspiration. For roughly fifteen years after Tsushima, the Meiji Constitution and Japanese institutional design were considered, throughout the colonized and semi-colonized world, the model for what an Asian society could accomplish if it modernized aggressively. The model would be discredited by Japanese aggression in the 1930s and the catastrophe of 1945, but in 1905 it was the model. Japan was, briefly, the world's leading example of successful non-Western modernization. The implications, both inspiring and dark, would play out across the twentieth century.

# Chapter 8 — Annexation, Death, and Legacy

The era ended with three events compressed into a few years: the formal annexation of Korea in 1910, the death of the Meiji Emperor in 1912, and the ritual suicide of General Nogi Maresuke on the night of the imperial funeral. Each event closed a different dimension of the Meiji project. Together, they marked the end of the founding generation and the transition to the post-Meiji Japan that would, within twenty years, slide into the militarist catastrophe.

This chapter is about the closing. It is also, by necessity, about the legacy: what the Meiji oligarchs had built, what they had failed to build, and what the consequences would be for the Japan of the twentieth century. The legacy is mixed — extraordinary achievement and catastrophic failure on the same operating system, as advertised throughout this book. Both halves deserve honest accounting.

---

The annexation of Korea was the culmination of forty years of gradual Japanese encroachment on Korean sovereignty.

The 1876 **Treaty of Ganghwa** had opened Korea to Japanese trade on terms similar to the unequal treaties imposed on Japan two decades earlier. Through the 1880s and early 1890s, Japan had built up commercial and political networks within Korea while the Qing Chinese government had attempted to maintain its tributary relationship through the resident **Yuan Shikai**. The Sino-Japanese War of 1894–95 had ended Chinese suzerainty over Korea — the Treaty of Shimonoseki had recognized Korean "complete independence," meaning independence from China and de facto subordination to Japan. The Russo-Japanese War of 1904–05 had ended Russian competition for Korean influence — the Treaty of Portsmouth had explicitly acknowledged Japanese paramountcy in Korea. The remaining steps to formal annexation took five years.

In November 1905, Japan imposed the **Eulsa Treaty** on Korea, making Korea a Japanese protectorate. The treaty stripped Korea of foreign-affairs authority — Japan now controlled all Korean diplomatic relations — and established a Japanese **Resident-General** in Seoul to oversee Korean internal affairs. The first Resident-General was **Itō Hirobumi**, who had been the most senior figure in the Meiji Constitution drafting and was now applying Meiji-style institutional reform to Korea on Tokyo's behalf. Itō's stated personal preference was for indirect Japanese influence over Korea while maintaining nominal Korean sovereignty — partial colonization rather than formal annexation. He believed that gradual modernization under Japanese supervision would produce a stable pro-Japanese Korean state at lower political cost than direct rule.

Korean resistance to the protectorate was significant from the start. The Korean **Emperor Gojong**, who had been forced to sign the Eulsa Treaty under coercion, attempted to appeal to the international community for support. He sent secret emissaries to **The Hague** in 1907 to address the Second International Peace Conference, formally protesting that the Eulsa Treaty had been imposed by force and was therefore invalid. The conference refused to receive the Korean emissaries (none of the great powers wanted to oppose Japanese policy in Korea, and Britain in particular was bound by the Anglo-Japanese Alliance). Itō, learning of the appeal, forced **Gojong** to abdicate in favor of his son **Sunjong** in July 1907, dissolved the Korean army (provoking armed resistance from disbanded soldiers that fed the **Righteous Army** guerrilla movement of 1907–10), and tightened Japanese administrative control over the Korean state.

The guerrilla resistance was extensive. From 1907 to 1910, an estimated 17,000 Korean fighters engaged Japanese troops and Korean police across the peninsula, and approximately 12,000 were killed before the resistance was largely suppressed. The Japanese army deployed roughly 50,000 troops to Korea over the period. The casualties on both sides were significant, and the brutality of the Japanese counterinsurgency campaign — including village-level reprisals, public executions, and forced confessions — established a pattern that would be repeated in Manchuria, China, and Southeast Asia in the following decades.

In October 1909, Itō Hirobumi was assassinated at **Harbin railway station** in Manchuria by the Korean nationalist **An Jung-geun**, who shot him three times during what was supposed to be a meeting between Itō and a Russian envoy. Itō was sixty-eight years old. He had been the Meiji oligarch most closely associated with constitutional government, restraint in foreign policy, and the indirect-rule preference for Korea. His death removed the last senior voice in Tokyo for a softer Korea policy. An Jung-geun was tried and executed in March 1910; he remains a national hero in modern South Korea. The path was now clear for formal annexation.

The **Japan-Korea Treaty of 1910**, signed August 22, 1910, and proclaimed August 29, formally annexed Korea to the Japanese empire. The 519-year Joseon dynasty (which had ruled Korea since 1392; Joseon Korea Ch 1) ended. Korea became the Japanese colony of **Chōsen** (the Japanese reading of "Joseon"), administered by a Japanese **Government-General** based in Seoul. The annexation would last 35 years, until Japan's defeat in the Pacific War in August 1945. The colonial period would produce extraordinary economic infrastructure development (industrial plant, railways, ports, schools, modern administrative apparatus), simultaneous extraordinary cultural suppression (forced adoption of Japanese names, suppression of Korean language in education, conscription of Korean labor for wartime industry, conscription of Korean women into military sexual slavery as the **comfort women** system), and a legacy of bilateral antagonism that still shapes East Asian politics today. Modern South Korean and Japanese governments continue to dispute, more than a century later, the legitimacy of the original annexation and the nature of subsequent colonial conduct.

The annexation was the formal completion of the Japanese imperial project. The empire now consisted of the home islands, Taiwan (since 1895), the Pescadores, the Liaodong lease (Port Arthur and the surrounding Kwantung Leased Territory), the South Manchuria Railway zone, southern Sakhalin (since 1905), and Korea (since 1910). Japan had become, in fifteen years, the only Asian colonial power. The Meiji slogan **fukoku kyōhei** ("rich country, strong army") had been achieved in both parts. The country was rich (industrially) and the army was strong (having defeated both China and Russia). The original purpose of the Restoration — to make Japan capable of resisting Western dictation — had been accomplished. What had been unclear in 1868 was that the project's logic would not stop at parity. The same institutional and ideological apparatus that had produced parity by 1900 would, by 1910, have produced an empire of its own. By 1931 it would produce the invasion of Manchuria; by 1937, the invasion of China; by 1941, the war with the United States; by 1945, the destruction of Hiroshima and Nagasaki and the end of the Japanese empire.

---

The Meiji Emperor died on **July 30, 1912**, aged fifty-nine, of complications from diabetes and uremia. His final illness had begun in early July; the public had been informed of the seriousness of his condition only in the final days. The nation went into formal mourning. The state funeral, held on **September 13, 1912**, was the most elaborate ceremonial event in Japanese history to that point, attended by representatives of every nation that maintained diplomatic relations with Japan and broadcast by international press to the entire world. The funeral procession through Tokyo took several hours; hundreds of thousands of Japanese citizens lined the route in silent mourning. The Western press treated the event as the death of one of the great monarchs of the age — comparable to Queen Victoria, on whom Meiji had been measured throughout his reign by Japanese and Western observers alike.

Mutsuhito (Emperor Meiji) had been a remarkable monarch in a specific sense. He had presided over the most rapid national transformation in modern history; he had attended cabinet meetings, signed the constitution, dressed in Western military uniform after 1872, opened the first Diet session, presided over military victories over China and Russia, accepted the annexation of Korea. He had not, however, exercised significant personal political power. The Meiji oligarchs had used him as the constitutional symbol of the new state without giving him operational authority over policy. His personal preferences on most major decisions are not knowable from the surviving documents — the structural deference of the Meiji state to imperial authority masked the actual political dynamics, and most of the relevant private correspondence was destroyed during the imperial-era information control of the early twentieth century. The historian Donald Keene's massive 2002 biography *Emperor of Japan: Meiji and His World* concluded that Mutsuhito was probably more politically active than the standard accounts suggest, but that the deliberate opacity around imperial decision-making makes a full reconstruction impossible.

What is clear is that Mutsuhito was the personal symbol around which the entire Meiji project had been organized. His death closed an era in a literal sense. The era name **Meiji** was retired; his son and successor took the throne as the **Taishō Emperor** ("great righteousness"), opening the brief Taishō era (1912–26) that would see the high tide of Japanese parliamentary government and the early erosion of the Meiji constitutional system. Mutsuhito's body was buried at the **Momoyama Imperial Mausoleum** outside Kyoto. The plans for the **Meiji Shrine** in the Yoyogi district of Tokyo, dedicated to his deified spirit, began immediately and the shrine was completed in 1920 — its surrounding forest planted with 100,000 trees donated from across Japan and the colonies, including the new colony of Korea.

---

On the evening of the imperial funeral, September 13, 1912, **General Nogi Maresuke** committed ritual suicide at his Tokyo home.

Nogi had been the commander at the siege of Port Arthur, where he had lost both his sons. He had served as president of the **Peers' School** (Gakushūin), educating the imperial princes, in the years after the Russo-Japanese War. He was sixty-three years old. According to the suicide note he left, he had been preparing to die since the **Battle of Kumamoto** in 1877, when as a young officer he had lost his regimental banner to Saigō Takamori's Satsuma rebels — a disgrace that he had concluded could only be expiated by his death, and that he had been waiting since 1877 for a suitable occasion to perform. The death of his emperor was that occasion. Nogi waited until the imperial funeral procession had passed through Tokyo's central avenue, then returned home with his wife **Shizuko** (who had agreed to die with him), wrote his suicide notes, dressed in formal military uniform, and committed seppuku. Shizuko cut her own throat with a dagger.

The **junshi** (ritual suicide following one's lord into death) was an ancient samurai practice that had been formally banned by the Tokugawa shogunate in 1663 and had not been performed publicly in Japan for over two centuries. Nogi's revival of the practice in 1912 — at the moment of the death of the emperor who had presided over the modernization of Japan — was a deliberate symbolic statement. He was offering himself as the last samurai of the imperial line, dying for an emperor whose new Japan had no formal place for samurai, in the traditional manner that samurai had honored their lords for a thousand years.

The reaction was extraordinary. Japanese public opinion was deeply divided. Many older Japanese — including ex-samurai, military officers, and traditionalists — celebrated Nogi as the noblest possible expression of imperial loyalty, the perfect samurai who had given his life for his lord even after the institutional samurai class no longer existed. Younger and more Westernized Japanese were horrified — newspapers carried editorials denouncing the suicide as feudal anachronism, as ritual murder of his wife, as a regression to a medieval mentality the Meiji project had supposedly transcended. The novelist **Mori Ōgai** wrote *The Abe Family* (Abe Ichizoku, 1913) explicitly in response to Nogi's death, examining the ethics of junshi from a deliberately ambivalent perspective. **Natsume Sōseki** wrote *Kokoro* (1914), one of the masterpieces of modern Japanese literature, around a parallel story of a Meiji-era suicide motivated by guilt and honor — the protagonist explicitly cites Nogi's suicide as the trigger for his own decision to die.

Nogi's death encapsulated, in one event, the unresolved tension at the heart of the Meiji project. The country had modernized at extraordinary speed. The institutional structures of Edo Japan — the warrior class, the four-tier social hierarchy, the daimyō system, the closed borders — had been dismantled. But the cultural sensibility of the warrior class — the conviction that the willing sacrifice of life for one's lord was the highest moral act, the ritual centrality of seppuku as an honorable death, the assumption that personal honor was inseparable from loyalty to the state — had not been dismantled. It had been preserved, repackaged, and elevated to the central organizing principle of the new imperial nationalism. Nogi was the perfect Meiji subject: a modernized military officer commanding an army with German rifles, a victor in the most modern war yet fought in Asia, a man who had read Western books and spoken European languages, who had also held within himself the absolute samurai ethic that made his suicide on the night of his lord's funeral the inevitable culmination of his life's logic. The Meiji project had produced him. The Meiji project would, three decades later, produce ten thousand kamikaze pilots whose willingness to die for the emperor would be the same ethic on a larger scale.

---

So what was the legacy?

In the narrowest accounting, the Meiji project had accomplished, in forty-four years, the most successful crash modernization in world history. A pre-industrial agrarian feudal monarchy had been transformed into a constitutional monarchy with industrial production, modern military forces, modern infrastructure, near-universal literacy, integrated international finance, and a colonial empire. The original strategic goal — preserving Japanese sovereignty against Western pressure — had been completely achieved. The unequal treaties had been revised. Japan was a recognized great power. No country in the modern era has matched the speed of the transformation, and few have matched its thoroughness.

In a slightly broader accounting, the Meiji project had produced a model of late industrialization that subsequent non-Western states would study and partially replicate for the next century. The South Korean and Taiwanese miracles of the 1960s–80s, the Singaporean transformation, the post-Mao Chinese economic restructuring, the late-twentieth-century Vietnamese reforms — all drew, more or less consciously, on the Meiji template of state-directed industrialization, strategic protection of pilot industries, recycled export earnings, tight integration of finance and industry, mass education at a national scale, and selective borrowing of foreign institutional designs. The Meiji oligarchs invented out of desperation what became the standard manual for Asian late industrialization.

In a still broader accounting, the Meiji project also produced the institutional and ideological substrate for the Japanese militarist disasters of 1931–45. The Meiji Constitution's structural separation of military command from civilian control gave the army and navy the constitutional independence to conduct foreign policy on their own initiative once the founding genrō died. The kokutai ideology and the Imperial Rescript on Education had built a population willing to die for the imperial state in extraordinary numbers and unwilling to question the moral legitimacy of imperial expansion. The Meiji-era zaibatsu had built the industrial base that could sustain continental aggression. The Meiji-era army had built the institutional culture and the doctrinal framework that would, in the 1930s, conclude that controlling Manchuria was a strategic necessity, that controlling China was a strategic necessity, that controlling Southeast Asia was a strategic necessity, that war with the United States was an acceptable risk in pursuit of these necessities. The Meiji-era navy had built the fleet that bombed Pearl Harbor in 1941. The same institutional and ideological apparatus that had produced equality with the Western powers by 1900 would, by 1945, have produced the destruction of Japan and the deaths of millions of people across East Asia and the Pacific.

The honest accounting therefore has to hold both halves together. Meiji Japan was an extraordinary achievement and a foundational catastrophe. The achievements — universal literacy, industrial modernization, sovereignty, constitutional government, international standing — were real and lasting. The catastrophes — the colonial brutality in Korea and Taiwan, the Manchurian and Chinese aggression, the Pacific War, the comfort women, the firebombings of Tokyo and Osaka and Yokohama, the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, the ten million Asian dead from Japanese military operations between 1931 and 1945 — were also real and lasting. Both products came out of the same project. The same Meiji constitution that built a parliament also gave the army the autonomy to seize Manchuria. The same Imperial Rescript that built mass literacy also produced the kamikaze pilots. The same zaibatsu industrial base that lifted Japan out of the agrarian past also built the warships that fought at Midway. The institutional and ideological design that the Meiji oligarchs settled on between 1868 and 1900 had both the success and the disaster latent within it.

The post-1945 American occupation rewrote the constitution, dismantled the zaibatsu, abolished State Shinto's official status, and de-deified the imperial line. The kokutai ideology was officially repudiated; the Imperial Rescript on Education was withdrawn from schools. The post-1945 Japan that emerged — democratic, demilitarized, oriented toward economic growth rather than military expansion — was deliberately built on different foundations. The post-1945 Japanese economic miracle of the 1950s–80s replicated many features of the Meiji developmentalist state model but in a constitutional and ideological context that ruled out the militarist outcome. It worked. The contemporary Japan of the early twenty-first century is one of the most peaceful, prosperous, and democratic societies in the world. But the contemporary Japan also still contains, encoded in its institutional and cultural DNA, the residues of the Meiji synthesis. The unbroken imperial line is still there, no longer divine but still ceremonially central. Yasukuni Shrine is still there, still controversial. The bureaucratic state is still recognizably descended from the Meiji bureaucracy. The keiretsu corporations are still recognizably descended from the zaibatsu. Modern Japan is the heir of Meiji Japan in ways that are easy to underestimate.

That is the legacy. A modernization project so successful that it produced the most rapid national transformation in history, and so flawed that it produced the most catastrophic Asian war of the twentieth century. Both halves of the inheritance are still alive. Both halves still matter. The Meiji oligarchs would have understood the achievement and would not have foreseen the catastrophe. They were building, consciously, the country that could survive in the world they saw. They built it. The world the country they built then went to war with — the world of 1941 — they could not have foreseen, and probably would not have approved of. But they built the operating system that produced it, and the operating system produced what it produced.

The era closed in 1912. The consequences are still running.

Cross-civ check: the Meiji legacy fits into a broader pattern of nineteenth- and twentieth-century state-building projects whose institutional designs produced both extraordinary achievements and catastrophic failures from the same operating logic. **Bismarckian Germany** is the obvious comparison — the same constitutional design (Prussian model with strong monarchy, weak parliament, autonomous military, strong civil service), the same combination of industrial modernization with militarist-nationalist ideology, the same transition from founding-generation political control to military-bureaucratic dominance, the same eventual catastrophic war. The German operating system produced the achievements of the Wilhelmine industrial economy and the catastrophe of 1914–18; the Meiji operating system produced the achievements of the late-Meiji industrial economy and the catastrophe of 1937–45. Both states then rebuilt themselves after defeat under American supervision and produced second-act success stories — the West German economic miracle, the Japanese postwar miracle. The structural parallels are unmistakable. **Soviet Russia** is the more remote comparison — a different ideological framework, but the same logic of crash modernization at extraordinary human cost producing an extraordinary military-industrial state that then committed catastrophic strategic and humanitarian errors. **Republican-period China** under the Kuomintang attempted a Meiji-style modernization in the 1920s and 1930s but lacked the institutional unity to execute it. The post-1949 Chinese Communist project succeeded in the modernization where the KMT had failed but at far higher human cost. **Nasser's Egypt**, **Park's South Korea**, **Atatürk's Turkey** (Ottoman Empire Ch 12), **Kemalist secularism** — every twentieth-century late-modernizing state has, in some sense, been working through the questions the Meiji oligarchs first put on the table in 1868. None of them have entirely answered the questions. The success-and-disaster ambivalence at the heart of the Meiji project is the success-and-disaster ambivalence at the heart of all of them.

That is the long arc. Meiji is over. What it built is not.

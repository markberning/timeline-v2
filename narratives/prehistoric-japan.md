# Prehistoric Japan — Pots, Rice, and Tombs

**Goal:** Narrative for Japan before recorded history (~14,000 BCE – 538 CE), covering the Jomon, Yayoi, and Kofun eras. Across roughly fourteen and a half millennia, the Japanese archipelago produced one of the world's longest continuous cultural traditions (the hunter-gatherer Jomon, who fired clay vessels millennia before farming and built 500-person settlements without grain), then absorbed wet-rice agriculture, bronze, and iron in a single bundle from Korea (the Yayoi transformation), then built the largest tomb on Earth by surface area without ever writing a sentence (the Kofun period and the rise of the Yamato court). The endpoint is 538 CE, when a Korean king's diplomatic gift — a golden Buddha statue and a set of sutras — ended Japanese prehistory in one delivery. Everything that came afterward in Japanese history (the emperor, the samurai, modern Japan) grew from roots planted in the millennia covered here.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

A note on names and pronunciation: the era covered here predates Japanese writing entirely. A note on "first" claims: this narrative makes several "oldest in the world" claims about Jomon pottery. As with every TL in this project, "oldest" means "oldest surviving evidence found so far." Pottery is fragile, archaeological sites are destroyed by erosion and construction, and what survives is a tiny fraction of what existed. The Japanese archipelago happened to produce pottery very early, and that pottery happened to survive, and archaeologists happened to find it. Those facts are genuine. But the absence of comparably old pottery elsewhere does not prove that nobody else was making it.

# Chapter 1 — The Last Ice Age and the First Pots

Fourteen and a half thousand years ago, somebody on the Japanese archipelago fired clay in a campfire and made a pot.

That sentence does not sound remarkable. It is one of the most remarkable facts in human history. At the time that anonymous potter was shaping wet clay with their hands, pressing it into a rough vessel, and waiting for the flames to harden it, nobody anywhere else on Earth was doing the same thing — or at least, nobody else whose work has survived for archaeologists to find. The oldest known pottery in the Near East dates to roughly 7,000 BCE, more than seven thousand years later. The oldest known pottery in Europe is later still. Pottery from Xianrendong Cave in China has been dated by some studies to as early as 18,000 BCE (potentially older than the Japanese finds), though the dating methodology is debated and the Chinese vessels are extremely fragmentary. The Japanese claim rests on multiple sites with well-dated pottery and is among the strongest in the world: fired clay vessels from across the Japanese archipelago, dated by radiocarbon to approximately 14,500 BCE, older than farming, older than cities, older than almost everything in human civilization.

And the people who made those pots were not farmers. They were hunter-gatherers, fishing the bays and gathering nuts in the forests, with no agriculture, no metal, no writing, no domesticated animals (except dogs), and no social hierarchy beyond the family band.

This is the strangest thing about prehistoric Japan, and the thread that runs through everything in this book. Every standard story about how civilization develops — agriculture comes first, then surplus, then towns, then specialized crafts, then pottery, then social complexity — runs in the wrong order on the Japanese islands. The Japanese archipelago invented (or co-invented) pottery thousands of years before anybody on the islands grew a grain of cultivated rice. They built 500-person settlements without farming. They worked jade across hundreds of miles of open ocean before they learned to write. They eventually got rice and metal in a single bundle from Korea, but only after they had already been complex foragers for ten thousand years. Then they built the largest tomb on Earth by surface area before they could read or write a single sentence in their own language. The Japanese sequence is stubbornly out of step with the rest of the world's.

This chapter sets the stage: the geography of the islands, the end of the last Ice Age that turned a peninsula into an archipelago, the people who were already here when the climate flipped, and the moment those people pressed clay into the world's oldest fired vessels.

---

To understand Japan, you have to understand the islands first.

The Japanese archipelago stretches roughly 1,800 miles (2,900 km) from north to south — about the same north-south distance as from Maine to Florida. The four main islands — **Hokkaido** (hoh-KYE-doh) in the north, **Honshu** (HON-shoo) the central main island, **Shikoku** (shee-KOH-koo) just south of Honshu across an inland sea, and **Kyushu** (KYOO-shoo) at the southwestern end facing Korea — run in a long arc separating the Pacific Ocean from the Sea of Japan. The total land area is about the size of California. The geography is nothing like California. Roughly 73% of Japan is mountainous, with narrow coastal plains, steep river valleys, and dense forest cover. Hokkaido sits at roughly the latitude of Montreal; the southern Ryukyu Islands sit at roughly the latitude of Miami. The climate ranges from cold temperate to subtropical across that span.

Three features of the geography mattered enormously for the Jomon people who would live here, and continue to matter today.

First, **the islands are extraordinarily wet.** Japan gets more rain than almost any other temperate country. Heavy rainfall fed forests dense with chestnut, walnut, oak, and dozens of other nut and fruit trees that produce enormous quantities of edible food on a predictable annual cycle. Nuts can be stored for winter. Stored food is the precondition for staying in one place rather than moving with the seasons. The Jomon did not need agriculture to settle down because the forests already produced storable carbohydrates on their own.

Second, **the coastline is enormous.** Japan has roughly 18,500 miles (29,800 km) of coastline, one of the longest of any country in the world relative to its land area. The coastline is broken into countless bays, inlets, and shallow estuaries, every one of them productive for shellfish, crab, sea urchin, and shore fishing. Off the Pacific coast, the warm **Kuroshio** (koo-roh-SHEE-oh, the "Black Current") flowing up from the south meets the cold **Oyashio** (oh-yah-SHEE-oh) flowing down from the north, mixing nutrients and producing one of the most productive marine ecosystems on Earth — tuna, bonito, salmon (in the north), whales, dolphins. The Jomon ate from this ocean for thirteen thousand years.

Third, **the islands are isolated but reachable.** Japan is separated from the Korean Peninsula by the Korea Strait, roughly 120 miles (200 km) wide at its narrowest, broken up by the small islands of Tsushima and Iki, which made island-hopping possible for early canoes. Japan is separated from Russian Sakhalin by the much narrower La Pérouse Strait at the northern end of Hokkaido. The archipelago is too far from the mainland to be conquered casually, but close enough that goods, people, and ideas could cross when somebody wanted to make the trip. This in/out semi-permeability — accessible to influence but not to invasion — would shape Japanese history for the next fourteen thousand years.

A useful comparison: the Pacific Northwest coast of North America, from southeast Alaska down through British Columbia to Washington State, has the same rough combination of dense temperate forest, heavy rainfall, indented coastline, and rich marine ecosystem. And the indigenous peoples of that coast (the Tlingit, Haida, Kwakwaka'wakw, and others) also developed complex hunter-gatherer societies with permanent settlements, monumental art, social hierarchy, and elaborate ritual life — without ever adopting agriculture. Where forest and sea are rich and reliable enough, farming is not necessary for sedentism. The Jomon are the East Asian demonstration of this principle. The Pacific Northwest peoples are the North American one. The two cases never had any contact with each other.

---

To understand prehistoric Japan, you also have to understand what the islands looked like during the last Ice Age — because for most of human prehistory, they were not islands at all.

During the height of the last glacial period, roughly 26,000 to 19,000 years ago, global sea levels were about 400 feet (120 meters) lower than today, with most of that water locked up in continental ice sheets covering northern Europe and North America. At that lowered sea level, the geography of East Asia was very different. The Korean Peninsula and the Japanese islands were joined by a land bridge across the shallow southern Korea Strait. Hokkaido was connected to Sakhalin Island and the Asian mainland in the north. The Sea of Japan was a partially enclosed inland body, fed by reduced rivers. Anyone walking from northeastern Asia to what is now Japan could do most of the trip on dry land.

The first humans arrived during this period. **Homo sapiens** had spread out of Africa starting around 70,000–60,000 years ago, reached East Asia by perhaps 50,000–40,000 years ago, and crossed into the Japanese islands while sea levels were low enough to make the journey straightforward. The earliest secure archaeological evidence for human occupation of Japan dates to approximately 38,000 years ago, with stone tools, hearths, and butchered animal remains from sites across Honshu. These were Paleolithic foragers — the same kind of small-band hunter-gatherer groups that occupied most of Eurasia in the Late Pleistocene, hunting now-extinct megafauna like the Naumann elephant (a relative of mammoths and modern elephants that roamed the Japanese islands) and gathering plants, with stone-tipped spears, no pottery, no settled villages, and no domestication beyond perhaps an early association with wolves that would eventually produce dogs.

These Paleolithic Japanese were genetically distinct from the populations that would later arrive from the continent during the Yayoi period (Chapter 4). Recent genetic studies — sequencing DNA from Jomon-era skeletons and comparing it to modern East Asian populations — show that the **Jomon people** (the descendants of these Paleolithic arrivals) had separated from the ancestors of mainland East Asians (the populations that would become Han Chinese, Koreans, and other continental groups) at least 38,000 years ago, possibly earlier. They were a genuinely distinct East Asian population, isolated on the islands, evolving culturally and biologically along their own track for tens of thousands of years.

To put the genetic distance in perspective: the Jomon and the later continental East Asians had diverged about as long ago as Europeans and East Asians had diverged from each other. They were not the same people in different costumes. They were a separate East Asian lineage that had been on the islands long enough to develop distinctive genetic and physical traits — slightly more robust skeletal frames, deeper-set eyes, denser facial hair, and a particular pattern of teeth that distinguishes Jomon-era skeletons from later arrivals. The strongest direct genetic descendants of the Jomon today are the **Ainu** (EYE-noo), the indigenous people of Hokkaido who maintained a distinct culture into the modern era, and the indigenous Ryukyu Islanders of the southern archipelago. Most modern Japanese carry significant Jomon ancestry mixed with the later continental contribution, but the Jomon component is highest in those two groups at the geographic edges where the later Yayoi migration penetrated least.

---

Around 19,000 years ago, the climate began to change.

The last glacial maximum ended, the great ice sheets started melting, and global sea levels began rising — slowly at first, then in pulses of dramatic change as ice dams broke and continental ice sheets collapsed. By around 11,700 BCE, the geological boundary that scientists use to mark the end of the Pleistocene (the long Ice Age epoch) and the beginning of the **Holocene** (the warm interglacial period we still live in) had been crossed. Sea levels were still rising — they would not stabilize at roughly modern levels until about 6,000 years ago — but the broad pattern was set. The land bridge between Korea and Japan had drowned. Hokkaido had been cut off from Sakhalin by the widening La Pérouse Strait. The Japanese archipelago, in its modern outline, had emerged.

The people who happened to be on the islands when the water rose were stranded there. Not stranded in any tragic sense — the climate was rapidly improving, the forests were expanding, the coastlines were filling with shellfish — but isolated. For the next ten thousand years, the population on the Japanese islands would be largely cut off from continental gene flow. They would develop their own culture, their own technology, and their own distinctive material world without significant input from the mainland. The Jomon period was a long evolutionary experiment in island culture.

This is the moment the world's oldest pottery enters the story.

---

Sometime between roughly 16,500 and 14,500 years ago — the exact dates are still being refined as new sites are excavated and more carbon samples are dated — somebody on the Japanese islands fired a piece of clay and discovered that it became hard and waterproof.

We know the names of the earliest sites where their work survived. The most famous is **Odai Yamamoto** (OH-dai yah-mah-MOH-toh), in Aomori Prefecture at the northern end of Honshu, where pottery shards were excavated in the late 1990s and dated by radiocarbon to approximately 14,500 BCE. The Odai Yamamoto pottery is fragmentary — small pieces of plain, undecorated, thin-walled vessels — but it is well-stratified, securely dated, and consistent with multiple other Japanese sites of similar age. Other early sites have been found across Kyushu, Honshu, and Hokkaido, with pottery dated to between 13,000 and 11,000 BCE. The pattern is not a single isolated invention at one location but a more gradual emergence of pottery use across the archipelago over roughly two thousand years, from around 14,500 BCE onward.

What were they doing with these pots?

Probably not what you would guess. The natural assumption — the assumption that Western archaeologists made for most of the twentieth century — is that pottery is for storage. You store grain in pots. Therefore pottery should follow agriculture, because before agriculture there is no grain to store. The Jomon pottery breaks that assumption completely. There was no agriculture in Japan in 14,500 BCE. There were no domesticated grains. There was barely any sedentary population. So why pots?

The current best answer is **cooking**. Specifically: cooking shellfish, fish, and nuts in boiling water. Chemical analysis of residues stuck to the inside of early Jomon pottery — fatty acid signatures preserved in the burnt crust — has identified marine resources (fish, shellfish, marine mammals) and plant foods including chestnuts and acorns. Boiling food in a pot does several useful things at once: it kills parasites in raw fish and shellfish, it leaches the bitter tannins out of acorns (making them edible), and it extracts more calories from tough plant material than open-fire roasting can. Pottery did not solve a storage problem. It solved a digestion problem. For coastal foragers eating large quantities of marine resources and acorns, a vessel that could be set in the coals and brought to a boil was a profound technological advance.

This also explains the timing. The transition into the warmer Holocene was creating new ecological opportunities — expanding forests with more nut trees, expanding shellfish beds in newly drowned shallow coastlines — and the people on the Japanese islands had a strong reason to develop better ways of cooking and processing the foods that those new ecologies provided. Pottery was the answer. It is, in retrospect, a technology that fits the moment and the place.

The earliest Jomon pottery is undecorated. The cord-marking that gave the era its name (jōmon literally means "cord pattern") came later, from about 8,000 BCE onward, when potters began rolling twisted cords of plant fiber across the wet clay surface before firing, leaving a delicate ridge-and-groove texture across the vessel walls. The cord-marking is the most distinctive surface decoration in prehistoric world ceramics — visually unmistakable, easy to recognize across thousands of fragments, and so specific to Japan that it served as the identifying signature for the entire 13,000-year cultural tradition when archaeologists first began to organize the material in the late nineteenth century.

The name "Jomon" itself was coined in the 1870s by **Edward Sylvester Morse**, an American zoologist who had come to Japan to study brachiopods (a kind of marine invertebrate). In 1877 he excavated the **Omori Shell Mounds** (OH-moh-ree) near Tokyo — the first scientific archaeological excavation in Japanese history — and described the cord-marked pottery he found there as "jōmon" pottery, using a Japanese term he had heard from his collaborators. Morse was looking for mollusks. He found an entire civilization instead. The label stuck.

The **Indus Valley** (Indus Valley Ch 1) civilization would not exist for another eleven thousand years. The **Maya** (Maya Civilization Ch 1) and other Mesoamerican civilizations were thirteen to fourteen thousand years in the future. The Jomon are the deep prehistoric backdrop against which every other civilization in this project is a recent arrival.

# Chapter 2 — Living the Jomon Way

By around 10,000 BCE, the climate had stabilized into the warm Holocene that we still live in. The Japanese forests had filled out into dense temperate woodland — chestnut, walnut, oak, beech, and a hundred other useful trees. The coastal estuaries were producing shellfish in industrial quantities. The salmon runs in the northern rivers were predictable enough to plan a year around. The Jomon people had pottery, polished stone tools, the bow and arrow, dogs, and increasingly elaborate techniques for processing wild plant foods. They were ready to settle down. And settle down they did, for the next eight thousand years, in a way of life so well-adapted to the islands that it did not need to fundamentally change until change was forced on them from outside (Chapter 4).

This chapter is about how that worked: where the Jomon lived, what they ate, what they made, and what they thought about while they were doing it.

---

The basic Jomon dwelling was a **pit house** (called **tateana-jukyo** in modern Japanese, tah-teh-AH-nah JOO-kyoh, literally "vertical-pit dwelling"). The construction was straightforward: dig a circular or rectangular pit two or three feet into the ground, set wooden posts around the perimeter, lash a frame of poles to the posts, and cover the frame with thatch or bark. The floor was at the bottom of the pit, with a central hearth for cooking and warmth. A typical Jomon pit house was about 15 feet (4.5 meters) across, big enough for a family of five or six, and would last for one or two decades before the timbers rotted out and the household built a new one nearby.

The architecture seems primitive. It was not. The sunken floor was a clever response to the Japanese climate. In winter, the surrounding earth provided insulation against cold air, and the central hearth could heat a small enclosed space efficiently. In summer, the pit floor stayed cooler than the outside air. Against typhoons (the powerful tropical cyclones that regularly slam into the Japanese islands from the Pacific), a pit house with a low profile and a heavy thatched roof was structurally far more stable than a tall above-ground building. The Jomon kept building these houses for ten thousand years because they worked. Variations of the same basic design were still being built in some rural areas of Japan into the early historical period.

Pit houses clustered into hamlets and villages, usually arranged around a central plaza or open space. Most Jomon settlements were small — twenty to fifty people, a handful of houses, occupied for a few generations and then abandoned as soils played out or fortunes changed. But some Jomon communities grew far beyond the scale of a typical forager band. The most spectacular example is **Sannai-Maruyama** (sah-NYE mah-roo-YAH-mah), in northern Honshu (modern Aomori Prefecture, the same region that produced the Odai Yamamoto pottery a few thousand years earlier).

Sannai-Maruyama was occupied continuously from roughly 3900 to 2300 BCE — sixteen hundred years in the same location. To put that in perspective: Sannai-Maruyama lasted longer than the entire span of recorded English history. It was not a temporary camp. It was a town. At its peak, it housed up to 500 people in hundreds of pit houses and aboveground longhouses, with dedicated zones for storage pits, burial areas, garbage middens, and ceremonial structures. The largest known structure on the site was a six-pillar building made from chestnut-wood pillars over a foot in diameter, set into deep post holes spaced regularly. The purpose of this six-pillar structure is debated — modern reconstructions show it as a tower of some kind, perhaps a watchtower, perhaps a ceremonial building, perhaps a marker visible from incoming canoes — but its construction required organized labor, careful timber management, and a level of social coordination far beyond anything you would associate with simple forager bands.

Sannai-Maruyama also shows long-distance trade. **Obsidian** (volcanic glass, prized for making razor-sharp cutting tools) from sources in Hokkaido and the Izu Islands has been found at the site. The Izu Islands are more than 350 miles (560 km) away by sea, with no land route — the obsidian moved by canoe across open ocean. **Jade** from the Itoigawa (ee-TOI-gah-wah) region on the Sea of Japan coast, over 300 miles (480 km) distant, was carved at Sannai-Maruyama into polished pendants and earrings. The Itoigawa jade-working tradition appears to be the oldest jade-working tradition in East Asia, predating the famous jade cultures of Neolithic China by several centuries. Amber, asphalt for waterproofing, and specialty stones for tools also moved through Jomon trade networks that connected communities across the archipelago and required navigation of waters that even modern small craft would not casually attempt.

The Jomon were not isolated villages scraping by on whatever the forest offered. They were networked communities with specialized production, long-distance maritime exchange, accumulated stores of nuts and dried fish, and enough surplus to invest in enormous construction projects and luxury goods. They were doing all of this without a single grain of cultivated rice.

---

What did they eat?

The Jomon diet has been reconstructed from a combination of evidence — bone chemistry from skeletal remains (which records what kinds of food a person consumed during their life), residue analysis of pottery (which records what was cooked in specific vessels), shell middens (the literally thousands of years of discarded shells around major coastal sites), and floral and faunal remains from waterlogged contexts. The reconstructed diet is varied and seasonal.

**Plants** were probably the largest single source of calories. Chestnuts and walnuts produced storable, calorie-dense food in the autumn that could be kept through the winter in lined storage pits. Acorns were important too, but they had to be processed extensively to leach out the bitter tannins — typically by grinding, soaking the meal in running water for days, then drying and storing the resulting flour. The Jomon mastered this process so thoroughly that acorns became one of the staple foods across most of the archipelago. Wild grapes, kiwifruit ancestors, wild yams, lily bulbs, ferns, and dozens of other plant foods rounded out the diet on a seasonal basis.

The Sannai-Maruyama site shows something even more interesting. Pollen analysis from the surrounding sediments shows a sustained increase in chestnut pollen throughout the period the site was occupied — far more than would be expected from a natural forest. DNA studies of preserved chestnut samples from the site show reduced genetic diversity compared to wild chestnut populations, consistent with deliberate human selection. The Sannai-Maruyama residents were apparently managing chestnut groves: planting, tending, perhaps grafting or selecting for desirable traits. This is **arboriculture** (tree-crop agriculture), and it blurs the conventional distinction between foraging and farming. The Jomon were not technically farmers, but they were actively shaping the forest to produce more of what they wanted to eat. The line is fuzzier than the textbook categories suggest.

**Marine resources** were the other great staple. Massive shell mounds (**kaizuka**, kai-ZOO-kah, in Japanese — "shell heaps") accumulated next to coastal settlements over thousands of years, growing into hills of discarded oyster, clam, and mussel shells dozens of feet thick. The Omori Shell Mounds near modern Tokyo, where Edward Morse did his 1877 excavation (Chapter 1), are one famous example among hundreds. Shellfish were the year-round protein staple. On top of that, the Jomon hunted larger marine animals — tuna and bonito from the open ocean, dolphins driven into shallow bays in mass hunts, occasional whales harvested when they beached or could be approached in canoes. Salmon runs in the northern rivers added enormous quantities of seasonal protein in late summer and fall, eaten fresh and also smoked or dried for winter.

**Land game** was a smaller but consistent contribution: deer, wild boar, hare, and various waterfowl and forest birds. Jomon hunting tools included the bow (probably arriving in the early Jomon period, replacing earlier spear and atlatl hunting) and stone-tipped arrows, with hunting dogs to track and flush game. The dogs themselves are an interesting case. Jomon archaeological sites consistently produce dog burials — the dogs interred with care, sometimes with grave goods, sometimes showing evidence of having been nursed back to health from injuries. These were not livestock. They were partners. Dogs are the only animal the Jomon domesticated, and the relationship appears to have been a working one: hunting companions that earned their keep and their burials.

A useful comparison: the Levantine Natufian culture (in modern Israel and Lebanon, around 12,500–9500 BCE) was the other great Old World case of complex hunter-gatherer settlement. The Natufians built stone-walled houses, harvested wild cereals with sickles, and kept dogs — but they got pottery much later (around 7000 BCE, four to five thousand years after the Jomon) and their settlements never grew as large as Sannai-Maruyama. The Jomon and the Natufians are independent demonstrations that hunter-gatherers, given the right ecology, can build sophisticated and persistent communities. The Jomon just did it bigger, with pottery, and for far longer.

---

Then there is the art.

Around 3500 BCE, potters in the **Niigata** (nee-GAH-tah) region of central Honshu began producing what may be the most visually dramatic ceramics in all of prehistory. **Flame-style pottery** (**kaen-doki**, kah-EN DOH-kee, literally "flame pots") features rims that explode upward in writhing, flame-like projections — coils and loops and tendrils of clay that twist above the vessel like frozen fire. The vessels are large (some over a foot tall), elaborately decorated, and absolutely impractical for daily cooking or storage. The flamboyant rims would break with ordinary use, and the deeply sculpted surfaces would be impossible to clean. These were almost certainly ceremonial objects — used in feasts, rituals, or display rather than in daily food preparation.

Flame-style pottery has no parallel anywhere in the prehistoric world. At the same time, around 3500 BCE, the potters of Mesopotamia (Mesopotamia Ch 2) were producing simple, functional vessels — competent but plain. Egyptian pottery was utilitarian. Chinese Neolithic pottery was elegant but restrained. Nobody else was making anything remotely like the Jomon flame pots. They represent an explosive investment of creative energy in clay, by people who had been making pottery for ten thousand years and had clearly decided that functional adequacy was not enough.

The other great Jomon art form is the **dogu** (DOH-goo), small clay figurines, typically five to ten inches tall, with exaggerated features: huge goggle-like eyes, swollen limbs, elaborate hairstyles or headdresses, and prominent breasts or bellies suggesting pregnancy. The most famous type, the **shakoki-dogu** (shah-KOH-kee DOH-goo, "snow-goggle dogu"), looks uncannily like a person wearing an Inuit-style snow-goggle eye-shield or a space helmet — a resemblance that has fueled decades of pseudoarchaeological alien-contact theories with zero scholarly support. The figurines were probably fertility symbols, ritual objects, or healing effigies. Many are found deliberately broken at the limbs or torso, suggesting they may have been ritually "killed" to absorb illness or misfortune from a person who was sick. Over 18,000 dogu have been found across Japan, overwhelmingly from the northern half of the archipelago, and their production intensified dramatically in the later Jomon period (Chapter 3) when the culture was under stress from climatic cooling.

The Jomon also developed lacquerware extraordinarily early. **Urushi** (oo-ROO-shee), the Japanese name for the lacquer derived from the sap of the lacquer tree (*Toxicodendron vernicifluum*, a relative of poison oak whose sap is highly toxic when wet but produces a durable, beautiful, water-resistant finish when properly applied and dried), appears in the Japanese archaeological record from at least 7000 BCE. Jomon craftspeople made lacquered combs, bowls, bracelets, and ornaments — sometimes dyed red with cinnabar or black with carbon — that have survived in waterlogged sites with their colors essentially intact. The lacquer tradition that the Jomon developed nine thousand years ago is the same tradition that still defines high-end Japanese craftsmanship today. The continuity is unbroken.

Cross-civ check: the long middle stretch of the Jomon period covered in this chapter — roughly 10,000 BCE to 1500 BCE — overlaps with most of what is usually called "the dawn of civilization" in other parts of the world. **Mesopotamia** went from pre-pottery Neolithic farming villages (~9000 BCE) through the rise of Sumer, the invention of writing, the world's first cities, the Akkadian Empire, and into the Old Babylonian period under Hammurabi. **Egypt** unified into a single kingdom under the first pharaoh around 3100 BCE, built the Great Pyramid around 2500 BCE, and went through the Old, Middle, and New Kingdoms. The **Indus Valley** civilization rose around 2600 BCE, built Mohenjo-Daro and Harappa, traded with Mesopotamia, and collapsed around 1900 BCE. **China** moved through the Yangshao and Longshan Neolithic cultures and into the early Bronze Age Shang Dynasty (~1600 BCE). The first Polynesian voyagers had not yet started crossing the Pacific (Polynesian Voyagers Ch 1 — they would not begin the great Pacific expansion until roughly 1500 BCE, the same moment our chapter ends). The Andes and Mesoamerica were both still pre-pottery and pre-agricultural for most of this period, slowly developing the early village cultures that would eventually produce the Olmec and the Norte Chico.

The Jomon were doing none of this. They were sitting on their islands, making pots, eating chestnuts and shellfish, and not building cities or empires or writing systems. The world around them was inventing civilization in the conventional sense. The Jomon were quietly demonstrating that there was a different way to live, and that a different way could last for thousands of years. The next chapter picks up around 2000 BCE, when the climate began to turn against them.

# Chapter 3 — The Late Jomon Contraction

Starting around 2000–1500 BCE, the climate of the Japanese archipelago shifted cooler and drier.

This was not a catastrophe. It was a slow, regional readjustment of temperature and rainfall patterns over several centuries — part of the broader cooling phase that climate historians call the **Neoglacial**, which affected much of the northern hemisphere in the second and first millennia BCE. The dense nut-bearing forests that had sustained the Jomon for millennia retreated, particularly in central and eastern Honshu, replaced by more sparse and seasonal vegetation. Salmon runs in the northern rivers became less reliable as river temperatures shifted. The abundant marine resources of the warmer mid-Holocene period — when sea levels and warmer currents had pushed productive ecosystems further north — became less productive in the regions where Jomon populations had concentrated.

The archaeological signal is unmistakable. Settlement counts in central and eastern Honshu drop sharply through the Late and Final Jomon periods (conventionally dated 2000–300 BCE). Population estimates derived from settlement density drop by roughly 50–70% across central Japan over those eighteen hundred years. Sannai-Maruyama itself was abandoned around 2300 BCE, after sixteen hundred years of continuous occupation, as part of the same general retreat. Communities in the colder north (Hokkaido) and the milder south (Kyushu and the Ryukyus) were affected less, and Jomon populations there persisted at higher densities, but the heartland of the great middle Jomon culture was contracting.

This is what cultures under stress often look like in the archaeological record: not a sudden collapse, but a gradual thinning out, with the most marginal settlements going first and the core areas hanging on at reduced scale. The Jomon were not disappearing. They were getting fewer, more dispersed, and (in some ways) more spiritually intense. The contraction lasted roughly a thousand years and overlapped, at the end, with the arrival of a completely different way of life from across the sea.

This chapter is about that long contraction — what it looked like, what the Jomon did about it, and how the stage was set for what came next.

---

The most visible Jomon response to environmental stress was an intensification of ritual.

This is a pattern that appears in many cultures under pressure: when material conditions get harder, when calories are scarcer and futures are less predictable, communities invest more time and energy in ceremonial activity. The mechanism is partly psychological (ritual provides a sense of control over uncontrollable circumstances), partly social (large-scale ceremonies bind together communities that might otherwise fragment), and partly economic (ritual systems can redistribute resources from periodic surplus to chronic shortage). The Late and Final Jomon archaeological record shows all three of these mechanisms at work.

**Stone circles** appeared in northern Honshu, notably at **Oyu** (OH-yoo) and **Komakino** (koh-MAH-kee-noh), starting around 2000 BCE and continuing into the first millennium BCE. The Oyu stone circles are the most famous: two large rings of boulders, roughly 150 feet (46 meters) across, with a smaller standing stone arrangement at the center of each ring (sometimes described as a "sundial" arrangement, though its actual function is uncertain). The circles are surrounded by burial pits, and the orientations of the central standing stones appear to align with sunrise positions at the summer or winter solstice. They were almost certainly used for seasonal gatherings — communities coming together at fixed astronomical points to bury their dead, perform ceremonies, exchange goods, and reaffirm their connection to one another and to the cosmos. Compared to earlier Jomon ritual sites (which tend to be smaller and less formally arranged), the Oyu stone circles represent a deliberate investment in monumental ceremony at a time when populations were declining and individual settlements were getting smaller. People were traveling long distances to gather at fixed ritual centers, and they were carrying or arranging multi-ton boulders to mark those centers permanently.

The cross-civ comparison is striking. The Oyu stone circles date to roughly the same period as **Stonehenge** in southern England (the main bluestone phase of which dates to around 2500–2200 BCE) and the megalithic monuments of Brittany and Ireland — all built by farming or transitional societies around the same time, all involving moving large stones into ceremonial arrangements with apparent astronomical orientations. There is no historical connection between these traditions. They are independent inventions of the same general idea — that the seasonal motion of the sun and moon should be marked in stone at gathering places where the dead are buried and the community comes together. The Jomon version is smaller and less famous, but it is the same impulse. And it is happening at the same moment that Mesopotamia and Egypt are building their first true monumental architecture (Mesopotamia Ch 4, Old Kingdom Egypt Ch 3) — a global moment of stone monument-building, distributed across cultures that had nothing to do with each other.

---

**Dogu figurine production intensified** during the Late and Final Jomon. The basic dogu form (Chapter 2) had existed for thousands of years, but the late period sees an explosion of new types and a sharp increase in total production. The famous **shakoki-dogu** type — the goggle-eyed figurines that look like a person wearing snow goggles or a space helmet — emerged in northern Honshu around 1500 BCE, in the heart of the Final Jomon contraction. Over 18,000 dogu have been found across Japan, with the bulk dating to this stressed late period.

Why goggle eyes? The honest answer is: nobody knows. The most popular scholarly interpretations connect the goggle-eyed look to actual Inuit-style snow goggles (slit eye-shields used to prevent snow blindness in extreme cold environments), arguing that the figurines represent shamanic or ritual practitioners wearing protective headgear in trance states. Another reading sees the bulging eyes as a stylized depiction of a fertility goddess or earth-mother figure. A third reading connects the goggle eyes to a specific ritual procedure (perhaps something involving smoke or hallucinogens) that produced visible eye changes. None of these interpretations can be confirmed without text — and there is no Jomon text. The dogu are mute. They represent a major investment of artistic and ritual energy by a culture under stress, and we can describe them in great detail without ever knowing what they meant to the people who made them.

Many shakoki-dogu and other late-period figurines are found deliberately broken — heads snapped off, limbs cracked apart, torsos shattered into pieces. The breakage patterns are too consistent to be accidental, and the broken pieces are often found buried separately, sometimes in different parts of the same site. The standard interpretation is that the figurines were ritually "killed" — broken on purpose, perhaps to absorb illness or misfortune from a sick person, perhaps to release spiritual energy, perhaps as part of an annual cycle of renewal in which old figurines were destroyed and new ones made. This is a kind of practice that anthropologists have documented in many traditional societies, where the destruction of a ritual object is itself the ritual act.

Burial practices became more elaborate too. Late Jomon graves include grave goods (jade beads, polished stone tools, occasional ceramic vessels), bodies arranged in specific orientations, and sometimes mass graves where multiple individuals were buried together with apparent ceremony. The funerary investment was not driven by social hierarchy — Jomon society remained essentially egalitarian, and there is no evidence of a chiefly elite buried with markedly richer grave goods than commoners. It was driven by community-wide ceremonial intensity. People were spending more time and resources on ritual response to death.

---

The contraction was not uniform across the archipelago.

In northern Hokkaido, the Jomon-derived populations actually thrived through the Late and Final periods. The cooler climate suited Hokkaido better than central Honshu, the marine resources off the northern coast remained productive, and salmon runs in Hokkaido rivers continued to provide reliable seasonal protein. Hokkaido's Jomon populations would later develop into the **Epi-Jomon** (post-Jomon) and eventually the **Satsumon** (sah-TSOO-mon) and **Ainu** cultures, which maintained a distinct hunter-gatherer-fisher way of life into the modern historical period. The Ainu are the genetic and cultural descendants of the Jomon people in the most direct sense — a continuous tradition that persisted in Hokkaido long after the Yayoi transformation had remade Honshu and Kyushu (Chapter 4). Even today, Ainu communities in Hokkaido carry the strongest Jomon ancestry of any modern population.

In the southern Ryukyu Islands, a similar pattern played out. The Ryukyus were too small and too geographically isolated to support a Yayoi-style agricultural society at first, and the Jomon-derived population there persisted with its own variant of the broader Jomon tradition for centuries longer. Modern Ryukyu Islanders (especially those from Okinawa and the smaller southern islands) carry Jomon ancestry significantly higher than the average modern Japanese.

In the geographic middle — Honshu, Kyushu, Shikoku — the contraction was sharpest, and it was here that the next chapter of Japanese prehistory would be written. By roughly 1000 BCE, Jomon populations across these central islands were a fraction of what they had been at the height of the middle Jomon period. The forests were smaller. The settlements were smaller and fewer. The trade networks that had once reached from Hokkaido obsidian to Itoigawa jade had thinned. The flame pots had stopped being made. The dogu were still being broken in ritual at northern sites, but the sheer cultural exuberance of the middle Jomon was gone.

Then, sometime around 900 BCE, the first foreign canoes appeared on the beaches of northern Kyushu.

---

A pause, before the chronology takes over: the Jomon period as a whole — from around 14,500 BCE to roughly 300 BCE — lasted approximately 14,000 years, which is one of the longest continuous cultural traditions in human history. To put that in perspective: the entire span of ancient Egyptian civilization, from the first pharaoh to Cleopatra, fits inside the Jomon period about four times over. The Roman Empire, from Romulus to the fall of Constantinople, fits inside the Jomon period roughly four times. Recorded Chinese history, from the Shang Dynasty to today, fits inside it about four times. The Jomon people lived in the same archipelago, using variations of the same basic material culture, for longer than the entire recorded history of every literate civilization combined.

When the Jomon ended, it ended not with collapse but with replacement. The Final Jomon population that the Yayoi immigrants encountered around 900 BCE was already smaller and more fragile than it had been a millennium earlier. The newcomers brought a technological package that solved the food problem the Jomon were struggling with (rice produced reliable surplus calories that did not depend on forest health), and the demographic balance tipped quickly. Within a few centuries the Jomon way of life had been transformed beyond recognition in the central and southern parts of the archipelago.

But the Jomon did not vanish. They left their mark in DNA (modern Japanese carry significant Jomon ancestry, with the highest concentrations in Hokkaido and the Ryukyus). They left their mark in culture — the lacquer tradition, the deep relationship to the sea, the persistent thread of nature-spirit veneration that would eventually crystallize into Shinto (the indigenous Japanese spiritual tradition that venerates nature spirits and the animating forces in trees, rocks, rivers, and mountains). They left their mark in the oldest pots on Earth, sitting in museum cases in Aomori and Tokyo, fired by hands that had no metal, no farming, no writing, and no idea that they were doing anything that the rest of the world would care about fifteen thousand years later.

Cross-civ check: the Late and Final Jomon period (roughly 2000–300 BCE) overlaps with the rise of the Shang Dynasty (Shang Dynasty Ch 1) and the Zhou Dynasty (Zhou Dynasty Ch 1) in China, the Vedic period in India (Vedic Period Ch 1), the New Kingdom in Egypt (New Kingdom Egypt Ch 1) and the Late Period that followed, the Mycenaean civilization in Greece (Mycenaean Civilization Ch 1) and the subsequent Greek Dark Age, the Phoenician trading network across the Mediterranean (Phoenicia Ch 1), and the rise of the Assyrian Empire (Assyrian Empire Ch 1). The Polynesian voyagers were just beginning their great Pacific expansion (Polynesian Voyagers Ch 1) from Taiwan and the Philippines, eventually crossing the open Pacific to colonize islands as far as Hawaii and New Zealand. The first kingdoms of the Andes were forming (Early Andean Civilizations Ch 4–5). The Olmec civilization was emerging in Mexico (Olmec Civilization Ch 1). Almost everywhere else in the world, complex societies were forming, expanding, fighting each other, leaving written records. On the Japanese islands, an old way of life was running down quietly, until the arrival of one new technology from Korea changed everything.

# Chapter 4 — The Yayoi Revolution

Around 900 BCE, and in a concentrated wave after about 300 BCE, the most dramatic transformation in Japanese history before the Meiji Restoration of the nineteenth century arrived on the beaches of northern Kyushu.

It did not arrive as an army. It arrived as rice.

**Wet-rice agriculture** (growing rice in flooded paddy fields fed by controlled irrigation, then draining the fields for harvest) came to Japan from the Korean Peninsula, brought by migrants who crossed the roughly 120 miles (200 km) of the Korea Strait. The crossing was not a single voyage by a single fleet. It was a sustained migration over centuries, probably moving in stages — first to the islands of **Tsushima** (tsoo-SHEE-mah) and **Iki** (EE-kee) in the strait itself, then onto the northern Kyushu coast, then gradually inland and eastward. These newcomers brought not only rice seeds and paddy-field technology but also bronze and iron tools, a different style of pottery, a different physical type, and a way of life built around settled farming, surplus production, and the social hierarchies that surplus inevitably creates.

The transition from Jomon foraging to **Yayoi** (yah-YOY) farming was, as noted, the most transformative event in Japanese history before the Meiji period of the 1860s. In the space of a few centuries, the Japanese archipelago went from a land of declining hunter-gatherer-fishers who had lived without agriculture for 13,000 years to a land of rice-farming villages organized into competing chiefdoms, armed with metal weapons, and fighting each other for control of the best paddy land. Everything that came afterward — the Yamato state, the emperor, Buddhism, the samurai, the shogunate, modern Japan — grew from roots planted in the Yayoi period. This chapter is about how that happened, who the new arrivals were, and what the rice did to Japanese society once it took hold.

---

The name "Yayoi" comes from a district in Tokyo where a distinctive style of pottery was first scientifically described in 1884. Compared to the exuberant flame pots and goggle-eyed dogu of the Jomon (Chapters 2–3), Yayoi pottery is restrained, functional, and elegant — thin-walled, smooth-surfaced, often reddish-brown, made for cooking rice and storing grain rather than for ritual display. The aesthetic shift from Jomon to Yayoi is one of the most visually striking transitions in the archaeological record: from wild creativity to practical efficiency, from art for art's sake to tools for the harvest. You can sort Jomon and Yayoi pottery into separate piles in five seconds without looking at the dating evidence — they look that different.

The cultural shift that the pottery records was equally drastic.

The critical question about the Yayoi transformation is: did new people arrive, or did existing Jomon communities adopt the new technology?

The answer, based on decades of archaeological and genetic research, is: both, but with a heavy emphasis on migration. The Yayoi people were physically different from the Jomon — taller on average (by perhaps two inches), with narrower faces, less robust skeletal frames, and different dental patterns, all consistent with continental East Asian populations and distinct from the Jomon-era population. Genetic studies of Yayoi-period skeletal remains, compared to both Jomon-period remains and modern East Asian populations, confirm that a substantial influx of people from the Korean Peninsula (and ultimately from continental East Asia further north) mixed with the existing Jomon population. The continental genetic contribution became dominant in most of western Japan, where the Yayoi arrival was earliest and most concentrated, while Jomon genetics persisted more strongly in the north (Hokkaido) and the far south (the Ryukyu Islands), as established in Chapter 3.

The mechanism of transition varied by region. In northern Kyushu, where the continental immigrants arrived first, the replacement was rapid — within a few generations, Yayoi-style sites replaced Jomon ones almost completely. The new villages clustered around rice paddies, used Yayoi-style pottery, buried their dead in distinctive jar coffins, and physically resembled mainland East Asians more than they resembled the Jomon people who had been there a century earlier. Further east on Honshu, the process was slower and more mixed, with Jomon and Yayoi communities existing side by side for centuries, trading, intermarrying, and gradually blending. In Hokkaido, the Yayoi package never fully penetrated at all — the island's cold climate was unsuitable for wet-rice farming, and Jomon-descended cultures (eventually developing into the Satsumon and then the distinct Ainu tradition) persisted into the historical period. In the Ryukyus, the Yayoi transformation arrived even later and even more partially.

The Korean connection is direct and well documented. The earliest Yayoi rice paddies, found at sites like **Itazuke** (ee-TAH-zoo-keh) and **Nabatake** (nah-bah-TAH-keh) in northern Kyushu, use techniques identical to contemporary rice farming on the Korean Peninsula at the same period (Ancient Korea Ch 2). The pottery styles show Korean parallels. The bronze and iron that accompanied the Yayoi migration came from Korean and Chinese sources. Korean immigrants of this era and later (known collectively in Japanese sources as **toraijin**, toh-RYE-jin, "people who came across") would continue arriving in Japan for centuries, bringing wave after wave of continental technology, and their cumulative contribution to early Japanese civilization was enormous — a fact that the Ancient Korea narrative addresses from the Korean side. The early Yayoi transformation is the first and most consequential of these waves, but it was not the last.

---

Why did this work so well, so fast, in a region where the Jomon way of life had been entrenched for ten thousand years?

The short answer is that Final Jomon Japan was vulnerable. The climatic stress of Chapter 3 had reduced Jomon populations across central and western Japan. The forests had thinned. The communities were smaller and more dispersed. When the Yayoi immigrants arrived with a technology that produced reliable surplus calories independent of forest health — a paddy field, properly managed, will produce roughly ten times the food per acre that even the most productive forest will produce, and the calories come on a predictable annual schedule — they had a substantial demographic advantage. Yayoi villages grew faster, supported larger families, and could expand into new river valleys. Jomon communities in the same regions were already shrinking. Within a few generations, the Yayoi villages outnumbered the Jomon ones, and within a few centuries the older way of life was gone from western Japan.

Rice changed the rules of Japanese society completely.

In the Jomon world, food came from the forest and the sea — abundant resources but ones that could not easily be accumulated, stored at large scale, or controlled by elites. You could not monopolize a salmon run the way you could monopolize a granary. You could not put a bay full of shellfish under armed guard. Rice was different. Rice could be grown in surplus, stored for years in raised granaries, measured in standardized units, taxed, redistributed by authority, exchanged across long distances, and used to support people who did not produce their own food. Whoever controlled the paddies and the granaries controlled the community. For the first time in Japanese history, wealth could be concentrated, and concentrated wealth meant concentrated power.

The archaeological evidence for this transformation is stark. Yayoi settlements show clear social stratification: some burials contain bronze weapons, jade ornaments, and Chinese mirrors, while most contain nothing. Settlements grew larger and became fortified — ditches, palisades, and watchtowers appear in the archaeological record for the first time. Yayoi-period skeletons from Kyushu sites frequently show evidence of violent death: arrowheads embedded in bone, sword cuts on skulls, decapitated bodies in defensive ditches. The Jomon period had shown remarkably little evidence of organized violence; people died of old age, illness, accidents, occasional assaults, but nothing that looks like systematic warfare. The Yayoi period is drenched in it.

This pattern — rice agriculture producing surplus, surplus producing inequality, inequality producing warfare, warfare producing fortified settlements and chiefly elites — is identical to what happened in Korea (Ancient Korea Ch 2) a few centuries earlier and in China (Shang Dynasty Ch 1, Zhou Dynasty Ch 1) earlier still. Japan compressed the transition into a few centuries. In the Near East, the shift from foraging to farming to warfare to states took over five thousand years. In China, it took roughly three thousand. In Japan, it happened in less than a thousand. The reason for the speed is that Japan did not have to invent any of the steps. The Korean immigrants imported the entire package as a working bundle: seeds, irrigation knowledge, paddy construction methods, granary designs, bronze and iron tools, weaving technology, the social pattern of the chiefly village. The Jomon population that survived just had to adapt to a new system that had already been worked out elsewhere.

A useful cross-civ comparison: when Roman wheat-growing arrived in northern Europe in the first centuries BCE, it transformed the Iron Age cultures of Gaul and Britain in much the same compressed way — within a few centuries, the older patterns of mixed pastoral-arable subsistence were reorganized around large-scale grain production, granaries, taxation, and Roman-style social hierarchy. The Yayoi transformation was the East Asian island version of the same general process: a more productive agricultural system imported from a more developed neighbor, restructuring everything beneath it.

---

Along with rice and iron came bronze, but the Japanese used bronze in a way that nobody else in the world ever did.

**Dotaku** (DOH-tah-koo) are bronze bells, some over three feet (one meter) tall, cast in the **Kinai** (KEE-nai) region (the area around modern Osaka and Nara, the political heartland of Japan for the next thousand years). They are elaborately decorated with scenes of daily life — harvesting, hunting, animals, buildings, longboats — and with geometric patterns. They were never rung. Most have no clappers. Many were buried on hilltops or in remote locations, deliberately hidden from daily life, sometimes alone and sometimes in groups of several bells together. Hundreds have been found across western Japan, none with surviving inscriptions to explain their purpose.

What were they for?

The honest answer is that nobody knows for certain. The standard interpretation reads them as ritual objects connected to the agricultural cycle — offerings to something, prayers for a good harvest, ceremonial deposits made at moments of seasonal transition or community crisis. The hilltop burial pattern suggests the bells were not stored as personal property but were deposited in the landscape, possibly to mark territory, possibly to communicate with whatever deity oversaw the rice growing in the valleys below. Some scholars argue that the bells started out as functional sound-making instruments (early small dotaku do appear to have been rung, with associated pendulum holes) and became progressively larger, more decorated, and less functional over the centuries — evolving into pure ritual objects whose value was symbolic rather than acoustic.

What is clear is that the dotaku represent a uniquely Japanese response to continental bronze technology. Korean and Chinese bronze of the same period was used for weapons, ritual vessels in the Chinese style (bronze tripods, wine cups), and personal ornaments. Japanese bronze was poured into these enormous bells that nobody rang and then buried in the hills. Nothing comparable exists in Korea, China, or anywhere else. The Yayoi people took the technology of bronze and used it for purposes that emerged from their own cultural priorities — and those priorities were not Korean or Chinese.

The distinction between bronze and iron in Yayoi Japan is also important. Japan received both metals at roughly the same time (unlike the Near East, where bronze preceded iron by nearly two millennia, or unlike China, where bronze preceded iron by about a thousand years), and the Japanese assigned them complementary functions: bronze became the metal for ritual objects (bells, mirrors, ceremonial weapons), while iron became the metal for practical tools (hoes, axes, sickles, arrowheads, swords for actual killing). This functional split — bronze for the gods, iron for the world — mirrors the Korean pattern of the same period (Ancient Korea Ch 2) and suggests that the Japanese were not blindly copying continental metalwork but making deliberate choices about how to use each metal. Bronze was beautiful and valuable but soft; iron was tougher and better for cutting. The Japanese sorted the two into separate cultural roles and stuck with the sorting for centuries.

---

By around 100 BCE, the Yayoi transformation of central and western Japan was essentially complete. The forests had been reduced to clear paddies wherever the river valleys allowed irrigation. The villages were larger, denser, fortified, and organized into petty chiefdoms competing for control of the best paddy land and the trade routes that brought continental goods. Population had grown enormously — estimates suggest the Yayoi-era population of Japan was perhaps three to five times the population of the Final Jomon period a millennium earlier. The dotaku bells were being buried in the hills. The shell mounds along the coast were still accumulating but were now next to towns rather than next to nomadic camps. The Jomon way of life had retreated to the cold north and the warm south, and was vanishing fast even there.

What this archipelago did not yet have was political unity, writing, or any way to communicate its existence to the rest of the world. That changes in the next chapter, when Chinese chronicles begin to mention the Japanese islands by name, when Chinese and Korean trade goods begin appearing in Yayoi elite burials, and when the first Japanese delegation travels to a Chinese imperial court and comes home with a tiny gold seal that an eighteenth-century farmer will pull out of a Kyushu rice paddy more than seventeen hundred years later.

Cross-civ check: the Yayoi transformation period covered in this chapter (~900 BCE to 100 BCE) overlaps with the late Zhou Dynasty and Warring States period in China (Zhou Dynasty Ch 5–8), the rise and unification of the Qin Dynasty (Qin Dynasty Ch 1) and the establishment of the Han Dynasty (Han Dynasty Ch 1), the Maurya Empire in India (Maurya Empire Ch 1) and the post-Maurya kingdoms that followed (Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 1), the early Roman Republic and its Mediterranean expansion (Ancient Rome Ch 1–4), the rise of Carthage as a Mediterranean trading power (Carthage Ch 1), and the height of the Persian Empire's Achaemenid period (Persian Empire Ch 1) followed by Alexander's conquests. In Korea, the Three Kingdoms period was just emerging from the earlier confederations (Ancient Korea Ch 3). The cascade of cultural transmission from China through Korea to Japan that defines the next thousand years of East Asian history was just beginning. Japan, at the receiving end of that cascade, was about to emerge from prehistoric isolation into the recorded history of its more powerful neighbors.

# Chapter 5 — A Hundred Warring Kingdoms

Around 100 BCE, the Chinese began to write about Japan.

Not warmly, and not in much detail. To the Han chroniclers in the Chinese capital, the Japanese islands were a far-eastern frontier — a chain of small foggy islands across a difficult strait, inhabited by tribal people of no obvious importance, useful mainly as a marker of how far Chinese civilization reached. Japan does not appear in Chinese sources as a coherent country. It appears as **Wa** (WAH), the Chinese name for the Japanese islands, written with a Chinese character that some later Japanese readers considered derogatory (it can be read with a meaning of "dwarf" or "submissive" — though the original meaning of the character was probably more neutral, perhaps referring to the bowing posture that Japanese envoys assumed at the Chinese court). The Chinese called the people **Wajin** (WAH-jin), "the Wa people," and described them as living in over a hundred small kingdoms, constantly at war with each other, governed sometimes by male chiefs and sometimes by female ones.

This is the first time Japan appears in a written historical record produced anywhere. The Japanese themselves had no writing yet (Chinese writing would not arrive in Japan for another five centuries — see Chapter 8), and would have no native records of their own existence until the **Kojiki** was compiled in 712 CE. For the entire period covered in this chapter — from roughly 100 BCE to 250 CE — almost everything we know about Japanese politics and society comes from Chinese chroniclers writing about a place they had visited only through occasional trade missions or had heard about secondhand from Korean intermediaries. The Chinese accounts are thin, biased, and full of details that may or may not be accurate. They are also the only thing we have. When you read about "Queen Himiko of Yamatai" in the next chapter, you are reading about a Japanese ruler whose existence is documented by a Chinese imperial historian writing decades after she died, in a language she never spoke, using a script she could not read. That is the documentary situation for late Yayoi Japan.

This chapter is about what the Chinese saw when they looked east — and what the archaeology shows about the Japanese society that the Chinese were describing.

---

The political picture that emerges from the Chinese sources, cross-referenced with the archaeology, is roughly this.

Yayoi Japan in the first centuries BCE and CE was a patchwork of small chiefdoms, each controlling perhaps a few river valleys' worth of rice paddies, with a population in the low thousands or tens of thousands. The Chinese estimate of "over a hundred kingdoms" is probably approximately accurate — the archaeological record shows hundreds of fortified Yayoi settlements distributed across western Honshu and Kyushu, each surrounded by smaller satellite villages, each apparently functioning as the political center of a small polity. None of these chiefdoms had centralized authority over more than a small territory. None had a written legal code. None had a standing professional army (though they could mobilize armed villagers when threatened). They were organized around chiefly families that controlled the best paddy land, the storehouses that held the surplus rice, and the rituals that legitimized their position.

The fortifications speak for themselves. The largest known Yayoi settlement, **Yoshinogari** (yoh-shee-noh-GAH-ree) in northern Kyushu, was a moated and palisaded town, with multiple defensive ditches surrounding the inner core, watchtowers at the corners, raised-floor storehouses to keep the grain dry and out of reach of vermin, an elite burial precinct where leaders were interred in large jar coffins with bronze weapons and ornaments, and an outer perimeter dotted with smaller residential clusters. Yoshinogari covered roughly 100 acres at its peak in the second and first centuries BCE. It was not a city in the Mesopotamian sense (no monumental temples, no palace complexes, no written records, no sophisticated craft specialization), but it was decisively more than a village. It was a fortified town with a chiefly elite, defensive infrastructure, and clear social stratification. And it expected to be attacked.

Yoshinogari was not unique. Similar fortified Yayoi settlements have been excavated at sites across Kyushu and western Honshu. The Yayoi countryside was an arms race. Skeletal remains from cemeteries near these settlements show high rates of violent death — arrowheads embedded in vertebrae, sword cuts on skulls, decapitated bodies, evidence of organized combat between groups rather than individual murders. The Jomon period (Chapters 2–3) had been remarkably peaceful by archaeological standards. The Yayoi was the opposite. The transition to rice agriculture had brought, along with surplus and wealth, the systematic violence that the rest of the world knew as a normal feature of agrarian life.

---

Trade with the continent intensified through this period.

Bronze mirrors are the diagnostic artifact. Chinese bronze mirrors of the Han period (after 206 BCE) appear in elite Yayoi burials across Kyushu and into western Honshu — round polished discs of bronze with elaborate cosmological designs on the back (depicting the four directional spirits, the constellations, the symbolism of imperial Chinese cosmology) and a smooth reflective surface on the front. These mirrors were prestige objects. They signaled connection to the Chinese court, sophistication, exotic provenance, and the legitimacy that comes from owning what your rivals could not. Yayoi chiefs accumulated them, displayed them, were buried with them. Local Japanese craftsmen began copying them, producing imitation bronze mirrors that reproduced the Chinese cosmological designs without much understanding of their meaning — the way a medieval European craftsman might copy an Islamic inscription in stylized loops without knowing it was Arabic. The copies multiplied. Bronze mirrors became one of the standard markers of elite status in late Yayoi Japan.

Other Chinese trade goods arrived too: bronze weapons and tools, jade and glass beads, Han-period coins (occasionally, as collectors' items more than as currency), and possibly silk (though silk does not preserve well in most archaeological contexts, so the evidence is indirect). The trade flowed both ways: Japanese pearls, gold dust, sulfur, and (according to one Chinese source) live monkeys went west to the Chinese market, carried by Korean intermediaries. The volume was small. The diplomatic and prestige importance was large. A chiefly Yayoi family that could display Chinese goods at a feast was advertising its connections, its wealth, and its willingness to project status outward.

---

In 57 CE, the first formally documented diplomatic contact between Japan and China occurred.

According to the Chinese **Book of the Later Han** (Hou Hanshu, hoh hahn-SHOO, a Chinese dynastic history compiled in the fifth century CE covering the Later Han dynasty from 6 to 189 CE), a delegation from the **Kingdom of Na** (NAH) — one of the small polities in northern Kyushu, probably based in or near modern Fukuoka — traveled to the Chinese capital and received a gold seal from Emperor Guangwu (gwahng-WOO). The seal, a small square of solid gold roughly an inch on each side with a knob carved as a coiled snake, was inscribed in five Chinese characters: "King of Na of Wa, [vassal of] Han." It signified the formal recognition by the Chinese emperor of the Na chief as a tributary king — a vassal in the Chinese tributary system, integrated into the Chinese diplomatic order at the lowest rung but integrated nonetheless.

This was a striking moment. A Japanese chieftain from a small polity in Kyushu had sailed across the strait to Korea and then traveled overland or by coastal ship all the way to the Han Chinese capital — a journey of well over a thousand miles each way through territory entirely outside his control — to acknowledge himself as the formal subordinate of an emperor he would never meet, and to bring home a small gold token of that relationship. The motivation was political prestige. A chief who could come home from China carrying a Chinese imperial seal had a legitimacy that none of his rivals possessed. The seal was the most valuable single object in northern Kyushu, not because gold was scarce (it was) but because the diplomatic recognition it represented could not be replicated locally.

Then the seal disappeared from the historical record for seventeen hundred years.

In 1784, a farmer named Jinbei (jin-BAY) was digging an irrigation ditch on the small island of Shikanoshima (shee-kah-noh-SHEE-mah) in Hakata Bay, near modern Fukuoka in northern Kyushu, when he turned up a tiny gold object that turned out to be a square seal with a snake-shaped knob. Jinbei brought it to the local lord, the Kuroda clan, who had it examined by scholars. The inscription on the bottom of the seal — five Chinese characters reading "Han Wei Nu Guo Wang" (the Chinese reading of "King of Na of Wa, [vassal of] Han") — matched the description in the Chinese Hou Hanshu exactly. The **Gold Seal of the King of Na**, now one of Japan's most famous archaeological artifacts and a designated National Treasure, is physical proof that the Chinese chronicles were telling the truth about early Japanese diplomacy. Some Chinese chronicler in the second or third century CE had recorded a piece of bureaucratic paperwork from the year 57. Seventeen centuries later, a Kyushu farmer dug up the corresponding object from a rice paddy. The two pieces of evidence fit together exactly.

This is the kind of moment that makes early Japanese history possible to write. The Chinese chronicles are full of details about Wa that we cannot otherwise verify. The Gold Seal anchors them in physical reality. If the Chinese were right about this very specific bureaucratic event in 57 CE, they were probably right about a lot of other things too. The seal is the documentary keystone of the entire late Yayoi period.

---

The Chinese chronicles continue to mention Wa periodically through the second century CE.

In 107 CE, another delegation arrived at the Han court, this time from a "King of Wa" (suggesting that one chief had become powerful enough to claim general overlordship over the Japanese islands, or at least over enough of them to project the claim to China), bringing 160 slaves as tribute. Whether the gift of slaves implies organized large-scale Wa raiding (capturing people from rival polities to send to China) or just a chiefly household supplying a substantial tribute from its own population is unclear. Either way, the scale of the offering — 160 people — gives a sense of the demographic resources that a leading Wa chief could command at this point. This was not a small village headman scraping together a token gift. This was a regional power.

By the late second century CE, the Chinese sources begin describing a period of intense civil war in Wa. The phrase used in the Chinese histories is essentially "the country was in turmoil for many years, and the people had no ruler." The chiefdoms were fighting each other on a sustained basis, and no single polity was strong enough to impose order. This is an interesting moment, because it is precisely the moment when, according to the same Chinese sources, the Japanese eventually solved the problem by selecting a female shaman ruler — and her story is what the next chapter is about.

---

A note on what we are reading and what we are not reading.

The Chinese chronicles of Wa are external observations of a foreign culture by writers who had never visited the islands themselves. They were working from secondhand reports brought by Korean intermediaries, occasional tribute-bearing envoys, and travelers' tales. They had political reasons to fit Wa into the Chinese tributary framework — describing Wa kings as recognizing the Chinese emperor's authority, recording tribute missions as if they were submissions, treating the Japanese rulers as petty vassals. They had cultural reasons to exoticize what they described — emphasizing strange customs, primitive practices, female rulers (which the Chinese found noteworthy because Chinese culture preferred male rulers), bodily decorations like tattooing (which the Chinese considered barbaric).

These biases do not invalidate the chronicles — the Gold Seal of the King of Na shows that the basic factual content is reliable — but they do shape how we should read them. When the Chinese describe Yayoi Japan as a hundred warring kingdoms, that is probably approximately accurate, and the archaeology supports it. When the Chinese describe Yayoi Japanese as living in pit houses, fishing the sea, growing rice, tattooing themselves, and burying their dead in jar coffins, the archaeology supports those claims too. When the Chinese describe specific political events — embassies, gifts, named rulers — they are reporting what the Wa themselves told the Chinese court, filtered through Chinese understanding. The reader should treat the Chinese sources as serious historical evidence with reasonable internal consistency, not as either gospel truth or pure invention. They are the best we have until Japanese historical writing emerges in the eighth century CE, and they are good enough.

---

The Polynesian voyagers were settling Tonga and Samoa (Polynesian Voyagers Ch 3) on their long eastward expansion. Japan in the late Yayoi was, in world-historical terms, a mid-sized agrarian society at roughly the same political stage as the Korean peninsula — a step or two behind Han China, a step or two ahead of the Polynesian island colonies, on its own developmental track but visibly converging with the broader East Asian pattern that would define everything that came next.

# Chapter 6 — Queen Himiko and Yamatai

Sometime in the late second or early third century CE, after years of civil war among the chiefdoms of Wa, the Japanese did something unusual in their part of the world. They picked a woman to rule them.

Her name was **Himiko** (hee-MEE-koh, sometimes written Pimiko in older Western scholarship), and she ruled a confederation called **Yamatai** (yah-mah-TYE). She is the first historically documented ruler of Japan — the first specific Japanese person whose existence is confirmed by a written source from any culture. The Japanese themselves did not record her existence (they had no writing yet). The Koreans did not record her existence (Korean writing was still developing, and what survives from this period does not mention her). She is documented entirely by Chinese chroniclers writing about her decades after her death, based on reports from Wei court officials who had hosted her envoys, in a Chinese text called the **Wei Zhi** (way-JUH, "Records of Wei") compiled around 297 CE.

The Wei Zhi account of Himiko is short — perhaps two thousand characters in classical Chinese, less than one printed page in English translation — but it is the densest single passage of information about prehistoric Japan that exists anywhere. Almost everything in the Yamatai story comes from those few pages. Almost every detail is contested. And the location of Yamatai itself remains, after a century and a half of intense Japanese scholarly debate, unresolved. This chapter is about what the Wei Zhi says, what the archaeology suggests, and why the Yamatai question still matters.

---

The Wei Zhi describes Himiko as a ruler who "occupied herself with magic and sorcery, bewitching the people," who came to power as the resolution of a long civil war among the chiefs of Wa, who was served by a thousand female attendants, and who lived in a palace heavily fortified with watchtowers and surrounded by armed guards. She was rarely seen in public. She had no husband. She communicated with the outside world through a single younger brother, who was the only adult male permitted into her presence. After she died (around 247 CE, give or take a few years), over a hundred attendants were sacrificed and buried with her. A male successor was chosen but the people would not obey him, and conflict broke out again. Order was finally restored only when a thirteen-year-old female relative of Himiko's named **Iyo** (EE-yoh) was installed as queen, and the Wei court resumed receiving Yamatai envoys.

The Wei Zhi also describes the diplomatic relationship in detail. In 238 CE, Himiko sent envoys to the Chinese Wei court (the Wei was one of the Three Kingdoms that divided China after the fall of the Han Dynasty, controlling the northern part of the country with its capital at Luoyang). The envoys offered tribute — slaves and cloth — and asked for formal recognition. The Wei court responded by granting Himiko the title of "Queen of Wa, friendly to Wei," along with a generous gift of one hundred bronze mirrors, gold, silk, swords, and a formal seal (a different seal from the one given to the Kingdom of Na in 57 CE — see Chapter 5 — and one which has not been found archaeologically, though numerous mirrors that may have come from this Wei gift have turned up in third-century Japanese burials). Himiko sent further envoys in 243 CE. After her death, Iyo sent envoys in 266 CE. Then the Chinese sources go quiet on Wa for nearly a century and a half.

---

Everything about Himiko is fascinating, and almost everything about her is debated.

Start with the basics. **Where was Yamatai?** This is the central unresolved question of late Yayoi / early Kofun Japanese archaeology, and Japanese scholars have been arguing about it for over a hundred and fifty years. The Wei Zhi gives travel directions for reaching Yamatai from Korea — sail from the Korean coast to Tsushima, from Tsushima to Iki, from Iki to northern Kyushu, then proceed by various combinations of sea and land travel for specific distances in specific directions. The directions are detailed but maddeningly ambiguous when you try to plot them on a real map. Different reasonable interpretations of the distances and the bearings put Yamatai in two completely different places: either in northern Kyushu (specifically in the area around modern Fukuoka or Saga, possibly on the same general site as Yoshinogari), or in the **Kinai** region of central Honshu (the area around modern Nara, the political heartland of the later Yamato state).

The two hypotheses have very different implications. If Yamatai was in northern Kyushu, it was a regional power — one of many warring chiefdoms, the largest of its peer group, but local in scope. The later Yamato state would then have emerged independently in central Honshu, perhaps influenced by Yamatai but not directly descended from it. If Yamatai was in the Kinai region, it was already centered where the later Yamato state would centralize — and it was probably a direct ancestor of the Yamato confederation that would unify Japan in the next two centuries. The political stakes of the question for Japanese national history are not small. The Kinai hypothesis links the imperial line directly back to Himiko. The Kyushu hypothesis breaks that connection. Both hypotheses have major Japanese scholars on their side. Both have been argued in extensive scholarly literature for over a century. Neither has been conclusively settled.

The archaeology has narrowed but not closed the debate. Several large early kofun (the keyhole-shaped burial mounds that would define the next era — see Chapter 7) in the Kinai region date to roughly the same period as Himiko's death, which is suggestive of a Kinai location. The **Hashihaka Kofun** (hah-shee-HAH-kah) in Nara Prefecture, dated by ceramic typology to roughly 250 CE and roughly 280 meters long, has been proposed as a candidate for Himiko's tomb specifically — its construction date matches her death date, and it is one of the earliest large keyhole-shaped tombs in Japan. The Imperial Household Agency, which administers most of the major kofun as imperial tombs, has not permitted full excavation of the Hashihaka Kofun (or of any other tomb officially designated as an imperial burial), so the identification remains tentative. But the candidate is interesting. If Hashihaka is Himiko's tomb, then Yamatai was in Kinai, then the later imperial line is directly continuous with the Yamatai confederation, then Himiko is essentially the founder of Japanese political continuity. That is a lot to hang on a tomb that has not been excavated, but it is the leading current hypothesis among Japanese archaeologists.

---

What was Himiko, politically?

The Wei Zhi describes her as a **shaman queen** — a ruler whose authority came not from military force or hereditary lineage but from the ability to communicate with spirits, divine the future, and channel supernatural power. The phrase the Wei Zhi uses, translated literally, is "served the way of the spirits and was able to bewitch the people." This is a description of charismatic religious authority. Himiko ruled because she had spiritual gifts that her contemporaries recognized as exceptional, and the political institutions of the Yamatai confederation were built around making those gifts effective.

This kind of ruler is well attested across the world. Northeast Asia, in particular, has a deep tradition of shamanic political authority — rulers who were also intermediaries with the spirit world, often female, often communicating through trance states or ecstatic rituals, often supported by extensive female religious establishments. Korean shamanic traditions of the same period (Ancient Korea Ch 2) show similar patterns: female religious specialists with significant social and political authority, organized around trance, divination, and intercession with ancestor spirits. Siberian shamanic traditions further north (which would influence the religious cultures of both Korea and Japan) had similar structures. Himiko fits this broader Northeast Asian pattern. She was not an isolated curiosity. She was the first surviving documented example in the Japanese archipelago of a religious-political role that was widespread in the surrounding region.

The political principle Himiko represents would define Japanese rulership for the next two thousand years in a particular way. Authority in early Japan derived not from military force alone but from spiritual legitimacy — the ability to mediate between the human world and the divine. The Yamato emperors who emerged in the next two centuries (Chapter 7) would claim descent from the sun goddess Amaterasu and would rule, in significant part, because of that divine ancestry rather than because of military conquest or administrative competence. The institutional combination — an emperor whose authority is sacralized by descent from the gods, supported by aristocratic clans whose own authority is sacralized by descent from lesser gods, all maintained by an elaborate apparatus of court ritual — runs in a continuous line from Himiko through every later iteration of the Japanese imperial system. The shaman queen of Yamatai is the first surviving glimpse of a political-religious pattern that would persist into the modern era.

---

A few specific details from the Wei Zhi are worth noting because they show the texture of late Yayoi Japanese society.

The Wei Zhi reports that Yamatai contained roughly 70,000 households (which would imply a total population of perhaps 200,000 to 350,000 if the household figure is reliable, though Chinese demographic estimates of foreign countries should always be treated with caution). It was the largest of about thirty named polities in Wa at the time, and it was acknowledged as the leading polity by most of the others — though three or four polities in the northern part of Wa were independently aligned with Wei and apparently outside Himiko's authority.

The Wei Zhi describes the Wa people as wearing tattoos extensively — facial tattoos, body tattoos, with patterns and placements that varied by social rank and region. Tattooing was reportedly used originally as a way to drive off marine predators when divers entered the water (an interesting functional explanation, whether or not it is historically accurate). The Wei Zhi also reports that Wa society had a clear social hierarchy with three or four distinct status classes — slaves at the bottom, commoners, lower nobility, and high nobility — visible in dress, housing, and behavior. Lower-status people had to step off the road and bow when meeting higher-status people. Marriage was monogamous for commoners and polygamous for the wealthy. Cremation was common; some areas practiced jar burial; tomb burial with mounds was beginning to appear in elite contexts.

The Wei Zhi also reports the diet (rice, fish, fowl, occasional meat — no large livestock except for chickens), the seafaring practices (canoes, fishing, trade across the Korea Strait), the climate (warm and wet, no snow in the regions the Wei Zhi describes — which is consistent with a Kyushu or western Honshu location, not a northern one), and the customs of marriage, divination, and burial. It is a remarkably detailed ethnographic account for a foreign country described by a culture that had never sent its own observers there.

---

Then there is what happened after Himiko died.

The Wei Zhi reports that her death was followed by a major political crisis. A male successor was selected — possibly her brother, possibly another male relative, the source is not specific — but the people of Yamatai would not accept him. Conflict broke out. The death toll, according to the Chinese account, was roughly a thousand people. The crisis was eventually resolved when Iyo (sometimes written Toyo in older sources), a thirteen-year-old female relative of Himiko's, was installed as the new queen, and order was restored. Iyo continued the diplomatic relationship with Wei, sending envoys to the Chinese court in 266 CE — the last formal Wa contact with China in the Wei Zhi record.

This succession crisis is informative. It tells us that Himiko's authority was not transferable to a male relative simply by virtue of family relationship. The political institution of Yamatai required a female religious figure at its head, and when that figure was lost, the institution failed until a new female religious figure could be installed. This is consistent with the broader pattern of Northeast Asian shamanic authority — the role was specifically female (or specifically required certain religious qualifications that were typically met by women), and it could not be filled by anyone outside that specific role.

The Yamato state that emerged in the following centuries (Chapter 7) would eventually evolve away from this pattern, settling on a male-dominated imperial succession with occasional female emperors as transitional or regency figures. But Himiko's example would not vanish entirely. Several of the most important early Japanese empresses — Suiko (reigned 593–628), Kogyoku (642–645 and again as Saimei 655–661), Jito (686–697), Genmei (707–715), Gensho (715–724), Koken/Shotoku (749–758 and again 764–770) — would rule as full reigning monarchs, not as regents or consorts. The female-ruler tradition that begins with Himiko persisted in Japanese political culture for at least seven centuries, in a way that has no real parallel in contemporary Chinese or Korean political tradition. China would have only one reigning empress (Wu Zetian, late seventh century) in its entire imperial history. Japan, in the same period, had nine.

---

A pause, before the chronology takes over: the Himiko story is the kind of historical episode that archaeology and textual sources illuminate in different and sometimes contradictory ways. The Wei Zhi gives us a detailed political and ritual account of a specific Japanese ruler in the third century CE. The archaeology gives us an explosion of large keyhole-shaped tombs in central Japan starting around 250 CE (Chapter 7), apparently representing the consolidation of larger political units in the same general period. Whether these are the same phenomenon — whether the Yamatai confederation under Himiko evolved into the Kofun-period Yamato state, with the imperial line running in unbroken continuity from Himiko to the present-day Japanese emperor — depends on questions that may never be fully resolvable. The Imperial Household Agency, for political reasons rooted in the modern Japanese constitution, has not permitted excavation of the kofun officially designated as imperial tombs. We may never know for certain whether Hashihaka is Himiko's grave. We may never know exactly how Yamatai turned into Yamato. What we do know is that by the early fourth century CE, the small chiefdoms of late Yayoi Japan had begun coalescing into larger units, and the resulting consolidation produced the political and monumental tradition that is the subject of the next chapter.

Cross-civ check: Himiko's reign and the Wei Zhi account (third century CE) overlap with the Three Kingdoms period in China (Six Dynasties Ch 1) — the Wei kingdom that hosted her envoys was one of the three contending Chinese successors to the Han, and would soon be replaced by the Western Jin Dynasty in 265 CE. The same period covers the early Three Kingdoms period in Korea (Ancient Korea Ch 4–5) when Goguryeo, Baekje, and Silla were consolidating into territorial states; the height of the Roman Empire's Crisis of the Third Century, with Rome going through over fifty emperors in fifty years (Ancient Rome Ch 11); the Kushan Empire in Central Asia and northern India; the late Parthian and early Sasanian periods in Iran (Persian Empire Ch 9–10); the Maya Classic period beginning in Mesoamerica (Maya Civilization Ch 3); and the Moche state forming in coastal Peru (Andean Kingdoms Ch 1). Himiko, sending envoys from her shaman court in Wa to the Wei court in Luoyang, was one of dozens of regional rulers across Eurasia and the Americas whose political authority was being shaped by the period's pattern of fragmenting empires and consolidating successor states. She is the first such ruler in the Japanese archipelago whose name we know. She is almost certainly not the first to have existed.

# Chapter 7 — The Kofun Mystery

Around 250 CE, enormous mounds began rising from the plains of central Japan.

They are called **kofun** (KOH-foon, meaning simply "old mounds"), and they give their name to the entire era from roughly 250 to 538 CE: the **Kofun period**. Some were round. Some were square. But the most distinctive and largest were shaped like keyholes — a circular mound joined to a trapezoidal platform, visible from above as the silhouette of an old-fashioned key. Seen from ground level, they look like overgrown hills, often forested with mature trees that have grown up over the centuries since the original construction. Seen from the air, the keyhole shape emerges with startling clarity. The keyhole form appears to be a uniquely Japanese invention. Nothing like it exists in Korea, China, or anywhere else in the prehistoric world. Somebody on the Japanese islands came up with the idea, and for the next two and a half centuries every powerful clan in central and western Japan adopted it as the standard form for elite burial.

This chapter is about those tombs — what they tell us, what we cannot read from them, and what they imply about the political consolidation of Japan in the centuries before writing arrived.

---

The kofun were big. Not metaphorically big. Big in a way that demands comparison with the great monuments of the ancient world.

The largest, the **Daisen Kofun** (DYE-sen KOH-foon) near modern Sakai City just south of Osaka, traditionally attributed to the semi-legendary Emperor **Nintoku** (NIN-toh-koo, late fourth or early fifth century CE), is roughly 1,600 feet (486 meters) long, covering about 80 acres (32 hectares) of ground area. By comparison, the base of the Great Pyramid at Giza covers about 13 acres. The Daisen Kofun is larger than the Great Pyramid by area — substantially larger — though much shorter in vertical height. It is surrounded by three concentric moats, with a fourth modern moat added later. Construction is estimated to have required around 2,000 laborers working for over fifteen years. The mound itself is built of layers of compacted earth, with stone facings and (originally) a covering of small stones that would have made it visible as a brilliant geometric shape from miles away before the trees grew over it. You cannot see its keyhole form from the ground. Only from the air, with twentieth-century aerial photography, did the full shape become visible. The Imperial Household Agency, which administers the Daisen Kofun as an imperial tomb, has not permitted full archaeological excavation, so we have never seen what is actually buried inside.

The Daisen Kofun is the largest tomb in the world by surface area. This is not hyperbole. It is a measured fact. And it was built by a polity that had no writing system, no coinage, no formal legal code, and no centralized bureaucracy in the Chinese sense. The kofun are monuments of pure political ambition. They proclaim the status of the buried family, anchor the family's lineage to a specific landscape, and announce that the buried ruler had access to enough labor to move enormous quantities of earth into a precise geometric shape. If you could mobilize 2,000 laborers for fifteen years, you were not a village chief. You were something more — something approaching a king.

The Daisen Kofun is the biggest single example, but it is not unique. Hundreds of large kofun were built across central and western Japan during the period 250–500 CE, with at least dozens of them in the multi-hundred-meter size range. The clustering is significant: the largest tombs are concentrated in the Kinai region around modern Nara and Osaka (the political heartland of the later Yamato state and of the modern Kansai region), with smaller but still substantial tombs scattered across northern Kyushu, the Inland Sea coast of western Honshu, and the eastern Kanto plain. The geographic pattern of kofun construction is essentially the political map of consolidating Yamato Japan: the largest tombs at the political center, smaller tombs at provincial centers, and increasingly small tombs at the periphery.

A useful cross-civ comparison: at roughly the same period that the Yamato chiefs were building the Daisen Kofun, the **Maya** in southern Mexico and Guatemala were building the great pyramid temples at Tikal, Calakmul, and other Classic Maya cities (Maya Civilization Ch 3–4). The Maya pyramids are taller and thinner than the kofun (the Tikal pyramids reach over 200 feet in height), but they served a similar political function: monumental architecture that demonstrated the power and divine status of the ruling family. The Maya had writing and recorded the names and dates of their kings on stelae at the base of their pyramids; the Yamato did not have writing and could not. We know the names of fifth-century Maya kings. We do not know the name of the Yamato chief actually buried in the Daisen Kofun, despite its later traditional attribution to Emperor Nintoku — and we may never know, because the tomb cannot be excavated. Two civilizations, separated by the Pacific Ocean and the entire Eurasian continent, built monumental burial structures in the same period for the same general reasons, with very different relationships to the written record.

---

The political entity that built the great kofun is called the **Yamato** (YAH-mah-toh) court, and it is the direct ancestor of the Japanese imperial institution that exists today.

The Yamato clan, based in the Kinai region around modern Nara and Osaka, gradually established political dominance over much of Honshu and northern Kyushu during the fourth and fifth centuries CE. Their authority was not the kind of authority you would associate with a Chinese-style centralized empire. They did not have provinces in the Roman or Han Chinese sense. They did not collect taxes through a state bureaucracy. They did not maintain a standing army. What they had was a network of subordinate clan leaders bound to the Yamato chief by marriage alliances, ritual exchange, occasional military expeditions, and a shared mythology of divine descent.

The Yamato clan claimed descent from the sun goddess **Amaterasu** (ah-mah-teh-RAH-soo), who would eventually be enshrined as the supreme deity of the Shinto pantheon. According to the mythology recorded centuries later in the **Kojiki** (KOH-jee-kee, "Record of Ancient Matters," compiled 712 CE) and the **Nihon Shoki** (nee-HON SHOH-kee, "Chronicles of Japan," compiled 720 CE), Amaterasu sent her grandson down from the heavens to rule the Japanese islands; his great-grandson **Jimmu** (JIM-moo) became the first emperor of Japan, founding the imperial line in 660 BCE.

The 660 BCE date is almost certainly fictitious. It appears to have been calculated by eighth-century Japanese court historians to align the founding of the Japanese imperial line with a particular cycle in Chinese cosmological calendrics — specifically, to make the Japanese imperial founding date 1,260 years (a complete cosmic cycle) before what the Japanese chroniclers calculated as the founding date of the great Chinese reform of 601 CE. The actual archaeological evidence is unambiguous: there were no chiefdoms of any significant scale in Japan in 660 BCE, certainly nothing that could be called an imperial polity, and the Yamato court emerged as a distinct political force only sometime in the third or fourth century CE. Emperor Jimmu, if he existed at all, was a mythological projection rather than a historical figure.

But the projection mattered. By tracing the imperial line back to 660 BCE, the eighth-century Japanese court was making a specific political claim: that the emperor's authority predated all foreign influence, that it came from the native gods of the Japanese islands rather than from Chinese-style imperial doctrine, and that it was older than anything China could claim about its own imperial origins. The reality was much more recent: the Yamato clan probably consolidated regional power in the third or fourth century CE, possibly in continuity with the Yamatai confederation under Himiko (Chapter 6). But by the time anyone in Japan could write, the official story had been firmly established — the emperor was the descendant of the sun goddess, the imperial line was unbroken since the age of the gods, and Japanese political authority was as old as the islands themselves. That story has shaped Japanese political identity ever since.

---

The kofun themselves tell us things that no text records, because we have hundreds of them and they were sealed in their original condition for over a millennium.

Around the perimeters of the great mounds, the Yamato and other powerful clans placed rows of **haniwa** (hah-NEE-wah, "circle of clay") — terracotta clay figures, typically one to three feet tall, depicting warriors, horses, houses, boats, dancers, musicians, shamans, and birds. Unlike Chinese tomb figurines (such as the famous Qin terracotta warriors buried with the First Emperor in 210 BCE — see Qin Dynasty Ch 5), the haniwa were not buried inside the burial chamber with the dead. They were placed outside, on the mound slopes and around the base, forming a kind of ceremonial boundary or guardian ring. Some kofun have hundreds of haniwa around their perimeters; the Daisen Kofun is estimated to have originally had as many as 30,000.

The haniwa are invaluable because they show us what Kofun-period people actually looked like — or at least how they wanted to be depicted. Warriors wear armor made of riveted iron plates, carry swords and bows, and wear helmets with visors and cheek guards. Horses are equipped with elaborate saddle gear, stirrups, bridles, and (sometimes) full body armor. Women wear hairstyles and jewelry that suggest court fashion: long unbound hair, beaded necklaces, decorative robes. The figures have simplified, almost cartoon-like faces — round eyes, straight noses, small slot mouths or no mouths at all — but their clothing and equipment are rendered in careful detail. From the haniwa, we know that Kofun-period warriors used mounted combat (a major shift from the Yayoi period, when fighting had been infantry-based), that horses were armored, that court dress included elaborate headgear, and that the military equipment of the period was heavily influenced by Korean models. Without the haniwa we would have almost no visual evidence at all for what Kofun-period Japanese society looked like, since Japanese painting and sculpture would not arrive until Buddhism did (Chapter 8).

---

That Korean influence runs deep through the entire Kofun period.

The Kofun era was a time of intense interaction between the Japanese archipelago and the Korean Peninsula. **Baekje** (BEHK-jeh, one of the Three Kingdoms of Korea, occupying the southwestern part of the peninsula) was Yamato Japan's closest ally and most important source of continental technology — see Ancient Korea Ch 5 for the Korean side of this relationship. Baekje princes were sometimes raised at the Yamato court as honored hostages-cum-students. Baekje envoys carried specialized craftsmen, scholars, and eventually religious teachers across the strait to Japan. Korean influence at the Yamato court was so pervasive that by the late Kofun period, perhaps a third of the aristocratic clans listed in later Japanese genealogies claimed Korean ancestry — either as recent immigrants who had risen to elite status, or as older lineages whose origins traced back to the early waves of toraijin (continental immigrants) discussed in Chapter 4.

The **Gaya** (GAH-yah) confederacy, a group of small polities on the southern tip of the Korean peninsula between Baekje and Silla, was another critical connection point. Gaya produced some of the finest iron in East Asia, mined and worked from rich ore deposits along the southern Korean coast, and Gaya iron was exported to Japan in large quantities through the Kofun period. Gaya served as a cultural and economic bridge between Yamato Japan and the broader Korean and Chinese world, with diplomatic ties to both Baekje and the Yamato court. Japanese involvement in Gaya politics during this period is documented but contested. The Nihon Shoki, compiled in 720 CE, claims that Yamato Japan established a colonial outpost in southern Korea called **Mimana** (mee-MAH-nah) in the late fourth century CE. Most modern scholars, both Japanese and Korean, consider this claim significantly exaggerated — probably a later projection of imperial Japanese pretensions onto the Kofun era, possibly with some kernel of truth in the form of Yamato diplomatic and trading outposts in Gaya territory rather than actual political control. The honest answer is that Japan and southern Korea were deeply interconnected through trade, migration, military alliance, and royal marriage during the Kofun period, in a relationship more complex than either later Japanese nationalist historiography (which exaggerated Japanese dominance) or later Korean nationalist historiography (which denied Japanese involvement) was willing to accept.

The horse-and-armor revolution of the fifth century is a particularly revealing case. Before about 400 CE, there is no archaeological evidence for horses or mounted combat in Japan. After about 400 CE, suddenly there are horses everywhere — horse bones in elite burials, horse haniwa around kofun mounds, horse trappings (saddle parts, bridle bits, stirrups) in grave goods, and the distinctive riveted iron-plate armor that goes with mounted combat. This shift is dramatic enough that one mid-twentieth-century Japanese archaeologist, **Egami Namio** (EH-gah-mee NAH-mee-oh), proposed that fifth-century Japan had been conquered by a wave of mounted Korean warriors who imposed themselves as the new ruling elite — the so-called "horse-rider theory" of Yamato origins. The horse-rider theory has fallen out of favor among modern scholars, who generally argue that the change reflects gradual cultural transfer and elite emulation rather than military conquest. But the underlying observation is sound: Korean military technology rapidly transformed the Yamato war machine in the fifth century CE, and the resulting cavalry-based military elite was visibly different from anything that had existed in Japan before.

---

The political organization of Yamato Japan was built around two interlocking systems: the **uji** (OO-jee) and the **be** (BEH).

The **uji** were extended kinship groups — clans claiming common descent from a divine ancestor (a kami of a specific place, function, or natural feature). Each major uji had a distinctive sphere of influence: the **Mononobe** (moh-NOH-noh-beh) clan controlled military and weapons production; the **Nakatomi** (nah-kah-TOH-mee) clan controlled court ritual and divination; the **Otomo** (oh-TOH-moh) clan controlled the imperial guards; the **Soga** (SOH-gah) clan controlled foreign trade and Korean immigrant labor; and the Yamato clan itself sat at the apex, claiming descent from the highest deity (Amaterasu) and exercising overlordship through marriage alliances and ritual centrality. The uji structure was simultaneously a religious system (each clan was identified with a particular kami), a political system (clan leaders held hereditary court positions), and an economic system (each clan controlled specific resources and specialized labor). The uji aristocracy was the actual government of late Kofun Japan, with the Yamato chief as first among many.

The **be** were occupational groups beneath the uji aristocracy — hereditary specialist communities of weavers, potters, scribes, smiths, salt-makers, fishermen, brewers, and dozens of other specialized crafts, attached to particular uji or directly to the royal court. The be system organized the working population of Yamato Japan into functional categories: the **kanuchi-be** (KAH-noo-chee BEH) made metalwork; the **tomo-be** were palace attendants; the **kinunui-be** (kee-noo-NOO-ee BEH) wove silk; the **ama-be** were fishermen; and so on. Many be were originally Korean immigrants whose specialized skills made them economically essential — a Baekje-trained smith brought to Japan to teach iron working would establish a smith-be, his descendants inheriting both the trade and the obligation to provide finished metalwork to their patron uji. The be system was not slavery (be members were free in personal status), but it was strongly hereditary, tied to specific occupations and specific uji patrons, and limited in social mobility. It was the early Japanese version of the kind of occupational caste organization that would emerge in various forms across pre-modern Eurasia.

Together, the uji-be system gave the Yamato court a workable structure for governing an island society without writing, without coinage, and without a centralized bureaucracy. Authority flowed down through clan leaders to occupational specialists. Goods and labor flowed up through the same channels. Rituals at the royal court bound the aristocratic clans together. It was a clan-federal system, not a centralized empire, and it would last in essentially this form until the centralizing reforms of the seventh century (Asuka-Nara Japan Ch 1) replaced it with a Chinese-style bureaucracy.

---

The Kingdom of Kush in Nubia was in its later Meroitic phase (Kingdom of Kush Ch 5–6) and would shortly fall to Aksum. The world that Yamato Japan was emerging into was a world of consolidating territorial states and post-imperial successor kingdoms, distributed across Eurasia and the Americas in roughly comparable patterns of political development. Japan in 500 CE was a mid-sized agrarian state organized around a clan-federal court, monumental burial, and intensive ties to neighbors across a narrow sea. The next two hundred years would transform it into something much more institutionally sophisticated. The trigger for that transformation arrived in 538.

# Chapter 8 — On the Threshold

In the 520s and 530s CE, Yamato Japan was on the edge of a transformation that nobody at the court could have fully anticipated.

This chapter is about that edge — the late Kofun period, the technological and political consolidation that was happening invisibly in the background, the decline of the great tomb-building tradition, the rise of the Soga clan to dominance at court, and the moment in 538 CE when a Korean envoy arrived at the Yamato court bearing a golden Buddha statue and a set of Buddhist sutras, ending Japanese prehistory in one diplomatic delivery.

Almost everything in the chapters that come after this one — the Asuka period, the Buddhist transformation, the Nara state, the Heian court, the samurai, the shogunate, the Meiji Restoration, modern Japan — runs forward from the events of this chapter. We are at the very edge of the prehistoric era, and the world the Yamato court is about to step into is recognizably the historical Japan that the rest of Japanese history will be about.

---

The first thing happening on the threshold is the slow decline of the great kofun tradition.

Through the late fifth and early sixth centuries, kofun construction in the Kinai heartland and the major regional centers became smaller, fewer, and less ambitious. The enormous keyhole tombs of the early to mid fifth century — the Daisen Kofun, the Ojin Kofun, and dozens of other massive monuments built for early Yamato chiefs — gave way to more modest mounds in the late fifth and sixth centuries. Provincial elite tombs continued to be built, sometimes in forms that imitated the Kinai keyhole shape and sometimes in distinctive local variations (square mounds, scalloped mounds, double-mounded forms). But the trend was unmistakable: tombs were getting smaller, the labor mobilized for them was decreasing, and clan-mound competition was giving way to something else.

The "something else" was political consolidation. As the Yamato court extended its authority more reliably over subordinate clans, the great families no longer needed to advertise their status through monumental tombs. They could rely on their position at court, their hereditary uji rank, their relationships with the imperial line, and their economic role through the be system (Chapter 7). The kofun had been built in the first place because the early Yamato confederation was an unstable competition among ambitious chiefs, each of whom needed visible monuments to assert his standing. By the early sixth century, the political system was institutionalized enough that visible monuments mattered less. The decline of the kofun is the archaeological signature of a maturing state.

Late kofun did continue, in reduced form, until the early seventh century CE — well past the chronological end of our narrative. But the great heroic age of monumental tomb-building was over by around 550. The next century would see a different kind of monumental architecture entirely: Buddhist temples.

---

The second thing happening on the threshold is the continued intensification of Korean technology transfer.

By 500 CE, **sueki** (SOO-eh-kee) stoneware had largely replaced the older softer earthenware Yayoi tradition for elite Japanese use. Sueki was a hard, gray, high-fired stoneware made on the potter's wheel in reduction-fired kilns — technology that required specialized Korean craftsmen and that produced vessels far stronger and more durable than anything previous Japanese pottery had achieved. The introduction of sueki around 450 CE, by Korean potters who established kiln complexes in central Japan, reshaped Japanese ceramics for the next several centuries. The local earthenware traditions did not disappear entirely (older-style Hajiki ware continued to be used for everyday cooking and storage), but for any serious or prestige use, sueki became the standard. The Japanese ceramic tradition would never again be without high-fired stoneware.

Iron production was the other major transfer. Through the fifth century, Yamato Japan had depended heavily on imported Korean iron (mostly from Gaya — see Chapter 7). By the late fifth and early sixth centuries, the Japanese were beginning to produce iron domestically using techniques learned from Korean smiths. **Bloomery furnaces** (a type of iron-smelting furnace that produces a spongy lump of iron — a "bloom" — that can then be worked and forged) appeared in the Chugoku region of western Honshu, using local iron sand (**satetsu**, sah-TEH-tsoo) collected from riverbeds and beaches. This was the beginning of the **tatara** (tah-TAH-rah) iron-smelting tradition that would persist as the technological foundation of Japanese metalwork through the medieval period and that would eventually produce the legendary swords of the samurai era. By 538 CE, Japan had iron production capability of its own, breaking the dependence on Korean imports and enabling the rapid expansion of armored cavalry forces and iron tools across the country.

Other technology arrived alongside the metal and the pottery: improved horse breeding (with the introduction of larger Korean horse stocks alongside the smaller native breeds), silk-weaving (which had been present at small scale earlier but was now scaling up under Korean instruction), specialized woodworking techniques for elite construction, and more sophisticated agricultural tools and irrigation methods. Korean immigrants — toraijin — continued to flow across the strait in significant numbers, settling especially in central Japan around the Yamato court, intermarrying with local elite families, and providing the specialized skilled labor that the increasingly complex Yamato state needed. By the early sixth century, perhaps a third of the named aristocratic clans at the Yamato court claimed Korean ancestry, either recent or several generations back. The Yamato court was simultaneously becoming more institutionally sophisticated and more demographically continental.

---

The third thing happening on the threshold is the emergence of historically secure imperial succession.

Through the third, fourth, and fifth centuries, the names of Japanese rulers come to us only through later mythological compilations (the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki, both compiled in the early eighth century — over two centuries after the events they describe), and the historicity of any given early emperor is uncertain. The traditional list of emperors begins with Jimmu in 660 BCE (Chapter 7) and continues through fourteen named emperors before reaching anyone whose existence is generally considered probable rather than legendary. **Emperor Sujin** (SOO-jin), traditionally the tenth emperor, is sometimes proposed as a possibly historical figure of the early to mid-fourth century CE. **Emperor Ojin** (OH-jin), traditionally the fifteenth emperor, is usually associated with the Ojin Kofun and dated to the late fourth or early fifth century. **Emperor Nintoku**, sixteenth in the traditional list, is associated with the Daisen Kofun. But all of these identifications are more tradition than evidence. We have no contemporary records of any of these rulers, and the chronology in the eighth-century chronicles is internally contradictory.

The first emperor whom most modern scholars consider historically secure — meaning his reign is reasonably well documented, his dates are reasonably reliable, and his existence is not in doubt — is **Emperor Keitai** (kay-TYE), who reigned from approximately 507 to 531 CE. Keitai is interesting because his accession appears to break the previous imperial line. According to the Nihon Shoki, the previous lineage had failed when Emperor Buretsu (BOO-reh-tsoo) died without an heir. Court factions, after considerable difficulty, eventually selected Keitai — described in the chronicle as a fifth-generation descendant of Emperor Ojin and as a regional aristocrat from Echizen province (in central Honshu, well outside the Kinai heartland). Modern scholars are skeptical of the genealogical claim that linked Keitai back to Ojin (it looks like a later interpolation to maintain the unbroken-line fiction); the more plausible reading is that Keitai was an outside warlord brought in by Yamato court factions to fill the throne after the previous lineage genuinely died out. Whether or not he was actually descended from Ojin, his accession marks the moment Japanese imperial history starts to become verifiable rather than mythical.

The years immediately after Keitai were turbulent. In 527 CE, a powerful chieftain named **Iwai** (EE-wai) in northern Kyushu rebelled against Yamato authority — possibly with backing from Silla, the Korean kingdom that was Baekje's rival and Yamato's enemy. The Iwai rebellion took two years of difficult campaigning to crush, and the episode revealed how thin the Yamato grip on Kyushu still was, and how deeply local clans were entangled in Korean politics. The Yamato court was a leading power on the Japanese archipelago, but it was not yet an unchallenged one.

---

The fourth thing happening on the threshold is the rise of the Soga clan.

The **Soga** (SOH-gah) clan, possibly of Korean immigrant descent (the question is debated — the Soga claimed descent from a legendary native ancestor, but several modern scholars argue for a Korean origin a few generations back), began their rise to power at the Yamato court in the early sixth century. Their strategy was novel for Yamato Japan. Rather than competing for traditional uji privileges (military command, ritual priesthood, palace guard duties), the Soga focused on controlling foreign trade and Korean immigrant labor. They established themselves as the patrons of toraijin communities in the Kinai region, channeling continental skilled workers into Soga-managed estates and Soga-allied workshops. They became the conduit through which continental wealth (silk, sueki ware, iron, gold, exotic luxury goods) flowed into the Yamato court economy.

This was a politically smart strategy. The other major clans — the Mononobe, the Nakatomi, the Otomo — held the traditional power positions that had defined the early Yamato system. The Soga held something newer and arguably more important: the relationship with the continental world that was the source of all the technology and prestige goods that the Yamato elite increasingly depended on. As Korean immigration intensified through the early sixth century, Soga influence grew. The Soga also used the standard aristocratic tactic of marrying their daughters to imperial princes, positioning themselves to produce future emperors with Soga grandfathers. By the 530s, the Soga were one of the most powerful clans at the Yamato court, and they were positioning themselves to champion a new technology that was about to arrive from Baekje — a technology that would justify their ascendancy and break the older clans' grip on traditional ritual authority.

That technology was a religion.

---

In **538 CE** — some sources say 552, with the discrepancy itself an artifact of the messy early Japanese historical record (the Nihon Shoki gives 552; the older Gangoji-engi monastery chronicle gives 538; modern scholars generally accept 538 as more reliable) — King **Seong** (SUNG) of Baekje sent a diplomatic gift to the Yamato court that would transform Japan more thoroughly than rice, bronze, iron, sueki ware, horses, or any of the other Korean technological imports that had reshaped the islands over the previous millennium.

He sent a golden Buddha statue and a set of Buddhist sutras.

This is the moment that ends Japanese prehistory. The reasoning is simple: from 538 CE onward, Japan begins to participate in a literate religious-political world (the Buddhist East Asian sphere) that produces continuous written records on Japanese soil — temple chronicles, sutra copies, dedicatory inscriptions, eventually the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki and the early Buddhist commentaries written by Japanese monks. The era of Chinese chronicles describing a foreign barbarian land called Wa is over. The era of self-documented Japanese history begins. Everything before 538 belongs to prehistory, in the strict sense that the Japanese themselves were not yet writing about themselves. Everything after 538 belongs to history.

The gift itself was modest. A small Buddha statue, made of gilt bronze (probably no more than a few feet tall, though the exact size is not recorded), and a set of Buddhist scriptures (sutras) — Mahayana texts in Chinese translation, the standard textual basis of East Asian Buddhism at the time. The statue and the sutras were accompanied by a letter from King Seong recommending that the Yamato court adopt Buddhism. The recommendation was not casual. Baekje had its own reasons for promoting Buddhism in Japan: a Buddhist Yamato would be a stronger diplomatic and ideological ally for Buddhist Baekje against Buddhist Silla and Buddhist Goguryeo (the two other Korean kingdoms — see Ancient Korea Ch 6). King Seong was making a religious-political offering at a moment when Baekje needed Yamato support against rival Korean powers, and the Buddha statue was the cover note on a strategic alliance proposal.

---

The arrival of Buddhism split the Yamato court into two warring factions. Both factions were already there, and the Buddha statue gave them something new and important to fight about.

The Soga clan championed the new religion. They saw Buddhism as a source of continental sophistication, magical power, and diplomatic advantage. Buddhist temples were impressive. Buddhist monks were literate and educated. Adopting Buddhism would signal to China and Korea that Japan was a civilized state worthy of respect rather than a barbarian periphery. And — more pragmatically — Buddhism was the religion of the Korean immigrant communities the Soga patronized, so Soga sponsorship of the Buddha statue would consolidate Soga authority over those communities and over the new ideological resources Buddhism brought with it.

The Mononobe and Nakatomi clans opposed it. They were the custodians of Japan's indigenous spiritual tradition (what would later be called **Shinto**, SHIN-toh, "the way of the gods" — the venerable but at that point still largely unsystematized native cult of the kami, the spirits associated with natural features, ancestors, and clan deities). The Mononobe and Nakatomi argued that adopting a foreign religion would anger the native gods and bring disaster on the Japanese islands. This was not just a theological position. It was also a self-interested one: the Mononobe and Nakatomi clans derived their hereditary court influence from their roles as guardians of the indigenous ritual system, and any major shift to a foreign religion threatened to undermine their institutional power base.

The dispute was settled not by theology but by politics and violence. Initial responses to the Buddha statue were mixed: when a plague broke out shortly after the statue arrived, the Mononobe blamed the foreign religion and threw the statue into a canal. When the plague persisted, the Soga retrieved it. The conflict simmered for nearly fifty years, escalating through successive generations of court intrigue, until the Soga clan, backed by military force and by the increasingly Buddhist sympathies of the Yamato royal family, destroyed the Mononobe in battle in 587 CE and established themselves as the dominant power at court. Buddhism was in. The native Shinto tradition would not be displaced (it would coexist and eventually fuse with Buddhism in characteristically Japanese syncretic patterns), but the question of whether to adopt Buddhism at all had been settled definitively in the Soga's favor.

---

That long settling process — the Soga consolidation, the Buddhist establishment, the centralizing reforms that built a Chinese-style state on top of the older Yamato clan-federal foundation — is the subject of the next narrative in this series, **Asuka-Nara Japan**. From 538 forward, the story has writers. From 538 forward, the dates are reliable. From 538 forward, the rulers have biographies and the institutions have charters and the buildings have dedicatory inscriptions. The Yamato court that received the Buddha statue from King Seong was still essentially a prehistoric polity, organized around clan loyalty and divine ancestry. The court that emerged from the next two centuries of Buddhist-influenced reform was something genuinely different: a Chinese-style imperial state, with a permanent capital, a written legal code, a state-sponsored Buddhist clergy, and a court culture that produced some of the most beautiful surviving art and literature of the early medieval world.

The boundary between prehistory and history is artificial. The people of 537 CE did not know they were living in prehistory. The people of 539 CE did not know they were living in history. But for the purposes of telling the story, we have to put the line somewhere, and the arrival of the Buddha statue from Baekje is the cleanest single event that marks the transition. Before it, Japanese sources do not exist. After it, they accumulate. Before it, we have to read about Japan through Chinese eyes. After it, the Japanese begin to write about themselves.

---

A pause, before we close the book: the fourteen and a half millennia covered in this narrative are the deep substrate of everything Japanese. The Jomon pottery tradition is still embedded in the Japanese aesthetic relationship to clay, to natural materials, to the marks of process visible on a finished object. The Jomon-derived spiritual sense of the sacredness of natural features — particular trees, rocks, waterfalls, mountains — survives in Shinto and in the broader Japanese cultural attitude toward landscape. The Yayoi rice-paddy economy is still the basis of Japanese rural life and of the cultural significance of rice as a sacred and central food. The Yamato imperial line traces back, in its own self-understanding, to the Kofun-period Yamato chiefs who built the great keyhole tombs, and the modern Japanese emperor (the longest continuously reigning monarchical line in the world by a large margin) carries that lineage forward as a living institution. The Korean immigration of the Yayoi and Kofun periods established a continental component in the Japanese gene pool and the Japanese aristocratic genealogy that has always been there since.

None of this means modern Japan is the Jomon, or the Yayoi, or the Kofun, in any direct way. Modern Japan is also the product of two thousand years of further transformation: Buddhism, Chinese-style centralization, the medieval feudal system, the Tokugawa peace, the Meiji modernization, and the postwar rebuild. But the substrate that all of those transformations were built on top of is the substrate this book has been about. Pots, rice, and tombs. The first pots fired by hunter-gatherers as the Ice Age ended; the first rice paddies dug by Korean immigrants on a Kyushu beach; the first keyhole tombs raised by ambitious Yamato chiefs on the plains of Kinai; and the gold-bronze Buddha statue that arrived in 538 to end one chapter of Japanese history and begin the next.

# Reconstruction — America's First Try at Multiracial Democracy, and Who Killed It

**Goal:** Narrative for the United States from the end of the Civil War in 1865 to the Compromise of 1877: twelve years in which the country tried to answer the question the war had left open. The war (Antebellum America Ch 18) had settled, in three-quarters of a million graves, that the United States would be free and not slave. It had not settled what freedom would *mean* for the four million people it freed. Reconstruction is the fight over that question, and it is three stories braided together that you have to hold at once. It is a story of constitutional revolution, three amendments in five years that rewrote what citizenship is and who the federal government answers to, the largest change to the founding document since the Bill of Rights. It is a story of grassroots Black freedom-building, formerly enslaved people who walked off plantations to find sold children, founded churches and schools and colleges, registered to vote, and within five years were sitting in the United States Senate. And it is a story of organized terrorism and a deliberate counter-revolution, a sustained insurgency of massacre and assassination that the federal government first fought and then chose to stop fighting, after which the people who had lost the war won the peace and then, for fifty years, won the history books too. The thesis of this book is that Reconstruction did not "fail" in the way a plant fails to grow. It was defeated, by violence, by a Supreme Court that disarmed the laws meant to stop the violence, and by a Northern public that got tired. Everything that follows is the case for that sentence.

A note on dates and measurements. This is a U.S.-focused narrative for a U.S.-based reader, so distances are given in miles first and kilometers in parentheses, and large numbers are anchored against something you can feel. Many of this book's most important numbers, especially how many people were murdered in the political terror, are genuinely uncertain, because the killers kept no records and the victims were people the law did not count. Where a figure is a real range, the text gives the range and says why it is wide.

A note on the politics and the sources, and read this one carefully, because Reconstruction is the rare history where the *historiography*, the story of how the story has been told, is itself one of the most important things in the chapter. For roughly fifty years, from about 1900 to the 1960s, American schoolchildren and college students were taught a specific account of Reconstruction: that it was a tragic era of "Negro misrule," in which ignorant former slaves, greedy Northern "carpetbaggers," and treacherous Southern "scalawags" looted and misgoverned a prostrate South until honorable white "Redeemers" rescued it. That account has a name, the **Dunning School**, after the Columbia historian William Dunning whose students wrote the state-by-state studies that built it, and it was not a neutral mistake. It was, in effect, the intellectual victory of the people who had overthrown Reconstruction by murder, written into the textbooks of the country they overthrew it in. The Black scholar **W. E. B. Du Bois** (pronounced "doo-BOYZ") demolished it factually in his 1935 book *Black Reconstruction in America*, and the historian Eric Foner and others built the modern scholarly consensus on the rubble. That consensus, sometimes called the **"Second Founding"** view, is close to the reverse of the Dunning School: Reconstruction was a serious, partly successful, and deliberately destroyed attempt to build a multiracial democracy, and the formerly enslaved were its protagonists, not its problem. This book follows the modern consensus, and it will be explicit, repeatedly, about where the old account came from and why it held on so long. Saying that Reconstruction was overthrown by organized white terrorism is not "taking a side" in a live academic debate. It is reporting the conclusion the evidence forces, and identifying a long-dominant counter-story as the propaganda it was.

A note on what survives. The documentary record of Reconstruction is, unusually for an era this old, rich on *both* sides of the color line, and that is itself a result of Reconstruction. Formerly enslaved people testified before Congress in volume, wrote to and through the Freedmen's Bureau, founded their own newspapers, and left pension and Bureau files by the hundreds of thousands. The thirteen-volume congressional report on the Klan is one of the fullest first-person records of organized terror anywhere in history. We are not, here, mostly reading the powerful describing the powerless. For the first time in this chain of American narratives, the people the history is about left a large record in their own voices, and this book leans on it.

# Chapter 1 — What the War Left Open

Begin at the end of something else, because Reconstruction does not have a clean starting line. It begins in the wreckage of a war whose ending this chain has already told (Antebellum America Ch 18), and the single most useful thing to do first is to be precise about what that war did and did not decide, because the entire twelve years that follow are an argument about exactly that.

Here is the war in one paragraph, and no more, because the previous narrative spent eighteen chapters on it. The United States created by the Revolution had been built around a contradiction its founders deliberately refused to resolve: a republic that declared all men created equal and wrote the protection of human slavery into its Constitution without using the word (Early American Republic Ch 5). For most of a century, every attempt to keep the question quiet, the Missouri Compromise, the Compromise of 1850, popular sovereignty, the Supreme Court's *Dred Scott* ruling, bought less time than the one before it, until in 1860–61 eleven Southern states seceded rather than accept the election of a president pledged only to stop slavery's *expansion*, and the result was the **Civil War** of 1861–1865, the deadliest war in American history. It killed somewhere around **750,000** people, the most careful modern estimate, with a range of roughly 650,000 to 850,000 because the Confederate records were destroyed and the figure is reconstructed from census data rather than counted. Anchor that: it is more Americans than died in the Revolution, the War of 1812, the Mexican War, both World Wars, Korea, and Vietnam combined. The Confederacy lost, totally. And in the losing, two things happened that set up everything in this book. Slavery was destroyed, first by the armies, then sealed permanently shut by a constitutional amendment. And the people who had been enslaved, roughly four million of them, became, all at once, free, in a country that had spent two centuries building its law, economy, and self-image around their bondage and had given almost no thought to what would come next.

So the war answered one question with total finality, and that question is worth stating exactly because the rest of the book turns on the difference between it and the next one. The question the war answered was: *shall the United States be a slave country or a free one?* The answer, written in three-quarters of a million graves and then in the **Thirteenth Amendment** (an *amendment* is a formal change to the Constitution, the country's supreme rulebook; the Thirteenth, ratified in December 1865, abolished slavery everywhere in the United States, permanently, beyond the reach of any president, court, or state), was: free. That was settled. It is not in dispute in this book or in serious scholarship. It is the floor.

But notice the question the war did *not* answer, because it is the question this entire narrative is about. Four million people were now free. *What does that mean?* Does "free" mean only "not owned," a person who can be worked, cheated, beaten, and killed but not literally bought and sold? Or does it mean a citizen, with the vote, equal standing in court, the protection of the law, and a fair shot at land and a living? Those are radically different countries, and in April 1865 the Constitution did not say which one the United States was going to be. It said slavery was over. It said nothing yet about citizenship, voting, equal protection, or land. That silence is the open wound this book is about, and every chapter is somebody trying to fill it, or to keep it empty.

Now stand in the physical place where the question had to be answered, because the abstraction "the postwar South" hides a humanitarian catastrophe. The Confederacy had been beaten not just defeated but *wrecked*, deliberately, as a matter of Union strategy in the war's final phase (Antebellum America Ch 18). Roughly two-thirds of Southern wealth had evaporated, and a large part of that "wealth" was, in the brutal accounting of the time, the cash value of the human beings who had just been freed: emancipation alone wiped out the single largest category of property in the region. Cities had been burned. Railroads were torn up, their rails heated and bent around trees so they could not be relaid, a Union tactic literally nicknamed "Sherman's neckties." Levees that held back the Mississippi had failed. Roughly a quarter of the South's military-age white men were dead. Confederate paper money was worthless paper. And into this devastated landscape, four million people walked out of slavery with, in the overwhelming majority of cases, no land, no money, no legal standing, often no surname recorded anywhere, and frequently no idea where their sold family members had been taken. The first acts of freedom for enormous numbers of freedpeople were heartbreakingly concrete: walking, sometimes hundreds of miles, to look for a wife or a child sold away years before, and lining up to legally marry a spouse the law had never let them legally marry. Tens of thousands placed newspaper advertisements for lost family for decades afterward. Hold onto that image, because it is the human center of this whole book: freedom, for the people who had it imposed on them, started as a search for the people slavery had taken.

A word now about who these four million people were, in a register this chain's writing rules require, because a newcomer to all of history needs it and because the propaganda this book has to fight depended on getting it wrong. They were not, as the Dunning School would later teach, a childlike mass incapable of citizenship. They were a population that had built and run the agricultural economy that made the antebellum South the richest agricultural region on earth; that had, despite laws against it in most slave states, preserved an enormous oral culture and, in a substantial minority of cases, taught themselves or each other to read in defiance of the law; that had its own churches, family networks, skilled trades, and a developed and tested political intelligence about exactly how American power worked, because their lives had depended on reading it correctly. When the chance came, within two years they were registering voters, running conventions, and arguing constitutional law on the floor of state legislatures. This is documented, in their own words, in the congressional record and the Bureau files. It is not a modern projection or a flattering reconstruction. It is what the sources show, and one reason this narrative is confident the Dunning version is propaganda is that the people who lived it left enough testimony to convict it.

Which is the place to do the thing this chain does in every Chapter 1: name the contested-history problem before the contested history starts, so you can read the rest with the right calibration. Most "first" and "biggest" claims in ancient history are really "first surviving evidence of," because archaeology is patchy and the deep past is mostly lost. Reconstruction has the opposite problem. It is recent, densely documented, and *over*-narrated, buried for two generations under a confident, detailed, and deliberately false account. So the caveat this book front-loads is not "the evidence is thin." It is the reverse: when you encounter a claim here that contradicts a version of Reconstruction you may have absorbed from old textbooks, popular movies, monuments, or family memory, the contradiction is usually not this book being revisionist. It is this book reporting the modern scholarly consensus, built on the primary record, against a story that was itself constructed, after the fact, by the side that overthrew Reconstruction, and then taught as fact for half a century. That story has a name and a chapter of its own (Chapter 16). The reason it has to be flagged in Chapter 1 is that you have probably already met it, and you need to know it is there.

One genuine "first" belongs here as the book's keynote, stated carefully the way the rules require. Reconstruction was, by any reasonable definition, the first serious attempt at a **multiracial democracy** in a major nation, meaning a government in which formerly enslaved people of a different race from the dominant group voted, held office, and helped write the laws on something approaching equal terms. The qualifiers matter, because there had been earlier emancipations and partial experiments. Revolutionary Haiti had abolished slavery and become an independent Black state in 1804; Britain freed the enslaved across its empire in 1833 (paying the *owners*, not the freed). The closest structural rehearsal, though, was right next door, in the British Caribbean: in Jamaica and Barbados the formerly enslaved were held in a compulsory "apprenticeship" to their old masters for years after 1834, then given a limited franchise, then watched that franchise narrowed back down by the white planter class through the 1860s, the same arc, immediate emancipation to partial inclusion to deliberate rollback, that the United States was about to run at vastly larger scale. What Reconstruction added to that pattern, no compensation to owners, full citizenship, the ballot, and the formerly enslaved actually sitting in legislatures and Congress, all at the scale of a continental nation, had no clear precedent. To feel the size of it: Black men got the constitutional vote in the United States in 1870, while in Britain universal manhood suffrage did not arrive until 1918 and South Africa would not hold a multiracial election until 1994. The tragedy this book documents is not that the experiment was never tried. It is that it was tried, and worked, briefly, and was then destroyed on purpose, which is a different and harder thing to look at, though the Jamaican rehearsal is a warning that the *kind* of failure was not unique to America, only its scale.

End the scene-setting with the cast of the conflict, defined now and developed later, so the name-drops in coming chapters are not cold. The fight over the open question had roughly four sides. There was the **president**, after April 1865 a Tennessee Democrat named Andrew Johnson, who wanted Reconstruction fast, lenient, and run by Southern whites, and whom you meet properly in Chapter 2. There were the **Radical Republicans**, a faction in Congress led by men like Thaddeus Stevens in the House and Charles Sumner in the Senate, who wanted a thorough remaking of the South into a place of genuine Black equality (Chapter 5); a "Radical" here meant someone who wanted to go to the *root* of the problem, not a bomb-thrower. There were the **freedpeople** themselves, who were not a passive object of the others' policy but an organized political force with their own demands, land, the vote, schools, safety, pressed through conventions, churches, and the ballot the moment they got it (Chapter 7). And there were the **ex-Confederates and their allies**, the planter class and the white South generally, who had lost the war, accepted on paper that slavery was over, and intended to recover by every other means, law, contract, fraud, and, when those failed, organized murder, everything that mattered about the old order (Chapters 10, 13, 14). Those four forces, in shifting alliances, are the whole drama. The next fifteen chapters are them fighting over the sentence the war refused to finish: *the formerly enslaved are free, and that means ______.*

# Chapter 2 — The Accidental President

The man who would shape the first two years of Reconstruction was not supposed to be president, and the manner in which he became one tells you most of what you need to know about why those two years went so badly.

On the night of April 14, 1865, five days after the Confederate general Robert E. Lee surrendered at **Appomattox** (a Virginia village; the surrender effectively ended the Civil War, Antebellum America Ch 18), President Abraham Lincoln was shot in the back of the head at Ford's Theatre in Washington by **John Wilkes Booth**, a famous actor and fervent Confederate sympathizer, and died the next morning. It was the first assassination of an American president. Booth was part of a conspiracy that also tried, the same night, to murder the secretary of state and the vice president; only Lincoln's killing succeeded. Eight conspirators were tried by a **military tribunal**, a panel of army officers acting as judge and jury under military law rather than a civilian court with a jury of citizens, and four were hanged that summer, including **Mary Surratt**, the first woman executed by the federal government. The mechanism matters as much as the grimness: trying civilians before officers instead of a jury became one of the defining constitutional fights of Reconstruction, the precise question of when the army may judge civilians, and here it is in the first paragraph of the era.

Pause on what was lost, specifically, not vaguely, because "if Lincoln had lived" is one of the great counterfactuals of American history and the honest version is more modest than the legend. Three days before he was shot, in his last public speech, Lincoln had endorsed voting rights for *some* Black men, especially soldiers and the literate, the first time any American president had publicly backed Black suffrage at all. Booth was in the audience, and one of his companions later recalled him snarling that it would be the last speech Lincoln ever made, a much-repeated line that rests on a single second-hand account and is best treated as probably true rather than certain. So the floor of Lincoln's developing position was already ahead of where most of the country was, and the ceiling is unknowable. What is *not* speculative is the part that mattered most: whatever Lincoln's exact Reconstruction policy would have been, he possessed two things his successor entirely lacked, immense political stature with the Northern public and the war's victors, and a demonstrated, almost uncanny skill at moving a divided country incrementally in a direction it did not initially want to go. The tragedy is not mainly that we lost a known good plan. It is that we lost the one politician with the standing and skill to *negotiate* whatever plan emerged, and got, instead, this man.

So: **Andrew Johnson**. Spend real time on him, because Presidential Reconstruction is largely his personality operating with almost no check for eight months, and you cannot understand it without understanding him. He was a genuine American self-made man of a hard kind, born poor in North Carolina in 1808, never a day in school, apprenticed to a tailor as a boy, taught to read partly by his wife, who built a political career in Tennessee out of pure resentment of the slaveholding planter aristocracy that looked down on men like him. That resentment is the key to half of what he did and is also why people kept misreading him. Johnson hated the planter class, the great slaveholders, with the fury of a man they had snubbed his whole life, and during the war he was the only U.S. senator from any of the seceding states who refused to walk out with his state and instead stayed in Washington, loyal to the Union, while the rest of the Southern senators resigned to join the Confederacy. That made him a Northern hero, and in 1864 Lincoln's Republicans put him on a "National Union" ticket: rather than run two Republicans, they paired Lincoln with a loyal Southern Democrat to signal that the war was a national cause and not a one-party crusade. A "unity ticket" of opposing-party men was unusual even then, and it is the accident that put a Tennessee Democrat one heartbeat from the presidency. Radicals initially liked him, because he talked about punishing "traitors." They had catastrophically misunderstood him. Johnson's hatred of the planter aristocracy was not a hatred of slavery or a sympathy for Black people. It was the opposite. He was a committed, explicit white supremacist who had owned enslaved people himself and who believed, sincerely and to the end, that the United States was a white man's country and that Black citizenship was an abomination. His quarrel with the planters was that they were aristocrats lording it over poor whites like him. He had no quarrel at all with the racial order they sat on top of; he simply wanted poor white men, not rich ones, to run it. Read that twice, because every disastrous decision in this chapter follows from it.

Now the structural accident that let one man's worldview run the country unchecked. Lincoln died in April 1865. Congress, by the calendar of the era, was out of session and not scheduled to reconvene until December, eight months later, and Johnson chose not to call it back early. For eight months, therefore, "Reconstruction" simply *was* whatever Andrew Johnson decided it was, by executive proclamation, with no legislature in the room to argue. Historians call this phase **Presidential Reconstruction**, and its content was the working-out of the worldview just described.

It had two main moves. The first was **amnesty and pardon**. In May 1865 Johnson issued a proclamation offering a blanket pardon, restoration of all property except enslaved people, to most ex-Confederates who swore a loyalty oath. He carved out exceptions, mainly the wealthiest planters (those with property over $20,000) and high Confederate officials, who had to apply to *him* personally for a pardon. On its face this looked like Johnson's class resentment in action: make the rich planters come grovel. In practice it became the opposite. The planters came, and Johnson, flattered by the spectacle of the aristocracy begging him for mercy, pardoned them by the thousands, often the same day, restoring their land and their political rights. Within months the exact class of men who had led secession were back in control of their plantations and running for office. The "punishment" of the planter class evaporated into a daily ritual of Johnson enjoying their submission and then handing them back their power.

The second move was the **restoration of state governments**, and this is where the open question of Chapter 1 got its first answer, an answer almost everyone in the North found intolerable. Johnson appointed provisional governors and set terms for the Southern states to rejoin the Union that were stunningly lenient: essentially, abolish slavery on paper, repudiate secession, void the Confederate war debt, and you are back, with almost no conditions about the four million freedpeople and, crucially, *no* requirement that Black men be allowed to vote or that ex-Confederates be kept out of power. The Southern states took the deal and immediately demonstrated what they meant to do with it. They held elections, under all-white electorates, and sent to Washington a Congress full of ex-Confederates, including the former vice president of the Confederacy, **Alexander Stephens**, the man who in a famous public address in Savannah in March 1861, since known as the **"Cornerstone Speech,"** had declared that the new Confederacy's "cornerstone" rested on the "great truth" that Black people were not equal to white, now elected to the United States Senate, fresh from a federal prison, by the state he had helped lead out of the Union. And, as the next chapter details, the restored legislatures' very first order of business was to pass laws designed to push the freedpeople as close back to slavery as the Thirteenth Amendment would allow.

Step back and see what Presidential Reconstruction had produced by the autumn of 1865, because the reaction to it drives the rest of the book. A Northern public that had just buried 360,000 of its own soldiers to win the war looked South and saw: the men who started the war pardoned and back in office; the freedpeople given freedom on paper and nothing else; Confederate generals and the Confederate vice president headed for Congress; and new state laws openly rebuilding bondage under another name. It looked, to an enormous number of Northerners who were not radicals at all, like the South had lost the war and was winning the peace, in a matter of months, with the active help of the president. That perception, more than any abstract commitment to racial equality, is what radicalized Northern politics and set up the collision in Chapter 4. Johnson had a theory of the Constitution and a worldview about race, and he was about to discover that the eight months of unchecked power were over, because in December 1865 Congress came back, and it was not in a forgiving mood.

# Chapter 3 — Slavery by Another Name

Before the political collision, look hard at what the restored Southern governments actually *did* with their freedom from oversight in 1865, because it is the concrete thing the politics was reacting to, and because it is the clearest possible answer to the question of whether the white South had, in any meaningful sense, accepted the result of the war. It had not. It had accepted the word "slavery" being illegal and set out, immediately and openly, to rebuild the thing the word had described.

The instrument was a set of laws historians call the **Black Codes**. Starting with Mississippi and South Carolina in late 1865 and spreading across the South, the restored white legislatures passed statutes that applied only to Black people and were engineered to recreate the labor system of slavery without the ownership. The mechanism is worth following in detail, because the cleverness of it is the point. The codes typically did several things at once. They required Black people to sign annual labor contracts, usually by January, and made it a *crime* to break one or to be found without one; a worker who left an abusive employer could be arrested. They passed **vagrancy** laws under which any Black person who could not, on demand, prove employment could be arrested, fined, and, since they had no money, hired out to a white employer to "work off" the fine, which is to say, compelled to labor for a private person under threat of jail. They passed **apprenticeship** laws letting courts bind Black children, often over their parents' objection, to white "guardians," frequently the family's former enslaver, until adulthood. They barred Black people from owning or renting farmland in some states, from many trades, from owning firearms, from testifying against whites, from assembling. Read the mechanism as a system and what it produces is this: a Black person in Mississippi in 1866 had to have a white employer or be a criminal; could be arrested for leaving that employer; could be jailed for being unemployed and then leased to an employer to pay the fine; and could not, in most disputes, testify against the white people doing all this. The word "slave" appears nowhere. The structure is recognizably slavery with the title deleted, and the Southern legislatures wrote it in the open, within months of Appomattox, as their first act of restored self-government. The design was not even original: requiring a Black worker to carry proof of a white employer or face arrest is, almost exactly, the South African "pass law" that would later be built into apartheid, and the forced annual labor contract closely echoed the four-year compulsory "apprenticeship" Britain imposed on the freed across its Caribbean colonies after 1834. The white South was reaching, independently, for the standard post-emancipation toolkit for keeping freed people working the same land for the same people. They were not being secretive about their answer to Chapter 1's question. The answer was: *free means not-owned, and nothing else, and we will spend our first legislative session making sure of it.*

This is the place to introduce the one federal institution that was actually on the ground trying to do the opposite, because it is central to everything and because its starvation and destruction is one of the book's recurring grim themes. In March 1865, before Lincoln died, Congress had created the **Bureau of Refugees, Freedmen, and Abandoned Lands**, universally called the **Freedmen's Bureau**, an agency placed inside the War Department and put under a Union general named **Oliver Otis Howard** (the "Christian general"; the founding of Howard University in Washington would be named for him). The Bureau's job, on paper, was staggering: feed, clothe, and provide medical care to four million freedpeople and destitute white refugees across a wrecked region the size of Western Europe (the eleven ex-Confederate states cover roughly 750,000 square miles, comparable to France, Germany, Italy, and Britain put together); supervise labor contracts so the Black Codes could not simply enslave people by paperwork; run courts where Black testimony actually counted; build schools; and, briefly, settle freedpeople on confiscated land. Now the catch, which tells you how serious the federal commitment really was in material terms: Congress gave the Bureau a mandate of *one year*, almost no dedicated budget, and a few hundred agents to cover the entire South. It was handed one of the largest social tasks in the country's history and the resources of a rounding error. It did remarkable things anyway, though the scale tells you how outmatched it was: roughly forty hospitals to serve four million people across that France-sized region is one hospital per hundred thousand freedpeople, an achievement and a rounding error at the same time. It ran those hospitals through deadly postwar smallpox and cholera epidemics, adjudicated tens of thousands of disputes in courts where, for the first time in Southern history, a Black person's word counted against a white person's, and, above all, midwifed an explosion of Black schooling this book returns to in Chapter 7. But the gap between its mandate and its means is one of the book's keynotes: again and again, the federal government willed an end to the South's racial order and refused to pay for it.

Now the single most consequential failure of the entire era, the one Black leaders at the time identified instantly and correctly as the hinge on which everything else turned: **land**. Run the logic the freedpeople themselves ran, because it was not sentimental, it was strategic and exactly right. A person who is legally free but owns nothing, has no money, and lives in a region where the only employers are the people who used to own them is not, in any economically meaningful sense, free. They will have to work for their former enslaver on their former enslaver's terms, because the alternative is starvation, and the vote, when it comes, can be coerced by the man who controls your livelihood and your housing. Freedpeople said this, plainly, in convention after convention: without land, freedom is a fiction. There had been a real, concrete chance to act on it. In January 1865, General **William Tecumseh Sherman**, after meeting Black ministers in Savannah who told him exactly the logic above, had issued **Special Field Order No. 15**, setting aside a strip of confiscated coastal land from Charleston down to Florida for settlement by freed families in 40-acre plots; the army later lent mules. This is the literal origin of the phrase **"forty acres and a mule."** By mid-1865 some 40,000 freedpeople were farming roughly 400,000 acres of it. And then Andrew Johnson, exercising the unchecked power of Chapter 2, simply gave it back. His pardons restored the land to its former Confederate owners, and Bureau commissioner Howard, by his own later account, was sent in person to the Sea Islands to tell freed families to their faces that the land they were farming and had been promised was being returned to the men who had enslaved them, an October 1865 meeting on Edisto Island that freed delegates answered with a written protest historians still quote. Stevens in the House proposed seizing the great plantations outright and giving freedpeople 40 acres each; Congress, unwilling to attack property rights even of traitors, refused. The "forty acres" promise is the most important thing that did *not* happen in this book, and Chapter 8 returns to *why* Northern Republicans refused it and what propertylessness then cost the people it was refused to.

What filled the vacuum where land redistribution should have been is the economic system that would define the South for the next eighty years. Understand its mechanism here, completely, because the rest of the book will assume you have it and Chapter 8 will move past it to what it did to people and to the region. Freedpeople flatly refused to work in the slavery-style **gang labor** of the plantation, overseers, sunup-to-sundown collective field work under the lash; refusing that was one of the first concrete contents they put into the word "freedom." Planters had land but, with Confederate money worthless and slaves emancipated, no cash to pay wages and no capital. The compromise that emerged across the late 1860s was **sharecropping**: a Black (or poor white) family worked a defined plot of a planter's land and, instead of wages, kept a *share* of the crop they raised, typically a half or a third, the planter taking the rest as rent. It gave the laborer something real, daily autonomy, family control over their own work and time, no overseer, and freedpeople fought for it deliberately for exactly that reason. But it was bound to a second mechanism that turned it into a trap: the **crop-lien**. A sharecropper had no cash and no land to borrow against, so to get seed, food, and tools between plantings he bought them on credit from a country merchant (often the same planter), who secured the debt with a *lien*, a legal claim, on the not-yet-grown crop, at effective annual interest that, once carrying charges and credit-price markups are counted, economic historians put commonly in the 40 to 60 percent range and sometimes well past 100. Here is the fraud mechanism in one sentence, because everything turns on it: the merchant kept the only ledger, set the price the goods were "charged" at and the price the cotton was "credited" at, and the cropper, by law usually forbidden a literate education and often unable to check the arithmetic, had no enforceable way to dispute the tally. At harvest the crop was sold, the merchant's figure was deducted, and an enormous share of croppers were simply told the year's labor had not cleared the year's debt, so they entered the next year already owing, legally bound to keep farming for the same creditor to work it off. Sharecropping plus the crop-lien produced a system in which a nominally free family could work full-time, for life, and never get out of debt or own anything, tied to the land and the merchant by law rather than by ownership. It was not slavery; the distinction is real and the people living it knew it, family autonomy and the absence of the lash mattered enormously. But as an answer to "what does freedom mean," it was a bitter one, and it is the direct consequence of the land that was promised and taken back.

So by the end of 1865 the white South had given its answer to the open question, in legislation and in economics: freedom would mean not-owned and nothing more, enforced by Black Codes and locked in by debt. The question now was whether the rest of the country would accept that answer. Congress came back into session in December 1865, looked at what Presidential Reconstruction had built, and refused to.

# Chapter 4 — The Break

The collision between the president and Congress that defines the next three years was not, at its root, an argument among saints about racial justice. It was triggered by something cruder and therefore more durable: a Northern public conviction that the South had lost the war and was being handed the peace, and the spectacle of unpunished massacre making that conviction unanswerable. This chapter is the break itself, the year, 1866, in which Presidential Reconstruction collapsed and Congress took the wheel.

When Congress reconvened in December 1865 it did the first concrete thing that signaled the war was on: it refused to *seat* the men the restored Southern states had elected. The Constitution makes each house of Congress the judge of the "elections, returns, and qualifications" of its own members, a clause normally a housekeeping technicality but, read at full stretch, a power for the sitting majority to refuse anyone it chooses; the courts have almost never policed it, so in practice the limit is political, not legal. The Republican majority used it to the hilt and simply declined to admit the ex-Confederate senators and representatives, the Confederate vice president among them, that Johnson's lenient process had sent. No Confederate-elected Southerner took a seat. That refusal was the procedural hinge of everything: it meant the South, for now, had no votes in Congress, which meant the Republican majority could legislate Reconstruction over a Southern void, and the only obstacle was the president's veto.

Congress's opening substantive moves were two bills, and the fight over them is the cleanest possible illustration of the break. The first extended and strengthened the **Freedmen's Bureau** (Chapter 3). The second was the **Civil Rights Act of 1866**, and you should understand exactly what it did because it is the seed of the constitutional revolution in Chapter 5. It declared, for the first time in American law, that all persons born in the United States were *citizens*, with one carved-out exception, "Indians not taxed," meaning members of Native nations who lived under tribal government and outside the ordinary tax rolls, whom the law treated as belonging to separate sovereign peoples rather than as U.S. citizens (Native Americans would not get blanket birthright citizenship until 1924), and that citizens had, regardless of race, the same rights to make contracts, sue, give evidence, and own property, and the equal protection of the laws. Read against Chapter 3, it is precisely a federal sledgehammer aimed at the Black Codes: a Mississippi law saying a Black man cannot testify against a white man or own land directly contradicts a federal law saying every citizen has those rights equally, and federal law wins. It was a direct answer to the white South's answer.

Johnson vetoed both. A **veto** is the president's constitutional power to reject a bill Congress has passed; Congress can override a veto and make the bill law anyway, but only by a two-thirds vote in *both* houses, a deliberately high bar. Johnson's veto messages were not narrow legal objections; they were a worldview. He argued the Civil Rights Act was unconstitutional federal overreach into matters belonging to the states and, explicitly, that it unfairly discriminated *in favor of* Black Americans against whites, language a modern reader will find eerily familiar. He was not hiding the worldview of Chapter 2; he was legislating it. And then, in April 1866, Congress did something that had never been done before: it **overrode** the veto of a major piece of legislation, mustering the two-thirds in both houses to make the Civil Rights Act law over the president's objection. It overrode the Freedmen's Bureau veto too, that July. The override of the Civil Rights Act is one of the genuine constitutional firsts in this book. Congress had overridden a handful of minor, narrow vetoes in earlier decades, but no president had ever been overruled on a *significant* national law; this was the first, and it marked the precise moment power over Reconstruction passed from the executive to the legislature. From here, the story's main political question becomes whether Congress can hold the two-thirds, because as long as it can, the president is, on Reconstruction, irrelevant.

What turned a Washington power struggle into a national moral verdict, though, was not the override. It was blood. In 1866 two events made the abstract argument about whether the South could be trusted to govern its freedpeople into something Northern voters could see. In May, in **Memphis**, a white mob with the police and firemen in it and at times leading it attacked the Black neighborhood of South Memphis for three days after a trivial street altercation involving recently discharged Black Union soldiers. The congressional investigators counted about 46 Black residents killed against 2 whites, at least five Black women raped, and roughly ninety homes, twelve schools, and four churches burned, the lopsidedness of those numbers being itself the testimony; the 46 figure is the congressional count and other tallies run a few lower, so call it the mid-40s. In July, in **New Orleans**, when Louisiana Republicans tried to reconvene a convention to enfranchise Black men, white mobs and police stormed the hall: delegates and the Black crowd outside were shot, clubbed, and in sworn testimony fired on as they raised their hands or jumped from windows. The death toll is given as a range, roughly 34 to 48, most of them Black, because the police controlled the scene and the body count, and Republican and Democratic investigators returned different figures for that reason. Congress investigated both in detail, and the reports did something specific and decisive: they let a Northern public read, in sworn testimony, exactly what Presidential Reconstruction's "let the white South govern itself" produced. Not abstract injustice, organized, police-led massacre of the people the war had freed.

Johnson's response sealed his own destruction. In the late summer and fall of 1866, with the crucial midterm congressional elections approaching, he did something no president had done, he went on a personal campaign speaking tour, the **"Swing Around the Circle,"** traveling the North to stump *against* the Fourteenth Amendment (Chapter 5) and the Republican Congress. It was a catastrophe. Johnson, thin-skinned and undisciplined, traded insults with hecklers from the platform, compared his own persecution to Christ's, and was widely judged to have degraded the dignity of the presidency itself. Earlier that year, on Washington's Birthday, he had given an impromptu speech naming Thaddeus Stevens and Charles Sumner, the leading Radicals, as traitors as dangerous as the Confederates. The combination, massacres in the South, a president campaigning against Black citizenship and behaving like a brawler, produced the verdict in November 1866: a Republican landslide that gave the party more than two-thirds of *both* houses of Congress. Anchor what that supermajority means mechanically, because it is the engine of the next two years: with a guaranteed two-thirds in both chambers, Congress could override any veto Johnson cast, at will, automatically. The president was now, on Reconstruction, a spectator. Congress had not just broken with Johnson; the 1866 elections had handed it the power to govern Reconstruction *without* him. The question stopped being whether Congress could act and became what it would do with near-total power. The answer was the most radical thing in the book: it rewrote the Constitution.

# Chapter 5 — The Second Founding

Now the constitutional revolution, which is the spine of the whole book and the part whose consequences are still being litigated in the United States today. Between 1865 and 1870 the country added three amendments to its Constitution that, taken together, changed what citizenship is, what the federal government is for, and whose side it is on. Modern historians call this the **"Second Founding,"** and the phrase is precise: the original Founding of 1787 had built a republic around a compromise with slavery (Early American Republic Ch 5), and these amendments are the attempt to refound it without that compromise. This chapter is the two amendments that did the heavy work and the laws that put the army behind them.

Start with the **Thirteenth Amendment**, the one already in place, because the others answer a problem it created. Ratified in December 1865, it abolished slavery and involuntary servitude everywhere in the United States, permanently, the final, constitutional answer to the question the war had answered with armies. It freed the last legally enslaved people in the loyal border states like Kentucky and Delaware, whom the Emancipation Proclamation, a war measure, had never reached; for several hundred thousand people, the literal last day of legal American slavery was the day in December 1865 the amendment was ratified. But notice the door it left open, because the door matters for a century. The Thirteenth bans slavery "except as a punishment for crime." Reread Chapter 3's Black Codes with that clause in mind: arrest a Black man for "vagrancy," convict him, and you may now constitutionally compel his labor, because he is a criminal. The Southern states drove a system through that loophole called **convict leasing**, arresting Black men in large numbers and leasing them to planters, railroads, and mines under conditions that contemporaries and historians agree were frequently *deadlier* than slavery, because a leased convict, unlike an owned slave, represented no capital the lessee would lose if he worked the man to death. The Thirteenth ended ownership and immediately revealed that ownership had not been the only mechanism of unfreedom. That is the problem the Fourteenth Amendment exists to solve.

The **Fourteenth Amendment** is the most important and most litigated provision added to the Constitution since the Bill of Rights, and you need to actually understand its first section, so here it is in plain language. It does four things. First, the **Citizenship Clause**: anyone born in the United States is a citizen of the United States and of the state they live in. That one sentence overturned, directly and by name, the *Dred Scott* decision of 1857, in which the Supreme Court had ruled that Black people could not be citizens at all (Antebellum America Ch 15); the Fourteenth begins by erasing the worst sentence the Court had ever written. Second, the **Privileges or Immunities Clause**: no state may abridge the privileges or immunities of citizens of the United States. Third, the **Due Process Clause**: no state may deprive any *person* of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. Fourth, the **Equal Protection Clause**: no state may deny any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws. Sit with what is structurally new here. The original Bill of Rights restrained the *federal* government; states could and did violate rights freely, and the Constitution did not care. The Fourteenth Amendment, for the first time, makes the *states* answerable to the federal Constitution for how they treat the people inside them, and makes the federal government the guarantor. This is the hinge of modern American constitutional law. Essentially every twentieth- and twenty-first-century civil-rights ruling you may have heard of, school desegregation, interracial marriage, one-person-one-vote, runs through the Equal Protection or Due Process Clause of this amendment, written in 1866 to protect freedpeople from the Black Codes. That reach, and the long century it took before the clause was made to mean what it says, is the subject of a later narrative in this chain, the twentieth-century movement that called itself, deliberately, a *Second Reconstruction* and opened by quoting this exact text back at the country, beginning with *Brown v. Board of Education* in 1954 and the assault on segregated public accommodations (the Civil Rights Era narrative, Chs 2 and 10). It is also, in one of the era's great ironies returned to in Chapter 12, the clause corporations would soon use far more successfully than freedpeople ever could, by arguing they were "persons" too.

A note the writing rules require: the Fourteenth was not purely a Radical project, and saying so matters for honesty. Its actual Section 1 language was largely drafted by a *moderate* Republican, John Bingham of Ohio, and the whole amendment was a negotiated compromise between Radicals who wanted more, immediate Black suffrage written in, harsher penalties on the South, and moderates who would only go so far. The other sections show the compromise: Section 2 *penalized* states that denied Black men the vote by cutting their congressional representation, rather than simply guaranteeing the vote, a Radical defeat. Section 3 barred anyone who had taken an oath to the Constitution and then joined the rebellion from holding office again unless Congress granted a two-thirds waiver, a clause that sat essentially unused for 150 years and then returned to the news in the early 2020s, when it was litigated over whether it disqualified officials connected to the January 6, 2021 attack on the Capitol. Section 4 guaranteed the federal war debt and voided all Confederate debts and any claim for compensation for freed slaves, constitutionally bankrupting the financial expectations of the slaveholders, a clause occasionally invoked in modern debt-ceiling arguments. The lasting architecture of Reconstruction was a moderate-Radical bargain, not a Radical dictation, which is part of why it could pass and part of why it had gaps.

The South's response to being offered the Fourteenth Amendment is the cleanest evidence of what the white South intended, and it forced the next escalation. Congress made ratification of the Fourteenth essentially the price of readmission. Every Southern state under Johnson's restored governments except Tennessee *rejected* it. The white South was offered citizenship and equal protection for the freedpeople as the condition of full return to the Union and said no. That refusal destroyed what was left of the moderate position and produced the most aggressive federal action of the entire era.

That "no" forced the single most aggressive act of federal power in this entire story, and it deserves to be seen, not just summarized. In March 1867, over Johnson's vetoes and using the automatic supermajority of Chapter 4, Congress passed the **Reconstruction Acts**, which begin **Congressional**, or **Radical**, **Reconstruction**. The acts declared the existing Southern state governments provisional and illegitimate, dissolved them as legitimate authority, and divided ten ex-Confederate states into **five military districts**, each placed under a U.S. Army general with the power to govern. Picture what that meant on the ground, because the abstraction "military districts" hides the most extraordinary thing the federal government did in the nineteenth century. In a county seat in Alabama or Mississippi in 1867, the highest authority was no longer the governor or the local judge. It was a Union officer, often a man who had fought the war that ended two years earlier, who could now remove a mayor, void a court ruling, halt a discriminatory ordinance, and order the army to protect a registration table. Federal soldiers, not local officials, ran the voter rolls: army-appointed boards of registration, with troops standing by, signed up roughly 700,000 Black and 660,000 white voters, and for the first time in the history of those states a Black man's name went onto the rolls under the protection of a bayonet rather than at the sufferance of the men who had owned him. To get back into the Union, a state now had to do exactly what the white South had just refused: hold a convention elected by *all* male citizens regardless of race, write a new constitution, and ratify the Fourteenth Amendment. The army would not leave until it did.

What happened in those conventions is the payoff, and it is worth seeing concretely rather than as a phrase. In 1867 and 1868, in statehouses across the South, men who two years earlier had been legally property, who in most of those states it had been a crime to teach to read, sat as elected delegates, in some states as majorities, drafting the supreme law of the state that had enslaved them. They wrote, against entrenched resistance, the first public school systems the South ever had, struck out the property requirements that had limited voting and officeholding to men of means, and produced what were, by a wide margin, the most democratic constitutions the region had ever known. This is the thing the Dunning School would spend fifty years lying about and which Chapter 7 tells in full. End the chapter here, on the register of what had just happened: in the span of two years the Constitution had been rewritten three times over and the country had, on paper and with the army standing behind the paper, finally answered the open question of Chapter 1 in the affirmative. Freedom meant citizenship, equal protection, and the ballot, and there were now Black men with rifles' worth of federal authority behind their names writing it into law. It was the high-water mark of the whole story, and almost everything in the rest of this book is the tide going back out. One man in the White House was already working with every power he had left to pull it out faster, and Congress, having stripped him of nearly everything else, now moved to take the office itself.

# Chapter 6 — The Trial of a President

In the spring of 1868 the United States did something it had never done and would not do again for 130 years, and then, after that long gap, three more times in the quarter-century after: it put the president on trial to remove him from office. The impeachment of Andrew Johnson is often told as a soap opera with a cliffhanger ending, and it was that, but it matters for two structural reasons this chapter has to make clear, what it was actually about, and what its outcome did and did not establish for every president since.

First, define the machinery exactly, because most people half-know it. **Impeachment** is not removal. The Constitution gives the House of Representatives the sole power to *impeach*, which means to formally accuse, by a simple majority vote; an impeached official stays in office. The case then goes to the Senate, which holds a trial; removal requires conviction by a *two-thirds* vote of the Senate. So impeachment is an indictment and the Senate trial is the trial, and the supermajority requirement for conviction is deliberately, almost prohibitively, high, because the framers wanted removing an elected president to be very hard. Hold the arithmetic: in 1868 there were 54 senators, so conviction required 36 votes, and acquittal required only 19.

Now what it was about, because the surface charge and the real conflict were different and the gap is the whole point. By 1867 Congress and Johnson were in total war (Chapters 4–5). The army was the instrument enforcing Reconstruction in the occupied South, and the army answered to the secretary of war, **Edwin Stanton**, a Lincoln holdover who sided fully with Congress and the Radicals. Johnson, as commander in chief, wanted Stanton gone so he could install someone who would soften military enforcement. Congress, to stop exactly that, had passed the **Tenure of Office Act** in 1867, a law forbidding the president to remove certain officials confirmed by the Senate without the Senate's consent. It was, fairly openly, a trap, of dubious constitutionality, designed so that if Johnson fired Stanton he would be breaking a federal law and could be impeached for it. In February 1868 Johnson sprang the trap on purpose: he dismissed Stanton, who refused to leave and barricaded himself in the War Department office. Within days the House impeached Johnson, 126 to 47, on eleven articles, nine of them built on the Stanton firing and the Tenure of Office Act.

Here is the part an honest account has to foreground: the formal charge was thin and most historians think the law Johnson "violated" was probably unconstitutional (the Supreme Court would say as much, about a similar law, in 1926). The real reason Congress wanted Johnson gone was not a technical violation of a tenure statute. It was that he was using every power of the presidency, vetoes, pardons, appointments, the bully pulpit, to sabotage Reconstruction and protect the ex-Confederate order, and a Northern majority had come to see him as, in effect, still fighting the war on the other side from inside the White House. The impeachment was substantively about that and procedurally about a doorknob. That mismatch is exactly what makes its outcome important.

The Senate trial ran about three months in the spring of 1868, the chief justice presiding, and a team of House "managers" prosecuting, the managers being the representatives the House appoints to argue its case at a Senate impeachment trial, in effect the prosecutors. They included **Benjamin Butler**, a politically slippery Massachusetts ex-general notorious from his wartime command of occupied New Orleans, and **Thaddeus Stevens**, the great Radical leader of Chapter 1, by now visibly dying (he would be dead within months and had himself carried into the chamber), determined to see Johnson removed before he went. And it came down, in May 1868, to a single vote. The Senate voted 35 to convict, 19 to acquit, *one vote short* of the 36 needed. Seven Republicans broke with their party to acquit, the most remembered being **Edmund Ross** of Kansas. Johnson stayed in office, finished his term as a politically destroyed and essentially powerless man (Grant was already the certain next president), and the Reconstruction Congress simply governed around him for his last months.

Now the thing the chapter exists to establish, because it is the durable consequence and it cuts against an easy reading. Ross's vote was mythologized in the twentieth century, most famously in *Profiles in Courage*, the 1956 best-seller of short biographical portraits of principled politicians published under then-Senator John F. Kennedy's name, which cast Ross as a lone hero defying his party to save the Constitution. Later historians, notably Ralph Roske and others working from the patronage record, have complicated that: they argue Ross used his pivotal position to extract **patronage**, the era's currency of politics, government jobs and contracts a powerful figure could hand to allies, in this case federal appointments Ross is documented to have sought and won for his Kansas associates around the time of his vote. The revisionist case is itself contested, not settled fact, and the honest reading is that Ross's motives were probably mixed, principle and self-interest both, which is why this book flags the heroism as debated rather than either celebrating or debunking it. But the *structural* result is not debatable and is genuinely important: the one-vote acquittal established, as a working constitutional norm that has held ever since, that a president will not be removed from office merely for pursuing policies, even disastrous and obstructive policies, that Congress hates. To remove a president you need something the country broadly accepts as a high crime, not a policy disagreement dressed in a statutory technicality. Every later impeachment fight in American history, and there have been several within living memory, operates inside the precedent set by these 36 votes that were not there. The acquittal arguably saved the presidency from becoming subordinate to congressional majorities, a parliamentary system installed by the back door, at the price of leaving an obstructive president in place for ten more months. Whether that trade was worth it is one of the genuine open arguments in this book, and the honest answer is that it cut both ways: it protected the independence of the office and it protected, for those ten months, a man using the office against the freedpeople.

With Johnson neutralized and Reconstruction's machinery in place, the camera now turns away from Washington and onto the thing all of this was supposedly *for*, and the thing the Dunning School lied about hardest: what the freedpeople actually did with the citizenship and the ballot once they had them.

# Chapter 7 — Black Reconstruction

In 1860 it was a crime in most of the South to teach an enslaved person to read. By 1867 the same people, plus their children, were registering voters by the hundreds of thousands, electing delegates, and sitting in constitutional conventions arguing the fine points of suffrage and taxation, and within three more years two of them sat in the United States Senate. Hold that compression in your mind, because the speed of it is the single fact that refutes the libel this chapter exists to bury. The phrase W. E. B. Du Bois used for what actually happened, made permanent by the title of his 1935 book, is **Black Reconstruction**: the brief period in which Black Southerners, free for the first time, built institutions, voted, governed, and pushed the country closer to its stated ideals than it had ever been, and were destroyed for it. The Dunning School inverted that into ignorant former slaves and thieving carpetbaggers misgoverning a prostrate South. The writing rules require naming a contested claim and then refuting it from the sources rather than just asserting the opposite, so that is what this chapter does, and the sources are unusually loud, because for the first time in this chain the people the history is about left a vast record in their own voices. The vehicle for the political mobilization was often the **Union League**. It had begun in the North during the war as a patriotic pro-Union club of the respectable middle class, and after 1867, carried south largely by Black veterans, Bureau agents, and traveling Republican organizers, it transformed into something quite different: a grassroots network that turned Black churches and schoolhouses into political clubs, often meeting at night and armed against attack, where freedmen learned the concrete mechanics of citizenship, how to register, how to mark a ballot, what a contract bound you to, what rights the new laws gave. It produced a disciplined, almost unanimously Republican Black electorate, which is exactly why, a few chapters from now, the League's local leaders are among the first people the Klan hunts. The demographic fact under all of this is the one Chapter 1 flagged: in 1860 enslaved people had been a *majority* of the population in South Carolina and Mississippi and over 40 percent in several other states, so the instant Black men could vote, the Southern electorate was transformed and, in several states, potentially Black-majority. That single fact, where the freedpeople actually were on the map, is why everything that follows became a war over the ballot box.

What they built with it, in the few years they had it, is concrete and worth listing because the specifics are the rebuttal. Around **2,000 Black men held public office** during Reconstruction. Sixteen sat in the U.S. Congress. Two became United States senators, both from **Mississippi**, the state with the largest enslaved population in the country in 1860, an irony so sharp it should stop you: the state that had been the densest concentration of American slavery sent, within a decade, two formerly subordinated Black men to the Senate. **Hiram Rhodes Revels**, an African Methodist Episcopal minister and Union chaplain, took his seat in February 1870, the first Black member of Congress in American history, and, in a detail that reads like deliberate historical irony but is simply what happened, the Senate seat he filled was one Mississippi had once given to **Jefferson Davis**, the president of the Confederacy. **Blanche K. Bruce**, born enslaved, took the other Mississippi Senate seat in 1875 and became the first Black senator to serve a full six-year term and to preside over the Senate. In the House, **Joseph Rainey** of South Carolina, born enslaved, became the first Black representative and the first to preside over that chamber. **Robert Smalls** had pulled off one of the war's most audacious escapes: in May 1862, an enslaved harbor pilot in Charleston, he waited until the white officers of the Confederate steamer *Planter* went ashore for the night, then put on the captain's hat, sailed the armed ship past the Confederate harbor forts giving the correct signals, picked up his own and the crew's families, and delivered the vessel to the blockading Union navy. He went on to a five-term South Carolina congressional career. **Pinckney Benton Stewart Pinchback**, known by his initials P. B. S., briefly served as acting governor of Louisiana, the only Black governor in American history until 1990. **Jonathan Jasper Wright** sat for seven years on the South Carolina Supreme Court. In the South Carolina House, Black members were, for a time, a numerical majority. Roughly 600 Black men served in Southern state legislatures. This is a level of Black political representation that, after Reconstruction was destroyed, the country would not see again for *a century*: no Black American won a Senate election anywhere in the country until Edward Brooke of Massachusetts in 1966, ninety-one years after Bruce, and in almost the same months the movement's integrationist coalition was fracturing toward Black Power (the Civil Rights Era narrative, Ch 15). That gap, a democracy a Black majority helped build and was then locked out of by terror and then by law, is not unique in kind. Apartheid South Africa later excluded a Black majority from the vote by statute for forty-six years, and newly independent Ceylon stripped its Tamil minority of citizenship and the franchise in 1948–49 through nominally neutral tests of exactly the sort the post-Reconstruction South would perfect. What was unusual about the American case was that the rights had been *granted and exercised first*, and then taken back.

Address the Dunning libel directly, because the writing rules require naming the contested claim and refuting it with evidence rather than just asserting the opposite. The charge was that these governments were uniquely corrupt and incompetent. The honest answer has three parts. One: some Reconstruction governments did have corruption, railroad-bond graft especially, where a legislature, lobbied and bribed, would pledge the state's credit, issuing bonds the taxpayers had to repay, to subsidize a railroad whose promoters then pocketed the money and sometimes never built the line. But that was the *ordinary* corruption of the Gilded Age, happening in New York, Washington, and across the white North on a far larger scale at the same time (Chapter 11), not a special Black pathology, and the men doing most of the actual stealing in the South were white. Two: the same governments created the South's *first public school systems*, rebuilt the wrecked infrastructure, wrote its most democratic constitutions, and abolished property qualifications, real achievements the Redeemers immediately gutted. Three: the Black legislators were not the ignorant figures of the caricature. Many were ministers, soldiers, artisans, and the formally educated; **Robert Brown Elliott** of South Carolina, a brilliant lawyer, delivered an 1874 House speech answering the ex-Confederate Alexander Stephens, who had risen to argue that civil-rights legislation exceeded Congress's power. Elliott took apart the constitutional argument point by point and then turned it personal and historical, noting that he, born to a people Stephens's Confederacy had called its "cornerstone" of inferiority, now stood on the House floor refuting the Confederacy's vice president on the meaning of the Constitution, the fact of the scene being itself the rebuttal of the entire libel. The "carpetbagger" (Northern newcomer, supposedly an opportunist with everything he owned in a cheap carpet bag) and "scalawag" (white Southern Republican, the word literally meant a worthless farm animal) were *slurs*, coined by the people who lost, accepted as descriptive fact by historians for fifty years. The newcomers were a mixed lot of teachers, veterans, investors, and reformers, not a uniform plague.

Now the part of Black Reconstruction that did not depend on holding office and that outlasted everything that did, because it is the human heart of the book and it survived even Redemption. Freedpeople built, with their own hands and almost no resources, the institutions that would carry Black America through the catastrophe coming in the second half of this book. They walked out of white-controlled churches en masse and built their own, above all the African Methodist Episcopal and the Black Baptist churches, which exploded from tens of thousands to hundreds of millions in aggregate membership over the era and became the central institution of Black civic, political, and educational life; a remarkable share of the Black political leadership just listed were ministers. They treated literacy as the practical substance of freedom and built **schools** with a hunger that startled Northern observers, often funding, building, and guarding the schools themselves when the Bureau could not. And they founded **colleges** that still exist and still matter: **Howard University** in Washington (chartered 1867), **Fisk University** in Nashville (1866), **Hampton Institute** in Virginia (1868), **Morehouse College** in Georgia (1867), and a whole cluster more, the historically Black colleges that would educate the Black professional class, doctors, lawyers, teachers, ministers, for the entire Jim Crow century. Fisk's student choir, the **Fisk Jubilee Singers**, set out in 1871 to raise money for the nearly bankrupt school by touring the North and then, in 1873, Britain, where on that tour they performed for Queen Victoria, and carried the spirituals composed in slavery into the world's musical canon. (Their arc, an oppressed people's distinctive art reaching the dominant society's grandest stages even as that society denied the people their rights, ran almost exactly parallel to the contemporary Welsh choral movement, which carried itself from coal-mining chapels to royal command performances in the very same decades.) The Singers are an emblem of the whole pattern: a thing of value made under bondage, carried into freedom, and refusing to die when the political freedom was killed. Keep these institutions in mind, because they are the answer to a question this book ends on, how anything survived at all.

That is what Black Reconstruction built. The rest of the book is the organized destruction of the political half of it, and the survival, against everything, of the institutional half.

# Chapter 8 — Forty Acres, and What Replaced It

Go back now to the thing Chapter 3 flagged as the most consequential failure of the era and give it the full chapter it deserves, because if you understand only one structural fact about why Reconstruction's gains were so fragile, it should be this one: the formerly enslaved were freed into propertylessness, in a place where the only people with property were the people who had enslaved them, and the federal government, having freed them, declined to change that. Everything about how vulnerable Black political power turned out to be runs back to this.

State the counterfactual precisely, because it is the most important "road not taken" in the book and it was not a fantasy. There was real confiscated and abandoned Confederate land, plantations whose owners had fled or forfeited, and there were concrete proposals to give it to the people who had worked it unpaid for generations. Sherman's **Special Field Order No. 15** (Chapter 3) had actually done it on the coast, 40-acre plots, the army's mules, some 40,000 people farming 400,000 acres by mid-1865. In Congress, **Thaddeus Stevens**, the fierce Pennsylvania Radical, proposed the most thorough version: confiscate the large plantations of leading Confederates outright and distribute the land to freedpeople in 40-acre allotments, with the rest sold to fund the war debt and pensions. Run Stevens's logic, because it was exactly right and the freedpeople had reached it independently: a Black population that owned its own land would have an economic base independent of the planters, could not be starved into signing coercive labor contracts, could not be evicted for voting Republican, and could defend its political rights because its livelihood did not depend on the goodwill of its former enslavers. Land was not a welfare program in this analysis. It was the material foundation without which the vote and citizenship were structurally hollow, and the people who would lose the vote said so, in advance, in plain words.

It did not happen, and the reasons are worth being exact about because they are not the reasons the Dunning School gave. Andrew Johnson's pardons (Chapter 2) returned the Sherman land and most confiscated land to its former Confederate owners through 1865 and 1866, the news carried in person, by his own account, by Bureau commissioner Howard. Stevens's confiscation plan was defeated in Congress, and not mainly by Southerners, who had no votes there yet (Chapter 4), but by *Northern Republicans* who balked at it. The objection that killed it was the sanctity of **private property**. Even most Republicans, even toward traitors, found the wholesale seizure and redistribution of land a more frightening precedent than they could accept; it looked, to nineteenth-century propertied Northerners, like an attack on the foundation of the social order itself. It is worth knowing this was not uniquely American cowardice but the Atlantic pattern: when Britain ended slavery in 1833 it paid £20 million to the slave *owners* and nothing to the freed, who got no land and became a propertyless plantation labor force in the same trap. The road not taken in the U.S. was not taken anywhere. There is also a hard irony the book should name, because it recurs in Chapter 11: the same Congress that could not bring itself to give freedpeople 40 acres of traitors' land was, in these same years, handing railroad corporations *tens of millions* of acres of public land. (British India was running the identical paradox simultaneously, granting railway corridors across peasant land while refusing any land reform that would touch the great landlords whose cooperation the Raj needed.) Land for the formerly enslaved was a dangerous assault on property; land for the railroads was policy. The Southern Homestead Act of 1866 was the fig leaf, it opened 46 million acres of public land in five Southern states to settlers including freedpeople, but the land was largely "pine barren" and swamp, sandy, acidic soil under scrub pine that grows trees but starves row crops, the freedpeople had no capital to clear and work it anyway, and the act was a near-total failure, repealed in 1876.

So what filled the space where land should have been is the system Chapter 3 laid out in full, **sharecropping** chained to the **crop-lien**, and the mechanism is not worth re-explaining; what is worth doing here is looking at what it produced over time, in aggregate and in a life, because that is the part Chapter 3 could not yet show. Take it as a life first. A freed couple signs on to a planter's plot in 1868. They are good farmers, they work hard, and at the first settling the merchant's ledger says they owe a little more than the crop brought. They sign again, because the law binds them to the creditor until the debt clears and there is no other land to go to. They are still on that plot, owing, in 1878; their children sign the contracts now; one of them dies still in debt on land the family never owned and could never buy. That was not an unlucky case. It was the modal outcome. Across the cotton South, the *typical* cropper family did not clear its debt in a given year, and the studies that have reconstructed plantation and merchant records find that the share of Black tenant families who climbed from sharecropping to renting to owning land was small and slow, advancing barely at all in the first generation after the war. The aggregate fact behind the individual one: in 1880, fifteen years after emancipation, the great majority of Black Southern farm families still owned no land at all and worked someone else's. The system also locked the whole region into cotton, and the reason is mechanical and worth stating because it is not obvious. The lien was secured against the crop, and the only crop a merchant would reliably lend against was cotton, because cotton was the dependable cash export with a price on every exchange; a cropper could not get credit to plant something else. So the South kept planting cotton on shrinking, exhausted soil with the same hoe-and-mule labor while the wheat-and-corn North and West mechanized, the reaper and the steel plow transforming yields the cotton South had neither the capital nor the credit nor the crop-flexibility to adopt. The region fell behind for decades, and that backwardness was a structural consequence of how emancipation was financed, not, as racist accounts claimed, of any deficiency in the land or its people.

One more institutional betrayal belongs here, briefly, because it did specific, lasting damage. The **Freedman's Savings and Trust Company**, chartered in 1865, was a bank set up to receive the savings of freedpeople, and they trusted it partly because its name and its Bureau associations made it look like a federal institution, which legally it was not. Tens of thousands of depositors put money in, individual families, Black churches, and **mutual-aid societies**, the dues-funded community self-help associations, common in Black life, that pooled small contributions to cover members' funerals, illness, and widows. The bank's white trustees then steered its deposits into reckless and self-dealing speculative loans, and it failed in 1874 in the wake of the Panic of 1873 (Chapter 11). The precise loss is often quoted as "$3 million wiped out," and the figure should be stated carefully: total deposits at failure were roughly $3 million, and depositors eventually recovered only a fraction of that over many years through slow government payouts, so the real loss to Black families was severe but not the whole sum and not instantaneous. The lasting damage was as much to trust as to capital: a population told that thrift was the road to freedom watched the institution that held its thrift loot and bury it, a lesson that soured Black engagement with American banking for generations.

So the economic verdict of Reconstruction, settled well before the political one, was this: freedom came without the material base that would have made it defensible, because the federal government feared property redistribution more than it valued Black security, and the system that filled the gap bound the freedpeople to the land and the merchant nearly as tightly as slavery had bound them to the master, by debt instead of by deed. Hold that, because it is *why* the political destruction in the second half of this book worked so completely. A people with no economic independence is a people whose political rights can be taken, because the man who owns your land and your debt can also control, or end, your vote. The Klan, when it came, was pushing against a structure that had already been deliberately left without a foundation. The same trap also explains a later exodus: the persistence of the crop-lien is what made the pull of Northern factory jobs so decisive in the Great Migration, the structural cause of the Harlem Renaissance and not a spontaneous response to wartime demand (the Roaring Twenties narrative, Ch 13).

# Chapter 9 — The Grant Years

In March 1869 the man who had won the Civil War became president, and for the first time since Lincoln the executive branch and Congress were on the same side of the open question. The presidency of **Ulysses S. Grant** is where Reconstruction's federal protection of Black rights reaches its high tide and then, across his two terms, begins the long slide that the rest of the book traces. Getting Grant right matters more than usual, because the Dunning School needed him to be a corrupt fool and modern scholarship has substantially rehabilitated him, and the gap between those two verdicts is itself a lesson in how this history was distorted.

Grant won the 1868 election on the slogan **"Let us have peace,"** pledged to protect Reconstruction, and the arithmetic of his victory is worth pausing on because it explains everything about why the ballot became the thing both sides would kill over. Grant's national popular-vote margin was *narrow*. The roughly 500,000 newly enfranchised Black men who voted, almost entirely for him, were plausibly the margin of his victory. Read that the way both parties read it instantly: Black suffrage was no longer an abstract question of justice, it was now the concrete foundation of national Republican power, which meant that to Southern Democrats, suppressing the Black vote was not incidental racism, it was the entire political strategy, and to Republicans, protecting it was self-preservation. The fight over Black voting that consumes the rest of the book is, among other things, a fight over who controls the United States, and after 1868 everyone involved understood that exactly.

The crowning constitutional act of the Grant era was the **Fifteenth Amendment**, ratified in February 1870, the third of the Reconstruction amendments and the completion of the "Second Founding." It forbade denying the right to vote on grounds of "race, color, or previous condition of servitude." For a few years it did exactly what it said: Black men voted across the South in enormous numbers and elected the officeholders of Chapter 7. But the writing rules demand precision on a "first" and this is a place to be precise about a fatal flaw, because the amendment's *wording* is the loophole the entire collapse runs through. The Fifteenth does not say "everyone gets to vote." It says you cannot deny the vote *because of race*. It is silent on poll taxes, literacy tests, property requirements, and "good character" clauses. A state that wanted to disenfranchise Black men could therefore, in a few years, simply do it by a different name, a poll tax and a literacy test apply to "everyone," and if they happen to fall almost entirely on Black voters, the amendment, as the Supreme Court would soon read it (Chapter 12), does not reach that. The radicals who wanted an affirmative, positive right to vote written into the Constitution lost that fight; what passed was a narrower negative prohibition with a hole in it the size of Jim Crow. That loophole governed disfranchisement for ninety-five years, until the Voting Rights Act of 1965 finally closed it, not by amending the Fifteenth but by giving it the enforcement machinery, federal examiners and preclearance, the 1870 Congress had refused to write (the Civil Rights Era narrative, Ch 12). To anchor the Fifteenth as a world "first," set 1870 against the rest of the hemisphere: Brazil would not even pass its gradual Free Womb Law until 1871 and would not abolish slavery until 1888; Cuba's slavery ran to 1886; the Ottoman reform edicts had nominally equalized Christians and Jews but touched no enslaved population resembling this one. A constitutional ban on denying the formerly enslaved the vote, in 1870, was without parallel anywhere. And the thing that makes Reconstruction a tragedy rather than merely an unfinished project is that this was a right *won and then taken back*, not a right never granted.

The Fifteenth Amendment also did something the book has to be honest about because it fractured a movement: it split the women's suffrage cause, and the split ran partly along an ugly racial line. The amendment enfranchised Black *men* and said nothing about women of any race. **Elizabeth Cady Stanton** and **Susan B. Anthony**, leaders of the women's-rights movement that had been building since before the war (Antebellum America Ch 8), opposed an amendment that enfranchised Black men while leaving all women out, and some of their rhetoric in doing so was explicitly, unattractively racist. **Lucy Stone**, a veteran abolitionist and suffrage organizer who had kept her own surname on marrying as a public statement, took the opposite side: the amendment was a real advance for Black men that should not be held hostage to the demand that women be included at the same moment. The movement split in 1869 into two rival national organizations, Stanton and Anthony's on one side and Stone's on the other, and stayed broken for two decades. The Supreme Court soon foreclosed the legal argument that women already had the vote under the Fourteenth Amendment, ruling in *Minor v. Happersett* (Chapter 12) that citizenship did not carry a right to vote, so a state could constitutionally refuse women the ballot; and in *Bradwell v. Illinois* the same year it signaled that the legal subordination of women generally was not something the new amendments would disturb. It is a case study in how a partial revolution forces bitter triage among the people it leaves out, and the book reports the racism in the suffrage split plainly rather than smoothing it over.

The Grant years are also where the wider country crowds into the frame, and the book has to widen the lens here, not as a digression but because the same army that did or did not protect freedpeople in the South was, at the same time, the army in the West, and the two operations were competing for one finite force. In May 1869 the **transcontinental railroad** was completed with a golden spike at Promontory Summit, Utah, joining the coasts by rail and remaking the national economy even as the South's future was being fought over. That railroad ran through the Plains, and the Plains were Native nations' treaty land, which sets up the parallel that matters. Grant's **"Peace Policy"** toward Native nations was billed as assimilation rather than extermination, but in practice the army kept breaking treaties to clear land for the railroad and the settlers, and Native resistance to that pressure produced the war in which, in June 1876, the Lakota and Cheyenne wiped out George Custer's cavalry detachment at the **Little Bighorn**, the army suffering the famous defeat, not inflicting it. Hold the two theaters side by side, because Chapter 14 turns on it. The post-1865 U.S. Army was small, never more than about 25,000 to 30,000 men for the entire country, and Grant's administration was running, with that one army, two simultaneous campaigns of opposite character: a campaign of conquest against Native nations in the West, which it resourced and pursued relentlessly, and a campaign of protection for Black citizens in the South, which it resourced grudgingly and would soon abandon. The same federal power that could be found in unlimited quantity to take the Plains could not, the country would decide, be spared to hold Mississippi. When a Southern governor's request for troops is refused in 1875 (Chapter 14), part of the answer to "why" is that the troops, and the political will to use them, were going west. And the first of the scandals that would corrode Grant's standing arrived early: the 1869 **Black Friday** gold-corner scheme, in which speculators tried to manipulate the gold market partly through Grant's brother-in-law. Hold the scandals; Chapter 11 is their reckoning. The point for now is that Grant himself was never shown to have stolen anything, the modern consensus is that he was personally honest and a poor judge of friends, but the wave of corruption around his administration would do real political damage to the cause he genuinely meant to protect, by handing its enemies the argument that the party of Reconstruction was rotten.

So by the early 1870s the picture is, briefly, at its best: three amendments in place, a sympathetic president, Black men voting and governing across the South, the institutions of Chapter 7 rising. And it was, at that exact moment, under sustained armed attack, by an organization that had been founded the year the war ended and had grown into something the United States had no prior category for, a paramilitary terrorist insurgency aimed at destroying multiracial democracy by murder. (The mid-nineteenth century did know mass political violence: in the very same year the Klan trials began, 1871, the French government drowned the Paris Commune in the blood of thousands of summarily executed civilians. But that was a state crushing a radical city; the Klan was the inverse, a stateless insurgency murdering to overthrow an elected biracial government, which is closer to genuinely new.) That is the next chapter, and it is the hinge of the book.

# Chapter 10 — The Klan Wars

This is the chapter where the book has to name plainly what most of its second half is about: organized terrorism. The destruction of Reconstruction was not an organic political tide. It was, in significant and documented part, a sustained campaign of murder, arson, rape, and assassination, waged by paramilitary organizations against Black voters, Black officeholders, white Republicans, schools, and churches, with the political objective of overthrowing elected governments and ending Black political participation by force. The book uses the word "terrorism" deliberately and accurately: the violence was organized, political, and designed to terrify a population out of exercising rights it legally held. This chapter is the first and most famous of those organizations, the **Ku Klux Klan**, and, just as importantly, the proof, supplied by the federal government itself in 1871, that the terror could be stopped when the government chose to stop it.

The Klan's origin is almost absurdly small against what it became, and the smallness is itself instructive. In late 1865 or early 1866, in Pulaski, Tennessee, a handful of bored young Confederate veterans, the founding lore says six, though that tidy number comes from the Klan's own later mythmaking and should be held loosely, started a social club. They took the Greek word *kuklos*, "circle," roughed it into the deliberately strange "Ku Klux," tacked on "Klan" for the alliteration, and gave themselves nonsensical titles and outlandish costumes. Within about two years it had metastasized, across the South, into a decentralized network of local "dens" coordinating through ritual, disguise, and night-riding. Its probable first national leader was the former Confederate cavalry general **Nathan Bedford Forrest**, a name worth knowing in full: a self-made slave trader before the war, a feared and brilliant cavalry commander during it, and the officer in command at the 1864 Fort Pillow massacre, where surrendering Black Union soldiers were killed, so the man who most likely led the first Klan brought to it a documented record of killing Black soldiers who had laid down their arms. His exact role as "Grand Wizard" is historically disputed, which is why the text says "probable," but the trajectory is not. Its decentralization was not a weakness; it was the design feature that made it deadly and deniable. There was no single command to decapitate, no membership list to seize; it was a method and a costume as much as an organization, which is exactly why state governments, even Republican ones with Black majorities, found it nearly impossible to suppress with ordinary policing. Local sheriffs were often members or sympathizers; juries would not convict; witnesses who testified were murdered.

What the Klan actually did is documented in extraordinary, sworn detail, and the book should lean on that, because the existence of the documentation is itself the rebuttal to fifty years of minimizing it. In 1871 a joint committee of Congress took thousands of pages of testimony and published a **thirteen-volume report** on Klan violence across the South. As a sworn, first-person, government-compiled record specifically of organized *insurgent* terror aimed at destroying an electoral democracy, it has few rivals anywhere; the great atrocity records of the same era, the British famine commissions in India, the later testimony on the Congo rubber regime, document state or corporate violence against subject peoples, not a paramilitary war on a functioning multiracial franchise, which makes the Klan report unusual in kind, not just in volume. It contains whippings, mutilations, rapes, the burning of schools and churches, and the targeted assassination of Black political leaders and white Republican officials, told under oath by victims and witnesses. The Klan's targets were not random; they were strategic. It went after exactly the people and institutions that made Black political power possible: Black men who voted or organized others to, Black officeholders and the white Republicans allied with them, the teachers in the freedmen's schools (Chapter 7), the schools and churches themselves. Black Union veterans, an organized, politically conscious cohort (Chapter 1), were a particular target. The violence spiked, predictably and by design, around *elections*. This was not racial hatred venting at random. It was a coordinated program to dismantle the electoral foundation of Reconstruction county by county, and in many places, by 1870, it was working.

Now the part of the chapter that matters most for the book's central argument, because it is the proof of the thesis. Faced with an open insurgency the states could not suppress, the federal government, in 1870–71, did something unprecedented: it made private terrorism a federal matter and brought the army to bear. Congress passed three **Enforcement Acts**. The first (1870) made it a federal crime to use force or intimidation to deny citizens the vote. The second (1871) put elections under federal supervision. The third, the **Ku Klux Klan Act** of April 1871, was the strongest: it let the *president* use the army against conspiracies to deny civil rights and *suspend habeas corpus* to do it. Define **habeas corpus**, because the suspension is the whole point: it is the ancient legal right that forces the government to bring an arrested person before a court and justify holding them, the core protection against being locked up without trial; suspending it lets the government detain suspected insurgents without immediately charging them, an extraordinary power the Constitution permits only in cases of rebellion. Congress, in 1871, formally classified Klan terror as a rebellion against the United States.

One institutional fact in this sequence deserves its own paragraph, because it is the book's voice at its sharpest and it is genuinely almost never taught. In 1870 Congress created the **Department of Justice** itself. The federal government had had an attorney general since 1789 but no department, no organized national legal arm, and the immediate reason it built one in 1870 was, in significant part, to give Washington the lawyers and marshals it needed to prosecute the Klan. The agency Americans now associate with everything from antitrust to terrorism was, at its founding, a Reconstruction civil-rights enforcement bureau. No other major government in the world in 1870 had a ministry with that purpose: the British Home Office had no civil-rights remit, the French Ministry of Justice no charge to protect a racial minority from its own neighbors. The United States built one, used it, and then spent a century forgetting why it had.

And then Grant *used* the powers. In October 1871 he suspended habeas corpus in nine South Carolina counties where the Klan was thickest, and federal marshals and troops arrested hundreds of Klansmen. Federal prosecutors brought thousands of indictments and won hundreds of convictions across the South in 1871–72. The effect was immediate and dramatic: the first Klan, as an organized force, was *broken*. It collapsed as a coherent organization by about 1872, and the 1872 elections across the South were comparatively peaceful, the calmest of the era.

Stop on that, because it is the load-bearing fact of the entire book. The terror was not an unstoppable force of nature. When the federal government chose to treat it as the armed insurrection it was, deployed prosecutors and troops, suspended habeas corpus, and sustained the effort, the terror dropped sharply and Black Southerners voted. The pattern across the whole era is consistent and it is the strongest evidence for this book's thesis: where federal enforcement was energetic, the violence fell and Reconstruction functioned; where federal enforcement was withdrawn, the violence surged and Reconstruction fell. The 1871 Klan trials are the clean experiment. They prove that what happened next, the surrender, the collapse, the abandonment, was not because the problem was unsolvable. It was solvable, and had just been solved, on a limited scale, in South Carolina in 1872. What changed was not the South's capacity for terror. What changed was the North's willingness to keep paying the price to stop it, and the reasons it stopped are the next chapter. The same principle runs through the Second Reconstruction in both directions: federal enforcement pushed by the movement eventually broke the terror, while federal inaction prolonged it (the Civil Rights Era narrative, Chs 9 and 15).

# Chapter 11 — The North Loses Interest

The single most important thing to understand about why Reconstruction was abandoned is that it was abandoned, not lost. Chapter 10 just demonstrated that the federal government had, by 1872, the legal tools, the proven method, and a recent success in suppressing the terror. What it lost over the next five years was not capacity. It was will. This chapter is about why the Northern public and political class, having paid in 750,000 lives to win the war and then mounted the most aggressive federal intervention in the country's history to secure its result, got tired and looked away. The reasons are not flattering and the book should not flatter them: corruption, exhaustion, racism that had never gone away, and, decisively, an economic catastrophe that made Northern voters care about their own ruin far more than about Black Southerners' rights.

Start with the corruption, because it did specific damage to the *moral standing* of the cause. The Grant era coincided with the Gilded Age, the era Mark Twain mockingly named for a society that was gold-plated, not gold, glittering and rotten underneath, and the scandals came in a relentless drumbeat. The **Crédit Mobilier** scandal, exposed in 1872, revealed that insiders of the Union Pacific railroad had skimmed enormous construction profits and spread discounted stock among congressmen to buy goodwill, the recipients including the sitting vice president, Schuyler Colfax, and a future president, James Garfield. The **Whiskey Ring** (1875) exposed federal officials and distillers diverting millions in liquor taxes, the conspiracy reaching Grant's own private secretary, Orville Babcock, the private secretary being roughly the era's equivalent of a White House chief of staff, the president's closest day-to-day aide. The **Belknap** affair (1876) caught the secretary of war taking kickbacks on military trading posts; he resigned minutes before the House impeached him. The **Sanborn** contracts, the **Black Friday** gold corner (Chapter 9), the looting of the Freedman's Bank (Chapter 8), the litany was constant. Grant himself was never shown to have profited, the consensus is that he was personally honest and disastrously loyal to crooked friends, but that distinction was lost on the public. The effect that matters for this book is specific: the party of Lincoln, the party of Reconstruction, the party that asked the North to keep spending blood and treasure to protect Black rights in the South on a *moral* argument, was now nationally synonymous with graft. It is very hard to sustain a costly moral crusade abroad, or in this case in the South, when the crusading party is being exposed as corrupt at home every few months. The scandals did not change anyone's view of racial justice directly. They drained the moral authority of the only institution defending it.

That drain produced an open revolt inside the party, and the revolt revealed how much of the North simply wanted to be done. In 1872 a faction of reform-minded Republicans, disgusted by Grant-era corruption and openly weary of the "Southern question," bolted as the **Liberal Republicans**, nominating the editor Horace Greeley, who was also endorsed by the Democrats. Grant crushed them and won a second term decisively. But the content of the revolt mattered more than its defeat. A significant, respectable slice of the Republican coalition, the educated, reforming Northern elite, had announced that it considered Reconstruction a tiresome, corrupting, and probably failed project that the country should wind down. They dressed this up in the language of "reconciliation" and "home rule" and "letting the South manage its own affairs," and the racism underneath the genteel language was real and only lightly buried: "home rule" meant white rule, and a great many Northerners were, by the early 1870s, perfectly willing to say so. (This was not a uniquely American exhaustion. In Britain in exactly these years Gladstone's great reforming Liberal ministry of 1868–74 ran out of moral energy and fell, in 1874, to a combined assault of scandal and economic strain, the same pattern, a reform coalition spent and turned out by corruption and hard times, which suggests a structural dynamic, not just American bad luck.) The 1872 **Amnesty Act** restored political rights to most of the roughly 150,000 ex-Confederates still barred under the Fourteenth Amendment, and that number needs calibration: it was not 150,000 statesmen but the broad pool of former officeholders and officers, from which the South now drew back its experienced white Democratic leadership at exactly the moment Northern enthusiasm for enforcement was draining, a gift to the counter-revolution timed almost perfectly to its needs.

And then came the thing that decided it: the economy collapsed. The crash actually began in Vienna in May 1873 and rolled west across Europe before it reached America; in September the major banking house of **Jay Cooke & Company**, overextended in railroad finance, failed and set off the **Panic of 1873**, which became the **Long Depression**, by some measures the longest economic contraction in American history, deep unemployment and falling wages persisting until roughly 1879. This was a *global* depression, hammering Austria-Hungary, Germany, and Britain at the same time, which matters here because it drained the surplus attention and money that protecting Reconstruction required everywhere at once. Run the political consequence, because it is the mechanism by which an economic event ended a civil-rights era. A Northern factory worker or farmer in 1874, out of work or watching wages fall, banks failing and savings gone, was being asked to keep paying for the expensive deployment of the army to protect the voting rights of Black people a thousand miles away. The depression made that ask politically impossible. National attention swung hard to jobs, debt, the railroads, and above all the **money question**, the bitter fight over **greenbacks versus the gold standard**: greenbacks were the paper dollars the government had printed during the war, not backed by gold, and debtors, farmers, and many workers wanted more of them, because more money in circulation meant easier credit and rising prices that made fixed debts lighter, while creditors and banks wanted a return to the **gold standard**, every dollar redeemable for a fixed weight of gold, which meant scarcer money, tighter credit, and debts that held their full value. It was, underneath the technical language, a fight over who absorbed the depression's pain, and to a ruined Northern voter in 1874 it was vastly more pressing than the South. In the 1874 midterms, with the depression at its worst and the scandals fresh, the Democrats captured the House of Representatives for the first time since before the war. That single election ended Congressional Reconstruction as a legislative engine: with the House in Democratic hands, no new enforcement legislation could pass, and the appropriations to fund the troops and prosecutors already authorized could be, and were, choked off. After 1874 the federal government still had the *laws* of Chapter 10 on the books. It simply stopped funding and using them.

So picture the actual end of it, because it did not look like a surrender; it looked like a clerk closing a file. In the South Carolina up-country in 1872 federal prosecutors had filled dockets with Klan indictments; two years later the appropriations were gone, the marshals were not hired, the cases were not brought, and the men who had been afraid to ride in 1872 understood, correctly, that no one was coming. The North had not been beaten in the field. It had read its own ledger and decided the line item was too expensive, and turned back to its banks and its railroads, and left the South's Republicans, Black and white, alone in the dark with the people who had spent ten years promising to kill them. What those people did once they were certain no one would stop them is the next two chapters.

# Chapter 12 — The Supreme Court Disarms the Constitution

While the political will was draining (Chapter 11), a parallel and equally decisive thing was happening in a marble building in Washington, and it gets its own chapter because Americans are often taught Reconstruction was killed only by bayonets and bargains and not also, just as fatally, by judges. The Supreme Court, between 1873 and 1876, took the three amendments of the "Second Founding" and read them so narrowly that the federal laws meant to enforce them were left, in practice, nearly powerless. By the time the troops were withdrawn (Chapter 15), the constitutional weapons were already broken. This chapter is that breaking, and it has to be precise, because the reasoning is the thing that did the damage and the reasoning outlived everyone involved.

Begin with the first cut, the **Slaughter-House Cases** of 1873, because it is technical and unglamorous and that is exactly why it was so destructive. The case itself was not about race at all; it was about a Louisiana law granting a monopoly to a single New Orleans slaughterhouse, which the city's other butchers sued to overturn. Their argument was not far-fetched at the time: the Fourteenth Amendment's sweeping language about the "privileges or immunities" of citizenship was widely read, by lawyers across the spectrum, as a broad new federal guarantee of ordinary civil rights against the states, and the butchers contended that the right to practice a lawful trade was exactly such a privilege the monopoly had taken from them. The Court, ruling against the butchers, read the **Privileges or Immunities Clause** so narrowly that it protected only a tiny set of purely *national* rights, things like the right to travel to the capital or use navigable waters, and emphatically *not* the broad civil rights of citizenship against state interference. Follow why that mattered far beyond butchers. The drafters of the Fourteenth had loaded much of the amendment's protective force into that clause, the idea that being a U.S. citizen carried substantive rights no state could abridge. Slaughter-House, in its first interpretation of the amendment, essentially read that clause out of the Constitution five years after it was ratified. The protective machinery the "Second Founding" had built had its main gear removed before it was ever really used, and removed in a case the public barely noticed because it was about meatpacking.

The same Court signaled in two cases that year that the new amendments would not disturb existing hierarchies of sex either, which the book includes because the writing rules require following a "first" honestly and because it shows the limits were not only racial. In **Bradwell v. Illinois** (1873), the Court upheld Illinois's refusal to license **Myra Bradwell** to practice law solely because she was a woman, and one justice added a *concurring opinion*, a separate opinion by a judge who agrees with the result but wants to give different or additional reasons, which carries no binding force but reveals the thinking, declaring that the "paramount destiny and mission of woman" was wife and mother, by divine design. In **Minor v. Happersett** (1875), the Court ruled unanimously that the Fourteenth Amendment's citizenship did *not* include a right to vote, so states could constitutionally deny the ballot to women, foreclosing the legal strategy of the suffrage movement (Chapter 9) until the Nineteenth Amendment in 1920. The new constitutional language of equality, the Court was establishing, would be read as narrowly as the justices could manage.

Now the cases that did the lethal damage to Black Reconstruction specifically, and they grow directly out of an atrocity this book covers fully in the next chapter, so meet it here only as the legal seed. On Easter Sunday 1873, a white mob in **Colfax, Louisiana**, massacred a large number of Black men, many after they had surrendered, in a dispute over a contested election (Chapter 13). Federal prosecutors, using the Enforcement Acts of Chapter 10, charged the perpetrators. The case reached the Supreme Court as **United States v. Cruikshank**, decided in 1876, and the holding is the single most consequential sentence in the legal destruction of Reconstruction, so understand it exactly. The Court held that the Fourteenth Amendment restrains only *states*, not *private individuals*. A state government may not deny equal protection; but a *private mob* murdering a hundred Black citizens is not "the state," and therefore, the Court reasoned, the federal government had no constitutional power to prosecute them, that was the job of Louisiana, the very state whose white population had just done the murdering. The federal convictions of the Colfax killers were thrown out. Decided the same term, **United States v. Reese** arose when two Kentucky election officials refused to let a Black man, William Garner, vote and were prosecuted under the Enforcement Acts; the Court struck down the key sections those officials were charged under and held that the Fifteenth Amendment did not confer a right to vote but only barred denial on a few named grounds, leaving poll taxes and literacy tests, exactly the future tools of mass disfranchisement, untouched.

Sit with what *Cruikshank* and *Reese* together accomplished, because it is the legal hinge of the book's second half. The federal government's entire toolkit for protecting Black Southerners, the Enforcement Acts, the Klan Act, federal prosecution of terrorists, depended on the federal government being able to reach *private* violence, because that is what the terror was: private mobs, not formal state action. *Cruikshank* held that it could not. In a single ruling, the Supreme Court informed every white paramilitary in the South that a sufficiently "private" massacre was beyond federal reach, and informed every federal prosecutor that the laws of Chapter 10 had just been judicially disarmed. The Court did not need to strike the amendments down. It simply read them so that the violence destroying Reconstruction fell precisely into the gap between what the amendments forbade (state action) and what was actually happening (private terror), and then announced that the gap was constitutional. The bayonets had not yet been withdrawn (Chapter 15); the Court had already taken the ammunition. None of this required striking the amendments down, and that is the point worth holding: courts have a recurring habit, well documented in this same period in Britain and across its empire, of leaving an ambitious reform statute formally on the books while interpreting it so narrowly that it loses its force, so what the Supreme Court did to the Fourteenth Amendment was a severe instance of a known judicial pattern, not a uniquely American act of bad faith. Now walk the rest of the line in one breath, because the chapter's job is to show the architecture complete. **1873**, Slaughter-House guts the Privileges or Immunities Clause. **1876**, Cruikshank puts private terror beyond federal reach and Reese opens the door to poll taxes and literacy tests. **1883**, the *Civil Rights Cases* strike down the 1875 federal law that had guaranteed Black citizens equal access to inns, theaters, and railcars (Chapter 15), ruling that Congress could bar discrimination by states but not by private businesses. **1896**, **Plessy v. Ferguson** completes it, upholding a Louisiana railcar-segregation law under the formula "separate but equal" and thereby constitutionalizing Jim Crow outright. From the meatpacking case to "separate but equal" is twenty-three years and four decisions, and the line through them is straight. The full Plessy story belongs to a later narrative in this chain, but the legal spine is built here: the "Second Founding" was hollowed out, clause by clause, while every word of it stayed on the page.

# Chapter 13 — Redemption

Now the counter-revolution in the field, with the political will gone (Chapter 11) and the courts having disarmed the law (Chapter 12). The white South's recapture of its state governments has a name its perpetrators gave it and were proud of: **Redemption**. The word is theological and was meant to be, the white South cast itself as a fallen people redeemed from the sin of Black and Republican government, "Redeemers" as saviors. This book uses the word because it is the historical term, while being exact about what the redemption actually consisted of: the violent overthrow, state by state, of elected governments by organized paramilitaries, after the federal government stopped resisting. This chapter is the open, armed phase, roughly 1873 to 1875, when the terror stopped wearing hoods and started wearing uniforms.

The change from the Klan to what replaced it is the key to the chapter and easy to miss. The first Klan (Chapter 10) was secret, disguised, deniable, and it had been *broken* by federal prosecution in 1871–72. What rose in its place after 1873 was different and more dangerous precisely because it was *not* secret. The **White League**, formed openly in Louisiana in 1874, did not wear hoods. It drilled in the open, in uniform, as the avowed paramilitary arm of the Democratic Party, with the publicly stated goal of overthrowing the Republican state government by force. So did the rifle clubs and the South Carolina **Red Shirts** (Chapter 15). The shift from hood to uniform is a precise index of the collapse of federal will: the Klan hid because in 1871 the federal government would prosecute it; the White League did not bother to hide because by 1874 it had correctly judged that the federal government would not. Open paramilitarism is what terror looks like once it has concluded it is safe, and that conclusion was a direct reading of Washington's retreat.

The atrocities of this phase are not a list to skim; the book has to make you see at least the central one, because it is the one that became the Supreme Court case that ended federal protection (Chapter 12). On Easter Sunday, April 13, 1873, in **Colfax, Louisiana**, in the aftermath of a violently disputed state election, a white mob with cannon attacked a group of Black men, many of them militia and freedmen, who had gathered to defend the parish courthouse. The defenders were overwhelmed; the killing did not stop when they surrendered. The death toll is genuinely uncertain and the honest range is wide for a specific reason, the killers kept no count and disposed of bodies; older and advocacy accounts run as high as 150, while the most careful modern reconstructions, including LeeAnna Keith's study of the massacre, settle nearer 60 to 100, most of them executed after laying down their arms. By any of those figures it is the deadliest single act of racial violence of the entire Reconstruction era, the **Colfax massacre**. Of the dozens charged, only a handful were tried, three convicted, and *all* convictions were thrown out by the Supreme Court in *Cruikshank* (Chapter 12). A massacre of that scale producing zero punishment was itself a message, broadcast across the South, that organized mass killing of Black citizens would be tolerated. The following year the White League gave the message an exclamation point: at **Coushatta, Louisiana**, in August 1874, it forced six *white* Republican officials to resign and then murdered them, along with Black witnesses, the killing of white officeholders signaling that the counter-revolution was now total. A month later, in September 1874, a White League force, contemporary estimates run loosely around several thousand, defeated the New Orleans police and state militia in open street battle, the **Battle of Liberty Place**, and held the Louisiana statehouse for three days. It was an outright armed coup against a state government, and what reversed it is the telling part: the League did not lose the fight, it stood down. President Grant ordered federal troops to New Orleans, and on word that the U.S. Army was coming the League simply withdrew and handed the building back, the Republican government restored not by combat but by the mere announced approach of federal soldiers, a measure of how much the army's presence still meant in 1874 and how much its absence would mean a year later. Louisiana by now had rival governors and rival legislatures, two competing claimants to each, because the violently disputed elections had produced parallel sets of "winners," each with its own certification, and the federal government had to choose which to recognize and back with troops; that unresolved doubleness made the state the era's worst case of contested legitimacy and the laboratory of the violence.

Watch the map go dark, because Redemption was a sequence, not an event, and the sequence is the argument. The border and upper South went first and earliest, Tennessee, Virginia, sometimes through less spectacular means. Then, as the depression and the scandals (Chapter 11) drained Northern will through 1873–74, the deep South began to fall to open force. Each state "redeemed" narrowed the territory where federal protection still reached and emboldened the paramilitaries in the states not yet taken. By the end of 1874 the Democrats had also taken the U.S. House (Chapter 11), so there was no longer a Washington majority that *wanted* to stop the sequence even if a president tried. The pattern in the field exactly matched the pattern in Washington: federal commitment receding, terror advancing into the vacuum, in lockstep, the red of Redemption spreading precisely as fast as the federal government chose to stop resisting it, and never faster.

Stand, for a moment, where it actually landed. A Black state legislator in 1874, a man who six years earlier had cast the first vote of his life and then helped write his state's constitution, could now read the sequence the way his enemies did: he could watch the neighboring state fall, see the federal garrison thin, hear the rifle clubs drilling openly in the next county, and know with precision that the floor was being removed under him one plank at a time, by men in Washington he would never meet, for reasons that had nothing to do with anything he had done. The terror was advancing, but the thing that made it unanswerable was not the terror. It was the silence behind it. He had been promised the protection of the strongest government on the continent, and he could feel it being withdrawn in real time, deliberately, while every word of the promise remained in the Constitution. That is the experience Redemption actually was for the people inside it: not a battle lost but a guarantor walking away. And the coldest single demonstration of that, an entire state lost not because it could not be held but because Washington decided, in writing, not to hold it, came in Mississippi the next year.

# Chapter 14 — The Mississippi Plan

Mississippi in 1875 gets a chapter to itself because it is the cleanest single proof of this book's central claim, that Reconstruction's collapse was a choice, not an inevitability. In Mississippi the white Democracy openly designed, named, executed, and *documented* a method for overthrowing an elected government by terror; the Republican governor asked the federal government for the troops that had worked everywhere they were used (Chapter 10); and the federal government, for stated reasons, said no. Everything ambiguous about the rest of the era is unambiguous here. You can watch the decision get made.

The stakes are the demographic fact this book has already established: Mississippi had a Black majority, and so in any honest election it was a Republican state, and it had been governing as one, with Black legislators and officials, the two Black U.S. senators of Chapter 7, and a reform Republican governor, **Adelbert Ames**, a Union general from Maine, honest, committed, and serious. To the white Democracy a functioning Black-majority democracy was both intolerable and, because of the arithmetic, unbeatable at an honest ballot box. So they decided not to do it honestly, and, unusually, they wrote down how.

The **Mississippi Plan** of 1875 was a strategy, discussed openly in the Democratic press, with three coordinated components. First, **economic coercion**: Black sharecroppers and laborers were told, by the men who controlled their land, debt, and jobs (Chapter 8), that voting Republican meant eviction, firing, and the merchant calling the lien, the structural propertylessness of Chapter 8 weaponized directly into vote control. Second, **open paramilitary violence**: armed white "rifle clubs" broke up Republican meetings by force, paraded with cannon to intimidate Black communities, and assassinated local Black leaders, with massacres at **Clinton** and **Vicksburg** killing dozens to hundreds. Third, and this is the chillingly modern part, **deniable provocation**: the rifle clubs were instructed to be the visible armed force while provoking Republicans into any act that could be framed as "Black violence" justifying the white "self-defense," a propaganda overlay that let the Northern press and public look away by pretending the aggressor and victim were unclear when they were not. It was a designed system for stealing a state, and its designers were not embarrassed by it; they regarded it as a triumph and exported it.

Now the decision, which is the whole reason this chapter exists. As the terror mounted through the summer and fall of 1875, Governor Ames did exactly what the law of Chapter 10 contemplated: he asked President Grant for federal troops to suppress what was, by any honest description, an armed insurrection against a state government, the same kind of insurrection that federal force had broken in South Carolina in 1871. And the Grant administration refused. The refusal was put in writing, and the stated reason survives in Attorney General Edwards Pierrepont's own words, conveyed to Mississippi's appeals: "the whole public are tired out with these annual autumnal outbreaks in the South." The phrase is genuine and appears in the documentary record of the episode; the further reasoning, that sending troops would cost the Republicans the pivotal Northern state of Ohio in that fall's election, is the well-attested political calculation behind the decision, drawn from the administration's own correspondence and contemporaries' accounts rather than from that single sentence. Read what the concession amounts to. It does not say the violence was not happening, or that troops would not work, the 1871 precedent of Chapter 10 had already proven they would. It says, in plain administrative prose, that the Northern public was *tired*, and that the political cost of acting, in Ohio, in an off-year election, outweighed the value of a Black-majority state's democracy. Mississippi was not lost because it could not be held. It was conceded, deliberately, for stated reasons of Northern political convenience, by the administration that had the tools and the proof that the tools worked, sitting in a desk drawer.

The result followed instantly. With no federal troops coming, and the small standing army of perhaps 25,000 men already committed to the Plains wars in the West rather than the South (Chapter 9), the Mississippi Plan worked exactly as designed. The 1875 elections were a rout achieved by terror; Black turnout collapsed under the guns and the economic threat; the legislature flipped to the Democrats, who then forced Governor Ames out in 1876 by threatening impeachment on trumped-up charges, a sham in the sense that the accusations were political pretexts manufactured to remove a governor the new majority could not legally fault, and Ames resigned in a bargain to have them dropped rather than fight a rigged trial. He left the state. He lived until 1933, which is long enough that he watched the very beginnings of the historians' turn against the Dunning version, the first scholarly challenges to it were appearing in the 1920s and early 1930s, and an aged Ames, asked about Reconstruction late in life, called its defeat what it was: a national crime, in which he had been abandoned. Mississippi became the template, written down, refined, and copied. South Carolina's Redeemers studied it for 1876 (Chapter 15). And it became, two decades later, the literal model for the disfranchisement constitutions, beginning with Mississippi's own in 1890, that wrote Black voting out of the South entirely through the loopholes the Supreme Court had blessed (Chapter 12). The Mississippi Plan is the book's thesis reduced to a single state and a single refused request: the machinery to defend multiracial democracy existed and had been proven to work, and the federal government, asked to use it, chose, in writing, to be tired instead.

# Chapter 15 — The Bargain

By 1876 only three Southern states still had Republican governments propped up by a remaining federal military presence: Louisiana, South Carolina, and Florida. Everything else had been "redeemed." This chapter is how the last three fell, and the mechanism is fitting for a book whose thesis is that Reconstruction was traded away rather than lost: it ended not with a battle but with a backroom deal, born out of a presidential election so close and so corrupted that the country had no agreed way to decide who had won, and resolved by a bargain in which the presidency was exchanged for the abandonment of the freedpeople.

First the field, because the last three states were not handed over; they were taken, in the open, and the reader should feel that before the bargain that ratified it. South Carolina, like Mississippi, was a Black-majority state, and in 1876 its Democrats ran the Mississippi Plan with local refinements. Their paramilitaries were the **Red Shirts**, who, like the White League, did not bother to hide; they wore literal red shirts as a uniform and a taunt, parading behind the ex-Confederate cavalry general **Wade Hampton III**, the Democratic candidate for governor, in a campaign that fused torchlit parades, economic threats, and murder into open political theater. It opened with the **Hamburg massacre** in July 1876. Hamburg was a small, almost entirely Black town on the Savannah River, just across from Georgia, governed and policed by Black officials; a Red Shirt force, using a trivial road-right-of-way dispute as the pretext the Mississippi Plan's playbook called for, surrounded the town's Black militia in their armory, shelled it with a cannon brought from Georgia, and after the militiamen surrendered, selected several and executed them one by one in the night, calling out names. The **Ellenton** killings, weeks of hunting that left dozens of Black residents dead, extended it. This was not the prelude to the campaign; it *was* the campaign, the Mississippi Plan with a wardrobe and a body count, and it worked. By election day South Carolina, like Louisiana and Florida, had been terrorized to the edge of, or past, a Democratic result, while the Republican state governments and federal observers contested the count. The same violence was producing the same disputed returns in all three remaining states at once.

Now the national crisis, and you have to understand the constitutional gap to see why it became a bargain. The 1876 presidential election pitted Republican **Rutherford B. Hayes** against Democrat **Samuel Tilden**. Tilden won the national popular vote outright and led the electoral count, sitting one electoral vote short of the presidency. The presidency turned on exactly the three unredeemed states, Louisiana, South Carolina, Florida, where the returns were genuinely disputed because the elections had been conducted amid organized terror and Republican election boards then threw out tainted returns, producing *two* sets of certified electoral votes from each of the three states, one Democratic, one Republican. Here is the constitutional problem the framers had not solved: the Constitution says electoral votes are counted before Congress, but it does not say *who decides* when a state sends two contradictory slates. There was no agreed mechanism. With a Democratic House and a Republican Senate, the two chambers could not agree, and the country drifted, through the winter of 1876–77, toward March 4 with no president-elect and loose talk of a second civil war.

The improvised resolution and the deal hidden inside it are the end of Reconstruction. Congress created a special **Electoral Commission** of fifteen to decide the disputed votes: five senators, five representatives, five Supreme Court justices. The party math was deliberately built to look balanced. The Republican Senate supplied three Republicans and two Democrats; the Democratic House supplied three Democrats and two Republicans; that left the five justices, and the design was four justices of known leaning, two from each party, plus a fifth, independent-leaning justice, David Davis, as the true swing vote. Then Davis was unexpectedly elected to the Senate and stepped off the commission, and the only remaining justices to replace him leaned Republican. With his exit the panel was, in effect, eight Republicans to seven Democrats, and it then awarded *every single* disputed electoral vote, in all three states, to Hayes, on straight 8–7 party-line votes, making Hayes president by exactly one electoral vote, the bare 185 to 184. The 8–7 was not a coincidence of fifteen honest judgments; it was the arithmetic the commission was, by accident and then by design, constructed to produce. A result that nakedly partisan, overturning the popular-vote winner, could not have been imposed on a furious Democratic House by procedure alone. What made the Democrats acquiesce instead of blocking it was an informal understanding negotiated in late-night meetings, the most famous a February 1877 dinner at Washington's Wormley Hotel, a leading hotel owned by James Wormley, a wealthy free Black Washingtonian, so that the deal abandoning Black Southerners was, with a bitterness contemporaries noted, brokered over a Black man's tablecloths. The arrangement is known to history as the **Compromise of 1877**. Be precise about what is settled and what is debated, because the distinction matters at the book's climax. What is *not* in serious dispute: the troops were withdrawn, the three Republican state governments fell at once, Hayes took office, and Southern Democrats got what they wanted. What *is* debated by historians is the older, tidy story that the Wormley dinner was a single decisive smoke-filled-room bargain; the newer view, which this book follows, is that the explicit deal-making mattered less than a convergence both sides had already reached, the North wanting out, the Democrats wanting the South, the Wormley meeting confirming a settlement more than creating it. Either way the substance is the same: the Democrats let Hayes take office, and in exchange the federal government withdrew the remaining troops protecting the Republican governments of Louisiana and South Carolina, plus vague promises of Southern patronage and internal improvements. The presidency for the abandonment of the last freedpeople still under federal protection. That was the trade, whether sealed in one dinner or arrived at over a winter.

It was paid immediately and exactly. In April 1877, President Hayes, mockingly called "His Fraudulency" for the manner of his election, ordered the army to stop protecting the Republican statehouses in Louisiana and South Carolina. They collapsed within days; Wade Hampton's Redeemers took South Carolina, the Democrats took Louisiana, and every Southern state was now under the control of the white Democratic Party. The constitutional amendments of the "Second Founding" remained, every word of them, in the Constitution. The Civil Rights Act of 1875, a last gasp pushed through Congress as a memorial to the dying **Charles Sumner**, the Massachusetts Radical senator of Chapter 1, beaten nearly to death on the Senate floor before the war for denouncing slavery and unbending on Black equality to the end, guaranteed equal access to inns, theaters, and transport; it too remained on the books, unenforced, until the Supreme Court struck it down in 1883 (Chapter 12). What ended was not the law. What ended was the willingness to enforce it. Reconstruction did not conclude with a repeal or a surrender ceremony. It concluded with a backroom understanding, after which the federal government simply put its weapons down and walked away from the people it had armed the Constitution to protect, and a coda fitting for the whole story: in 1875 the state of Tennessee had sent the disgraced Andrew Johnson (Chapter 2) back to the United States Senate, the only former president to date ever to serve there, the South's verdict on the 1860s rendered by returning the man who had tried hardest to stop them. The freedpeople were now alone with the Redeemers, the constitutional promises intact on paper and dead in fact. What that meant, and how the country then spent fifty years lying about it, is the last chapter.

# Chapter 16 — The Long Afterlife

A book whose thesis is that Reconstruction was *defeated*, not failed, owes you the aftermath, because the defeat had two parts and the second one is the part most Americans, until very recently, never learned. The first part was the destruction of Black political power in the South, accomplished by 1877 and completed over the following two decades. The second part, and in some ways the more remarkable historical crime, was the rewriting of the story itself, so thoroughly that for half a century the country taught its children that the people who overthrew multiracial democracy by murder had been the heroes. This chapter is both, and the long, slow, still-incomplete reckoning with them, and the bridge into the industrial America the next narratives in this chain pick up.

Take the political afterlife first, briefly, because a later narrative in this chain tells it in full. With federal protection withdrawn (Chapter 15), the Redeemer governments moved, over the next twenty-odd years, from controlling the South to formally writing Black citizens out of it. The Supreme Court had already shown how (Chapter 12): the Fifteenth Amendment barred denying the vote *by race* but not by poll tax, literacy test, or "understanding" clause. Beginning with Mississippi's new constitution in 1890, the old Mississippi Plan made permanent and legal, Southern states rewrote their constitutions to disfranchise Black men through exactly those race-neutral-on-paper devices, and Black voter registration in some states fell from majorities to near zero within a decade. In 1896, in **Plessy v. Ferguson**, the Court completed the arc Chapter 12 began and upheld "separate but equal," constitutionalizing the system of legally enforced segregation and subordination called **Jim Crow**. The name itself is telling: it came from a grotesque white blackface minstrel character of the 1830s, a mockery turned into the label for a regime. Jim Crow was the comprehensive legal and social order of Southern white supremacy that ran from the 1890s into the 1960s. The constitutional amendments of the "Second Founding" were not repealed, you cannot easily repeal an amendment, they were left intact and inert, sleeping in the document for sixty years until the civil rights movement woke them up, which is the subject of a later narrative in this chain. The single most important practical legacy of Reconstruction turned out to be that the words stayed on the page, available, when the country was finally willing to mean them again.

Now the historiographical crime, which this chain's writing rules require be confronted head-on the way the predecessor narrative confronts the Lost Cause, because they are the same project. As the political war was won by the Redeemers, a cultural war was won alongside it. Ex-Confederate veterans' groups and, above all, the **United Daughters of the Confederacy**, a women's heritage organization founded in 1894 that grew to tens of thousands of members and worked through schools, textbook-vetting committees, monument campaigns, and children's auxiliaries, promoted from the 1870s onward the **Lost Cause**: the doctrine that the Confederacy had fought nobly for "states' rights" rather than slavery, that slavery had been benign and the enslaved content, and that Reconstruction had been a vindictive Northern tyranny of carpetbag corruption and Black misrule from which the white South had been heroically redeemed. This was not folklore; it was an organized memory campaign, and the closest structural parallel in modern history is the German *Dolchstoßlegende*, the "stab-in-the-back" myth after the First World War, which likewise recast a military defeat as betrayal by domestic enemies, rehabilitated the defeated order, blamed scapegoated minorities, and was inscribed into monuments and schoolbooks, with catastrophic results. Defeated military establishments, barred from winning the war again, set out to win the memory of it, and the Lost Cause and the *Dolchstoßlegende* are near-twins in method. The Lost Cause was inscribed physically, in the wave of Confederate monuments erected mostly during the Jim Crow decades around 1900–1920 (not, mostly, right after the war), placed deliberately on courthouse lawns as statements about who controlled public space. And it was made academically respectable. Around 1900–1910, the **Dunning School**, the Columbia historian William Dunning and his students, produced scholarly state studies that put a university imprimatur on exactly the Redeemers' version: Reconstruction as a tragic era of Black incapacity and Northern vindictiveness, rightly ended. From there it became mass culture: D. W. Griffith's enormously influential 1915 film **The Birth of a Nation** dramatized the Dunning story with the Klan as heroic rescuers, and it was, by general agreement among historians, the clearest case in American history of a feature film directly helping to rebuild a terrorist organization, the modern Klan revival dating from the year of its release. (That second Klan could present itself as respectable and win mainstream political power in the 1920s precisely because fifty years of Lost Cause and Dunning revisionism had prepared the cultural ground; its national rise is the subject of the Roaring Twenties narrative, Ch 10.) It was screened at the White House for President **Woodrow Wilson**, a Southern-born admirer of the Dunning view whose own historical writing the film quoted on its title cards; the screening itself is well documented, while the much-repeated line that Wilson called it "history written with lightning" is of doubtful provenance and probably apocryphal, so the safe statement is that Wilson, the sitting president, screened the film and his administration did nothing to distance itself from it, not that he is reliably recorded endorsing it in those famous words. That film could incite political violence was not a uniquely American discovery, governments from Soviet Russia to Weimar Germany understood and exploited the same power of mass cinema in the same era, but *Birth of a Nation* is the starkest case of the art directly regrowing the organization. For roughly fifty years, this was simply what American textbooks said. Generations of Americans, of every region and race, were taught that the chapter you have just read happened the other way around.

The correction is itself part of the story and the book should end honest about how slow and recent it was. The first full-scale demolition came from the people the lie was about. In 1935 **W. E. B. Du Bois** published *Black Reconstruction in America*, a vast, documented argument that Reconstruction had been a heroic and sabotaged democratic experiment, that the freedpeople were its agents and not its problem, and that the Dunning version was propaganda. What the white academic mainstream did with it is itself part of the historiographical crime, and the book should not soften it: the profession did not refute Du Bois, it dismissed him, on the grounds that a Black scholar writing about Black freedom was by definition too angry, too political, too partisan to be doing real history, the exact move that had let the Dunning School pass its own propaganda off as neutral scholarship. *Black Reconstruction* was, for roughly thirty years, left off the syllabus and out of the citations. Only around the 1960s, pushed by the civil rights movement and by historians such as **Kenneth Stampp**, a University of California historian whose 1965 *The Era of Reconstruction* brought the revised view into the mainstream textbook, and **John Hope Franklin**, the preeminent Black American historian of the era and author of the standard survey *From Slavery to Freedom*, did mainstream textbooks finally abandon the Dunning interpretation, roughly a century after the events and thirty years after Du Bois had already made the case, and been ignored for making it. Eric Foner's synthesis in the 1980s established the modern consensus this book has followed throughout: Reconstruction as "America's unfinished revolution," and the three amendments as a **"Second Founding"** of the republic that the original 1787 compromise with slavery had left incomplete. Be plain about the scale of the delay, because it is the final proof of the book's thesis. It took the United States about ninety years, from 1877 to roughly the 1960s, for its official account of Reconstruction to come back into line with what the primary sources had said all along. That is how complete the defeat was: it was not enough to overthrow the democracy; the overthrow then captured the history of itself for three more generations. When something in this book contradicts an older story you absorbed somewhere, this is why.

End on the bridge, because this chain continues and Reconstruction hands the country forward in a specific shape. The same April 1877 that saw the last troops leave the South saw, weeks later, those same federal forces sent to break the **Great Railroad Strike of 1877**. It was the first *national* strike in the sense the word had not had before: not one shop or one line walking out, but rail workers stopping trains across many states at once, in a coordinated wave, so that for the first time the federal government faced a labor action it treated as a national emergency, and answered with the army, troops firing on strikers in several cities. The federal bayonet withdrawn from protecting freedpeople was, within weeks, leveled at workers, to protect capital against labor. The same trade-off, coalition stability and property placed ahead of Black rights, returned in the New Deal's accommodation of the Southern bloc and in the 1964 Atlantic City compromise (the Civil Rights Era narrative, Chs 1 and 11). The contrast with the wider world is the sharpest single illustration of what kind of choice this was: in the very same years Britain was *legalizing* peaceful picketing and building a lawful labor movement, while the United States, fresh from abandoning racial democracy, pivoted its coercive power straight onto industrial labor. That pivot is the whole next era in a sentence. With the Southern question buried, American national politics reorganized around industry, railroads, immigration, money, and the conflict between capital and labor, the era Mark Twain named the **Gilded Age**, and the country became, with astonishing speed, a global industrial power, even as the unfinished business of racial democracy was sealed in the basement for two generations.

And here is the last turn of the knife, the one the book has gestured at twice and should now name plainly. The Fourteenth Amendment's words did not merely sit dormant; they were taken up and used, vigorously, for sixty years, just not for the people they were written for. Between roughly 1877 and the late 1930s, the clause drafted to shield freedpeople from hostile states was wielded overwhelmingly by *corporations*, which the courts treated as "persons," to strike down railroad regulation, wage and hour laws, and other protections of actual workers. The single most successful litigant under the freedpeople's amendment, for two generations, was the business corporation. Only in the 1930s did a sustained campaign, the NAACP's long, deliberate litigation strategy, begin reclaiming the Fourteenth for its original purpose, the work a later narrative in this chain takes up as its central drama, from *Brown v. Board* in 1954 to the Voting Rights Act of 1965 (the Civil Rights Era narrative, Chs 2 and 12). So the constitutional tools forged in these twelve years sat in the document the whole time, available but turned to other uses, until the civil rights movement of the twentieth century reopened the question this book has been about, what does freedom mean for the people slavery freed. It did not have to invent new constitutional language. It reached back and picked up the language written here, in the 1860s, by a Congress and a generation of freedpeople who had answered the question correctly and been defeated for it. That is why this is called the unfinished revolution, and why the next narratives in this chain are, in a real sense, still the same story.

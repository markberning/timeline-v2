# The Hittite Empire — The Forgotten Superpower

**Goal:** Narrative for the first TL in the Anatolian Succession chain. Covers roughly 2500 to 700 BCE, from the pre-Hittite Hatti civilization through the rise and fall of the Hittite Empire to the Neo-Hittite successor states, plus the modern rediscovery story. The through-line is a single question: how does a civilization become one of the most powerful in the ancient world and then vanish so completely that for three thousand years, nobody knew it had existed? The Hittites were the first Indo-European empire, the only power to fight pharaonic Egypt to a standstill, the inventors of the world's first peace treaty, and rulers of a thousand-god pantheon that absorbed every deity it encountered. They adapted cuneiform to write the earliest known Indo-European language, pioneered iron smelting that foreshadowed the entire Iron Age, and built a diplomatic system so sophisticated that a copy of their peace treaty hangs in the United Nations headquarters today. Then the Bronze Age Collapse wiped them from the map so thoroughly that when 19th-century archaeologists found their capital, they didn't know whose it was. The Hittites prove that a civilization can be world-historic and still be completely forgotten.

**Status:** First draft, 8 chapters.

# Chapter 1 — The Land Before the Hittites

Before the Hittites arrived, somebody else was already there.

That sentence sounds obvious, but it matters enormously for understanding what the Hittite Empire became. The Hittites did not build their civilization from scratch on empty land. They walked into a world that already had cities, trade networks, metalwork traditions, and religious practices stretching back more than a thousand years. They absorbed that world so completely that they named their own empire after the people they had replaced. For the next five centuries, the Hittites called their kingdom **Hatti** (HAH-tee) and their land the "Land of Hatti," even though they were not Hattians. It would be as if the English had conquered France and then spent the next five hundred years calling their kingdom "France" and referring to themselves in official documents as rulers of the Franks.

Understanding who the Hittites replaced, and how thoroughly they absorbed the culture they found, is the starting point for everything that follows.

---

**Anatolia** (modern Turkey) is one of the great crossroads of the ancient world. The word itself comes from the Greek *anatole*, meaning "sunrise" or "the east," because for the Greeks looking across the Aegean Sea from their mainland, Anatolia was where the sun came up. The landmass is a vast peninsula roughly 300,000 square miles (780,000 square kilometers) in area, about three times the size of the United Kingdom, bounded by the Black Sea to the north, the Aegean Sea to the west, the Mediterranean to the south, and the highlands of Armenia and the upper reaches of the Tigris and Euphrates rivers to the east. Its eastern border shades into the northern edge of Mesopotamia (the land between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers in modern Iraq, where the world's first cities emerged around 3500 BCE; Mesopotamia Ch 1-3). Its western coast faces the Aegean islands and, across the water, Greece. Anatolia is literally the bridge between Europe and Asia, and every empire that has ever tried to control the Near East has had to deal with it.

The central Anatolian plateau, where the Hittite Empire would eventually be centered, is a high, arid steppe roughly 3,000 to 4,000 feet (900 to 1,200 meters) above sea level, ringed by mountain ranges on all sides. The climate is continental: brutally cold winters with heavy snow, hot dry summers, and a short growing season that makes agriculture harder than in the irrigated river valleys of Mesopotamia or the rain-fed coastal zones of the Mediterranean. Annual rainfall on the central plateau runs about 12 to 16 inches (300 to 400 mm), enough for barley and wheat in a good year but marginal in a bad one. The landscape is vast, treeless, and wind-scoured, with deep river valleys cutting through the rock. It is not a generous land. The people who thrived there were tough.

But Anatolia's real wealth was underground. The mountains ringing the plateau contained some of the richest mineral deposits in the ancient Near East: copper, silver, gold, lead, and (critically for later chapters) iron. Copper from the Taurus Mountains in the south and silver from deposits near the coast made Anatolia a magnet for trade from the earliest periods. When the Bronze Age economy ran on copper and tin, Anatolia had the copper. When the Iron Age economy replaced bronze with iron, Anatolia had that too. Control of Anatolian metals was a strategic prize that drew merchants, invaders, and empire-builders for millennia.

A quick note on "firsts" before we go further. Ancient history is full of claims about who did what first, and virtually all of them should be read as "first surviving evidence of" rather than "first time this ever happened." What we find is shaped by what happens to survive: a city destroyed by fire may have its clay tablets baked into permanent ceramic, preserving them for thousands of years, while a city peacefully abandoned may have its records crumble to dust. The Hittites left spectacular records because their capital was eventually burned and buried, preserving the archive. Other civilizations may have done comparable things earlier and left no trace. Keep that caveat in mind whenever this narrative says "first" or "earliest."

---

The people living in central Anatolia before the Hittites arrived are called the **Hattians** (HAT-ee-uns), and they are one of the most tantalizing mysteries in ancient Near Eastern history. We know they existed because the Hittites themselves told us. Hittite cuneiform texts refer to the **Hattic language** as a separate, sacred tongue used in religious rituals, distinct from the Hittite language that the rulers spoke in daily life. Modern linguists have identified Hattic as a language isolate, meaning it belongs to no known language family. It is not related to the Semitic languages of Mesopotamia (Akkadian, Babylonian, Assyrian), not related to the Indo-European languages that the Hittites spoke, not related to the Hurrian or Sumerian languages, not related to anything else we have ever found. The Hattians spoke a language that was, as far as we can tell, uniquely their own.

What we know about the Hattians comes mostly from archaeology and from the ritual texts that the Hittites preserved in the Hattic language. The Hattians occupied central Anatolia from at least the third millennium BCE, probably much earlier, and they built a sophisticated culture centered on fortified towns, metalworking, and a religious tradition that the Hittites would later absorb wholesale. The most spectacular surviving evidence of Hattian civilization comes from a site called **Alaca Höyük** (ah-LAH-jah huh-YOOK), about 125 miles (200 km) northeast of Ankara, where a series of royal tombs dating to roughly 2400-2200 BCE yielded some of the most stunning metalwork in the ancient world.

The Alaca Höyük tombs contained gold jewelry, bronze weapons, and most strikingly, a collection of bronze and gold "sun disc standards" (elaborate metal discs mounted on long poles, possibly carried in processions or placed atop temples) that have no parallel anywhere else in the ancient Near East. These are not crude objects. They show sophisticated casting, inlay work, and artistic design that required generations of accumulated skill. Alongside the standards, the tombs held bronze figurines of bulls and stags (both animals that would remain central to Anatolian religion for the next two thousand years), gold diadems, and the remains of individuals who had clearly been wealthy and powerful.

The Alaca Höyük tombs tell us that by 2400 BCE, at least, central Anatolia had a stratified society with a wealthy elite, skilled metalworkers, and religious practices centered on animal symbolism and solar imagery. This was not a primitive backwater waiting to be civilized by incoming Indo-Europeans. It was a developed culture with its own traditions, its own art, its own gods, and its own political structures. When the Hittites arrived, they did not impose civilization on barbarians. They moved into someone else's house and redecorated.

---

The other crucial piece of Anatolia's pre-Hittite world was commercial, not cultural, and it came from the east.

Starting around 1950 BCE, merchants from the city of **Assur** (ah-SHOOR) on the Tigris River in northern Mesopotamia established a network of trading colonies across Anatolia. These were not military outposts or diplomatic missions. They were commercial districts, called **karums** (kah-ROOMS; literally "quay" or "harbor" in Akkadian, even though most of them were nowhere near the sea), attached to existing Anatolian cities under negotiated agreements with local rulers. The largest and most important karum was at a city called **Kanesh** (kah-NESH; modern Kültepe, in central Turkey, about 150 miles or 240 km southeast of where Hattusa would later stand).

The Assyrian merchants at Kanesh left behind over 23,000 cuneiform tablets documenting their business (Assyrian Empire Ch 2). These tablets record tin shipments from Iran, textile exports from Assur, loan contracts, insurance agreements, tax disputes, and personal letters between merchants abroad and their wives managing the family business back home. They reveal that Anatolia in the early second millennium BCE was thoroughly integrated into the broader Near Eastern commercial world. Tin flowed from eastern Iran through Assur to Anatolia. Copper from Anatolian mines flowed back east. Silver and gold moved in both directions. The Anatolian kingdoms were not isolated hilltop communities. They were nodes in an international trade network stretching from Afghanistan to the Aegean.

For the Hittite story, two things about the Kanesh karum matter enormously.

First, the Assyrian merchants brought **cuneiform** (the wedge-shaped writing system invented by the Sumerians around 3400 BCE and later adapted across the entire Near East; Mesopotamia Ch 3). Before the karums, Anatolia had no writing system. After the karums, cuneiform was available as a technology that anyone who learned it could adapt to their own language. The Hittites would do exactly that, adapting Mesopotamian cuneiform to write their Indo-European language and producing the earliest known written records in any Indo-European tongue (Chapter 2).

Second, the Kanesh tablets contain the earliest known references to people with recognizably Hittite (that is, Indo-European) names living in Anatolia alongside the indigenous Hattian population. Names like Pithana and Anitta appear in the tablets as rulers of Anatolian city-states who were doing business with the Assyrian merchants. These names are linguistically Indo-European, not Hattic, which means that by 1900 BCE at the latest, the people who would become the Hittites were already in Anatolia, already ruling some cities, and already participating in the commercial economy. The question of exactly when they arrived and from where is one of the great debates in ancient Near Eastern studies.

---

This brings us to the question that made the Hittites famous among linguists: how do we know they were Indo-European?

**Indo-European** is a language family (a group of languages descended from a common ancestor) that includes most of the languages spoken in Europe and western Asia today: English, French, German, Spanish, Russian, Greek, Latin, Persian, Hindi, and dozens of others. It is the largest language family in the world by number of speakers. The ancestral language from which all these languages descend is called **Proto-Indo-European**, and it was probably spoken by pastoral nomads somewhere on the Pontic-Caspian steppe (the vast grasslands north of the Black Sea and Caspian Sea, in modern Ukraine and southern Russia) sometime between 4500 and 3000 BCE. Nobody wrote Proto-Indo-European down. We reconstruct it by comparing its descendant languages, the same way biologists reconstruct ancestral species from their modern descendants.

The Hittites matter for this story because their language, when it was finally deciphered in 1915 (Chapter 8), turned out to be the oldest written Indo-European language ever found. The decipherment was done by a Czech linguist named Bedřich Hrozný (pronounced "BED-rzhikh HROZ-nee"), who was reading cuneiform tablets from Hattusa and noticed that certain words looked suspiciously like words in known Indo-European languages. The breakthrough sentence was deceptively simple: a line about bread and water that contained words recognizable from Latin and English cognates. Hrozný realized he was looking at an Indo-European language written in Mesopotamian cuneiform script, and that realization fundamentally changed our understanding of the ancient Near East.

Before Hrozný's decipherment, scholars had assumed that the Bronze Age Near East was dominated by Semitic-speaking peoples (Babylonians, Assyrians, Canaanites) and a few language isolates (Sumerians, Hattians). The discovery that one of the region's great empires spoke an Indo-European language forced a complete rethinking of migration patterns, cultural contacts, and linguistic history. The Hittite language is now classified as part of the **Anatolian branch** of Indo-European, and it is as different from Akkadian (the Semitic language of Mesopotamia) as English is from Arabic or Chinese.

But here is the crucial point: being linguistically Indo-European did not make the Hittites culturally European. The Hittites wrote in Mesopotamian cuneiform. They worshipped a Hattian storm god. They built their capital on a Hattian sacred site. They governed through a system of vassal treaties borrowed from Mesopotamian diplomatic practice. They dressed in Anatolian clothing, ate Anatolian food, and practiced Anatolian agricultural techniques. Linguistically, they were distant cousins of the Greeks and the Romans. Culturally, they were Anatolians who had absorbed the Hattian world they had entered. Language family tells you about origins. It does not tell you about identity.

How the Indo-European speakers came to Anatolia is debated. Some scholars favor a gradual infiltration over centuries, with small groups of pastoralists filtering south through the Caucasus Mountains (the range between the Black Sea and the Caspian Sea) or west across the Bosphorus strait (the narrow waterway connecting the Black Sea to the Mediterranean, cutting through modern Istanbul). Others argue for a more organized migration. The archaeological evidence is ambiguous: there is no single "arrival" layer in Anatolian excavations, no sudden break in material culture that screams "new people showed up." What the evidence shows is a gradual linguistic shift, where Indo-European names start appearing alongside Hattic names in the commercial records, and over time, the Indo-European speakers moved from being one population among many to being the ruling class. They may have looked, dressed, and lived almost identically to their Hattian neighbors while speaking a fundamentally different language, in the same way that Norman French-speakers and Anglo-Saxon English-speakers coexisted in medieval England for generations before their languages blended.

By roughly 1750 BCE, the stage was set. Central Anatolia had a Hattian cultural substrate with sophisticated metalworking, religious traditions, and urban centers. It had an international trade network linking it to Mesopotamia and beyond. And it had a ruling class that spoke an Indo-European language, used cuneiform to write it, and was about to build something new on the foundations of everything that had come before. The next chapter tells the story of the man who cursed the site of Hattusa, and the king who rebuilt it anyway.

# Chapter 2 — The First Kings

Around 1750 BCE, a king named **Anitta** (ah-NEE-tah) of the city of **Kussara** (koo-SAH-rah) did something dramatic. He conquered the city of Hattusa, burned it to the ground, and placed a curse on anyone who dared to rebuild it. His inscription, recovered from the ruins of Kanesh, is the earliest known text written in the Hittite language, and it reads with the blunt ferocity of a man who wanted to make sure his enemies stayed dead: "At night I took the city by force. I sowed cress in its place. Whoever becomes king after me and resettles Hattusa, let the Storm God of Heaven strike him down."

Cress. He planted a salad green on the ashes, the ancient equivalent of salting the earth, a ritual statement that this place was finished, cursed, done. The Storm God himself would punish anyone foolish enough to rebuild.

About a hundred years later, a king named **Hattusili I** (hat-too-SEE-lee; his name literally means "man of Hattusa") rebuilt Hattusa, moved his capital there, and founded the Hittite Old Kingdom. He either did not know about Anitta's curse, did not care, or figured the Storm God had better things to do. The Storm God, for his part, does not appear to have followed through on the threat. Hattusa would remain the Hittite capital for the next four hundred years, through the rise, peak, and catastrophic fall of the empire.

The gap between Anitta's destruction of Hattusa and Hattusili I's refounding of it is one of the murkier periods in Hittite history. Anitta himself is a fascinating figure: an Indo-European-speaking king who conquered most of central Anatolia from his base at Kussara, a city whose location has never been definitively identified. His inscription at Kanesh describes military campaigns, the conquest of rival cities, and the destruction of Hattusa with enough specificity to be taken as broadly historical, though the details are impossible to verify independently. He may have been the first ruler to unite a significant portion of central Anatolia under a single authority, but his kingdom did not survive him. After Anitta, the record goes dark for a century.

---

Hattusili I (ruled approximately 1650-1620 BCE) is the first Hittite king we can see clearly. He chose Hattusa as his capital for reasons that were probably more strategic than sentimental. The site sits on a rugged, defensible ridge in a narrow river valley, surrounded by steep rocky outcrops that form natural walls on three sides. At an elevation of roughly 3,600 feet (1,100 meters), it commands a broad view of the surrounding countryside. The river provided water. The rock provided fortification. And the location, in the heartland of the old Hattian cultural zone, gave Hattusili I a claim to legitimacy over the indigenous population. By naming himself after the city and the city after the Hattian land, he was signaling that his kingdom was not a foreign conquest but a continuation of what had been there before. It was a shrewd political maneuver, and it worked.

The city Hattusili I built (and his successors expanded enormously) eventually grew into one of the largest fortified cities in the ancient Near East. At its peak, Hattusa covered roughly 400 acres (160 hectares), roughly the size of 300 football fields, and was surrounded by a massive double wall nearly 4 miles (6 km) in circumference with over a hundred towers. The walls enclosed a lower city (with the Great Temple and residential quarters), an upper city (with dozens of temples built during the empire's later centuries), and a fortified royal citadel called **Büyükkale** (boo-YOOK-kah-leh; "Great Fortress" in Turkish) perched on the highest point of the ridge. The famous **Lions Gate** (flanked by massive stone lion sculptures that still stand today) and the **Sphinx Gate** guarded entrances to the upper city. A tunnel roughly 230 feet (70 meters) long, the **Yerkapı** (YER-kah-puh) postern, ran underneath the southern wall, allowing defenders to launch surprise sorties against besiegers. Population estimates for the city at its height range from 40,000 to 50,000 people, making it comparable to the great cities of Mesopotamia and Egypt.

But all of that lay in the future. In Hattusili I's time, Hattusa was a new foundation built on cursed ground, and the king was more interested in expansion than in architecture.

---

Hattusili I was a conqueror. He campaigned south into Syria, capturing the wealthy city of **Aleppo** (already ancient and important, a major trade center connecting Mesopotamia to the Mediterranean coast) and pushing Hittite power far beyond the Anatolian plateau for the first time. These campaigns brought the Hittites into direct contact, and conflict, with the kingdoms of northern Syria and Upper Mesopotamia, including the **Hurrians** (HOOR-ee-uns), a people who spoke yet another unrelated language (Hurrian, which belongs to no known language family and is as different from both Hittite and Akkadian as Korean is from Arabic). The Hurrians would become one of the Hittites' most persistent rivals, and eventually one of their most important cultural influences (Chapter 4).

Hattusili I also left behind something unusual for a Bronze Age king: an autobiography of sorts. His "Testament" or "Political Testament" describes his reign, his military campaigns, and most revealingly, his family troubles. The document records how Hattusili originally designated his nephew as heir, then disinherited him when the nephew turned against him, and finally chose his grandson **Mursili** (moor-SEE-lee) as his successor. The text drips with frustration and betrayal. Hattusili addresses the assembled nobles directly, warning them to support his chosen heir, recounting the treachery of his relatives, and lamenting that his own family has been his worst enemy. It is one of the earliest surviving documents in which a ruler speaks frankly about the messy reality of court politics, and it established a pattern that would define the Hittite Old Kingdom: brilliant military expansion abroad, vicious political infighting at home.

The institution that made Hittite court politics uniquely volatile was the **panku** (PAHN-koo), an assembly of nobles that played a role in royal governance that scholars still debate. Some historians describe it as a proto-parliament, a check on royal authority that had to approve the king's decisions. Others argue it was more of a rubber stamp that the king convened when he wanted to legitimize decisions he had already made. What is clear is that the Hittite king was not an absolute monarch in the Mesopotamian or Egyptian sense. He ruled with the cooperation (or at least the acquiescence) of a warrior aristocracy that could, and regularly did, depose kings who lost their confidence. The result was a political system that was more dynamic and more unstable than anything in Egypt, where the pharaoh's divine status placed him above challenge, or Babylon, where palace coups happened but were not structurally encouraged.

---

The reign of Hattusili I's grandson and successor, **Mursili I** (ruled approximately 1610-1590 BCE), produced one of the most audacious military operations in ancient history.

In approximately 1595 BCE (this date follows the "Middle Chronology" used by most scholars; other chronological systems place it as late as 1531 BCE), Mursili I marched his army out of Hattusa, south through Syria, and east down the Euphrates River on a campaign that covered roughly 600 miles (1,000 km) of hostile territory. His target was the most prestigious city in the ancient world: **Babylon** (Mesopotamia Ch 7). And he took it.

The sack of Babylon by Mursili I ended the First Babylonian Dynasty, the dynasty that had produced **Hammurabi** (hah-moo-RAH-bee; the king famous for his law code, who had ruled Babylon roughly 1792-1750 BCE; Mesopotamia Ch 7). By 1595 BCE, Hammurabi's dynasty was already in decline, but Babylon remained the cultural and economic center of Mesopotamia. No one expected a highland Anatolian kingdom to reach that far. The raid was the ancient equivalent of a land power in Ukraine suddenly sacking Paris: geographically implausible, strategically insane, and yet it happened.

How exactly Mursili pulled it off is debated. He may have allied with the **Kassites** (KAS-ites; a people from the Zagros Mountains who would go on to rule Babylon for the next four centuries after the Hittites left; Mesopotamia Ch 8). The logistics of the campaign, moving a Bronze Age army 600 miles through hostile territory and then 600 miles back, suggest either careful advance planning with supply depots and local allies, or a much smaller raiding force than the texts imply. The Hittites looted Babylon's treasures and carried off the statue of **Marduk** (MAR-dook; the patron god of Babylon, whose statue was understood to literally embody the god's presence on earth; Mesopotamia Ch 9). In Mesopotamian theology, stealing a city's god was the ultimate humiliation, a theological knockout that declared the conqueror's gods more powerful than the conquered city's.

But Mursili I could not hold what he had taken. The Hittite army retreated back to Anatolia with its loot, leaving a power vacuum in Babylon that the Kassites filled. The sack of Babylon was a spectacular raid, not a permanent conquest, and it had the unintended consequence of destabilizing Mesopotamia for generations. Meanwhile, in 1274 BCE, the Hittites would reach another famous military milestone, this time against Egypt (Chapter 5). But Mursili I never lived to see it.

He was assassinated.

---

And this is where the pattern that Hattusili I's Testament had warned about repeated itself, violently. Mursili I was murdered by his brother-in-law **Hantili** (han-TEE-lee), who seized the throne. Hantili was in turn murdered by his own son-in-law **Zidanta** (zee-DAN-tah). Zidanta was murdered by his son **Ammuna** (am-MOO-nah). The Hittite Old Kingdom descended into a spiral of palace assassinations that reads like a particularly grim season of a prestige television drama, except that every character is related to every other character by marriage and none of them live long enough to develop a personality in the surviving records.

The violence was not random. It was structural. The Hittite monarchy had no clear rule of succession. The king could choose any male relative as his heir, the panku assembly had to approve the choice (or at least not actively oppose it), and ambitious relatives who were passed over had powerful incentives to act before the choice was finalized. The combination of an elective element (the panku) and a hereditary element (the royal family) created a system where every succession was a potential civil war. Compare this to Egypt, where the principle of divine kingship passing from father to son was so deeply embedded that even female pharaohs like Hatshepsut had to justify their rule through elaborate theological arguments, or to Mesopotamia, where succession was less stable than Egypt but more stable than Hatti because strong bureaucratic institutions could survive a change of ruler. The Hittites had the worst of both worlds: a royal family that expected to keep power, and a nobility that expected to have a say in who held it.

The man who finally broke the cycle was **Telipinu** (teh-LIH-pee-noo; ruled approximately 1525-1500 BCE), and the tool he used was a document. Telipinu himself had come to power by overthrowing the assassins of his predecessor, which made him, paradoxically, a usurper who was trying to end usurpation. He issued the **Edict of Telipinu**, one of the most remarkable political documents from the ancient world: a formal decree establishing rules for royal succession, mandating that the throne pass to the king's firstborn son (or, in the absence of sons, to other specified relatives in a fixed order), and forbidding the nobles from killing members of the royal family.

The Edict opens with a historical prologue that is, effectively, the Hittite Old Kingdom's own history of itself. Telipinu reviews the reigns of his predecessors, notes which ones were murdered and by whom, traces the disastrous consequences of each assassination for the kingdom's military and economic health, and then presents his succession rules as the logical solution. The document is not a constitution in the modern sense (it has no enforcement mechanism beyond the gods' displeasure), but it is one of the earliest surviving attempts by a ruler to solve a political problem through written law rather than raw force. The Code of Hammurabi (Mesopotamia Ch 7) predated it by about two centuries, but Hammurabi's code was a collection of judicial precedents. Telipinu's Edict was something different: a structural reform of the political system, aimed at changing how power was transferred rather than how crimes were punished.

Did it work? Partially. The assassinations slowed down after Telipinu, though they did not stop entirely. More importantly, the Edict established a principle that later Hittite kings could invoke: that there were rules governing succession, that those rules had been written down, and that violating them was not just politically dangerous but religiously impious, an offense against the gods who had witnessed the document. When Hattusili III usurped the throne three hundred years later (Chapter 5), he went to extraordinary lengths to justify his actions in writing, precisely because the Edict of Telipinu had established the expectation that succession should follow rules. Even the kings who broke the rules felt compelled to explain why.

---

The period after Telipinu, roughly 1500 to 1400 BCE, is the least well-documented phase of Hittite history. Scholars call it the "Middle Hittite Period" or the "Dark Age" of the kingdom, and it was characterized by external pressure from multiple directions. The **Kaska** (KAS-kah) peoples, tribal groups from the forested mountains north of Hattusa, raided Hittite territory relentlessly. The Kaska were not a unified kingdom but a loose collection of tribes who refused to submit to Hittite authority and periodically overran northern Hittite cities, possibly sacking Hattusa itself at least once. They were the Hittites' permanent headache, a threat that no king ever definitively solved and that persisted for the entire life of the empire.

From the southeast, the **Hurrian** kingdoms (particularly the powerful kingdom of **Mitanni**, mih-TAH-nee, centered in northern Syria and Upper Mesopotamia) pressed against Hittite territory. The Mitanni had mastered chariot warfare and built a formidable military state that rivaled both the Hittites and Egypt. For much of the Middle Hittite Period, the Hittites were on the defensive, losing territory in Syria that Hattusili I and Mursili I had conquered a century earlier.

Meanwhile, on the world stage, this was the age of Egypt's New Kingdom. Pharaohs like **Thutmose III** (ruled approximately 1479-1425 BCE) were campaigning deep into Syria, building an Egyptian empire that overlapped with Hittite ambitions in the same contested territory. China's **Shang dynasty** (Ancient China Ch 3-4) was at its height, working bronze and developing the earliest Chinese writing on oracle bones. In the Aegean, the Minoan civilization on Crete was in its golden age (Minoan Civilization Ch 4-5). The international world of the Late Bronze Age was taking shape: an interconnected system of great powers that communicated through cuneiform diplomacy, traded across vast distances, and fought over the rich cities of the Levant and Syria. The Hittites, during their Middle Period, were struggling to remain part of this system.

It took a remarkable king to pull them back into it. His name was Suppiluliuma, and his story is the subject of the next chapter.

# Chapter 3 — The Empire Rises

In the early 1300s BCE, the Hittite Empire was a second-tier power. A generation later, it was one of the two or three most powerful states on Earth. The man responsible for this transformation was **Suppiluliuma I** (soo-pee-loo-lee-OO-mah; ruled approximately 1355-1322 BCE), and he is, by any measure, the greatest king in Hittite history. He rebuilt the army, reconquered lost territory, destroyed the Hittites' most dangerous rival, extended the empire from the Aegean coast to the Euphrates River, and established a diplomatic network that treated the Hittite king as the equal of the pharaoh of Egypt. He also fathered a son who was murdered by the Egyptians, unleashed a plague that killed himself and devastated his kingdom for twenty years, and left behind a political legacy that his successors spent a century managing. Suppiluliuma's reign is the hinge of Hittite history: everything before it was prologue, and everything after it was consequence.

The empire Suppiluliuma inherited was already recovering from its Middle Period slump. His father, **Tudhaliya I** (too-DAHL-ee-yah; ruled approximately 1430-1400 BCE), had begun the process of reasserting Hittite power through campaigns against the **Arzawa** (ar-ZAH-wah) confederation in western Anatolia and the persistent Kaska raiders in the north. Tudhaliya I also strengthened Hittite ties with the kingdom of **Kizzuwatna** (kiz-zoo-WAHT-nah) in southeastern Anatolia, a Hurrian-influenced state that served as a cultural bridge between the Hittite and Hurrian worlds. Through Kizzuwatna, Hurrian religious practices, myths, and rituals flowed into Hittite culture, a process that would eventually produce the remarkable syncretic religious system described in Chapter 4.

But Suppiluliuma I turned recovery into dominance. His primary target was the **Mitanni** kingdom, the Hurrian-speaking superpower that had been the Hittites' chief rival for control of northern Syria and Upper Mesopotamia. Mitanni was powerful enough to correspond with Egypt as an equal. It had married princesses into the Egyptian royal family. It controlled the rich caravan cities of Syria and the trade routes linking Mesopotamia to the Mediterranean coast. For the Hittites to become a true great power, Mitanni had to fall.

---

Around 1340 BCE, Suppiluliuma launched a devastating campaign against Mitanni that fundamentally reshaped the political map of the Near East. The campaign unfolded in stages. First, Suppiluliuma rebuilt Hattusa's defenses and secured the Hittite heartland against the ever-present Kaska threat. Then he moved south and east, systematically peeling away Mitanni's Syrian vassals. The kings of **Aleppo**, **Ugarit** (oo-gah-REET; a wealthy port city on the Syrian coast), and other Syrian cities switched their allegiance from Mitanni to the Hittites, calculating that Suppiluliuma's rising power was more dangerous to resist than to accommodate. This was the Hittite diplomatic method in action: offer vassal treaties with clear mutual obligations, and make the alternative (military destruction) vivid enough to encourage acceptance.

The final blow came when Suppiluliuma crossed the Euphrates and apparently overran **Washukanni** (wah-shoo-KAH-nee), the Mitanni capital (the Hittite sources are fragmentary on this point, and whether Suppiluliuma actually sacked the city or merely reduced Mitanni's Syrian vassals is debated; the capital's location has never been definitively identified). Mitanni did not disappear overnight. A rump state survived as a buffer between the Hittites and the rising Assyrian power to the east. But as an independent great power, Mitanni was finished. Suppiluliuma installed vassal kings across Syria, placed his own sons on the thrones of Aleppo and **Carchemish** (KAR-keh-mish; a strategically vital city on the Euphrates, which would later become the most important Neo-Hittite successor state; Chapter 8), and established Hittite dominion over a territory stretching from the Aegean coast to the banks of the Euphrates.

The conquest of Mitanni brought the Hittites into the elite club of Bronze Age "Great Powers" alongside Egypt, Babylon, and (still just barely) Assyria. The proof of this status comes from the **Amarna Letters** (ah-MAR-nah; a collection of about 380 cuneiform tablets found at Tell el-Amarna in Egypt, the site of the pharaoh Akhenaten's short-lived capital). The Amarna Letters are the diplomatic correspondence of the Late Bronze Age international system, covering roughly 1360-1330 BCE, and they reveal a world of Great Kings who addressed each other as "Brother" and exchanged elaborate gifts, marriage proposals, and passive-aggressive complaints about the quality of the gold being sent.

The Hittite letters in the Amarna archive show Suppiluliuma corresponding with the pharaoh as an equal, demanding to be addressed with the same titles of respect, and complaining when he was not. One letter from a Hittite king (probably Suppiluliuma) reproaches the pharaoh for not sending a proper gift to congratulate him on his accession to the throne. The tone is that of offended dignity between peers, not supplication to a superior. The Amarna Letters are one of the best windows into Bronze Age international relations, and they show the Hittites firmly established as a superpower that Egypt had to take seriously.

---

And then came the incident that might have changed everything.

Around 1323 BCE, the young pharaoh **Tutankhamun** (too-tahn-KAH-moon) died in Egypt without an heir. What happened next is one of the most extraordinary episodes in ancient history, and scholars are still debating the details. A letter arrived at the Hittite court from an Egyptian queen, identified in the Hittite text as **Dahamunzu** (dah-hah-MOON-zoo; this is not an Egyptian name but the Hittite rendering of the Egyptian title *ta hemet nesu*, meaning "the king's wife"). The most common identification is **Ankhesenamun** (ahn-keh-SEH-nah-moon), Tutankhamun's widow and daughter of the famous pharaoh Akhenaten.

The letter, as preserved in a Hittite-language summary composed by Mursili II decades later, said (in scholarly paraphrase): "My husband has died. A son I have not. But to you, they say, your sons are many. If you would give me one of your sons, he would become my husband. Never shall I pick out a servant of mine and make him my husband. I am afraid."

This was staggering. An Egyptian queen was asking a foreign king to send a prince to become pharaoh of Egypt. In three thousand years of Egyptian history, nothing remotely like this had happened before. Egypt did not import kings. Egypt conquered foreigners; it did not marry them into the royal family at the highest level. The letter implies a court in crisis: a widowed queen, a power struggle among factions at court, and a woman desperate enough to seek help from Egypt's most dangerous rival rather than accept a marriage to a courtier she considered beneath her.

Suppiluliuma was suspicious. "Such a thing has never happened to me in my whole life!" he reportedly said, according to the Hittite record of the affair (written by his son Mursili II as part of a chronicle called the "Deeds of Suppiluliuma"). He sent an envoy to Egypt to investigate. The queen wrote again, this time with evident frustration: "Why did you say 'they deceive me' in that way? Had I a son, would I have written about my own and my country's shame to a foreign land? You did not believe me and have even spoken thus to me. He who was my husband has died. A son I have not. Never shall I take a servant of mine and make him my husband. I have written to no other country, only to you have I written. They say your sons are many: so give me one son of yours. To me he will be husband, but in Egypt he will be king."

Suppiluliuma relented. He sent his son **Zannanza** (zan-NAN-zah) to Egypt.

Zannanza was murdered on the way.

Who killed him is unknown. The prime suspect in most scholarly analyses is **Ay** (EYE), the elderly courtier who did in fact become the next pharaoh after Tutankhamun and who had the most to lose from a Hittite prince taking the Egyptian throne. But the evidence is circumstantial. The Hittite texts blame Egypt collectively. Suppiluliuma was furious. He launched retaliatory raids into Egyptian-controlled territory in Syria, and his armies brought back Egyptian prisoners of war.

Those prisoners brought something else: a plague.

---

The **Great Plague of Hatti** devastated the Hittite Empire for roughly twenty years, from around 1322 to about 1295 BCE. It killed Suppiluliuma I himself and swept through the royal family and the broader population with horrifying efficiency. The nature of the disease is unknown (suggestions range from tularemia to bubonic plague to an unknown pathogen), but its effects were catastrophic. The Hittite texts explicitly blame the plague on the Egyptian prisoners of war, which, if accurate, makes this one of the earliest documented cases of disease transmission through warfare.

Suppiluliuma's son and successor, **Mursili II** (moor-SEE-lee; ruled approximately 1321-1295 BCE), inherited a kingdom in crisis. He was young when he came to the throne (the texts describe him as still a boy, and vassal kings who thought they could exploit his inexperience immediately revolted), but he proved to be a formidable ruler in his own right. Mursili II spent his reign campaigning relentlessly to hold the empire together, fighting the Kaska in the north, rebels in western Anatolia, and Egyptian-backed insurgents in Syria. He reconquered the Arzawa lands in the west and forced the western Anatolian kingdoms back into the Hittite vassal system.

But it is Mursili II's religious texts, not his military campaigns, that make him one of the most memorable figures in ancient Near Eastern history. His **Plague Prayers** are among the most emotionally raw religious documents from the ancient world. Addressed to the Storm God of Hatti and the other gods of the Hittite pantheon, the prayers are a sustained, anguished attempt to understand why the gods have sent the plague and what must be done to end it.

"Storm God of Hatti, my lord, and you gods, my lords! It is only too true that man is sinful. My father sinned and transgressed against the word of the Storm God of Hatti, my lord. But I have not sinned in any respect. It is only too true, however, that the father's sin falls upon the son. So my father's sin has fallen upon me."

Mursili confesses sins he did not personally commit. He pleads for divine mercy. He bargains: if the gods will end the plague, he will perform specific rituals, pay specific debts, honor specific obligations. He questions divine justice openly, asking why the gods punish the innocent along with the guilty, while simultaneously maintaining that the gods must be right because they are gods. The prayers prefigure the Book of Job in the Hebrew Bible by several centuries, and they parallel the Mesopotamian poem *Ludlul bel nemeqi* ("I Will Praise the Lord of Wisdom," Mesopotamia Ch 9), which asks the same agonizing question: why do the righteous suffer? Both texts reveal a theological sophistication that goes far beyond the simple "I conquered, therefore my god is powerful" logic of most Bronze Age royal inscriptions.

The plague eventually burned itself out, as plagues do. Mursili II held the empire together through sheer tenacity. But the damage was real: two decades of depopulation, military weakness, and diplomatic disruption that the empire spent another generation recovering from. The greatest Hittite king had built a superpower. The greatest Hittite tragedy had nearly destroyed it. The next chapter steps back from the political narrative to examine the extraordinary religious and cultural world that the empire had built, one in which a thousand gods from a dozen different peoples shared space in a single, endlessly expanding pantheon.

---

# Chapter 4 — The Thousand Gods

The Hittite Empire had more gods than any other civilization in the Bronze Age. This is not an exaggeration. Hittite texts refer to the **"Thousand Gods of Hatti"** as a collective entity, invoked in treaties, oaths, and curses as the combined divine witness to human agreements. The phrase was partly formulaic (you did not have to name all thousand individually every time), but it reflected a real theological reality: the Hittite pantheon was enormous, constantly growing, and genuinely inclusive of foreign deities in a way that had no parallel in the ancient world.

Other Bronze Age civilizations absorbed foreign gods occasionally. The Egyptians identified foreign deities with their own (the Canaanite storm god Baal was equated with the Egyptian Set, for example). The Mesopotamians had a large pantheon, but it was organized hierarchically, with clear rankings and a fixed set of major deities who stayed at the top (Mesopotamia Ch 2-3). The Hittites did something different. They collected gods the way some modern institutions collect honorary degrees: every conquered or allied people's deities were added to the official roster, given temples or cult places, and included in the great festivals. Hittite rulers did not demand that subject peoples abandon their gods. They demanded that those gods be recognized as part of the Hittite system, attending the great divine assembly alongside everyone else.

The result was a religious system of staggering complexity. The archives at Hattusa contain texts in at least eight languages (Hittite, Hattic, Hurrian, Akkadian, Sumerian, Luwian, Palaic, and one or two others), many of them ritual texts preserving prayers, hymns, and ceremonies in the original language of the god being addressed. The Hattic language, spoken by the pre-Hittite population of central Anatolia (Chapter 1), survived almost exclusively as a liturgical language in Hittite religious texts, in the same way that Latin survived in the Catholic Church long after nobody spoke it as a daily language. Hittite priests performed Hattic rituals for Hattic gods, Hurrian rituals for Hurrian gods, and Mesopotamian rituals for Mesopotamian gods, all within the same state religious system. It was bureaucratic polytheism taken to its logical extreme.

---

At the center of this sprawling pantheon stood two deities who were, at least officially, the most important.

The **Storm God of Hatti** was the supreme male deity of the Hittite state. His name was written in different ways depending on which language you were writing in: in Luwian texts (Luwian was a closely related Anatolian language widely spoken across the empire), he is called **Tarhunna** (tar-HOON-nah); the native Hittite form of his name is less certain and may have been similar; in Hurrian texts, he is identified with **Teshub** (teh-SHOOB); in cuneiform texts using Sumerian logograms, he appears as the ideogram for "storm god." He was the god of weather, thunder, lightning, and rain, the divine force that controlled the agricultural cycle and the military fortunes of the kingdom. In a land where rainfall was marginal and droughts could be catastrophic (Chapter 1), the storm god's favor was literally a matter of survival. He was also the divine patron of kingship: the Hittite king ruled as the storm god's earthly representative, and every military campaign was undertaken with the storm god's blessing. The king's most common title was "My Sun" (a title borrowed from Mesopotamian and Egyptian royal conventions), but the god he served was the storm god, and the storm god's approval was what made a king legitimate.

His consort was the **Sun Goddess of Arinna** (ah-REEN-nah; Arinna was a sacred city near Hattusa whose exact location is debated). The Sun Goddess was, in many ways, the more important deity of the pair. She was the "queen of the Land of Hatti, queen of heaven and earth," according to official Hittite texts, and her name appears in the most solemn oaths and treaties, often before the Storm God's. The Hittite royal family appears to have had a special devotion to the Sun Goddess that may reflect the pre-Hittite Hattian religious tradition, in which solar deities (represented by the gold sun disc standards found at Alaca Höyük; Chapter 1) played a central role. The Sun Goddess of Arinna was not subordinate to the Storm God; she was his equal or, in some formulations, his superior. The Tawananna, the Hittite Great Queen (an office with its own independent religious and political authority, retained even after the king's death), was the Sun Goddess's primary human representative, just as the king was the Storm God's.

The office of Tawananna (tah-wah-NAH-nah) itself was a unique Hittite institution. In most Bronze Age monarchies, the queen's power derived entirely from her husband's. When the king died, the queen lost her position to the new king's wife. In the Hittite system, the Tawananna retained her title, her religious functions, and her political authority even after her husband's death. A new king could not simply replace her. She had to die, be formally deposed (which required serious charges and a formal trial), or voluntarily retire. This meant that a strong Tawananna could outlast multiple kings and exercise influence across generations. The most famous example is **Puduhepa** (poo-doo-HEH-pah; Chapter 5), whose diplomatic correspondence with the Egyptian court shows a queen operating as an independent political actor, co-signing treaties and negotiating directly with Ramesses II.

---

The Hurrian influence on Hittite religion was transformative. The Hurrians (the people who had built the Mitanni kingdom that Suppiluliuma destroyed; Chapter 3) spoke a language unrelated to Hittite, practiced a religion with its own distinct mythology, and had been filtering into Hittite territory through the southeastern kingdom of Kizzuwatna for centuries. As Hurrian priests, scribes, and religious specialists entered the Hittite world (through diplomacy, marriage alliances, and the incorporation of Hurrian territories into the empire), they brought their gods with them.

The most important Hurrian contribution to Hittite religion was the **Song of Kumarbi** (koo-MAR-bee), a mythological cycle that scholars believe is one of the direct ancestors of Greek mythology. The Song tells the story of divine succession through violence: the sky god **Anu** (AH-noo; the same Anu who appears in Mesopotamian mythology; Mesopotamia Ch 2) is overthrown by **Kumarbi**, who bites off Anu's genitals and swallows them. From this act, Kumarbi becomes pregnant with the Storm God (Teshub), who is eventually born and overthrows Kumarbi in turn. The Storm God then becomes king of the gods.

If this sounds familiar, it should. The Greek poet **Hesiod** (HEE-see-ud; who wrote his poem *Theogony*, "Birth of the Gods," around 700 BCE) told a strikingly similar story: the sky god Ouranos is castrated by his son Kronos, who swallows his own children, until Zeus (a storm god) is born, overthrows Kronos, and becomes king of the gods. The parallels are too close to be coincidence. Most scholars believe the Greek succession myth was borrowed, directly or through intermediaries, from the Hurro-Hittite tradition, which was itself borrowed in part from Mesopotamian myths about divine succession. The Hittites, sitting at the crossroads of Mesopotamia and the Aegean, were the transmission belt through which Near Eastern mythology reached the Greek world.

The Song of Kumarbi is not a simple one-to-one source for the Theogony (the transmission path was probably complex, involving multiple intermediary cultures including the Phoenicians), but the connection is one of the clearest examples of cultural transmission across the Bronze Age world. Ideas, like trade goods, moved along the same routes. And the Hittites, by absorbing Hurrian mythology into their own religious system, preserved stories that would eventually shape the foundations of Western literary tradition.

---

The physical expression of Hittite religion is best preserved at **Yazılıkaya** (yah-zuh-luh-KAH-yah; the name means "inscribed rock" in Turkish), an open-air rock sanctuary about a mile northeast of Hattusa. Yazılıkaya is not a building. It is a natural outcrop of limestone with two natural chambers (essentially narrow roofless corridors between rock walls) whose walls have been carved with relief sculptures of the entire Hittite pantheon in procession.

In the main chamber (Chamber A), two processions of gods approach each other from opposite walls and meet in the center. On the left, male deities march in a line, headed by the Storm God Teshub, standing on two mountain gods and wearing a horned cap (the standard divine headgear in Near Eastern art). On the right, female deities process toward the center, headed by the Sun Goddess of Arinna, standing on a panther. The two processions meet at a central panel where the Storm God and the Sun Goddess face each other, flanked by their divine attendants. Behind them, dozens of lesser deities file in order of rank, each identified by a hieroglyphic label. The overall effect is of a divine assembly, a court of the gods frozen in stone.

The smaller chamber (Chamber B) is more enigmatic. It contains a dramatic relief of the god **Sharruma** (shah-ROO-mah; the son of the Storm God and the Sun Goddess) embracing the Hittite king **Tudhaliya IV** (ruled approximately 1237-1209 BCE), who commissioned the sanctuary's final decorations. Another relief shows a massive sword-god (a deity whose body is formed by a sword blade, with the handle becoming his head and shoulders) plunged into the rock. Twelve identical gods march in a row along one wall. The imagery in Chamber B is clearly funerary or ritual, but its exact meaning is debated. Some scholars believe it was a mortuary chapel for Tudhaliya IV. Others see it as a sacred space for mystery rituals. The lack of readable inscriptions in the chamber leaves interpretation uncertain.

What Yazılıkaya shows beyond any doubt is the scale and ambition of Hittite religious art. This was a state that invested enormous resources in maintaining relationships with hundreds of deities, that depicted its entire pantheon in permanent stone, and that understood its king as standing in intimate personal relationship with the gods. The embrace of Tudhaliya IV by Sharruma is one of the most striking images in ancient Near Eastern art: a god literally hugging a king, arm around his shoulder, like a father holding a child. It is warmer, more intimate, and more personal than anything in Egyptian or Mesopotamian royal art, and it reflects a Hittite understanding of the divine-human relationship that was genuinely different from its neighbors.

---

The daily religious life of the Hittite state was governed by a festival calendar of extraordinary density. The most important festival was the **AN.TAH.SUM** (a Sumerian logogram used in Hittite texts; the festival's Hittite name may have been different), a spring celebration that lasted a staggering 38 days. During the festival, the king himself traveled from Hattusa to a series of cult centers across the Hittite heartland, performing rituals at each one, making offerings to local gods, and ensuring that the divine machinery of the kingdom was properly maintained for the coming agricultural season. The king was not a passive participant in these rituals. He was the chief priest of the state religion, and his physical presence at each cult center was required for the rituals to be effective.

This had practical implications. A Hittite king who was ill, on campaign, or politically weakened might be unable to perform the required festivals, which would create a religious crisis on top of whatever other problems he was dealing with. Conversely, a king who performed the festivals punctually and elaborately demonstrated his legitimacy, his health, and his control of the kingdom's heartland. The festival calendar was both a religious obligation and a political performance.

The Hittites also maintained a vast library of ritual texts, many of them remarkably specific. Texts survive for rituals to cure illness, to protect against witchcraft, to purify a house after a death, to ensure success in war, to improve a failing marriage, to protect travelers on the road, and to appease angry gods whose temples had been neglected. These texts often specify the exact materials required (particular types of bread, specific animals for sacrifice, specific colors of wool for binding spells), the exact words to be spoken (often in Hattic or Hurrian, even when the surrounding text is in Hittite), and the exact gestures to be performed. They are, in effect, instruction manuals for priests, and their survival in the Hattusa archive is one of the richest sources for understanding daily religious practice in the ancient world.

The Hittite approach to religion was pragmatic rather than dogmatic. The Hittites did not develop a systematic theology. They did not produce philosophical treatises about the nature of the gods. They did not, as far as we can tell, have a concept of orthodoxy (correct belief) distinct from orthopraxis (correct practice). What mattered was that the rituals were performed correctly, the gods were fed and housed and honored, and the reciprocal relationship between the divine and human worlds was maintained. When things went wrong (plagues, military defeats, droughts), the assumption was that someone had offended a god, and the correct response was to identify the offense, perform the appropriate purification ritual, and restore the broken relationship. Mursili II's Plague Prayers (Chapter 3) are the most emotionally intense example of this logic, but they are not exceptional. The Hittite archive is full of texts where kings and priests try to figure out which god they have offended and what they need to do to fix it.

This pragmatic, inclusive, ritual-focused approach to religion helps explain why the Hittite pantheon grew so large. Adding a new god to the roster was not a theological problem. It was a practical measure: if a conquered people's god was powerful enough to help them resist Hittite armies, then that god was clearly powerful and should be honored rather than offended. Bringing foreign gods into the Hittite system was not generosity. It was risk management. The thousand gods of Hatti were, in a sense, a divine insurance policy, covering every possible source of supernatural power that the empire might need to draw upon.

The next chapter returns to the political narrative and the crisis that would produce the Hittites' most famous legacy: the world's first peace treaty.

# Chapter 5 — Kadesh and the World's First Peace Treaty

By the late 1300s BCE, the Hittite Empire and Egypt had been circling each other for decades. Both were superpowers. Both claimed sovereignty over Syria, the wealthy corridor of trade cities and agricultural land stretching from the Euphrates to the Mediterranean coast. And both knew that sooner or later, the circling would stop and the collision would come.

The collision happened in 1274 BCE, at a fortified city called **Kadesh** (kah-DESH; modern Tell Nebi Mend in western Syria, on the Orontes River). It was, by most measures, the largest chariot battle in ancient history, and possibly the most documented battle from any period before the Greeks and Romans began writing detailed military histories. Both sides described the battle in extensive texts. Both sides claimed to have won. Neither side was telling the whole truth.

---

The Hittite king who marched to Kadesh was **Muwatalli II** (moo-wah-TAHL-lee; ruled approximately 1295-1272 BCE), the son of Mursili II (Chapter 3). Muwatalli was a competent but controversial ruler. His most provocative decision had nothing to do with Egypt: he moved the Hittite capital from Hattusa south to a city called **Tarhuntassa** (tar-hoon-TAH-sah; a city in southern Anatolia whose exact location is still debated), closer to the Syrian front where the real action was. The move was strategically logical (Hattusa was far from the empire's southern frontier and vulnerable to Kaska raids from the north) but politically inflammatory. Hattusa was the sacred city of the Storm God, the seat of the panku assembly, and the symbolic heart of the kingdom. Moving the capital was like a medieval English king deciding to govern from Calais because it was closer to France. The decision created a rival power center at Tarhuntassa that would haunt Hittite politics for generations (and may have contributed to a civil war in the empire's final decades; Chapter 7).

Muwatalli left his brother **Hattusili** (hat-too-SEE-lee; the future Hattusili III) in charge of the northern territories, including the management of the perennial Kaska threat. This arrangement was practical — Hattusili was a capable commander — but it gave Hattusili an independent power base that he would eventually use to seize the throne.

The Egyptian pharaoh marching toward Kadesh was **Ramesses II** (RAM-eh-seez; ruled approximately 1279-1213 BCE, one of the longest-reigning pharaohs in Egyptian history). Ramesses was young (probably in his late twenties), ambitious, and eager to reclaim the Syrian territories that Egypt had lost to the Hittites during the previous generation. He led an army of roughly 20,000 men organized into four divisions (named after Egyptian gods: Amun, Ra, Ptah, and Sutekh), with a core of around 2,000 chariots. The Egyptian chariot carried two men (a driver and an archer), was lighter and faster than the Hittite model, and was optimized for mobile archery, swooping past enemy formations and peppering them with arrows.

Muwatalli's army was comparable in size, perhaps larger, with contingents drawn from the empire and its vassal states. The Hittite chariot was different from the Egyptian one: heavier, carrying three men instead of two (a driver, a shield-bearer, and a spearman). The Hittite chariot was designed not for shooting but for shock, charging directly into enemy formations and breaking them with mass and impact. The two chariot designs reflected two different tactical philosophies: Egypt favored mobility and ranged combat; the Hittites favored mass and close combat. Kadesh would put both philosophies to the test.

---

The battle began with a Hittite intelligence operation that, if the Egyptian account is to be believed, was brilliantly executed. As Ramesses approached Kadesh from the south with his army strung out along the march route, two Bedouin tribesmen (whom the Hittite texts do not mention, but the Egyptian texts describe in detail) came into the Egyptian camp and told Ramesses that the Hittite army was far to the north, nowhere near Kadesh. Ramesses believed them. He pushed ahead with his lead division (Amun) and made camp near the city, confident that he had time to concentrate his forces before the Hittites arrived.

The Bedouin were Hittite agents. The Hittite army was not far to the north. It was hiding behind the city of Kadesh, on the east bank of the Orontes River, less than two miles away.

The Egyptian account describes what happened next as a disaster that was saved only by Ramesses's personal heroism (take that with a significant grain of salt, since the text was commissioned by Ramesses himself and carved on temple walls across Egypt). The Hittite chariot force, possibly as many as 2,500 to 3,500 chariots, smashed into the second Egyptian division (Ra) as it was still marching in column toward the camp, scattering it completely. The Hittite chariots then rolled into the Egyptian camp itself, catching the Amun division in the middle of setting up their tents.

The Egyptian texts describe Ramesses personally rallying his troops, mounting his chariot, and leading a counterattack that drove the Hittites back. Modern scholars are skeptical of the one-man-army version but acknowledge that something turned the battle around. The most likely explanation is that a force of Egyptian allies (the "Ne'arin," possibly a garrison from the coastal city of Amurru) arrived at the right moment and struck the Hittite chariots in the flank while they were dispersed and looting the Egyptian camp. Looting too soon, before the enemy was actually defeated, was a common tactical error in chariot warfare, and the Hittite forces at Kadesh appear to have made it.

The battle ended inconclusively. Ramesses held the field (barely), but he had not taken Kadesh and his army was badly mauled. Muwatalli had not destroyed the Egyptian army but had demonstrated that the Hittites could match Egypt in the open field. Both sides withdrew. Ramesses returned to Egypt and covered his temples with enormous carved reliefs depicting the battle as a personal triumph, complete with images of Ramesses single-handedly scattering the Hittite army while the god Amun guided his arrows. The Egyptian "Poem of Kadesh" and "Bulletin of Kadesh," recorded on temple walls at Luxor, Karnak, Abu Simbel, and the Ramesseum, are the most extensive battle narratives surviving from the ancient world.

The Hittite account of the battle, preserved in fragmentary texts from Hattusa, is less detailed but tells a different story: a Hittite victory, with the Egyptian army forced to retreat. The truth, as nearly all modern scholars agree, is that Kadesh was a draw, a bloody and strategically indecisive engagement that demonstrated neither side could conquer the other's Syrian territories by force.

---

The years after Kadesh were marked by continued low-level conflict in Syria, internal political upheaval in the Hittite Empire, and a gradually dawning realization on both sides that fighting each other was more expensive than it was worth.

The Hittite upheaval came first. After Muwatalli II's death (around 1272 BCE), his son **Urhi-Teshub** (OOR-hee teh-SHOOB; also known by his throne name Mursili III) took the throne. He was young, politically weak, and made the mistake of antagonizing his uncle Hattusili, the brother of Muwatalli whom the dead king had left in charge of the north. Around 1267 BCE, Hattusili overthrew Urhi-Teshub and seized the throne as **Hattusili III**.

This was, by any standard, a usurpation — a direct violation of the succession principles laid down by Telipinu's Edict (Chapter 2). Hattusili knew it. And his response was extraordinary: he wrote a document, now called the **Apology of Hattusili III**, that is the oldest known political autobiography in history. The Apology is a sustained defense of Hattusili's actions, framed as a narrative of his life from childhood through the usurpation, in which he claims that he was persecuted by Urhi-Teshub, denied his rightful position, and ultimately driven to seize power in self-defense. The central argument is theological: the goddess **Ishtar** (the Mesopotamian goddess of love and war, who had been adopted into the Hittite pantheon) had favored Hattusili from birth, guided his career, and sanctioned the usurpation as an expression of divine will.

The Apology is a fascinating document because it reveals the constraints under which even a Hittite usurper operated. Hattusili did not simply seize power and dare anyone to complain. He felt compelled to justify his actions in writing, to explain why the normal rules of succession did not apply in his case, and to present his usurpation as divinely ordained rather than personally motivated. The Edict of Telipinu, two hundred years old by this point, still had enough moral authority that a successful usurper had to write a book explaining why it was acceptable to ignore it. The document reads like a Bronze Age PR campaign, and it is one of the most revealing texts about how ancient rulers understood legitimacy and the relationship between power and law.

---

Hattusili III, having secured his position, turned his attention to diplomacy. And around 1259 BCE (the exact date depends on the chronological system used for Ramesses II's reign), he and Ramesses II produced the document that the Hittites are most famous for: the **Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty**, the oldest known international peace treaty in history.

A copy of the treaty hangs in the United Nations headquarters in New York, a gift from Turkey. It is there because the treaty is genuinely remarkable. It is not a surrender document. It is not a trade agreement dressed up as peace. It is a mutual defense pact between equals, negotiated over years of diplomatic correspondence, and it addresses the full range of issues that modern peace treaties address: mutual non-aggression, mutual defense against third-party attack, the return of political refugees, the status of border territories, and the divine witnesses who would punish violations.

The treaty survives in two versions: an Akkadian cuneiform version found in the Hattusa archive, and an Egyptian hieroglyphic version carved on the walls of the Temple of Karnak in Luxor. The two versions are not identical (each side's version is slightly more favorable to itself), but they are close enough to confirm that a real negotiation took place and that both sides agreed on the substantive terms. Both versions invoke the gods of both nations as witnesses and guarantors. Both versions include provisions for the extradition of fugitives. Both versions commit each party to defend the other against external attack.

The treaty held. For the remaining decades of both empires' existence, there was no war between Egypt and Hatti. In 1246 BCE, the peace was sealed with a **royal marriage**: Hattusili III's daughter traveled from Hattusa to Egypt and married Ramesses II. The journey was celebrated in inscriptions on both sides, and Ramesses gave the princess an Egyptian name. The marriage was a diplomatic masterstroke, binding the two royal families together in a way that made future conflict personally costly for the rulers who would have to wage it.

The treaty was negotiated not only by Hattusili III but also by his wife, **Queen Puduhepa** (poo-doo-HEH-pah), whose independent diplomatic correspondence with the Egyptian court survives in the archive. Puduhepa co-signed the treaty (her seal appears on the Hittite version alongside Hattusili's), wrote directly to Ramesses II about the terms, and conducted her own parallel negotiations about the royal marriage. She also corresponded with Ramesses's chief queen, **Nefertari** (neh-fer-TAH-ree), in letters that mix diplomatic formality with what sounds like genuine warmth. Puduhepa is one of the best-documented women in the Bronze Age, and her role in the peace treaty demonstrates the real political power that the Tawananna institution could confer.

The Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty is often cited as a "first," and it is worth examining that claim precisely. It is the oldest surviving treaty between two recognized sovereign states that includes mutual defense obligations, refugee provisions, and enforcement by divine oath. Earlier treaties exist (the Hittite vassal treaties, for example, go back centuries, and there are Mesopotamian treaties that predate even those), but those are typically arrangements between a dominant power and a subordinate, not pacts between equals. What makes the Egyptian-Hittite treaty distinctive is its symmetry: both parties are treated as peers, both make equivalent commitments, and both acknowledge the other's legitimacy. In that specific sense, it is the first known treaty between equals, and its diplomatic architecture (bilateral obligations, third-party enforcement, refugee clauses) anticipates the structure of international law by three thousand years.

The peace with Egypt was the high-water mark of Hittite diplomacy and, arguably, of the entire Bronze Age international system. Two empires that had fought the largest battle in recorded history chose to negotiate rather than continue fighting, produced a written agreement that governed their relationship for decades, and sealed it with a marriage that created personal ties between the ruling families. It worked. The next chapter explores the broader world that made this kind of diplomacy possible: the trade networks, technologies, and cultural connections that tied the Bronze Age civilizations together into an interconnected system. A system that was about to collapse.

# Chapter 6 — How the Bronze Age Worked

In 1982, a sponge diver named Mehmet Çakir was diving off the coast of southwestern Turkey, near the town of Kaş (KAHSH), when he found something on the seabed roughly 150 feet (45 meters) below the surface: the remains of an ancient ship, its cargo scattered across the rocky bottom. The wreck, called the **Uluburun shipwreck** (oo-loo-boo-ROON) after the nearby cape, would take eleven years of excavation (1984-1994) and become the single most important archaeological find for understanding how the Bronze Age world worked.

The ship had sunk around 1300 BCE, give or take a few decades, during the peak of the Hittite Empire. Its cargo came from at least seven different cultures, spanning nearly the entire known world of the Late Bronze Age.

There were 10 tons of Cypriot copper (354 ingots in the distinctive "oxhide" shape used across the eastern Mediterranean, each weighing roughly 60 pounds or 27 kg). There was approximately one ton of tin (probably from Afghanistan, shipped through Mesopotamian intermediaries). Together, these metals could have produced roughly 11 tons of bronze, enough to equip a small army. There were Canaanite storage jars (the standard shipping containers of the ancient Mediterranean) filled with resin from the pistachio tree, used in incense and food preservation. There was African ebony. There were Egyptian gold rings, scarabs (carved seal-amulets in the shape of beetles), and a gold scarab bearing the name of the Egyptian queen **Nefertiti** (neh-fer-TEE-tee). There were Mycenaean Greek pottery and swords. There were Baltic amber beads (amber from the shores of the Baltic Sea, more than 2,000 miles or 3,200 km from where the ship sank, that had been traded southward through a chain of intermediaries). There were Nubian glass ingots. There were hippopotamus and elephant ivory. There were bronze tools, weapons, and a set of nested stone weights conforming to multiple different weight standards (Egyptian, Mesopotamian, Aegean) so that the merchants could do business in any port.

The Uluburun ship was, in effect, a floating cross-section of the Late Bronze Age economy. Its cargo connected Egypt and Nubia to Mesopotamia, the Aegean to the Levant, the Baltic to the Mediterranean, and all of them to the Hittite-controlled coast of Anatolia, where the ship went down. The vessel itself was built in the Levantine tradition (planks joined edge to edge with wooden pegs, a technique associated with Canaanite or Cypriot shipbuilders), but it carried goods destined for markets across the entire eastern Mediterranean.

---

The Uluburun wreck illustrates a critical point about the Hittite Empire: it did not exist in isolation. The Late Bronze Age (roughly 1600-1200 BCE) was one of the most interconnected periods in human history before the modern era. A network of diplomatic, commercial, and cultural ties linked Egypt, the Hittite Empire, Babylon, Assyria, the Mycenaean Greek kingdoms, the city-states of the Levant and Cyprus, and dozens of smaller polities into a single system. This system was held together by three things: trade, cuneiform, and the chariot.

**Trade** was the economic engine. The Bronze Age economy ran on two metals: copper and tin. Copper was relatively abundant (major sources included Cyprus, Anatolia, and Oman). Tin was not. The tin required to alloy with copper to make bronze came from a handful of distant sources, most importantly Afghanistan and possibly Cornwall in southwestern England, and it had to be shipped overland and by sea across thousands of miles to reach the workshops where bronze was made. Whoever controlled the tin trade controlled the Bronze Age military-industrial complex. The Hittites controlled tin routes through Anatolia and, through their vassal network in Syria, dominated the trade flowing between Mesopotamia and the Mediterranean.

But tin was only the most strategic commodity. The trade networks also carried textiles, grain, wine, olive oil, timber, ivory, glass, precious metals, exotic stones, and luxury goods of every kind. Port cities like **Ugarit** (oo-gah-REET; on the Syrian coast, a Hittite vassal) served as hubs where goods from all over the known world were exchanged. Ugarit's archive contains texts in at least seven languages, reflecting the cosmopolitan character of a city where Egyptian merchants, Hittite officials, Babylonian scribes, Cypriot copper traders, and Aegean sailors all did business under the roof of a single port authority.

**Cuneiform** was the communication system. The remarkable thing about Late Bronze Age diplomacy is that it operated in a shared language: Akkadian, the Semitic language of Mesopotamia, written in cuneiform script. The Hittites, the Egyptians, the kings of Cyprus, the rulers of Syria, and every other major player in the system used Akkadian as the language of international correspondence, even though most of them did not speak it as their native tongue. The Hittite king wrote to the pharaoh in Akkadian. The pharaoh wrote back in Akkadian. The vassal treaties, the trade agreements, the marriage negotiations, and the bitter complaints about the quality of gold being shipped were all conducted in this borrowed language. The Amarna Letters (Chapter 3) are the best-known collection, but cuneiform correspondence has been found at sites from Hattusa to Ugarit to Amman to Babylon.

This shared diplomatic language created a shared diplomatic culture. The Great Kings of the Late Bronze Age addressed each other as "brother," exchanged gifts as tokens of respect and friendship, negotiated marriages between royal families, and conducted their disputes through formal channels. The system was not perfect (wars happened, as Kadesh demonstrated; Chapter 5), but it provided a framework for managing interstate relations that was far more sophisticated than anything before it and that would not be matched in scale until the European diplomatic system of the 17th century CE.

---

The **chariot** was the military technology that made the system possible and shaped its politics. Every major power of the Late Bronze Age was a chariot power. Egypt, the Hittites, Mitanni, Assyria, Babylon, and the Mycenaean Greeks all depended on chariot forces as the core of their armies. Building and maintaining a chariot force was enormously expensive: each chariot required skilled carpenters, wheelwrights, leatherworkers, and metalworkers to build; trained horses to pull it; and trained crews to fight from it. Only wealthy, centralized states could afford chariot armies, which meant that the chariot was simultaneously a weapon, a status symbol, and a barrier to entry that kept smaller polities from challenging the great powers.

The Hittites contributed a key innovation to chariot warfare: the **three-man chariot**. While the standard Egyptian chariot carried two crew (a driver and an archer, optimized for speed and mobile archery), the Hittite chariot carried three (a driver, a shield-bearer, and a spearman, optimized for shock combat). The heavier Hittite chariot was slower than the Egyptian model but packed more punch in close contact. The difference played out at Kadesh (Chapter 5), where the Hittite chariots smashed through the Egyptian column with devastating effect but were too slow to prevent the Egyptian counterattack.

The Hittites also employed one of history's most remarkable military specialists: **Kikkuli** (kee-KOO-lee), a horse trainer from the defeated kingdom of Mitanni who was hired by the Hittite court to write a manual on horse conditioning and chariot training. Kikkuli's text, dating to around 1345 BCE, is the **oldest known manual on horse training** in the world. It describes a seven-month conditioning program for chariot horses involving interval training (alternating periods of exercise and rest), controlled diet, and progressive increases in workload that modern equestrians have described as surprisingly sophisticated. The manual uses Hurrian technical terms for many of the training concepts (further evidence of the Hurrian influence on Hittite military culture) and was clearly intended as a professional text for royal horse trainers, not a casual guide.

Kikkuli's manual illustrates an important point about the Bronze Age great powers: expertise was mobile. A horse trainer from a conquered kingdom could be employed by the conquering power. A scribe trained in one cuneiform tradition could adapt to another. A metalworker from Cyprus could ply his trade in Ugarit or Hattusa. The Late Bronze Age international system was not just a network of trading goods; it was a network of trading skills, knowledge, and human talent. This mobility of expertise was one of the system's great strengths, and its disappearance during the collapse (Chapter 7) was one of the collapse's most devastating consequences.

---

The Hittites also left behind a **law code** that reveals much about the character of their civilization. The Hittite laws (compiled in their earliest form around 1500 BCE and revised at least once, probably under Tudhaliya IV around 1230 BCE) are remarkably different from the Mesopotamian legal tradition that most people think of when they hear "ancient law code."

The most famous ancient law code is Hammurabi's (Mesopotamia Ch 7), which is built on the principle of retributive justice: an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth, with harsh penalties (including death and mutilation) for many offenses. The Hittite laws take a fundamentally different approach. They emphasize **restitution** over punishment. Where Hammurabi's code says "if a man puts out the eye of another man, his eye shall be put out," the Hittite code says, in effect, "if a man puts out the eye of another man, he pays twenty shekels of silver." The emphasis is on compensating the victim, not on inflicting equivalent harm on the offender.

The Hittite laws also distinguish between intentional and accidental harm, a distinction that is less consistently made in Mesopotamian law. A deliberate assault is punished more severely than an accidental injury. A premeditated murder carries a higher penalty than a killing in a fight. This distinction suggests a legal culture that was interested in the mental state of the offender, not just the physical outcome of the offense.

The revised version of the law code (probably from the reign of Tudhaliya IV, around 1230 BCE) went even further, reducing penalties for many offenses below the levels set in the earlier version. The revision explicitly notes the changes: "formerly, the penalty was X; now, the penalty is Y." This self-conscious legal reform, documented in the text itself, is remarkable. It means the Hittites were not just applying inherited laws mechanically; they were reflecting on their legal tradition, deciding it was too harsh, and deliberately making it less so. The direction of change (from harsher to less harsh) is the opposite of what many people assume about ancient legal systems.

None of this means the Hittite legal system was gentle by modern standards. It still included provisions for slavery, corporal punishment, and the death penalty for certain offenses (particularly those involving the king or the gods). But the overall character of Hittite law, compared to its Mesopotamian contemporaries, is strikingly focused on compensation, proportionality, and the distinction between intent and accident. Scholars have debated why. One plausible explanation is demographic: the Hittite heartland, with its harsh climate and limited agricultural productivity, may have had a smaller population than the riverine civilizations of Mesopotamia and Egypt. In a society where every able-bodied person mattered, compensating a victim and keeping the offender productive may have made more practical sense than executing or mutilating the offender and losing his labor. Pragmatism, rather than advanced moral philosophy, may explain the law code's relative leniency.

---

One more technology deserves attention before we leave the Hittite economic and technological world: **iron**.

The Hittites are often credited with inventing iron smelting, or at least with being the first to produce iron objects in significant quantities. The truth is more complicated. Iron smelting (the process of extracting metallic iron from iron ore by heating it with charcoal in a furnace) was known in small-scale, experimental form across the Near East from at least 2500 BCE. But iron was extremely difficult to work. It required higher temperatures than copper or tin, and the resulting metal was often inferior to bronze unless it was properly carburized (heated with carbon to produce steel). For most of the Bronze Age, iron was a curiosity, rarer than gold and used mainly for ornamental purposes.

What the Hittites appear to have done is develop the techniques required to produce iron in usable quantities and at a quality sufficient for tools and weapons. The evidence for this includes a famous document known as the **"Iron Letter"**, a text in which a Hittite king (probably Hattusili III, Chapter 5) responds to a request for iron from a foreign ruler. The letter says, essentially: we do not have iron available right now, but we are producing it and will send some when the current batch is ready. The text implies that iron production was controlled by the Hittite state, that it was a scarce and valuable commodity, and that foreign rulers wanted it badly enough to write to the Hittite king personally.

The Hittites may have closely guarded their iron-working techniques as a strategic advantage. Whether they actually maintained an iron monopoly (as older scholarship sometimes claimed) is debated. Some scholars argue that the "Iron Letter" has been over-interpreted and that Hittite iron production was not significantly more advanced than iron production elsewhere. Others maintain that the Hittites had a genuine edge in iron technology that gave their military a material advantage. What is not debated is that after the Hittite Empire collapsed (Chapter 7), iron technology spread rapidly across the Near East, and within a few centuries, iron had replaced bronze as the standard material for weapons and tools. The Bronze Age became the Iron Age. Whether the Hittite collapse caused the spread of iron (by dispersing Hittite smiths and their techniques) or merely coincided with it is one of those questions that sounds simple but has no simple answer.

The interconnected world that the Uluburun ship sailed in, that the diplomats at Kadesh fought over, that the thousand gods of Hatti sanctified, and that iron and bronze built, was about to come apart. The next chapter tells the story of the end.

# Chapter 7 — The Fall

Sometime around 1200 BCE, the world the Hittites knew stopped working.

The phrase scholars use is the **Bronze Age Collapse**, and it is one of the most dramatic civilizational downturns in human history. Within a span of roughly fifty years, every major power in the eastern Mediterranean either collapsed entirely, shrank catastrophically, or was fundamentally transformed. The Hittite Empire vanished. Mycenaean Greece fell into a dark age that lasted four centuries. The Kassite dynasty in Babylon was overthrown. Ugarit was destroyed and never rebuilt. The Egyptian New Kingdom survived but lost its Asian empire and entered a long decline. The international diplomatic system, the trade networks, the shared cuneiform culture that had connected these civilizations for centuries, all of it disintegrated. The world that had produced the Battle of Kadesh, the Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty, and the Uluburun trading ship simply ceased to exist.

The Hittite Empire's collapse was the most complete. Egypt survived. Babylon survived. Assyria survived (barely). The Hittites did not. Hattusa was burned and abandoned. The royal archive was scattered. The king list stopped. The great temples, the lions gate, the thousand gods, all of it went silent. For the next three thousand years, until 19th-century archaeologists dug up the ruins, nobody knew the Hittite Empire had ever existed. Their disappearance was so thorough that scholars sometimes use the Hittites as the archetypal example of how completely a major civilization can vanish.

What happened?

---

The honest answer is: we are not entirely sure, and any account that claims certainty is oversimplifying.

The traditional narrative pins the collapse on the **Sea Peoples**, a term that modern scholars have inherited from the Egyptian sources. In the 1170s BCE, pharaoh **Ramesses III** (not the Ramesses II of Kadesh fame, but his grandson, ruling roughly a generation later) recorded massive battles against coalitions of invaders arriving by land and sea from the north. The Egyptian texts name these groups: the Peleset (possibly the Philistines), the Tjeker, the Shekelesh, the Denyen, the Weshesh, and others. The reliefs at the mortuary temple of Ramesses III at **Medinet Habu** (meh-DEE-net HAH-boo) in Egypt show dramatic scenes of naval battles and land engagements, with the invaders depicted in distinctive feathered or horned helmets fighting from oxcarts and warships.

But who were the Sea Peoples? The frustrating truth is that nobody knows for certain. The Egyptian names are labels, not identities, and scholars have spent a century trying to match them to known ethnic or geographic groups. Some may have been Mycenaean Greeks, displaced by the collapse of the palace system in the Aegean. Some may have been Anatolians, possibly even subjects of the Hittite Empire itself, displaced by the famine and instability that preceded the collapse. Some may have been Italians, Sardinians, or Sicilians, peoples from the western Mediterranean who appear in scattered references but are poorly documented. The Sea Peoples were probably not a unified force with a common agenda. They were more likely a diverse collection of displaced populations, refugees, raiders, and opportunistic attackers who converged on the collapsing eastern Mediterranean from multiple directions.

The theory that the Sea Peoples destroyed the Hittite Empire by direct attack is tempting but oversimplified. A single letter found at Ugarit, written just before the city's destruction, pleads for military help and mentions enemy ships, but it does not specify who the attackers are. The destruction of Hattusa shows evidence of deliberate burning, but the identity of the burners is unknown. Some scholars have suggested that the Kaska peoples from the north (the Hittites' perennial internal enemy; Chapter 2) delivered the final blow to Hattusa, not the Sea Peoples at all.

---

The more sophisticated modern understanding of the Bronze Age Collapse treats it not as a single event but as a **systems collapse**, a cascade of mutually reinforcing failures that no single state could withstand. The influential model, developed by the historian Eric Cline in his book *1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed* (2014), identifies several simultaneous stressors.

**Drought and famine.** Climate studies using tree-ring data, pollen cores, and sediment analysis suggest that the eastern Mediterranean experienced a prolonged drought around 1200-1150 BCE. In a region where agricultural production was already marginal (especially on the high Anatolian plateau, where the Hittite heartland depended on adequate rainfall; Chapter 1), a multi-decade drought could have been catastrophic. The Hittite archive itself contains desperate letters from the empire's final years, including messages from the king begging Egypt for emergency grain shipments. "It is a matter of life and death!" one letter reportedly says. Egypt sent grain, but the shipments were clearly not enough.

**Trade disruption.** The Bronze Age economy depended on long-distance trade, especially the tin trade that made bronze possible (Chapter 6). If the trade routes were disrupted by raiders, pirates, political instability, or simply the collapse of the intermediary states that facilitated commerce, the entire economic system could seize up. Without tin, you could not make bronze. Without bronze, you could not make weapons, tools, or the luxury goods that elites used to maintain their political authority. The disappearance of tin trade may have been both a symptom and a cause of the collapse: as states weakened, trade routes became unsafe, which made states weaker, which made trade routes more unsafe.

**Internal rebellion.** Several of the states that collapsed show evidence of internal unrest before the final destruction. In the Hittite case, the rivalry between the royal line at Hattusa and the cadet branch based at Tarhuntassa (the alternate capital established by Muwatalli II; Chapter 5) may have divided the empire at the worst possible moment. A large bronze tablet found at Hattusa records a treaty between the Hittite king **Tudhaliya IV** (too-DAHL-ee-yah; ruled approximately 1237-1209 BCE) and his cousin **Kurunta** (koo-ROON-tah), the king of Tarhuntassa. Some scholars believe that Kurunta briefly seized the Hittite throne itself, creating a civil war that weakened the empire from within just as the external threats were mounting.

**Earthquake.** There is geological and archaeological evidence for earthquake damage at multiple sites across the eastern Mediterranean in this period, including Troy, Mycenae, and several Anatolian cities. While earthquakes alone do not destroy empires (civilizations in earthquake-prone areas rebuild), earthquakes on top of drought, famine, trade disruption, and military pressure might have been the final straw for fragile systems already under stress.

**The cascade.** The key insight of the systems-collapse model is that these stressors were not independent. They reinforced each other. Drought caused famine. Famine caused population displacement. Displaced populations raided trade routes. Disrupted trade routes weakened states. Weakened states could not defend against raiders. Raiders destroyed more cities, displacing more populations. Each failing state took its trade partners and vassals down with it. The collapse was not a single disaster but a death spiral, and no individual state, no matter how powerful, could break the spiral alone because the system itself was the problem.

---

For the Hittite Empire specifically, the collapse appears to have unfolded in the final decades of the 13th century BCE and the early decades of the 12th.

The last clearly documented Hittite king is **Suppiluliuma II** (soo-pee-loo-lee-OO-mah; ruled approximately 1207-1178 BCE, named after the great Suppiluliuma I of Chapter 3). His fragmentary inscriptions record a naval victory off the coast of **Alashiya** (ah-lah-SHEE-yah; probably Cyprus), one of the earliest described naval battles in history. The inscription suggests that the Hittites were still fighting, still winning individual battles, even as the empire crumbled around them. But individual victories could not stop the broader collapse. The trade networks were dying. The grain was not arriving. The vassals were revolting or simply failing to respond. The international diplomatic system that had sustained Hittite power for centuries had ceased to function.

Around 1178 BCE, Hattusa burned.

The archaeological evidence shows that the city was deliberately destroyed. The fire was not accidental. Buildings were burned selectively, with the royal citadel and the main temple complex showing the heaviest damage. Some areas of the city may have been abandoned before the burning. The great archive was scattered but not completely destroyed (which is why we can read it today; the fire baked many of the clay tablets, preserving them). Who set the fires is unknown. The Kaska people from the north are one candidate. Sea Peoples are another. Internal rebels are a third. It may have been a combination: a city weakened by famine, depopulation, and political fragmentation, delivered a final blow by raiders who found relatively little to take.

The city was never rebuilt. The Lions Gate still stands, but the city behind it was dead.

---

The comparison to other collapses is instructive. The Roman Empire took centuries to decline and fall, and its eastern half survived for another thousand years. The Han dynasty in China collapsed and was eventually reconstituted. The Egyptian New Kingdom declined but did not vanish. The Hittite collapse was different: fast, total, and permanent. The empire did not shrink into a rump state. It did not limp along in reduced form. It simply ended.

Why so complete? Several factors made the Hittite Empire more vulnerable than its contemporaries. First, geography. The Anatolian plateau was marginal agricultural land that depended on adequate rainfall. A prolonged drought hit the Hittites harder than it hit Egypt (which had the Nile) or Babylon (which had irrigated river valleys). Second, political structure. The Hittite monarchy, with its powerful nobility, its tradition of usurpation, and the rival power center at Tarhuntassa, was more fragile than the Egyptian pharaonic system or the Babylonian bureaucracy. Third, location. Hattusa sat at the center of the empire but was surrounded by hostile territory: the Kaska to the north, the newly unstable western Anatolian kingdoms to the west, and the increasingly aggressive Assyrians to the southeast. When the system collapsed, the Hittite heartland was exposed on all sides.

But the most important factor may have been the nature of the Hittite Empire itself. The Hittite system was built on vassal treaties, personal loyalty to the Great King, and the economic benefits of belonging to the international trade network. When the trade network collapsed, the economic incentive to remain a Hittite vassal evaporated. When the Great King could no longer project military power, the personal loyalty evaporated. The vassal system that had held the empire together was a mechanism that worked beautifully in good times and failed catastrophically in bad ones. There was no deep institutional structure, no bureaucratic spine (like the Elamite administration that the later Persian Empire would inherit; Elamite Civilization Ch 8), to hold things together when the top collapsed.

The result was the most complete disappearance of a major civilization in ancient history. Within a century of Hattusa's burning, nobody was writing in Hittite cuneiform. Within a few centuries, the memory of the empire had faded to scattered references in Assyrian and Egyptian texts that nobody could fully interpret. Within a millennium, the Hittites were forgotten entirely. The civilization that had sacked Babylon, fought Ramesses to a standstill, and invented the peace treaty had vanished as completely as if it had never existed.

But not entirely. In the ruins of the empire's southern and southeastern territories, fragments of the Hittite world survived. The next chapter tells the story of what came after.

# Chapter 8 — After the Empire

The Hittite Empire was gone. But the Hittites, as a culture and a people, were not.

In the decades and centuries after Hattusa burned, a series of small kingdoms in southeastern Anatolia and northern Syria preserved elements of Hittite civilization: the Luwian language, the hieroglyphic script, the artistic traditions, the political vocabulary, and the memory of having once been part of something larger. Modern scholars call these the **Neo-Hittite states** (or, more precisely, Syro-Hittite states, since they blended Hittite, Luwian, and Aramaean elements), and they lasted from roughly 1178 to 700 BCE, when the last of them were absorbed by the Neo-Assyrian Empire.

The story of the Neo-Hittite states is the story of a civilization's afterlife. It is also, unexpectedly, the bridge that connects the Hittites to the modern world, because it was the Neo-Hittite remains, not the buried ruins of Hattusa, that first alerted 19th-century scholars to the existence of a forgotten empire.

---

The most important Neo-Hittite state was **Carchemish** (KAR-keh-mish), a city on the upper Euphrates River that had been one of the most important Hittite provincial capitals during the empire. Suppiluliuma I had placed his own son on the throne of Carchemish (Chapter 3), and the city's royal dynasty survived the empire's collapse. After Hattusa fell, Carchemish became the de facto successor to the Hittite imperial tradition: its kings used Hittite royal titles, maintained Hittite religious practices, and governed through the same kind of vassal relationships that had structured the empire.

The kings of Carchemish also did something that turned out to be historically crucial: they continued to use the **Luwian hieroglyphic script**.

**Luwian** (LOO-wee-un) was an Indo-European language closely related to Hittite, spoken across much of southern and western Anatolia during the empire's lifetime. The Hittites had used two writing systems simultaneously: **cuneiform** (borrowed from Mesopotamia, written on clay tablets, used for official records, treaties, and correspondence) and a local **hieroglyphic script** (carved on stone monuments and pressed into clay seals, used for public inscriptions and royal display). The hieroglyphic script is called "Luwian hieroglyphic" or "Anatolian hieroglyphic" because most of the surviving texts in this script are in the Luwian language, though the script was also used for Hittite and possibly other languages.

When the empire collapsed and cuneiform knowledge was lost (cuneiform required professional scribes trained in a tradition that depended on institutions that no longer existed), the Luwian hieroglyphic script survived. It was simpler, could be carved directly onto stone without the intermediary technology of clay tablets and styluses, and was maintained by the literate elites of the Neo-Hittite courts. The result was that the Neo-Hittite states wrote in a different script and mostly a different language (Luwian rather than Hittite) from the empire, but they wrote, and their inscriptions provide the primary evidence for the political history of the region between the empire's fall and the Assyrian conquest.

---

Besides Carchemish, the most notable Neo-Hittite states included:

**Sam'al** (sahm-AHL; modern Zincirli Höyük, in southeastern Turkey), a kingdom that produced remarkable bilingual inscriptions in Luwian and Aramaic, reflecting the cultural blending that characterized the post-imperial world. The ruins of Sam'al include a well-preserved royal palace and monumental gate sculptures that combine Hittite artistic traditions with Aramaean and Assyrian influences. The site's bilingual texts were among the first clues that helped scholars connect the Neo-Hittite states to the earlier empire.

**Tabal** (tah-BAHL), a kingdom in central Anatolia near the old Hittite heartland, that maintained Luwian language and culture and was one of the last Neo-Hittite polities to be absorbed by Assyria. Tabal was not a direct successor of the Hittite royal line (unlike Carchemish), but its location in the Hittite homeland and its use of Luwian hieroglyphs made it a cultural heir.

**Tell Halaf** (also known as Guzana, goo-ZAH-nah), a Neo-Hittite/Aramaean city in northeastern Syria famous for its elaborate palace facade with carved portal animals and relief panels. Tell Halaf represents the fusion of Hittite and Aramaean artistic traditions at their most vivid: massive stone figures in a style that draws on both traditions without being purely either.

These states shared several features. They were small (none controlled more than a few hundred square miles), independent, and frequently in conflict with each other and with their Aramaean neighbors. They operated in a world fundamentally different from the one the Hittite Empire had dominated. The great Bronze Age international system was gone. The trade networks were gone. The Great Kings who had addressed each other as "brother" were gone. In their place was a patchwork of small kingdoms, tribal confederations, and city-states jockeying for position in a region that the great powers had temporarily vacated.

The Aramaeans (air-uh-MEE-uns), whose migration into the Near East during the Bronze Age Collapse had contributed to the empire's downfall, were now the Neo-Hittite states' closest neighbors and most common rivals. Over time, the Aramaean language (which would eventually become the common tongue of the entire Near East, used by Jesus of Nazareth a millennium later) spread into the Neo-Hittite kingdoms, creating bilingual communities where Luwian and Aramaic coexisted. The inscriptions from Sam'al, in both Luwian and Aramaic, are the clearest surviving evidence of this linguistic blending.

The Neo-Hittite states also produced a distinctive tradition of **monumental sculpture** that is one of the most visually striking legacies of the post-imperial Hittite world. Portal lions and sphinxes guarded city gates. Carved stone reliefs depicting royal processions, divine figures, battle scenes, and hunting expeditions decorated palace facades and public spaces. The style blends Hittite artistic conventions (the gods with horned caps, the lions at the gates, the hieroglyphic inscriptions) with Aramaean and increasingly Assyrian elements. When the Neo-Assyrian Empire conquered these states, Assyrian artists borrowed from the Neo-Hittite sculptural tradition, creating a chain of artistic influence that ran from the Hattian sun discs of Alaca Höyük through the Hittite lions of Hattusa through the Neo-Hittite portal sculptures to the massive winged bulls of the Assyrian palaces at Nimrud and Nineveh (Assyrian Empire Ch 4).

---

The end of the Neo-Hittite states came at the hands of the **Neo-Assyrian Empire**, the most powerful military force the ancient world had yet seen (Assyrian Empire Ch 4-7). Through the 8th century BCE, Assyrian kings systematically conquered the small kingdoms of Syria and southeastern Anatolia, incorporating them into the Assyrian provincial system. **Carchemish fell in 717 BCE** to Sargon II of Assyria (Assyrian Empire Ch 5). **Sam'al** was absorbed around 713 BCE. **Tabal** fell in the same period. By 700 BCE, five centuries of Hittite cultural continuity had ended. The Neo-Hittite kingdoms became Assyrian provinces, their royal families were deported or eliminated, and their distinct cultural traditions were absorbed into the Assyrian imperial culture.

The last traces of anything recognizably "Hittite" disappeared into the Assyrian administrative system. The name "Hatti" survived in Assyrian and Babylonian texts as a geographical term for Syria and the Levant, a ghost of the once-great empire whose memory lingered in place names even after its substance was gone. The Hebrew Bible mentions the **"Hittites"** (or "children of Heth") as one of the peoples living in Canaan during the time of Abraham, though the relationship between these biblical Hittites and the Anatolian empire is debated — they may have been a local Canaanite group, or they may reflect a distant memory of the Neo-Hittite states that had bordered the Israelite kingdoms.

And then silence. For the next three thousand years, the Hittite Empire was forgotten.

---

The rediscovery of the Hittites is one of the great detective stories of archaeology, and it unfolded in stages over more than a century.

The first clues came from the Neo-Hittite sites, not from Hattusa itself. In the 1810s and 1820s, European travelers in Syria and Anatolia began reporting carved stone reliefs and inscriptions in an unknown hieroglyphic script. The Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt (the same man who rediscovered the ancient city of Petra in Jordan) noted inscribed stones at the site of Hama in Syria in 1812. In the 1870s, the Irish missionary William Wright and the American consul Augustus Johnson recovered carved stone blocks from Hama and shipped them to museums in London and Istanbul. The hieroglyphs on these stones matched inscriptions found at other sites across Syria and southeastern Turkey, but nobody could read them.

Simultaneously, archaeologists were beginning to explore Anatolia. In 1834, a French explorer named Charles Texier visited a ruined site near the Turkish village of Boğazköy (boh-AZ-kuh-ee) and found massive stone walls, carved gate lions, and the remains of what was clearly a major ancient city. Texier thought he had found a Median city. He had actually found Hattusa, but he had no way of knowing it.

The breakthrough came from an unexpected direction. In 1887, a peasant woman digging in her garden at **Tell el-Amarna** in Egypt (the site of Akhenaten's capital) found a cache of cuneiform tablets that turned out to be the diplomatic archive of the Egyptian court, the Amarna Letters (Chapter 3). Among these letters were correspondence from a king of "Hatti" — a powerful ruler who addressed the pharaoh as "brother" and demanded to be treated as an equal. Suddenly, the scattered references to "Hatti" in Egyptian and Assyrian texts clicked into place. There had been a great empire called Hatti, based somewhere in Anatolia, that had rivaled Egypt during the Late Bronze Age. The problem was that nobody knew where Hatti was or who the Hattians had been.

In 1893, the French archaeologist **Ernest Chantre** conducted brief excavations at Boğazköy and found cuneiform tablets. In 1906, the German archaeologist **Hugo Winckler** (VINK-ler) launched the first systematic excavation of the site and hit the jackpot: thousands of cuneiform tablets, including a copy of the Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty, written in Akkadian, that matched the version already known from the walls of the Temple of Karnak in Egypt. The discovery proved beyond doubt that Boğazköy was Hattusa, the capital of the empire that the Egyptians and Assyrians had called "Hatti." The forgotten empire had been found.

But finding the city and reading the tablets were two different problems. The tablets from Hattusa were written in at least eight languages, and while the Akkadian texts (used for international correspondence) could be read immediately, the bulk of the archive was in an unknown language written in cuneiform. Scholars could read the cuneiform signs (because the signs were the same ones used in Mesopotamia), but the words they spelled were in a language nobody recognized.

---

The decipherment came in 1915, during the First World War, from a Czech linguist named **Bedřich Hrozný** (BED-rzhikh HROZ-nee). Working in Vienna with photographs of Hattusa tablets, Hrozný was puzzling over a sentence that included the word *nu NINDA-an ezzatteni*, where NINDA was the Sumerian logogram for "bread" (a known quantity, since the Hittites used Sumerian logograms in their cuneiform just as the Mesopotamians did). Hrozný reasoned that if the sentence was about bread, the verb *ezzatteni* might mean "to eat." And *ezzatteni* looked suspiciously like the Latin word *edere* ("to eat") and the Old English word *etan* (same meaning, ancestor of the modern English word "eat"). Another word in the text, *watar*, looked like the English word "water" and the German word *Wasser*.

The resemblance was not coincidence. Hrozný had identified an Indo-European language, the oldest one ever found in written form. His publication of the decipherment in 1915 electrified the scholarly world (or would have, if the scholarly world had not been preoccupied with a war at the time). The Hittite language was subsequently classified as belonging to the Anatolian branch of Indo-European, making the Hittites the earliest known Indo-European-speaking civilization.

The decipherment transformed the study of the ancient Near East. Suddenly, the thousands of tablets from Hattusa could be read: laws, treaties, rituals, prayers, myths, royal annals, diplomatic correspondence, horse training manuals, medical texts, and the Plague Prayers of Mursili II (Chapter 3). A civilization that had been completely unknown in 1900 became one of the best-documented cultures of the Bronze Age by 1950. The Hittites had left behind an archive of over 30,000 cuneiform tablets covering nearly every aspect of their civilization, and it had been sitting in the ground, waiting to be found, for more than three thousand years.

The parallel to other great rediscoveries is striking. Arthur Evans's excavation of Knossos and reconstruction of the Minoan civilization (Minoan Civilization Ch 8) happened in the same decades as the Hittite rediscovery. Heinrich Schliemann's excavation of Troy and Mycenae had happened a generation earlier. The late 19th and early 20th centuries were the golden age of Near Eastern and Mediterranean archaeology, a period when entire civilizations were being pulled from the ground and inserted into the historical record for the first time. But the Hittite rediscovery stands out even in this context because of the scale of what was found. The Minoans left behind palaces and frescoes but no readable texts (Linear A remains undeciphered; Minoan Civilization Ch 8). The Hittites left behind palaces, art, and an archive of 30,000 readable documents. They are the best-documented "lost" civilization in history.

---

What did the Hittites leave behind? Beyond the archive itself, three legacies deserve mention.

First, the Hittites demonstrated that an empire could be built on diplomacy as much as force. The vassal treaty system, the Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty, the Tawananna institution, the law code's emphasis on restitution, the thousand-god pantheon's absorption of foreign deities — all of these point to a civilization that understood management, negotiation, and inclusion as tools of governance. The Hittites could be brutal in war (the sack of Babylon proved that), but their preferred mode of empire-building was the treaty, the alliance, and the managed integration of conquered peoples. The later Persian Empire, which would adopt and vastly expand this approach (Persian Empire Ch 1-2), was following a model that the Hittites had pioneered.

Second, the Hittites were the crucial link in the chain of cultural transmission between Mesopotamia and the Aegean. The Song of Kumarbi connects Mesopotamian theogony to Hesiod. The Luwian hieroglyphic script preserves a writing tradition that is neither cuneiform nor alphabetic. The chariot technology, the diplomatic conventions, the legal traditions, the religious practices that the Hittites absorbed from the east and filtered toward the west made Anatolia the bridge between the world's oldest civilizations and the Mediterranean cultures that would eventually produce classical Greece and Rome.

Third, the Hittite collapse is a permanent warning about the fragility of civilization. The Hittites were not a minor polity that overreached. They were a superpower with a functioning army, a sophisticated bureaucracy, a vast archive of knowledge, and diplomatic relationships with every other great power of their age. They had been dominant for centuries. And they vanished so completely that their very existence was forgotten. The Bronze Age Collapse that destroyed them was not caused by a single enemy or a single disaster but by the failure of the system itself, a cascade of interconnected crises that no single actor could control. In a world of interconnected economies, climate stress, supply-chain disruption, and political fragmentation, the Hittite experience has an uncomfortable resonance.

The Hittite Empire collapsed around 1178 BCE. The land of Anatolia did not remain empty. Over the following millennia, it passed through the hands of the Phrygians, the Lydians, the Persians, the Greeks, the Romans, the Byzantines, and finally the Ottoman Turks, who conquered Constantinople in 1453 and made Anatolia the heartland of an empire that would last until 1922. The Anatolian Succession chain stretches from the Hittites to the Ottomans, and though the connections between them are not direct (the Hittites left no descendants who claimed their name), the land itself is the through-line: the same crossroads, the same mountains, the same strategic position between Europe and Asia, the same mineral wealth, the same challenge of governing a vast territory from a central plateau.

The Hittites would not have recognized the world the Ottomans built. But they would have recognized the terrain. And they would have understood, perhaps better than anyone, that even the most powerful civilizations can be forgotten.

A copy of their peace treaty hangs in the United Nations. It is worth remembering what it represents: not just the oldest peace agreement in history, but the last great achievement of a civilization that the world would forget for three thousand years, brought back from silence by archaeologists, linguists, and a Czech scholar who noticed that the Hittite word for "water" sounded like the English one. The Hittites are proof that history is not only what we remember. It is also what we rediscover.

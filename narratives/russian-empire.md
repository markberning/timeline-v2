# The Russian Empire — The Long Fuse to 1917

**Goal:** Narrative for the Russian Empire, from the boy-tsar Peter dodging a palace massacre in 1682 to the last Romanov scrawling his abdication in a railway carriage in 1917. Two and a third centuries in which one family hauled the largest land empire on earth into the modern world at terrifying speed, built a capital on a frozen swamp, defeated Napoleon, produced Tolstoy and Dostoevsky and the periodic table, expanded across a sixth of the planet's land surface, and did all of it on the backs of tens of millions of human beings who owned nothing, including themselves. The central thesis runs through every chapter like a buried cable: the Russian Empire was a contraption that could generate dazzling grandeur at the top only by keeping a vast unfreedom at the bottom, and the two could not coexist forever. This is the story of a state that kept choosing splendor over reform until the choice was taken away from it, and of the long fuse, lit somewhere around 1825 and burning all the way to February 1917, that finally reached the powder.

# Chapter 1 — The Size of the Problem

Start with a number that does not behave like a number.

In 1914, on the eve of the catastrophe that ends this book, the Russian Empire covered about 8.6 million square miles (22.4 million square kilometers). That is roughly a sixth of all the land on the planet. It is more than twice the size of the entire United States including Alaska. If you stood on the empire's western edge in Poland and started walking east at a steady pace, you would cross eleven of the world's twenty-four time zones before you reached the Pacific, and it would take you the better part of a year. A telegram from St Petersburg to the Pacific port of Vladivostok in the 1890s, before the railway, could take longer to be acted upon than a letter from London to Boston. This is not a colorful detail. It is the central engineering problem of the entire narrative, and almost everything that follows is, in one way or another, an answer to the question: how do you govern *that*, with the technology of 1700, or 1800, and a population most of whom cannot read?

The answer the Russian state arrived at, and clung to with a desperation that finally killed it, was a particular machine. It had three load-bearing parts, and you need all three in your head before Peter is even born, because he inherits them and the empire dies still trying to run on them.

The first part is **autocracy** (from Greek, "self-rule," meaning rule by one person answerable to no law, no parliament, and no one). The Russian ruler was not a king in the western European sense, hemmed in by nobles with charters and assemblies that voted taxes. The Russian ruler was the **tsar** (TSAR, from "Caesar," the title the rulers of Moscow had adopted to claim the inheritance of the Roman emperors after Constantinople fell to the Ottomans in 1453). Tsarist autocracy was not a tendency or a style. It was a formal doctrine, written into law, that the tsar's will was the only source of legitimate authority in the state, and that any limitation on it was a kind of blasphemy. Western Europe had spent the late Middle Ages growing institutions that could say *no* to a monarch (Parliament in England, the Estates in France, town charters everywhere, the subject of the European narratives). Russia had grown almost none. When this narrative reaches 1906 and Russia finally gets something resembling a parliament, it will be 600 years after England got one, and it will arrive too late to matter.

The second part is **Orthodoxy**. Russia was a Christian country, but it belonged to the Eastern Orthodox Church, the branch of Christianity that split from the Catholic (Roman) West in 1054 and looked to Constantinople, not Rome, for its model. After Constantinople fell to the Muslim Ottomans in 1453 (the event told from the conqueror's side in the Ottoman Empire narrative), Russian churchmen developed the idea that Moscow was the "Third Rome," the last surviving seat of true Christianity, with a sacred duty to preserve it unchanged. The claim had a dynastic prop: in 1472 the Moscow grand prince Ivan III had married Zoe (Sophia) Palaiologina, a niece of the last Byzantine emperor, so Moscow could argue it had inherited Byzantium's imperial legitimacy by blood as well as by faith just as the Ottomans took its capital. The practical effect, for our purposes, was that the Russian church was not a rival power center that could check the tsar (as the Catholic papacy repeatedly checked western kings). It was, increasingly, an arm of the state that sanctified the tsar's authority as God-given. Hold that; in Chapter 4 Peter abolishes the church's leadership outright and runs it as a government department, and almost nobody can stop him, because the institution that might have had no independent ground to stand on.

The third part, and the one this entire book is really about, is **serfdom**. By 1649, a legal code called the *Ulozhenie* (oo-loh-ZHEN-yeh) had finished a long process of binding the Russian peasant to the land he worked and to the lord who owned that land. A **serf** was not technically a slave in the way a person on an American plantation was chattel property with no legal personhood whatsoever. The distinction is real but, for the human being living it, thin. A serf could not leave the estate without permission. He could be bought, sold, mortgaged, gambled away, and (in practice though the law grew coy about it) sold apart from his family. He owed the lord either heavy labor on the lord's own fields (*barshchina*, bar-SHCHEE-nah) or heavy payments in cash and kind (*obrok*, ob-ROHK), often both. He could be flogged at the lord's discretion and conscripted into the army for what amounted to a life sentence. By the early nineteenth century, somewhere around 23 million people, more than a third of the entire population of the empire, lived in this condition, and roughly as many again were "state peasants" bound to the crown in a milder version of the same. To put 23 million in an American frame: it is more than five times the roughly four million people enslaved in the American South on the eve of its civil war, and that war is the bloodiest event in United States history. Russia carried a bound population on that scale, white and Orthodox and indistinguishable by sight from the people who owned them, for two full centuries, and the question of what to do about it is the unexploded ordnance under every chapter of this story.

So here is the thesis, stated once, plainly, so you can watch it operate for the next nineteen chapters and decide for yourself whether it holds. The Russian Empire was a machine for converting the unfreedom of the many into the grandeur of the few, and it was extraordinarily good at the grandeur. It built St Petersburg, one of the most beautiful cities ever made, on a swamp where tens of thousands of conscripted laborers died in the building of it. It produced, in a single century, *War and Peace*, *The Brothers Karamazov*, the periodic table of the elements, and the largest empire on earth, while the people who grew the food that fed all of it could be flogged for leaving the village. The grandeur was real. The unfreedom was real. The wager of the Russian state, made over and over by tsar after tsar, was that you could keep the first without ever fully surrendering the second. The fuse on that wager was lit, as best we can date it, on a December morning in 1825, and it burned for ninety-two years.

Now the caveats, because this is a book that would rather tell you what it doesn't know than pretend.

On measurement: this is a US-based reading app, so distances come in miles first, kilometers in parentheses. We will anchor the genuinely alien magnitudes (the size of the empire, the length of the Trans-Siberian Railway, the body counts of the famines and the wars) in things an American reader can feel, and we will not insult you by anchoring a mile.

On certainty: Russian imperial history has the opposite problem from ancient history. It is not that the sources are too few. For the later period they are an avalanche: state archives, censuses, the greatest novels ever written, the memoirs of revolutionaries, the diaries of the last tsar. The problem is that almost every important event in this story has been fought over by people who needed it to mean something. Soviet historians for seventy years were required to read the entire empire as a doomed tyranny inevitably producing the glorious revolution of 1917. Émigré and Western Cold War historians often read the same events as a basically reformable society derailed by fanatics and bad luck. Post-Soviet Russian historians have swung between a nostalgic rehabilitation of the tsars and a continued reckoning. When this narrative says "this is debated," it means it, and it will tell you which way the wind is currently blowing. A specific warning the reader should keep loaded the whole way through: some of the most *vivid* stories in Russian history, the ones everybody knows, are the least reliable. Peter the Great did not necessarily personally swing the axe on the streltsy. Catherine the Great did not die the way the cruelest joke about her says she did. Rasputin's powers, Anastasia's escape, the precise number of Napoleon's men who froze on the retreat: the more cinematic the claim, the harder we will press it.

One last bit of scene-setting: where Peter is standing when this story opens, and what he is standing on.

The state Peter inherits is not yet the Russian Empire. It is **Muscovy**, the Grand Duchy of Moscow, the principality that over roughly two centuries had swallowed its neighbors and thrown off the overlordship of the Mongol successor states (the "Tatar yoke," the long domination by the Golden Horde and its heirs, told from the steppe side in the Mongol Empire narrative). Muscovy in 1672 is large, cold, landlocked in every direction that matters, and, by the standard of the Dutch Republic or France or even Sweden, technologically and administratively a century or more behind. It has no warm-water port, no real navy, almost no modern manufacturing, a tiny educated class, and an army that is partly a medieval cavalry of service nobles and partly the **streltsy** (STREL-tsee, "the shooters"), hereditary musketeer regiments based in Moscow who double as a fractious urban militia and who are about to put a seven-year-old boy through the most formative trauma of his life. The Muscovite tsar is already an autocrat in doctrine. The church is already a near-arm of the state. The peasant is already, by 1649, legally bound, the end of a process whose legal ancestors reach back to the *kholopy* (slaves) and tribute-owing peasants of Kievan Rus', the medieval Slavic state out of which Moscow grew, told in its own narrative. The three load-bearing parts are all in place. What is not yet in place is the grandeur. The boy who will supply it, with an energy and brutality that has few parallels in the history of any country, is born in 1672, and in 1682, at the age of nine, he watches men he knows get torn apart on the spears of the streltsy in the Kremlin. We open there.

# Chapter 2 — The Boy Who Saw the Spears

The man this whole book turns on was born **Pyotr Alekseyevich Romanov** (PYOH-ter al-ek-SAY-yeh-vich), on 9 June 1672, in Moscow. English flattens Pyotr to Peter, and he becomes, before the end, **Peter the Great**, but in 1672 he is just the fourteenth child of Tsar Alexei (a-LEK-say) Mikhailovich and the first child of Alexei's second wife, Natalya Naryshkina (na-RISH-kee-na). The number of children and the two wives matter enormously, because they set up the murder Peter is about to witness.

When Tsar Alexei died in 1676, the throne went to Peter's much older half-brother **Fyodor III** (FYOH-der), a sickly, learned young man who reigned six years and died in 1682 at twenty, childless. That left two candidates from Alexei's two marriages: **Ivan**, the surviving son of the first wife, fifteen years old, half-blind, with a serious speech impediment and what the sources describe as some degree of intellectual disability, and **Peter**, the son of the second wife, nine years old, healthy, and obviously bright. Behind the two boys stood two clans: the **Miloslavskys** (mee-loh-SLAHV-skee), the family of the first wife, backing Ivan, and the **Naryshkins**, Peter's mother's people, backing Peter. This is the Russian court's recurring disease, and you will see it again and again: with no fixed, ironclad rule of succession, every transfer of the crown becomes a faction fight, and the faction that controls the capital's soldiers wins. Right now the capital's soldiers are the streltsy.

A council of churchmen and boyars (BOH-yarz, the old hereditary aristocracy of Muscovy) chose the nine-year-old Peter over the disabled Ivan. The Miloslavsky clan, frozen out, had one extraordinary asset: **Sophia Alekseyevna** (so-FEE-ya), Ivan's full sister, Peter's half-sister, a clever, ambitious, formidably educated woman of twenty-five at a time when royal women were expected to be invisible. Sophia and her allies spread a rumor through the streltsy barracks that the Naryshkins had murdered Ivan. The streltsy, already furious over unpaid wages and abusive colonels, did not need much. In May 1682 they stormed the Kremlin (the fortified palace-and-cathedral citadel at the heart of Moscow, the seat of Russian power then and now).

What the nine-year-old Peter saw over the next three days is the single most important psychological fact in this narrative, so it is worth being precise about what we can and cannot say. The traditional account, which comes substantially from later memoirs and from a foreign eyewitness or two and has been polished by every biographer since, is that Peter was made to stand on the Red Staircase of the palace beside his mother while the streltsy demanded their victims, and that several men were thrown down onto the upraised pikes of the soldiers below and hacked apart in front of him, including one of his Naryshkin uncles and the boyar Artamon Matveyev, who had been his mother's guardian and was killed almost at the child's feet. The broad outline (a multi-day massacre in the Kremlin, leading Naryshkins butchered, the boy present for at least some of it) is solidly attested. The most cinematic specifics (exactly what Peter saw, his facial expression, the lifelong facial tic and convulsions later attributed to this moment) are the kind of detail that hardens in the retelling, and a careful reader should hold them loosely while accepting the core: a nine-year-old watched his family's men murdered by the state's own soldiers and was powerless. Whatever the exact mechanism, the man who emerges from this never trusts Moscow, never trusts the streltsy, and carries a hatred of the old Kremlin world that will, twenty-one years later, lead him to build an entirely new capital 400 miles (640 km) away on an empty marsh just to get out of this one.

The settlement after the 1682 revolt was bizarre even by Russian standards. To satisfy everyone, both boys were crowned: **Ivan V** and **Peter I**, joint tsars, a dual monarchy with a specially made double throne (it survives in the Kremlin Armory). Ivan was the senior in title and incapable in fact; Peter was junior and a child. Real power went to the regent: **Sophia**, ruling in the brothers' names from 1682, the first woman to hold power in Russia in her own right rather than as a queen consort, which the conservative Muscovite establishment found close to scandalous. Sophia's regency (1682–1689) was not incompetent. Her chief minister and probable lover, Prince Vasily Golitsyn (ga-LEET-sin), was a westernizing intellectual who negotiated a "Treaty of Eternal Peace" with Poland and signed Russia's first treaty with China (the 1689 Treaty of Nerchinsk, which fixed the far-eastern border and is one of the earliest treaties any European-style state signed with the Qing on something like equal terms). But Golitsyn also led two disastrous military expeditions against the Crimean Tatars, the Muslim khanate on the Black Sea steppe whose horsemen rode deep into Russian and Ukrainian farmland to seize captives, tens of thousands a year in bad decades, and sell them through the slave markets of Crimea into the Ottoman world, and the failures weakened Sophia at exactly the moment Peter was growing up.

And how Peter grew up is the other half of this chapter. Sophia kept him and his mother out of the way, in the royal village of Preobrazhenskoye (pre-ah-bra-ZHEN-sko-yeh) outside Moscow. This was meant to neutralize him. It did the opposite. Left almost entirely unsupervised by the formal Kremlin world he hated anyway, the teenage Peter educated himself by obsession. He raised two companies of "play soldiers" from stable boys and young nobles, drilled them with real weapons and real (occasionally fatal) mock battles, and these "play regiments," the **Preobrazhensky** and **Semyonovsky**, grew into the first units of a new model army and would become the elite Guards regiments that, for the next century, make and unmake tsars. He fell in with the foreigners of the **German Quarter** (the *Nemetskaya Sloboda*, the walled suburb where Moscow's Dutch, German, Scottish, and English merchants, soldiers, and engineers were required to live, "German" being the Russian catch-all for "western foreigner," literally "the mute ones," people who couldn't speak Russian). There he met the Swiss adventurer Franz Lefort and the dour Scottish general Patrick Gordon, drank enormously, learned shipbuilding from a Dutch sailor, found a derelict English sailboat in a barn and taught himself to tack against the wind on a pond, and conceived the two lifelong fixations that organize his entire reign: ships, and the West. A Russian tsar who could sail, swing an axe, set a bone, pull a tooth, and turn wood on a lathe, and who learned almost none of it in a palace, is not normal, and Russia is about to find out exactly how not normal.

The dual monarchy resolved itself in 1689. Peter, now seventeen, married and theoretically of age, came into open conflict with Sophia over whether she would keep ruling. Sophia tried to rally the streltsy again; this time it failed. Warned of a possible move against him, Peter fled in the night to the fortified **Trinity Monastery**, the great pilgrimage church-fortress about 45 miles (70 km) northeast of Moscow, with stone walls thick enough to stand a siege, in a famous scene of a half-dressed tsar galloping to safety that is reported consistently enough to trust in outline. From there, with the loyalty of the foreign officers and his play regiments, he outlasted Sophia. Her support melted. She was forced into the Novodevichy Convent in Moscow; Golitsyn was exiled to the Arctic. Ivan V remained co-tsar in name, harmless and increasingly unwell, until his death in 1696, after which Peter ruled alone. He was twenty-four. He had a country the size of a continent, no warm-water port, no navy worth the name, an army half-medieval, a treasury that strained to pay it, and an idea, formed in a German suburb and on a leaky boat, of what Russia ought to become. The first thing he wanted was the sea.

The early try was the **Azov campaigns** (1695–1696). Azov (a-ZOFF) was a Turkish fortress at the mouth of the Don river, controlling access from Russia's south to the Sea of Azov and beyond it the Black Sea. Peter's first assault in 1695 failed, badly and instructively, because he had no ships to blockade the fortress from the sea while he besieged it from land, so the Ottomans simply resupplied it by water. Peter's response prefigures his whole method: over a single frantic winter he built a fleet, more or less from nothing, hauling shipwrights and timber to the Don, and in 1696 took Azov. It was a small victory over a frontier fort, but it taught Peter two permanent lessons. First, sea power was not optional. Second, to get it he needed western knowledge, western specialists, and western money, and he was not going to get those by reading about them. He was going to go and see. In 1697 the tsar of Russia did something no Russian ruler had ever done. He left the country, in disguise, to learn a trade.

# Chapter 3 — The Window

In March 1697, an enormous Russian diplomatic mission of about 250 people set out for Western Europe. It was called the **Grand Embassy**. Its official purpose was to build a coalition against the Ottoman Turks. Its real purpose, only thinly hidden, was that the tsar of Russia wanted to go to the West and learn how it worked with his own hands, and he could not do that as a tsar. So Peter went along incognito, traveling as a junior member of the delegation under the name "Peter Mikhailov." The disguise fooled no one (he was six feet seven inches tall, which in 1697 made him a giant nearly everywhere he went, and his presence was an open secret in every court). But the fiction let him do what no reigning monarch could: skip the ceremony and go work in a shipyard.

He did exactly that. In the Dutch Republic, the most advanced commercial and maritime society on earth at the time, Peter spent a brief, frustrated stint at the Zaandam shipyards and then four months at the great yards of the Dutch East India Company in Amsterdam, working as a common carpenter, axe in hand, learning hull construction. He moved on to England, where he studied the more theoretical English approach to ship design, met with Quaker leaders, reportedly looked in on a session of Parliament (an institution Russia had no equivalent of and would not for two centuries; England's dated to 1265, so Russia was already more than four hundred years behind the model Peter was inspecting, and would be six hundred behind it by the time it finally got a parliament of its own in 1906), and toured the Royal Society, the Mint (where Isaac Newton was then Warden), and the Greenwich Observatory. He recruited, by the boatload, the human capital Russia lacked: shipwrights, naval officers, engineers, mathematicians, surgeons, gunners, several hundred foreign specialists hired and shipped east. The Grand Embassy is the moment the West stops being something Russian rulers occasionally borrow from and becomes a program Russia is going to be force-marched through. It is also, for the thesis of this book, the founding act: Peter wants European technique at the top with a ferocity that never wavers, and he is entirely uninterested in importing the European thing underneath the technique, the slow growth of institutions and law and limits on the ruler. He wants the cannon, not the parliament.

The Embassy was cut short in 1698 by news from home: the streltsy had revolted again. Peter raced back. The revolt had already been put down by his generals before he arrived, but his response is one of the defining and most contested episodes of his reign, so handle it carefully. Peter ordered a mass investigation under torture and then a mass execution. Over the autumn of 1698, more than a thousand streltsy were executed in and around Moscow, many hanged in long rows, some beheaded, the bodies left on display for months as a deliberate terror. The streltsy as a force were effectively destroyed. Now the contested part. There is a persistent, vivid tradition that Peter personally took up the axe and beheaded streltsy with his own hands, and ordered his courtiers and nobles to do the same to bind them to him in blood. This story comes substantially from the diary of an Austrian diplomat, Johann Korb, who was in Moscow at the time but was not an eyewitness to the executions themselves and was relaying secondhand court gossip in a work the Russian government later found so offensive it tried to suppress it. Some serious historians accept that Peter may have wielded the axe; others regard the specific image of the tsar as executioner as exactly the kind of atrocity-detail that hostile foreign reporting inflates. What is not in doubt, and is the part that matters: Peter ordered and stage-managed a mass political terror against the soldiers who had traumatized him as a child, and he did it with a cold thoroughness that announced what kind of ruler Russia now had. He also, on his return, personally cut off the beards of his leading boyars at a reception, the first shot in a culture war we will get to in Chapter 4.

Then came the war that made the empire. To understand it you need the strategic problem. Russia in 1700 was sealed off from the Baltic Sea, and therefore from the easy maritime trade routes to Western Europe, by **Sweden**. This is jarring to a modern reader who thinks of Sweden as a quiet northern country, but in 1700 the Swedish Empire was the dominant military power of northern Europe, controlling Finland, the eastern Baltic coast (modern Estonia and Latvia), and territory all around the sea, with a small, superbly trained army and, on its throne, a teenage military prodigy, **Charles XII**, who would turn out to be one of the most brilliant and reckless commanders of the age. Peter formed a coalition with Denmark and Saxony-Poland to carve up the Swedish Baltic holdings, and in 1700 declared war. This was the **Great Northern War**, and it ran for twenty-one years.

It began as a humiliation. Late in 1700, Peter besieged the Swedish fortress of **Narva** on the Baltic. Charles XII, eighteen years old, marched a much smaller Swedish army up to the Russian siege lines and attacked at once under cover of a blinding snowstorm blowing into the Russians' faces, drove dense assault columns straight through the over-extended Russian line before it could react, and broke the besieging army in a single short winter afternoon, its foreign officers surrendering and its raw conscripts stampeding for a collapsing bridge. The **Battle of Narva** was a catastrophe that confirmed every Western prejudice about Russian backwardness. But Charles then made the decision that loses the war and, eventually, his life: instead of finishing Russia off, he turned south to deal with Saxony-Poland, the enemy he considered more serious, and left Peter alone for six years. Peter used every day of it. He rebuilt the army from the ground up on the European model, melting down church bells for cannon when he ran short of bronze, drilling regular infantry, and grinding away at the Swedish Baltic garrisons while Charles was busy elsewhere. By the time Charles turned back to deal with Russia, it was a different Russia.

And in the middle of this war, in 1703, on a set of cold, flat, flood-prone islands in the marshy delta where the Neva river meets the Baltic, on land he had just taken from Sweden and did not yet securely hold, Peter founded a city. **St Petersburg.** The decision is, on its face, insane. The site was a malarial swamp, frozen much of the year, flooded much of the rest, on the empire's exposed northwestern frontier, far from the population and resources of the country. Peter built his capital there anyway, and the reasons are the thesis of this book in architectural form. He wanted a port on the Baltic, a "window to Europe," a Russian city that faced west and looked nothing like Moscow. He wanted to be physically out of Moscow, the city of the Kremlin and the streltsy and the priests and the beards, the whole world of 1682. So he conscripted labor on a colossal scale: tens of thousands of peasants and Swedish prisoners and soldiers were marched in to drive piles into the bog, dig canals, and die. The death toll is one of those famous numbers the reader should hold with tongs. Contemporary and later accounts threw around figures up to 100,000 dead "built on bones," and the phrase "the city built on bones" became proverbial. Modern historians regard the highest figures as unverifiable and probably inflated, while accepting that the human cost of building St Petersburg in the early years was severe, with deaths from disease, exposure, and exhaustion certainly in the many thousands. The exact arithmetic is lost; the moral of it is not. The single most beautiful city the Russian Empire ever produced was raised, deliberately, on a swamp, by forced labor, at a cost in lives nobody bothered to count. Peter began his own palace complexes there almost at once; the great pleasure-palace of **Peterhof**, with its gravity-fed fountains and gilded statuary meant to answer Versailles, was begun in this period and finished by his successors, and so was the cathedral that would hold the tombs of the emperors. The grandeur and the bones arrived together, in the same decade, in the same place. They always do, in this story.

The war turned in 1709. Charles XII, having forced his way through Poland, invaded Russia itself, aiming at Moscow, then swung south into Ukraine hoping for support from the **Cossacks** (KOSS-aks, the semi-autonomous warrior communities of the southern steppe frontier, whom we will meet repeatedly) under their elected leader, the hetman Ivan Mazepa, a long-trusted ally of Peter who, seeing Charles ascendant and Cossack autonomy shrinking under Russian centralization, gambled on Sweden for an independent Ukraine. He brought far fewer Cossacks than he had promised, lost the bet at Poltava, and died in Ottoman exile a few months later; in the Russian state's official memory he became the archetypal traitor, anathematized by the church for two centuries. Peter applied the strategy Russia applies to every great invader for the next two and a half centuries, the strategy that will beat Napoleon in Chapter 10 and Hitler outside this book entirely: trade space for time, scorch the earth, let the distance and the winter do the work, and force the enemy to fight far from home, starving, in the cold. Charles's army, weakened, supply-starved, and far smaller than it had been, finally gave battle at **Poltava** (pol-TAH-va) in Ukraine on 8 July 1709. The Russians, now numerous, well-drilled, and well-supplied, destroyed the Swedish army. Charles XII escaped with a handful of men into Ottoman territory; the bulk of his army surrendered days later. **Poltava** is one of the genuine hinge-battles of European history. Sweden's century as a great power ended on that field, and Russia's began. The news of Poltava is the moment the courts of Europe realized a new great power had appeared in the east, and started, with some alarm, treating it as one.

The war dragged on another twelve years, because Charles XII was incapable of admitting defeat and kept fighting from Ottoman exile and then from Sweden until he was killed in 1718 (shot in the head besieging a Norwegian fortress, by whom is still argued; some Swedes have long suspected one of his own men, which is unprovable and probably unprovable forever). Along the way Peter overreached badly in the **Pruth River campaign** of 1711, when he marched south against the Ottomans, got his outnumbered, thirst-stricken army surrounded on the Pruth river in present-day Moldova, and escaped total disaster only because the Ottoman grand vizier, his own troops restless and his supply lines long, preferred a quick advantageous settlement to a battle of annihilation, and let Peter march out in exchange for a humiliating treaty that handed Azov, his very first conquest, back to the Turks (a deal lubricated, by the persistent story, with a large bribe and the jewels of Peter's wife Catherine). It was a sharp lesson that the man who would beat Sweden could still badly mismanage a war, and a useful corrective to the legend of Peter as infallible. But the Baltic was the prize, and the Baltic he won. At sea, the new navy he had willed into existence won its first major fleet victory over the Swedes at **Gangut** (GAN-goot) in 1714, proving the ships were not just for show. Finally, in 1721, Sweden gave up. The **Treaty of Nystad** (NEW-stat) ceded to Russia the eastern Baltic coast, the modern Baltic states and the territory around St Petersburg, a permanent warm-enough-water frontage on the sea, the thing every Russian ruler since had wanted and none had gotten. Russia was now, unarguably, a European great power.

That same year, the Senate and Synod formally offered Peter, and Peter accepted, the titles **Emperor of All Russia** and "the Great" and "Father of the Fatherland." The state stopped being the Tsardom of Muscovy and became, in its own official self-description, the **Russian Empire**. The word matters. Muscovy was a grand duchy that had grown large. The Russian Empire was a self-conscious imperial project on the European model, with a window on the sea, a navy, a European-drilled army, a half-built western capital, and an emperor who had personally learned to caulk a hull. The grandeur was now undeniable. What it cost, and who paid, is the next chapter.

# Chapter 4 — Revolution From Above

Peter the Great is the only ruler in this book who tried to change his country faster than it could be changed, and the wreckage and the achievement of that attempt are still being argued about three centuries later. The standard shorthand, "Peter westernized Russia," is true and useless. What he actually did was attempt, by decree and force, to clamp a modern European-style state onto a society that had not generated any of the things such a state usually grows out of, and to do it inside one lifetime. This chapter is about the machinery he built and the people it ran over, because both halves are the thesis.

Start with the machine, because Peter was, above all, an institution-builder. The old Muscovite government was a tangle of overlapping departments (the *prikazy*) staffed by hereditary clerks and run on personal connection. Peter replaced it, in stages, with a rationalized bureaucratic state copied substantially from Sweden and the German states. He created the **Governing Senate** in 1711 as the supreme administrative and judicial body to run the country, originally while he was away at war and then permanently. He replaced the old departments with **the Colleges**, a set of (eventually twelve) functional ministries, each run by a board rather than a single official, on the Swedish model: a College of War, of the Admiralty, of Foreign Affairs, of state revenue, and so on. He carved the empire into provinces (*gubernii*) with appointed governors to push tax and conscription down into the country. To make sure the new machine actually obeyed him, he created a parallel surveillance system, the *fiskaly* and then the office of the *Procurator-General*, officials whose job was to watch the other officials, the seed of a Russian habit (a state agency that secretly watches the state, and everyone else) that runs straight through this book to the Third Section and the Okhrana.

The single most consequential institution he built was the **Table of Ranks**, promulgated in 1722. This is worth slowing down for, because it reorganized the entire Russian elite for the next two centuries. The Table of Ranks was a grid: fourteen parallel ranks in the military, the civil service, and the court, from the lowest commissioned grade up to field marshal and chancellor. The rule was that status now flowed from *service to the state*, not from birth. A nobleman's son started at the bottom and rose by serving; a commoner who reached a certain rank in the army or bureaucracy was automatically ennobled. In one stroke Peter converted the old hereditary boyar aristocracy from a class that owned its status by blood into a service class that owed its status to the crown. This is the deep structure of the grandeur-and-unfreedom machine. The nobility's privileges, above all the right to own serfs, were tied to its service to the autocrat. The serf served the noble; the noble served the state; the state was the emperor. It was a pyramid of obligation with one man at the top and 23 million at the bottom, and Peter built the part of it that locked the nobility in. Catherine, in Chapter 7, will cut the nobility loose from its end of the bargain while leaving the serfs chained to theirs, and that asymmetry is the moral crack the whole edifice eventually falls into.

To pay for the army and the navy and the wars, Peter needed money the old tax system could not raise, so he changed how Russia was taxed in a way that pressed the bond on the serf tighter. The old tax was assessed by household, which encouraged peasants to cram many people into one taxable "household." Peter replaced it in 1718–1724 with the **soul tax** (the *podushnaya podat*), a flat tax levied on every individual male "soul" (peasant or townsman) recorded in periodic censuses called "revisions." To collect it the state had to count and fix every peasant in place, which deepened serfdom by making the serf, in effect, a permanent tax-unit registered to a place and a master. The soul tax also, for the first time, swept up previously freer categories of rural people into the bound mass. It funded the empire and it tightened the chain in the same act, which is Peter's whole reign in one fiscal instrument.

Now the culture war, the part everyone remembers, and the part it is easy to get wrong by treating it as comic. On returning from the Grand Embassy, Peter set out to make the Russian elite *look* European, on the theory that a service class that looked and lived like the Muscovite past would think like it. He personally sheared the beards of his boyars, then in 1705 instituted the **beard tax**: a Russian man who wished to keep his beard had to pay an annual fee and carry a small bronze token, stamped with a picture of a beard, proving he had paid. This sounds like an eccentric joke. It was not received as one. In Orthodox belief, going back centuries, the beard was close to a religious obligation, the mark of a man made in God's image; shaving it was, for many devout Russians, something near sacrilege. The beard tax, the order that nobles and townsmen wear Western (Hungarian, then German, then French) dress instead of the long Russian caftan, the order that women appear unveiled at the new compulsory mixed social gatherings (*assemblies*) Peter imported, all of it landed on traditional and religious Russia as an assault on the soul of the country by its own ruler. To Peter's enemies, and to the Old Believers (we will get to them), he was not just a reforming tsar. He was, literally, the **Antichrist**, and that belief was widespread and sincere. The culture war was not cosmetic. It opened a fissure, between a Westernized elite that increasingly spoke French and an enserfed mass that did not, that never closed and that Russian writers a century later (Chapter 12) would name as the country's central wound.

He also took on the church directly, and won, in the move that proves how little could check a determined autocrat. The Russian Orthodox Church was led by a **Patriarch**, an office of immense moral authority. When the Patriarch died in 1700, Peter simply did not allow a new one to be elected. He left the office vacant for twenty-one years, and then in 1721 abolished it altogether, replacing the Patriarch with the **Holy Synod**, a committee of churchmen run, in practice, by a lay government official (the Ober-Procurator) appointed by the emperor. The Russian church became, formally and openly, a department of the Russian state, and it stayed one until 1917. No western monarch of the period could have decapitated his national church this casually; the western churches had independent ground to stand on and the institutional habit of saying no. The Russian church did not, for exactly the reasons laid out in Chapter 1, and Peter exposed that with a single administrative stroke. The cost fell hardest on the **Old Believers** (the *raskolniki*), Russians who decades earlier had broken with the official church rather than accept the liturgical reforms the Patriarch Nikon imposed in the 1650s, corrections meant to bring Russian practice back into line with the Greek originals: spelling Jesus's name differently, crossing oneself with three fingers instead of two, processing in a different direction, revising service books. To devout traditionalists these were not small edits but the deliberate corruption of the only true faith, worth dying for, and many did, by the thousands, some by self-immolation. They were now doubly hated as religious dissidents under a state church run by the "Antichrist." They were persecuted, taxed punitively (Old Believer men paid a double soul tax, and there was a tax on their beards too), and driven to the empire's edges, where, ironically, their disciplined, literate, tight-knit communities became some of its most successful merchants and frontier settlers, a pattern of persecuted minorities turned into economic engines that this book will see again with the empire's Jews.

And Peter built the apparatus of knowledge, because a modern state needs trained people and he had almost none. He reformed the calendar, ordering Russia in 1700 to count years from the birth of Christ rather than from the supposed creation of the world and to celebrate the new year on 1 January like the rest of Europe (Russia kept the older Julian calendar itself, which by the twentieth century ran 13 days behind the West, which is why the "October" Revolution of 1917 happened, by the Western calendar, in November, a confusion that haunts every date in the last chapters of this book). He simplified the alphabet into a cleaner **civil script** for secular printing, separating it from the old ecclesiastical letters, which made secular books and the first Russian printed newspaper, *Vedomosti*, cheaper to produce. He founded technical and naval schools, sent young nobles abroad to study on pain of losing their status, and chartered the **Russian Academy of Sciences** in St Petersburg, which opened in 1725 just after his death. The Academy is a perfect emblem of the whole reign: a world-class scientific institution, stocked at first almost entirely with imported German and Swiss scholars because Russia could not yet supply its own, decreed into existence by a state that needed the output and did not have the society underneath it. Within a generation it would have one genuinely Russian colossus in it (Lomonosov, Chapter 5), and within two centuries it would be world-class. The grandeur was being engineered top-down at forced speed, and it worked, and it cost what it cost.

Peter died in February 1725, at fifty-two, in agony from a urinary and bladder disease that had plagued him for years; the much-loved story that he caught his fatal chill wading into the icy Neva to help rescue drowning sailors is the kind of redemptive deathbed scene this book has taught the reader to distrust, and most historians treat it as embellishment on an illness that was already killing him. He left no clear heir, which is itself a Petrine catastrophe: in 1722, having had his own son Alexei tortured and condemned for opposing his reforms (Alexei died in prison under circumstances the regime never honestly explained, one of the darkest and least defensible episodes of the reign), Peter issued a law allowing the reigning emperor to name any successor he chose, and then died without naming one. He had broken the old succession custom and put nothing reliable in its place. The throne of the largest empire on earth was now an open question to be settled by whoever controlled the Guards regiments in the capital, the play-soldiers he had raised in a village forty years before. For the next thirty-seven years, that is exactly how Russia chose its rulers.

# Chapter 5 — The Age of the Guards

The thirty-seven years between Peter the Great's death in 1725 and Catherine the Great's coup in 1762 are usually told as a confusing parade of forgettable rulers, and they are confusing, so this chapter will give you the through-line first and the parade second. The through-line is this: Peter destroyed the old rule of succession and replaced it with nothing, and into that vacuum stepped the only institution in the capital with both the weapons and the proximity to decide things, the **Guards** (the Preobrazhensky and Semyonovsky regiments Peter had raised as a boy, now the elite household troops, stationed in St Petersburg, recruited heavily from the nobility, and acutely aware that they made and unmade emperors). For four decades, Russian successions are settled by which faction the Guards back at three in the morning. This is the period that proves the autocracy had a hidden dependency: the autocrat was absolute, but the autocrat was chosen by a few thousand soldiers, and everyone in the elite knew it. It is also the period in which the nobility, the service class Peter had chained to the state, methodically negotiates its chain looser while the serf's stays exactly where it is.

The parade, briskly, with each ruler tagged so you can keep them straight.

**Catherine I** (reigned 1725–1727), not to be confused with the far more famous Catherine the Great who comes later. This Catherine was Peter's second wife, born a servant of obscure Baltic peasant origin who was taken when Russian troops overran her town in the Great Northern War, passed through the households of a Russian field marshal and then Menshikov, and became Peter's mistress and finally his wife and crowned empress, one of the more improbable rags-to-throne climbs in European history. With the backing of Peter's chief lieutenant **Menshikov** (MEN-shih-koff) and the Guards, she was installed as ruler in her own right after Peter's death, the first woman to rule the empire. She was not a stateswoman; Menshikov, a man who had risen from selling pies in the street to prince by sheer ability and corruption, effectively ran the country, and a new top body, the Supreme Privy Council, ran it with him.

**Peter II** (reigned 1727–1730), Peter the Great's grandson, the son of the tortured Alexei, a boy of eleven at accession. Menshikov tried to control him by betrothing him to his own daughter; the rival Dolgoruky clan outmaneuvered him, and **Menshikov fell** spectacularly, stripped of everything and exiled to Siberia, where he died, a textbook demonstration of how vertiginous the climb and the fall could be in this system. The boy-emperor moved the court back to Moscow, a small symbolic victory for the old anti-Petrine party, and then died of smallpox at fourteen, ending the direct male line of the Romanovs. With him the dynasty's succession became, for the rest of this book, a matter of cousins, in-laws, and Germans.

**Anna** (reigned 1730–1740), Peter the Great's niece, Duchess of the Baltic backwater of Courland. Her accession is the single most interesting constitutional moment in the entire eighteenth century and almost nobody knows it, so slow down. The Supreme Privy Council, dominated by great aristocratic families, offered Anna the throne only if she signed a set of written **"Conditions"** sharply limiting her power: no declaring war, no spending state money, no marrying or naming an heir, no executing nobles, without the Council's consent. For a few weeks in early 1730 it looked as though Russia might get a constitutional, oligarchic monarchy a full 175 years before it got the Duma. Anna signed. Then she discovered that the broader nobility and, crucially, the Guards detested the idea of being ruled by a handful of magnate families more than they feared an autocrat, and within weeks she publicly tore the Conditions up and restored full autocracy to roar of approval from the Guards. This is the autocracy's clearest near-death-and-recovery before 1905, and the lesson the regime drew from it, that the broad gentry would always back an autocrat over an aristocratic oligarchy, shaped Russian politics for two centuries. Anna's reign is also remembered, fairly or not, for the **Bironovshchina** (bee-ron-OFF-shchee-na), the supposed reign of terror and German favoritism named after her Baltic German favorite Ernst Biron. Modern historians think later Russian nationalist writers exaggerated the "German yoke" framing, but the resentment of foreign favorites at court was real and recurs.

Then a brief, grim interlude: **Ivan VI**, an infant great-grandnephew of Peter, proclaimed emperor in 1740 at two months old under a regency, deposed at fifteen months in 1741, and then, in one of the cruelest stories in the dynasty, kept in secret solitary confinement for the rest of his life, never told who he was, and finally killed by his guards in 1764 during a botched attempt to free him. He is the bound prisoner at the bottom of the dynasty's own house, and a reminder that the grandeur-and-unfreedom logic could reach the imperial family itself.

**Elizabeth** (reigned 1741–1762), Peter the Great's daughter, took the throne in the period's most theatrical coup. In November 1741 she walked into the barracks of the Preobrazhensky Guards at night, in person, and led the grenadiers to arrest the infant Ivan VI's regency (the rallying line tradition gives her, to the effect of "you know whose daughter I am, follow me," is the kind of cinematic quote this book treats with caution; that she went to the barracks herself and the Guards followed her is the solid part). The Guards made her empress on the spot. The image, a Petrine daughter personally leading the household troops to seize the crown, is the whole age in one scene. Elizabeth's twenty-year reign was, by the standards around it, stable and consequential. She was vain, pious, pleasure-loving, and politically shrewder than her reputation. She founded **Moscow University** in 1755 (still Russia's leading university, still named for her co-founder), at the urging of the one towering native intellect this period produced.

That intellect was **Mikhail Lomonosov** (loh-moh-NOH-soff), and he deserves a paragraph because he is the proof that the top-down knowledge machine Peter built had begun, by mid-century, to manufacture Russians and not just import Germans. Lomonosov was born around 1711, the son of a fisherman from the far Arctic north, walked, by the durable story, much of the way to Moscow to get an education, and became a chemist, physicist, astronomer, geologist, historian, and poet of genuine international stature, a Russian Benjamin Franklin in range and roughly his contemporary. He did real science (he argued for a wave theory of heat and a conservation-of-matter principle, observed the atmosphere of Venus during a 1761 transit), reformed Russian literary language and verse, fought the German-dominated Academy of Sciences for the dignity of Russian scholars, and championed the founding of Moscow University. One generation after Peter chartered an Academy he had to staff with foreigners, it had produced a world-class Russian polymath. The machine worked. The question this book keeps asking is what it was worth, given who paid for it, and Lomonosov, the fisherman's son who rose through the one institution that judged talent over birth, is one of the better answers the empire ever gave.

Two more strands of this period feed forward. First, art and stone. Elizabeth's reign is when Russian imperial grandeur acquires its definitive look, through the Italian architect **Bartolomeo Rastrelli** (rah-STREL-lee), who built for her a wedding-cake Russian Baroque of blue, white, and gold: the rebuilt **Winter Palace** in St Petersburg (the vast imperial residence that becomes the literal target of the revolution in the last chapters), the **Catherine Palace** at Tsarskoye Selo with its famous Amber Room, the Smolny Convent. Rastrelli's buildings are the architectural form of the thesis, sublime surfaces raised by a serf economy, and you should keep that double vision through every palace in this book.

Second, the army went to general European war and proved something. In the **Seven Years' War** (1756–1763, the first genuinely global war, fought from Europe to North America to India), Russia under Elizabeth joined the coalition against the Prussia of **Frederick the Great** (Frederick II, king of Prussia 1740–1786, the soldier-philosopher who had turned a medium-sized German kingdom into a feared military power and was rated by his own enemies the finest general of the age), then the most admired military machine in Europe. Russian armies beat Frederick's repeatedly, most crushingly at **Kunersdorf** in 1759, and even briefly occupied Berlin in 1760. Frederick, the finest general of the age, was pushed to the edge of total defeat and, by the accounts of those around him, came close to despair. Then Elizabeth died, in January 1762. Her heir was the next ruler in the parade, and he changes everything by changing nothing more dramatically than any palace coup in the whole period.

**Peter III** (reigned 1762, six months), Elizabeth's nephew, a German-born grandson of Peter the Great, raised in the duchy of Holstein, a man whose obsessive hero-worship of Frederick the Great is about to alter European history. On taking the throne in the middle of a war Russia was winning against Prussia, Peter III abruptly made peace with his idol Frederick, returned all Russia's conquests, and switched sides, an act so astonishing it is still called in German history "the Miracle of the House of Brandenburg," because it saved Prussia from destruction and arguably made possible the Prussia that becomes Germany a century later.

But the act of his short reign that matters most to this book had nothing to do with Prussia. In 1762 Peter III issued the **Manifesto on the Freedom of the Nobility**, which abolished the nobility's compulsory state service. Remember the Petrine bargain: the noble owed service to the state, and in exchange got the right to own serfs. Peter III cut the first half of that deal. The nobility was now free of its obligation. The serf was not. From 1762 the educated, serf-owning class held its privileges with no corresponding duty, and the bound peasant held his bondage with no corresponding protection. That asymmetry, the noble freed while the serf is not, is the precise structural injustice that Russian writers, radicals, and finally revolutionaries will gnaw on for the next 155 years. It is the single most important sentence in this chapter, and it is why a six-month emperor nobody respected gets a paragraph to himself.

Peter III himself was personally unpopular, erratic, contemptuous of Russian Orthodoxy and Russian feeling, and within six months he was overthrown and shortly afterward killed, by a conspiracy fronted by the Guards and led by his own wife, a minor German princess who had been quietly preparing for exactly this for eighteen years. Her name was Catherine, and she is the subject of the next four chapters.

# Chapter 6 — The German Girl Who Became Russia

She was born **Sophie of Anhalt-Zerbst** in 1729, in Prussian Stettin, the daughter of a minor German prince in the service of the King of Prussia, with no fortune, no great connection, and no drop of Russian blood. She died in 1796 as **Catherine II**, **Catherine the Great**, autocrat of the largest empire on earth, the most formidable ruler Russia had between Peter and the twentieth century, and a woman who corresponded as an intellectual equal with the most famous philosophers in Europe while presiding over the harshest tightening of serfdom in Russian history. Catherine is the chapter where the central contradiction of this book stops being a structural diagram and becomes a single person's biography, because she believed, sincerely and at length, in the European Enlightenment's program of reason, law, and human dignity, and she ruled a state that ran on the opposite of all three, and she knew it, and she chose the state. To understand why, you have to take both halves of her seriously, which the propaganda (both the flattering and the filthy kind) never does.

She arrived in Russia in 1744 at fourteen, brought in as a bride for the future Peter III. She did the things a foreign princess survives by doing: she converted to Orthodoxy with conspicuous devotion, learned Russian obsessively, and read. While her husband played with toy soldiers and admired Frederick of Prussia, Catherine spent two decades in an unhappy marriage reading Voltaire, Montesquieu, the French *philosophes*, and the entire library of the European Enlightenment, and building, very quietly, a network in the Guards and the court. When Peter III took the throne in 1762 and rapidly alienated almost everyone, Catherine moved. **Catherine's coup of 1762**, in June, was a Guards coup of the now-classic type: the Guards regiments, organized by her allies (the five Orlov brothers, well-connected Guards officers with a following among the rank and file, the most prominent of them Grigory, who was her lover), proclaimed her empress, arrested her husband, and forced his abdication. Peter III died a few days later, in custody, under circumstances the regime declared to be "hemorrhoidal colic" and almost nobody believed; he was near-certainly killed by his guards, with Catherine's foreknowledge probable and her direct order unproven and probably unprovable. This matters because Catherine's entire legitimacy was a problem she never solved. She was a German with no Russian blood and no hereditary right whatsoever, who took the throne by overthrowing and (at minimum) acquiescing in the murder of her husband, who was himself the legitimate emperor, while her own son Paul, the actual Romanov heir, was alive and a child. She ruled for thirty-four years as, strictly, a usurper, and she knew it, and a good deal of her conduct, including the grandeur, is best read as a permanent campaign to be so magnificent and so successful that the question of right would not be asked out loud.

Now the Enlightenment half, taken at full strength because it was real. Catherine read voraciously and corresponded for decades with **Voltaire**, the most famous writer in Europe and the era's emblem of rational, anticlerical, reforming thought, who flattered her extravagantly as the philosopher-empress, the proof that an enlightened absolute monarch could do more good faster than any messy parliament. She corresponded with the encyclopedist **Diderot**, bought his library when he needed money and paid him to keep it as his salary, and brought him to St Petersburg for months of conversation. She bought the art collection that became the **Hermitage**, one of the greatest museums on earth, founded as her private gallery in the Winter Palace in the 1760s. She built and endowed; she founded the Smolny Institute, among the first state-run institutions in Europe for the formal education of noblewomen (a finishing-school curriculum of languages, manners, music, and some arithmetic, modest by later standards but genuinely novel as a *state* commitment to schooling girls), and patronized the Academy of Sciences, whose directorship she gave to **Princess Yekaterina Dashkova** (DASH-koh-va), an early ally from the 1762 coup and a serious intellect in her own right, and one of the first women anywhere to head a national academy of sciences. She presented herself to Europe, with great skill, as the living argument for **enlightened absolutism**, the eighteenth-century theory that the fastest road to a rational, prosperous, humane society was not democracy but a wise, philosophically educated absolute monarch imposing reform from the top. Russia under Catherine was that theory's showcase, and the showcase was not entirely fake. It was, however, a bargain, and the philosophes were slow to see it: as the Enlightenment narrative puts it from the other side, patronizing Voltaire was a great deal cheaper than freeing a serf, and it bought a far better European press than freeing one would have, and the movement let itself be consumed as prestige by a ruler who implemented almost none of it. By the end of the century several of the philosophes had come to see the performance for what it had been. She secularized vast church lands, expanded schooling, reformed provincial administration, promoted inoculation against smallpox (and had herself publicly inoculated in 1768 to lead by example, which in 1768 was a genuinely bold scientific gesture), and built and beautified on a scale that announced Russia as a full member of the European cultural order.

The high-water mark of the Enlightenment Catherine, and the place where its limits become unmistakable, is the **Nakaz** and the **Legislative Commission**. In the 1760s Catherine personally wrote an enormous treatise, the **Nakaz** (na-KAHZ, "Instruction"), to guide a sweeping reform of Russian law. It was, in long stretches, lifted almost word for word from Montesquieu and from the Milanese jurist Cesare Beccaria, whose 1764 *On Crimes and Punishments* argued against judicial torture and the death penalty and effectively founded modern criminal-law reform. Beccaria's book was not just an idea on the page; in these same years it was reshaping actual law elsewhere in Europe, in Habsburg Tuscany and then in Austria itself, where Joseph II abolished judicial torture outright. Catherine quoted Beccaria approvingly and changed nothing, which is the contrast in one sentence. The Nakaz floated ideas radical for Russia: equality before the law, restraint of cruelty, the welfare of the people as the purpose of the state, even a famous passage that conceded liberty was a natural good, called the mass enserfment of human beings an evil, and stopped carefully short of saying it should actually be ended. In 1767 she convened a **Legislative Commission**, an assembly of 564 deputies elected from nobles, townsmen, state peasants, and non-Russian peoples (though pointedly *not* the privately owned serfs, who had no voice at all) to use the Nakaz as the basis for a new law code. It was the broadest consultative body Russia had assembled, and European observers were dazzled.

It produced nothing. After eighteen months of debate that exposed exactly how irreconcilable the empire's interests were, especially that the nobility would defend serfdom to the death and that the Nakaz's hints against it were politically impossible, Catherine prorogued the Commission in 1768 (using the convenient pretext of a new war with the Ottomans) and never effectively reconvened it. No new code came of it. This is the precise hinge of the chapter and one of the precise hinges of the whole book. Catherine had, in writing, intellectually conceded the case against serfdom, and then concluded, correctly in terms of holding power, that she could not touch it. Her throne rested on the support of the serf-owning nobility (the same Guards-and-gentry constituency that had made her and could unmake her), and the Enlightenment program ran straight into the load-bearing wall of the regime, which was unfreedom. She chose the wall. The reader should resist both the cynical reading (she never meant any of it) and the naive one (she was a frustrated liberal). The truthful reading is the uncomfortable one: she sincerely believed in reform and sincerely valued her power more, and when they collided she discovered, like every reforming Russian ruler in this book after her, that the empire was built so that you could not have very much of the first without endangering the second. The choice is sharpest set against her fellow "enlightened despots," the other rulers Europe filed under the same label. Frederick the Great of Prussia (Chapter 5) kept his serfs because his army needed them; Joseph II of Austria abolished serfdom in the Habsburg lands in 1781, within a few years of Catherine's Legislative Commission. Catherine had, in effect, a working example of a contemporary autocrat doing the thing the Nakaz had argued for, and a working example of one who refused, and she chose Frederick's path. Alexander I will discover it (Chapter 9). Alexander II will partially break it and be murdered for it (Chapters 14–15). Nicholas II will refuse to learn it at all (Chapter 19). Catherine is where the pattern is set, by the most intelligent ruler the dynasty produced, which is exactly why it is so damning.

The limit she had reasoned her way to in 1768 was about to be demonstrated to her from below, in blood. Catherine the reformer had written about the dignity of the peasant in the privacy of the Nakaz; five years later, in 1773, a Cossack claiming to be her murdered husband Peter III, come back to free the people, told her what the peasants actually wanted, with pikes, and her response set the chain tighter than Peter the Great ever had. That is the next chapter.

# Chapter 7 — Pugachev and the Machinery of Bondage

This is the chapter where the book stops describing serfdom and makes you stand inside it, because the largest popular revolt in Russian history before 1917 cannot be understood as anything other than 23 million people's answer to the question Catherine's Legislative Commission refused to ask. First the machinery, in concrete human terms, because "serfdom" is a word that hides what it names. Then the revolt. Then the regime's response, and let that response arrive as the discovery it was for the people who lived it.

So: what was it actually like to be a serf in Catherine's Russia? Forget the word "serf," which sounds medieval and distant, and look at the legal and daily reality. A privately owned serf, around 1770, belonged to a noble landowner the way the land did. He could be bought and sold. By Catherine's reign, serfs were openly advertised for sale in newspapers, sometimes individually, sometimes alongside livestock and furniture, and although selling family members separately was officially frowned upon it demonstrably happened. The serf owed his lord either *barshchina*, unpaid labor on the lord's own land (commonly three days a week, sometimes far more, and the lord set the terms), or *obrok*, a money or produce rent (which at least let the serf work for himself the rest of the time, and on some estates take up a trade or trade in towns), and frequently a punishing mix of both. The lord was the serf's judge and police: he could flog him, imprison him on the estate, and, by a series of Catherine-era decrees, exile a troublesome serf to Siberia or hand him over for army recruitment, which meant a 25-year term that the village mourned like a death because the man usually never came back. A serf could not legally marry without the lord's leave, could not leave the estate without a written pass, could not, until far too late in this book, own property in a way the law would defend against his owner, and, by a 1767 decree issued the very year of the enlightened Legislative Commission, could be sentenced to hard labor merely for submitting a petition against his own master. To anchor the scale one more time: this was the legal condition of more than a third of the population of the largest country on earth, somewhere around 23 million people in the private serf category alone, a bound population larger than the entire population of Britain at the time, and held that way not on distant plantations across an ocean but in the villages that fed the capital. The American reader's nearest handle is plantation slavery in the antebellum South, and the comparison is instructive precisely where it breaks: the Russian serf was the same religion, the same language, the same appearance as his owner, which made the bondage harder to rationalize and, in the end, harder for educated Russia to keep not-seeing. Russia abolished it in 1861. The United States abolished its slavery in 1865, by a war that killed about 750,000 people; the British Empire had abolished slavery in its colonies in 1833. Keep those three dates side by side, because Chapter 14 turns on them, and notice the oddity already: of the three, Russia was by far the least industrial, the one with the weakest economic motive to free its labor, and yet it acted earliest and without a war. The reason is in this chapter, not in any ledger. It was fear of a repeat of what you are about to read.

Now add the other engine of revolt: the frontier. South and east of the settled core lived the **Cossacks**, communities of free, self-governing, militarized frontiersmen, many descended from peasants who had fled serfdom to the open steppe, organized into "Hosts" (the Don, the Yaik, the Zaporozhian) that had historically traded military service to the tsar for autonomy. Catherine's centralizing state was steadily eroding that autonomy, conscripting Cossacks into regular service, interfering with their self-rule. The Yaik Cossacks on the Ural river were in a state of smoldering fury about exactly this in the early 1770s. Add to that frontier the Bashkirs and other non-Russian peoples resentful of land seizures, Old Believers hiding from the state church, and serfs assigned in brutal conditions to the Ural mining and metal works (above all the empire of the Demidovs, a family that had risen from a single Tula blacksmith to own a sprawling private industrial domain of iron foundries worked by serfs bound to the factory rather than the field, Chapter 18's industrial story in its grim prehistory). The southeast of the empire in 1773 was dry tinder. It needed a spark, and Russian popular culture provided the standard one: the pretender.

**Yemelyan Pugachev** (poo-gah-CHOFF) was a Don Cossack and army deserter who, in 1773, declared that he was not Pugachev at all. He was **Peter III**, Catherine's murdered husband, the true emperor, who had not died but escaped, and who had now returned to punish the wicked nobles, abolish serfdom, end conscription and the soul tax, and give the people the land. This was not a one-off con; "the returned true tsar" was a recurring template in Russian peasant politics (in the chaotic "Time of Troubles" around 1605–1612, before the Romanovs, at least three different men had claimed to be Dmitry, a dead son of Ivan the Terrible, and one briefly took the throne; there had since been other false Peters too), rooted in a popular belief that the tsar was the people's father and that their misery must be the nobles' doing against his true will. Pugachev's genius was to weaponize that belief at the exact moment the southeast was primed to explode. **Pugachev's Rebellion** (1773–1775) became the largest popular uprising in Russian history before the twentieth century. It started among the Yaik Cossacks, spread like fire to Bashkirs, Tatars, Old Believers, factory serfs, and finally the mass of agricultural serfs along the Volga, raised armies tens of thousands strong, briefly stormed and burned the city of Kazan, issued mock-imperial "decrees" freeing the serfs, and for the better part of two years (1773–1775) kept a vast, shifting swath of the southeastern empire in open revolt while the regime, also fighting the Ottomans, scrambled. Where Pugachev's forces passed, serfs settled accounts with their owners directly: estate houses burned, landlords and officials were hanged, and for a season the machinery of bondage ran backward across a region the size of a large European country.

It was crushed, as it had to be once the regular army was freed up from the Turkish war and turned on it. Pugachev's improvised peasant armies could not stand against trained troops; he was defeated, betrayed by his own lieutenants, handed over in a cage, taken to Moscow, and in January 1775 publicly executed (quartered, the sentence formally softened to beheading first, the bodily details of which are exactly the kind of thing later accounts dramatize and should be read with that in mind). His name was to be erased; even the Yaik river was renamed the Ural and the host renamed, an attempt to delete the event from the map that, characteristically, only fixed it more firmly in memory.

Here is the chapter's whole argument, and one of the spine moments of the book. The Pugachev terror did not move Catherine the philosopher-empress toward emancipation. It moved her, hard, the other way. The revolt confirmed for her and for the entire noble class their deepest fear: that beneath the serene Enlightenment surface sat a vast, illiterate, vengeful mass that, if the chain were loosened even slightly, would rise and slaughter them in their houses. The regime's conclusion was not "this system is unjust and dangerous and must be reformed." It was "this system is dangerous and must be held down harder." In the decade after Pugachev, Catherine extended serfdom into Ukraine where it had been weaker, reorganized provincial government to put more armed force at the local nobility's disposal, and, in 1785, issued the **Charter to the Nobility**, which codified into permanent law everything the nobility had been winning piecemeal since Peter III freed them from service in 1762: confirmed freedom from compulsory state service and from the soul tax and from corporal punishment, guaranteed property rights including absolute property in their serfs, the right to assemble in provincial corporations, immunity from confiscation. A parallel **Charter to the Towns** the same year gave the merchant class a defined, lesser set of privileges. The Charter to the Nobility is the legal capstone of the grandeur-and-unfreedom machine. It made the serf-owning nobleman a free man with rights against the crown itself, at the very moment it left the serf with fewer rights against his owner than ever. The two halves of the Petrine bargain had now fully come apart: the noble was free and protected, the serf was bound and exposed, and that gap was now written into the empire's fundamental law by its most enlightened ruler in direct reaction to the peasants who had tried to close it themselves. Every revolutionary in the last half of this book is, in some sense, a delayed echo of Pugachev, and the regime spent the next 142 years afraid of him.

A coda on conscience, because the period also produced the first Russian who said all this out loud. In 1790 a nobleman and customs official named **Alexander Radishchev**, educated partly in Leipzig on Catherine's own scholarship and steeped there in exactly the Enlightenment writers she patronized, published, on a private press, a book called *A Journey from St Petersburg to Moscow*: a fictional traveler's account that used the form of a road trip to detail, soberly and without melodrama, the daily cruelty of serfdom and the case against autocracy. It is generally regarded as the first frontal moral indictment of the system by a member of the privileged class itself, the genuine ancestor of the entire Russian conscience-stricken intelligentsia to come. Catherine, the correspondent of Voltaire, read it, annotated her copy with fury (her notes call him a rebel and, by the much-repeated summary of them, a man worse than Pugachev), and had Radishchev arrested, sentenced to death, the sentence commuted to Siberian exile. Around the same time she shut down the publisher and freemason **Nikolai Novikov**, who had built the most ambitious independent printing and philanthropic network in Russia, journals, cheap books, schools, and famine relief organized privately through Masonic lodges, the first sustained example of educated Russians acting on the public good without and outside the state, which is exactly why it frightened her, and imprisoned him without trial. The message was exact and it closes the chapter: the Enlightenment was welcome in Russia as the private hobby of the empress and as a glaze on the state, and it was a crime the instant a Russian applied it to the actual condition of the Russian people. That is the box the next century's writers and revolutionaries are born into.

# Chapter 8 — The Empire Doubles

While Catherine the Great was tightening the chain at home (Chapter 7), she was, with the other hand, expanding the empire faster than any Russian ruler since Peter, and arguably faster in raw territory and population than anyone before her. This chapter is about that expansion, and it carries a second strand of the thesis: imperial grandeur in the Russian system did not only mean palaces and the Hermitage. It meant land, and it meant the systematic conversion of conquered peoples into subjects of the autocracy, which is grandeur measured in square miles and in the absence of consent. By the time Catherine died in 1796, the empire had added something like 200,000 square miles and on the order of seven million people, and it had wiped a thousand-year-old European country off the map entirely. That last achievement is one she shared with two partners, and it deserves the cold look it usually doesn't get.

Start with the south, because the south is where the grandeur was loudest. Catherine fought two long wars against the **Ottoman Empire** (the Turkish-ruled Muslim empire that controlled the Black Sea, the Balkans, and the Middle East, the subject of its own narrative), and won both. The first **Russo-Turkish War** (1768–1774) produced a victory dramatic enough to rearrange the region. A Russian fleet did something no Russian fleet had ever attempted: it sailed the entire length of Europe, out of the Baltic, down past Britain and through the Atlantic, around Gibraltar into the Mediterranean, thousands of miles from any Russian port or base, a navy barely two generations old undertaking the kind of long-range projection only the established maritime powers were thought capable of, and burned an Ottoman fleet trapped in the bay of **Chesma** in 1770 while Russian armies overran the Black Sea steppe and the Crimean Peninsula. Britain and France watched closely and uneasily: Chesma was the first proof that Russian sea power could reach the Mediterranean at all, an alarm that fed, eighty years later, directly into their decision to intervene against Russia in the Crimean War (Chapter 13). The **Treaty of Kuchuk-Kainarji** (koo-CHOOK ky-NAR-jee) in 1774 was a landmark: Russia gained territory on the Black Sea, the right to a navy on it, and, fatefully, a vaguely worded claim to protect Orthodox Christians within the Ottoman Empire, a clause Russia would use as a lever against the Turks for the next 140 years and that lights the fuse on the Crimean War in Chapter 13. The treaty also detached the **Crimean Khanate**, the last surviving major fragment of the Mongol successor states and a Muslim Tatar state that had raided Russia for slaves for three centuries, from Ottoman protection as a nominally independent buffer. Nominal independence lasted nine years. In 1783 Catherine simply **annexed Crimea** outright, ending the khanate, the historic completion of Russia's centuries-long contest with the steppe slave-raiders, and a strategic prize: a warm-water foothold and the naval base of Sevastopol. From St Petersburg this was a triumph; from Istanbul, as the Ottoman narrative tells it, the same two wars were the opening of the long Ottoman decline, and the loss of Crimea, with the Black Sea turned from an Ottoman lake into an open sea Russia could sail at will, was the single most strategically devastating amputation in Ottoman history. The same event, read as ascent from one capital and as the beginning of the end from the other, is one of the cleanest examples in this whole series of why the cross-references exist.

The man who built the new south was Catherine's most important favorite and partner, **Grigory Potemkin** (poh-TYOM-kin), who many historians believe she secretly married, though the marriage rests on inference and circumstantial correspondence rather than a surviving record, and belongs in the "probable but unproven" column this book keeps for the colorful claims. He governed the conquered southern lands, christened **Novorossiya** ("New Russia"), and oversaw an enormous state-driven colonization: founding cities (Yekaterinoslav, Kherson, and the great port of Odessa was begun in this push), settling the empty steppe with Russian, Ukrainian, German, and other colonists, and building a Black Sea fleet from nothing. Potemkin is also the source of one of history's most durable and instructive myths. The story goes that when Catherine toured the new south in 1787, Potemkin had fake village façades, "Potemkin villages," erected along her route and populated with happy peasants moved from site to site, to deceive her into thinking the region was prospering. The phrase "Potemkin village" is now permanent English for a deceptive front. The trouble is that historians have largely concluded the story is, at minimum, hugely exaggerated and probably originated as hostile propaganda from Potemkin's diplomatic rivals; the development of the south was real, if uneven, and the lavishness of the tour was theatrical statecraft aimed at impressing the foreign ambassadors traveling with Catherine more than at fooling Catherine herself. The myth is included here precisely because it is so satisfying and so unreliable, which is a useful reflex to keep for the rest of the book: a too-perfect story about Russian autocratic pretense is exactly the kind of thing this narrative slows down to check. The second **Russo-Turkish War** (1787–1792) confirmed the conquests, made the reputation of the greatest Russian general of the age, **Alexander Suvorov** (soo-VOR-off), who never lost a battle in a long career and whose storming of the Ottoman fortress of **Izmail** in 1790 became legendary for its ferocity, and pushed the frontier to the Dniester.

The southern wars were conquest, the ordinary if brutal business of empires taking territory from a rival empire. What happened next was something colder and, morally, worse: not the defeat of a state but its premeditated erasure. Now the cold part: **Poland**. In 1772 the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth was the largest state in Europe by area, an old, culturally rich, but politically paralyzed republic-monarchy whose constitution gave any single nobleman in its parliament the power to veto and dissolve any session (the notorious *liberum veto*), originally a prized guarantee that no king could ride over the nobility, which by the eighteenth century had curdled into total paralysis because any reform that might have removed it could itself be vetoed, and because Poland's neighbors actively bribed deputies to keep the country ungovernable and defenseless. Its three powerful neighbors, Catherine's Russia, Frederick the Great's Prussia, and Habsburg Austria, solved the inconvenience of a large country in the middle of them by eating it. In three **Partitions of Poland**, in 1772, 1793, and 1795, the three monarchies divided the entire Commonwealth among themselves until, in 1795, Poland ceased to exist as a state, and stayed off the map for 123 years. Russia took the largest share, the eastern lands including most of Lithuania, Belarus, and right-bank Ukraine. There was a desperate Polish attempt to stop it: in 1794 a national uprising under **Tadeusz Kościuszko** (kosh-CHOOSH-koh), who had served the American Revolution as a military engineer, designing the fortifications at Saratoga and West Point, before coming home to fight for Poland's survival (there is a Kościuszko bridge in New York and a county in Indiana named for him, the modern handle), fought the partitioning powers and lost, which triggered the final, third partition. The destruction of Poland is the largest single act of imperial expansion in this book and the one that ages worst, both morally and strategically. Morally, because it was the deliberate murder of a sovereign European nation by its neighbors for convenience. Strategically, because it absorbed into the Russian Empire millions of Poles, Lithuanians, Catholic and Uniate Christians, and the bulk of Eastern European Jewry, none of whom had any loyalty to the tsar, all of whom became a permanent internal problem, and it bequeathed the empire its largest concentrated grievance: a proud nation, with its own language, church, and memory of statehood, swallowed and determined not to be digested. Poland revolts in Chapter 12 (1830) and again in Chapter 14 (1863), and the "Polish question" gnaws at the empire until the empire is gone.

One more frontier, in the opposite direction, almost as an afterthought, which is how the empire treated it. While the court fought Turks and partitioned Poland, Russian fur traders, the *promyshlenniki*, having crossed all of Siberia in the previous century chasing sable and sea otter (the Siberian fur trade was, for two centuries, one of the Russian state's largest sources of hard wealth, the reason Russia bothered with Siberia at all), pushed across the Bering Strait into North America. In the 1780s and 1790s they established the first permanent Russian settlements in Alaska. **Russian America** was a real colony, run as a commercial monopoly chartered as the Russian-American Company, reaching at its furthest down the Pacific coast to a fort in northern California. It is the strangest limb of the empire and the most revealing about how the empire actually worked: a continent away from St Petersburg, run for fur profit by a chartered company, settled by a few hundred Russians and a much larger forced and semi-forced native labor force, governed barely at all. It is also, for the American reader, the setup to one of the most consequential real-estate decisions in United States history, the sale of Alaska in 1867, which gets its full treatment in Chapter 17. For now, note only the shape: by 1796 the Russian Empire ran, however thinly, from the new Polish lands in the west to the coast of California, the largest contiguous land empire on the planet, and it had been assembled, in Catherine's reign, by a German-born usurper who corresponded with Voltaire and could not free a single serf. The empire was now enormous, and enormity in this book is never only strength: every conquest added subjects who had not consented and grievances that did not dissolve, so that each expansion made the empire at once larger and harder to hold together, bigger and weaker in the same act. That is the imperial version of this book's whole thesis, and it had just been demonstrated on a continental scale. In Chapter 9 the empire nearly destroys itself at the top, twice in fifteen years, while the man it most needs to fight is assembling an army in France.

# Chapter 9 — The Mad Tsar and the Hopeful One

Catherine the Great died in November 1796, and the throne passed to the one person in the empire she had least wanted to have it: her son **Paul I** (PAWL). The relationship between Catherine and Paul is the psychological key to this chapter, so set it up plainly. Catherine had taken the throne in 1762 by overthrowing Paul's father, Peter III. By strict hereditary right the crown in 1762 arguably belonged to the infant Paul, not to his German mother who had no Romanov blood at all. Catherine ruled for thirty-four years anyway, kept Paul away from power and from his own children, and is widely believed to have considered passing the throne over him directly to her grandson Alexander. Paul grew up the rightful heir, sidelined, watching, embittered, half-convinced his mother had murdered his father and stolen his crown, and increasingly erratic. When he finally became emperor at forty-two, he ruled like a man settling scores, which he largely was.

Paul reigned only five years (1796–1801) and his reign is genuinely contradictory, which is why "the mad tsar" is a label to handle with care rather than repeat. He did one thing of permanent, stabilizing importance: in 1797 he issued the **Pauline Law of Succession**, which finally abolished Peter the Great's disastrous 1722 rule (the one that let an emperor name any heir and that had produced seventy years of palace coups). In its place Paul fixed a strict, automatic order of succession by male primogeniture. After 1797 there were no more disputed successions and no more Guards-made emperors; every ruler from Alexander I to Nicholas II followed automatically by the rule. It is one of the most consequential pieces of constitutional engineering in the book, and it is genuinely odd that the man usually written off as a lunatic is the one who finally solved the dynasty's single most destabilizing flaw, partly out of personal grievance at how the lack of a rule had let his mother do what she did to him. Paul also tried to relieve some of the worst pressure on the serfs (a 1797 decree recommended limiting forced labor to three days a week, the first time the state ever tried to put any ceiling at all on a lord's demands, though it was advisory and largely ignored), curbed some noble privileges, and tried to impose Prussian-style discipline and order on a court he found corrupt and disrespectful.

But he did it with an arbitrary, paranoid harshness that made him enemies everywhere he had power, which was everywhere. He persecuted the nobility he distrusted, reversed his mother's policies seemingly to spite her memory, banned French books and fashions to keep out revolutionary contagion, and lurched in foreign policy. He sent the great Suvorov (Chapter 8) on a brilliant, brutal campaign through Italy and across the Alps against Revolutionary France in 1799, then abruptly switched course, reconciled with Napoleon, broke with Britain, and in his last months ordered a Cossack army to set off across the steppe toward British India, thousands of miles of unmapped desert, mountain, and hostile khanates with no supply line, roads, or accurate maps, a scheme so unworkable it was abandoned the moment he was dead and that confirmed for the elite that the emperor's judgment had failed. He had also alienated, specifically, the officers of the Guards and the St Petersburg nobility, the exact constituency that had made and unmade every ruler of the eighteenth century, by humiliating and threatening them. They reverted to type one last time. In March 1801, a conspiracy of senior officers and officials broke into Paul's bedroom in the newly built, fortress-like Mikhailovsky Castle he had built specifically because he feared assassination, and killed him (officially "apoplexy"; in fact strangled and beaten). The **assassination of Paul I** is the last successful palace coup in Russian history, the Pauline succession law it followed having made future ones pointless. Its darkest feature is the part that shadows the next chapter: Paul's son and heir, **Alexander**, was in the palace that night, knew of the conspiracy, and had given a conditional blessing to his father's removal on the explicit understanding (the conspirators' assurance, which he chose to believe) that Paul would only be forced to abdicate, not killed. When he was told his father was dead, Alexander, by the consistent testimony of those around him, collapsed in a guilt that shadowed him for the rest of his life. The new emperor of Russia began his reign as a probable, if unintending, accomplice to his father's murder. Keep that, because it explains a great deal about the strange, haunted man who is about to beat Napoleon.

**Alexander I** (the first emperor of that name, grandson of Catherine the Great, son of the murdered Paul, ruled 1801–1825) is one of the most enigmatic figures in this book, and contemporaries already called him that; the poet Pushkin would later nail him in a phrase as a "weak and wily ruler," and his own friends despaired of ever knowing what he actually believed. He came to the throne at twenty-three, raised by tutors Catherine had chosen, soaked in Enlightenment ideals by Frédéric-César de La Harpe, the Swiss republican Catherine had hired (she wanted her grandson fashionably educated, not guessing how fully the tutor would take, instructing the future autocrat for over a decade in natural rights, the rule of law, and the wrongness of slavery), and he opened his reign as the great hope of liberal Russia. He gathered a circle of young, reform-minded friends (the "Unofficial Committee") and seriously discussed, in private, things no Russian ruler had let himself discuss in public: a constitution, the rule of law, the eventual ending of serfdom. He relaxed his father's repression, reopened the borders to books and travel, founded new universities, and reorganized the central government into modern ministries. For a few years it genuinely looked as though the most powerful autocrat in Europe might voluntarily limit his own autocracy.

The man who turned that hope into actual blueprints was **Mikhail Speransky** (spe-RAN-skee), and he is the chapter's tragic protagonist because he is the empire's recurring lost chance personified. Speransky was not a noble; he was a village priest's son who rose by sheer administrative genius, exactly the kind of talent Peter's service ladder was supposed to surface, and Alexander made him his chief adviser. Between 1808 and 1812 Speransky drafted a comprehensive plan to convert Russia, gradually and from the top, into something like a law-governed constitutional monarchy: an elected hierarchy of assemblies rising to a national legislative body, a separation of administrative, judicial, and legislative functions, civil rights graded by property, the autocracy retained but bound by law. It was cautious, it kept the emperor strong, and it would still have been the single largest constitutional reform in Russian history. Almost none of it was enacted. What survived was one institution, the **State Council** (established 1810), an appointed advisory body for reviewing legislation, useful but a shadow of the plan. The rest died of the same disease that killed Catherine's Legislative Commission: the nobility hated it, regarded Speransky as a low-born upstart dismantling their world, and the international situation (the looming war with Napoleon) gave Alexander every excuse to set reform aside for safety. In 1812, on the eve of the invasion, Alexander sacrificed Speransky to noble pressure and to whispers (probably false) that he was a French sympathizer, and exiled him. The pattern from Chapter 6 repeats exactly: the reform was drafted, the autocrat half-believed in it, the load-bearing wall of the regime (the serf-owning nobility and the autocracy's own survival instinct) stopped it, and the moment passed. By 1812 Russia had a hauntingly guilty emperor, a shelved constitution, an exiled reformer, and 23 million serfs, and Napoleon Bonaparte, master of Europe, was massing the largest army the world had ever seen on its western border.

A word on how that border had moved, because it sets up the trap that destroys Napoleon. Alexander's first decade was a string of wars. He fought Napoleon in alliance with Austria and was beaten badly alongside the Austrians at **Austerlitz** in 1805 (the battle Napoleon rated his own masterpiece, and a disaster for the Russian army), and then, fighting on after Austria dropped out, was beaten again at Friedland in 1807. That second defeat forced the **Treaty of Tilsit** (1807). Its setting was pure theater and a humiliation dressed as a courtesy: a raft was moored in the middle of the Niemen river, the border, and the two emperors met on it alone, Napoleon the master of Europe and Alexander the defeated party pretending to negotiate as an equal. On that raft Alexander, having no real choice, became Napoleon's reluctant ally and agreed to join the Continental System, Napoleon's economic blockade of Britain, a concession that wrecked Russian trade and that Alexander spent the next five years looking for a way out of, which is one of the roads to 1812. The alliance was hollow from the first day; the blockade wrecked Russian trade, neither emperor trusted the other, and both prepared for the war they knew was coming. In the breathing space, Alexander expanded: a war with Sweden (1808–1809) took **Finland**, which became an autonomous Grand Duchy under the tsar, keeping its own laws, Lutheran church, and assembly (Alexander could afford generosity to a small, quiet, strategically loyal Finland in a way he never could to a large, proud, rebellious Poland that sat astride the road to the heart of the empire, which is why the two conquests were treated so differently); the arrangement, for a century, made Finland the freest corner of the empire and, eventually, a refuge for revolutionaries; a war with the Ottomans took **Bessarabia** (modern Moldova) in 1812; a long war with Persia, beginning in 1804, pushed Russian power into the Caucasus toward the mountain war that Chapter 17 covers. The empire kept getting bigger right up to the moment of maximum internal failure: a guilt-ridden emperor who had let his reformer be driven out, a constitution drafted and shelved, and 23 million people still owned, the chapter's real verdict, all of it sealed in 1812. And then, in June of that year, the largest invasion army in the history of the world to that point crossed the Niemen heading east, and the empire's size stopped being a problem to be governed and became, for the first time, a weapon.

# Chapter 10 — 1812

In June 1812, **Napoleon Bonaparte** crossed the Niemen river into the Russian Empire with the largest army Europe had ever assembled, somewhere on the order of 600,000 men drawn from France and the entire continent it controlled, the **Grande Armée**. (The exact figure is debated and the round numbers should be held loosely, but the order of magnitude, the better part of two thirds of a million men, is solid.) Five and a half months later, a starving, frozen remnant variously estimated in the low tens of thousands recrossed the same river going the other way. The destruction of the Grande Armée in Russia is one of the largest military catastrophes in recorded history, the event that broke Napoleon and made Russia, for the next forty years, the dominant land power of Europe. It is also the most mythologized episode in this entire book, retold by Tolstoy in the greatest novel ever written about war, scored by Tchaikovsky with actual cannon, and turned by Russian memory into a sacred national epic, the "Patriotic War." So this chapter does two jobs: it tells you what happened, and it keeps flagging which famous parts are solid and which are the legend doing its work.

First, what Napoleon wanted and why it was a trap. Napoleon did not primarily want to conquer and hold Russia; that was logistically impossible and he knew it. He wanted to force Alexander I, by a fast, crushing battlefield victory near the border, to come back to heel and re-enter the Continental System. His entire method, perfected across a decade of European wars, depended on bringing the enemy's main army to a decisive battle quickly and destroying it. The Russian commanders, above all Barclay de Tolly, the cool, methodical war minister of Scottish émigré descent who commanded the main army at the start, did the one thing that defeated that method: they refused the decisive battle and retreated, deeper and deeper into the enormous country, trading the empire's vast space (Chapter 1's central problem) for time, and burning supplies behind them so the invader's enormous army could not feed itself off the land. This was the same strategy that beat Charles XII of Sweden at the end of the road to Poltava a century earlier (Chapter 3), and it is no accident; the Russian command knew that history. It was also wildly unpopular at the time. To ordinary Russians and much of the army, retreating without fighting while the enemy burned his way toward Moscow looked like cowardice or treason, and the pressure to *stand and fight* became politically irresistible. Alexander replaced Barclay with a commander the army and the public trusted viscerally: the old, one-eyed, shrewd, fatalistic **Mikhail Kutuzov** (koo-TOO-zoff), a veteran of Suvorov's wars. Kutuzov privately understood the retreat was working and would have happily continued it. He could not. He had to give Russia its battle.

He gave it at **Borodino** (bor-oh-dee-NOH), a village about 70 miles (110 km) west of Moscow, on 7 September 1812. Borodino is one of the bloodiest single days of combat in history before the twentieth century: roughly a quarter of a million men engaged, and casualty estimates commonly put the combined dead and wounded at around 70,000 to 75,000 in one day, though every specific number from that day is contested and the higher figures should be treated as estimates, not measurements. The outcome was tactically a draw bleeding toward a French victory: by nightfall the Russians had been pushed back and Kutuzov, to preserve his army from annihilation, withdrew, leaving the road to Moscow open. Napoleon held the field, which by the conventions of the age made it his victory, and it did him no good at all, which is the entire point and the deepest lesson of the campaign. The Russian army was battered but not destroyed; it withdrew intact and dangerous. Napoleon had won the kind of battle that always before had ended a war, and the war did not end, because the Russian state did not collapse and the Russian army did not surrender, and he was now eight hundred miles from home with winter coming.

Kutuzov then made the decision that defines him and horrified contemporaries: at a council of war at the village of Fili, he chose to abandon Moscow without further fighting, reportedly with words to the effect that the loss of Moscow was not the loss of Russia but the loss of the army would be. (The famous council and the sentiment are well attested; the exact wording, like most famous lines in this book, has been smoothed by retelling.) In mid-September Napoleon entered Moscow, the old capital and the symbolic heart of the country, expecting that taking it would force Alexander to negotiate. He found a city largely emptied of its people. And then Moscow burned. The **burning of Moscow**, which destroyed perhaps two thirds of the city over several days, is one of the genuinely unresolved questions of the campaign and a perfect case study in why this book hedges. Three explanations have been argued for two centuries: a deliberate Russian scorched-earth policy, most likely ordered or enabled by the city's governor Rostopchin who had removed the fire-fighting equipment; accidental fires spread by chaos, looting, and a wooden city; and French negligence. The honest current position is that a deliberate Russian element is widely accepted as at least a major cause, that Rostopchin's role is probable, that he himself gave contradictory accounts over his life, and that a single clean answer is not available. What is not in doubt is the strategic effect: Napoleon sat in the ashes of Moscow for five weeks, sending peace feelers that Alexander, stiffened now into total refusal, simply ignored. There was no one to negotiate with and nothing to hold. In mid-October, with winter closing and his army unsupplied in a burned city, Napoleon began the retreat.

The retreat is where the catastrophe actually happened, and where the legend most needs checking. The popular memory, the one Tchaikovsky scored, is "General Winter": the idea that the Russian cold, almost supernaturally, destroyed the Grande Armée. The truth is more brutal and more to this book's point. The army was already gutted before the hard frosts came, by the thing this narrative keeps returning to: distance. Hunger, disease (typhus above all), exhaustion, the disintegration of supply across hundreds of miles of devastated country, and the relentless harassment of Cossacks and armed peasants on the flanks had wrecked it through the warm autumn weeks. Then the cold arrived and finished what the space had started, and it did so with a horror that is not exaggerated. The nadir was the crossing of the **Berezina** (be-re-ZEE-nah) river in late November. With Russian armies converging and the bridges destroyed, the army's combat engineers, the pontonniers, waded into the ice-choked river and built two trestle bridges by hand, working in water that killed many of them within days, and the army crossed under attack in a multi-day chaos of drowning, trampling, and slaughter that left tens of thousands dead and made "Berezina" a synonym for catastrophe in French to this day. By the time the survivors recrossed the Niemen in December, the Grande Armée as a fighting force had ceased to exist. The space had eaten it, exactly as it had eaten Charles XII, exactly as the empire's planners had always feared the space would eat *them*; for once the impossible size of Russia was the empire's weapon instead of its burden.

Russia did not stop at its own border, and this is the strategic hinge for the next two chapters. Alexander I, against Kutuzov's instinct (Kutuzov wanted to let Napoleon limp home and not bleed Russia further to liberate Germany), chose to pursue the war into Europe. The **campaign of 1813–1814** turned the Patriotic War into the War of the Sixth Coalition: Russia, now joined by a recovering Prussia, Austria, and Britain, pushed west across Germany. The decisive blow fell at the **Battle of Leipzig** in October 1813, the "Battle of the Nations," the largest battle in European history before the First World War, where the allied armies decisively beat Napoleon and broke his hold on Germany. In March 1814 the allied armies entered Paris, and Alexander I of Russia, the haunted son of a murdered father who had begun his reign dreaming of constitutions, rode through the streets of the French capital as the most powerful monarch in the world and the principal author of Napoleon's downfall. Napoleon abdicated and was exiled (he would return for the brief, final act that ends at Waterloo in 1815, but the Russian campaign is what actually broke him). For Russia the meaning was immense and double-edged in exactly this book's way. The empire had achieved the absolute summit of its grandeur. It had destroyed the man who conquered Europe and now stood as the continent's arbiter, its army the most respected on earth, its emperor the figure everyone in Europe waited on. And it had done it without changing a single thing about itself internally: the serf who had marched to Paris and seen, with his own eyes, a Europe without serfdom, came home in 1815 to a village where he was still owned. Some of those soldiers, and some of the young officers who led them, brought the contradiction back across the border with them and could not put it down. The summit of the grandeur and the lighting of the long fuse are about to happen in the same ten years, and they are the same story. That is Chapter 11.

# Chapter 11 — Gendarme of Europe

For ten years after 1815, Russia was the most powerful state in the world, and it used that power, almost entirely, to prevent change anywhere. This chapter is short by the standards of this book because its content is essentially one argument with one demonstration, and the demonstration is the most important single morning in the whole narrative: 14 December 1825, the day the long fuse was lit in public. But you need the decade of reaction first, because the Decembrists are its product.

At the **Congress of Vienna** (1814–1815), the conference where the victorious monarchies redrew the map of Europe after Napoleon, Alexander I of Russia (Chapter 10's victor, now Europe's arbiter) was the dominant figure. Russia's main territorial gain was the bulk of what had been Poland, reconstituted as the **Kingdom of Poland**, usually called **Congress Poland**, in personal union with the Russian crown. In a move that captures Alexander's lifelong contradiction perfectly, he granted Congress Poland a written constitution, an elected parliament (the Sejm), and civil liberties considerably more generous than anything in Russia itself, so that the autocrat of an empire with no constitution was simultaneously a constitutional monarch in his Polish province. It pleased almost no one and it set up the explosion of 1830 (Chapter 12).

Alexander's deeper project at Vienna was ideological. Increasingly mystical and conservative in his later years, he proposed the **Holy Alliance**, a treaty signed by the rulers of Russia, Austria, and Prussia pledging to govern as Christian brothers on Christian principles. In its plain words it was a vague piece of religiose idealism that the hard-headed Austrian statesman Metternich privately mocked. In its effect it became the ideological flag of the post-1815 order: the principle that monarchy was divinely sanctioned, that revolution and liberalism were sins to be suppressed wherever they appeared, and that the great powers had a right and duty to intervene across borders to crush them. Russia, the strongest land power on earth, became the muscle behind that principle, the **gendarme of Europe** (a *gendarme* is an armed state policeman; the phrase, used at the time and after, casts Russia as the continent's cop). For the next forty years, through Alexander and especially through his brother Nicholas, Russia's role in European affairs was to be the great immovable conservative weight, the army that could be marched to put down a revolution, and it did exactly that, most starkly in Hungary in 1849. The empire that had liberated Europe from Napoleon spent the next generation as the chief jailer of European liberty, which is the foreign-policy face of the grandeur-and-unfreedom thesis: Russia could project colossal power, and the only thing it knew how to use that power *for* was to keep things from changing, including at home.

At home the reaction had a specifically grotesque institution: the **military colonies**. This was the scheme of Alexander's late-reign favorite, the brutal artillery officer Count Arakcheyev (ah-rak-CHEH-yeff), whose name became a Russian byword for mindless harsh discipline (*Arakcheyevshchina*). To make the huge standing army cheaper, whole districts of state peasants were converted into permanent military settlements where peasant families lived under total military regimentation: their farming, their dress, their houses, their daily routine, even marriage, ordered by army regulation, the men soldiers and farmers at once, the children raised to the regiment. It was an attempt to turn a chunk of the peasantry into a self-funding army by abolishing the boundary between the village and the barracks entirely, and it was so hated that it produced brutal revolts. The military colonies are worth a paragraph because they are the regime's instinct in its purest form: faced with the cost of the grandeur (a vast army), the solution was not reform or representation but a deeper, more total regimentation of the unfree. It is the thesis rendered as a settlement plan.

(One bright thread crosses this dark decade and deserves its sentence, because the empire's grandeur was never only military: in 1820 a Russian naval expedition under the Baltic German officer **Bellingshausen**, sent out under Alexander I, made what many specialists rank as the first confirmed sighting of the continent of **Antarctica**, days ahead of a British and an American claimant in a priority dispute that has never been fully settled, an achievement of the same expansionist energy that drove the empire across Siberia, here pointed at the bottom of the world.)

Now the morning. Alexander I died suddenly in November 1825 in the southern town of Taganrog, far from the capital, of an illness (the abruptness and the remoteness later spawned a durable Russian legend that he had not really died but secretly retired to live as a Siberian holy man called Fyodor Kuzmich; this is folklore, with no reliable evidence, included here only as a classic example of the Russian "the true ruler did not really die" myth this book has flagged before, the same template that produced Pugachev's false Peter III). His death produced a brief, bizarre succession crisis even under the Pauline law, because the next brother, Constantine, had secretly renounced his rights, and the throne therefore fell to the third brother, **Nicholas**, but the secrecy meant that for three weeks in December 1825 it was genuinely unclear in the capital who the emperor was, and the army had been sworn to Constantine before being told to re-swear to Nicholas. Into that three-week gap stepped the men this whole book has been walking toward.

They are called the **Decembrists**, after the month. They were not peasants and not professional revolutionaries. They were aristocratic army officers, many of them the very men who had marched to Paris in 1814, members of the Guards regiments and the cream of the service nobility, who had spent the years since 1815 in secret discussion societies (the Union of Salvation, the Union of Welfare, the Northern and Southern Societies) reading the European liberalism their own emperor was suppressing and arguing about how to give Russia a constitution and end serfdom. They are the precise human form of Chapter 10's closing image: the educated Russian who had seen, with his own eyes, a Europe without serfdom and an emperor bound by law, and could not come home and un-see it. They were also not an isolated Russian eccentricity. The early 1820s were a continent-wide wave of liberal revolt against exactly the restored order Russia was policing: the secret Carbonari societies rose in Naples and Piedmont in 1820–1821, a constitutional revolt broke out in Spain in 1820, the Greeks rose against the Ottomans in 1821. The Decembrist officers read the same European liberal literature and knew of these movements; 1825 was the Russian beat of that drum. The grim symmetry is that the same Russia whose officers were trying to import a constitution was, as a state, the muscle suppressing constitutions everywhere else, and would march an army to crush the next European revolutionary wave outright when Hungary rose in 1849. Their plans were various and not fully agreed (a constitutional monarchy in the northern group's program, a radical republic with land reform in the southern leader Pestel's), but the core was constant: limit or end the autocracy, and free the serfs. The confused succession of December 1825 handed them an opening they were not really ready for, and they took it anyway. On 14 December 1825, the day the Guards regiments in St Petersburg were to swear allegiance to Nicholas I, a few thousand soldiers led by the conspiring officers refused the oath and assembled under arms in the Senate Square, demanding "Constantine and Constitution" (a slogan many of the rank-and-file soldiers reportedly only half-understood, some apparently told that "Constitution" was Constantine's wife, a detail that is probably partly legend but captures the gulf between the educated conspirators and the men they led, a gulf that recurs for the rest of this book).

The **Decembrist revolt** failed in a single afternoon. The leaders hesitated, the planned coordination collapsed, the chosen "dictator" of the rising never showed up, and Nicholas I, on his first day as emperor, after some hesitation, ordered loyal artillery to fire grapeshot into the square. The rebels were scattered and killed in some number; the revolt in the south was put down within weeks. Nicholas personally involved himself in the investigation and interrogation of the prisoners. Five of the ringleaders were hanged, among them the southern radical Pavel Pestel, who had drafted the most far-reaching republican program, and the poet Kondraty Ryleyev (a botched, horrific execution in which several ropes broke and the men had to be hanged a second time, one of them reportedly saying that in Russia they could not even do this properly, a line that is widely repeated and impossible to fully verify but has become emblematic). Over a hundred more were stripped of rank and exiled to hard labor and settlement in Siberia, where many spent decades, followed in a famous and genuinely documented gesture by a number of their wives who voluntarily gave up their titles and went into Siberian exile with them, an act that became a permanent moral touchstone in Russian culture.

Here is why this short chapter is one of the load-bearing chapters of the entire book. The Decembrist revolt failed completely, militarily and politically, in an afternoon. It changed nothing in 1825. And it changed everything afterward, because it was the first time the grandeur and the unfreedom were brought into open, armed collision by the empire's own elite, in the open, in the capital, and the regime's answer was grapeshot and the gallows. Before December 1825 the case against the system had been a private book burned by Catherine (Radishchev, Chapter 7), a shelved plan by an exiled adviser (Speransky, Chapter 9), a conversation among the emperor's young friends. After December 1825 it was a martyrdom, a heritage, and a permanent question that no Russian of conscience could stop asking, passed down deliberately as a torch: from the Decembrists to the writers of Chapter 12, to the radicals of Chapter 15, to the revolutionaries of Chapter 18, to the men who finally finished the empire in Chapter 20. This is the moment the fuse is lit. Everything after this is the fuse burning, and the next emperor's entire thirty-year reign is one long, increasingly desperate attempt to stamp it out.

# Chapter 12 — Official Nationality

**Nicholas I** (the first emperor of that name, third son of Paul I, younger brother of Alexander I, ruled 1825–1855) became tsar on the morning he ordered grapeshot fired into the Decembrists in Senate Square, and that fact organized the next thirty years of Russian history. Every other ruler in this book before him came to the throne by inheritance or coup and then decided what kind of ruler to be. Nicholas decided in his first hours, under fire, and never wavered: his life's work would be to make sure that 14 December 1825 never happened again. He was hard-working, personally austere, militarily obsessed, sincerely convinced that autocracy was a sacred trust and that any concession to it was the first crack in a dam holding back chaos. He is the purest expression of the regime's central choice in this entire book: offered, at the start of his reign, the clearest possible warning that the system needed to change, he concluded instead that it needed to be defended harder, and he spent thirty years and the whole machinery of the state proving the point. Russians and historians have called his reign the "apogee of autocracy." It is also, not coincidentally, the reign in which the Russian intelligentsia, the class of people who would eventually destroy the autocracy, is born.

The regime got an official ideology, the first time Russian autocracy explicitly stated a doctrine rather than just assuming one. Nicholas's education minister, Sergei Uvarov, formulated it in 1833 as a triad: **Orthodoxy, Autocracy, Nationality** (*Pravoslavie, Samoderzhavie, Narodnost*). This is the doctrine of **Official Nationality**, and it is worth unpacking because it is the regime's own statement of the thesis of this book, from the regime's side. *Orthodoxy*: the state church as the moral foundation of Russian life. *Autocracy*: the unlimited tsar as the only possible government for Russia, divinely sanctioned, the antithesis of Western constitutions. *Nationality* (*narodnost*, hard to translate, roughly "national character" or "the people-ness"): the claim that the Russian people were uniquely, organically devoted to the first two, that the bond between the loving tsar-father and his trusting people was the special genius of Russia and had no need of the legalistic, contractual, revolutionary apparatus of the West. Official Nationality was, in effect, a sophisticated argument that the grandeur and the unfreedom were not a contradiction at all but a harmony, that Russia's strength came precisely from *not* having what Europe had. It is the most coherent intellectual defense the autocracy ever produced of itself, and the entire history of the next eighty years is the slow, grinding refutation of it, starting with the Crimean War in the very next chapter.

To enforce it, Nicholas built the machinery the regime would use, in various forms, until 1917. He created the **Third Section** of the Imperial Chancery, the empire's first true centralized political police: a secret-police bureau, backed by a uniformed Corps of Gendarmes, charged with surveillance of opinion, censorship enforcement, monitoring of suspect persons, and the management of dissent. The Third Section is the direct institutional ancestor of the later Okhrana (Chapter 18) and, beyond this book, of the Soviet security organs; the Russian habit of a powerful secret state agency watching the population, traceable back to Peter's *fiskaly* (Chapter 4), here becomes a permanent, professional fixture. Censorship became pervasive and capricious, administered by an "censorship terror" of overlapping agencies. He codified the empire's tangled laws into the great **Digest of Laws** (the actual monumental work was done by Speransky, the reformer Alexander I had exiled in 1812, Chapter 9, now brought back from disgrace by Nicholas to organize the autocracy rather than to limit it, the one piece of his life's project that was ever realized), which imposed order but pointedly did not include a constitution; it organized the autocracy, it did not limit it. And he confronted the empire's largest concentrated grievance head-on. When the news of the 1830 revolutions in France and Belgium reached Congress Poland (Chapter 11), the Poles rose in the **November Uprising** of 1830–1831, a full-scale national war. Nicholas crushed it militarily, then abolished the Polish constitution his brother had granted, dissolved the Polish army and Sejm, closed Polish universities, and ruled Poland thereafter as occupied territory. The "Polish question" did not go away; it went underground and detonated again in 1863 (Chapter 14). The pattern is the chapter's pattern: a demand for liberty answered with force and the removal of what limited liberties existed.

And here is the paradox that makes this reign the hinge of the book's cultural argument. The most repressive reign of the nineteenth century is also the foundational age of Russian literature, the moment Russia goes from having essentially no world-class literature of its own to producing, within two generations, the greatest novels ever written. This is not a coincidence and it is not in spite of the repression. It is, in a specific and demonstrable way, *because* of it. When every other avenue of public life, no parliament, no free press, no legal politics, no independent associations, is closed by the state, the only place a thinking Russian can examine the moral condition of the country is in literature and the "thick journals" that printed it, read between the lines past the censor. The dynamic is not uniquely Russian (the French Revolutionary Terror, hunting its own thinkers, drove Condorcet to write his great treatise on human progress while in hiding from the guillotine, in the Enlightenment narrative); repression has a way of displacing thought rather than ending it. But Russia ran the experiment at a scale and for a duration that made the displaced thought into a national literature. Literature in Nicholas's Russia became the substitute for the politics the regime forbade, and Russian writers became, by default, the conscience and the opposition the country was not allowed to have any other way. The class this produced got a name, **the intelligentsia** (a Russian coinage, roughly "the thinking, educated, morally engaged class"), and it is one of the most consequential social formations in modern history, the carrier of the Decembrist torch (Chapter 11) for the rest of this book.

The titans arrive in this reign. **Alexander Pushkin** (POOSH-kin, 1799–1837) is the one Russians put above all the others, the writer who effectively created the modern Russian literary language and is to Russia roughly what Shakespeare is to England, an analogy Russians use themselves. His verse novel **Eugene Onegin**, his historical drama, his lyric poetry, set the standard for everything after. He had a glancing, dangerous relationship with the regime (some early liberal verse, friends among the Decembrists, personal censorship by Nicholas himself, who appointed himself Pushkin's personal censor in a gesture that was equal parts patronage and surveillance). He died in 1837 at thirty-seven, of wounds from a duel defending his wife's honor against a French officer, a death the whole of literate Russia experienced as a national catastrophe and that many believed, then and since, the court establishment had at minimum done nothing to prevent. The young officer-poet **Mikhail Lermontov** wrote a furious poem blaming high society for Pushkin's death, was promptly arrested and exiled to the Caucasus for it, wrote the first great Russian psychological novel there, and was himself killed in a duel in 1841 at twenty-six. Two of Russia's greatest poets, dead in duels four years apart, both in their twenties or thirties, both having tangled with the regime, is itself a kind of statement about the era. And **Nikolai Gogol** wrote, under this same censorship, **Dead Souls** (a comic-grotesque novel whose plot, a swindler buying up the legal title to dead serfs who are still on the tax rolls, is the most devastating possible satire of a society that literally counts and trades human beings, and which got past the censor partly because it was funny) and *The Government Inspector*, a play about provincial corruption that Nicholas I himself reportedly attended and laughed at, apparently missing that the joke was the regime.

The intelligentsia did not stay literary; it began to argue about Russia itself, and the argument it started in Nicholas's reign is still, in some form, going on. The thinking class split into two camps. The **Westernizers** held that Russia was a backward European country whose only hope was to catch up with the West: adopt its science, its law, its eventual liberty, complete the project Peter the Great had begun. The **Slavophiles** held the opposite: that Peter's Westernization had been a wound, that Russia had a unique spiritual destiny rooted in Orthodoxy and the peasant commune and the organic bond between tsar and people, and that salvation lay not in copying Europe but in recovering an authentic Russian path. (Note how close the Slavophile position sits to Official Nationality, and how the Slavophiles nonetheless ended up censored too, because even loyal independent thought was suspect; that is how total the closure was.) The **Slavophiles versus Westernizers** debate is the master template of Russian intellectual life from then on; almost every later movement in this book, the Narodniks, the Marxists, the liberals, even the revolutionaries, is a descendant of one side or the other, and the question they were really arguing, is Russia's salvation to become Europe or to become more itself, is the same question the empire dies without answering in Chapter 20.

The regime watched all of this with mounting suspicion and a heavy hand, and one episode shows exactly how heavy. In 1849 the Third Section arrested the members of the **Petrashevsky Circle**, the discussion group that gathered at the apartment of Mikhail Petrashevsky, a minor foreign-ministry official and Fourierist utopian socialist, whose actual crime was meeting to read and debate banned Western socialist writings, talk, essentially. Twenty-one of them, including a twenty-eight-year-old writer named **Fyodor Dostoevsky**, were sentenced to death and taken to a public square, where, in front of the firing squad, in the last seconds, a courier galloped up with a pre-arranged imperial "pardon" commuting the sentence to Siberian hard labor. The mock execution was theater, deliberate psychological terror staged by the state. Dostoevsky did four years in a Siberian prison camp and several more as a soldier; the experience reshaped him and, through him, world literature (Chapter 16). The Petrashevsky affair is the perfect closing image for this reign: men arrested for reading the wrong books, marched to a fake execution to teach the country a lesson about the price of thought. Nicholas I had built, by 1849, the most thoroughly controlled state in Europe, an apparatus that could reach into a discussion circle and stage a man's death to frighten him. It looked, from the inside, like total success. Within four years it would be exposed, by a war the regime started confident of victory, as a rotten and backward shell, and Nicholas I would die a broken man, possibly by his own hand, while his army was being beaten on his own soil. That is the next chapter, and it is the chapter where the thesis stops being an argument and becomes a body count.

# Chapter 13 — The War That Told the Truth

The Crimean War (1853–1856) is the shortest era this book gives a whole chapter to, and it gets one because it is the moment the entire argument of the book stops being something a reader has to take on the narrator's word and becomes something the Russian state itself could no longer deny. For forty years, since 1815, Russia had been the gendarme of Europe, the strongest land power on earth, the showcase of Official Nationality, the proof (the regime believed) that autocracy and unfreedom produced strength, not weakness. The Crimean War was the experiment that tested that claim against reality, and the claim failed, in public, on Russian soil, in front of the whole world. Everything in the second half of this book, the Emancipation, the Great Reforms, the revolutionaries, the desperate late-imperial scramble to industrialize, flows from the wound this war opened, because it was the war that told Russia the truth about itself.

The causes are tangled and this book will keep them brief, because the diplomatic detail matters less than the result. The proximate trigger was a quarrel over which Christians, Orthodox (backed by Russia) or Catholic (backed by France), had custody of the holy sites in Ottoman-ruled Jerusalem and Bethlehem, a dispute Nicholas I inflated, on the strength of Russia's old claim to protect Orthodox Christians in the Ottoman Empire (the lever from the 1774 Treaty of Kuchuk-Kainarji, Chapter 8), into a confrontation with the weakening Ottoman Empire that he expected to be a short, victorious Russian advance. Nicholas badly misjudged two things. He misjudged that Britain and France, despite being rivals with each other, would not tolerate Russia dismembering the Ottoman Empire and dominating the eastern Mediterranean and the route to British India, and he misjudged that Austria, whose 1849 revolution Russia had marched in and crushed for it, would repay the favor; instead Austria stayed hostile-neutral, which left Russia diplomatically isolated, the gendarme of Europe with no friends in Europe. What had looked like a quick chastisement of the Turks became Russia, alone, at war with the Ottoman Empire, Britain, France, and eventually the Italian kingdom of Sardinia. There is a sharp irony in the matchup that the Ottoman narrative draws out from its side. The nineteenth century's two great "sick" empires, the one Nicholas I himself had reportedly called the "sick man of Europe" and his own, fought this war on opposite sides, and both came out of it having learned the same lesson: that their survival now depended less on their own strength than on the patronage of the industrial powers of western Europe. Russia was about to discover from the losing side what the Ottomans already knew from long experience.

The fighting that mattered came to the Crimean Peninsula, Russia's own territory, the warm-water prize Catherine had annexed in 1783 (Chapter 8), where the British and French landed an expeditionary force in 1854 to take the great naval base of **Sevastopol** (se-VAS-toh-pol), home of the Black Sea Fleet. The **siege of Sevastopol** lasted almost a year, from late 1854 to September 1855, and it is the heart of the war and of this chapter. The Russian defense was, at the level of individual courage, magnificent; the city held for eleven months against the combined might of the leading industrial powers of the age, and the defenders, soldiers, sailors, and the Russian surgeon Nikolai Pirogov, the founder of field surgery in Russia, pioneering battlefield triage and the first large-scale use of ether anesthesia under fire, became a permanent Russian legend of endurance. One of the junior artillery officers in the defense was a young count named **Leo Tolstoy**, who turned what he saw into the *Sevastopol Sketches*, a piece of unsparing war reporting that made his name and that no censor seems to have fully grasped was an indictment (Chapter 16). The heroism was real. The system behind it was the point, and the system lost.

Here is the mechanism of the defeat, and it is the thesis made physical. Russia was fighting on its own soil, far closer to its own heartland than its enemies were to theirs, and it still could not adequately supply or reinforce its own army, while Britain and France could project and sustain power across the entire length of Europe and the Mediterranean. Why? Because the things that win industrial wars were exactly the things autocratic, serf-based, agrarian Russia had refused to grow while it was busy being the gendarme of Europe. The British and French armies were moved and supplied substantially by railway and steamship; Russia had almost no railways (a single line between the two capitals, finished only in 1851, and essentially nothing reaching the south), so Russian troops and supplies moved to Crimea by ox-cart over dirt roads, taking longer to travel within their own country than the enemy took to cross a continent and a sea. This was not ignorance of what railways could do. Germany, Belgium, and France had been building state-backed railway networks since the 1830s, and the military value of rail had already been demonstrated in the Austrian and Prussian mobilizations of 1848–1849, which Russian officers had watched closely. Russia was not vaguely behind "the West"; it was specifically behind the industrial-policy states it had spent two decades observing modernize, held back not by lack of knowledge but by the same autocratic-fiscal paralysis this whole book has been describing. Russian soldiers carried smoothbore muskets against British and French rifled weapons with several times the effective range. Russian industry could not produce munitions, equipment, or modern arms at anything like the rate of the industrialized West. The Russian financial and administrative system, built for autocratic control rather than mobilization, could not raise or spend money at the necessary scale without wrecking the currency. The serf army was huge and brave and led, with exceptions, by a promotion system that rewarded loyalty and connection over competence. Every one of these failures traces directly back to a choice the regime had made, over and over, across this whole book: the choice of autocratic and social control over the modernization that would have threatened it. Official Nationality had said Russia was strong *because* it had not become Europe. Sevastopol said Russia was being beaten, on Russian ground, *because* it had not become Europe. The war was the audit, and the books did not balance.

Sevastopol fell in September 1855. Nicholas I did not live to see it. He died in February 1855, in the middle of the war he had started, of pneumonia, a broken man who knew his life's certainty had failed; there is a persistent contemporary rumor, never proven and rejected by most historians, that the proud emperor, unable to bear the humiliation, ordered his doctor to give him poison. The rumor persists not on evidence but on its narrative perfection, the man who had staked everything on the doctrine of strength choosing not to outlive its public refutation, which is exactly why this book flags it as the kind of too-shapely deathbed claim it has taught the reader to distrust. What is not in dispute is that he died a broken man, watching the visible collapse of everything he had built his reign on, even his defenders concede that much. He was succeeded by his son, **Alexander II** (not to be confused with Alexander I, the victor over Napoleon and his great-uncle), who inherited a losing war and the necessity of ending it. The **Treaty of Paris** (1856) was a humiliation calibrated precisely to wound: Russia lost its right to keep a navy or fortifications on the Black Sea, the warm-water foothold itself neutralized, and the gendarme of Europe was publicly demoted in front of the continent it had policed for forty years.

This chapter is short because its meaning is single and overwhelming. The Crimean War killed something more important than the roughly half a million men (the figure is an estimate; disease, not combat, killed most of them on every side, and Russia, defending on its own ground with the worst supply, is generally reckoned to have lost the most, on the order of a quarter to a third of a million, more than the British and French combined) who died in it. It killed the regime's central belief about itself. For the educated Russian, for the new emperor, for the intelligentsia, even for many conservatives, the war proved, with the unanswerable evidence of defeat on home ground, that the system, autocracy plus serfdom plus the suppression of everything that might have modernized the country, did not in fact produce strength. It produced a giant that could not feed its own soldiers in its own country. The whole second half of this book is the answer to one question that the smoke over Sevastopol forced onto the throne: if the system as it stands cannot even defend the empire, can the empire afford to keep the system? Alexander II's answer, the most courageous answer any Russian autocrat gave to that question and the one that got him blown apart on a St Petersburg street twenty-five years later, is the next chapter.

# Chapter 14 — The Tsar Liberator

This is the chapter the whole first half of the book has been building toward, because it is the chapter in which the Russian Empire finally, after two centuries, does the thing it had refused to do, and discovers that doing it badly is in some ways worse than not doing it at all. **Alexander II** (the second emperor of that name, son of Nicholas I, grandnephew of Alexander I, ruled 1855–1881) was a dutiful, conscientious, intelligent man, conventionally raised by Nicholas I to revere the autocracy and genuinely devoted to the duty of the throne, with a streak of pragmatic realism his father had lacked: he could look at a verdict he did not like and accept it. And he had just inherited one. The Crimean War (Chapter 13) had returned its unanswerable judgment: the system as it stood could not defend the country. Faced with the same collision every reforming ruler in this book has faced, Catherine, Alexander I, the choice between the grandeur and the unfreedom, Alexander II became the one autocrat who chose, on the central question, to give up the unfreedom. It made him "the Tsar Liberator." It also, by the specific way it was done, lit a faster-burning length of the fuse, and within twenty years the people he had freed and the radicals who claimed to speak for them were hunting him through his own capital with bombs.

Start with the decision itself, because Alexander II's own words frame it better than any paraphrase. Pressing the reluctant Moscow nobility to accept reform in 1856, he told them, in substance, that it was better to abolish serfdom from above than to wait for it to abolish itself from below. The exact words vary across the sources, but the sentiment is solidly documented, and it is the strategic core of the entire reform. This is the thesis stated by the regime itself, out loud, for the first time: the unfreedom at the bottom was no longer just unjust, it was *dangerous*, a Pugachev (Chapter 7) waiting to happen on a national scale, and the grandeur at the top could no longer be safely maintained on top of it. Emancipation was not, primarily, an act of conscience. It was an act of regime self-preservation, and understanding that is the key to understanding why it was done in the particular crippled way it was done.

The **Emancipation of the Serfs** was proclaimed in the Emancipation Manifesto of 19 February 1861 (3 March by the Western calendar). On paper it freed somewhere around 23 million privately owned serfs, with the state peasants and others following over the next two decades, in total well over forty million people. In raw scale it is the single largest act of human liberation by legislation in history up to that point, and the comparison this book has been setting up since Chapter 7 must be made here, in this paragraph, because the timing is the whole point. Britain had abolished slavery in its empire in 1833, by act of Parliament, with compensation paid to the slave owners. The United States abolished slavery between 1863 and 1865, by the Emancipation Proclamation and the Thirteenth Amendment, at the cost of a civil war that killed roughly 750,000 Americans, the bloodiest event in that country's history. Russia freed a bound population several times larger than the American one, by autocratic decree, peacefully, in 1861, two years *before* Lincoln's Proclamation and four years before the Thirteenth Amendment. For one moment, on the single measure of "did you legally free your unfree millions, and how violently," the most backward great power in Europe was, astonishingly, ahead of the United States. That is the grandeur of the act, and it is real, and Russians were right to feel it.

But the mechanisms ran in opposite directions, and the difference is the whole story. British abolition in 1833 came *after* industrialization, partly because industrial wage labor had made Caribbean slave-worked sugar economically marginal, as the Industrial Revolution narrative traces. American abolition in 1865 came *through* an industrial war, the railways and ironworks of the North grinding down an agrarian slave society. Russian emancipation in 1861 came *before* industrialization, with almost no industrial economy pressing for it; it was not the result of economic change but a political pre-emption of revolt, and it would later supply the unbound, mobile labor that Witte's factories ran on (Chapter 18). Britain freed its slaves because industry no longer needed them; Russia freed its serfs so it could begin to industrialize at all.

Now the mechanism, because the mechanism is where the act was sabotaged, and this book's whole method is to insist on mechanism over slogan. The serfs were freed. They were not, in any meaningful sense, given the means to live free. The settlement, hammered out by reform bureaucrats against ferocious noble resistance, worked roughly like this. The freed peasant received personal liberty (he could no longer be bought, sold, flogged at a lord's whim, or denied marriage) immediately, and that was real and mattered. But the land, the only thing a peasant could actually live on, was handled so as to protect the nobility. The peasants received allotments of land, but typically *less* land than they had been farming for their own subsistence as serfs (the landlords kept the best and largest portions, and in many regions the peasants ended up with less than before), and they did not get it free. They had to pay for it, through a system of **redemption payments**: the state compensated the landlords up front for the land, and the peasants then had to repay the state, with interest, over a period set at forty-nine years. Until enough was paid, peasants were in many cases "temporarily obligated," still owing labor or dues to the former lord. And crucially, the land in most of Russia was not given to individual peasants at all but to the **village commune** (the *mir* or *obshchina*), the collective village body, which was made jointly responsible for the redemption debt and the taxes and which controlled and periodically redistributed the land among its households and could refuse a peasant permission to leave. The peasant had been freed from the lord and bound, instead, to the commune and the debt. He could not freely leave the village; he could not freely own or sell his strip of land; he was poorer in land than before and now in decades of debt for it. The chain had not been struck off. It had been re-forged in a new shape and handed to a new keeper.

The result was a disappointment so profound it became one of the engines of the revolution. The peasants, who had genuinely expected the Tsar Liberator to give them the land, their land, the land they and their ancestors had worked, free, reacted to the actual terms with bewilderment and then anger. A persistent peasant belief held that the *real* emancipation, the true tsar's golden charter giving them all the land, was being hidden from them by the wicked nobles and officials, the exact Pugachev-era "the good tsar's true will is being concealed" myth this book flagged in Chapter 7, now attached to 1861. There were hundreds of disturbances. The most notorious came at the village of Bezdna, where peasants gathered around a man who claimed the real golden charter freed them completely, refused to disperse, and were fired on by troops; scores were killed for not believing that the emancipation they had been handed was the real one. The educated radicals drew the conclusion that would organize the rest of this book: that the autocracy was structurally incapable of giving the people real freedom, that even its greatest reform was a fraud designed to preserve the landlords and the debt, and that the thing would have to be taken, not granted. Emancipation, the regime's bid to defuse the danger from below, instead convinced the most determined of its critics that reform from above was impossible and only revolution would do. That is the precise hinge into Chapter 15.

But 1861 was not the end of Alexander II's reform; it was the start of a remarkable burst of it, the **Great Reforms**, and this book has to give them their due, because they were the genuine article and their fate is the chapter's deepest lesson. Freeing the serfs forced the state to rebuild institutions that had assumed serfdom. The **zemstvo reform** of 1864 created elected local self-government councils at the district and provincial level, with real responsibility for schools, roads, public health, and agriculture, the first institutions in Russian history in which Russians elected representatives to manage their own local affairs. The franchise was real but rigged in the gentry's favor: voting was by separate curiae for landowners, townsmen, and peasant communes, with the seats weighted so that the relatively few noble landowners returned a heavily disproportionate share of the delegates. It was a small, deliberately limited school of self-government in a country that had none. The **judicial reform** of 1864 was the most radical of all: it created, more or less from nothing, a modern Western-style court system with independent judges, public trials, equality of all classes before the law, and trial by jury, in the country that had had the Third Section and serf justice a few years earlier. The military reforms of war minister Dmitry Milyutin, the reform-minded general who ran the army ministry for two decades and rebuilt it after Crimea, completed in 1874, replaced the brutal 25-year conscription of serfs with universal, all-class, far shorter military service, and modernized the army that Crimea had disgraced. Censorship was relaxed (Chapter 16's literature pours through the gap), universities got autonomy, and the first higher education courses for women in Russia were established. For about a decade it genuinely looked as though Russia might be reforming itself, from the top, into a modern, law-governed society without a revolution, the thing every reformer in this book had dreamed and none had achieved.

And then the chapter's iron point. Every one of the Great Reforms stopped at the same wall, the wall this entire book is about. Russia got elected local councils but no national parliament; the zemstvo could fix a district road but could not touch a national law, and when zemstvo liberals asked, politely, to "crown the edifice" with some form of national representation, Alexander II refused, flatly, every time. Russia got independent courts and trial by jury but the autocracy remained legally unlimited and could, and increasingly did, simply route political cases around the new courts through administrative and military justice. Russia got everything that modernized the country *except* the one thing that would have shared power, because sharing power was the line no autocrat in this book would cross, the same line Catherine would not cross with the Nakaz (Chapter 6) and Alexander I would not cross with Speransky (Chapter 9). The Great Reforms modernized the body of Russia and deliberately left the autocratic head untouched, and that mismatch, a society being given courts, councils, education, and a freed (if cheated) peasantry, while political power stayed the absolute possession of one man, is precisely the unstable compound that the rest of this book detonates. Alexander II had done more to dismantle the unfreedom than any ruler in two hundred years and had still refused to touch the autocracy, and for that incomplete courage the people who thought even his reforms were a lie spent the 1870s building the bombs. The hunting of the Tsar Liberator is the next chapter.

# Chapter 15 — Going to the People

This is the chapter where the fuse, lit by the Decembrists in 1825 (Chapter 11) and carried through the literature of Chapter 12, finally reaches the people who will spend the next four decades trying to blow up the empire, and it is also the chapter where Russia, more or less, invents modern terrorism. The argument runs: the disappointment of the Emancipation (Chapter 14) convinced a generation of educated young Russians that the autocracy could not be reformed and must be destroyed; they tried, in sequence, three strategies, propaganda, peasant insurrection, and assassination, and the third one worked, in the narrow sense that they killed the Tsar Liberator in 1881, and failed totally, in the wide sense that his murder produced not revolution but the harshest reaction of the century (Chapter 18). Watching them cycle through those strategies is watching the revolutionary movement that eventually wins in 1917 learn its trade.

First the cultural mutation, because the revolutionaries of the 1860s were a new psychological type and contemporaries felt it as a rupture between generations. The men of the 1840s, the Westernizers and Slavophiles (Chapter 12), had been aristocratic, philosophical, literary, given to talk. The new men of the 1860s were often *raznochintsy* (raz-no-CHEEN-tsee, "people of various ranks," educated young Russians of non-noble origin, priests' sons, minor officials' sons, the products of Alexander II's expanded schooling), poorer, harder, contemptuous of their elders' refined hand-wringing, and committed to science, materialism, and a ruthless rejection of every inherited authority, religion, the family, the state, sentiment itself. They got their label from a novel: in 1862 **Ivan Turgenev** published **Fathers and Sons**, whose hero Bazarov, a young man who believes in nothing he cannot dissect, gave Russia and the world the word **nihilist** (from Latin *nihil*, "nothing," one who recognizes no authority, accepts no principle on faith). The radicals embraced the insult as a badge. Their practical bible was another novel, written in a prison cell: **Chernyshevsky's** *What Is to Be Done?* (1863), a clumsily written but incandescently influential book whose model revolutionary, the iron ascetic Rakhmetov who sleeps on nails to harden himself, became the template a generation consciously tried to live by. (The title was later deliberately reused by Lenin, Chapter 18, a direct torch-pass the reader should notice.) The literary thread of Chapter 12 has now turned operational: the novels are not describing the revolutionaries, they are producing them.

The first strategy was propaganda and the famous attempt to merge with the people. The dominant idea among the radicals of the 1860s and 1870s was **Narodism** (from *narod*, "the people"), a distinctively Russian agrarian socialism whose central wager, inherited partly from the Slavophiles and the radical exile Alexander Herzen (a wealthy nobleman's son, hounded by Nicholas I's police for his politics until he left Russia for good in 1847 and settled in London, from where his uncensored journal *The Bell* was smuggled back in and became, for a decade, the first great free Russian voice), was that Russia could leap straight to socialism without a capitalist stage, because the peasant **commune** (the *mir*, Chapter 14) was already a natural socialist institution; all that was needed was to awaken the peasants. In the spring and summer of 1874, thousands of idealistic young men and women, students, dressed as workers and peasants and went into the countryside, the movement called "**going to the people**," to live among the peasants, teach them, and rouse them to revolution. It was a near-total failure and the failure is instructive. The students arrived expecting to find a peasantry seething for revolution and instead found one that was devoutly Orthodox, monarchist, suspicious of educated outsiders who could not handle a scythe, and far more likely to want the students' land redistributed *by the tsar* than to want the tsar overthrown. When agitators preached atheism or attacked the emperor, peasants were variously baffled, scandalized, and alarmed; in a striking number of cases the villagers themselves tied up the young revolutionaries and marched them to the police, the people the movement had idealized handing it over to the state. The first secret-police mass round-ups followed, and hundreds of the "go to the people" radicals ended up in the dock in mass political trials (the **Trial of the 193** the most famous), which, by giving the defendants a public platform, the regime's own reformed courts (Chapter 14) having made trials public, turned martyrs into celebrities and radicalized more people than the trials punished, a self-defeating dynamic the autocracy never solved.

The failure of "going to the people" split the movement, and the split is the chapter's pivot. The radicals organized into a disciplined underground society called **Land and Liberty** in the late 1870s, and then, in 1879, it broke in two over a single question: what to do now that the peasants would not rise. One faction (which became *Black Repartition*, led by Plekhanov, who matters enormously in Chapter 18) wanted to keep up patient propaganda. The other, larger and more impatient faction concluded that since the masses could not be roused and the autocracy could not be reformed, the answer was to strike directly at the autocracy itself, to kill the tsar and his servants, on the theory that systematic assassination would either provoke the revolution or so terrorize the regime into concessions. They called themselves **The People's Will** (*Narodnaya Volya*), and they are widely regarded as the first organization in modern history to adopt systematic political assassination as a deliberate, theorized program rather than an isolated act, the direct ancestors of a great deal of what the twentieth century would call terrorism. The atmosphere had been prepared: in 1866 a lone student, Karakozov, had already fired the first shot at Alexander II (and missed, triggering a wave of reaction); in 1878 Vera Zasulich shot and seriously wounded the governor of St Petersburg in revenge for the flogging of a political prisoner, and a St Petersburg jury, in one of the most astonishing moments of the whole period, *acquitted* her, an open public verdict that the regime's own new courts would not convict a political assassin the public sympathized with, after which the regime simply stopped sending such cases to juries. The theory of terror also had its monster-text in the *Catechism of a Revolutionary* (around 1869), associated with Sergei Nechaev, a young provincial schoolteacher's son whose distinctive horror was not extremism but instrumentality: he treated other revolutionaries as disposable tools, fabricated a phantom central committee to control his followers, and had a wavering member of his own cell murdered to bind the rest in blood and silence. His cold doctrine that the revolutionary may use any means and any person without scruple so horrified Dostoevsky, basing the killing on the real case, that he built the novel *Demons* around it (Chapter 16). The escalation from a missed shot in 1866 to an organized assassination bureau in 1879 is the radicalization of the entire movement compressed into thirteen years.

The People's Will then did something no one had done before: it sentenced the emperor of Russia to death and spent two years methodically trying to carry out the sentence. They tried explosives on his train and blew up the wrong one (the imperial train had already passed; the bomb wrecked the baggage train following it). They got a member, Stepan Khalturin, employed as a carpenter inside the Winter Palace itself, who smuggled in dynamite over weeks and detonated it under the imperial dining room; the tsar survived only because his dinner happened to be running late and the room was still empty when the charge went off, while the explosion tore through the guardroom below and killed about a dozen soldiers and wounded dozens more, the **Winter Palace bombing** of 1880, an attack inside the emperor's own home that announced the autocracy could be reached anywhere. The regime's response to the terror was itself revealing of the thesis: in 1880 Alexander II created an emergency "Supreme Commission" under Mikhail Loris-Melikov, an Armenian-born general who had made his name in the Caucasus and the Turkish war and who, unusually for a soldier-administrator, believed the terror could only be beaten by pairing repression with cautious political concession, and by early 1881 the aging Tsar Liberator had actually approved a cautious proposal, the so-called "Loris-Melikov constitution," which was not a constitution at all but a modest step toward including elected representatives in reviewing legislation, the smallest possible crack toward the national representation the zemstvo liberals had begged for since 1864 (Chapter 14). He signed off on it on the morning of 1 March 1881 (13 March, Western calendar).

That afternoon, the People's Will killed him. As his carriage drove through St Petersburg, a bomber threw a device that wrecked the carriage and wounded the escort. Alexander II, by the well-attested account, was unhurt and got out to attend to the wounded, the humane reflex of the man who had freed the serfs, and a second bomber, standing close, threw a second bomb directly at his feet. The **assassination of Alexander II** tore the lower half of his body apart; he was carried, conscious and dying, back to the Winter Palace, to the same study, and died within hours, on the very day he had approved the first hairline step toward representative government in Russian history. The symbolism is so total it would be rejected as too neat if it were fiction: the most reforming tsar in two hundred years, murdered by revolutionaries on the afternoon he authorized the reform they said could never come, in the palace his terror had already bombed once. His son built a church on the exact spot where the blood fell, the Church of the Savior on Spilled Blood, and his son was the man who answered the assassination not with the dead tsar's constitution but with its incineration, who tore up Loris-Melikov's project, hanged the assassins, and turned the full machinery of the state to crushing everything Alexander II had loosened. The People's Will had bet that killing the tsar would bring the revolution closer. It set it back, by the most repressive reign since Nicholas I, by a full generation. But it had also proved, to everyone watching, that the autocracy could be physically reached and physically killed, and that lesson did not die with the men who were hanged for it. It went underground, was studied, and was inherited, like every other length of this fuse, by the people who finally used it in 1917. Before we get to the reaction, though, the book owes the reader the other thing this same half-century produced, the thing that makes Russia matter to people who never cared about tsars at all: the greatest outpouring of art the modern world has seen. That is the next chapter.

# Chapter 16 — The Golden Age

This chapter breaks the chronology on purpose, because its subject does not fit on a timeline. Across roughly the second half of the nineteenth century, the same Russian Empire that was hunting and being hunted in the chapters around this one produced what is, by very wide agreement, the single greatest concentration of literature any country has produced in any comparable span, plus world-altering music and science, and it did so *while* the autocracy held and the unfreedom only half-lifted. This is not a digression from the thesis. It is the thesis at its most uncomfortable, because the grandeur in this chapter is not palaces and conquests, which are easy to see through. It is *War and Peace* and the periodic table, achievements of permanent value to all of humanity, produced by the same machine that flogged peasants and hanged students, and the honest reader has to hold both at once without letting either cancel the other. That double vision is the whole point of the chapter.

Why here, why then, why so concentrated? The argument this book has been building since Chapter 12 pays off here. In a country with no parliament, no free press worth the name, no legal politics, and a censor reading everything, the novel became the only place the largest moral questions a society can ask, what is a just life, what do we owe each other, can violence ever be redemptive, what is Russia for, could actually be asked, by writers, in front of a reading public that brought to fiction the desperate seriousness other countries brought to politics and religion. Russian literature is so heavy because it was carrying the weight of everything the autocracy forbade anyone to say any other way. The repression did not produce the genius, but it shaped what the genius was *for*, and that is why the Russian novel feels different in kind from the English or French one: it is arguing about how to live, under penalty, for readers with nowhere else to take the argument.

The two giants first. **Leo Tolstoy** (1828–1910), the count who had been an artillery officer at Sevastopol (Chapter 13), wrote, in the decade after the Emancipation, **War and Peace** (serialized from the 1860s), an attempt to put an entire society, several hundred characters across the Napoleonic Wars (his account of 1812, Chapter 10, is where most of the world's mental image of it comes from), inside one book, and **Anna Karenina** (1870s), commonly cited by other novelists as the most technically perfect novel ever written. Then, at the height of his fame, in his fifties, the most celebrated novelist alive, Tolstoy fell into a crisis so total he kept a rope hidden so he would not hang himself, and came out of it a radical Christian anarchist who renounced rank, property, copyright in his own books, the Orthodox Church, the army, the courts, and the autocracy itself, and tried, not very successfully, to live as a peasant on his own estate. He preached a literal nonviolence and non-cooperation with the state that reached a young Indian lawyer named Gandhi, who corresponded with him near the end of his life and built a movement on the idea, so that a line runs from this Russian count's late conversion to the twentieth century's largest experiment in nonviolent resistance. The Orthodox Church, the state church of Chapter 1, formally cut him off from communion in 1901 (the "excommunication of Tolstoy," technically a declaration that he had separated himself), an event so large that the censored press could barely contain the public reaction; the most famous man in Russia, possibly in the world, openly against the church and the autocracy, and untouchable because killing or jailing Tolstoy was unthinkable. When he died in 1910, fleeing his own home and aristocratic life at eighty-two, his death was an international event and a political one. **Fyodor Dostoevsky** (1821–1881) is the other pole. The young man marched to the fake firing squad in 1849 and sent to a Siberian prison camp (Chapter 12) came back with a shattered, religious, anti-rationalist intensity and wrote the novels that map the modern psyche from the inside: **Crime and Punishment** (the student who reasons his way into murder and is destroyed by his own conscience), *Demons* (a savage portrait of the Nechaev-style revolutionary nihilism of Chapter 15, written by a man who had himself been a radical and recoiled), and **The Brothers Karamazov**, his last and largest, whose chapter "The Grand Inquisitor", a parable in which a returned Christ is arrested by the Church for offering people a freedom they did not want, has been argued over by philosophers and theologians ever since. Dostoevsky against Tolstoy, faith against reason, the abyss against the moral law, is itself one of the permanent arguments of world literature, and it happened inside one country inside thirty years.

They were not alone, which is the point of a golden-age chapter. **Ivan Turgenev** (Chapter 15's source of the word "nihilist") was the great novelist of the liberal Westernizing conscience and the gentle elegist of a dying gentry world, the Russian most read in Western Europe in his own lifetime and the bridge by which Europe discovered there even *was* a Russian literature. **Anton Chekhov** (1860–1904), a doctor and the grandson of a serf who had bought his own family's freedom (a biographical fact that is itself this book's whole thesis in one family tree), reinvented the short story and, in plays like **The Cherry Orchard**, wrote the long, soft requiem of the gentry order, a class talking elegantly while the estate is sold out from under it, which is also, if you tilt it, the empire itself, ten years before the end. His plays were made by a new institution, the **Moscow Art Theatre**, whose director Stanislavski developed a method of psychologically realistic acting that, transmitted to America, became the dominant approach to screen acting in the twentieth century (the modern handle: the "Method" of Brando and De Niro descends, by a traceable line, from a Moscow theater staging Chekhov in the 1890s).

The same surge ran through music, and Russian music's defining quarrel rhymes exactly with the Slavophile-Westernizer split of Chapter 12. A group of nationalist composers known as **The Mighty Handful** (or "The Five"), self-taught amateurs by training and proud of it, set out to build a specifically Russian classical music out of folk song and Orthodox chant rather than German technique; their monument is **Mussorgsky's** opera *Boris Godunov*, a raw, unconventional drama of a guilt-haunted tsar and the suffering Russian people that broke European operatic rules on purpose. Against them, more cosmopolitan and conservatory-trained, stood **Pyotr Tchaikovsky** (chy-KOFF-skee), who became the most internationally beloved composer Russia ever produced, the man who wrote *Swan Lake*, *The Nutcracker*, the symphonies, and the **1812 Overture**, the piece, complete with scored cannon fire, that gave the West its sonic image of Russia beating Napoleon (Chapter 10) and that, by a long irony, now mostly plays over American fireworks on the Fourth of July. Painting had its own nationalist rebellion, the **Wanderers** (*Peredvizhniki*), who in 1863 walked out of the state Imperial Academy of Arts, the official body that controlled training, exhibition, and patronage and required its prize students to paint approved mythological and classical subjects, in order to paint instead what they saw, peasants, prisoners, priests, the actual Russia, and to take it directly to the provinces in traveling exhibitions rather than to the salons of the capital. Their work was bought up by **Pavel Tretyakov**, a self-made Moscow textile merchant who set out deliberately to assemble a national collection of Russian art and then handed the whole gallery to the city of Moscow, where it still is, so that the definitive collection of imperial Russia's painting was preserved not by a tsar but by a merchant, which is itself this book's thesis in one bequest.

And the same machine produced world-class science, which matters to this book because science is the part of the grandeur the autocracy most desperately needed and least knew how to grow. **Dmitri Mendeleev** (men-de-LAY-eff), working at St Petersburg, published in 1869 the **periodic table** of the elements, arguably the single most important organizing idea in the history of chemistry; its power, the detail that made the world believe it, was that Mendeleev left gaps for elements not yet discovered and *predicted their properties*, and when those elements (gallium, germanium) were found and matched his predictions, the table was vindicated as not a chart but a law of nature. Earlier, the mathematician **Nikolai Lobachevsky**, working in provincial Kazan, had been among the independent creators of **non-Euclidean geometry**, a revolution that overturned 2,000 years of assumed truth about space and laid groundwork that Einstein's physics would later need. **Ivan Pavlov** won, in 1904, one of the first Nobel Prizes in physiology, and then, through the experiments on **conditioned reflexes** in dogs that made "Pavlovian" an English adjective, founded a science of how learning physically works. (And in the same years a deaf provincial schoolteacher, Konstantin Tsiolkovsky, was quietly working out the mathematics of rocket-powered spaceflight, a thread that belongs to the next narrative, not this one.)

Hold the chapter's two facts together one last time, because this is the place in the book to do it without flinching. The periodic table, *The Brothers Karamazov*, Pavlov's Nobel, *The Cherry Orchard*, the conditioned reflex, *War and Peace*, all of it, was produced inside the same empire, in the same decades, by the same society, that hanged the men of Chapter 15, that ran the Pale of Settlement and the pogroms of Chapter 17, and that was, even as Chekhov wrote, marching toward the catastrophe of Chapter 20. The grandeur in this chapter is not a fraud the way the Potemkin villages were a fraud (Chapter 8). It is real, permanent, and given freely to all of humanity. And it does not redeem the unfreedom, and the unfreedom does not erase it, and any honest account of the Russian Empire has to carry both without resolving them, because the empire itself never resolved them. It produced the greatest novels ever written about the moral life and never managed to organize a moral political order, and in 1917 the second failure consumed the country that had achieved the first. The chapters that remain are about how the consuming happened.

# Chapter 17 — The Empire of All the Russias

So far this book has mostly told the story from St Petersburg, looking down. This chapter turns the empire inside out and looks at it from its edges, because "the Russian Empire" was never just Russians. The tsar's official title ran to dozens of lands, "Emperor and Autocrat of All the Russias, of Moscow, Kiev, Vladimir, Novgorod; Tsar of Kazan, Tsar of Astrakhan, Tsar of Poland, Tsar of Siberia," and on, a recited inventory of conquests. By the empire's last census, in 1897, of its roughly 125 million people only about 44 percent counted as Great Russians in the narrow sense; even folding in Ukrainians and Belarusians as "Russian," the official habit, brought it to only about two thirds, and tens of millions were neither. It was, like the Habsburg and Ottoman empires it competed with, a vast multi-ethnic patchwork, and the thesis of this book has a second face here: the same logic that kept the peasant unfree at home drove an expansion abroad that absorbed tens of millions of non-Russians who never consented and, in the empire's last decades, tried to make them all into Russians by force, manufacturing exactly the nationalist grievances that helped tear the empire apart in Chapter 20. This is the empire as the largest contiguous prison-house and treasure-house of peoples on earth, and you cannot understand 1917 without it.

Take the directions in turn. **South, into the Caucasus.** The Caucasus is the wall of high mountains between the Black and Caspian seas, one of the most ethnically and linguistically dense regions on the planet, dozens of peoples and languages in an area the size of a couple of US states. Russia spent roughly half a century (the **Caucasian War**, conventionally dated from around 1817 to 1864) conquering it, and the conquest produced one of the great resistance figures of the nineteenth century: **Imam Shamil** (sha-MEEL), the Avar religious and military leader who, from the 1830s to 1859, united the Muslim mountain peoples of Chechnya and Dagestan in a holy war against Russian expansion and held the mountains against the empire for a quarter of a century with guerrilla warfare before finally being captured in 1859. The regime did not execute him; it pensioned him, paraded him through Russian cities to admiring crowds, and let him die on pilgrimage in the Ottoman holy cities. The clemency was a calculated message, aimed partly at the watching mountain peoples (resistance, once over, would be forgiven rather than avenged) and partly at European opinion, which had followed Shamil as a romantic freedom-fighter; a hanged Shamil would have been a martyr, a comfortable old pensioner would not. The conquest's darkest chapter was the fate of the **Circassians** of the northwest Caucasus. In the 1860s the Russian state, having decided their coastal homeland was strategically intolerable, drove the bulk of an entire people off its land and into the sea. Scholarly estimates of those expelled to the Ottoman Empire range from several hundred thousand to over a million, and the deaths, from starvation, exposure, disease, and drowning in overloaded boats during the deportation itself, are credibly put in the hundreds of thousands. Many specialists describe it plainly as ethnic cleansing, and some as genocide. It is the single worst atrocity in this book, the empire spent a century minimizing it, and the descendant communities have never forgotten it. This is the conquest's true face, and the book states it plainly rather than leaving it as "pacification."

**East and southeast, into Central Asia and Siberia.** Across the middle decades of the century, Russian armies absorbed the Muslim khanates and emirates of Central Asia, **Tashkent** taken in 1865, **Bukhara** and Samarkand reduced to vassalage, the **Khanate of Khiva** stormed in 1873, the oasis of **Merv** annexed in 1884, until the empire's southern frontier ran up against Afghanistan and the borders of British India. This brought Russia into a long, mostly cold strategic rivalry with the British Empire across Central Asia that the British called the "Great Game," and it nearly came to war at the **Panjdeh incident** of 1885, when Russian troops seized a contested oasis on the ill-defined Afghan frontier and Britain, treating Afghanistan as the shield of its Indian empire, came within a diplomatic hair of fighting Russia over it before the border was settled by negotiation. Meanwhile **Siberia**, conquered for fur over the previous two centuries (the fur trade, Chapter 8), was being turned from a penal dumping ground and trapper's frontier into something the state wanted to actually populate and exploit, a transformation that hangs on one machine, addressed below. The empire reached the Pacific, founding the port of **Vladivostok** ("Ruler of the East," the name is a small confession of the whole enterprise) in 1860.

And then the empire did something almost unique in imperial history: it voluntarily *sold* a piece of itself. **Russian America**, the Alaskan colony (Chapter 8), was remote, barely populated by Russians, expensive to defend, no longer profitable now that the sea otters were nearly hunted out, and, the St Petersburg calculation went, indefensible against Britain (whose Canada was next door) in any future war and better turned into cash and into a friendship with the rising United States. In 1867 Russia sold Alaska to the United States for 7.2 million dollars, about two cents an acre. Derided in the American press at the time as "Seward's Folly" after the secretary of state who bought it, it became, after gold and then oil, one of the great bargains in the history of states. For this book the sale is diagnostic: the empire that would not surrender an inch of autocratic power or a single serf without crisis would cheerfully sell an entire North American territory, because land at the far edge was negotiable in a way that power at the center never, ever was.

The machine that changed everything in the east was the **Trans-Siberian Railway**, begun in 1891 and substantially completed by the early 1900s (with a final link finished around 1916). It is the physical answer to the problem this whole book opened with in Chapter 1, the impossible size of the empire. It runs from Moscow to Vladivostok on the Pacific, roughly 5,700 miles (9,200 km), and remains to this day the longest single continuous railway line on earth; to anchor it for an American reader, it is more than twice the distance from New York to Los Angeles, a single rail line longer than the entire United States is wide, more than doubled. It collapsed a journey that had taken months into a journey of about a week, made it possible to move armies and settlers across the continent, opened Siberia to mass peasant resettlement (millions of land-hungry peasants moved east along it in the empire's last decades, a relief valve for the land hunger the Emancipation had left, Chapter 14), and drew Russia into the Far Eastern ambitions, a railway shortcut across Manchuria, a warm-water naval base at Port Arthur, that lead directly into the catastrophe of the war with Japan in Chapter 19. The Trans-Siberian is the single most impressive engineering grandeur of the late empire and, like every grandeur in this book, it carried the seed of a disaster, in this case the overreach into Asia that Japan punished in 1905.

Now the part of the multi-ethnic empire that bears most directly on the fuse, because it created millions of people with a specific reason to want the empire gone. As the late empire grew more anxious and more reactionary (Chapter 18), it adopted a policy called **russification**: the active pressure on non-Russian peoples, Poles, Ukrainians, Finns, Baltic peoples, Georgians, Armenians, and others, to adopt the Russian language and Orthodox religion and surrender their own institutions and autonomy. Polish and Ukrainian-language education and publishing were restricted. The **Ems Ukaz**, a secret decree Alexander II signed in 1876 while taking the waters at the German spa of Bad Ems, banned the printing and import of almost all Ukrainian-language books and the use of Ukrainian on the stage, on the official theory that Ukrainian was not a real language but a peasant corruption of Russian invented by Polish agitators. The Ukrainian national poet **Taras Shevchenko**, himself born a serf and bought out of bondage by admirers, had already, a generation earlier, been arrested and sentenced to ten years of military exile under Nicholas I, forbidden to write or paint, essentially for having made Ukrainian a literary language at all. Finland's cherished autonomy was eroded. The Uniate Church (Eastern-rite Catholics) was forcibly absorbed into Orthodoxy. Russification did not assimilate these peoples; it did the opposite, it *created* or hardened modern nationalist movements among Poles, Ukrainians, Finns, and others by giving each of them a concrete, daily, language-and-faith grievance against the empire, and in 1917 those nationalisms helped the empire come apart along exactly these seams.

And then there is the empire's specific treatment of its Jews, which is the chapter's, and arguably the book's, sharpest single indictment, and must be stated without softening. When Russia partitioned Poland (Chapter 8) it acquired the largest Jewish population in the world. Rather than grant them the rights of subjects, the empire confined the great majority of its Jews, by law, to a designated western region called the **Pale of Settlement**, an area roughly the size of a few large US states, outside which Jews were generally forbidden to live or own land, subject on top of that to quotas on education and bans on many professions. The Pale was, in effect, a country-sized legal ghetto, maintained for over a century. After the assassination of Alexander II in 1881 (Chapter 15), in which a Jewish woman's marginal involvement was seized on as a pretext, a wave of **pogroms**, violent mob attacks on Jewish communities involving killing, rape, and the destruction of homes and businesses, swept the southwestern empire, and pogroms recurred in worse waves around 1903–1906. The historical debate over how far the central government actively organized the pogroms versus tolerated, encouraged, and failed to punish them is genuine and ongoing; what is not in dispute, and is the point, is that the imperial state maintained the legal cage, fostered the climate, and protected the perpetrators far more reliably than the victims. The consequences ran straight into the rest of this book and far beyond it: the pogroms and the Pale together drove the largest Jewish emigration in history, much of it to the United States (a great many American Jewish families trace their arrival precisely to the wave of flight set off by the pogroms of 1881 onward), pushed others toward the new Zionist movement, and pushed a disproportionate number of young Jews, with overwhelming reason to want the autocracy destroyed, into the revolutionary movements of Chapter 18. The empire manufactured, by deliberate policy, some of its own most determined enemies. That sentence is the whole thesis of this chapter, and very nearly the whole thesis of the book: the grandeur, the railway across a continent, the empire of all the Russias, was bought at a cost in unfreedom so wide that it eventually included tens of millions of people who had every reason to help light the powder, and in Chapter 20 they did.

# Chapter 18 — The Counter-Reformation

The People's Will killed Alexander II on 1 March 1881 (Chapter 15) expecting the revolution. What they got was his son, and his son spent thirteen years systematically destroying as much of his murdered father's reform as he could reach, on the explicit theory that the Tsar Liberator's mistake had been liberating anything at all. **Alexander III** (the third emperor of that name, son of Alexander II, ruled 1881–1894) is the regime's clearest answer, after Nicholas I (Chapter 12), to the question this book keeps asking the autocracy: shown that the system is failing, will you change it or harden it? Alexander III hardened it with a conviction and a competence that make him, in a grim way, the most successful reactionary in the book. He was physically enormous, personally honest, domestically devoted, contemptuous of constitutions and intellectuals and everything Western, and certain that Russia's salvation lay in undiluted autocracy, militant Orthodoxy, and Russian nationalism. His reign was outwardly stable, even peaceful (he avoided major wars and was called, with some irony, "the Peacemaker"). That outward stability is the chapter's trap, because underneath it the regime was simultaneously freezing political life solid *and* unleashing an economic transformation that would shatter that frozen surface from below, and the contradiction between those two policies, repression on top, capitalism underneath, is the specific compound that 1905 (Chapter 19) ignites.

The mind of the reaction had a name: **Konstantin Pobedonostsev** (po-be-do-NOS-tsev), Alexander III's former tutor, the lay head (Ober-Procurator) of the Holy Synod (the government department that ran the church, Chapter 4), and the most influential ideologue of late autocracy. Pobedonostsev was not a thug; he was an intelligent, cultured, deeply pessimistic reactionary who genuinely believed that democracy, parliaments, juries, a free press, and equality were the "great lie of our time," that they led only to chaos and the rule of demagogues, and that the Russian masses needed the firm, paternal, sacred authority of autocracy and church the way a child needs a parent. He drafted the manifesto, issued weeks after the assassination, that publicly buried Loris-Melikov's near-constitution (Chapter 15) and reasserted unlimited autocracy as God's order. Under him and Alexander III came the **Counter-Reforms**: the elected zemstvo and town governments (Chapter 14) were brought under tighter bureaucratic control and the franchise narrowed in favor of the nobility; a new official, the "land captain," was created to put appointed gentry authority back over the peasants, partially undoing the personal liberty of 1861 in daily practice; university autonomy was crushed and tuition raised to keep out the poor (the *raznochintsy* of Chapter 15 were the explicit target); censorship tightened; the independent press was throttled. Russification (Chapter 17) intensified, the Pale and the disabilities on Jews were enforced harder and the pogroms of 1881 went essentially unpunished, and the secret police, reorganized after 1881 as the **Okhrana** (the empire's last and most sophisticated political-police agency, the lineal heir of the Third Section, Chapter 12, and a direct institutional ancestor of the Soviet security state), became extremely effective, penetrating revolutionary groups with informers and double agents so thoroughly that for two decades it kept the organized revolutionary movement small, fragmented, and largely in exile or prison. By the standard of holding the political surface still, the counter-reformation worked. The revolutionary movement of the 1880s was nearly strangled.

And then the regime did the thing that made all the strangling beside the point. The same state that was freezing politics decided, out of strategic necessity, to force-march Russia into the industrial revolution, eighty years after Britain, decades after Germany and the United States, and roughly in step with Meiji Japan, which had started from a comparable standing start at almost the same time. The difference between the two, which the Industrial Revolution narrative draws out at length, is the difference that decides 1905. Japan rebuilt its state, a constitution, a parliament, a modern bureaucracy, a broadened political class, in order to *run* the industrialization, so that the new economy produced a political coalition with a stake in the regime. Russia used its unreconstructed autocratic state to *own* the industrialization while refusing to share a particle of political power, so that the new economy produced a working class with no voice and nothing to lose. Same method, opposite institutional choice, and Japan would collect on the difference at Tsushima. The Crimean War (Chapter 13) had taught the lesson; the danger of remaining a non-industrial great power in an industrial age was now an article of state faith. The man who acted on it was **Sergei Witte** (VIT-te), finance minister through the 1890s, the most consequential economic statesman in the empire's history. **Witte's industrialization program** was a deliberate, state-driven crash modernization, and it was not improvised: it was, almost point for point, the standard nineteenth-century latecomer playbook that Germany and the United States had already run and Meiji Japan was running at the same moment, the protective-tariff, infant-industry doctrine the German economist Friedrich List had set out for exactly this situation (the Industrial Revolution narrative traces the same playbook across all of them). Witte put Russia on the **gold standard** in 1897, which both stabilized the currency and plugged Russia into the British-anchored global financial system through which capital could flow worldwide, to attract foreign investment Russia could not generate at home, which came mainly from France and Belgium, France because the 1894 Franco-Russian alliance made lending to Russia a strategic act backed by the French state, Belgium because its banks and heavy-industry firms were aggressively exporting capital; he used protective tariffs, state orders, and direct state enterprise to build heavy industry; and he made the **Trans-Siberian Railway** (Chapter 17) and railway-building generally the spine of the whole effort, because railways consumed the coal, iron, and steel that built the industrial base. It worked, by the numbers, spectacularly: in the 1890s Russian industrial output grew at among the fastest rates in the world, the **Baku oil** fields (in the Caucasus, Chapter 17) made the empire, around the turn of the century, briefly the world's largest oil producer, before American output and Baku's own labor unrest and the 1905 upheavals knocked it back, and huge new industrial concentrations grew up around St Petersburg, Moscow, the Ukrainian coal-and-steel basin, and Baku. The grandeur of the late empire is, substantially, Witte's furnaces and rails.

Here is the detonator the chapter has been assembling, stated as plainly as the book can. State-forced industrialization created, very fast, a new class that the autocracy had no place for and no way to control: an urban industrial **proletariat** (the wage-working class of the factories, the term from the European socialist movement), concentrated in huge numbers in a few cities, working twelve-hour-plus days for low wages in dangerous conditions, packed into barracks, with no legal trade unions, no right to strike, no political voice, and, unlike the scattered peasantry, gathered together in the thousands where their grievances could be organized. Witte built the working class; Pobedonostsev guaranteed it had no legal way to express a single demand. The autocracy had spent a century terrified of the peasant Pugachev (Chapter 7) and the educated radical (Chapter 15), and now, by its own industrial policy, it manufactured a third and far more dangerous adversary in the one place, the modern city, where revolutionaries could actually reach them. And the new class arrived precarious. Russia's countryside was already being squeezed by the same force squeezing farmers across the whole industrial world: the long agrarian depression of the 1873–1896 period, when a flood of cheap American prairie wheat collapsed grain prices everywhere and gutted farm incomes from Britain to Germany to the Russian black-earth belt (the Industrial Revolution narrative covers the European side). Elsewhere it caused emigration and political radicalization; in Russia, where the regime's relief machinery was incompetent and the peasantry already land-poor from the cheated Emancipation, the same global price collapse plus a bad harvest produced something far more lethal, the **Great Famine of 1891–1892**, in which somewhere on the order of hundreds of thousands of people died (the figure is debated and was deliberately obscured by the regime, which long refused even to use the word "famine"), and the government's visible incompetence in relief drove educated society decisively into opposition. The famine is usually dated as the moment a generation of the intelligentsia gave up on the regime entirely. One of them was a young law graduate from the Volga town of Simbirsk named Vladimir Ulyanov. Four years earlier, in 1887, his older brother Alexander, a university student, had been hanged for his part in a People's Will splinter group's plot to assassinate Alexander III with bombs concealed in hollowed-out books; the execution radicalized the family and pointed the surviving brother, in time, toward a colder and more disciplined kind of revolution than his brother's had been.

That generation now had a new theory, and its arrival is the chapter's last movement, because it is the theory that wins in 1917. Older Russian radicalism had been **Narodism** (Chapter 15), the bet on the peasant commune. The new theory was **Marxism**, the European doctrine of Karl Marx holding that history advances through class struggle, that capitalism inevitably produces an industrial working class that will overthrow it, and that socialism is the scientific destiny of the industrial age. Marxism's appeal to Russian radicals after the failure of "going to the people" was exact: Witte's industrialization was visibly *creating* the very industrial proletariat Marx said would be the revolutionary class, so Russia's forced capitalism could be read not as a defeat for socialism but as the necessary stage *toward* it. The first Russian Marxist organization, the **Emancipation of Labour** group, was founded in exile in 1883 by Plekhanov (the patient-work advocate of the 1879 split, Chapter 15), who now became the founding theorist of Russian Marxism. Out of these circles, in 1898 and effectively in 1903, came the **Russian Social Democratic Labour Party** (the RSDLP), the Marxist party. At its 1903 congress (held in Brussels and London because it was illegal in Russia) the party split, ostensibly over a narrow question of membership rules, actually over a fundamental difference of revolutionary temperament, into two factions whose names come from the Russian for "majority" and "minority": Lenin's group won a single decisive vote late in the congress, after some delegates had walked out, and seized the label "majority" (*bolsheviki*) for good, sticking its rivals with "minority" (*mensheviki*) even though the names flattered one side and were never an accurate running headcount. The **Bolsheviks** ("majority") were led by that same Vladimir Ulyanov, now using the alias **Lenin**, who argued for a small, disciplined, professional, centrally controlled party of revolutionaries. The **Mensheviks** ("minority") favored a broader, more open, more democratic party on the Western social-democratic model. This 1903 split, an obscure quarrel among a few dozen émigrés in a Brussels hall, is one of the most consequential events of the twentieth century, because the Bolshevik faction it created is the one that takes power in 1917 and runs the state that comes after this book. Alongside the Marxists, the heirs of the Narodnik tradition organized as the **Socialist Revolutionary Party** (the SRs), who kept the focus on the peasantry and revived the People's Will tactic of political assassination. By the time the elderly Alexander III died in 1894 of kidney disease, his counter-reformation looked like a complete success: the autocracy was solid, the press was silenced, the revolutionaries were in exile, the economy was booming. Underneath, the regime had built a working class with no voice, ruined a generation's faith in a famine, and let its most determined enemies organize abroad into the party that would finish it. He left all of it, the booming furnaces, the frozen politics, the buried fuse, to his son, who was utterly unequipped for any of it, and whose name was Nicholas. That is the last act.

# Chapter 19 — The Year It Cracked

**Nicholas II** (the second emperor of that name, son of Alexander III, the last tsar, ruled 1894–1917) is the most consequential incompetent in this book, and the word has to be used carefully because his failure was not stupidity or cruelty but something the regime could survive even less: a sincere, gentle, dutiful, devoutly Orthodox man of limited imagination who believed, with absolute conviction, that God had personally entrusted him with unlimited autocratic power and that his sacred duty was to hand that power, undiminished, to his son. He came to the throne at twenty-six in 1894, unprepared and admitting it, and he spent his reign applying the answer Nicholas I and Alexander III had given, hold and harden, to a country that the railways, the factories, the famine, the working class, and the revolutionary parties of Chapter 18 had made completely unlike the country they had ruled. He inherited the buried fuse and he refused, to the last possible day and past it, to believe it was lit. This chapter is the year it became impossible not to see, and the year is 1905, the dress rehearsal that the regime survived just well enough to learn nothing from.

The omens were early and ignored. At his coronation festivities in 1896 a crowd crush at **Khodynka** field in Moscow killed well over a thousand people; the new tsar, advised it would be undiplomatic to cancel, attended a French embassy ball that same evening, and the contrast, a thousand subjects trampled in the morning, the tsar dancing at night, attached itself to him permanently as a kind of folk verdict. The deeper trouble was that Nicholas II combined his father's iron belief in autocracy with none of his father's force, and pursued, against Witte's advice, the reckless Far Eastern adventurism the Trans-Siberian had made possible (Chapter 17): pressing Russian power into Manchuria and Korea, straight into the ambitions of **Japan**, which had just done in forty years the institutional and industrial modernization Russia had refused to do, the Meiji transformation that is its own narrative. Vyacheslav Plehve, the hardline interior minister who ran the empire's police and repression in these years, is supposed to have said he wanted "a small victorious war to stem the tide of revolution", a line so apt it is quoted everywhere and documented nowhere reliably, and which this book passes on with that warning attached. Whatever he actually said, he got a war. It was neither small nor victorious.

The **Russo-Japanese War** (1904–1905) is the Crimean War's lesson (Chapter 13) delivered a second time, harder, and to a regime that had spent fifty years insisting it had learned it. Japan opened the war in February 1904 with a surprise night torpedo attack on the Russian Pacific squadron lying at **Port Arthur**, striking before any declaration of war, a stroke many later pointed to as the model for Pearl Harbor. On land, the Japanese besieged and took Port Arthur and then beat the Russian army at the colossal **Battle of Mukden** (1905), one of the largest land battles in history to that point. At sea came the single most concentrated humiliation in the empire's military history: Russia sent its Baltic Fleet halfway around the world, an 18,000-mile voyage, to relieve the Pacific, and the Japanese navy under Admiral Togo destroyed almost the entire fleet over two days at the **Battle of Tsushima** in May 1905, the decisive damage done in a few shattering hours of the first afternoon. A European great power, the gendarme of Europe, the conqueror of Napoleon, had been comprehensively beaten on land and annihilated at sea by an Asian power the European empires had regarded forty years earlier as a quaint feudal curiosity. The **Treaty of Portsmouth** (1905) ended the war on terms that confirmed the defeat. It was mediated, tellingly, by the United States: President Theodore Roosevelt, wanting neither power dominant in the Pacific where American interests were growing, brokered the talks in New Hampshire and earned a Nobel Peace Prize for it, the first sign that the center of gravity in great-power diplomacy was beginning to cross the Atlantic. The strategic meaning was global, the first modern defeat of a European empire by a non-European one, and it electrified Asia. It also carried a specific lesson that the reformers in every "backward" empire of the era read at once: industrialization paired with institutional reform (Japan) beat industrialization without it (Russia). Reform-minded Ottomans, Chinese, and Persians all absorbed that lesson from 1905; the one regime that did not absorb it was the one that had just been beaten teaching it. The domestic meaning was that the regime had lost the last argument it had, the argument from strength, and had lost it while the new working class it had built was already in the streets.

Because by the time Tsushima sank, the **Revolution of 1905** was already underway, and its trigger is one of the most precisely datable moments in the book. On 9 January 1905 (22 January, Western calendar), a huge, peaceful, unarmed crowd of St Petersburg workers and their families, carrying icons and portraits of the tsar and singing hymns, marched toward the Winter Palace to present a humble petition. They were led by a priest, Father Gapon, who ran a workers' organization that the police themselves had originally sponsored as a controlled, loyal, tsar-revering alternative to the revolutionary unions, the idea being a safety valve they controlled. The valve failed because the grievances it gathered were real: when a strike at a huge St Petersburg plant escalated and the organization's tens of thousands of members looked for redress, Gapon channeled them not into revolution but, exactly as the regime had wanted, into a humble, loyal appeal to the tsar himself, and the regime then shot the loyal appeal in the street, converting its own controlled workers, at a stroke, into people with nothing left to be loyal to. The petition was loyal in tone; it asked for an eight-hour day, a living wage, and, fatally for the regime's mythology, a popularly elected assembly to share in making the country's laws. It addressed the tsar as "Sire" and appealed to him as the people's father, the exact Pugachev-and-1861 belief (Chapters 7, 14) that the good tsar would help if only he knew. The troops guarding the palace approaches opened fire on the crowd. Hundreds were killed (the exact toll is disputed and was minimized by the regime; the order of magnitude, hundreds dead, is solid). It is called **Bloody Sunday**, and its real historical work was psychological and total: it killed, in a single morning, the foundational myth of the entire autocracy, the sacred bond between the tsar-father and his trusting people that Official Nationality (Chapter 12) had made the regime's whole theory of itself. After Bloody Sunday the worker who had carried the tsar's portrait to the palace and been shot for it no longer believed the tsar was his father. That belief had held the unfreedom together, in the peasant's head and the worker's, since before Peter. Bloody Sunday is where it died, and once it was dead the regime had only force left.

The year 1905 then became a general convulsion. Strikes spread across the empire; there were peasant risings, mutinies in the armed forces (the most famous: the crew of the Black Sea battleship **Potemkin**, pushed past endurance by being served maggot-ridden meat and then by the shooting of a sailor who protested, killed their officers, seized the ship, sailed it for days as a rebel fortress unable to find a port that would take them, and finally surrendered it to Romania, the episode Eisenstein later turned into the most influential propaganda film ever made), national revolts on the russified peripheries (Chapter 17), and assassinations by the SRs. In the autumn a general strike paralyzed the country, and in St Petersburg the striking workers spontaneously elected a council to coordinate, the **St Petersburg Soviet** (*soviet* simply means "council"), which in its last weeks, after its first chairman was arrested, was led by a young revolutionary named Leon Trotsky, an institution invented in 1905 that the reader should mark, because the soviets are the form the 1917 revolution takes and the word that names the state that follows this book. With the army still tied down and trickling back from Manchuria, the regime, for the only time in this book, genuinely lost control, and Nicholas II was forced, by Witte's urgent advice and against every fiber of his belief, to do the thing every autocrat in this book had refused: concede. The **October Manifesto** of 1905 promised civil liberties and, decisively, an elected legislature, the **State Duma**, with real legislative power, without whose consent no law could be made. On paper, on 17 October 1905, Russia stopped being an autocracy. Six hundred years after England, the empire had a parliament.

It did not last as promised, and how it was hollowed out is the chapter's final lesson and the precise reason the fuse kept burning. The Manifesto split the opposition exactly as it was designed to: liberals who wanted a constitutional order (organizing as the **Kadet** party) were partly satisfied and partly co-opted; the revolutionary left was not, and the Moscow workers' **December uprising** of 1905 was crushed by the army with artillery, the regime having recovered its nerve and its troops. Then Nicholas II spent the next decade taking the concession back by increments. The **Fundamental Laws of 1906**, issued just before the first Duma met, reasserted that the emperor's power remained "autocratic" and gave him sweeping authority to rule by decree, veto, and dissolve, gutting the Manifesto's spirit before it was a year old. The first two Dumas, too radical for the regime, were dissolved within months. Then, in 1907, the regime simply rewrote the election law by illegal decree to massively over-weight the propertied and Russian voters, engineering a compliant third Duma, an act contemporaries openly called a coup. The lesson the regime took from surviving 1905 was not "we must share power to survive" but "we survived, therefore we need not share power," the identical conclusion Catherine drew from Pugachev and Alexander III from his father's murder, the book's deepest and most fatal pattern, made one last time, with the last chance.

There were two men in the last decade who embody the regime's final choice between its possible futures. One was **Pyotr Stolypin** (sto-LIH-pin), prime minister from 1906, a tough, intelligent conservative who paired ferocious repression of revolution (the field courts-martial that hanged so many that the noose was nicknamed "Stolypin's necktie") with the most serious attempt since Alexander II to actually fix the regime's foundational problem. **Stolypin's land reforms**, his "wager on the strong," tried to break up the peasant commune (the *mir*, Chapters 14–15), let enterprising peasants consolidate and privately own their land, and create a class of prosperous, property-owning, conservative farmers who would have a stake in the existing order rather than in burning it, the one late, intelligent attempt to undo the half-done Emancipation and turn the dangerous peasant into a contented citizen. It needed, by Stolypin's own estimate, twenty years of peace to work. It got about five. In 1911 Stolypin was shot dead at the Kiev opera, in front of the tsar, during a gala performance. The assassin was a revolutionary who was also a paid Okhrana informer, and the police security around the event had let him in. The regime's own secret service had, in effect, delivered the killer of the one minister seriously trying to save the regime, which is as exact a picture as this book can offer of how rotten the autocracy's own machinery had become by 1911. With Stolypin dead, the land reform lost its driver and never got another. The regime's other face was **Grigori Rasputin** (ras-POO-tin), the single most mythologized figure in modern Russian history, around whom almost everything vivid is contested. The verifiable core is this: Rasputin was a Siberian peasant holy man, a self-styled mystic of dubious clerical standing, who gained extraordinary influence at court for one reason this book can state with confidence, because it is genuinely tragic and genuinely consequential. The heir to the throne, the **Tsarevich Alexei**, suffered from **hemophilia**, the inherited disorder in which blood does not clot and a minor injury can be fatal. It ran through the descendants of Queen Victoria, and the empress Alexandra, Victoria's granddaughter, had carried it into the Russian dynasty. The boy's condition was a desperately guarded state secret. Rasputin appeared, repeatedly, able to calm the boy and stop his crises when court doctors could not (the mechanism remains debated, suggestion, the avoidance of aspirin, and coincidence have all been proposed; the cures themselves are well attested even though their cause is not). To the devout, frantic empress this made him a man of God sent to save her son and the dynasty, and her absolute faith in him gave a disreputable Siberian peasant real influence over appointments at the apex of the empire. The lurid stories, the orgies, the political mastermind, the famous unkillable death (the tale that he was fed poison, then shot repeatedly, and still had to be drowned), are mostly the work of his enemies and of an enormous later legend industry, and the reader should treat the cinematic Rasputin as exactly that. But the *structural* fact under the legend is real and is this book's thesis in its final, most concentrated form: the survival of the entire Russian state had come to rest on the secret illness of one child and the influence of one mystic over one frightened mother, because the autocracy was, by 1913, so totally personal, so unshareable, so cut off from any institution that could function without it, that the dynasty's medical secret had become a strategic vulnerability of the largest empire on earth. In 1913 the regime staged the magnificent **Romanov Tercentenary**, three hundred years of the dynasty celebrated with immense ceremony and genuine, if shallow, popular display, the grandeur performing confidence over a state with a hollow center, a fuse at its powder, and, eighteen months away, the war that would end it.

# Chapter 20 — The Fuse Reaches the Powder

This is the chapter the entire book has been a fuse toward, and it is, in the end, shorter and faster than the catastrophe deserves, because that is how it actually happened: a regime that had survived for two and a third centuries by always choosing grandeur over reform took six weeks, in the late winter of 1917, to disintegrate completely, and almost no one, including the revolutionaries, saw it coming until it was over. The argument of this final chapter is the argument of the whole book, collected and detonated: the First World War did not destroy a healthy empire. It applied an unbearable load to a structure that had been engineered, deliberately and repeatedly, with the fatal flaw still in it, and the structure failed exactly along the seam this book has traced from the Decembrists in 1825, the seam between the grandeur the state demanded and the unfreedom it refused to surrender to pay for it.

Russia entered the **First World War** in August 1914, on the side of France and Britain against Germany and Austria-Hungary, in a wave of patriotic enthusiasm that briefly looked, to the regime, like vindication; the capital even changed its German-sounding name from St Petersburg to the Russian **Petrograd**, a small linguistic act that, in retrospect, reads like the empire trying to look more Russian as it walked into the fire. The enthusiasm did not survive contact with the war. The war exposed every structural weakness this book has catalogued, the same audit the Crimean War had run in 1855 (Chapter 13) and the Japanese war in 1905 (Chapter 19), now run a third time at industrial scale and with the entire empire on the table. Russia had the largest army in the world by raw numbers and could not equip it; soldiers were sent to the front, by soldiers' memoirs, officers' reports, and the dispatches of horrified Allied liaison officers alike, without enough rifles, told to pick up the weapons of the fallen. The opening campaign in 1914 produced an immediate catastrophe at the **Battle of Tannenberg** in East Prussia, where a German force encircled and destroyed an entire Russian army (General Alexander Samsonov, commanding the doomed Second Army, walked into the forest during the rout and shot himself rather than report the scale of the disaster). The empire's one genuine military bright spot was the work of General Aleksei Brusilov, an unusually capable and original commander who, in the **Brusilov offensive** of 1916, abandoned the doomed standard tactic of one massive battering-ram assault and instead struck the Austro-Hungarian line in many places at once with short, surprise bombardments, so the enemy could not tell where to send reserves; it was a brilliantly planned and initially stunningly successful attack that demonstrated the Russian army could still fight superbly when competently led, and it bled the army white for gains that could not be held and that the system could not exploit, a perfect final miniature of the whole empire: capable of brilliance, structurally incapable of converting brilliance into a durable result.

The home front is where the empire actually died, and the mechanism is the thesis one last time. Total war required total mobilization of the economy and society, exactly the thing the autocracy was least able to do, because doing it meant trusting and empowering the society it had spent a century refusing to share power with. The transport system (the railways of Chapter 17, built for grandeur and distance, not for total war) buckled under military priority, so that grain existed in the countryside while the cities and the army went hungry. Inflation gutted wages. Industry, mobilized chaotically, could not supply both the front and the population. Educated society, organized in voluntary war-relief bodies (the *zemgor*, the union of zemstvos and towns, run by the local-government men; and the War Industry Committees, run by reform-minded industrialists and engineers to organize private factories for war production), did much of the actual work of keeping the army supplied and asked, reasonably, for a government it could trust, a "ministry of confidence." Nicholas II refused, as every autocrat in this book refused, to share power even to save the war, and then made the single most destructive decision of his reign: in 1915 he personally took command of the army at the front, against almost everyone's advice. Militarily it changed little, since he had no command experience and the actual generalship continued to be done by his staff. Politically it was fatal, in two linked ways. It welded the autocrat's own sacred person directly to every future defeat, so that each lost battle was now visibly *his* defeat rather than a general's. And it removed him physically to distant military headquarters for months on end, leaving the day-to-day government of the capital in the hands of the unpopular German-born empress Alexandra and, through her, of Rasputin (Chapter 19), at the precise moment the regime most needed to look competent and Russian. The German-born empress running the capital on the advice of a Siberian mystic, while the tsar lost battles at the front, was a gift to every enemy the regime had, and the rumors (of treason, of a German plot in the palace, mostly false but universally believed) did more damage than the truth could have. In December 1916, a clique of aristocrats, fronted by Prince Felix Yusupov and including the Grand Duke Dmitri Pavlovich, the tsar's own cousin, murdered Rasputin, in a killing so lurid in the later telling (poison, multiple gunshots, drowning) that it has become folklore and should be treated as such; the verifiable point is that the empire's own ruling elite had concluded the dynasty was destroying itself and had started killing the dynasty's own intimates to try to save it. It did not work. Nothing the regime did in its last winter worked, because the problem was not Rasputin or any one decision; it was the two-hundred-year-old refusal that this whole book has tracked.

The end, when it came, was almost anticlimactic, which is its most important feature. There was no grand revolutionary assault on the Winter Palace in February 1917 (that image belongs to October, to the next story, and is itself partly stagecraft). The **February Revolution** began as a bread queue and a strike. In late February 1917 (early March, Western calendar), in a Petrograd worn down by cold, hunger, war-weariness, and inflation, food shortages and strikes merged with International Women's Day demonstrations into mass protests that grew, day by day, with no central organization, no party directing them, no plan. The revolutionaries who would later run the country were mostly in exile or prison and as surprised as anyone. Nicholas II, at military headquarters, ordered the Petrograd garrison to fire on the crowds and disperse them, the Bloody Sunday solution (Chapter 19), the autocracy's last instrument, force. And the garrison refused. That is the actual moment the Russian Empire ended: not a battle, not a storming, but the soldiers of the capital, themselves mostly recently conscripted peasants and workers, deciding, regiment by regiment over a couple of days, that they would not shoot the crowd and would instead join it. Once the army in the capital would not enforce the autocracy, there was no autocracy, because force was the only thing it had left after Bloody Sunday had killed the myth that had held it before. The instant the troops crossed over, two bodies materialized to fill the vacuum, and the reader must mark them because they are the spine of the next story: the **Provisional Government**, formed by liberal and moderate Duma politicians to run the state, and, reborn from 1905, the **Petrograd Soviet** of workers' and soldiers' deputies, which actually commanded the loyalty of the troops and the streets. From the first day, real power was split between them, "dual power," and the soviet's famous **Order No. 1**, putting military units under the authority of elected soldiers' committees, signaled at once where the guns actually answered.

Nicholas II tried to return to Petrograd, was stopped, and on 2 March 1917 (15 March, Western calendar), in a railway carriage at Pskov, with his own generals and the Duma's emissaries telling him he had no support left anywhere, the last Emperor of All the Russias signed his **abdication**. He abdicated first for himself, then, on being told the boy could not be parted from his parents and would not survive without care, for his hemophiliac son Alexei as well (Chapter 19), and offered the throne to his brother Grand Duke Mikhail, who declined it the next day unless a future constituent assembly confirmed it, which it never would. With those two signatures, in two days, the Romanov dynasty that had ruled Russia since 1613 and the autocracy that had defined the state since long before Peter simply ended. There was no fight for it. After three hundred years and two and a third centuries of empire, when the moment came, no one of consequence was willing to die to keep a tsar on the throne, and that absence, the silence where the defense of the autocracy should have been, is the final verdict of this book. The grandeur had never reached the people who would have had to fight for it, because the price of the grandeur had always been their unfreedom, and in the end they declined to pay any more. The former emperor and his family were placed under arrest. Their fate, the imprisonment, the move to Siberia, and the killing of the entire family by Bolshevik captors at Yekaterinburg in July 1918 (an event around which an entire mythology of escaped survivors, above all the false Anastasias, grew up and persisted for decades until modern forensic and DNA work established that the whole family died, a final entry in this book's long catalogue of too-good-to-be-true Russian stories that turn out to be legends), belongs to the story that begins where this one ends.

And that is the bridge, and the last thing this book owes the reader. The February Revolution did not produce a stable free Russia, the outcome the Decembrists and the reformers and the liberals of this entire book had hoped for across ninety-two years. The Provisional Government, committed to continuing the catastrophic war and unwilling to settle the land question the peasants had wanted since 1861, lost the support of the soldiers and the streets across the chaotic spring and summer of 1917 to the one party that promised, simply, "Peace, Land, Bread," and an end to the war now: the Bolsheviks, the disciplined faction Lenin had built out of the 1903 split in a Brussels hall (Chapter 18). In October 1917 (November, Western calendar) they took power in Petrograd in an armed seizure, then, when the long-promised Constituent Assembly, the freely elected national convention meant to write Russia's permanent constitution, finally met in January 1918 with a non-Bolshevik majority, dispersed it by force within a day of its first session, and began constructing, on the ruins of the empire this book has described, a new state, the world's first avowedly communist one, that would govern the same vast territory, with the same instinct for an unsharable central power and an all-seeing security organ traceable in a straight institutional line from Peter's *fiskaly* through the Third Section and the Okhrana, for most of the twentieth century. The tensions this book has tracked, the impossible size, the grandeur demanded, the freedom withheld, did not end with the empire. They were inherited, in a new and in some ways harsher form, by the regime that replaced it, and that inheritance, what the Soviet state did with the country and the contradictions the Russian Empire bequeathed it, is the subject of its own narrative, which begins exactly here, with the soldiers who would not fire and the tsar who signed his name in a railway carriage and went quietly into the dark.

The long fuse, lit on a December morning in 1825 by aristocratic officers who had seen a freer world and could not unsee it, had taken ninety-two years to burn. In February 1917 it reached the powder, and the most magnificent and most unfree empire the modern world had built came apart in six weeks, almost without a shot fired in its defense, because in the end the grandeur had belonged to so few and cost so many that, when the moment came, there was no one left who thought it was worth saving.

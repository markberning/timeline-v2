# The Chinese Revolution — How a Collapsed Empire Became a Communist Superpower in One Lifetime

**Goal:** Narrative for the Chinese Revolution (1912–1976), the period from the abdication of the last emperor through the warlord wars, the May Fourth awakening, the long duel between Nationalists and Communists, the Japanese invasion, the civil war, and the twenty-seven years in which Mao Zedong remade Chinese society from top to bottom — land reform, the Korean War, the Hundred Flowers, the Great Leap Forward and the famine it caused, and the Cultural Revolution. This is the story the previous book in this chain handed off: the Qing dynasty fell in 1912, and what followed was not order but sixty-four years of revolution that ran through two parties, three civil wars, the deadliest invasion in Chinese history, the deadliest famine in human history, and a decade in which the state turned on itself. It is the most documented and the most politically contested half-century in China's long record, and the book walks the actual sequence without flattening it into either the official triumph or the anti-Communist tragedy, because it was, like the Qing before it, both at once.

**Status:** First draft, 15 chapters.

A note on names and pronunciation: this book uses modern pinyin romanization, the standard the People's Republic adopted in the 1950s (Chapter 11). A handful of names are more famous in their older spelling and are given both ways on first use, because the older form is what English readers will recognize from older books — Mao Zedong was long spelled Mao Tse-tung, Chiang Kai-shek is the old Cantonese-based spelling of a name pinyin would write Jiang Jieshi, Peking is Beijing, Canton is Guangzhou, Chungking is Chongqing. Pinyin is not phonetic for English readers, so unfamiliar names get a pronunciation in parentheses on first use (Zhou Enlai is "joh en-LYE," Jiang Qing is "jyang ching," Qing is "ching").

A note on contested numbers: more than any previous book in this series, this one runs through events whose death tolls, causes, and even basic facts are the subject of live, hostile political dispute — between the Chinese government and its critics, between China and Japan, between the Communist and Nationalist accounts, and among honest historians working from incomplete records. The Great Leap famine, the Cultural Revolution death toll, the Nanjing Massacre, the Tibet question, the Long March legends, and the death of Lin Biao are all contested in ways that matter. Where a number or a story is disputed, the text says so and gives the range rather than picking the most dramatic version. The events are recent and the archives are partly open, so the numbers are far better than the population figures of ancient empires — but they are still estimates with real error bars, and the ones that are genuinely uncertain are flagged rather than smoothed over.

A note on whose revolution this was: for decades this period was taught two ways that barely spoke to each other — a Communist story of national liberation and a Cold War story of totalitarian catastrophe. Both contain real history and both were also propaganda. The current scholarly picture, built since the Chinese archives began opening in the 1980s, is messier and more interesting than either: a genuine social revolution that millions supported, run by a party that delivered national unification and then inflicted the worst peacetime catastrophes in the country's history on the people it had mobilized. This book tries to hold both halves at once.

# Chapter 1 — The Republic That Wasn't

The previous book in this chain ended on a specific morning. On February 12, 1912, in the Forbidden City in Beijing, the regents of a six-year-old boy named **Puyi** (pronounced "poo-EE") signed an edict of abdication, and with it ended not only the **Qing dynasty** (pronounced "ching") — the Manchu empire that had ruled China since 1644 — but the entire institution of imperial monarchy in China, an unbroken chain of dynasties stretching back over two thousand years to the first emperor in 221 BCE (Qing Dynasty Ch 13; the imperial system itself, Qin Dynasty Ch 2). No one in the room could quite believe that the thing that had always been the framework of Chinese political life — the Son of Heaven, the **Mandate of Heaven** (the ancient theory that Heaven grants a virtuous dynasty the right to rule and withdraws it from a corrupt one) — was simply over. This book is about what came next, and the short answer is: not a republic, not order, and not for a very long time anything resembling a stable Chinese state. The revolution that toppled the emperor took six weeks. The revolution to decide what would replace him took the next sixty-four years and killed more Chinese people than any war in the country's history.

The man whose name is attached to the founding of the Republic was **Sun Yat-sen** (pronounced "soon yaht-SEN"), and his real role is one of the first places where the legend and the history diverge. Sun was a Cantonese doctor, Western-educated, Christian, who had spent two decades in exile — Japan, Hawaii, London, San Francisco — raising money among overseas Chinese and plotting failed uprisings against the Qing. In 1905 in Tokyo he had merged several anti-Manchu revolutionary societies into a single organization, the **Tongmenghui** (pronounced "tong-meng-HWAY"), the Revolutionary Alliance, which is the organizational ancestor of every party discussed in this book. He gave the revolution its slogan, the **Three Principles of the People** — *minzu*, *minquan*, *minsheng*, usually translated nationalism, democracy, and the people's livelihood (the third was a vague socialism, land reform without saying so too loudly). What he did not do was lead the rising that actually brought the dynasty down. When the decisive revolt broke out he was in Denver, Colorado, and read about it in a newspaper.

That revolt was the **Wuchang Uprising** (pronounced "woo-CHAHNG"). On October 10, 1911 — a date the Republic would keep as its national day, the "Double Ten" — a bomb went off prematurely in a revolutionary cell's headquarters in the Yangtze city of Wuchang, the membership lists were seized by the Qing police, and the exposed soldiers in the local garrison decided their only option was to mutiny immediately. It worked. Within weeks province after province declared independence from the Qing in a chain reaction. This is worth pausing on, because it is the mechanism the rest of the book keeps repeating: a province "declared independence" not by a popular vote but when its provincial assembly and its military governor calculated that the dynasty was finished and that announcing for the revolution was safer than dying for a lost cause. Power in 1911 China did not flow from the people; it flowed from whoever commanded the soldiers in a given province. The single most important fact in this entire book is that the empire had built, in its last decade, modern provincial armies — and when the center collapsed, those armies did not. They became the warlords of Chapter 2.

---

Sun Yat-sen hurried home and on January 1, 1912, was inaugurated in Nanjing (pronounced "nahn-JING," the old spelling Nanking) as provisional president of the **Republic of China**, the first republic in a major continental Asian state (the short-lived Philippine Republic of 1899 narrowly preceded it). He held the office for six weeks. Real power was not in Nanjing with the idealists; it was in the north, with the one man who commanded the empire's best army.

That man was **Yuan Shikai** (pronounced "ywen shr-KAI"). Yuan was the Qing general who had built and personally commanded the **Beiyang Army** (pronounced "bay-YAHNG," meaning "northern ocean"), the most modern military force in China, drilled on the German model. In the 1911 crisis the Qing court recalled him to crush the revolution, and Yuan, who could read a situation, instead brokered a deal that made him the indispensable man to both sides. To the Qing he was the only general who could save them; to the revolutionaries he was the only man who could deliver the emperor's abdication without a long war. He extracted his price from both. He got the Qing to abdicate (the deal included a generous "Articles of Favorable Treatment" letting Puyi keep the Forbidden City, his title, and a pension — he stays in that palace until 1924 and matters again in Chapter 5). And he got Sun Yat-sen to step aside and hand him the presidency. The Republic was, from its sixth week, headed by the empire's strongman, not its revolutionaries. The contrast with the broadly contemporary collapse of the other great land empires is the spine of this whole story: the **Ottoman Empire** ended at almost the same moment and was rebuilt by an army officer, Mustafa Kemal Atatürk (the general who founded modern Turkey), into a secular single-party national state (Ottoman Empire Ch 12); China would take far longer and far more blood to reach its own single-party national state, and would do it twice.

The revolutionaries' answer to Yuan was a parliament and an election. The Tongmenghui was reorganized into a normal political party to contest China's first national elections — the **Kuomintang** (pronounced "kwo-min-TAHNG," the Nationalist Party, often abbreviated KMT; in pinyin Guomindang). It was led not by Sun but by a brilliant young organizer named **Song Jiaoren** (pronounced "soong jyow-REN"), who built the KMT into the clear winner of the 1912–13 parliamentary elections and was, at thirty, about to become premier and box Yuan in constitutionally. In March 1913, at the Shanghai railway station, on his way to take office, Song Jiaoren was shot and died two days later. The trail of payments led upward toward Yuan Shikai's premier and, most historians conclude, to Yuan himself, though it was never proven in a court — which tells you everything about whether China in 1913 had courts that could try the head of state. The first attempt to make the Republic work through ballots and parliaments ended with the winner murdered on a train platform.

---

What followed set the pattern for the next fifteen years. The KMT's southern provinces rose against Yuan in the summer of 1913 in what is called the **Second Revolution**. It was crushed in two months — Yuan had the Beiyang Army, the rebels had enthusiasm — and Sun Yat-sen fled back into exile in Japan. Yuan then did what strongmen do: he banned the KMT, dissolved the parliament, rewrote the constitution to make himself president for life, and took a large secret "Reorganization Loan" from a consortium of foreign banks to pay his army, mortgaging Chinese customs revenue to do it. The Republic was now a dictatorship with a parliament-shaped hole where its legitimacy should have been.

Then, in 1915, two things happened that broke Yuan. The first came from outside. Japan, which had spent fifty years transforming itself from a feudal society into an industrial empire (Meiji Japan, the whole arc) and had already taken Taiwan and Korea, used the cover of the First World War — the European powers were busy killing each other and could not intervene in Asia — to present Yuan's government with the **Twenty-One Demands**, a secret list that would have turned China into a Japanese protectorate: Japanese control over Manchuria and Shandong, Japanese advisers embedded in the Chinese government, Chinese promises to buy Japanese arms. Yuan stalled, leaked the demands to provoke international embarrassment, and accepted a reduced version. It was not enough to save him and it was enough to brand him: the day he accepted became, for a generation of students, the "Day of National Humiliation," a phrase that connects directly back to the larger **century of humiliation** narrative the Qing book traced from the Opium Wars onward (Qing Dynasty Ch 7) and forward to everything Mao would later say about why China had to be strong.

The second thing Yuan did to himself. In December 1915, persuaded by flatterers and his own ambition, he announced that the Republic was a mistake and that he would become emperor of a new dynasty. It was a catastrophic misreading of his own country. The thing the 1911 revolution had genuinely killed was the monarchy's prestige; even Chinese who wanted a strongman did not want a new Son of Heaven. The southern provinces revolted in the **National Protection War**, his own generals declined to fight hard for his throne, and within three months Yuan cancelled the monarchy, and in June 1916 he died — officially of kidney failure, effectively of humiliation. He had been emperor for eighty-three days of a dynasty no one recognized. With him died the last institution holding China together: the Beiyang Army had obeyed Yuan personally, and now Yuan was gone. The army did not disband. It divided. That is Chapter 2.

# Chapter 2 — The Warlord Era

For the next twelve years China had, on paper, a national government in Beijing — a president, a cabinet, a parliament, a flag, a seat at international conferences, recognition by foreign powers, control of the customs revenue. It is one of the most useful illusions in modern history to see through clearly, because the entire struggle of this book is the struggle to make that paper government real. In fact, from Yuan Shikai's death in 1916 to the Nationalist reunification in 1928, China was a patchwork of territories run by generals with private armies, and the government in Beijing was simply whichever general's faction currently held the capital. This is the **Warlord Era** (the Chinese term, *junfa*, "military cliques"), and it is the soil everything else in the book grows out of.

The mechanism is worth understanding because it explains why the revolution had to be military. When Yuan died, the Beiyang Army he had personally commanded had no single successor it would all obey. It fractured along the lines of which generals had trained under which patrons. The result was a set of regional **cliques** — not ideological parties but personal networks of officers bound by loyalty, marriage, and money. The two that mattered most in the north were the **Zhili clique** (pronounced "jr-LEE," based around the capital region) and the **Anhui clique** (pronounced "ahn-HWAY"), which fought each other for control of Beijing. Behind them, in the northeast, was the **Fengtian clique** (pronounced "fung-TYEN") run out of Manchuria by **Zhang Zuolin** (pronounced "jahng dzwo-LIN"), a former bandit who had risen to command the richest industrial region in China and who survived by playing the Japanese, who wanted Manchuria, against everyone else. Zhang is a useful single figure to track: he ends this chapter's era by being blown up in his private railway car in 1928 by Japanese officers who decided he was no longer pliable enough — the first move in the Japanese campaign that drives Chapters 5 through 8.

A warlord's power rested on three things: soldiers, the silver to pay them, and territory to extract the silver from. There was no national project. A warlord taxed his region — often years of land tax in advance, often a fresh tax for every campaign — to fund an army whose only purpose was to hold or expand the region that paid for the army. The wars were frequent, the battles often theatrical and bloodless at the top (generals bribed each other's subordinates more often than they fought them) and ruinous at the bottom (conscription, requisitioning, banditry, and the wreckage of the rural economy). The reader should hold onto one image: a Chinese peasant in 1923 owed taxes to a government in Beijing he had never seen, a provincial warlord who actually collected, a local garrison commander, and a landlord — and any of them might also be press-ganging his sons. This is the rural misery that the Communists would later convert into the most powerful political weapon of the century.

---

The Beijing government in these years did two things that mattered beyond its borders. First, in 1917, it entered the **First World War** on the Allied side — not by sending soldiers to fight but by sending labor. Roughly 140,000 Chinese men, the **Chinese Labour Corps**, dug trenches, hauled shells, and buried the dead on the Western Front in France. China entered the war for a specific, rational reason: a seat at the peace conference, and the expectation that as a victor it would get back the German concessions on its own soil — the leased territory and railway rights in **Shandong** (pronounced "shahn-DOONG"), the coastal province that is also the birthplace of Confucius, which Germany had bullied out of the Qing in the 1890s. Hold that expectation; it detonates in Chapter 3.

Second, the Beijing era produced one genuinely absurd episode that shows how dead the monarchy was. In July 1917 a pigtailed traditionalist warlord named Zhang Xun, invited to Beijing to mediate a political dispute, instead marched his troops in and reinstalled the now eleven-year-old Puyi on the throne. The **Manchu Restoration** lasted twelve days. Other warlords, who had no interest in a restored emperor above them, sent an army (and, in a detail that belongs in the story, dropped a few small bombs from an airplane on the Forbidden City, one of the first aerial bombardments in Chinese history), Zhang Xun fled to a foreign legation, and Puyi abdicated for the second time before his twelfth birthday. The lesson everyone drew was the same one Yuan had taught: the imperial form was finished. The open question — the question this whole book answers — was what political form could actually rule China, and the warlord years proved it was not going to be a parliament either. China had a government too weak to govern and a society with no agreed way to choose one. Into that vacuum, in the same years, came two things: a Japanese-flavored industrial economy on the coast, and a revolution in ideas among the young.

---

The coastal economy is the part most readers will not expect. While the countryside ground down and the warlords fought, the **treaty ports** — the cities, Shanghai above all, where foreign powers had since the Opium Wars held enclaves with their own law and police (Qing Dynasty Ch 7) — boomed. The First World War cut off European imports and European competition, and Chinese-owned mills, banks, shipping lines, and factories grew fast in the gap. Shanghai in the 1920s was one of the largest cities on earth, with skyscrapers, a stock exchange, a film industry, gangsters, and a Chinese commercial and professional middle class that had not existed two generations earlier. This matters for two reasons. It created the urban working class that the Communist Party would first try to organize (Chapter 4). And it created, alongside the mission schools and colleges the Christian churches had built across China, a Westernized, literate, restless young elite — the students. The intellectual ferment of these students, fed by translations like **Yan Fu's** rendering of Thomas Huxley and Herbert Spencer that injected "survival of the fittest" into Chinese political thought as a national life-or-death imperative, is the subject of the next chapter, and it is where the actual revolution — the one that reorganized how hundreds of millions of people lived — begins.

# Chapter 3 — May Fourth and the New Culture

If you had to point to a single day when modern China's mind changed, the conventional answer is May 4, 1919, and the conventional answer is roughly right, as long as you understand that the day was the eruption of something that had been building for years. That something is called the **New Culture Movement**, and it is the most important thing in this book that is not a war.

The New Culture Movement was a revolt of the young against the entire inherited civilization. Its organ was a magazine, **New Youth** (*Xin Qingnian*), founded in 1915 by **Chen Duxiu** (pronounced "chen doo-SHYOO"), a fierce essayist who would later co-found the Communist Party and then be expelled from it. Its argument was radical to the point of vertigo: that China was being humiliated and dismembered not because it lacked guns or factories — the Qing reformers had said that and failed (Qing Dynasty Ch 10) — but because its core culture, two thousand years of **Confucianism** (the ethical and political system, built on hierarchy, filial obedience, and rule by a scholar-official class trained in the classics, that had been the operating system of every Chinese dynasty), was a cage. The slogans were "Mr. Science" and "Mr. Democracy" (*Sai xiansheng* and *De xiansheng*), and the targets were the Confucian family, the subordination of women, arranged marriage, the classical written language itself, and the idea that the past was a model rather than a dead weight.

The language point sounds technical and was actually the most far-reaching. For two millennia, serious Chinese writing had been done in **classical Chinese** (*wenyan*), a terse literary language as distant from ordinary speech as Latin is from spoken Italian — learnable only through years of study of the classics, which is precisely what made the old scholar-official class a class. The New Culture writers, led by the American-trained philosopher **Hu Shi** (pronounced "hoo SHR"), argued for writing in **vernacular Chinese** (*baihua*), the actual spoken language. This was not a style preference. A literature written the way people spoke could be read by people who had not spent a decade memorizing Confucius. It is the precondition for mass literacy, mass politics, mass propaganda, and a national readership — everything that comes later in this book. The movement's greatest writer, **Lu Xun** (pronounced "loo SHUN," the pen name of Zhou Shuren), proved the point by writing the first great works of modern vernacular fiction, savage short stories like "Diary of a Madman" and "The True Story of Ah Q," in which traditional Chinese society is portrayed, respectively, as cannibalism dressed up as virtue and as a nation of self-deluding losers congratulating themselves on imaginary moral victories. Lu Xun is to modern China roughly what no single figure is to any Western literature — its conscience, claimed by every later faction including the Communists who would have jailed him.

---

Then came the betrayal that turned a cultural movement into a political one. In 1919 the **Paris Peace Conference** met to settle the First World War. China, an Allied victor, sent a delegation expecting the return of the German concessions in Shandong. Instead, the conference — honoring secret wartime deals, and a secret agreement Japan had extracted from the bankrupt Beijing warlord government itself — awarded Germany's Shandong rights to **Japan**. The news reached Beijing and on **May 4, 1919**, around three thousand students from Peking University and other colleges marched to Tiananmen, the gate of the old imperial palace, denounced the government's pro-Japanese officials, beat one of them, and burned a minister's house. The government arrested students; the arrests triggered larger protests; the protests triggered a merchants' strike and a workers' strike in Shanghai; and within a month the government had backed down, refused to sign the Versailles Treaty, and dismissed the officials. It was the first time in modern Chinese history that street action by students, merchants, and workers had reversed a decision of the state. It is called the **May Fourth Movement**, and "May Fourth" became shorthand for an entire generation's conviction that the Chinese people, organized, could remake the country — and that the existing government, which could not even defend the country at a peace table, had to go.

The May Fourth generation now went looking for a method, and it split, roughly, two ways. One road was liberal, gradualist, education-first — Hu Shi's road, "study problems, not isms." The other road had just been demonstrated, spectacularly, by a country most Chinese intellectuals had ignored: in 1917 the **Bolshevik Revolution** in Russia had shown a disciplined revolutionary party seizing a vast, backward, peasant empire and, crucially, renouncing (in propaganda, at least) the old Tsarist privileges in China. To young Chinese radicals furious at the Western powers who had just handed Shandong to Japan, **Marxism-Leninism** offered something the liberal road did not: an explanation of why the powerful nations preyed on the weak ones (imperialism, the highest stage of capitalism), and an organizational technology — the disciplined vanguard party — for doing something about it.

---

The first major Chinese intellectual to take Bolshevism seriously was **Li Dazhao** (pronounced "lee dah-JAO"), the head librarian of Peking University, who ran a Marxist study group. Among the people around that library, in the lowest job, registering newspapers for a salary of eight dollars a month, was a tall, bookish assistant from Hunan province named **Mao Zedong** (pronounced "maow dzuh-DOONG," older spelling Mao Tse-tung), then in his mid-twenties, who would later say it was in this period that he became a Marxist. Hold that detail; the man sweeping up in the library runs the country for the second half of this book.

The study groups became a party with outside help. The Soviet Union had created the **Comintern** (the Communist International, the Moscow-run organization for fomenting and directing Communist parties worldwide), and Comintern agents came to China with money, organizational expertise, and a strategy. In July 1921, in a girls' school in the French Concession of Shanghai (and, when police got suspicious, finishing on a tourist boat on a lake), a dozen or so men representing perhaps fifty members across China held the **First Congress of the Chinese Communist Party**. Mao Zedong was one of the delegates. Set the scale honestly: this was a tiny sect of intellectuals in a country of more than 400 million, with no army, no peasants, no plan beyond labor organizing. What turned it into a contender was the same Comintern that founded it — and the Comintern's strategy was not to build the little Communist Party on its own. It was to marry it to the much larger Nationalist Party of Sun Yat-sen. That marriage, and the bloodbath it ended in, is Chapter 4.

A note the reader should carry forward: the same Soviet Comintern was at this moment also reorganizing the Nationalists, and the Leninist party model — a disciplined hierarchy with a party-controlled army and a party cell in every unit — became the template for *both* Chinese parties. Almost everything that follows is two Leninist parties, born from the same advisers, fighting each other for fifty years. India, decolonizing at the same time, took the opposite road — a mass party that accepted elections and gave up power when it lost them (Modern India, the Congress arc) — and the divergence between the Chinese and Indian answers to the same problem (how does a humiliated Asian giant become modern and strong?) is the single most illuminating comparison in this book, returned to whenever the two roads visibly fork.

# Chapter 4 — The First United Front and the Northern Expedition

By 1922 Sun Yat-sen had been a revolutionary for thirty years and had almost nothing to show for it. He had a base in Canton (Guangzhou, the southern port city), a rival government no one recognized, no reliable army of his own — he depended on whichever southern warlord currently tolerated him — and a party, the Kuomintang, that was a loose club of notables, not a fighting machine. The Western powers he had courted for decades had never given him serious help. The country that did was the Soviet Union, and the price of the help was an alliance with the Communists.

This is the **First United Front**, agreed in principle in the 1923 **Sun-Joffe Manifesto** (Adolph Joffe was the Soviet envoy), and it is one of the strangest arrangements in revolutionary history: the Communists, on Comintern orders and over the objections of many of their own members, joined the Kuomintang *as individuals*, keeping their own party secretly intact inside the larger one — a "bloc within." Sun got Soviet money, Soviet weapons, and Soviet advisers, led by the formidable Comintern agent **Mikhail Borodin** (pronounced "buh-roh-DEEN"), who reorganized the flabby KMT into a disciplined Leninist party with a propaganda department, a party-controlled press, and, decisively, a party army.

That army began at the **Whampoa Military Academy** (pronounced "wahm-POH," the old spelling of Huangpu), founded near Canton in 1924 with Soviet funding and a Soviet curriculum that drilled officers in political loyalty as hard as in tactics. Its commandant was a rising Nationalist officer who had trained in Japan and visited the Soviet Union, **Chiang Kai-shek** (pronounced "chyang kai-SHEK," the name pinyin renders Jiang Jieshi). Its director of the political department — the man teaching the cadets *why* they fought — was a young Communist named **Zhou Enlai** (pronounced "joh en-LYE"). The two men running the school that built the Republic's army were the leaders of the two parties that would fight each other to 1949 and, across the Taiwan Strait, far beyond it. Whampoa is where the entire later civil war's officer corps, on both sides, was forged.

---

Then Sun Yat-sen died. In March 1925, of liver cancer, in Beijing, he left a famous deathbed political will (its most-quoted line: "the revolution is not yet successful; comrades must still strive") and a party with no agreed successor and a built-in fault line: a left wing comfortable with the Soviet alliance and the Communists inside it, and a right wing that loathed both. The contest to inherit Sun's mantle ran for two years, and the winner was the man with the army. Chiang Kai-shek was not Sun's obvious heir — he was a soldier, not a founding ideologue — but he commanded Whampoa's loyalty, and in a revolution built on a party army, that was the decisive asset. In 1926 he moved against the Communists and Soviet advisers in a sharp, limited purge (the "Canton Coup," or Zhongshan Incident) that curbed their influence without yet breaking the alliance, because he still needed Soviet guns for the campaign he had been preparing his whole career.

That campaign was the **Northern Expedition**: the long-promised military march out of the southern base to smash the warlords and reunify China under the Kuomintang. It launched in 1926, and it worked far faster than anyone expected. The reason it worked is the reason this period matters. The Nationalist armies were preceded by political organizers — many of them Communists, working the bloc-within — who ran ahead into the cities and the countryside, organized strikes that paralyzed warlord rear areas, and built **peasant associations** and labor unions that turned the population against the warlords before the army arrived. The most consequential of these organizers filed a report in early 1927 that the orthodox Communists found alarming. **Mao Zedong**, sent to investigate the peasant movement in his home province of Hunan, came back arguing that the revolution's real force was not the small urban working class that Marxist theory designated but the vast, furious, land-hungry peasantry — "a hurricane," he wrote, that would sweep all before it, and that the party should ride rather than restrain. Orthodox Marxism said revolutions are made by industrial workers in cities. Mao's heresy — that in China the peasants were the revolution — is the single idea that, twenty years later, wins him the country. It is also, fifty years later, the idea behind the catastrophes of Chapters 12 through 14.

---

The United Front died the way it was always going to die, in the cities the Communists had organized. By early 1927 the Nationalist armies were approaching the lower Yangtze. Communist-led workers in **Shanghai** rose and seized the Chinese parts of the city, delivering it to the advancing army. Chiang Kai-shek arrived at the head of that army with a different plan. Shanghai was the financial capital, and its money — the bankers, the industrialists, and the Green Gang, the criminal syndicate that ran the docks and the opium and the labor rackets — wanted nothing to do with Communists organizing their workers. On April 12, 1927, Chiang's forces and Green Gang gunmen turned on their Communist allies in a coordinated massacre, killing union activists and party members in the streets of Shanghai over several days. Similar purges followed across Nationalist-held China. The death toll ran into the thousands and the political effect was total: the urban Communist Party was decapitated. Within months even the rival left-Nationalist government at Wuhan, which had tried to keep the alliance, also expelled the Communists, and the Comintern's whole China strategy lay in ruins. The Communists, what was left of them, fled to the countryside. Chiang completed the Northern Expedition, took Beijing in 1928, won the nominal submission of the remaining northern warlords, and established a Nationalist government with its capital at Nanjing. On paper, China was reunified for the first time since 1916. In reality, Chapter 5 is the story of how thin that paper was.

---

# Chapter 5 — The White Terror and the Nanjing Decade

The decade from 1927 to 1937 is the closest thing the Republic ever got to working, and measuring exactly how close is one of the genuine historical debates. The Nationalist government in Nanjing — this is the **Nanjing Decade** — built things: a central bank and, by 1935, a unified national paper currency (the *fabi*) replacing the silver chaos; thousands of miles of road and railway; modern ministries; a foreign service that began clawing back the unequal treaties (Qing Dynasty Ch 7) the Qing had signed; the beginnings of an industrial base. Its admirers, then and now, argue it was a normal developing state given a fair chance to modernize China the way Meiji Japan had modernized (Meiji Japan, the reform arc) before war destroyed it. Its critics argue it never controlled more than a few core provinces, that the "reunification" was a set of deals with warlords who kept their armies, that it taxed the cities and ignored the countryside where most Chinese lived, and that it was corrupt, repressive, and narrow. The honest reading is that both are true, and the decade ran out of time before the question could be settled, because in 1937 Japan invaded and the test became survival.

The government's defining trait was that it was built on a massacre and never stopped. The April 1927 purge (Chapter 4) was the start of what is called the **White Terror** — a sustained campaign of arrests, surveillance, and executions of Communists and suspected leftists that ran for years and effectively destroyed the Communist Party's urban organization. Chiang Kai-shek's regime was anti-Communist to its core, and its security state — secret police, military tribunals, censorship — was real. This is the context for everything the Communists did next: driven out of the cities, hunted, they had two choices, dissolve or reinvent themselves somewhere the Nationalist state could not reach. They chose the countryside, and the reinvention is the most consequential thing in the chapter.

The military half of the reinvention was forced into being in the second half of 1927 by a string of disasters. Communist-led uprisings — a mutiny at Nanchang (which the People's Liberation Army still dates its founding to, August 1, 1927), an "Autumn Harvest" rising in the Hunan countryside led by Mao, and a doomed insurrection that briefly held Canton — were all crushed. The survivors of these failures, instead of regrouping for another urban try, retreated into remote mountain country. Mao took the remnant of his Autumn Harvest force to a bandit-friendly mountain base on the Jiangxi-Hunan border. There he was joined by a defecting Nationalist officer, **Zhu De** (pronounced "joo DUH"), and together they founded what became the **Red Army** and worked out the doctrine that would win the war: a peasant army that avoids the enemy's strength, controls the countryside rather than the cities, trades territory for time, treats civilians well enough that they feed and hide it, and survives to fight when conditions favor it. Mao compressed it into a jingle every cadre memorized: "Enemy advances, we retreat; enemy halts, we harass; enemy tires, we attack; enemy retreats, we pursue."

---

The political half of the reinvention was a state. By 1931 the Communists had stitched their scattered mountain bases into a contiguous territory in the poor, mountainous southeast and proclaimed the **Chinese Soviet Republic** — the **Jiangxi Soviet** (pronounced "jyahng-SHEE") — a functioning rural government with Mao as its chairman, its own land redistribution (taking land from landlords and richer peasants and giving it to the poor, which is the policy that made peasants fight for it), its own currency, its own army, its own purges. That last word matters. The Jiangxi Soviet also produced the **Futian Incident** of 1930–31, a brutal internal hunt for supposed enemy agents in which the leadership tortured and killed large numbers of its own cadres on confession evidence — a small, early, clearly documented rehearsal for the logic that would later run nationwide in the Yan'an Rectification (Chapter 8) and the Cultural Revolution (Chapter 14). The reader should mark it now: the violence the party would later turn on the whole country it first turned on itself, in Jiangxi, when it was a few tens of thousands of people in the hills.

Two outside shocks frame the decade. The first is the one that decides everything: **Japan**. In September 1931, Japanese officers of the army stationed in Manchuria to guard the South Manchurian Railway staged a bombing of their own railway near Mukden (Shenyang) and used it as the pretext to overrun all of Manchuria — an industrial region the size of France and Germany combined, China's richest. They installed the last Qing emperor, the now twenty-five-year-old **Puyi** (last seen abdicating for the second time in Chapter 2), as the puppet ruler of a puppet state they named **Manchukuo**. The League of Nations condemned it and did nothing; Japan walked out of the League. The **Mukden Incident** is the moment the Japanese militarism built up in the Japan books (Meiji Japan Ch 7–8, and narrated from the Japanese side in Japanese Economic Miracle Ch 1) becomes the central fact of Chinese life, and it forced the central political question of the next six years onto Chiang Kai-shek: fight Japan now, or destroy the Communists first? Chiang's answer — "first internal pacification, then external resistance," the Japanese are a disease of the skin but the Communists are a disease of the heart — is the most fateful and most criticized decision of his career, and it is what makes the Long March both necessary and survivable.

Because Chiang chose the Communists first. From 1930 he threw five successive **Encirclement Campaigns** at the Jiangxi Soviet. The first four, fought the way the Red Army wanted — luring large conventional forces deep into hostile mountains and destroying them piecemeal — failed. The fifth, from 1933, did not, and how it succeeded matters: advised by German officers, Chiang abandoned the deep thrusts and instead built a slowly tightening ring of thousands of concrete blockhouses connected by roads, strangling the Soviet's territory and economy by blockade rather than battle. By the autumn of 1934 the Jiangxi Soviet was being squeezed to death, its own leadership (then dominated by Soviet-trained returnees who had pushed Mao aside and insisted on fighting conventionally) had no answer, and the only choice left was to break out and run. That breakout is the Long March, and it is Chapter 6.

---

# Chapter 6 — The Long March

In October 1934, roughly 86,000 people — the Red Army, the party leadership, the bureaucracy of the Jiangxi Soviet, porters carrying everything from printing presses to silver — broke through the weakest point in Chiang Kai-shek's blockhouse ring and walked west into the unknown. About a year later, after one of the most extraordinary military retreats in history, somewhere between 4,000 and 8,000 of them reached a poor, remote base area in the northwest. That is the **Long March**, and it is simultaneously the founding epic of the People's Republic, the making of Mao Zedong, and one of the most thoroughly mythologized events in modern history. This chapter tries to give you the real march, the legendary march, and the reason the gap between them is itself important.

The real march was a catastrophe that the survivors turned into a victory by surviving. The breakout was not a daring plan; it was a rout dressed as a strategic transfer. The early months were the worst. Burdened with everything they owned and led by a planning committee dominated by the Comintern's military adviser, a German Communist code-named Otto Braun, and the Soviet-trained Chinese leaders who had displaced Mao, the column moved slowly and predictably and walked into a trap. At the **Xiang River** (pronounced "shyahng") in late 1934, fighting across against waiting Nationalist armies, the Red Army lost more than half its strength — from over 80,000 to around 30,000 in a few weeks. The disaster broke the leadership's authority, and at a hastily convened meeting in January 1935 in a captured town called **Zunyi** (pronounced "dzoon-EE"), the survivors effectively fired the Braun group and shifted military control to Mao Zedong. The **Zunyi Conference** is the hinge of the entire book: it is the moment Mao took the leadership he would hold, with no real challenger, until he died in 1976. It was not a formal coronation — Mao maneuvered into dominance over the next two years — but every later Communist account dates his unchallengeable authority to that cold meeting in a borrowed house.

---

What followed Zunyi was a year of movement that reads like an adventure novel and was, for the people in it, a grinding ordeal of starvation, frostbite, desertion, and combat. Under Mao the column stopped moving predictably. It feinted, doubled back, crossed and re-crossed rivers to shake pursuers, and pushed through some of the harshest terrain in Asia. Two episodes became the core of the legend. The first was the crossing of the **Dadu River** (pronounced "dah-DOO") at **Luding Bridge** in May 1935 — by the official story, a suicide assault by twenty-two volunteers swinging hand over hand across the chains of a partly dismantled chain-link bridge under machine-gun fire. The second was the crossing, later in 1935, of the high snow mountains and then the **grasslands**, a vast, freezing, trackless high-altitude bog where men drowned in mud and died of cold and hunger in numbers that may have exceeded the combat losses.

Here the contested-history rule has to be applied directly, because the Long March is where Communist mythmaking is densest. The traditional Chinese account gives the march a precise heroic figure — 25,000 *li*, about 8,000 miles (the figure usually cited in English is "6,000 miles," and the discrepancy is itself a clue) — and a series of perfectly cinematic set pieces. Careful historians, including the British-Chinese writer Sun Shuyun, who retraced the entire route in the 2000s and interviewed the last survivors, and earlier historians working from the Red Army's own records, have argued that the total distance is inflated, that the Luding Bridge battle was real but far less heavily opposed than the legend (some accounts, including remarks attributed to participants decades later, suggest the far bank was lightly held), and that the march's importance was less military than political and mythic. None of this means the Long March did not happen or was not an astonishing feat of endurance — the survival of the army through the grasslands alone is not in dispute, and the human cost was staggering. It means that the Long March's most important product was not a military result but a story: a founding myth of suffering, faith, and vindicated leadership that the party would tell for the rest of the century, the way Rome told the story of Aeneas. A reader should believe the endurance and the deaths and treat the cleanest, most heroic individual set pieces as legend hardened around a true core.

---

The march also settled the leadership question by attrition. The other major Communist force, the Fourth Front Army under Mao's rival **Zhang Guotao** (pronounced "jahng gwoh-TAO"), had more men than Mao's column when the two met in 1935. The two leaders could not agree on where to go or who was in charge; Mao's group went north, Zhang's went its own way, and Zhang's army was subsequently shattered in fighting in the west. Mao reached the destination with the smaller force but the surviving leadership and the political initiative; Zhang was finished as a rival and eventually defected. The destination was a pre-existing Communist base in the dry loess hills around the town of **Yan'an** (pronounced "yen-AHN") in the impoverished northwest, which becomes the Communist capital for the next decade and the place from which Mao re-builds.

The Long March mattered, finally, because of timing. While the Communists were nearly destroyed and then nearly starved, Japan was tightening its grip on north China beyond Manchuria, and Chinese public opinion — especially the students, who marched in the **December 9th Movement** of 1935 demanding the government stop fighting Communists and start fighting Japan — was turning hard against Chiang's "internal pacification first." This pressure produced the single most improbable event in the book. In December 1936, Chiang flew to Xi'an (pronounced "shee-AHN") to order his commanders to press the final extermination of the now-cornered Communists. Those commanders — chiefly the "Young Marshal" Zhang Xueliang, son of the murdered Manchurian warlord Zhang Zuolin (Chapter 2), whose homeland Japan had stolen and who wanted to fight Japan, not fellow Chinese — instead **kidnapped Chiang Kai-shek** and held him for two weeks. The **Xi'an Incident** was resolved, with Communist negotiators including Zhou Enlai in the room, by Chiang's agreement to end the civil war and form a united front against Japan. It is the hinge that may have saved the Communist Party from destruction: a few months from a possible final defeat, they were rescued not by their army but by the Japanese threat and a warlord's son's grievance. Seven months later Japan invaded in full, and the war that makes the Communists is Chapter 7.

---

# Chapter 7 — The War of Resistance Begins

On the night of July 7, 1937, Japanese and Chinese troops exchanged fire near a stone bridge outside Beijing that Europeans called the **Marco Polo Bridge** (after the Venetian traveler whose thirteenth-century account first described it to Europe; the Chinese name is Lugouqiao). Small clashes like this had happened before and been contained. This one was not. Within weeks it had escalated into the full Japanese invasion of China — the **Second Sino-Japanese War**, which the Chinese call the **War of Resistance Against Japan**, and which is, in Chinese reckoning, the start of the Second World War, two years before Europe's. It would last eight years, kill somewhere between 15 and 20 million Chinese (the range is wide because most of the dead were uncounted rural civilians, and it is a genuine estimate, not a precise figure), and it is the event that, more than any other, destroyed the Nationalists and made the Communists. Everything from here to 1949 is downstream of this war.

The **Second United Front** — the Xi'an-brokered truce (Chapter 6) — held, just barely, for the duration. The Communist forces were nominally folded into the National Revolutionary Army as the **Eighth Route Army** in the northwest and the **New Fourth Army** in the lower Yangtze, flying the Nationalist flag while keeping their own command, their own territory, and their own party absolutely intact. The arrangement was a fiction both sides maintained because neither could afford open civil war while Japan was overrunning the country, and it broke down in stages across this chapter and the next.

The war's opening phase was a Nationalist catastrophe that was also, perversely, a strategic success. Chiang Kai-shek made a decision historians still argue about: rather than fight Japan in the north where geography favored the invader, he opened a second front by committing his best, German-trained divisions to the defense of **Shanghai** in the autumn of 1937. The **Battle of Shanghai** lasted three months and was a bloodbath that destroyed the modern core of the Nationalist army — but it forced Japan into a long war on Chinese terms, drew the world's attention, and proved China would not collapse in the "three months" Japanese planners had predicted. It was a strategic logic paid for in the destruction of the only modern army China had, and the Nationalists never fully recovered the loss.

---

Then came the atrocity that has its own permanent place in this story and in the politics between China and Japan to this day. After Shanghai fell, the Japanese army advanced on the capital, **Nanjing**, which fell in December 1937. Over the following weeks Japanese forces carried out a mass killing and mass rape of prisoners and civilians that is called the **Nanjing Massacre** (older English: the Rape of Nanking). The number of dead is one of the most bitterly contested figures in twentieth-century history. The postwar Allied war-crimes tribunal and the standard Chinese figure is on the order of 200,000 to 300,000 killed; some Western historians have argued for a lower range; and a strain of Japanese nationalist writing has minimized or denied it outright, which is itself a live diplomatic wound between the two countries. What is not in serious historical dispute among non-revisionist scholars is that a large-scale massacre of tens of thousands at minimum, accompanied by systematic mass rape, happened. This book uses the standard 200,000–300,000 range while flagging that the precise figure is contested and that the contestation is political, not merely academic. The massacre matters beyond the body count: it is the single most powerful piece of evidence in modern China's enduring conviction, the one Mao and every leader since has invoked, that a weak China gets butchered, and that this must never be allowed to happen again.

The Nationalist government retreated up the Yangtze, first to Wuhan (which fell in 1938 after a long defense) and finally deep into the interior to **Chongqing** (pronounced "chong-CHING," older spelling Chungking), a fog-bound mountain city behind the Yangtze gorges, which became the wartime capital for the rest of the war and was bombed from the air for years in one of history's first sustained campaigns of terror bombing against a civilian capital. Along the retreat the Nationalists made one of the war's most terrible decisions: in 1938, to slow the Japanese advance, they dynamited the dikes of the **Yellow River** — the river the Chinese call "China's Sorrow" for exactly this reason (the same river whose floods recur across the Chinese books, Ancient China Ch 1). The flood drowned an estimated several hundred thousand Chinese civilians and made millions homeless, and it bought only a temporary delay. It is the clearest single illustration of the war's logic: a Chinese government destroying a vast swath of its own people and country to slow an enemy it could not stop.

---

The Communists fought a completely different war, and the difference is why they won the peace. While the Nationalists fought (and largely lost) the big conventional battles and bore the overwhelming majority of the casualties against the main Japanese army — a fact the later Communist narrative downplayed and honest history restores — the Communist Eighth Route Army spread out behind Japanese lines across the north China countryside. They did not try to hold cities. They built **base areas**: rural districts where the party organized the peasants, ran a government, cut rents and interest (a deliberately moderate policy during the united front, not yet the land seizures of Jiangxi or 1947), trained militias, and fought a guerrilla war of ambush, sabotage, and assassination against the overstretched occupier. The population in those base areas experienced the Communists as the people who governed, protected, and lightened the burden on them while the Japanese burned and the Nationalists were absent. Membership in the party went from around 40,000 in 1937 to well over a million by 1945. This is the mechanism, stated plainly: the war did not just weaken the Nationalists and strengthen the Communists as armies; it transferred the loyalty of tens of millions of rural Chinese, and that transfer is what 1949 is made of.

The one time the Communists fought a large conventional campaign, the **Hundred Regiments Offensive** of 1940, it did real damage to Japanese rail and mines but drew a devastating "kill all, burn all, loot all" reprisal campaign across the base areas and was, in the longer view, a lesson the party absorbed: husband your strength, let the Nationalists and Japanese exhaust each other. The fiction of the united front then cracked in the open with the **New Fourth Army Incident** of January 1941, in which Nationalist forces ambushed and destroyed the headquarters column of the Communists' southern army, killing thousands. After that the two "allies" barely cooperated at all and largely watched each other while nominally fighting Japan. The long, grinding middle of the war — the Allied alliance, the famine, the rot — is Chapter 8.

---

# Chapter 8 — The Long War and the Two Chinas

On December 7, 1941, Japan attacked Pearl Harbor, and China's isolated war became one theater of a world war. For Chiang Kai-shek this was, on paper, salvation: China was now an ally of the United States and Britain, would receive American aid and a American theater commander, and would end the war (the 1943 **Cairo Conference**, where Chiang met Roosevelt and Churchill as a notional Great Power) with the unequal treaties finally abolished and a permanent seat promised at the future United Nations. China had, after a century, formally escaped the legal architecture of its humiliation (Qing Dynasty Ch 7). And yet the four years from 1941 to 1945 are the years the Nationalist regime hollowed out from the inside, and understanding why is the key to the civil war.

The American alliance brought General Joseph Stilwell ("Vinegar Joe"), an acid-tongued China hand sent to make the Chinese army fight effectively. His relationship with Chiang was a slow-motion collision. Stilwell saw a regime hoarding American supplies and its best troops to fight Communists and to survive the war rather than win it; Chiang saw an arrogant foreigner who did not grasp that China had already been fighting alone for years and that the regime's survival *was* the strategy. The **Stilwell crisis** of 1944, which ended with Roosevelt recalling Stilwell at Chiang's insistence, poisoned the American view of the Nationalists at exactly the moment American opinion mattered for the postwar settlement. Meanwhile a separate American mission, the **Dixie Mission**, went to Yan'an in 1944 and reported back that the Communists looked disciplined, popular, and effective — corrosive praise that shaped how Washington would (and would not) intervene in the civil war to come.

---

The regime's hollowing was concrete, not just diplomatic. Cut off from the coast and its customs revenue, the Nationalist government paid for the war by printing money, and the result was a **hyperinflation** that ran for years and accelerated toward the end of the war into the destruction of the salaried middle class, the bureaucracy, and the officer corps — the very people a state depends on. A professor's monthly salary that bought a sack of rice before the war bought a few cigarettes by the end of it. Corruption was not a moral failing layered on top of this; it was the rational response to it, since an official whose salary was worthless had to extract income some other way. And then, in 1942–43, came the **Henan Famine**, in which drought, the war, and continued Nationalist grain requisitioning against starving peasants killed an estimated two to three million people in a single province while officials covered it up and the army kept taking grain. The Henan famine did to the Nationalists' rural legitimacy what the war did to their armies. (Hold it next to the **Bengal Famine** of 1943, in which British wartime policy and a stricken colonial administration let one to three million die in eastern India in the same window — two of the deadliest famines of the war, both in Asia, both downstream of empire and war, both presaging the even larger Chinese famine of Chapter 13. Modern India Ch 4.)

The Communists spent these years doing the opposite of hollowing out: they were consolidating. In their Yan'an base, while the Nationalists fought the main war, Mao ran the **Yan'an Rectification Movement** of 1942–44. On its surface it was a study campaign — cadres read assigned texts, examined their thinking, and corrected "errors." In substance it was the forging of the Maoist party: a sustained process of indoctrination, group criticism and self-criticism, written confessions, and, at its sharp end under the secret-police chief Kang Sheng, a terror of forced confessions and accusations that destroyed real and imagined enemies and broke the independent-minded. Rectification is one of the most important things in this book and most readers have never heard of it, because it is the template. The basic technology of mass thought reform — everyone studies the correct line, everyone confesses, everyone criticizes everyone, the leader's authority becomes total, and a security apparatus extracts confessions — is invented here, in the 1940s, in a few square miles of loess hills, and then run on the entire nation in the 1950s (Chapter 10) and at apocalyptic scale in the 1960s (Chapter 14). The 1945 Seventh Party Congress, at the end of rectification, wrote **Mao Zedong Thought** into the party constitution. The party that would rule China was now, ideologically and organizationally, finished. It only needed the country.

---

The war ended faster than anyone in China expected and in a way that handed the Communists an enormous prize. In its last full year Japan launched **Operation Ichigo** (1944), a massive offensive that smashed Nationalist armies across central and southern China, captured the American air bases the campaign was partly meant to neutralize, and exposed how badly the regime had decayed — a final humiliation that Communist propaganda would never let the Nationalists forget. Then, in August 1945, the United States dropped two atomic bombs, the **Soviet Union** declared war and poured more than a million troops into Japanese-held **Manchuria** in a one-week blitz, and Japan surrendered. The sudden collapse left a vacuum across the most industrialized region in China, Manchuria — and the Soviet army was sitting on it, with Chinese Communist forces moving in behind, inheriting Japanese arsenals, factories, and a strategic base on the Nationalists' northern flank. The Nationalists had won the war and were about to lose the peace, and the four years in which they lost it are Chapter 9.

---

# Chapter 9 — The Civil War and 1949

In the late summer of 1945 China had won, after eight years and at least 15 million dead, the longest and bloodiest war in its modern history. It had four months of something like hope. By the summer of 1946 it was in an all-out civil war between the same two parties that had been fighting since 1927, and within three and a half years one of them controlled the most populous country on earth and the other had fled to an island. The speed of the Communist victory — most observers in 1946, including Stalin, expected the Nationalists to win or at best a divided China — is the thing this chapter has to explain.

It opened with negotiations no one believed in. Mao Zedong flew to Chongqing in August 1945 — his first time in a Nationalist-controlled city in eighteen years, and a genuinely tense personal gamble — for six weeks of US-brokered talks with Chiang Kai-shek that produced a signed agreement and changed nothing, because each side was simultaneously racing its armies to grab Japanese-surrendered territory, above all Manchuria. The United States then sent its most prestigious soldier, General **George Marshall**, on a year-long mission through 1946 to broker a coalition government and a unified army. The **Marshall Mission** failed completely, because the thing it was trying to arrange — two Leninist party-armies voluntarily merging into one neutral national army — was something neither party would ever actually do, since for both of them the party *was* the army and surrendering it meant surrendering everything. The deep reason the Chinese Revolution ran for fifty years and India's independence struggle did not end in civil war is visible right here: India's competing forces accepted, in the end, a single national army and electoral politics; China's two armed parties could each only conceive of total victory (Modern India Ch 5–6, the contrast is the book's recurring thesis). Marshall went home; the United States, disillusioned with the Nationalists, gave Chiang aid but never the decisive intervention he counted on.

---

The war itself, 1946–49, inverted faster than the maps suggest. In 1946–47 the Nationalists looked like they were winning: they had more troops, American equipment, the cities, the railways, and they captured the Communist capital Yan'an itself in 1947 (Mao abandoned it without a fight — trading space for time, the doctrine from Chapter 5, now run at national scale). But holding cities and rail lines in a peasant country meant garrisoning a million points and controlling none of the space between them, while the Communist armies — now renamed the **People's Liberation Army** — moved freely through a countryside that fed and hid them. And in 1947 the party turned its single most powerful weapon back on full power. It abandoned the moderate wartime rent reduction and ordered radical **land reform** across the areas it controlled: landlords' land seized and redistributed to poor peasants, often through violent mass "struggle" meetings in which villagers were mobilized to denounce, beat, and sometimes kill landlords. It was brutal and it was, as a war strategy, devastatingly effective: a peasant who had received a landlord's land and feared its return if the Nationalists won had an existential personal stake in the Communist victory and a son in the PLA. Land reform converted the rural majority into a recruitment and supply base on a scale no army in Chinese history had ever commanded.

The end came in three enormous campaigns over the winter of 1948–49. In Manchuria, **Lin Biao** (pronounced "lin BYOW") — a brilliant, reclusive Communist commander who matters greatly in Chapters 14 and 15 — destroyed the Nationalists' best remaining armies in the **Liaoshen Campaign**. In the central plains the **Huaihai Campaign** (pronounced "hwai-HAI"), a battle involving well over a million men on each side and decided as much by mass peasant logistics — millions of civilians moving supplies on wheelbarrows for the PLA — as by combat, annihilated the Nationalist forces between the Yellow River and the Yangtze. In the north the **Pingjin Campaign** took Tianjin by storm and Beijing by negotiated surrender. The Nationalist state, its currency already destroyed by a final catastrophic 1948 "gold yuan" reform that wiped out whatever urban savings the wartime inflation had left, simply came apart. The PLA crossed the Yangtze in April 1949 and took the Nationalist capital Nanjing. Chiang Kai-shek's government, some two million soldiers and civilians, the national gold reserve, and the cream of the imperial art collection from the Forbidden City withdrew across the strait to **Taiwan**, the island a Ming-loyalist pirate, Koxinga, had first made a refuge from a new mainland regime exactly three centuries earlier (Qing Dynasty Ch 1) and which Japan had ruled for fifty years until 1945, having taken it from the Qing in 1895 (Meiji Japan Ch 7). The civil war did not end. It froze, and it is, technically, still frozen.

On October 1, 1949, from the **Tiananmen** gate in Beijing, Mao Zedong proclaimed the **People's Republic of China**. The line history remembers — "the Chinese people have stood up" — was the whole argument of the century in five words: the humiliation that ran from the Opium Wars (Qing Dynasty Ch 7) through the Twenty-One Demands (Chapter 1) through Nanjing (Chapter 7) was over, by force, under this party. What that party then did with a unified China — first the rebuilding, then the catastrophes — is the second half of this book.

---

# Chapter 10 — New China: Land Reform and Korea

The first three years of the People's Republic, 1949–1952, are the years its own citizens, even many who later suffered terribly under it, often remembered as the time things visibly got better — and any honest account of this period has to hold that alongside what the consolidation cost. After a century of foreign predation (Qing Dynasty Ch 7–13), forty years of warlords and civil war (Chapters 2–9), and an eight-year invasion (Chapters 7–8), there was, suddenly, one government that ran the whole country, stopped the hyperinflation within about a year, suppressed banditry, curbed the opium trade, and built a functioning administration down to the village. For hundreds of millions of people the single most important political fact of 1950 was that the wars had stopped and a state existed. That is the foundation of the regime's real, and not merely propagandized, early legitimacy.

The provisional constitution, the **Common Program**, promised a moderate "**New Democracy**": a multi-class coalition with a mixed economy, minor non-Communist parties given ceremonial seats, private business tolerated for now. It lasted about as long as it was useful. The transformation under it was anything but moderate. The central act was nationwide **land reform**, the Jiangxi and 1947 policy (Chapters 5, 9) now run across the entire countryside from 1950 to 1952. Roughly 40 percent of China's cultivated land changed hands. The process was deliberately violent: villagers were organized into "speak bitterness" meetings to denounce landlords, "struggle" them, and frequently execute them. The death toll of land reform is contested and large — estimates of those killed run from several hundred thousand to one to two million and higher. It accomplished two things at once that the rest of the book turns on: it destroyed the landlord class that had been the basic structure of rural Chinese society for two thousand years, and it implicated tens of millions of ordinary peasants directly in the regime's violence, binding them to it. Land reform was simultaneously the largest redistribution of property in human history and the largest political mobilization of mass violence the country had yet seen, and it was both on purpose.

---

Two other transformations of these years deserve their own weight. The **Marriage Law of 1950** was, in its long effects, one of the most consequential single laws of the century: it banned arranged marriage, child betrothal, concubinage, and the sale of women, and gave women the right to divorce and to own property. Millions of women used it, especially the divorce provision, against the wishes of husbands and in-laws — there was a backlash, including violence and suicides, and enforcement was uneven and later subordinated to politics — but the law struck directly at the Confucian patriarchal family that the New Culture Movement had attacked with essays thirty years earlier (Chapter 3) and that no government had touched. Whatever else the revolution did, it ended the legal framework of one of history's most durable systems of female subordination, and that is not erased by what the regime did later.

The other was the consolidation of the frontiers, which is where the regime's map became the contested map of the present. In 1949–50 the PLA entered **Xinjiang** (pronounced "shin-jyahng," the vast Muslim-majority northwest) and **Tibet**, regions that had been at most loosely held by the late Qing and effectively independent or autonomous since 1912. Tibet is the sharpest case and has to be stated in both framings, because the framing *is* the dispute: the Chinese government's position is that the PLA "peacefully liberated" a region that had always been part of China, formalized by the 1951 **Seventeen Point Agreement** signed under military pressure by representatives of the young **Dalai Lama** (Tibet's spiritual and temporal leader); the Tibetan exile position, and that of many outside historians, is that this was the military annexation of an independent state. Both framings describe the same events; which words you use is a political act. This book's job is to tell you that the events — PLA entry, a coerced agreement, incorporation — are not in dispute, and that the question of whether that was liberation or conquest is exactly the kind of contested claim where the honest move is to give you both sides rather than pick the vivid one. The 1959 sequel, when a Tibetan uprising fails and the Dalai Lama flees to India, is in Chapter 12.

---

Then the new state went to war with the United States, and that is the event that hardened everything. In June 1950 **North Korea** invaded the South; a US-led United Nations force counterattacked, destroyed the North Korean army, and drove toward the Chinese border on the Yalu River. Mao, against the advice of much of his own leadership and despite the country's exhaustion, decided China could not allow a hostile American army on its Manchurian frontier, and in October 1950 sent hundreds of thousands of troops — officially "**Chinese People's Volunteers**," a fiction to avoid formal war with the US — across the Yalu. The Chinese intervention drove the UN forces back down the peninsula in one of the most shocking reversals in American military history, and the war then ground to a stalemate near the original border until an armistice in 1953. The cost to China was immense (estimates of Chinese military dead run into the hundreds of thousands, including Mao's own eldest son), and the consequences were decisive: it confirmed the new regime as a great military power that had fought the strongest country on earth to a draw; it locked China and the United States into two decades of total hostility (which is why Chapter 15's American opening is such a shock); it bound China tightly to the Soviet Union (whose split with China, Chapter 13, then matters all the more); and at home it justified, under the banner of the **"Resist America, Aid Korea"** mobilization, a sweeping internal crackdown — the **Campaign to Suppress Counterrevolutionaries** (hundreds of thousands more executed) and the **Three-Anti and Five-Anti campaigns** that broke the urban business class. The Korean War is also the bridge to the Korean side of this story, the war that permanently divided the peninsula and built two opposite states on it (Korean Modern, the division arc). By 1953 the consolidation was complete, the country was unified, mobilized, and frightened of its government, and Mao turned to the question that would destroy millions: how fast could China be made socialist?

---

# Chapter 11 — From the First Plan to the Hundred Flowers

With the Korean War settled and the old social order broken, the regime turned from consolidation to construction, and for a few years it looked like it was working. The model was the Soviet Union, and the relationship was, briefly, real and substantial. Under the **First Five-Year Plan** (1953–1957), Soviet advisers, Soviet loans, and a famous package of around 150 large Soviet-built industrial projects (the "**156 projects**") gave China the foundations of a heavy-industrial economy almost from nothing — steel, machine tools, power, vehicles, the first modern industrial workforce. Industrial output grew fast. This is the one period in this book where the planned economy delivered something like what it promised, and it should be stated plainly because so much of what follows is catastrophe: the early-to-mid 1950s built real factories and trained real engineers, and the people who ran them were the technocrats Mao would spend the next two decades attacking.

In parallel the regime built the structure of the socialist state. The **1954 Constitution** created the formal apparatus — a National People's Congress, a state structure — under the unchanging reality of party supremacy. The first serious elite power struggle of the People's Republic, the purge of the regional bosses **Gao Gang** and Rao Shushi in 1953–54, showed that the rules for losing a fight at the top were lethal and would stay that way. And the agricultural countryside, only just handed its land in the land reform that defined Chapter 10, was pushed within five years back off it: through 1953–56 peasants were moved, with increasing pressure, from mutual-aid teams into cooperatives and then into full collective farms, where land and tools were pooled and the household no longer farmed its own plot. The land reform had given hundreds of millions of peasants their own land for the first time; **collectivization** took it back within half a decade. Mao personally drove the pace, overriding more cautious colleagues, in a 1955 push he called the "high tide." A reader should mark this: the moderation of "New Democracy" (Chapter 10) was over, the pace was now set by Mao's conviction that the masses' enthusiasm could outrun material constraints, and that conviction, unchecked, becomes the Great Leap of Chapter 12.

---

By the mid-1950s the urban economy had also been socialized — the **Socialist Transformation** of 1955–56 absorbed nearly all remaining private firms into joint state-private enterprises, ending the mixed economy the Common Program had promised — and the regime had finished, with startling speed, turning a war-wrecked agrarian country into a Soviet-style command economy and one-party state in roughly seven years. The state also pushed through reforms aimed at the deep problem the New Culture Movement had identified (Chapter 3): mass illiteracy. It promoted **simplified Chinese characters** (reducing the stroke count of common characters to make them easier to learn), a standard national spoken language (*putonghua*, "common speech"), and a Roman-alphabet phonetic system, **pinyin**, to teach pronunciation — the romanization this entire book is written in. These were genuine attempts to make literacy possible for the majority, continuous with Hu Shi's vernacular revolution of the 1910s, and they substantially worked.

Then came the episode that defines the chapter and tells you the most about how the regime would now operate. In 1956 Khrushchev secretly denounced Stalin's crimes and cult of personality to the Soviet party; the speech leaked and detonated across the Communist world (it is part of why the Sino-Soviet relationship later breaks, Chapter 13). Mao, partly to distinguish China's road and partly out of genuine confidence, launched in 1956–57 the **Hundred Flowers Campaign** — "let a hundred flowers bloom, let a hundred schools of thought contend" — explicitly inviting intellectuals to criticize the party and help correct its errors. After hesitation, they did, with a frankness that built fast in the spring of 1957: criticism of corruption, of party arrogance, of the suppression of dissent, with the boldest voices questioning one-party rule itself. Whether Mao planned this as a trap from the start or was genuinely surprised and enraged by the scale of the criticism is one of the real unresolved historical debates, and the honest answer is that the evidence points both ways and historians disagree. What is not in dispute is what he did next. In mid-1957 the campaign was abruptly reversed into the **Anti-Rightist Campaign**: the people who had spoken were labeled "rightists," and an estimated 550,000 or more intellectuals, scientists, writers, and officials were purged, sent to labor camps or internal exile, their careers and often their families destroyed, for years or decades. The man who ran the Anti-Rightist Campaign on the ground was the party general secretary, **Deng Xiaoping** (pronounced "dung shyao-PING") — the same Deng who is purged in Chapter 14, returns in Chapter 15, and after this book runs the China that repudiates Maoism. The Anti-Rightist Campaign silenced, for twenty years, exactly the technical and intellectual class that might have told Mao the truth about the disaster he was about to cause. That disaster is Chapter 12.

# Chapter 12 — The Great Leap Forward

In 1958 Mao Zedong launched a campaign to overtake Britain in industrial output in fifteen years and to reach full communism in something like the lifetime of people then alive, by mobilizing the sheer collective will of 600 million people. It was called the **Great Leap Forward**, and it produced the largest famine in human history. This chapter is about how a campaign meant to make China rich and powerful almost destroyed it, and the central thing to understand is that it was not an accident of nature or a failure of effort. It was caused, in its essentials, by political decisions, and the political system was specifically organized so that no one could stop it.

The Leap's economic logic was magical thinking elevated to state policy. Mao had concluded from collectivization (Chapter 11) that the binding constraint on Chinese development was not capital, technology, or expertise — the expertise had just been purged as "rightist" — but insufficient revolutionary will, and that will, properly mobilized, could substitute for all of it. The countryside was reorganized in 1958 into giant **People's Communes**, each pooling tens of thousands of households' land, labor, tools, and even cooking into a single militarized unit: private plots abolished, private kitchens replaced by communal mess halls where, briefly, peasants were told to eat all they wanted for free. Tens of millions of peasants were pulled off the harvest and onto vast water-control and infrastructure projects and into producing steel in hundreds of thousands of crude **backyard furnaces**, melting down their own pots, tools, and door hinges to hit steel quotas. The steel was mostly useless. The crops, with the labor diverted and the autumn 1958 harvest in many places left to rot in the fields, were not brought in.

---

The mechanism that turned a bad harvest into mass death is the part that must be explained, not just stated, because it is the heart of the catastrophe and it is a story about information, not weather. Under intense political pressure to prove the Leap was working, local officials reported grossly inflated grain output — "**sputnik fields**" supposedly yielding ten or a hundred times the real amount, named for the Soviet satellite to signal miraculous progress. The state then set its grain procurement — the quota it physically took from each area to feed the cities, the army, and exports that paid for Soviet machinery — as a percentage of the *reported* harvest. So the regions that had lied most extravagantly had nearly all of their *real*, much smaller harvest taken away. Peasants who had grown the grain were left with nothing, and were often forbidden to leave or to admit there was a famine, because admitting it was "right deviation" and could be lethal for the official who did. People starved to death sitting on the procurement records that proved the grain had existed. A few details fix the irrationality in the mind: the **Four Pests Campaign** had peasants kill so many sparrows (a grain-eating pest) that locust and insect populations exploded and ate the crops the campaign was meant to protect — a textbook case of an ecological intervention backfiring at national scale.

The political system then did the one thing guaranteed to maximize the death toll: it punished the truth. In 1959, at a leadership conference at the mountain resort of **Lushan** (pronounced "loo-SHAHN"), the defense minister **Peng Dehuai** (pronounced "pung duh-HWAI") — a blunt soldier who had commanded in Korea and had toured his starving home region — gave Mao a private, relatively mild letter describing the real damage the Leap was doing. Mao took it as a challenge to his authority, circulated it, and destroyed Peng, purging him and anyone who had agreed with him and launching a fresh nationwide campaign against "right opportunism." The lesson every official in China instantly absorbed was that reporting the famine was career and possibly life-ending, while inflating success was safe. After Lushan the procurement machine kept grinding for two more years against a harvest that was collapsing, and the death toll, which might have been catastrophic but contained, became the worst in recorded history. Lushan is the purest illustration of this book's recurring point about the Maoist system: the same concentration of unchecked power in one man that had won the civil war now meant that a country of hundreds of millions had no mechanism by which anyone could tell the leader he was wrong. India suffered serious food crises in the same era. But as a functioning electoral democracy with a free press, it never had a famine remotely on this scale, because officials could not suppress the news and voters could punish failure (India's last great famine was Bengal in 1943, Modern India Ch 4; by the 1970s the electoral state had ended recurrent famine entirely, Modern India Ch 7). That contrast is the sharpest single test in this book of the two roads first forked in Chapter 3. The famine itself, its scale, and its aftermath are Chapter 13.

---

# Chapter 13 — The Great Famine and the Retreat

The **Great Chinese Famine** of roughly 1959 to 1961 is the deadliest famine in human history and one of the deadliest events of any kind. The number of excess deaths — people who died who would otherwise have lived — is contested, and the contestation is exactly the kind this book has to handle carefully, because the range is enormous and politically loaded. The Chinese government's implied figure, derived from census gaps, is on the order of 15 to 17 million. Mainstream demographic studies cluster around 30 million. The Chinese journalist Yang Jisheng, who spent years in the archives for his book *Tombstone*, estimated about 36 million; the historian Frank Dikötter, working from local party archives, argued for at least 45 million. Honest scholars disagree by a factor of three. What is not in dispute, even at the low end, is that this was a man-made famine that killed tens of millions of people in peacetime, that it was caused by policy and prolonged by the suppression of the truth (Chapter 12), and that it was, by any of the estimates, several times deadlier than the Nanjing Massacre and on the order of, or worse than, the total Chinese death toll of the entire eight-year war against Japan. This book uses "tens of millions, with most scholarly estimates between 30 and 45 million" and flags that the precise number may never be settled because the regime that caused it controlled the records.

The famine was not evenly spread; it concentrated where local leaders enforced the Leap most fanatically. The clearest documented case, the one that strips away any ambiguity about cause, is the **Xinyang Incident** (pronounced "shin-yahng") in Henan province, where roughly a million people died in 1959–60 in a prefecture whose granaries held grain the whole time — grain that was not released because admitting the famine was politically forbidden and the local leadership had built its career on the Leap's supposed success. Xinyang is the famine in miniature: not a failed harvest but a state extracting and withholding food from people it had forbidden to flee or complain.

---

The catastrophe did to the leadership what no opposition had been able to do: it cracked Mao's authority from inside the party. By 1960–61 the disaster was undeniable even within the system, and the practical men of the party — chiefly **Liu Shaoqi** (pronounced "lyoo shao-CHEE"), the head of state, and Deng Xiaoping, the general secretary, with Zhou Enlai and Chen Yun — took effective control of economic policy and pulled the country back from the edge. They dismantled the worst of the communes, restored private plots and rural markets, allowed peasants to farm household land again, and stopped the insane procurement. The economy began to recover by 1962. At the **Seven Thousand Cadres Conference** in 1962 — a vast work meeting of officials from across the country — Liu Shaoqi said, to Mao's face and the assembled party, that the disaster was "three parts natural, seven parts man-made," openly contradicting the line that blamed the weather. Mao retreated tactically, made a limited self-criticism, and stepped back from day-to-day affairs while Liu and Deng ran the recovery. But he did not concede the argument, and he never forgave the men who had been proven right at his expense. The reader should hold this clearly: the Cultural Revolution of Chapter 14 is not a random eruption. It is, in large part, Mao Zedong's answer to the men who saved the country from his greatest failure and to a party apparatus he now believed had turned "revisionist" — willing to choose pragmatism over permanent revolution, the way he believed the Soviet Union had.

The Soviet thread snapped in exactly these years and made everything worse. The **Sino-Soviet split**, building since Khrushchev's de-Stalinization (Chapter 11) and through disputes over nuclear weapons, ideology, and who led world communism, became open in 1960 when Moscow abruptly withdrew all its advisers and blueprints, leaving hundreds of projects half-built in the middle of the famine. China was now isolated from both superpowers at once. In the same window it fought and won a short, sharp Himalayan border war against India in 1962 — the **Sino-Indian War**, a humiliation on the Indian side that recurs in that book (Modern India Ch 6) — asserting frontier strength even as its own people starved, a juxtaposition that captures the regime exactly. By the mid-1960s the economy had recovered, Liu and Deng's pragmatic management was visibly working, and Mao, sidelined, watched a party run by men he had decided were China's Khrushchevs. He began, through a rural rectification drive called the **Socialist Education Movement** and a coded literary feud over a historical play (*Hai Rui Dismissed from Office*, widely read as a defense of the purged Peng Dehuai), to prepare an attack not on a policy but on the party itself. That attack is the last great catastrophe of the Mao era, and it is Chapter 14.

# Chapter 14 — The Cultural Revolution Unleashed

In 1966 Mao Zedong, then in his early seventies, did something no ruler in history had quite done: he turned the population of his own country loose to destroy the political party and the state apparatus that he himself had built and still nominally led. The **Cultural Revolution** — formally the Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution, 1966 to 1976 — is the hardest event in this book to compress, because it was not one thing. It was a succession struggle at the top, a genuine mass movement of millions of young people, a civil war inside the party, a decade of persecution, and a national trauma whose scars shape Chinese politics now. This chapter covers the explosive first phase, 1966 to 1969; the long, grim wind-down is Chapter 15.

Mao's purpose has to be stated carefully because it sits behind everything. After the famine (Chapter 13) he believed that the revolution was being strangled not by enemies outside but by the party itself — that men like Liu Shaoqi were "capitalist roaders" leading China back toward inequality and bureaucratic rule the way he believed Khrushchev's Soviet Union had betrayed communism, and that the only cure was to let the masses, especially the young, rebel against the party hierarchy and remake it from below in permanent revolution. Whether this was sincere ideological conviction, a calculated bid to destroy the rivals who had eclipsed him after the famine, or both at once is debated; the most persuasive readings hold it was genuinely both, and that the two motives were inseparable in Mao's mind. The instrument he had ready was the army. The defense minister **Lin Biao** (the brilliant Manchuria commander from Chapter 9) had spent years turning the People's Liberation Army into a machine of Mao-worship, and had produced the single most printed object of the century, the **Quotations from Chairman Mao** — the **Little Red Book**, the pocket anthology of Mao sayings, billions of copies, the physical totem of the cult. The cult of personality the New Culture Movement's heirs had once mocked in emperors (Chapter 3) had been rebuilt around one man to a degree no Chinese emperor ever achieved.

---

The movement was launched from the top and then deliberately handed to the street. In 1966 a Mao-controlled group issued the **May 16 Circular** declaring the party penetrated by enemies, and a radical inner body, the Central Cultural Revolution Group — including Mao's wife **Jiang Qing** (pronounced "jyang ching") and the Shanghai radicals who become the Gang of Four in Chapter 15 — took charge of the campaign over the normal party structure. The young responded. Students formed **Red Guard** units, wrote **big-character posters** (handwritten wall denunciations, the era's medium of accusation, starting at Peking University), and were told that "to rebel is justified." Mao reviewed millions of them in eight enormous **Tiananmen** rallies in 1966, sacralizing the movement. The Red Guards attacked the "**Four Olds**" — old customs, culture, habits, ideas — which in practice meant smashing temples, churches, mosques, monuments, libraries, and art across the country (a wave of cultural destruction the Chinese books note as the worst since the Qin book's burning of the books, Qin Dynasty Ch 6), and attacking, beating, imprisoning, and killing teachers, intellectuals, "class enemies," and officials. Anyone with a bad class label, a foreign connection, or an old book could be a target. Families informed on each other. The country's schools and universities simply closed, for years.

Then it consumed the leadership it was aimed at. **Liu Shaoqi** — the head of state of the People's Republic, the man who had told the truth about the famine — was denounced as the "number one capitalist roader," subjected to mass struggle sessions, beaten, denied medical care, and left to die alone in a prison cellar in 1969, his death unannounced for years. Deng Xiaoping was purged and sent to work in a tractor factory. Countless lesser officials were destroyed. The violence then turned on itself: rival Red Guard factions, each claiming to be the most loyal to Mao, fought each other with escalating weapons until parts of China in 1967 approached civil war (the **Wuhan Incident** of 1967, where an army-backed faction seized central emissaries, nearly tipped it over), and Mao had to send in the army to suppress the very movement he had unleashed. The Red Guards' reward for their fervor was the **Down to the Countryside Movement**: from 1968, some 17 million urban young people were shipped to remote villages to "learn from the peasants" — partly genuine ideology, largely a way to disperse a force that had become uncontrollable and to empty the cities of unemployed, radicalized youth. They are China's "lost generation," and one of them, sent down to a poor village in his teens at the end of the 1960s, was a future leader named Xi Jinping, which is one of the threads this book hands forward to its sequel.

---

Two things must be added so the chapter is not only horror. First, the deadliest phase of the Cultural Revolution was not the famous Red Guard street chaos of 1966–67 but the bureaucratic terror that followed it, the **Cleansing of the Class Ranks** of 1968–69, an organized purge run through work units and revolutionary committees that killed on the order of a million people with far less public drama — the pattern, familiar by now in this book, that the quiet administrative phase of a campaign is deadlier than its loud one. Total Cultural Revolution deaths are, like the famine, genuinely uncertain: scholarly estimates of those killed range from several hundred thousand to one and a half or two million, with many more imprisoned, tortured, or driven to suicide. The decade's economic and human waste — a generation denied education, the technical class destroyed twice (the Anti-Rightist and now this), institutions shattered — is a major part of why China entered the 1980s so far behind East Asian neighbors who had spent the same decades growing (the divergence with Japan's postwar miracle and the rising "Asian Tigers" is the starkest measure of the cost, Japanese Economic Miracle, the whole arc).

Second, the same regime that was tearing itself apart also, in these years, detonated its first **atomic bomb** (1964) and later a hydrogen bomb and a satellite, and ran the **barefoot doctors** program that pushed basic health care into villages and contributed to a real, large rise in life expectancy across the Mao era as a whole — a reminder that the period's ledger has genuine entries on both sides and that flattening it into pure catastrophe is as false as the official triumph. The first phase ended at the **Ninth Party Congress** in 1969, which declared victory, rebuilt a militarized party, and wrote **Lin Biao** into the constitution, by name, as Mao's successor. Putting a man's name in the constitution as the next ruler is the kind of thing that does not survive contact with Mao, and what happened to Lin Biao opens the strange last act, Chapter 15.

# Chapter 15 — The Long Twilight

The last seven years of Mao's life are a slow, murky endgame: an ailing ruler, a frozen and exhausted country, a leadership consumed by a succession fight conducted in code, and, cutting across all of it, the single most unexpected diplomatic reversal of the Cold War. The Cultural Revolution did not end so much as decay, and the question that organizes these years is the question the whole book has been circling — who, and what, comes after Mao — answered, finally, only by his death.

The first shock was the fall of the heir. In 1969 China and the Soviet Union, the two great Communist powers, fought a series of bloody border battles, most famously at **Zhenbao Island** (pronounced "jen-BAO") on the Ussuri River, that brought them to the edge of full war and left China facing the terrifying prospect of a Soviet attack with no great-power friend in the world. Then **Lin Biao**, the constitutionally designated successor (Chapter 14), died. The official account — and it is, this book must stress, the official account, with key facts still genuinely uncertain and disputed by serious historians — is that Lin Biao, sensing Mao turning against him, was implicated in a bungled coup and assassination plot (code-named "Project 571"), and that in September 1971 he fled toward the Soviet Union in a plane that ran out of fuel and crashed in Mongolia, killing everyone aboard. What is certain is that Mao's chosen successor, the man written into the constitution two years earlier, was suddenly and permanently gone, denounced as a traitor. The psychological effect on the country is hard to overstate: if Vice-Chairman Lin, "Chairman Mao's closest comrade in arms," was a traitor, the entire sacred narrative of the Cultural Revolution was a lie, and ordinary people knew it. The Lin Biao affair is where mass belief in Maoism, already strained, quietly broke.

---

Into the strategic terror of the Soviet threat came the reversal. Both Mao and the United States, separately, worked out that an enemy's enemy was worth talking to: the US wanted out of Vietnam and leverage against Moscow; China wanted a counterweight against the Soviet army on its border. The signaling began with an absurd, perfect gesture — an American table-tennis team invited to China in 1971, "**ping-pong diplomacy**" — ran through a secret visit by Henry Kissinger (President Nixon's national security adviser), and culminated in **President Nixon's visit to China** in February 1972, in which the leader of the country that had fought China in Korea and embargoed it for two decades shook hands with Mao in Beijing. In the same period the People's Republic took the **China seat at the United Nations**, including the permanent Security Council seat, from the Nationalist government on Taiwan (1971). After a century that began with foreign powers dictating terms to a prostrate Qing (Qing Dynasty Ch 7–12), China rejoined the great-power system on its own terms, while still in the wreckage of the Cultural Revolution — the regime's deepest continuity, the pursuit of strength against humiliation, running straight through its worst decade.

The last years were a deathbed succession fight between two camps around the dying Mao. On one side were the Cultural Revolution radicals led by Mao's wife Jiang Qing — the **Gang of Four**, the Shanghai-based faction that controlled propaganda and culture and wanted the revolution kept permanently boiling. On the other were the survivors and rebuilders, centered on the premier **Zhou Enlai** — the Whampoa political director of Chapter 4, the regime's indispensable diplomat and administrator, the man who had spent decades both serving Mao and quietly limiting the damage, a figure historians still argue about (enabler or moderating brake, and the honest answer is both). Zhou, dying of cancer, maneuvered to bring back **Deng Xiaoping** — purged in the Cultural Revolution, now rehabilitated in 1973 to run the government — and Deng began pushing the **Four Modernizations** (agriculture, industry, defense, science), the explicit program of pragmatic development that becomes, after this book, the blueprint for everything. The radicals fought back with coded campaigns ("Criticize Lin Biao, Criticize Confucius," an attack on Zhou in historical fancy dress) and had Deng purged a second time in early 1976.

1976 settled it by removing the principals. **Zhou Enlai** died in January; the spontaneous mass mourning for him in Tiananmen Square in April — the **Tiananmen Incident** of 1976 — turned into an open demonstration against the radicals and was suppressed, but it showed which way popular feeling ran. In July a catastrophic earthquake destroyed the city of **Tangshan** (pronounced "tahng-shahn"), killing on the order of a quarter of a million people — and in a country whose oldest political idea was that Heaven signals the fall of a dynasty through disasters (the **Mandate of Heaven**, the thread that opened this book in Chapter 1 and ran through every Chinese dynasty before it, Qing Dynasty Ch 13), the coincidence was not lost on anyone. On September 9, 1976, **Mao Zedong died**. Within a month his designated compromise successor, Hua Guofeng (a colorless provincial administrator Mao had elevated late precisely because he threatened no faction), allied with the military and party elders, **arrested the Gang of Four**, ending the Cultural Revolution by force. Deng Xiaoping, twice purged, returned within two years and set China on the road of market reform, single-party rule, and accelerating power that is the subject of the next book in this chain.

The revolution that began when a six-year-old emperor abdicated in 1912 ended, in one sense, when an old revolutionary died in 1976 and the radicals he had unleashed were led away in handcuffs. In sixty-four years China had gone from a collapsed empire carved up by foreigners to a unified, nuclear-armed, UN Security Council power that no one dictated terms to — and had paid for it with the warlord wars, the Japanese invasion, the civil war, the worst famine in human history, and the Cultural Revolution, tens of millions of its own people dead in peacetime at the hands of the state that had also lifted it off its knees. Modern China's borders, its single-party state, its obsession with never being weak again, and its still-frozen quarrel with the island across the strait all date from this exact stretch. What that unified, scarred, ambitious country did when it finally turned its full energy from revolution to wealth and power is where this story goes next.

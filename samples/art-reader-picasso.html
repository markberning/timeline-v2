Cubism, or how to break a face
↑ From Post-ImpressionismCézanneAfrican masks
↓ To FuturismConstructivismAbstract

In the summer of 1907, Pablo Picasso finished a painting that almost nobody liked. Five women, five sets of impossible angles, masks where the faces should be. Even his friends thought he had lost it.

He hid it in the studio for nine years.

What looks now like the obvious thing to do next — break a face into pieces, show several angles at once, let the picture argue with itself — was, at the time, basically a personal insult to every painter who had ever lived.

The trick Picasso had pulled, and that Georges Braque pulled along with him over the next decade, was to take linear perspective — the idea that ruled European painting from Brunelleschi forward — and just ignore it. A face has six sides now. A guitar has its strings and its back at the same time. The painting tells you, gently, that you have been looking at things wrong since 1420.

By 1912 the two of them were painting so similarly they had to sign the backs of each other's canvases just to remember whose was whose.

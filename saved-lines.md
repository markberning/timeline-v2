## From mesopotamia-rewrite.md — Chapter 1

> you can grow more grain per acre than almost anywhere else in the ancient world.

> You needed someone to organize the work, store the surplus grain, and arbitrate disputes about water rights. You needed, in other words, the inventions we now call government, law, writing, mathematics, and the city — and you needed all of them more or less at the same time.

> Mesopotamia did not become civilized because its people were brilliant. Its people became brilliant because the land forced them to invent civilization or starve.

> Sumerian was still being taught in scribal schools two thousand years after the last native Sumerian speaker died, the way Latin was still taught in European schools long after the fall of Rome.

> Throughout most of its history, Mesopotamia was not unified. It was a patchwork of independent city-states — individual cities, each ruling the farmland immediately around them, each with their own king and patron god, each warring constantly with their neighbors over land, water, and prestige.

> Eridu established the template for all subsequent Mesopotamian religion: temples are permanent places, not permanent buildings. The physical structure can change, but the sacred spot must never move.

> Her symbol was an eight-pointed star (representing Venus, which appears alternately as the "morning star" and "evening star" — the Sumerians knew these were the same planet millennia before Western astronomy figured it out).

# Elam Summary Fact-Check

Cross-check of `narratives/elamite-civilization.summaries.json` against `narratives/elamite-civilization.md`. Overall quality is high — most bullets compress the narrative cleanly. A handful of drift cases are flagged below.

---

## Chapter 1 — Two Elams

**Bullet 8** (starts with "Queen Puabi's headdress and the other..."):
- [STRONG] **Claim:** Puabi's headdress and the Royal Tombs of Ur were full of "Elamite-routed lapis lazuli, Harappan-routed carnelian, and **Iranian tin**."
- **Problem:** The narrative does not list tin among the materials in the Puabi/Royal Tombs inventory, and the tin it does describe comes from Badakhshan in northern Afghanistan, not Iran. The narrative's actual trio is "Badakhshani lapis, Harappan carnelian, and Anatolian/Iranian gold."
- **Source says:** "...Queen Puabi's headdress and the other Royal Tombs of Ur (Mesopotamia Ch 4), assembled from Badakhshani lapis, Harappan carnelian, and Anatolian/Iranian gold all routed through the trade network Elam sat on top of."
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Iranian tin" with "Anatolian/Iranian gold" (and optionally replace "Elamite-routed lapis lazuli" with "Badakhshani lapis" to match the source, though the routing claim itself is correct).

## Chapter 2 — Naram-Sin's Stone

**Bullet 5** (starts with "Naram-Sin broke the treaty and sacked..."):
- [STRONG] **Claim:** Naram-Sin "commissioned the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin to celebrate the campaign" — i.e., to celebrate his sack of Susa / the Elam invasion.
- **Problem:** The narrative is explicit that the Victory Stele commemorates Naram-Sin's conquest of the Lullubi (a Zagros mountain group), not the Elam campaign. The narrative groups them as "part of the same campaign cycle," but the stele itself is a Lullubi victory monument.
- **Source says:** "This invasion also produced one of the most spectacular surviving Akkadian artworks: the Stele of Naram-Sin, that same Victory Stele we just mentioned, which was carved in commemoration of the conquest of a people called the 'Lullubi' (an obscure mountain group in the northern Zagros, but close enough to Elam to be part of the same campaign cycle)."
- **Suggested fix:** "Naram-Sin broke the treaty and sacked Susa a second time; his Victory Stele — carved to celebrate a separate campaign against the Lullubi mountain people in the same campaign cycle — is the same stele Shutruk-Nahhunte would drag back to Susa as war loot more than a thousand years later."

## Chapter 5 — The Ziggurat Generation

**Bullet 5** (starts with "While Untash-Napirisha was finishing Chogha Zanbil..."):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** Ramesses II was "carving the four 66-foot-tall seated colossi of himself into the cliff face at Abu Simbel."
- **Problem:** The narrative describes Abu Simbel as "colossal rock-cut temples at Abu Simbel into the cliffs of Nubia" without specifying "four 66-foot-tall seated colossi." The specific dimensions and count are historically accurate but are not present in the source narrative; this is detail added in compression.
- **Source says:** "Ramesses II was carving the colossal rock-cut temples at Abu Simbel into the cliffs of Nubia (Ancient Nubia Ch 7)..."
- **Suggested fix:** "While Untash-Napirisha was finishing Chogha Zanbil, Ramesses II was carving the colossal rock-cut temples of Abu Simbel into the cliffs of Nubia — two Late Bronze Age monument-building competitions running in parallel at opposite ends of the Near East." (Remove the unattested "four 66-foot-tall seated colossi" specifics.)

## Chapter 6 — Sack of Babylon

**Bullet 8** (starts with "Nebuchadnezzar I did not recover the Code..."):
- [STRONG] **Claim:** Both the Code of Hammurabi and the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin "would stay at Susa until French archaeologists excavated them in 1901."
- **Problem:** The narrative gives two different excavation dates. The Code of Hammurabi was excavated in 1901 by Jacques de Morgan; the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin was excavated in **1898** by the same French team. The bullet collapses both to 1901.
- **Source says:** Of the Code: "It stayed at Susa until 1901 CE, when the French archaeologist Jacques de Morgan excavated it..." Of the Naram-Sin stele: "Taken from the Akkadian cultural heritage sites of lower Mesopotamia, carried to Susa, installed in an Elamite temple, excavated in 1898 by the same French team..."
- **Suggested fix:** "Nebuchadnezzar I did not recover the Code of Hammurabi or the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin during the sack... and both would stay at Susa until French archaeologists excavated them at the turn of the twentieth century (the Naram-Sin stele in 1898, the Code of Hammurabi in 1901)."

## Chapter 7 — Til-Tuba

**Bullet 1** (starts with "The Neo-Elamite period was a fragmented 550-year dark age..."):
- [STRONG] **Claim:** The Neo-Elamite period was "a fragmented **550-year dark age**."
- **Problem:** The bullet conflates two different spans. The Neo-Elamite period proper runs roughly 1100–539 BCE (~560 years), but the actual "dark age" the narrative names is much shorter. Chapter 6 specifies the Elamite Dark Age as roughly 1100–750 BCE, "a 350-year quiet period." Describing the entire 550-year Neo-Elamite period as a dark age misrepresents the narrative — the late eighth and seventh centuries BCE are precisely when Elam re-emerges and fights Assyria.
- **Source says:** Ch 6: "Between roughly 1100 and 750 BCE, Elam is going through what later historians call the Elamite Dark Age — a 350-year quiet period about which we know very little." Ch 7: "The Neo-Elamite period (roughly 1100–539 BCE) is a very different world..."
- **Suggested fix:** "The Neo-Elamite period replaced the old Sukkalmah-style centralized state with a loose confederation of highland and lowland city-states, each with its own royal line, all sharing the old pantheon and scribal tradition — the first three and a half centuries of this era are a near-silent 'Elamite Dark Age' before the kingdom reappears in the eighth century BCE."

## Chapter 8 — The Persian Echo

**Bullet 1** (starts with "Here is the fact most general histories miss..."):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Cyrus the Great's Cyrus Cylinder lists **four generations of his ancestors** as kings of Anshan."
- **Problem:** The narrative lists Cyrus himself plus three ancestors (father Cambyses, grandfather Cyrus I, great-grandfather Teispes) — i.e., four generations total in the royal line, but only three generations *of ancestors*. The phrasing "four generations of his ancestors" is off by one.
- **Source says:** "...Cyrus calls himself king of Anshan, son of Cambyses (who was also king of Anshan), grandson of Cyrus I (king of Anshan), great-grandson of Teispes (king of Anshan), of 'an enduring royal line' — the standard translation... chains all four generations together through the Anshan title."
- **Suggested fix:** "...Cyrus the Great's Cyrus Cylinder chains four generations of his royal line — himself, his father, grandfather, and great-grandfather — together as kings of Anshan, 'of an enduring royal line.'"

## Chapter 1 — The City on the Tigris

1. **"a small city called Assur was already old"** — How old? The text says "nobody living there could remember a time before the city existed" but gives no founding date or earliest archaeological evidence — When was the earliest settlement at Assur?

2. **"the rolling hills and grasslands of the northern uplands"** — What does northern Mesopotamian landscape actually look like? The text says "rolling" but gives no visual anchor — Is this like the Scottish Highlands? The American Great Plains? What grows there besides barley?

3. **"He had no elaborate mythology, no colorful origin story"** — What did the Ashur cult actually look like in practice? What did worshippers do? What were the rituals? The text says what Ashur *wasn't* but not what worship of Ashur *was* — What did an ordinary Assyrian do to worship Ashur?

4. **"mountain peoples of the Zagros — Hurrians, Gutians, Kassites, and later Medes and Persians"** — Hurrians and Gutians are undefined. The text introduces them as "mountain peoples" but gives no sense of who they were, when they were active, or what made them distinct — Who are the Hurrians and Gutians specifically?

5. **"mass-produced grain managed by temple bureaucracies and palace complexes"** — What does "temple bureaucracy" look like in practice? Who are the people in it? What do they actually do day-to-day? — How does a temple bureaucracy manage grain production?

6. **"the cedar forests of Lebanon to the west"** — Why is cedar important? The text lists it as a trade good but doesn't explain what cedar was used for or why it was worth trading over long distances — What was cedar used for and why was it valuable?

7. **"a military culture and a political identity centered on the god Ashur that gave their civilization a harder, more aggressive edge"** — Empty claim. What does "harder, more aggressive edge" look like concretely before the empire period? The text asserts this character existed but gives no examples from the early period — What specific practices or behaviors made early Assyria "harder" than the south?

8. **"semi-mythical ancestors who 'lived in tents'"** — Who are these ancestors? Are any named? How many generations of "tent-dwellers" are on the list? — What do the legendary entries on the King List actually say?

9. **"Failure to celebrate the Akitu was considered a sign of political crisis"** — Empty claim without a concrete example. Did this ever actually happen? When? — Can you give a specific instance when the Akitu was disrupted and what happened?

10. **"the Akkadian habit of recording royal achievements in boastful inscriptions on palace walls"** — This is a useful note but "boastful inscriptions" is vague. What does a typical inscription actually say? — What does an early Akkadian-style boast inscription at Assur look like?

## Chapter 2 — The Merchant Princes

1. **"Assyrian merchants were running what may have been the world's first international trade network"** — "First" claim without cross-cultural comparison. What about earlier Egyptian trade with Byblos, or Indus Valley trade networks? — Were there no comparable long-distance trade networks before 1950 BCE?

2. **"insurance against loss was available, at a price: the tablets document what scholars have identified as formal insurance contracts"** — Which scholars? This is a specific scholarly identification that needs at least a one-line attribution — Who identified these as insurance contracts, and is that identification universally accepted?

3. **"joint-stock ventures"** — Which scholars have called them this? The text says "scholars have called" but names none — Who uses the term "joint-stock ventures" for these arrangements?

4. **"roughly 200 to 300 donkeys per caravan"** — Suspiciously specific range. Where does this number come from? — Is the 200-300 donkey count from the tablets or a modern estimate?

5. **"The mortality rate for donkeys was high"** — How high? 10%? 50%? The text asserts high mortality without any concrete number or example — What percentage of donkeys died on a typical journey?

6. **"there were at least 20 karums scattered across Anatolia"** — Undefined: how big were the other karums? Were they all the same structure as Kanesh? — Were all 20 karums the same size and structure, or was Kanesh uniquely large?

7. **"His name was Shamshi-Adad I"** — The transition from the merchant network to Shamshi-Adad is abrupt. The text says "while the merchants had been building their trade network in Anatolia, a warlord had been building a territorial state" — but doesn't explain the relationship between the merchants and Shamshi-Adad. Did he tax them? Conscript them? Ignore them? — What was Shamshi-Adad's relationship to the Assyrian merchant class?

8. **"almost certainly an Amorite"** — The Amorites are briefly parenthetical-defined but "Semitic-speaking people from the Syrian steppe who had been migrating into Mesopotamia for generations" is thin. When did this migration start? Why? — What drove the Amorite migrations and when did they begin?

## Chapter 3 — The Long Wait

1. **"the Mitanni were a Hurrian-speaking kingdom"** — Hurrian is described as "an entirely different language family" but still unexplained. Who are the Hurrians as a people? Where did they come from? When did they first appear? — Who are the Hurrians beyond their language?

2. **"riding the military technology that defined the late Bronze Age: the chariot"** — How does a chariot work in battle? The text mentions "composite bows and light, fast, spoke-wheeled chariots" but doesn't explain the tactics. Does the chariot charge into infantry? Circle at range? — How did chariot warfare actually work tactically?

3. **"the Amarna Letters, a famous archive of Bronze Age diplomatic correspondence found in Egypt"** — Found when? By whom? The Amarna Letters are cited as a key source but given no discovery context — When were the Amarna Letters discovered?

4. **"the mysterious Sea Peoples"** — Described as "coalitions of raiders and displaced populations" but this is still vague. Are any specific groups named? Where did they come from? — Who were the Sea Peoples specifically?

5. **"One influential modern theory frames it as 'system collapse'"** — Whose theory? The text says "influential modern theory" but names no scholar — Who proposed the "system collapse" framework for the Bronze Age Collapse?

6. **"hunted dolphins — a charmingly eccentric detail"** — This is fun but unexplained. Why is dolphin-hunting eccentric for an Assyrian king? What were dolphins to the ancient world? — Why is an Assyrian king hunting dolphins unusual?

7. **"including some of the earliest known mandates for female veiling"** — What classes of women were required to veil? The text says "certain classes" but doesn't specify — Which women had to veil and which didn't, beyond the prostitute/slave exception?

8. **"Israel and Judah were establishing themselves in the power vacuum"** — The kingdoms of Israel and Judah are mentioned without any definition. When did they form? By whom? — What are the kingdoms of Israel and Judah and how did they come into existence?

9. **"Kar-Tukulti-Ninurta"** — The city is named but the text says the nobility saw it as "an insult to the old capital." Why exactly? Kings moved courts before. What made this different? — Why was building a new capital across the river specifically seen as an insult to Ashur?

## Chapter 4 — The Iron Army

1. **"resettled depopulated farmland"** — How does one "resettle depopulated farmland"? Who gets moved in? Where do they come from? — What does resettlement look like in practice during this reconquest period?

2. **"the Assyrian army was essentially permanently deployed"** — How was the agricultural economy sustained with the army always in the field? Who farmed? — If the army campaigned 31 of 35 years, who was growing the food?

3. **"sowing their fields with salt"** — Did this actually happen or is it metaphorical? The text presents it as fact but scholars debate whether ancient salting of fields was practical — Was salting fields a real practice or symbolic language?

4. **"the Nimrud ivories, now divided between the British Museum and the Iraq Museum"** — What do the ivories depict? The text calls them "carved ivory plaques" from Phoenician, Syrian, and Egyptian workshops but gives no description of what's on them — What do the Nimrud ivories actually look like?

5. **"Sammu-ramat served as regent"** — How did she wield power in practice? The text says she "may have accompanied military campaigns" but hedges everything. What did she actually do that's documented? — What specific actions by Sammu-ramat are recorded in the sources?

6. **"the rise of court eunuchs as a distinct and increasingly powerful class"** — How were eunuchs created? Who decided which boys were castrated? Was it voluntary? The text explains the political logic but not the human reality — How did someone become a court eunuch in Assyria?

7. **"Semiramis — a mythical warrior-queen who supposedly built Babylon, conquered India, and invented the eunuch system"** — Which Greek writers tell the Semiramis legend? The text says "Greek writers" without naming any — Who are the Greek writers who created the Semiramis legend?

8. **"Urartu built impressive mountain fortresses, irrigation systems, and metalwork"** — Empty claim. What do the fortresses look like? What irrigation systems? What metalwork? — Can you give one concrete example of Urartian architecture or metalwork?

7. **"a famous letter to the god Ashur (a literary form unique to Assyrian royal inscriptions — the king reports to his god as a subordinate reports to a superior)"** — This is fascinating but the parenthetical interrupts. It deserves its own sentence.

## Must-Fix Items

1. **Ch 2, line 61 (Skeptic):** "The closest comparison in the ancient world is the Persians leveling the terrace at Persepolis around the same time (Persian Empire Ch 2), but Persepolis used the wealth of an empire that stretched from Egypt to India. The Zapotec did it with the resources of a single mountain valley." Persepolis was begun ~518 BCE by Darius I. Monte Albán was founded ~500 BCE. These ARE roughly contemporary. However, the claim that this is "the closest comparison in the ancient world" ignores other major landscape-engineering projects like Teotihuacan's Street of the Dead layout, the Nazca Lines, or various Maya platform-and-causeway complexes. More importantly, Monte Albán was built over centuries, not in a single campaign. The comparison implies a single construction effort rivaling Persepolis, which overstates the case. **Fix:** qualify that the comparison is approximate and that Monte Albán's leveling took centuries of cumulative work, not a single imperial construction campaign.

2. **Ch 2, line 81 (Skeptic):** "Building J's pointed end aligns with the position on the horizon where the bright star Capella rose around 275 BCE." This astronomical alignment claim is attributed to Anthony Aveni and others, but it is debated. Some scholars argue the alignment is coincidental or that the relevant celestial target was different. The narrative presents the Capella alignment as "the leading theory" without naming the alternative views. **Fix:** add "proposed by the archaeoastronomer Anthony Aveni" and note that other scholars have questioned whether the alignment is intentional or which celestial target was the actual referent.

3. **Ch 4, line 171 (Skeptic):** "Its Pyramid of the Sun (roughly 200 feet/60 meters tall)." The Pyramid of the Sun at Teotihuacan is approximately 216 feet (66 meters) tall. The "roughly 200 feet/60 meters" is close enough for informal prose, but the metric conversion is inconsistent: 200 feet = 61 meters, and the actual height rounds better to 65 meters. **Fix:** use "roughly 216 feet (66 meters)" or "over 200 feet (65 meters)" for accuracy.

4. **Ch 7, line 317 (Skeptic):** "8 Deer Jaguar Claw (1063–1115 CE)" — the birth year is sometimes given as 1063 CE (which matches the Codex Zouche-Nuttall) but his death year is commonly cited as 1115 CE. However, some sources give slightly different dates due to calendar-correlation debates. The dates should carry a qualifier. **Fix:** add "according to the Mixtec codices" or "by the standard calendar correlation" before the dates.

5. **Ch 8, line 345 (Skeptic):** "The Aztec Empire reached the Valley of Oaxaca in 1458 CE, when armies under Motecuhzoma I swept south." The Aztec campaigns into Oaxaca are generally dated to the reigns of Motecuhzoma I (r. 1440–1469) and later Ahuitzotl. The 1458 date is specific and appears in some sources but is not universally agreed upon. **Fix:** soften to "in the late 1450s or early 1460s" or add "according to the Aztec tribute records."

6. **Ch 5, line 207 (Newcomer):** Cocijo is described as "the Zapotec equivalent of the Maya rain god Chaac and the Aztec rain god Tlaloc." The Maya are a shipped TL, but the Aztecs are not yet shipped. The Aztec rain god Tlaloc is introduced here with pronunciation but no further context about WHO the Aztecs are. A newcomer reading the Zapotec TL may have no idea what "the Aztecs" refers to. **Fix:** add a brief clause: "the Aztecs (a later central Mexican empire, 14th–16th centuries CE)." The Maya already get a cross-link.

## Should-Fix Items

1. **Ch 1, line 5 (Newcomer):** The elevation is given as "about 5,000 feet (1,500 meters)." The actual elevation of the Valley of Oaxaca is approximately 5,085 feet (1,550 meters). This is close enough, but the metric conversion 5,000 ft = 1,524 m, not 1,500 m. **Fix:** either "about 5,000 feet (1,525 meters)" or "about 5,100 feet (1,550 meters)."

2. **Ch 1, line 22 (Skeptic):** "Structure 28, standing over 15 feet (4.5 meters) tall." Structure 28 at San José Mogote is a real archaeological feature, but the specific height claim of "over 15 feet" should be verified against Flannery & Marcus's publications. The number is plausible but specific enough to warrant a check. **Fix:** verify against Kent Flannery and Joyce Marcus, *Zapotec Civilization* (1996) or add "approximately."

3. **Ch 3, line 115 (Newcomer):** The Danzantes parallel with Aztec tzompantli (skull racks) is mentioned without defining the Aztecs for the reader. The Aztecs appear multiple times in the narrative (Ch 3, 5, 7, 8) but are never given a single clear introduction. The first mention in Ch 3 line 115 says "the Aztecs displayed the skulls of sacrificial victims on skull racks called tzompantli" without explaining who the Aztecs were. **Fix:** at the first substantive mention of the Aztecs (either here or in Ch 5), add a one-clause definition: "the Aztecs (the last great Mesoamerican empire, centered in central Mexico from the 14th to 16th centuries CE)."

4. **Ch 3, line 135 (Newcomer):** "the 260-day ritual calendar that combined 20 named days with 13 numbers" — the piye calendar is well explained here, but the parallel explanation of the yza (365-day calendar) follows in the same paragraph. The sentence "A person's birthday in the piye was their calendrical name for life" is a key concept that could use one more clarifying sentence. A newcomer might wonder: did everyone in the civilization share one of 260 possible names? Were names unique? **Fix:** add a brief note like "multiple people could share the same calendrical name, but additional names or titles distinguished individuals."

5. **Ch 4, line 155 (Story Editor):** "The city grew relentlessly. By around 400 CE, Monte Albán's population had reached an estimated 25,000 people." The chapter starts in 200 BCE and the first population figure is from 400 CE — a 600-year jump in one paragraph. The growth from "several thousand" (Ch 2, 450 BCE) to 25,000 (400 CE) is an 850-year arc that is not paced. **Fix:** add an intermediate population benchmark, e.g., "by the first century CE, the population had likely reached 15,000" or similar, to break up the temporal jump.

6. **Ch 5, line 209 (Newcomer):** "Lyobaa" is defined as the Zapotec underworld and then it says "The city of Mitla, which would later become the Zapotec religious capital (Chapter 7), was believed to be built directly above the entrance to Lyobaa." Mitla has not been introduced before this point. A brief geographic note would help: where is Mitla relative to Monte Albán? **Fix:** add "(in the Tlacolula arm of the valley, about 25 miles/40 km east of Monte Albán)."

7. **Ch 6, line 257 (Skeptic):** "When that great metropolis collapsed around 550 CE (from some combination of internal unrest, environmental stress, and perhaps external attack)." The collapse/burning of Teotihuacan is conventionally dated to around 550–600 CE. Some scholars now argue for a date closer to 550 CE based on ceramic chronology, while others prefer ~600 CE. The text uses 550 CE confidently. **Fix:** use "around 550–600 CE" or "in the mid-sixth century CE."

8. **Ch 7, line 303 (Story Editor):** The Mitla stone mosaic description is excellent, but the paragraph runs long (a single block from "Thousands of precisely cut stone pieces..." through "...a textile art made eternal in rock"). This is one of the narrative's strongest passages, but it could benefit from a paragraph break after "...a single miscut stone would break the pattern." **Fix:** insert a paragraph break for visual breathing room.

9. **Ch 7, line 313 (Newcomer):** The Mixtec are introduced with their language family ("same Oto-Manguean family as Zapotec") and compared to "Spanish and Portuguese" for mutual intelligibility. This is good. But the lost-wax casting technique is defined parenthetically in the next paragraph (Ch 7 line 327) but first mentioned here without definition: "especially gold, using the lost-wax casting technique that had arrived in Mesoamerica from South America." **Fix:** move the parenthetical definition up to the first mention, or at least add "(a technique explained below)" as a forward pointer.

10. **Ch 6, line 273 (Story Editor):** Yagul gets two paragraphs — one introducing it and one expanding on it. The second paragraph ("Yagul deserves closer attention") repeats some information from the first (hilltop, ball court). **Fix:** merge the two Yagul paragraphs into one, cutting the repeated hilltop/defensive logic detail.

11. **Ch 8, line 363 (Newcomer):** "Francisco de Orozco" is introduced with pronunciation but with no context beyond his name. The reader doesn't know his rank, role, or relationship to Cortés. **Fix:** add "a captain serving under Cortés" or similar.

12. **Ch 4, line 193 (Cross-Cultural):** The cross-civ check mentions "the Maurya and early Gupta periods in India" but the Gupta Empire is not a shipped TL and the Maurya Empire is. A cross-link to "Maurya Empire Ch X" would strengthen the connection. **Fix:** add "(Maurya Empire Ch 8)" or similar after the Maurya mention.

13. **Ch 8, line 359 (Skeptic):** "a few hundred Spanish soldiers, their indigenous allies (who did most of the actual fighting), and the devastating impact of European diseases (especially smallpox, which killed millions of indigenous people who had no immunity)." The claim that indigenous allies "did most of the actual fighting" is the modern scholarly consensus (Restall, Townsend) but stating it flatly without attribution could read as a contested claim to some readers. **Fix:** add "as modern historians have increasingly emphasized" or similar qualifier.

## Nice-to-Have

1. **Ch 1 (Cross-Cultural):** The cumulative "firsts" caveat paragraph (lines 12–13) is well-placed and well-written. However, it could explicitly name the parallel Olmec TL (already cross-linked) plus the Early Andean TL as comparisons for "first writing" and "first urbanism" claims across the Americas. Currently the paragraph is broad; a sentence naming the 2–3 strongest alternative claimants would make the caveat more concrete.

2. **Ch 2 (Cross-Cultural):** The Persepolis comparison is good. A parallel to the Maya practice of massive limestone platform construction (e.g., El Mirador's Danta platform, ~300 BCE, estimated 2.8 million cubic meters of fill) would strengthen the "ambitious landscape engineering" comparison within the Americas.

3. **Ch 3 (Cross-Cultural):** The writing-origins discussion is thorough. A brief note that Old World writing also had a "multiple simultaneous inventions" pattern (Mesopotamia ~3400 BCE, Egypt ~3250 BCE, Indus ~2600 BCE, China ~1250 BCE) is already hinted at but could be made more explicit with TL cross-links to Mesopotamia Ch 3 and Indus Valley Ch 5.

4. **Ch 4 (Cross-Cultural):** The Teotihuacan-Monte Albán diplomatic relationship is well-drawn. A missed comparison opportunity: the Oaxaca Barrio at Teotihuacan is analogous to merchant/diplomatic enclaves in other ancient civilizations — the Assyrian kārum at Kanesh (Assyrian Empire Ch 1) or Harappan merchants in Mesopotamia (Indus Valley Ch 7). A single sentence cross-referencing one of these would reinforce the pattern.

5. **Ch 5 (Story Editor):** The jaguar-kingship paragraph at the end of Ch 5 (line 241) is a nice thematic note but feels slightly disconnected from the surrounding material. It follows human sacrifice and precedes the cross-civ check. Consider moving it closer to the discussion of Cocijo and the deity pantheon, where animal symbolism fits more naturally.

6. **Ch 6 (Story Editor):** The opening three sentences of Ch 6 ("Cities don't die the way people do...") are among the strongest writing in the entire narrative. No changes needed — just noting this as a highlight.

7. **Ch 7 (Cross-Cultural):** The Mixtec codices are compared to "medieval European chronicles." This is a good comparison. An additional comparison to the Genealogical Lienzos could be drawn to the Polynesian oral genealogies (Polynesian Voyagers Ch 3–4) as parallel systems for legitimizing political authority through ancestry — both cultures without alphabetic writing developed sophisticated genealogical record-keeping.

8. **Ch 8 (Cross-Cultural):** The conquest/survival theme would benefit from an explicit comparison to the Nubian pattern — the Kingdom of Kush also survived the loss of its capital (Napata to Meroë), foreign conquest (Assyrian invasion, 663 BCE), and cultural pressure while maintaining identity for centuries. The parallel is strong and both are shipped TLs.

9. **General (Story Editor):** The narrative uses no em-dashes for definitions (good — fully compliant with writing rules). Zero instances of "as far as we can tell" or "we met" phrasing. Parenthetical definitions are used consistently and correctly throughout. Pronunciations are provided for all unfamiliar terms on first use. Miles-first convention is followed everywhere. These are all clean passes.

10. **General (Newcomer):** The word "Mesoamerica" is used frequently but never explicitly defined. It first appears in line 5 ("one of the most productive agricultural zones in highland Mesoamerica") without a definition. The Olmec TL presumably defines it, but a newcomer starting with the Zapotec TL would not know what region "Mesoamerica" covers. **Fix (nice-to-have):** add a brief parenthetical on first use, e.g., "highland Mesoamerica (the cultural region spanning central and southern Mexico through Central America)."

## Summary

This is a strong, well-structured narrative with excellent prose, thorough scene-setting, and rigorous handling of contested claims. The cumulative "firsts" caveat in Ch 1 is exemplary and the writing-origins discussion in Ch 3 is the best handling of a contested scholarly debate in any shipped TL. The Ch 6 opening is outstanding prose. The six must-fix items are primarily factual precision issues (dates, measurements, attribution of astronomical claims) and one newcomer-accessibility gap (Aztecs undefined). None require structural changes. The narrative fully complies with writing rules on em-dashes, parenthetical definitions, pronunciations, miles-first, and uncertainty language variety. Cross-cultural connections are solid but could be deepened in a few spots, particularly with the shipped Nubian and Assyrian TLs.

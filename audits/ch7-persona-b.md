## Chapter 7 — The Old Babylonian Age: Hammurabi's Babylon

1. `[STRONG]` **"The Kanesh karum alone has yielded over 23,000 cuneiform tablets"** — The figure in wide circulation is approximately 23,500, but the chapter's companion claim that the Mari archive yielded "over 25,000 cuneiform tablets" in the same breath produces a suspiciously tidy pair of round-number archives. The Kanesh figure is plausible but should be verified; estimates vary significantly in the literature and some put the total higher.

2. `[STRONG]` **"One letter from a merchant's wife back in Assur scolds him for not sending enough money home"** — Presented as a specific, real letter from the Kanesh archive. This kind of too-perfect domestic anecdote is exactly what AI invents to humanize historical records. There are genuine letters from women in the Kanesh archive, but the specific content described here (wife scolding husband about money) should be traced to a specific published translation, not accepted as is.

3. `[STRONG]` **"One letter records Zimri-Lim furiously complaining that a rival king sent him a smaller diplomatic gift...demanding to know exactly how many horses were sent to the other king"** — Extremely vivid and specific anecdote attributed to the Mari tablets. The Mari archive does include gift-diplomacy correspondence, but this level of narrative specificity — the fury, the horse-count demand as a political crisis — reads as a dramatized paraphrase or invention. Needs a real citation.

4. `[STRONG]` **"Hammurabi's own prologue to the code explicitly states that he erected the stele 'so that the strong might not oppress the weak'"** — Presented as a direct quotation from the prologue. The actual prologue epilogue language is more elaborate and varies by translation. This is a common paraphrase/condensation that circulates as a quote, but it may not be a real word-for-word translation from any standard scholarly edition.

5. `[STRONG]` **"If a free man destroys the eye of another free man, his eye shall be destroyed. If he breaks a free man's bone, his bone shall be broken."** — Presented in quotation marks as if a direct translation. This is plausible as a paraphrase of Laws 196–197, but the smooth, parallel English phrasing feels like a polished reconstruction rather than any specific scholarly translation. Should be verified against a named translation (e.g., Roth's *Law Collections from Mesopotamia and Asia Minor*).

6. `[STRONG]` **"A 2017 study of Plimpton 322 by scholars at the University of New South Wales suggested it was actually a trigonometric reference table"** — There was a 2017 paper by Daniel Mansfield and N.J. Wildberger (UNSW) making this claim. The claim itself is real, but the framing "if this interpretation is correct (and it is debated)" understates the reception: the paper was widely criticized by Assyriologists as overclaimed. The concern here is whether the affiliation and year are accurate, and whether calling it simply "debated" misrepresents the scholarly response.

7. `[CHECK]` **"A shekel of silver (about 8.3 grams, roughly the weight of three pennies) could buy you about 300 liters of barley"** — The 8.33g shekel weight is documented, but "300 liters of barley" is a suspiciously precise purchasing-power figure. Old Babylonian grain prices varied enormously by period and region. This level of precision suggests either a specific source is being cited accurately, or a single data point is being generalized as typical.

8. `[CHECK]` **"loans carried interest at rates typically around twenty percent per year"** — The Code of Hammurabi does cap interest (Law 89 sets limits around 20–33% depending on commodity), but "typically around twenty percent" as a descriptor of actual market rates conflates the cap with the norm. The specific figure is plausible but may be presenting the ceiling as the average.

9. `[CHECK]` **"Esagila (eh-SAH-gih-lah; meaning 'house whose top is high')"** — The standard scholarly translation of Esagila is "house of the raised head" or "house whose head is lofty" (from Sumerian *é* = house, *sag* = head, *íl* = raised). "House whose top is high" is a paraphrase that captures the spirit but is not quite right and should not be presented in parentheses as if it were a direct translation.

10. `[CHECK]` **"the text literally says the gods couldn't sleep because of the racket"** — The Atra-Hasis Epic does contain a passage about the noise of humanity disturbing the gods. But "literally says" is doing a lot of work — what the tablet contains is a poetic Akkadian text, and what we have is a translation. The content is broadly accurate but the word "literally" implies a directness that obscures the translation layer.

11. `[SOFT]` **"At each of the underworld's seven gates, Inanna is forced to remove one piece of her divine regalia — her crown, her lapis lazuli necklace, her breastplate, her gold rings"** — The Descent of Inanna does feature seven gates and the removal of one item at each gate. But the specific list given here (crown, lapis lazuli necklace, breastplate, gold rings) covers only four items; the full canonical list has seven distinct items. The partial list is not invented, but the ordering and naming of specific items may reflect one particular translation or a selective reconstruction.

**Total findings: 5 STRONG, 4 CHECK, 1 SOFT**

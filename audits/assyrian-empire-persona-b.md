## Chapter 1 — The City on the Tigris

1. `[CHECK]` **"roughly 250 miles (400 km) north of Babylon"** — The straight-line distance from Ashur (Qal'at Sherqat) to Babylon is closer to 200 miles. 250 may be a road distance, but worth verifying the specific number.

2. `[SOFT]` **"the same word, the same name, city and god inseparable from the beginning"** — The theological claim that city and god were "inseparable from the beginning" is an interpretive statement. Some scholars argue the god Ashur may have originally been a mountain deity adopted by the city. Low priority but worth noting.

## Chapter 2 — The Merchant Princes

1. `[CHECK]` **"over 23,000 cuneiform tablets"** — This is a widely cited number, but the exact count varies across publications (some say 22,000+, some say 23,500+). The "over 23,000" is conventional but should be checked against the most recent Kültepe excavation reports.

2. `[CHECK]` **"typically 200 to 300 donkeys per caravan, each carrying about 130 pounds (60 kg)"** — The 200-300 donkey figure is suspiciously large for a single caravan. Most scholarly sources describe smaller caravans. The individual donkey load (~60 kg) is plausible for tin/textiles. The caravan size may be conflating total animals in a trading season with a single expedition.

3. `[STRONG]` **"One famous letter from a woman named Taram-Kubi scolds her husband: 'You keep writing, "You must send me fine textiles!" but the textiles I send you are always fine. It's never good enough for you.'"** — This reads like a modern paraphrase rather than a scholarly translation. The Taram-Kubi letters are real and well-documented, but the specific wording here feels smoothed for narrative convenience. The actual translation may be significantly different. Verify against published translations (e.g., Klaas Veenhof or Mogens Trolle Larsen).

4. `[STRONG]` **"Another merchant's wife writes: 'Since you left, the house has fallen down. Send money for repairs.'"** — Same concern. This reads like a modern idiom rather than an ancient text. "The house has fallen down" may be accurate (complaints about crumbling houses appear in the Kanesh letters), but the phrasing "Send money for repairs" feels suspiciously modern. Verify specific translation.

5. `[CHECK]` **"Insurance against loss was available, at a price: the tablets document what scholars have identified as formal insurance contracts"** — The characterization of these arrangements as "formal insurance contracts" is debated. Some scholars see them as risk-sharing agreements rather than insurance in the modern sense. The distinction matters.

6. `[CHECK]` **"joint-stock ventures"** — The term "joint-stock ventures" is used by some Assyriologists (notably Larsen) but others prefer "partnership agreements" or "commission arrangements." The parallel to the Dutch East India Company is provocative but the text's own caveat is appropriate. Mainly flagging because the term itself may be anachronistic.

7. `[CHECK]` **"How long do we have to keep guiding you? Are you a child, not a man? Don't you have a beard on your chin?"** — The Shamshi-Adad letter to Yasmah-Addu is real and well-known. The "beard on your chin" detail is in published translations. But verify the exact phrasing — this reads like a composite or paraphrase of what may be multiple letters.

## Chapter 3 — The Long Wait

1. `[CHECK]` **"42 campaigns in his first five years"** — Tiglath-Pileser I's annals do claim an extraordinary number of campaigns, but "42 in five years" is a specific claim worth verifying against the actual annals. The number is plausible for raiding expeditions but suspiciously high.

2. `[CHECK]` **"hunted dolphins"** — The Tiglath-Pileser I dolphin-hunting claim appears in multiple popular accounts but the original inscription may refer to a different marine animal (the Akkadian term is debated). Some scholars translate it as "nahiru" which could be dolphin or another cetacean.

3. `[CHECK]` **"what may have been the world's first royal zoo and botanical garden at Assur"** — "First royal zoo and botanical garden" is a strong claim. Earlier collections of exotic animals existed in Egypt and possibly Mesopotamia. The "first" qualifier needs hedging.

4. `[SOFT]` **"the Hittites had closely guarded iron-working knowledge during their imperial period, and the collapse dispersed both the technology and the smiths"** — The idea that the Hittites had a "monopoly" on iron-working is increasingly contested in modern scholarship. Iron-working may have developed independently in multiple locations. The narrative presents the old view as settled fact.

## Chapter 4 — The Iron Army

1. `[STRONG]` **"69,574 guests"** — The text itself flags this as "a suspiciously precise number" and then defends it. The number does come from Ashurnasirpal's Banquet Stele (a real inscription), and scholars do take the food quantities seriously. This is fine as presented — the text's own skepticism is appropriate.

2. `[CHECK]` **"sowing their fields with salt"** — The text presents this as factual. The practice of salting conquered fields in the ancient world is debated — some scholars consider it symbolic rather than practical (salt was expensive and the amount needed to render farmland infertile would be enormous). Worth noting that this may be literary convention.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Sammu-ramat served as regent approximately 810-805 BCE"** — The dates and nature of Sammu-ramat's regency are debated. Some scholars argue she was more of a powerful queen mother than a formal regent. The characterization as "the only woman to wield supreme power in the entire history of the Assyrian state" is strong — verify.

4. `[CHECK]` **"the Nimrud ivories, now divided between the British Museum and the Iraq Museum"** — Some Nimrud ivories were looted from the Iraq Museum during the 2003 invasion. The current division may not be accurate as stated.

## Chapter 5 — The World Empire

1. `[CHECK]` **"4 to 4.5 million people were forcibly relocated"** — This is a frequently cited estimate in popular accounts, often attributed to Bustenay Oded's work. But the number derives from Assyrian royal inscriptions that are widely acknowledged to be inflated. More cautious estimates are lower. The text acknowledges possible inflation but still presents 4-4.5 million as the scholarly estimate.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the ancient world's first true standing army"** — "First standing army" is a strong claim. Egypt's New Kingdom had permanent professional soldiers. The characterization of earlier armies as purely seasonal may overstate the case. The text hedges with "in the Assyrian sense" but the "first" claim could be challenged.

3. `[CHECK]` **"27,290 Israelites"** — From Sargon's annals. The text appropriately flags this as "a suspiciously precise number" and hedges ("the actual count is unknowable, but the order of magnitude is probably right"). Fine as presented.

4. `[SOFT]` **"The Persian Royal Road that Herodotus described running 1,700 miles (2,700 km) from Sardis to Susa followed many of the same routes the Assyrians had built"** — The claim that the Persian Royal Road followed Assyrian routes is plausible but the degree of overlap is debated. "Many of the same routes" may overstate the archaeological evidence.

## Chapter 6 — Sennacherib and the Siege of Jerusalem

1. `[STRONG]` **"The Aqueduct of Jerwan — a 300-meter-long stone bridge"** — The Jerwan aqueduct is real and well-documented (Jacobsen and Lloyd's 1935 study). But "some scholars argue the Jerwan aqueduct was the first true aqueduct bridge of its type anywhere in the world" needs attribution. Which scholars?

2. `[CHECK]` **"Stephanie Dalley of Oxford University has made a provocative argument that Sennacherib's gardens at Nineveh — not Nebuchadnezzar's Babylon — were the actual Hanging Gardens"** — Stephanie Dalley is real, and this is her published argument. The attribution is accurate. The text appropriately presents it as debated.

3. `[CHECK]` **"military swimmers crossing rivers using inflated animal skins as flotation devices, breathing through reed tubes while fully armed. These are the earliest known depictions of combat diving"** — The reliefs depicting swimmers with inflated skins are real. But calling them "combat diving" is a stretch — they appear to be crossing rivers, not diving. And "earliest known depictions" should be verified against Egyptian and other Near Eastern evidence.

4. `[CHECK]` **"30 talents of gold, 800 talents of silver"** — Sennacherib's annals say 30 talents of gold and 800 talents of silver. The biblical account (2 Kings 18:14) says 30 talents of gold and 300 talents of silver. The discrepancy is well-known but the text only gives the Assyrian figure without noting the biblical disagreement.

5. `[CHECK]` **"possibly bubonic plague, possibly cholera or dysentery"** — The disease identification is speculative. Some scholars have suggested tularemia (based on the Herodotus mouse story). The text presents plague/cholera/dysentery without noting other candidates.

## Chapter 7 — The Last Great King

1. `[CHECK]` **"symptoms that some modern scholars have tentatively identified as lupus"** — The lupus identification for Esarhaddon's illness appears in some medical history literature but is highly speculative. Other diagnoses have been proposed. Flagging for verification of which scholars proposed this.

2. `[STRONG]` **"the Story of Ahiqar"** — The Story of Ahiqar is real and the Elephantine papyrus copy is documented. But the claim that it is "after the Epic of Gilgamesh, possibly the most widely distributed piece of ancient Near Eastern literature" is a strong superlative. The Gilgamesh epic's distribution is well-documented; ranking Ahiqar second needs evidence.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Ashurbanipal actually bragged about his literacy in his royal inscriptions"** — This is well-documented and accurate. The specific claims about reading Sumerian and solving mathematical problems are from his inscriptions. Fine.

4. `[CHECK]` **"over 30,000 tablets"** — The Library of Ashurbanipal tablet count varies across sources (some say 20,000-30,000 fragments, with the number of distinct "texts" being much smaller). "Over 30,000 tablets" may conflate tablets with fragments.

5. `[CHECK]` **"the Saros cycle — the 18-year-and-11-day repeating pattern of lunar eclipses"** — The Saros cycle is real. Whether it was "discovered" from records in Ashurbanipal's library specifically, or from the broader Babylonian astronomical tradition, is a nuance worth checking.

6. `[STRONG]` **"Ashurbanipal's inscription about this campaign is one of the most viciously triumphalist pieces of writing in ancient history: he boasts of making the land 'empty,' of sowing fields with salt"** — "Sowing fields with salt" appears again (also in Ch 4 for Ashurnasirpal). The text says "scholars debate whether this was literal or symbolic" — good. But the specific quote "the voice of man, the tread of cattle and sheep, the happy shouts of merry-making — I made them cease" needs verification against published translations. The phrasing is suspiciously poetic.

7. `[CHECK]` **"the severed head of Teumman, the Elamite king killed at the Battle of Til-Tuba in 653 BCE"** — The Garden Party relief and the Teumman head are well-documented. But the date of the Battle of Til-Tuba is usually given as 653 BCE in some sources and 654/653 in others. Minor point.

## Chapter 8 — The Fall

1. `[STRONG]` **"became so excited that he began pulling off his clothes in the middle of the British Museum workroom"** — The George Smith undressing story is widely repeated in popular accounts of Assyriology. However, the original source for this anecdote is unclear — it may derive from a single Victorian-era account that has been embellished in retelling. Some modern Assyriologists note there is no contemporary documentation of this event. Flag for verification.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the detailed records that characterize the rest of Neo-Assyrian history dry up around 635 BCE"** — The dating of when Assyrian records become sparse is approximate. Some scholars place the gap slightly earlier or later. The "around 635 BCE" is reasonable but imprecise.

3. `[CHECK]` **"When the Greek general Xenophon marched past the ruins of Nineveh, the local villagers could not identify what city had once stood there"** — Xenophon's Anabasis does describe passing large ruins near the Tigris, and his guides reportedly did not know the city's name. But some scholars debate whether the ruins Xenophon described were actually Nineveh or nearby Nimrud. The identification is conventional but not certain.

4. `[CHECK]` **"the alliance was sealed by a royal marriage between a Median princess and Nabopolassar's son"** — The marriage alliance between the Medes and Babylonians is reported by later sources (Berossus via Josephus) but is not confirmed in contemporary Babylonian Chronicles. The text hedges ("according to some sources" and "whether or not the marriage actually happened"), which is appropriate.

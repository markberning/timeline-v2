## Chapter 2 — Two Grains, Two Worlds

**Bullet 2** (starts with "At Cishan in the Taihang foothills..."):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "tens of thousands of kilograms of carbonized millet — the earliest large-scale grain storage anywhere in the world."
- **Problem:** The bullet passes along the impressive figure without carrying any of the narrative's explicit hedging. The narrative flags the total as "sometimes contested and may overstate the total," but the bullet reads as settled fact.
- **Source says:** "The originally reported figures — around 88 pits and roughly 50,000 kilograms (about 110,000 pounds) of stored grain — are sometimes contested and may overstate the total, but even conservative estimates put Cishan in a class of its own for its era."
- **Suggested fix:** "At Cishan in the Taihang foothills, archaeologists found dozens of underground storage pits whose carbonized millet, even at conservative estimates, represents the earliest large-scale grain storage anywhere in the world."

## Chapter 3 — The Painted Pottery World

**Bullet 5** (starts with "Traces of silk fiber found..."):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Traces of silk fiber found at late Yangshao and Liangzhu sites date to around 3500 BCE."
- **Problem:** The narrative attributes the earliest evidence to "a cocoon cut in half with a stone tool, recovered from Shanxi province," not explicitly to a "late Yangshao" site. The bullet names a cultural attribution that the narrative itself does not make.
- **Source says:** "a cocoon cut in half with a stone tool, recovered from Shanxi province, dates to around 3600 BCE, and silk thread fragments from the Liangzhu culture (Chapter 5) are not much younger. By 3500 BCE, silk was being made and worked somewhere in Neolithic China."
- **Suggested fix:** "A stone-cut cocoon from Shanxi dates to around 3600 BCE and silk thread fragments from Liangzhu are only slightly younger — Chinese silk production began thousands of years before anywhere else, pairing with Indus cotton as the two signature Asian fibers."

## Chapter 6 — Longshan — Walls, Warlords, and the Yellow Emperor

**Bullet 3** (starts with "At Shimao in Shaanxi..."):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "builders buried pits of sacrificed young women under the city walls as foundation offerings around 2000 BCE."
- **Problem:** The narrative specifically describes **decapitated heads** (skulls) buried in the foundations, not whole sacrificed bodies. "Pits of sacrificed young women" naturally reads as complete-body burials, which drops a distinctive and grisly detail the narrative emphasizes.
- **Source says:** "archaeologists have found decapitated human heads, deliberately buried in the foundations. Several pits containing dozens of skulls each, concentrated on young women, arranged in deliberate rows."
- **Suggested fix:** "At Shimao in Shaanxi, builders buried pits of decapitated skulls — concentrated on young women — under the city walls as foundation offerings around 2000 BCE, a direct parallel to the contemporary Royal Tombs of Ur, while the Indus cities did nothing of the kind."

## Chapter 7 — The Flood and the First Dynasty

**Bullet 6** (starts with "The Xia tradition established..."):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "The Xia tradition established the template of dynastic succession and the Mandate of Heaven."
- **Problem:** The narrative explicitly hedges on the Mandate of Heaven, saying it "was, according to tradition, formally crystallized during the Zhou dynasty a thousand years later" and that only "the *idea* behind it" was "already implicit in the Xia tradition." The bullet flattens that hedge into "established."
- **Source says:** "The Mandate of Heaven is one of the central concepts in Chinese political thought, and it was, according to tradition, formally crystallized during the Zhou dynasty a thousand years later. But the *idea* behind it... was already implicit in the Xia tradition."
- **Suggested fix:** "The Xia tradition established the template of dynastic succession and the idea later formalized by the Zhou as the Mandate of Heaven — that rulers must earn their right to rule and can be legitimately overthrown when they lose it, a pattern that would repeat for four thousand years."

Overall the summaries track the narrative tightly — dates, distances, site sizes, ruler names, and ratios all match. No hallucinated details. The only drift is in a few places where bullets drop explicit hedges (Cishan grain total, Mandate of Heaven attribution) or smooth out a specific grisly detail (Shimao decapitated heads → "sacrificed young women"). The chapter 3 silk bullet assigns a cultural label ("late Yangshao") the narrative itself doesn't commit to, but the underlying dates are consistent.

## Chapter 3 — The First City — Uruk and the Sumerian Breakthrough

**Bullet 8** (starts with "The 13-meter-tall White Temple of"):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "The 13-meter-tall White Temple of Uruk, built around 3200 BCE, was probably the tallest building on earth."
- **Problem:** The 13 meters is the height of the platform, not the temple itself. The narrative is precise: the single-room temple "sat on top of a thirteen-meter-high whitewashed platform."
- **Source says:** "a single-room mud-brick shrine dedicated to Anu ... it sat on top of a thirteen-meter-high whitewashed platform (roughly a four-story building) that rose out of the flat city like an artificial mountain. It was probably the tallest building in the world at the time."
- **Suggested fix:** "The White Temple of Uruk, built around 3200 BCE atop a 13-meter-high whitewashed platform, was probably the tallest building on earth."

## Chapter 4 — The Age of City-States

**Bullet 5** (starts with "The Royal Tombs of Ur,"):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "excavated by Leonard Woolley in the 1920s, contained ... 74 attendants who died alongside their queen."
- **Problem:** Two small imprecisions. Woolley's excavation ran 1922–1934, not just "the 1920s." And the narrative describes the 74 skeletons as accompanying "the primary burial ... their ruler" — the ruler's sex is not specified in the narrative. Queen Puabi is named earlier in a different tomb context.
- **Source says:** "excavated by the British archaeologist Leonard Woolley between 1922 and 1934 ... In the largest tomb, Woolley found 74 skeletons arranged in rows ... All of them had apparently died alongside the primary burial to accompany their ruler into the afterlife."
- **Suggested fix:** "The Royal Tombs of Ur, excavated by Leonard Woolley between 1922 and 1934, contained ... 74 attendants who died alongside a ruler."

## Chapter 5 — The First Empire — Sargon and Akkad

**Bullet 8** (starts with "Around 2193 BCE, mountain raiders"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "the real killer was the 4.2 kiloyear event, a century-long drought that also wrecked the Indus Valley and Liangzhu China at roughly the same moment."
- **Problem:** "At roughly the same moment" misrepresents the narrative's careful chronology. The narrative places Liangzhu's collapse on the *leading edge* of the event (2400–2300 BCE, before Akkad's fall) and the Indus Valley decline *several centuries later*. They were not simultaneous.
- **Source says:** "The same drought — now called the 4.2 kiloyear event — also contributed to the Indus Valley Civilization's decline several centuries later ... the Liangzhu hydraulic civilization collapsed around 2400–2300 BCE on the leading edge of the event ... One global climate disruption, four Bronze Age civilizations affected."
- **Suggested fix:** "the real killer was the 4.2 kiloyear event, a century-long drought that rippled through the Indus Valley and Liangzhu China across the same climate window."

## Chapter 7 — The Old Babylonian Age — Hammurabi's Babylon

**Bullet 5** (starts with "Around 1764 BCE Hammurabi switched"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Around 1764 BCE Hammurabi switched sides — defeating Rim-Sin of Larsa, then betraying his former ally Zimri-Lim and burning Mari to the ground."
- **Problem:** Off-by-one date drift. The narrative dates the move to 1763 BCE and Mari's destruction to 1761 BCE. The summary blob at the top of the chapter also says 1764. Minor but worth flagging — the narrative's in-text date is 1763.
- **Source says:** "Around 1763 BCE, Hammurabi moved. He defeated Rim-Sin of Larsa ... He destroyed Mari around 1761 BCE."
- **Suggested fix:** "Around 1763 BCE Hammurabi switched sides — defeating Rim-Sin of Larsa, then betraying his former ally Zimri-Lim and burning Mari to the ground by 1761."

## Chapter 9 — Assyria's First Rise

**Bullet 10** (starts with "Around 1000 BCE, Babylonian astronomers"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Around 1000 BCE, Babylonian astronomers compiled the MUL.APIN tablets ... and used 18-year Saros cycle patterns to predict lunar eclipses — which gave rise to the ... substitute king ritual."
- **Problem:** Compression telescopes two separate events 200–300 years apart. The narrative places MUL.APIN at ~1000 BCE but explicitly dates the Saros-cycle eclipse-prediction breakthrough to "the 8th or 7th century BCE."
- **Source says:** "Around 1000 BCE, Babylonian astronomers compiled the MUL.APIN tablets ... Once the accumulated observational data reached a critical mass, around the 8th or 7th century BCE, Babylonian astronomers discovered the Saros cycle — an 18-year-and-11-day repeating pattern after which lunar eclipses occur in nearly the same sequence."
- **Suggested fix:** "Around 1000 BCE, Babylonian astronomers compiled the MUL.APIN tablets, the oldest comprehensive star catalog in existence; two centuries later they worked out the 18-year Saros cycle and began predicting lunar eclipses — which gave rise to the strangely brilliant substitute king ritual."

## Chapter 11 — Assyria at its Zenith

**Bullet 3** (starts with "In 722 BCE, Tiglath-Pileser's successor"):
- [STRONG] **Claim:** "Tiglath-Pileser's successor Sargon II captured Samaria and deported 27,290 Israelites."
- **Problem:** Wrong succession. Sargon II was **not** Tiglath-Pileser III's successor. Tiglath-Pileser III was succeeded by his son Shalmaneser V, who started the siege of Samaria. Sargon II (Shalmaneser V's brother) took the throne in a coup during that siege and completed the capture in 722 BCE. The narrative spells this out explicitly.
- **Source says:** "Tiglath-Pileser III's successor was his son Shalmaneser V (ruled 727–722 BCE, a short and somewhat obscure reign) — and Shalmaneser V in turn was succeeded by his younger brother, who took the throne name Sargon II ... In 722 BCE, one of Sargon II's first great achievements as king was completing a siege that Shalmaneser V had started: the capture of Samaria."
- **Suggested fix:** "In 722 BCE, Sargon II — who seized the Assyrian throne in a palace coup during the siege — completed the capture of Samaria and deported 27,290 Israelites."

**Bullet 10** (starts with "In 646 BCE Ashurbanipal unleashed"):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "salting the fields and scattering the bones of Elamite kings."
- **Problem:** Bullet asserts field-salting as fact; the narrative explicitly hedges on whether the salting was literal or a rhetorical/propaganda flourish.
- **Source says:** "the rhetorical — and possibly literal — salting of fields (the inscription's field-salting claim has become one of Ashurbanipal's most quoted images, though modern scholars debate whether it was an actual agronomic sabotage at scale or a symbolic gesture inflated by Assyrian propaganda)."
- **Suggested fix:** "In 646 BCE Ashurbanipal unleashed the Destruction of Elam, rhetorically salting the fields and scattering the bones of Elamite kings from their tombs."

## Chapter 12 — Babylon Resurgent

**Bullet 10** (starts with "Nebuchadnezzar died in 562 BCE,"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "after a shaky succession the throne was seized by Nabonidus ... during which the Akitu New Year festival couldn't be performed at all."
- **Problem:** The bullet implies the throne was seized by Nabonidus directly after Nebuchadnezzar's death "after a shaky succession." The narrative is more specific: Nebuchadnezzar died 562 BCE, there was "a brief succession of short reigns and internal instability," and Nabonidus took the throne in 556 BCE — six years later. The bullet's phrasing is close but glosses over the 6-year gap. Acceptable compression, but worth noting.
- **Source says:** "Nebuchadnezzar died in 562 BCE after his 43-year reign. His immediate successors were weak. After a brief succession of short reigns and internal instability, the throne of Babylon was seized in 556 BCE by ... Nabonidus."
- **Suggested fix:** No fix required if editors accept the compression; otherwise add "in 556 BCE" to anchor the date.

The bullet file is factually solid overall. The only unambiguous drift is Chapter 11 bullet 3 mis-identifying Sargon II as Tiglath-Pileser III's direct successor (skipping Shalmaneser V). Everything else is minor compression, telescoped chronology, or dropped hedging.

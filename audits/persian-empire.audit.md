## Story Editor evaluation (Persona D)

### Chapter 1 — Cyrus the Great

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Cyrus invented the absorption model of empire — conquering by adopting, not destroying — and set the template for everything that followed.

**What works:**
- The opening is excellent: "a tribal king in a mountain valley" to "the largest empire the world had ever seen" in two paragraphs. Immediate stakes.
- The Elamite inheritance thread is beautifully established — connecting Cyrus's "King of Anshan" title back to the previous TL gives this chapter a built-in foundation.
- The Cyrus Cylinder section is the emotional peak, and the comparison to Assyrian royal inscriptions ("bone-collecting, temple-burning, field-salting triumphalism") lands hard.
- The Jewish section (Second Temple, mashiach, Zoroastrian theological traffic) is genuinely illuminating.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the Cambyses paragraph at the very end feels like a rushed appendix. Two sentences to cover the conquest of Egypt and a succession crisis is thin for what's supposed to be the bridge to Darius. Could use 2-3 more sentences to give Cambyses enough weight that the reader feels the empire genuinely wobbling before Darius saves it.

**Momentum map:** Strong open, strong through Babylon and the Cylinder, slightly flat at the eastern campaign summary, good close with the Axial Age cross-civ.

### Chapter 2 — Darius and the Empire's Machinery

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Darius built the administrative machinery that turned a military conquest into a functioning multicultural state — and the Elamite bureaucracy was the engine underneath.

**What works:**
- The Behistun decipherment section is a standout — "Behistun is to cuneiform what the Rosetta Stone is to hieroglyphs" is a perfect one-liner.
- The satrapy system is explained as "a masterpiece of institutional distrust" with concrete mechanism (military commander separate from satrap, royal secretary, King's Eyes and Ears). This is process, not just result.
- Persepolis and the Apadana reliefs — the observation that the reliefs "celebrate diversity rather than homogeneity" contrasted with Assyrian palace reliefs is the chapter's thesis in visual form.
- The Fortification Archive as "the smoking gun for Elamite-to-Persian administrative continuity" closes the loop with the previous TL.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Zoroastrianism section, while excellent in content, is doing heavy theological lifting. It introduces dualism, Ahura Mazda, Angra Mainyu, and the entire Jewish-Zoroastrian influence pipeline in one block. This is appropriate given the chapter's scope, but it runs the risk of feeling like a tangent from the "empire's machinery" throughline. A single transitional sentence linking Zoroastrianism to Darius's administrative ideology (which is partially there with the *drauga* point) could be strengthened.

**Momentum map:** Strong open with the coup narrative, peaks at Behistun decipherment, sustained through satrapies and Persepolis, slight tangent at Zoroastrianism (though the content is strong), good close with Marathon setup.

### Chapter 3 — The Greek Wars

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Persian Wars were less important to the Persian Empire than they were to Greek identity — the Greeks won, wrote the history, and turned a frontier skirmish into civilization's founding myth.

**What works:**
- The opening reframing — calling the standard Greek-freedom-vs-Persian-despotism narrative "propaganda" — is bold and immediately establishes this chapter's angle.
- Marathon, Thermopylae, and Salamis are all given their dramatic due without losing the Persian perspective. The "300 Spartans" moment is handled with the right amount of respect and skepticism.
- The "aftermath was anticlimactic" section is the chapter's most important paragraph: the Persian Empire "barely noticed" the Greek defeats. This recentering is the whole point.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Ionian Revolt section could use slightly more from the Persian perspective. We get the Greek account (Aristagoras's botched Naxos campaign, Athens and Eretria sending ships) but not much about why the Persian system failed to prevent the revolt or how the Persians experienced it internally. This is a minor gap — the sources are Greek, so it's understandable.

**Momentum map:** Strong provocative open, builds through the Ionian Revolt, peaks at Marathon/Thermopylae/Salamis, good anticlimactic close that recenters the narrative.

### Chapter 4 — The Long Afternoon

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** The century-long erosion of the Achaemenid system — not dramatic collapse, but gradual loss of institutional cohesion — is what made Alexander's conquest possible.

**What works:**
- The opening metaphor ("the cost of maintaining the system starts to approach the revenue the system generates") is a smart framing for institutional decline.
- The Anabasis/Ten Thousand section is a well-told adventure set piece that doubles as strategic foreshadowing for Alexander.
- The final paragraph on Darius III — "He was a capable administrator who had been dealt a bad hand" — is a strong corrective to the Alexander-propaganda version.

**What doesn't work:**
- The middle section (Great Satraps' Revolt, Egypt breaking away, Artaxerxes III's reconquest) is the weakest stretch. It reads as a compressed chronological list: this revolt, then that revolt, then this king assassinated that one. The individual beats don't have enough space to develop stakes. The Bagoas-Darius III poisoning scene is vivid but it's surrounded by thin material. This section needs either more narrative texture on 1-2 events (pick the most dramatic and expand) or an explicit structural concession ("the details blur, and that's the point — the pattern is what matters").
- The Artaxerxes I section is particularly flat — "a long and largely stable reign" followed by luxury descriptions. One concrete anecdote or policy decision would bring it to life.

**Momentum map:** Good open with the "least dramatic period" honesty, sags in the middle (Artaxerxes I through the Satraps' Revolt), picks up with the Anabasis set piece, good close with Darius III.

### Chapter 5 — Alexander and the End

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Alexander destroyed the Persian Empire in three years, but the civilization underneath survived — and the burning of Persepolis destroyed a culture's written heritage while accidentally preserving its administrative records.

**What works:**
- The Alexander character sketch in the opening is vivid and efficient: "He carried a copy of the Iliad with him on campaign, and he slept with it under his pillow alongside a dagger."
- Issus and Gaugamela are well-paced battle narratives that keep the Persian perspective foregrounded (Darius's flight as rational survival, not cowardice).
- The Persepolis burning section is the chapter's emotional peak — the paradox of fire destroying the palace but preserving the Fortification Archive is a genuinely great historical irony.
- "The most destructive act of cultural vandalism in the ancient world" — strong editorial voice.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Diadochi section at the end is very compressed. The transition from "Alexander dies" to "three successor kingdoms" happens in one paragraph. This is defensible (it's not the Persian story anymore) but the Seleucid inheritance deserves 2-3 more sentences on how they tried and failed to be Persian kings — it sets up Chapter 6.

**Momentum map:** Explosive open, peaks at Gaugamela, second peak at Persepolis burning, compressed but functional close.

### Chapter 6 — The Parthian Rise

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A nomadic people from the steppe seized an Iranian province, adopted Iranian culture wholesale, and rebuilt the empire from the east — plus they happened to sit on the most lucrative trade route in the ancient world.

**What works:**
- The Seleucid-as-"Greek on top, Iranian underneath" framing is sharp and sets up the Parthian takeover cleanly.
- The feudal confederacy vs. bureaucratic state comparison with the Achaemenids is well explained — it's the structural thesis of the chapter.
- The Silk Road middleman position is concretely drawn with the Nisa example (ivory drinking vessels + fire altars + horse equipment).
- The Carrhae section is superb. The tactical explanation of cataphracts + horse archers vs. Roman infantry is vivid and mechanistic. The "Parthian shot" etymology is a great modern hook.

**What doesn't work:**
- Carrhae probably belongs in Chapter 7 (Parthia vs. Rome) rather than here. Having the defining Rome battle in the chapter titled "The Parthian Rise" front-loads the Rome conflict before the chapter titled for it. The chapter would still be strong without Carrhae — Mithridates I's conquests and the Silk Road position are enough — and Chapter 7 would benefit from opening with Carrhae as its establishing set piece.

**Momentum map:** Solid open, builds through Mithridates I, peaks at Carrhae, good close with the horse-culture metaphor. But the Carrhae peak arguably belongs in the next chapter.

### Chapter 7 — Parthia vs. Rome

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** For three centuries, neither Rome nor Parthia could deliver a knockout blow — and the Euphrates held as the most important border in the ancient world.

**What works:**
- The opening framing of the Euphrates as the world's most important border is immediately compelling.
- The "depressingly predictable pattern" (Rome invades, captures Ctesiphon, can't hold it, withdraws) is a smart structural summary that prevents the chapter from becoming a list of wars.
- The structural explanation for why Rome couldn't hold Mesopotamia (supply lines, cavalry mismatch, hostile population) is excellent — this is mechanism, not just outcome.
- The Armenian compromise (Arsacid prince crowned by Roman emperor — "absurd on its face but it held") is a great detail.

**What doesn't work:**
- Because Carrhae was already covered in Chapter 6, this chapter's military narrative starts with a series of "then this emperor tried and failed" episodes (Caesar planning, Antony failing, Trajan capturing then losing, Severus sacking, Carus dying) that reads as a list. The chapter lacks a single dramatic set piece of its own — Carrhae should have been saved for here.
- The transition to the Arsacid decline and Ardashir's rise at the end is good but very compressed. The reader jumps from "the Zoroastrian priesthood was frustrated" to "in 224 CE, a local king rebelled" without enough buildup of the internal pressures. The Arsacid late period deserves 1-2 more paragraphs of internal detail to make the Sassanid revolution feel earned rather than sudden.

**Momentum map:** Strong open, sags in the middle (the parade of failed Roman invasions), recovers with the structural analysis of why Rome failed, compressed close.

### Chapter 8 — Ardashir and the Sassanid Revolution

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Ardashir didn't just change the dynasty — he changed Iran from a tolerant confederacy into a centralized Zoroastrian theocracy, and his son Shapur proved it worked by capturing a Roman emperor.

**What works:**
- The opening line ("did not just change the dynasty. He changed the idea of what Iran was for") is one of the best chapter openers in the narrative.
- Kartir's inscriptions — "a proud account of state-sponsored religious persecution" — are handled with appropriate chill.
- The Shapur/Valerian capture is the dramatic peak, and the Naqsh-e Rostam relief description is vivid and purposeful (linking Sassanid victory to Achaemenid tombs above).
- The Mani section is excellent — the tension between cosmopolitan synthesis and orthodox centralization is the chapter's intellectual core.
- Bishapur's Roman mosaics as the chapter's closing image ("beat the Romans on the battlefield, then hire their craftsmen") perfectly echoes the absorption thesis.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the Ardashir backstory (Fars, Anahita temple, priest-king) could be slightly expanded. He's the chapter's protagonist but gets less character development than Shapur. One or two more sentences about Ardashir's actual revolt against the last Arsacid would help.

**Momentum map:** Strong open, builds through Kartir, peaks at Shapur/Valerian, sustained through Mani, elegant close with Bishapur.

### Chapter 9 — The Sassanid Golden Age

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Sassanid golden age produced a civilization that didn't just rival Rome militarily but outperformed it culturally — Gundishapur saved Greek philosophy, Persian chess and silk conquered the world, and the Khwaday-Namag became the seed of the Shahnama.

**What works:**
- The Gundishapur section is the narrative's intellectual centerpiece. The chain "Greek texts → Sassanid Gundishapur → Abbasid Baghdad → medieval Cordoba and Toledo → Renaissance Europe" is a genuinely important argument and it's stated clearly.
- Khosrow I is given enough space to feel like a real character (tax reform, canal-building, military reorganization, the Taq Kasra, the Spring of Khosrow carpet).
- The chess etymology and silk production sections are well-placed lighter material that keeps the chapter from becoming purely military/institutional.
- The Khwaday-Namag → Shahnama connection is a strong close that sets up the cultural-afterlife thesis of Chapter 10.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Shapur II section at the top is somewhat compressed. The "crowned before he was born" detail is vivid but the seventy-year reign is then reduced to Julian's invasion and the Christian persecution. One more concrete policy or event from the middle of his reign would prevent it from feeling like "long reign → Julian dies → Christian persecution → moving on."

**Momentum map:** Good open with Shapur II, builds through Gundishapur (the peak), sustained through Khosrow I and cultural achievements, strong close with the Shahnama connection.

### Chapter 10 — The Last Persian Empire

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Sassanid Empire exhausted itself fighting Rome, was conquered by the Arabs in a single generation, and then — in the narrative's master stroke — the Persians reconquered their conquerors culturally.

**What works:**
- Khosrow II's rollercoaster reign is efficiently told — the brief recreation of Achaemenid extent followed by total collapse is dramatic.
- The al-Qadisiyyah battle narrative is vivid and well-paced (the named days, the sandstorm, the elephant panic).
- Yazdegerd III's death at Merv ("killed for his clothes by a miller who didn't know who he was") is a perfect narrative image for the end of an empire.
- The cultural-afterlife section is the payoff of the entire ten-chapter arc. The thesis — "the Persians were conquered militarily, they reconquered their conquerors culturally" — is earned by everything that came before.
- The final three paragraphs are among the best closing paragraphs in any TL in the project. "Three dynasties. Twelve centuries. One idea." This is how you end a book.

**What doesn't work:**
- The 602–628 war is compressed to the point where Heraclius's counterattack feels like a deus ex machina. Two more sentences on what Heraclius actually did would help the reader understand why the Sassanid overextension collapsed so fast.

**Momentum map:** Strong open with Khosrow II, builds through the Arab conquest battles, peaks at Yazdegerd's death, sustains through the cultural-afterlife thesis, excellent close.

---

### Overall Assessment

This is the strongest first draft of any TL in the project so far. The ten-chapter arc is coherent and building: Cyrus establishes the absorption thesis, Darius builds the machinery, the Greek Wars test it, the Long Afternoon erodes it, Alexander destroys the political entity but not the civilization, the Parthians rebuild from the east, Rome tests the rebuilt structure, the Sassanids centralize and refine, the golden age produces lasting cultural exports, and the Arab conquest destroys the empire a final time while the cultural tradition survives. Every chapter knows what it's about, every chapter has a throughline, and the overall thesis ("absorb what you conquer") is stated in Chapter 1, demonstrated through Chapters 2-9, and paid off in Chapter 10.

The voice is consistently strong — informal but not flippant, authoritative but not lecturing. The writing varies between battle narrative, institutional analysis, cultural survey, and theological explanation without losing its register. Cross-chapter references are terse and well-placed.

The single biggest structural improvement would be moving the Carrhae battle from Chapter 6 to Chapter 7, which would solve Chapter 7's lack of a dramatic set piece and tighten Chapter 6's focus on the Parthian rise proper. Chapter 4 (The Long Afternoon) is the only chapter that needs more narrative texture in its middle section. Everything else is minor.

**Chapter grades:** 1 STRONG, 2 STRONG, 3 STRONG, 4 GOOD, 5 STRONG, 6 STRONG, 7 GOOD, 8 STRONG, 9 STRONG, 10 STRONG. Eight STRONG, two GOOD. No WEAK or REWRITE chapters.

## Skeptic findings (Persona B)

### Chapter 1 — Cyrus the Great

1. `[CHECK]` **"Herodotus says Tomyris had Cyrus's body decapitated and his head dipped in a skin full of blood"** — This is Herodotus's account and is flagged by the text as possibly apocryphal. But the specific detail of the blood-filled skin is very vivid — verify this is genuinely from Herodotus and not a later embellishment.

2. `[SOFT]` **"the Persian mythological tradition (which turned him into a founding hero)"** — Vague attribution — which specific mythological tradition? The Shahnama? Earlier oral tradition?

3. `[CHECK]` **"Zoroastrian concepts — angels, Satan, the apocalypse, bodily resurrection, the judgment of the dead — appear to have entered Jewish theology through contact with Persian religion"** — This is a common scholarly claim but "most scholars trace these concepts to Zoroastrian influence" is a strong consensus claim. The actual scholarly landscape is more divided — some scholars see independent Israelite development for some of these concepts. The text should hedge slightly more.

### Chapter 2 — Darius and the Empire's Machinery

4. `[CHECK]` **"the total runs to something like 14,560 talents of silver per year"** — Suspiciously specific number for an ancient tribute total. If from Herodotus, say so explicitly; if not, this feels invented. The "something like" hedge is good, but the precision (14,560 not 14,500 or 15,000) is suspicious.

5. `[SOFT]` **"neither snow nor rain nor heat nor darkness of night"** — Attributed to Herodotus's description of the Persian postal system, and the USPS connection is noted. This is a well-known attribution — the actual Herodotus passage (Histories 8.98) does say something very close to this, but the exact modern English rendering varies by translation. Low risk.

6. `[CHECK]` **"72 stone columns, each 65 feet (20 meters) tall"** — Very specific dimensions for the Apadana. The column count and height should be verified — common sources give varying numbers (some say 36 columns in the main hall plus additional columns in porticos totaling 72; height estimates vary from 19 to 20 meters).

7. `[CHECK]` **"the Royal Road was the longest maintained highway in the ancient world"** — "Longest maintained highway" is a superlative claim. The Royal Road was certainly long, but was it formally "maintained" in a way that distinguishes it from, say, Egyptian trade routes or Chinese roads of similar antiquity? The claim needs mild hedging.

### Chapter 3 — The Greek Wars

8. `[CHECK]` **"he took a bow, shot an arrow into the sky, and said: 'Zeus, grant me vengeance upon the Athenians.' Then he ordered a servant to repeat to him three times at every dinner: 'Master, remember the Athenians.'"** — Attributed to Herodotus, and this is indeed from Herodotus (Histories 5.105). However, the specific phrasing "Zeus" is notable — Darius would not have invoked Zeus; Herodotus is Hellenizing. The text presents this as a direct quote without noting the Hellenization. Low risk but worth noting.

9. `[CHECK]` **"running, according to Herodotus, the last 200 yards to close the gap before the Persian archers could thin their ranks"** — The "200 yards" figure. Herodotus says the Athenians charged "at a run" from a distance that shocked the Persians, but the specific "200 yards" may be a modern military estimate, not Herodotus's number. Verify whether this is from Herodotus or modern reconstruction.

10. `[CHECK]` **"losing about 6,400 dead to the Athenians' 192"** — These numbers are from Herodotus (6.117 for the 6,400 Persian dead, 6.117 for the 192 Athenian dead). The numbers are real Herodotean citations, though modern scholars debate whether Herodotus's Persian casualty number is inflated. The text should note the source.

11. `[SOFT]` **"Stranger, tell the Spartans that we lie here, obedient to their orders"** — This is a famous epitaph traditionally attributed to Simonides. Multiple English translations exist; this one is standard. Low risk.

### Chapter 4 — The Long Afternoon

12. `[CHECK]` **"the Nanda army was reportedly 200,000 strong, which is why Alexander's troops refused to go further"** — The 200,000 figure for the Nanda army comes from late classical sources (Plutarch, Diodorus, Curtius) and is almost certainly inflated. The text uses "reportedly" which helps, but presenting it as the reason Alexander turned back oversimplifies — the army's mutiny had multiple causes.

### Chapter 5 — Alexander and the End

13. `[CHECK]` **"He carried a copy of the Iliad with him on campaign, and he slept with it under his pillow alongside a dagger"** — The Iliad-under-the-pillow story comes from Plutarch (Life of Alexander 8.2), writing 400 years after Alexander. The detail about the dagger specifically should be verified — some versions mention a dagger, others don't.

14. `[CHECK]` **"losing more men to heat and thirst than he had lost in all his battles combined"** — The Gedrosian Desert crossing casualties. This comparison is commonly made in popular history but the actual numbers are debated. The ancient sources (Arrian, Strabo) give varying estimates, and some modern scholars dispute that the Gedrosian losses exceeded total battle casualties. This needs mild hedging.

15. `[SOFT]` **"died there on June 10, 323 BCE"** — The specific date "June 10" for Alexander's death. The Babylonian astronomical diary gives a date that is typically converted to June 10 or 11, 323 BCE. The precision is justified but should perhaps say "around June 10."

### Chapter 6 — The Parthian Rise

The Achaemenids (roughly 559–330 BCE) are only ~230 years. If you count the Sassanids (224–651 CE) that's ~427 years, still shorter. Is the writer counting a modern dynasty? 17. `[CHECK]` **"the English expression 'parting shot' derives"** — The "parting shot" / "Parthian shot" etymology is widely repeated but some etymologists consider it folk etymology rather than established derivation. The text presents it as fact.

18. `[SOFT]` **"polo may have been invented in Parthian Iran"** — Hedged with "may have been" which is appropriate, but the evidence for Parthian-era polo vs. Sassanid-era or even later is thin.

### Chapter 7 — Parthia vs. Rome

### Chapter 8 — Ardashir and the Sassanid Revolution

21. `[CHECK]` **"paid an indemnity of 500,000 gold denarii — one of the largest ransoms in Roman history"** — The 500,000 denarii figure. Some sources say 500,000 aurei (gold coins), not denarii; the difference in value is enormous (1 aureus = 25 denarii). The specific number should be verified and the denomination clarified.

22. `[STRONG]` **"Roman sources (hostile, naturally) say Shapur humiliated him, used him as a human footstool when mounting his horse, and eventually had him flayed alive and his skin stuffed and displayed in a temple"** — The "flayed alive and skin stuffed" account of Valerian's fate. This comes from Lactantius (De Mortibus Persecutorum), a Christian polemicist writing decades later with an explicit anti-pagan agenda. The text says "Roman sources" but should note these are specifically late Christian sources with an axe to grind. The footstool claim is from different sources than the flaying claim — they shouldn't be presented as a single coherent account.

23. `[CHECK]` **"Mani was imprisoned and died in captivity around 274 CE"** — The date and manner of Mani's death are debated. Some traditions say he was flayed alive (possibly conflated with the Valerian story), others say he died in prison. "Around 274 CE" — some sources say 276 or 277 CE.

### Chapter 9 — The Sassanid Golden Age

24. `[STRONG]` **"crowned before he was born — legend says the nobles placed the crown on his pregnant mother's belly — and reigning for seventy years"** — The crown-on-the-belly legend is widely repeated in popular accounts but its earliest source should be identified. Is this from the Shahnama? From al-Tabari? The "seventy years" reign length (309–379) is also worth verifying — some scholars give slightly different regnal dates.

25. `[CHECK]` **"Julian was killed by a spear in a skirmish during the retreat"** — The cause of Julian's death is contested. Some ancient sources say he was killed by a Roman Christian soldier on his own side, not in a Persian skirmish. The text presents "killed by a spear in a skirmish" without acknowledging the controversy.

26. `[CHECK]` **"the first known teaching hospital in the world"** — This is a strong "first" claim for Gundishapur. Some scholars argue that earlier Greek or Indian medical training institutions could qualify, and the evidence for Gundishapur as a "teaching hospital" in the modern sense is largely from later Islamic-era sources projecting backward. Needs hedging.

27. `[CHECK]` **"the largest single-span vault in the world until the 20th century"** — Strong superlative for the Taq Kasra. This is a commonly cited claim but should be verified. Some sources say it was the largest unreinforced brick vault, not the largest vault of any type. The Hagia Sophia's dome (537 CE) and later Gothic vaults might complicate this claim.

28. `[STRONG]` **"The Shahnama is the longest poem ever composed by a single author — over 50,000 couplets"** — This "longest poem by a single author" claim is commonly made for the Shahnama but is contested. The Indian Mahabharata is far longer (~100,000 shlokas), though it's traditionally attributed to multiple authors/a tradition. The distinction "by a single author" is the key qualifier — but even with that, is there certainty Ferdowsi wrote all 50,000+ couplets alone?

29. `[CHECK]` **"the Bakhtshooa family — Nestorian Christian physicians from Gundishapur — served as the personal doctors of the Abbasid caliphs for six generations"** — "Six generations" of the Bukhtishu family as court physicians. The commonly cited number varies between sources — some say seven or eight generations spanning from the 8th to 11th centuries. The core claim is real but the specific "six" should be verified.

### Chapter 10 — The Last Persian Empire

30. `[CHECK]` **"a devastating twenty-six-year war with the Byzantine Empire (602–628 CE)"** — The 602–628 CE dating of the war. The traditional dating begins with the Persian invasion after the murder of Emperor Maurice in 602 CE, but some scholars date the actual outbreak of major hostilities to 603 or 604 CE. "Twenty-six-year" matches the 602–628 range.

31. `[CHECK]` **"reportedly by a local miller who killed him for his clothes and jewelry without knowing who he was"** — The miller story for Yazdegerd III's death. This is from Islamic-era sources (al-Tabari, Ferdowsi). The text appropriately hedges with "reportedly" and notes it "may be apocryphal." Good handling.

32. `[SOFT]` **"Iran is the only country in the Middle East that was conquered by the Arabs and kept its own language"** — This is a commonly made claim but needs qualification. Other conquered regions also retained pre-Arabic languages (Kurdish, Berber, Coptic for centuries). The claim may be specifically about Persian surviving as a prestige literary/administrative language, which is different from simply "kept its own language."

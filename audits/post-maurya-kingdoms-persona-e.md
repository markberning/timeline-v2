## Part 1: Forward Findings (new TL gaps)

### Chapter 2 — The Greek Kings

1. **"The story begins in Bactria (BAK-tree-ah), the region of modern northern Afghanistan and southern Uzbekistan/Tajikistan that the Greeks called 'the land of a thousand cities'"** — The narrative notes that Alexander left garrisons and the Seleucids claimed the east, but doesn't cross-reference the Maurya-Seleucid treaty (Chandragupta gave Seleucus 500 elephants; those elephants won the Battle of Ipsus). The battle's outcome directly enabled the Seleucid political vacuum that allowed Indo-Greek independence. Maurya Empire Ch 1 covers this explicitly. A brief note here would sharpen the causal chain. — Maurya Empire, Chapter 1.

2. **"Demetrius I (deh-MEE-tree-us), king of Bactria around 200–180 BCE, was the first to push south across the Hindu Kush into the Indian subcontinent"** — No mention that this invasion came into the same territorial footprint Chandragupta had seized from the Greeks a century earlier. The narrative covers it in the Maurya Empire ch 8 note about "Greco-Bactrian expansion" but the post-maurya narrative never closes that loop explicitly. — Maurya Empire, Chapter 8.

3. **"the cultural situation in 2nd-century-BCE India: Greeks were becoming Buddhists and Hindus"** — The mechanism of Buddhist conversion among the Greeks in Gandhara was directly seeded by Ashoka's northwestern missions (Third Buddhist Council, missionary expedition to the "Yona" Greeks in Bactria; Maurya Empire Ch 6). The narrative treats Greek-Buddhist synthesis as if it arose spontaneously. A cross-reference noting that Ashoka deliberately sent missionaries to this population would complete the causal chain. — Maurya Empire, Chapter 6.

4. **"Cross-civ check: the Indo-Greek kingdoms (roughly 180–10 BCE) overlap with the late Roman Republic's expansion across the Mediterranean"** — The cross-civ check at the end of the chapter mentions Rome and Han dynasty but not the Parthian Empire, which was the direct eastern neighbor of the Indo-Greek world and the intermediary between Rome and India in both trade and diplomacy. Persian Empire Ch 6-7 covers the Parthian Silk Road connection. — Persian Empire, Chapters 6–7.

### Chapter 3 — Invaders from the Steppe

5. **"The Sakas (SAH-kahs) were Iranian-speaking pastoral nomads from Central Asia — linguistically related to the Scythians who had dominated the Pontic steppe north of the Black Sea (Scythians Ch 1–5)"** — The parenthetical is correct but minimal. The narrative could note that the specific animal-style art, horse burial traditions, and mounted archery the Sakas brought to India were the same traditions the Scythians had developed across the Pontic steppe for centuries. The Sakas were not just linguistically related but culturally continuous. Scythians Ch 1, 3 cover the shared material culture. — Scythians, Chapters 1, 3.

6. **"Around 170 BCE, the Xiongnu (SHYOONG-noo; the nomadic confederation that menaced the Han dynasty on China's northern frontier; Ancient China Ch 8) defeated the Yuezhi"** — The chapter explains the Xiongnu-Yuezhi connection, but doesn't note that at the same moment the Xiongnu were pushing Yuezhi west, they were also driving the Han dynasty to build the first long walls and send Zhang Qian west (Chapter 4 of the same narrative). The Han-Kushan connection in Chapter 4 begins here, in Chapter 3's domino effect. A brief bridging note would strengthen the structural coherence. — Ancient China, Chapter 8.

7. **"The Sakas were pushed into the Indian subcontinent... some through the mountain passes into the Indian subcontinent via Gandhara and the Bolan Pass"** — The Bolan Pass is mentioned without noting that Scythian-related peoples had been using the passes of the Hindu Kush as their route of entry since at least the 7th century BCE, when the Cimmerians and then Sakas were disrupting the Near East. The geographical corridor being used here is the same one the Achaemenids had governed as a satrapy (Gandhara). Persian Empire Ch 1-2 covers the Achaemenid background. — Persian Empire, Chapters 1–2.

8. **"The Sakas gradually expanded from the northwest into western and central India, establishing a line of rulers known as the Western Satraps (from the Persian administrative title satrap, meaning governor)"** — "Satrap" is glossed as "meaning governor" but doesn't note that this title was borrowed directly from the Achaemenid Persian administrative system that had governed the same northwestern territories for two centuries. This is a concrete legacy of Persian administration surviving into post-Maurya India. Persian Empire Ch 2 covers the satrapy system. — Persian Empire, Chapter 2.

### Chapter 4 — The Kushan Empire

9. **"Zhang Qian's mission failed diplomatically — the Yuezhi, comfortably settled in Bactria, had no interest in returning east to fight old enemies — but his journey is remembered as the event that opened the Silk Road"** — The narrative doesn't note that the Silk Road Zhang Qian helped open was also connected westward to the Parthian Empire (Persian Empire Ch 6), which served as the overland corridor from Kushan territory to the Roman world. Han-Kushan-Parthian-Roman is the full chain. — Persian Empire, Chapter 6.

10. **"Kanishka I ruled from approximately 127 to 150 CE... his territory stretched from Central Asia to Varanasi in the Ganges heartland. At its peak, the Kushan Empire was one of the four great powers of the ancient world — alongside Rome, Parthian Iran, and Han China"** — The Parthian Empire is named but not characterized. At the time of Kanishka, Parthia (Persian Empire Ch 7) was simultaneously fighting Rome across the Euphrates. The Kushans' relationship with Parthia was not just geographic proximity — Kushan gold coins were calibrated to the Roman aureus, which Parthia also used in trade. The commercial triangle (Rome → Parthia → Kushan → Han) is the real story. — Persian Empire, Chapter 7; Ancient Rome, Chapters 6–7.

11. **"In 68 CE (a date that is traditional and approximate), Buddhist monks and merchants traveling through Kushan territory brought Buddhism to Han dynasty China"** — The narrative presents this as Kushans transmitting Buddhism eastward, but the Han dynasty narrative (if completed) would complement this. The existing Ancient China narrative doesn't reach this period, but the post-Maurya text could note that the Buddhist transmission to China is the most consequential eastward cultural flow in Asia's history — comparable in scale to the Rome-India pepper trade described in Chapter 7. Worth a brief comparative note.

12. **"Cross-civ check: at Kanishka's peak (roughly 130–150 CE), the Roman Empire under Hadrian and Antoninus Pius was at its own zenith"** — The cross-civ check correctly identifies the four-empire chain. But it doesn't mention that Roman India trade (Chapter 7) was operating simultaneously with this Kushan period. Kanishka's empire was the land link of the same trade system. The Rome-India maritime connection and the Rome-India-Kushan-China land connection should be cross-referenced here. — Ancient Rome, Chapters 7–8 (indirect).

### Chapter 5 — Gandhara and Mathura

13. **"The Gandhara school (roughly 1st century BCE to 5th century CE) produced the most immediately recognizable Buddha images in the ancient world — and the most surprising. A Gandhara Buddha looks Greek."** — The mechanism of Greek artistic influence is explained (2 centuries of Greek rule), but doesn't note that this same Greek artistic vocabulary was the product of Hellenistic traditions that reached India via the same cultural corridor the Achaemenids had opened. Persepolis itself synthesized Greek, Elamite, Babylonian, and Egyptian elements. The aesthetic eclecticism at Gandhara is continuous with the Achaemenid model of absorbing diverse artistic vocabularies. — Persian Empire, Chapter 2.

14. **"narrative relief panels that read like comic strips carved in stone — sequential scenes telling a story from left to right, a convention borrowed from Roman sarcophagus decoration"** — Roman sarcophagus reliefs are mentioned as the source of sequential narrative conventions, but the mechanism of how Roman artistic conventions reached Gandhara is not explained. The Indo-Roman trade (Chapter 7) and the Kushan-Roman diplomatic contacts (Chapter 4) are the vectors. A brief note connecting to those chapters would improve coherence.

### Chapter 6 — The Deccan Powers

15. **"Some guilds functioned essentially as banks, accepting deposits and paying interest, a financial practice more sophisticated than anything in the contemporary Roman world"** — This claim begs for comparison. Roman banking (argentarii, trapezitai) was sophisticated and involved lending, deposits, and letters of credit. The Ancient Rome narrative (Ch 6-7) covers the Roman commercial economy. The claim as stated is too confident without at least acknowledging Roman financial practices. — Ancient Rome, Chapters 6–7.

16. **"The Satavahanas ruled from Pratishthana and controlled trade routes between India's western coast (the Arabian Sea ports that connected to Rome) and the eastern coast (the Bay of Bengal ports that connected to Southeast Asia)"** — The Rome connection is flagged but not connected to the Roman perspective. Ancient Rome Ch 7 (or post-Maurya Ch 7) covers the Roman-Indian trade in detail. A brief forward reference here ("discussed in Chapter 7") would help the reader see the Satavahanas' position in the larger system.

### Chapter 7 — Rome, Trade, and Tamil Kings

17. **"The Roman historian Pliny the Elder (PLIN-ee; 23–79 CE) complained that Rome was spending 50 million sesterces annually on Indian goods"** — Pliny's complaint is well-placed here, but Pliny is identified as a Roman encyclopedist. For readers coming from Ancient Rome narrative, Pliny appears in a different context (natural history, Roman culture). A brief note connecting to Roman Ch 6-7 time period (Neronian-Flavian Rome) would ground the temporal connection. — Ancient Rome, Chapters 6–7.

18. **"The Indian Ocean was becoming, during this period, the busiest maritime zone on the planet. India sat at its center. To the west, Roman ships sailed from Egypt. To the east, Indian and Southeast Asian ships carried goods to the Malay Peninsula, the Indonesian archipelago, and eventually to Chinese ports."** — The China connection is raised but not developed. The post-Maurya narrative's Chapter 4 establishes the Kushan Silk Road; this chapter establishes the maritime route. Together they describe a complete Eurasian trading system. A brief cross-reference connecting Chapter 4's land route to Chapter 7's sea route as complementary halves of the same system would be structurally powerful.

### Chapter 8 — Seeds of the Golden Age

19. **"The Sassanid Empire under Ardashir I conquered the western Kushan territories in Bactria and Afghanistan around 230 CE"** — The Sassanids are described without noting that their rise (Persian Empire Ch 8) was itself a revolutionary reorganization of Iranian power that ended the Parthian-era Silk Road dynamics. The Kushano-Sassanian entity that followed the Sassanid conquest of Bactria is discussed, but the broader context — that this is the same Iranian civilization described in the Persian Empire narrative, now in its third major form — could be noted. — Persian Empire, Chapter 8.

20. **"The Bhagavad Gita (BHAH-gah-vahd GEE-tah; 'Song of the Lord'), composed around 100 BCE and inserted into the Mahabharata, presented a theology of personal devotion (bhakti) to a personal god"** — The bhakti devotional model is described as revolutionary, which it was for India. But the comparison to other ancient religions developing accessible lay devotionalism in the same period is missing. The period 100 BCE–300 CE sees devotional religion become accessible to laypeople across Eurasia: Roman mystery cults (Isis, Mithras), early Christianity, Mahayana Buddhism, and Indian bhakti all emerged in the same centuries. The parallel would be illuminating. — Ancient Rome, Chapter 9 (Christianity context).

## Part 2: Backward Findings (reference TL gaps)

### maurya-empire.md — Chapter 6 — The Buddhist Emperor

1. **"The Gandharan mission would eventually prove hugely significant. The interaction between Buddhism and Hellenistic Greek culture in Gandhara produced the Gandhara school of Buddhist art"** — The Maurya Empire narrative introduces Gandhara art as a consequence of Ashoka's mission, but now that the post-Maurya narrative exists with a full chapter (Ch 5) on the Gandhara and Mathura schools, the Maurya mention should add a forward cross-reference: "(covered in depth in Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 5)". The Maurya text currently treats it as a brief aside when it's actually one of the most important artistic developments in world history. — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 5.

2. **"Other missions went to Gandhara and Kashmir in the northwest, to the Himalayan regions, to the Yona (Greek) territories in Bactria... Whether Buddhist monks actually reached these Mediterranean courts is debated"** — The Maurya narrative raises the Yona (Greek) mission but doesn't follow up. The post-Maurya narrative (Ch 2) now covers Menander's conversion and the Milindapanha as the payoff of exactly this mission — a Greek king who engaged seriously with Buddhist thought. The Maurya narrative should note: "(the long-term result of Bactrian Buddhist contact is covered in Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 2)". — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 2.

### maurya-empire.md — Chapter 8 — The Fall

3. **"Around 200 BCE, the Greco-Bactrian king Demetrius I invaded the weakening northwestern Maurya territories, eventually establishing Indo-Greek kingdoms in the Punjab and the Indus valley"** — The Maurya narrative names Demetrius I and the Indo-Greek kingdoms in a single sentence. The post-Maurya narrative now has a full chapter (Ch 2) on the Indo-Greeks, the Milindapanha, and the bilingual coinage. The Maurya text should cross-reference: "(the full story of the Indo-Greek kingdoms is in Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 2)". — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 2.

4. **"The Shunga dynasty (SHOON-gah) that Pushyamitra founded controlled a much-reduced territory... The Shunga period is traditionally (and controversially) associated with a Brahmanical revival"** — The Maurya narrative covers Pushyamitra's coup and the Brahmanical revival in a single paragraph. The post-Maurya narrative (Ch 1) devotes an entire chapter to this, including the nuanced argument that the Shungas patronized Buddhist art (Sanchi toranas, Bharhut stupa) while shifting state ideology. The Maurya text should add: "(for the fuller story of Shunga-period Buddhism and art, see Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 1)". — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 1.

### scythians.md — Chapter 8 — Decline and Legacy

5. **"Cross-civ final check: the decline of the Scythians (~350–110 BCE) overlaps with the conquests of Alexander the Great (334–323 BCE), the Qin unification of China (221 BCE), the Maurya Empire in India under Ashoka (268–232 BCE)"** — The Scythians cross-civ check mentions the Maurya Empire but doesn't note that the Sakas (closely related to the Scythians) would eventually invade India and establish the Western Satraps (post-Maurya Ch 3). The domino chain — Scythians displaced by Sarmatians → Sakas displaced into India — is covered in post-Maurya Ch 3. Add: "(the Sakas, closely related to the Scythians, were displaced from Central Asia by the same Sarmatian migration pressure and entered India; Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 3)". — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 3.

6. **"The Sarmatians who displaced the Scythians would themselves be displaced by the Huns around 370 CE, who would be displaced by the Turks, who would be displaced by the Mongols."** — The Scythians narrative's final cross-civ check traces the steppe succession but doesn't connect the Yuezhi migration (the step before the Huns) to India. The Yuezhi who displaced the Sakas became the Kushans (post-Maurya Ch 4) — one of the most important empires of the ancient world. The Scythians text should add to this chain: "(the Yuezhi, pushed west by the Xiongnu, became the Kushans in India; Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 4)". — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 4.

### persian-empire.md — Chapter 6 — The Parthian Rise

7. **"The Silk Road (that network of trade routes connecting China, Central Asia, India, and the Mediterranean that would transform Eurasian commerce for the next fifteen hundred years)"** — The Persian Empire narrative covers the Parthian Silk Road connection but doesn't note that the Kushan Empire controlled the Indian end of this same system. Post-Maurya Ch 4 now covers the Kushan Silk Road in detail. The Persian Empire narrative should add at its Silk Road discussion: "(the Indian terminus of this system was controlled by the Kushan Empire; Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 4)". — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 4.

### persian-empire.md — Chapter 8 — Ardashir and the Sassanid Revolution

8. **"The Kushano-Sassanian kingdom — a hybrid Sassanid-Kushan administration in the conquered western territories"** — The Persian Empire narrative mentions the Kushano-Sassanians briefly. Post-Maurya Ch 8 now covers the Kushan decline from the Sassanid conquest angle in greater detail. Add a cross-reference: "(the Kushan side of this conquest is described in Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 8)". — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 8.

### ancient-rome.md — Chapter 7 (or wherever Roman-Indian trade appears)

9. **"Roman-Indian trade"** — The Roman narrative should note, wherever it covers Augustan/Julio-Claudian commercial expansion, that the Indian trade (described from the Indian side in Post-Maurya Kingdoms Ch 7) was funneled through the Kushan Empire's Silk Road network and directly through Tamil kingdoms. Pliny's complaint about 100 million sesterces flowing east is in the Roman primary source base; the post-Maurya narrative now provides the Indian counterpart (Pliny in Ch 7, Periplus, Arikamedu, Tamil kingdoms, Roman coin hoards). — Post-Maurya Kingdoms, Chapter 7.

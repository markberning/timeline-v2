## Story Editor evaluation (Persona D)

### Throughline

The ocean is a road, not a barrier. The Polynesians built a civilization across 10 million square miles of open water using nothing but memorized knowledge, carried their entire culture in canoes, and then nearly lost everything when the voyaging stopped and colonialism arrived — until a handful of navigators brought it back. The throughline is clear, consistent, and emotionally resonant across all 8 chapters.

### What works across the whole manuscript

- **The structural arc is excellent.** Ch 1-2 build the culture, Ch 3 is the technical showpiece, Ch 4-6 are the expansion, Ch 7 is the flowering, Ch 8 is loss and revival. This is a classic rise-peak-fall-redemption structure and it works.
- **The voice is strong and consistent.** Informal, authoritative, never condescending. "They went anyway" (used twice, Ch 1 and Ch 4) is the manuscript's signature phrase and it earns both appearances.
- **Cross-civ comparisons land well.** Phoenicians, Minoans, Egyptians, Chinese — each comparison illuminates rather than decorates.
- **The "they carried their civilization in their heads" motif** threads through every chapter and gives the narrative its emotional spine. The loss of navigational knowledge in Ch 8 hits hard precisely because Ch 3 spent so long building the reader's respect for the system.
- **Ch 8's Hokule'a ending is genuinely moving.** The Mau-to-Nainoa handoff is a perfect human-scale coda to a civilizational story.

### What doesn't work across the whole manuscript

- **Chapters 4-6 lose protagonist specificity.** Ch 1-3 have a clear "character" (the Lapita, then the culture itself, then the navigation system). Ch 4-6 become survey chapters — lots of islands, dates, and settlement sequences but fewer human stories. The Marquesas section has some character (fierce warriors, valley isolation) but the Tahiti and Cook Islands sections are thin on vivid detail.
- **The emotional stakes flatten in the middle.** Ch 1 ("they went anyway") and Ch 8 (the revival) have real emotional punch. Ch 4-6 are informative but not moving. The reader knows the Polynesians will reach these islands — the question is how, and the answer is often "they sailed there."
- **No named individuals until Ch 7-8.** The first specific named person in the entire narrative is 'Aho'eitu in Ch 5. The first person whose story the reader follows is arguably Mau Piailug in Ch 8. The manuscript is about a people, not persons, which is historically appropriate but narratively challenging.

### Momentum map (whole manuscript)

Ch 1 (strong) → Ch 2 (steady) → Ch 3 (strong) → Ch 4 (dip) → Ch 5 (recovery) → Ch 6 (strong) → Ch 7 (strong) → Ch 8 (excellent)

## Chapter 1 — The Lapita People

**Grade: STRONG**

**Opening:** Excellent. Drops the reader directly into the moment of departure. "They were heading into water that, as far as they knew, went on forever. They went anyway." This is one of the best openings in the whole project.

**Momentum:** Strong throughout. The chapter covers a lot of ground (Austronesian origins, pottery, canoes, obsidian trade, agriculture, Near/Remote Oceania, ecological impact) but the pacing works because each section has a clear point and a clear payoff.

**Stakes:** Immediately established. These people are sailing into the unknown with their whole world packed in canoes.

**"So what?" test:** Passed. The Lapita are presented as the foundation of everything that follows. The ecological impact section at the end adds moral complexity that prevents the chapter from being purely celebratory.

**Ending:** Good but slightly soft. "The next chapter tells the story of how..." is functional but not a hook. The chapter's real ending is the ecological impact paragraph, which is a strong beat — the explicit "next chapter" line after it slightly deflates.

**Redundancy:** The "firsts" caveat paragraph is well-placed and appropriate. No significant redundancy.

**Opening:** Effective. The Long Pause is an interesting framing device — "the great sailing culture hit pause" — and the honest admission that nobody knows why is trust-building.

**Momentum:** Steady but not gripping. The chapter is essentially an inventory of cultural features (mana, tapu, chiefs, oral tradition, kava, tapa, tattooing, Polynesian outliers) organized thematically rather than narratively. The "slave raids were worse than deforestation" point is a strong revisionist beat.

## Newcomer findings (Persona A)

### Chapter 1

1. **Empty claim — no mechanism for canoe improvement:** "Lapita canoe technology had advanced to the point where voyagers could make reliable crossings of 200 to 300 miles" — how did it advance? What changed about the canoes? The reader gets the result but not the process.

2. **"Always been there" hand-wave — Melanesian presence:** "Melanesian peoples (the dark-skinned populations of New Guinea, the Solomons, and nearby islands) had been living in the western Pacific for at least 40,000 years" — where did they come from? How did they get there? The reader is told they were there but not how they arrived.

3. **Undefined term:** "intervisible islands" — never defined. The reader can probably guess but it's a technical term used without explanation.

4. **Long parenthetical (17 words):** "(a small species, *Rattus exulans*, that stowed away on canoes)" — should be its own sentence.

5. **Process described by result:** "the Lapita transformed their new environments to fit their existing way of life" — the next sentences describe what they brought, but the actual process of clearing forests and establishing gardens is summarized, not shown.

### Chapter 2

6. **Empty claim — how did pottery decline?** "Around 500 BCE, pottery production in the Fiji-Tonga-Samoa region began to decline" — what does decline look like? Did fewer people make it? Were the kilns abandoned? This is a result, not a process.

7. **Long parenthetical (16 words):** "(the origin of the English word 'taboo'), the system of sacred prohibitions that governed daily life" — borderline but the double function (etymology + definition) makes it dense.

8. **Unclear mechanism — how genealogies were verified:** "subsequent archaeological dating has confirmed that many of the genealogical timelines match the physical evidence surprisingly well" — which genealogies? Which sites? A single example would make this concrete.

9. **Process by result — cultural portability:** "You did not need a specific island to be Polynesian. You needed the language, the genealogies, the mana system, the kava ceremony, and the knowledge of the sea" — this is a beautiful claim but it's asserted, not demonstrated. How did an outlier community maintain Polynesian identity while surrounded by Melanesians? One example would ground it.

### Chapter 3

10. **Undefined term:** "azimuth" — used in "the next star in the sequence would be rising at the same azimuth." Not defined; many readers won't know this word.

11. **Process well-explained (positive note):** The star compass, wave piloting, and bird navigation sections are the manuscript's best examples of explaining mechanisms, not just results. The reader genuinely understands how each technique works.

12. **Long parenthetical (19 words):** "(a triangular sail with the apex pointing down and the wide end up)" — should be its own sentence.

13. **Unclear pronoun antecedent:** "it matches the oral tradition" — what specifically matches? The walking experiment matches the tradition that moai walked? This is in Ch 3's canoe section context but the pronoun is slightly ambiguous. [Note: this is actually in Ch 7, misread during first pass — see Ch 7 finding #28.]

### Chapter 4

14. **Undefined proper noun on first use:** "Thor Heyerdahl" — gets a parenthetical about his raft but no dates or nationality until "20th-century Norwegian explorer" which is embedded in a clause rather than being the introduction. The name appears before the identification.

15. **Empty claim — "calculated risk-taking":** "It was calculated risk-taking by people who understood their ocean better than any other culture on Earth" — the chapter asserts this but the actual calculation (how they decided when to turn back, what provisions they needed, how they assessed risk) is thin. Ch 3 covers the navigation but the logistics of a failed voyage are mostly left to the reader's imagination.

16. **Process described by result — Tahitian religious development:** "The Tahitians developed a more elaborate religious hierarchy, with powerful priestly classes and large-scale marae temple complexes" — how did they develop it? What drove the elaboration? This is presented as a fact, not a story.

17. **Repeated uncertainty phrase:** "is debated" appears 4 times in this chapter alone ("the radiocarbon evidence is debated," "whether Hawaiki refers to... is debated"). Needs varied uncertainty language per WRITING-RULES.md.

### Chapter 5

18. **Undefined term:** "confederacies" — "(Bau, Rewa, Verata)" are named but never described beyond the word "confederacies." What is a Fijian confederacy? How is it organized?

19. **Long parenthetical (20 words):** "(hand-woven pandanus mats that served as a form of prestige currency across Polynesia)" — should be its own sentence.

20. **Empty claim — tribute system mechanics:** "'inasi... was the economic engine of the Tu'i Tonga empire" — we learn what was paid (mats, tapa, food) but not how it was collected, transported, or redistributed. The logistics of collecting tribute across hundreds of miles of ocean are actually fascinating and unexplained.

21. **Architectural motivation missing:** "The langi at Mu'a are the largest monumental architecture in Polynesia outside Easter Island" — why stepped? Why coral limestone? Why rectangular? The dimensions are given but the design choices are not motivated.

22. **Numbers need anchoring:** "300,000 to 800,000" for Hawaii's population — the enormous range is noted ("the range is enormous because the evidence is thin") which is good, but neither end is anchored to anything the reader can feel.

### Chapter 6

23. **Long parenthetical (18 words):** "(the Roaring Forties, a band of powerful westerly winds between 40 and 50 degrees south latitude that generate enormous seas)" — should be its own sentence.

24. **Undefined term:** "carrying capacity" — used in the Easter Island section without definition. A reader unfamiliar with ecology may not know this term.

30. **Long parenthetical (22 words):** "(marriages between half-siblings, specifically children of the same high-ranking father by different mothers)" — should be its own sentence.

31. **Empty claim — Arioi infanticide:** "children born to Arioi were, according to some accounts, killed at birth, though how universally this rule was enforced is debated" — this is a startling claim that gets a hedge but no explanation. Why were they childless? What was the theological reasoning?

### Chapter 8

32. **"Is debated" repetition (manuscript-wide):** "is debated" appears at least 10 times across the manuscript. This is the single most repeated uncertainty phrase and needs rotation per WRITING-RULES.md. Alternatives: "remains contested," "scholars disagree," "the evidence is divided," "the question is unresolved."

33. **Process well-explained (positive):** The knowledge-loss mechanism is the manuscript's most powerful passage: "The master navigators who knew the routes to distant islands grew old and died. Their apprentices, if they had any, knew only the local waters." This is mechanism, not result.

34. **Undefined scholar on first use:** "Andrew Sharp, a New Zealand historian" — gets a description but no dates. When did he write? "1956 book" gives the date of publication but not Sharp's life dates.

35. **Empty claim — "cultural renaissance":** "it ignited a cultural renaissance across Polynesia" — the Hawaiian specifics follow (hula, language revival) but the claim of "across Polynesia" is asserted without evidence from Tonga, Samoa, Tahiti, or New Zealand (Te Aurere is mentioned later but only as one canoe).

## Skeptic findings (Persona B)

### Chapter 1

1. **[CHECK] Suspiciously specific number:** "over 13,000 miles (21,000 km)" for the spread of Austronesian languages from Madagascar to Easter Island. The great-circle distance from Madagascar to Easter Island is approximately correct, but the phrasing "widest geographical spread of any pre-modern language family" is a strong claim. Indo-European spread across Eurasia is given as "roughly 5,000 miles (8,000 km)" from Ireland to India, but Indo-European also reached western China (Tocharian) and Sri Lanka, which would stretch the range. The comparison is directionally correct but the specific numbers should be verified.

2. **[STRONG] Well-handled uncertainty:** "The older model, sometimes called the 'express train' hypothesis" and "The newer 'slow boat' model" — these are real scholarly models with real names. The DNA evidence summary is appropriately hedged.

3. **[SOFT] Generic atmospheric prose:** "They were heading into water that, as far as they knew, went on forever" — this is vivid writing but unfalsifiable. We don't know what the Lapita knew or believed about the ocean's extent. Minor — the prose is honest about this being inference.

### Chapter 2

4. **[CHECK] Suspiciously specific claim — genealogical accuracy:** "subsequent archaeological dating has confirmed that many of the genealogical timelines match the physical evidence surprisingly well" — this is a real scholarly finding (Patrick Kirch and others have demonstrated correlations between genealogical generation counts and radiocarbon dates), but "surprisingly well" is vague. How well? Within a century? Within 50 years? The claim is broadly true but the precision of the match varies enormously by island group.

5. **[STRONG] Honest hedging:** "Getting a name wrong was not a mistake. It was a desecration." This could sound like invention but the cultural significance of genealogical accuracy in Polynesian society is extremely well-documented in ethnographic literature.

6. **[SOFT] Too-neat packaging:** "the cultural toolkit that Polynesians packed into their canoes alongside the taro and the breadfruit seedlings" — the metaphor of culture as a "toolkit" is modern framing. The Polynesians didn't conceptualize their practices as a portable package. Minor — this is a legitimate narrative device.

### Chapter 3

7. **[CHECK] Suspiciously specific number — "over 200 stars":** "A trained navigator memorized the rising and setting points of over 200 stars along the horizon" — the number varies by source. Some accounts say 150-220. "Over 200" is within the range of published estimates but pushes toward the high end. The claim is not fabricated but the precision is slightly oversold.

8. **[CHECK] Specific scholar comparison:** "Modern scholars who have studied Polynesian star navigation have compared its complexity to learning an entire university curriculum by heart" — which scholars? This is a common popular-press comparison but attributing it to "modern scholars" without a name is vague. The claim is directionally true but the comparison may originate from popular science writing rather than formal scholarship.

9. **[STRONG] Legitimate technical detail:** "Some Pacific Island navigators reportedly lay down in the hull of the canoe and felt the swells through their bodies" — this is well-attested in the ethnographic literature (especially David Lewis's *We, the Navigators*).

10. **[CHECK] Specific speed claim:** "The Fijian drua... could reach speeds of 15 knots in favorable conditions, faster than most European sailing ships of the same era" — 15 knots for a drua is cited in some sources but is an upper-bound estimate, not a routine speed. "Most European sailing ships of the same era" is a broad comparison — a drua at 15 knots would be faster than an average merchantman but not faster than a purpose-built warship. The claim is defensible but slightly oversold.

11. **[STRONG] Well-sourced cultural practice:** The Marshall Islands stick charts are well-documented museum artifacts. The caveat that "whether the Polynesians proper used stick charts is debated" is honest and appropriate.

### Chapter 4

12. **[STRONG] Kon-Tiki refutation is accurate:** The dismissal of the accidental-drift theory with specific evidence (carried transplant crops, breeding animals, sailed against prevailing currents) is factually solid.

13. **[CHECK] Specific speed comparison:** "it took the Phoenicians roughly the same amount of time to colonize the Mediterranean, a body of water one-tenth the size" — the Mediterranean is roughly 970,000 square miles; the area the Polynesians covered in the 300-800 CE expansion is much larger but "one-tenth the size" as a comparison of the two bodies of water depends on what area you're measuring. The Pacific is 63 million square miles total but the Polynesians didn't settle all of it. The comparison is evocative but the "one-tenth" ratio is approximate at best.

14. **[SOFT] Atmospheric certainty:** "The navigator stood or sat at the stern, reading the stars, the swells, the wind, and the birds" — we don't know the navigator's exact position on the canoe for these ancient voyages. Later ethnographic evidence suggests the stern but projecting this backward 1,700 years is an inference.

### Chapter 5

15. **[CHECK] Specific weight claims — Ha'amonga stones:** "each upright weighs roughly 30 to 40 tons, and the lintel weighs approximately 9 tons" — these are the commonly cited figures but they are estimates based on volume and assumed density. The stones have not been precisely weighed. "Roughly" and "approximately" are appropriate hedges but the numbers themselves may be ±20%.

16. **[STRONG] Tu'i Tonga oral traditions are well-documented:** The 'Aho'eitu foundation narrative is one of the most thoroughly recorded Polynesian origin stories, collected by multiple early European observers.

17. **[CHECK] Population estimates:** "Some estimates put pre-contact Tahiti's population at 100,000 or more, Hawaii at 300,000 to 800,000" — the Hawaiian range is real (David Stannard estimated 800,000-1,000,000; others have argued for lower figures). Tahiti at 100,000 is on the higher end of estimates. The ranges are genuine scholarly ranges but presenting them without noting who estimated what leaves them feeling authoritative rather than debated.

18. **[SOFT] Unattributed practice:** "launching one was a ritual event involving feasting, prayer, and sometimes human sacrifice" — human sacrifice at drua launchings is attested in some early European accounts but the frequency and context are debated. "Sometimes" is an appropriate hedge.

### Chapter 6

19. **[CHECK] Fishpond count:** "Over 400 fishponds were constructed across the Hawaiian Islands" — this is the commonly cited number and is based on archaeological survey, but some estimates go higher (488 is sometimes cited). "Over 400" is defensible.

20. **[CHECK] Moa size claims:** "the largest of which stood over 10 feet (3 meters) tall and weighed up to 500 pounds (230 kg)" — the largest moa species (*Dinornis robustus*) is estimated at up to 3.6 meters tall and around 230 kg. "Over 10 feet" (3 meters) and "500 pounds" (230 kg) are slightly conservative rather than exaggerated, which is fine.

21. **[CHECK] Sweet potato radiocarbon dating:** "Radiocarbon dating of sweet potato remains in the Cook Islands has produced dates around 1000 CE" — this refers to the Mangaia cave deposits studied by Hather and Kirch. The dates have been questioned by some scholars who argue for contamination. The narrative appropriately notes the evidence "is still debated."

22. **[STRONG] Sweet potato hypothesis is well-presented:** Both hypotheses (human transport, natural drift) are given fair treatment. The narrative avoids picking a winner while noting that human transport is the more widely held position.

### Chapter 7

23. **[CHECK] Moai statistics:** "nearly 900 moai" — the commonly cited number is 887, so "nearly 900" is accurate. "The largest completed moai, called Paro, stands 33 feet (10 meters) tall and weighs about 82 tons" — Paro's height is variously reported as 10-12 meters depending on whether the pukao (topknot) is included. 82 tons is within the range of estimates. "An unfinished moai... would have stood roughly 69 feet (21 meters) tall" — this refers to "El Gigante" and the 21-meter estimate is standard.

24. **[CHECK] Specific claim — Ahu Tongariki built in 15th century:** "It was built in the 15th century" — Ahu Tongariki's construction is dated to roughly the 15th century based on archaeological context but the precise dating is uncertain. The claim is defensible but not precise.

25. **[SOFT] Generic AI-sounding prose:** "one of the most enigmatic artifacts in human history" — this phrase about rongorongo is the kind of superlative that AI writing tends to overuse. It's not inaccurate but it's a cliche of popular archaeology writing.

26. **[CHECK] Kumulipo line count:** "an epic creation chant of over 2,000 lines" — the Kumulipo is traditionally said to contain 2,102 lines (in the version transcribed by Kalakaua). "Over 2,000" is accurate.

27. **[CHECK] Pi'ilani and the Alaloa:** "Pi'ilani of Maui, who unified the island around 1450 CE and built the Alaloa" — Pi'ilani's dates are uncertain (traditional genealogies place him in the 15th century) and the Alaloa is a real archaeological feature, but whether Pi'ilani specifically commissioned its construction or whether it was built over multiple generations is debated. The attribution is the traditional one.

### Chapter 8

28. **[CHECK] Easter Island population peak:** "which may have peaked at 10,000 to 15,000" — this is within the range of scholarly estimates. Some estimates go lower (7,000-9,000) and some higher (up to 20,000). The range given is reasonable but not the only range proposed.

29. **[STRONG] Jared Diamond revision is well-handled:** The narrative presents Diamond's *Collapse* thesis fairly and then introduces the archaeological revision (rock-garden agriculture, more gradual decline) accurately. This is good scholarship.

30. **[STRONG] Peruvian slave raids:** "the Peruvian slave raids of the 1860s, which removed roughly a third of the island's population" — this is well-documented. The 1862-63 raids removed approximately 1,500 people from a population of roughly 3,000-4,000. "Roughly a third" is accurate.

## Story Editor evaluation (Persona D)

## Chapter 1 — Before Angkor: Funan, Chenla, and Indianization

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A great civilization grew up inland on the lower Mekong because a reversing lake handed it free protein and free fertile soil, and onto that material foundation a non-conquering wave of Indian ideas was selectively grafted.

**What works:**
- The reversing-lake opening is one of the best scene-setters in the document. "the main river backs up so hard that it shoves water the wrong way up the tributary, and the Tonlé Sap River **reverses and flows backward**" is genuinely vivid, and the chapter earns its thesis sentence: "the whole civilization is, in the end, an argument about water." That single image is load-bearing for ten chapters and the chapter knows it.
- The Indianization mechanism is handled with real rhetorical control: it poses the reader's own objection ("the reader will reasonably suspect this is a story of conquest: it wasn't") and then answers it as a "three-way pull." The Kaundinya/Soma legend lands the abstraction in one concrete image — "The foreign sacred man weds the indigenous water-goddess" — and the payoff ("the multi-headed naga serpent will be carved onto every balustrade at Angkor seven centuries later") plants a seed that the rest of the book harvests.
- The ending does real work: "It is waiting for someone to walk up a mountain and declare it whole. A man named Jayavarman is about to." Momentum into Chapter 2 is unmistakable.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Funan-to-Chenla stretch (the K.557 inscription, Isanapura, Land Chenla / Water Chenla) sags slightly relative to the lake and Indianization sections — it briefly becomes the thing the document elsewhere avoids, a sequence of named sites. It is short enough not to be fatal, but the Sanskrit-vs-Old-Khmer "poetry/accounting" observation is the only spark in that stretch; consider compressing the site list and foregrounding that contrast earlier.

**Momentum map:** Huge energy in the opening lake passage and the Indianization argument; a shallow dip through the Funan/Chenla site catalogue; a strong recovery into the cliffhanger close.

## Chapter 2 — Jayavarman II and the God-King

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Khmer Empire's founding moment is a 250-year-later charter myth we can't fully trust, but the structural fact it encodes — kingship physically fused with the divine, proved in built stone — is real and sets the formula for six centuries.

**What works:**
- The opening frames the entire epistemological game in two sentences: "almost everything about it is reported to us by a single inscription carved 250 years after it supposedly happened. Hold both of those facts in your head at once, because the gap between them is where the historians live." This is the document's contested-claims ethic delivered as narrative tension rather than as a hedge.
- The "Water, ancestors, mountain" formula at the chapter's end is the single best structural device in the whole book. "A king proved he was a king by giving his people a reservoir, his dynasty a shrine, and his god a mountain... Hold onto that triad." It hands the reader a key that unlocks every subsequent chapter.
- The Harihara detail — "a single deity who is Shiva and Vishnu fused into one body, left side and right side — a very Khmer solution to the question of which great Hindu god to back: both, in one statue" — is the kind of compressed, characterful insight that makes popular history sing.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the devaraja disambiguation paragraph ("Other scholars argue the term refers more modestly to a state deity...") is the one place the chapter's argumentative momentum slows into seminar mode. It's defensible and well-handled ("The distinction sounds academic; it is actually the whole question of what kind of thing a Khmer king was"), but it is the chapter's lone soft patch.

**Momentum map:** Strong tension in the mountaintop framing, a brief scholarly plateau in the devaraja debate, then a sharp upward close on the three-build formula.

## Chapter 3 — The Hydraulic City: Yasodharapura and the Barays

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Angkor wasn't founded once — it was a moving palimpsest of royal cities — and at its heart sit the giant reservoirs whose purpose (irrigation? cosmology? both?) is the central technical argument that will decide how the empire's end is explained.

**What works:**
- The Mount Meru explanation is the clearest single passage of architectural exposition in the document: "The temple *is* Mount Meru, modeled at buildable scale. Its tower or towers are the peak where the gods live; its enclosure walls are the mountain ranges that ring the world; its moat is the cosmic ocean." This pays dividends in Chapters 5 and 7 and is worth every line.
- The Banteay Srei digression is a deliberate, well-judged change of pace — "breaks every rule in this book and is better for it... The biggest temple in the world is in this story, and so is one of the smallest, and the small one is arguably the more perfect." It's emotional relief from scale, and the Malraux planting ("he tried to *steal* several tons of its carvings") is a clean Chapter 10 hook.
- The chapter explicitly defers its central question and then delivers it ("Park that question for a moment; it gets its own argument at the end of this chapter" → "Now the argument this chapter has been deferring: what were the barays *for*?"). The structure is visible and confident.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Koh Ker / succession-soap paragraph is necessary but slightly over-explained; the "resting heartbeat" framing is good ("treat it as the empire's resting heartbeat rather than as crisis") but the surrounding sentences restate the no-succession-rule point more times than it needs (it returns in Ch 4 and Ch 5). Tighten here to avoid early redundancy with later chapters.

**Momentum map:** Builds steadily through the Meru exposition, peaks emotionally at Banteay Srei, then closes on a deliberately unresolved scholarly cliffhanger ("Keep the baray debate in your pocket. We are going to need it.").

## Chapter 4 — The Eleventh Century: the Khleang Kings, the Baphuon, and Suryavarman I

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** An under-remembered king tried to fix the empire's fatal flaw — no bureaucracy — by manufacturing one out of a mass loyalty oath chiseled into a wall, while the era's monuments and revolts show the building machine straining at every turn.

**What works:**
- The oath of 4,000 is the chapter's beating heart and it's superbly handled: "It is a king without a bureaucratic machine trying to *manufacture* one out of ritual, stone, and the fear of the gods — government by mass loyalty oath, literally set in the palace wall so no one could claim not to have sworn it." The Song China contrast ("China on a meritocratic paper bureaucracy, Angkor on sacred personal oaths chiseled into a doorway") is exactly the kind of cross-civilizational comparison that makes the point land.
- "The Khmer state was a machine for converting an agricultural surplus into stone and conquest, and the more aggressively a king ran the machine, the more it shook" — a genuine thesis sentence that the chapter sets up and the book pays off in Chapter 7.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter is structurally a three-part list (Suryavarman I → Udayadityavarman II / Baphuon / revolts → Preah Vihear) rather than a single arc. The Udayadityavarman II paragraph reads as catalogue ("his state temple, the Baphuon... Udayadityavarman II also built the West Baray...") and the Preah Vihear coda, though vivid ("still, today, a place people are willing to shoot each other over"), is bolted on as a separate item rather than integrated. The structural fix: lead and close on the oath/bureaucracy-fragility argument and subordinate the monuments to it, so the chapter has one spine instead of three beads on a string.
- Minor redundancy: the "no succession rule → civil war" point opens this chapter ("which by now should not surprise you") having already been made twice in Chapter 3. It's becoming a verbal tic.

**Momentum map:** Flat-strong on the opening civil war, peaks hard at the oath of 4,000, then loses shape across the monument list before a vivid-but-detached Preah Vihear button.

## Chapter 5 — Suryavarman II and Angkor Wat

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The empire's signature monument is a usurper-king's walk-through model of the universe and his own tomb — the absolute peak of the build-bigger formula, finished just as the dynasty collapsed into the succession crisis that nearly ended everything.

**What works:**
- The murder-king opening is the best character introduction in the document: "notice what propaganda chooses to brag about: not a peaceful inheritance, but a spectacular act of dynastic violence reframed as a god's eagle destroying a snake. Khmer kingship did not pretend succession was orderly. It made the seizure itself sacred." That reframing of the recurring succession theme into something thematically active (rather than the tic it had become in Ch 3–4) rescues the motif.
- The Angkor Wat "walked through as a passage from the everyday into the center of the universe" passage makes the building experiential rather than measured, and the Suryavarman II bas-relief portrait close is one of the document's genuine emotional peaks: "a near-photograph from the twelfth century of the man who built the largest temple on Earth, looking out at his troops, on the wall of his own tomb."
- The final lines weaponize the book's recurring succession motif into a real cliffhanger: "the throne fell into exactly the kind of contested, weak-handed succession this book has taught you to expect. This time the empire would pay for it almost everything."

**What doesn't work:**
- The "records that get broken" paragraph (largest-religious-monument, with the Chartres/Hagia Sophia/Song-cities comparisons stacked together) momentarily becomes a superlatives ledger and slows the build toward the cosmos-and-tomb revelation. The comparisons are individually good but arrive in a clump; threading one or two into the walk-through passage rather than front-loading them all would keep the momentum aimed at the emotional payoff.

**Momentum map:** Strong, propulsive throughout; a brief measuring-tape lull in the "firsts" paragraph; a powerful emotional and forward-driving close.

## Chapter 6 — The Cham Catastrophe: the 1177 Sack and the Interregnum

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** For four years the civilization that built the largest religious monument on Earth was a defeated, occupied territory of a maritime rival — the single "almost" on which everything great that follows depends.

**What works:**
- This is the most propulsive chapter in the book and it knows exactly what it is: "For five chapters this book has shown you the Khmer building. This is the chapter where the Khmer almost stop existing." It is short, focused, single-arc, and never lists anything.
- The stakes are stated at full volume and earned: "There is no Angkor Wat on a national flag, no Bayon, no hospitals, no Zhou Daguan travelogue, in the timeline where 1177 is permanent... This is the hinge." The counterfactual makes the reader feel the contingency of everything they've read and everything coming.
- The close is the strongest hand-off in the document: the naval-victory reversal, the man "who had been, as far as the record shows, nowhere," and the Bayon-reliefs-as-war-diary payoff ("Crocodiles in the lake eating the men who fell in... We turn to him now.").

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the Austroasiatic/Austronesian language-family paragraph, while thematically justified (it's the "hinge of the catastrophe" because it explains the maritime capability), restates the Chapter 1 comparison at near-full length ("as far apart as an Englishman and a Malay sailor"). It could be trusted to the reader's memory and cut to its functional core — the maritime-vs-riverine contrast — to keep this lean chapter even leaner.

**Momentum map:** Pure forward drive from first line to last; the only slack is the linguistic recap in the middle.

## Chapter 7 — Jayavarman VII: the Bodhisattva King and the Bayon

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** An old man crowned over a burned capital reinvented Khmer kingship from god-king to compassion-king — and that single religious idea functioned as a building permit for the most ambitious public-works program in the civilization's history, possibly the one that broke it.

**What works:**
- The "building permit" insight is the conceptual engine of the chapter and one of the sharpest ideas in the document: "A god-king builds a temple to house his divine essence. A bodhisattva-king builds *hospitals, roads, rest-houses, reservoirs, and a city for his people*, and calls all of it an act of compassion." It converts a theological shift into narrative causation.
- The Bayon faces are handled with exemplary honesty-as-drama: "The smile you are looking at is, on purpose or by accident, an unsolved problem, and the people who study it for a living will tell you so." The three readings are presented as live tension, not a hedge.
- The exhaustion-thesis section models the book's contested-claims discipline at its best: "you should be suspicious of how powerful it is, because vividness, this book keeps warning, is often the tell of a contested claim dressed as a fact." It refuses the satisfying moral and is more satisfying for it.

**What doesn't work:**
- The monument paragraph ("here is the list, because the scale is the point") is the chapter's one genuinely list-shaped stretch — Angkor Thom, Bayon, Ta Prohm, Preah Khan, Neak Pean, the hospitals, the roads, the Champa annexation — and it runs long. The chapter justifies it pre-emptively, but the sheer pile-up of numbered superlatives (12,640, ~1,000 teachers, 102 hospitals, 121 rest-houses) briefly turns prose into inventory. Fix: subordinate the list to the "building permit" thesis by tagging each work to the compassion-ideology rather than presenting them as a catalogue of records.

**Momentum map:** Strong thesis-driven opening, an enumerative plateau through the works list, then a powerful argumentative recovery on the exhaustion debate and a sharp close ("the very next thing the empire did was start arguing with itself about his religion").

## Chapter 8 — The Empire at Its Height: Society, Trade, and Zhou Daguan

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** At its glittering thirteenth-century zenith — captured by the one foreign eyewitness who ever recorded it — the empire was already being hollowed out from below by a quiet, popular religious shift that removed the entire reason to be a god-king state.

**What works:**
- The "worm in the apple" structural device is excellent and sustained: the chapter announces the Theravada turn early ("Hold that thought through the description of the glittering city that follows, because it is the worm in the apple"), describes the glitter, then deliberately "pull[s] the worm out" at the end. It gives an otherwise descriptive chapter a dramatic spine.
- Zhou Daguan is sold on exactly the right terms: "When a civilization leaves almost nothing perishable behind, one good travelogue is worth a library." The "surprisingly woman-run commercial city" detail is the kind of vivid, specific fact that makes the dead city briefly alive.
- The closing argument is the document's intellectual climax and it's beautifully phrased: "Nothing was conquered. No temple was stormed... The entire religious justification for the Angkorian way of being a state... just evaporated from below, one village monastery at a time. That, more than any army, is what ended Angkor."

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the Zhou Daguan "what did he see?" paragraph is a rapid-fire catalogue (processions, tiers of society, houses, trade goods, justice, festivals, heat, fish, rice) that, for one paragraph, becomes the encyclopedia-dump the chapter otherwise transcends. It's redeemed by the woman-traders detail and the Tonlé Sap callback, but selecting two or three textures and rendering them as scene would beat the inventory.

**Momentum map:** Strong framing and religious-whiplash opening, a brief descriptive flattening in the Zhou Daguan inventory, then a rising, thesis-delivering close that is the high point of the book's argument.

## Chapter 9 — The Long Retreat and the 1431 Abandonment

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** "The fall of Angkor" in 1431 is doubly wrong — it was the loud end of a two-century wind-down that had already changed the civilization into something else, and Angkor was never actually abandoned at all.

**What works:**
- The opening is a model of how to start a chapter on a negative ("The most important sentence in this chapter is a sentence about something that *stopped*"). The "silence in the stone after that date" is a genuinely affecting image and the right way to dramatize an absence.
- The Ayutthaya-as-self-raised-undertaker argument is the cleanest payoff of a seed planted five chapters earlier (Suryavarman I's western expansion in Ch 4): "Empires sometimes raise their own undertakers; the Khmer raised this one over four hundred years." The book's long-range setups are paying off.
- The two-part "the textbook framing is wrong in two ways" structure is clear and the second correction ("Angkor was never abandoned") is delivered with restraint and sets up Chapter 10 perfectly, with the Japanese graffiti and Ang Chan's inscriptions as concrete proof rather than assertion.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor redundancy: this chapter re-states the "no king ever built again after Jayavarman VII" point that already closed Chapter 7 and the Theravada-killed-the-temple-economy point that closed Chapter 8. The recaps are deliberate scaffolding, but two consecutive chapter-openings ("the very next thing the empire did was start arguing" / "The most important sentence in this chapter is a sentence about something that stopped") lean on the same prior beat. Trust the reader slightly more and compress the recap.

**Momentum map:** Strong throughout; the energy is in the two myth-corrections, building cleanly toward the Chapter 10 hand-off ("not *when* Angkor fell, but *whether 'fell' was ever the right word at all*").

## Chapter 10 — The Collapse Debate and Afterlife: the Jungle, Mouhot, LiDAR, and Angkor as Symbol

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Angkor didn't fall — it underwent a slow convergence of pressures, was never lost, and its afterlife (looting, the Khmer Rouge, the lasers, the flag) makes it a living argument that Cambodians have chosen to put at the center of who they are.

**What works:**
- The chapter declares its two jobs and executes both: the collapse-debate "meet the defendants" section is genuinely good intellectual history ("The climate evidence did not replace Groslier so much as supply the trigger his mechanism was missing"), and the Maya cross-reference earns its place ("'collapse' is almost always too simple a word").
- The Mouhot "rediscovery" demolition is the satisfying payoff of a thesis seeded since Chapter 1: "He discovered nothing... The 'rediscovery' myth says more about who gets to count as having 'known' a thing than it does about Angkor." This is the book having a point of view, which is what makes it more than a chronicle.
- The ending is the strongest in the document and ties the whole book's ring structure shut: "We started this book by admitting how little survives... The temple on the flag is not a monument to a dead civilization. It is an unfinished argument that the people who live there have decided to put at the center of who they are." It delivers on the framing essay's promise and the Khmer-Rouge-to-flag passage ("the only national flag on Earth that depicts a building... People still die over it") gives the abstract argument real emotional weight.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter is doing a great deal — collapse debate, da Madalena, Mouhot, EFEO/anastylosis, Malraux, Khmer Rouge, the flag, Preah Vihear, UNESCO/APSARA, LiDAR — and the middle stretch (EFEO through UNESCO) briefly accelerates into a connected-but-dense sequence of institutions and acronyms. It never fully collapses into a list because each item carries an argument, but it is the one place a reader could tire before the superb LiDAR finale. Fix: tighten the institutional middle (the EFEO/anastylosis and UNESCO/APSARA passages can lose a sentence each) to protect the runway for the ending.

**Momentum map:** Strong on the debate, a dense but argument-bearing institutional middle, then a powerful accelerating close through LiDAR to the book's best final paragraph.

---

## Overall assessment

This is a genuinely strong piece of popular history that works as *reading*, not just as a reference. It has a real argument — that "collapse" is the wrong word and that Angkor is a living, contested thing — and that argument is seeded in Chapter 1, threaded through every chapter, and paid off in Chapter 10's ring-closing finale; the document has a point of view and the courage to refuse satisfying monocausal stories, which is rare and valuable. The voice is consistent and confident throughout (the "Hold onto that triad," "Keep the baray debate in your pocket," "pull the worm out" devices give the whole book a guiding-hand intimacy), and the chapters genuinely build: the water thesis, the build-bigger formula, the succession flaw, and the Theravada worm are all long-range setups that detonate on schedule. The single recurring weakness is the monument/superlative inventory paragraph — it appears in Chapters 4, 5, 7, and 8, always pre-justified ("the scale is the point") but always the spot where momentum sags into catalogue. The single biggest improvement would be to systematically subordinate those lists to the chapter's thesis (tag each monument to the argument it serves rather than enumerating records), which would convert four soft patches into four more arguments and make an already-strong book very nearly seamless.

## Part 1: Forward Findings (new TL gaps)

### Chapter 1 — Before Angkor: Funan, Chenla, and Indianization

1. **"the elites of Southeast Asia adopted an enormous bundle of Indian culture"** — The single biggest cross-civ gap in the whole narrative: Indianization is introduced with no reference to the *source*. gupta-empire.md Ch 8 ("Greater India") is explicitly built around exporting "the cultural template the Guptas exported east — across the Bay of Bengal to Funan, Champa, and Java" and even names "the great **Khmer Empire** that built Angkor Wat." The mechanism khmer Ch 1 describes (trade + prestige + utility, no army) is the *identical* mechanism gupta Ch 8 lays out. This needs a direct cross-ref to gupta-empire Ch 8, and ideally medieval-india (which carries the Bay-of-Bengal traffic forward). — gupta-empire (c. 320–550 CE source period); medieval-india.

2. **"Indian temple architecture"** and the later Mount Meru / shikhara material (Ch 3) — The Khmer temple-mountain is a direct descendant of the Gupta shikhara-and-garbha-griha template. gupta-empire.md Ch 6/Ch 8 states the temple "was conceived as a **mountain** — specifically as Mount **Meru**" and that "you can read every Hindu temple in India and across Indianized Southeast Asia for the next 1,500 years as a variation on the Gupta template … to the Angkor Wat complex." khmer Ch 3 explains Mount Meru as if it were a purely Khmer/cosmological idea with no architectural genealogy. Cross-ref gupta-empire Ch 6. — gupta-empire (Gupta age, the shikhara invented ~460–500 CE).

3. **"Indianization … comparable in scale to the spread of Greek culture … or the spread of Chinese writing and Confucian statecraft into Korea (Ancient Korea Chapter 4)"** — The Korea analogy is good and the cite is correct, but the *closest structural twin* in the reference set is missing: ancient-korea.md's recurring thesis is "borrow the technology, reject the sovereignty" (Ch 3, "the tools and refused the identity"). That phrasing maps almost exactly onto khmer Ch 1's "looked Indian on the surface and stayed thoroughly Khmer underneath." Worth tightening the parallel rather than just name-dropping. — ancient-korea (Han commanderies / Three Kingdoms, ~108 BCE–668 CE).

4. **"a Brahmin from across the sea named Kaundinya marries a local naga princess … named Soma, and their line rules"** — The Funan founding legend is told one-sidedly. srivijaya.md Ch 1 covers Funan and Oc Eo from the maritime side; the Kaundinya-Soma legend is the *charter myth of the same kingdom* whose port (Oc Eo) srivijaya describes archaeologically. A cross-ref to srivijaya Ch 1 here would let the reader see Funan as both the mainland prologue (khmer) and the maritime prologue (srivijaya). The narrative already does this gesture for the trade-route point ("Srivijaya Chapter 1") but not for the foundational legend/Indianization mechanism. — srivijaya Ch 1.

5. **"the southern, coastal half seems to have fallen under the influence of a sea power the inscriptions call simply 'Java' — most likely the Sailendra dynasty associated with the Srivijayan world (Srivijaya Chapter 3)"** — Correct and well-cited. But note for consistency: srivijaya.md Ch 3 itself flags the Sailendra-Srivijaya relationship as one of "the great puzzles of early Southeast Asian history" (branches? marriage? vassalage?). khmer states the link more confidently ("associated with the Srivijayan world") than srivijaya does. Minor — soften to match, or note the uncertainty is shared. — srivijaya Ch 3.

### Chapter 2 — Jayavarman II and the God-King

6. **"Egypt's pharaohs were living gods (Old Kingdom Egypt Chapter 2), the Maya lords were k'uhul ajaw … (Maya Civilization Chapter 4), and Mesopotamian kings occasionally claimed divinity (Mesopotamia Chapter 5)"** — The comparison set is excellent and the cites are accurate (old-kingdom-egypt Ch 1–3 = Horus incarnate / ma'at; maya Ch 4 = k'uhul ajaw; mesopotamia Ch 5 = Naram-Sin's divine determinative). One refinement: the *distinctive Khmer feature* the narrative claims ("the justification held for six hundred years") has a sharp foil in old-kingdom-egypt — the pharaonic living-god system held for *five centuries* and then collapsed (OKE Ch 7), and mesopotamia Ch 5–6 shows Naram-Sin/Shulgi divinity as a brief, shocking rupture that did *not* hold. The contrast ("the Khmer version lasted; the Akkadian one was a one-off; the Egyptian one also ran ~5 centuries before the same kind of multi-causal collapse") would sharpen the point rather than just listing parallels. — old-kingdom-egypt Ch 1–3 & Ch 7; mesopotamia Ch 5–6.

7. **"a stepped pyramid representing the cosmic mountain, housing a god (most often a linga …)"** — The temple-mountain-as-cosmic-mountain idea has an unflagged Mesopotamian twin: the ziggurat. mesopotamia.md Ch 2 explains the ziggurat as a hand-built artificial mountain because "their gods lived 'up,' but there was no 'up' to go to" — the *bond between heaven and earth*. This is the same theology of a built mountain bridging heaven and earth that khmer Ch 3 develops for Mount Meru. A cross-ref (mesopotamia Ch 2) would be one of the most illuminating in the book. — mesopotamia (Eridu/Uruk, ~5000–3200 BCE; ziggurats through ~575 BCE).

### Chapter 3 — The Hydraulic City: Yasodharapura and the Barays

8. **"a vast engineered landscape in which the giant reservoirs fed a network of canals that irrigated enough rice to support an enormous population"** — The hydraulic-civilization thesis is presented with no cross-civ anchor. mesopotamia.md Ch 1–3 is the canonical "civilization that lived or died by water management" case ("Farming Mesopotamia required cooperation on a scale that simple villages couldn't manage … canals dug by hundreds of laborers, maintained for generations"), and mesopotamia Ch 13 even ends on canal-network collapse (Mongol cutting of the canals, 1258). The Groslier "hydraulic city" debate would land far harder with a mesopotamia Ch 1–3 cross-ref. — mesopotamia (irrigation from ~3500 BCE).

9. **"around 928 a usurping king, Jayavarman IV, abandoned Angkor entirely and built a brand-new capital 75 miles … at Koh Ker, crowned by Prasat Thom, a steep seven-tiered sandstone pyramid that looks more like a Mesoamerican structure"** — "Looks more like a Mesoamerican structure" is a dangling visual claim begging for a real cross-ref. maya-civilization.md (La Danta at El Mirador, Tikal's temples, Ch 1–4) and the broader Mesoamerican pyramid tradition are right there in the reference set. Either cite maya-civilization or drop the Mesoamerican gesture; as written it invokes a comparison the reader can't follow. — maya-civilization (Preclassic–Classic, ~600 BCE–800 CE).

### Chapter 4 — The Eleventh Century: the Khleang Kings, the Baphuon, and Suryavarman I

10. **"Compare it to the way the contemporary Song dynasty in China was running a vast professional civil service recruited by written examination (Tang & Song China Chapter 6)"** — Strong comparison, accurate cite (tang-song Ch 6 = Song expansion of the jinshi exam). One enhancement: tang-song Ch 6/Ch 8 stresses the exam system was *exported* across East Asia (Korea, Vietnam, Japan) and tang-song's own cross-civ checks repeatedly pin Angkor as the Song's near-contemporary ("the Khmer Empire at Angkor was building Angkor Wat … contemporary with … the Northern Song's most active building period"). The contrast is mutually acknowledged, which is good — consider noting Vietnam specifically adopted the Chinese bureaucratic model, sharpening the "two operating systems on the same peninsula" point given Dai Viet is a chain neighbor. — tang-song-china Ch 6/Ch 8.

11. **"Suryavarman I pushed Khmer power into the Mon kingdom of Lavo … and across much of what is now Thailand's central plain"** + the Tai material in Ch 9 — The Tai migration / Tai-state formation is treated as a purely Khmer-frontier story. tang-song-china.md Ch 1/Ch 9 and the Chinese-dynasty chain cover the southward Tai dispersal pressure out of southern China; srivijaya.md Ch 7 covers the parallel mainland-vs-maritime power shift. The "empires plant their own undertaker" point (Ch 9) would benefit from situating the Tai arrival in the broader push of Tai-speakers off the southern Chinese highlands. Minor but illuminating. — tang-song-china; (Tai dispersal, ~11th–14th c.).

### Chapter 5 — Suryavarman II and Angkor Wat

12. **"the largest religious monument ever built … its moated enclosure is about 1.5 by 1.3 km, roughly 200 hectares … there is no serious rival of comparable age and intactness"** — The superlative is hedged well and the narrative *does* anchor it against Chartres/Notre-Dame and Hagia Sophia (Byzantine Empire Ch 3, correctly cited — HS held the largest-enclosed-interior record per byzantine Ch 2).

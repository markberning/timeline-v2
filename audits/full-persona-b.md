## Chapter 2 — Before the Cities

1. `[CHECK]` **"seventeen successive temples stacked directly on top of each other"** — The number 17 is repeated as if precise; excavation reports cite varying counts depending on how "temple" is defined. Worth verifying the specific number against the Safar/Lloyd excavation publications.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Lapis lazuli… from a single source: the Sar-i Sang mines in Badakhshan"** — The "single source" claim is commonly stated in popular history but may overstate certainty; there are other ancient lapis sources (e.g., Russia, Chile) though Badakhshan dominates. The claim is defensible but absolute phrasing invites scrutiny.

3. `[SOFT]` **"more than 3,000 kilometers from southern Iraq… roughly the distance from New York to Los Angeles"** — The New York–LA analogy is a rhetorical choice that checks out (~3,940 km), but the 3,000 km figure for Badakhshan–southern Iraq is on the low side (straight-line is roughly 3,300 km; overland trade routes would have been considerably longer). Minor but worth a check.

## Chapter 3 — The First City: Uruk and the Sumerian Breakthrough

1. `[CHECK]` **"most archaeologists estimate 40,000 to 80,000 by 3200 BCE"** — Uruk population estimates in the literature vary widely. The high end of 80,000 is cited by some scholars but is disputed; others put the figure closer to 20,000–40,000. Presenting 40,000–80,000 as the mainstream estimate deserves a citation check.

2. `[CHECK]` **"ten to twenty times larger than any settlement that had ever existed anywhere on earth"** — This is an unusually strong comparative claim. It assumes we know the population of every contemporary settlement worldwide. The Tell Brak caveat follows immediately, which is good, but the "ten to twenty times" multiplier reads as invented precision.

3. `[CHECK]` **"cuneiform would go on to be used for over 3,400 years… The final surviving cuneiform tablets date from the 1st century CE"** — The latest known cuneiform tablet is often cited as 75 CE (an astronomical text). "1st century CE" is accurate, but "3,400 years" presupposes a start date of ~3400 BCE; some accounts put first cuneiform closer to 3200 BCE, which would shorten the span. Minor precision question.

4. `[SOFT]` **"a base-60 counting system whose descendant survives today in every clock and every compass"** — The Sumerian sexagesimal origin of our 60-minute hour is well-established, but "every compass" (360 degrees) is more indirect; the specific Babylonian transmission path for degrees is real but more contested than the clock claim. Fine as popular history, but slightly oversold.

## Chapter 4 — The Age of City-States

1. `[STRONG]` **"Eannatum (ruled approximately 2454–2425 BCE)"** — These reign dates appear nowhere in mainstream scholarly sources with this level of precision. Eannatum's dates are highly uncertain; even the most detailed Sumerian king list cross-references produce ranges of decades, not specific years. The precision is almost certainly false.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the phalanx… the Sumerians were using it fifteen hundred years before the Greeks"** — The phalanx-formation claim from the Stele of the Vultures is genuinely in the literature, but "fifteen hundred years" assumes the Greek hoplite phalanx emerged no earlier than ~750 BCE. Some scholars date early Greek phalanx development to ~800–750 BCE, giving roughly a 1,650-year gap. The "fifteen hundred years" figure is a round approximation that overstates the precision but directionally correct.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Lugal-zage-si of Umma (ruled 2358–2334 BCE)"** — Like Eannatum's dates above, these appear more precise than the evidence warrants. The end date (2334, when Sargon defeats him) is better attested; the start date of his reign is based on king-list reconstructions with significant uncertainty.

4. `[STRONG]` **"74 skeletons arranged in rows — soldiers in copper helmets, ladies-in-waiting in golden headdresses, musicians still holding their instruments"** — The number 74 and the specific breakdown of who was holding what is worth checking against Woolley's actual excavation reports. Some accounts give different totals for the "Great Death Pit" (PG 1237). The 74 figure is widely cited but the detailed description of what each group was doing may be more reconstructed than witnessed.

5. `[CHECK]` **"CT scans of some skulls, conducted in the 2000s, revealed blunt-force trauma"** — This result is cited in some popular science pieces but I cannot confirm a peer-reviewed study in the 2000s specifically on the Royal Tomb skulls using CT scanning. The claim is plausible but the sourcing is vague enough to warrant verification.

6. `[CHECK]` **"the shekel (a unit of weight equal to about 8.3 grams, originally derived from the weight of a specific number of barley grains)"** — "8.3 grams" is the figure most commonly cited for the Neo-Babylonian/Old Babylonian shekel, but the weight varied by period and region. The "barley grain" derivation is traditional but disputed in Assyriological literature. Fine as a general statement, but "about 8.3 grams" implies more uniformity than existed.

## Chapter 5 — The First Empire: Sargon and Akkad

1. `[STRONG]` **"Sargon… maintained a standing army of 5,400 professional soldiers 'who ate bread before him daily' (as one Akkadian inscription puts it)"** — The 5,400 figure and the "bread" quote appear in reconstructions of Akkadian royal inscriptions, but the original texts are fragmentary and the number comes from a legendized tradition about Sargon rather than a contemporary administrative record. The phrase is presented as a direct inscription quote, which is potentially misleading about how solid this number is.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Enheduanna (ca. 2285 BCE) predates every other known named author in human history by more than a century"** — The "more than a century" claim is broadly accepted but the specific century gap depends entirely on how one dates competing candidates. The claim is directionally right but the precision suggests more certainty than the chronology supports.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Naram-Sin… pushed the empire's borders further… possibly as far as the Indus Valley through the existing Meluhha trade network"** — The suggestion that Naram-Sin personally campaigned to the Indus Valley is speculative enough to warrant flagging. Trade contact is documented; a military campaign that far is not mainstream.

4. `[SOFT]` **"a six-foot-tall pink limestone monument now in the Louvre"** — The Victory Stele of Naram-Sin is indeed in the Louvre and is indeed roughly six feet tall (about 2 meters), and the stone is variously described as pink sandstone or limestone depending on the source. "Pink limestone" is a slight mismatch with most descriptions, which call it sandstone. Minor.

5. `[CHECK]` **"Over thirty finely-carved diorite statues of Gudea survive"** — "Over thirty" is the figure sometimes cited but varies in literature; the Louvre alone holds about 20, with others scattered in other collections. A count of "over thirty" is plausible but worth verifying.

## Chapter 6 — The Sumerian Renaissance

1. `[CHECK]` **"The outer layer alone used more than 720,000 baked bricks"** — This figure has the feel of a specific architectural calculation. It appears in some popular accounts of the Great Ziggurat but I cannot verify a primary source. It is the kind of suspiciously round-ish precise number worth tracing.

2. `[CHECK]` **"A man who cut off another man's foot owed 10 shekels of silver. A man who cut off another's nose owed 40 shekels."** — These specific penalties are cited in scholarly summaries of the Code of Ur-Nammu, and the code itself is genuinely fragmentary and preserved on later copies. The nose/foot figures appear in some reconstructions. However, since only ~40 laws survive from a damaged tablet, citing specific fines as if they are confirmed is worth verifying against the actual transliteration.

3. `[STRONG]` **"he had run from Nippur to Ur and back in a single day… roughly 160 kilometers each way… making the claimed round trip about 320 kilometers"** — The text correctly caveats this as possibly symbolic, but the 160 km figure is presented as a confident modern measurement. The actual distance depends on ancient route geometry; modern road distance (Nippur site to Ur) is closer to 190–200 km. The "160 km" figure is repeated as if established when it is an approximation that understates the distance.

4. `[SOFT]` **"The administrative tablets surviving from his reign number in the tens of thousands"** — "Tens of thousands" is a widely cited claim for Ur III tablets generally, but attributing this specifically to "surviving from his reign" is slightly loose; many Ur III tablets are undated or cover multiple reigns.

## Chapter 8 — The Long Peace: Kassite Babylon

1. `[CHECK]` **"a thousand-kilometer march (roughly the distance from New York to Chicago) down the Euphrates to attack Babylon"** — New York to Chicago is about 1,200 km by road, closer to 1,150 km straight line. The distance from the Hittite heartland (central Anatolia/Hattusa) down the Euphrates to Babylon is variously estimated at 1,500–2,000 km depending on the route. "Thousand-kilometer" is a round figure that understates the distance and the corresponding achievement.

2. `[STRONG]` **"the Kikkuli Text… is a detailed horse-training handbook for chariot teams written around 1400 BCE, explaining how to condition horses over a 214-day program of feeding, exercise, and rest"** — The Kikkuli Text is real and the "214-day program" figure appears in academic literature. However, the precise day count (214) comes from scholarly interpretation of a heavily damaged and partially reconstructed text. Presenting it as a clean "214-day program" implies more textual clarity than the source supports.

3. `[CHECK]` **"the Amarna Letters — an archive of 382 clay tablets accidentally discovered in 1887 by a local woman digging for fertilizer"** — The "382 tablets" count varies across sources (some give 379, some 382, some higher depending on whether fragments are counted separately). The "local woman digging for fertilizer" discovery story is the traditional account but has been questioned; some sources describe a local farmer or a woman gathering sebakh (decomposed mudbrick used as fertilizer). The detail is colorful enough to have been embellished.

## Chapter 9 — Assyria's First Rise

1. `[CHECK]` **"His annals claim 42 campaigns in his first five years of rule"** — This figure for Tiglath-Pileser I is cited in some popular histories but is the kind of very specific royal boast claim whose exact number should be traced to a specific inscription. Royal annals figures are often the product of later compilation and may not parse cleanly into a "42 in 5 years" format.

2. `[CHECK]` **"MUL.APIN is a systematic catalog… it lists 66 stars and constellations"** — The "66 stars and constellations" figure is cited in some overviews of MUL.APIN, but different sources give different counts (some say 18 constellations along the lunar path, others give different totals for the full catalog). The 66 figure is plausible but worth verifying.

3. `[STRONG]` **"the substitute king ritual… When the dangerous astrological period passed (typically about 100 days), the substitute king was killed"** — "Typically about 100 days" for the substitute king period is stated as if this is well-documented standard practice, but the actual documented instances of the ritual vary significantly in duration and outcome. Some scholars argue the substitute was killed; others argue he was merely dismissed. The "100 days" figure in particular sounds like a synthesized rule rather than a documented duration.

## Chapter 10 — The Assyrian War Machine

1. `[STRONG]` **"the feast lasted ten days and hosted 69,574 guests… 1,000 cattle, 1,000 calves, 10,000 sheep, 15,000 lambs, 10,000 ducks, 10,000 fish"** — The Ashurnasirpal II banquet inscription is real and survives (it is a genuine document, as the text notes). However, the food quantities listed here should be checked against the actual inscription carefully. Some accounts give different specifics — for instance, the number of ducks varies in different translations. The text correctly identifies 69,574 as the guest count, which is the figure in the inscription, but the food tallies as listed here are worth checking against a standard translation.

2. `[CHECK]` **"The Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III… depicts Jehu, king of Israel… kneeling in submission before Shalmaneser"** — This claim is accurate and widely cited. However, there is a genuine scholarly debate about whether the figure labeled "Iaua son of Omri" (Jehu) is actually Jehu or one of his officials/envoys. The text presents it as settled that this is Jehu himself. This is not a fabrication but a simplification of a contested identification.

3. `[CHECK]` **"The combined coalition force reportedly included more than 50,000 infantry, 4,000 chariots, and 1,000 camel troops"** — These are the figures from Shalmaneser III's own annals, which notoriously inflate enemy numbers. Presenting them as "reportedly" is correct but readers may not grasp how unreliable Assyrian troop-count propaganda is. Not fabricated, but the lack of skepticism about Assyrian numbers is worth noting.

## Chapter 11 — Assyria at Its Zenith

1. `[CHECK]` **"Tiglath-Pileser III's own inscriptions claim 154,000 deportees from a single Babylonian campaign"** — This figure appears in discussions of Tiglath-Pileser III's deportation numbers, but Assyrian inscription numbers are notoriously inflated. Presenting a royal boast as an estimate of real deportees invites the same skepticism as the Qarqar troop numbers. The framing here ("claim") is appropriate but the specific number deserves a flag.

2. `[STRONG]` **"Sargon II deported 27,290 Israelites from the northern kingdom"** — The 27,290 figure comes directly from Sargon II's own annals. It is suspiciously precise for a Bronze Age deportation count and is almost certainly propagandistic inflation or a rounded figure from administrative records. The text presents it without skepticism alongside the phrase "27,290 Israelites" as if it is a census.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Sennacherib… boasts that he trapped Hezekiah 'like a bird in a cage' in Jerusalem and extracted a massive tribute — 30 talents of gold, 800 talents of silver"** — The "bird in a cage" quote is real (it is from the Taylor Prism). However, the tribute figures vary between the Assyrian account (30 gold / 800 silver) and the biblical account (30 gold / 300 silver for 2 Kings 18:14). The text uses only the Assyrian figures without noting the discrepancy.

4. `[STRONG]` **"'I have learned the hidden wisdom of Nabu, the god of scribes… I can solve complex mathematical problems and read the obscure Sumerian tablets that come from before the flood' (a composite paraphrase from several of his inscriptions — the boast is real, but no single text contains exactly these words)"** — The parenthetical disclaimer is present and honest. Still flagging as STRONG because the composite quote is unusually specific and literary-sounding, and "tablets that come from before the flood" is a vivid phrase whose exact Akkadian source should be traceable. The admission that it is a composite paraphrase protects the text, but the phrasing may be more Claude than Ashurbanipal.

5. `[CHECK]` **"George Smith… became so excited that he began undressing — pulling off his clothes in the middle of the British Museum workroom"** — This story is widely repeated but its original source is murky. The primary account is a memoir by a colleague written years after the event. The "undressing" version is one of several competing accounts (others describe him as simply running around the room shouting). Colorful enough to have grown in the telling.

6. `[CHECK]` **"'Woe to the city of blood, full of plunder, never without victims'" (Book of Nahum)** — This is presented as a direct biblical quote. The closest verse is Nahum 3:1 ("Woe to the city of blood, full of lies, full of plunder, never without victims" in NIV). The text omits "full of lies" from the middle. Minor but a quote presented as direct should be exact.

## Chapter 12 — Babylon Resurgent

1. `[CHECK]` **"Nabopolassar describes himself as 'the weak, the feeble, who from childhood was nothing special'"** — This is presented as a direct quote from Nabopolassar's accession inscription. Such self-deprecating royal formulas do exist in Mesopotamian texts, but this phrasing sounds unusually modern. The specific inscription and its standard translation should be verified.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Sennacherib's Nineveh… walls stretched 12 kilometers around the city, stood 20 meters high, and had 15 gates, each named after a god"** — The 12 km perimeter and 15 gates are figures that appear in standard descriptions of Nineveh, sourced to Sennacherib's own inscriptions and archaeological survey. The "20 meters high" for the walls is less commonly cited at this precision and worth checking.

3. `[CHECK]` **"the Jerwan Aqueduct — a 280-meter-long stone bridge… each about 18 meters wide at the base"** — The Jerwan aqueduct dimensions appear in Jacobsen and Lloyd's 1935 excavation report. "280 meters long" and "18 meters wide" are cited in various places; worth confirming these are the excavation figures and not a later misquotation of them.

4. `[STRONG]` **"built by Nebuchadnezzar for his Median wife Amytis… She missed the forested, mountainous landscape of her homeland in the Zagros"** — "Amytis" as Nebuchadnezzar's Median wife and the motivation for the Hanging Gardens is given only by late classical sources (Berossus, Ctesias), neither of whom is particularly reliable. The text does immediately note that no contemporary Babylonian source mentions the Hanging Gardens, but it still presents "Amytis missed her homeland" as a biographical fact rather than a late legend.

5. `[CHECK]` **"The glazing… a brilliant deep-blue glaze achieved with a copper-based pigment (the exact chemistry is debated — some analyses suggest Egyptian blue, a calcium copper silicate, while others point to a lapis lazuli-derived compound)"** — The Ishtar Gate glaze chemistry is actively studied. "Lapis lazuli-derived compound" is unusual phrasing; the blue of the Ishtar Gate bricks is generally attributed to cobalt-based or copper-based colorants, not lapis lazuli. The Egyptian blue identification is more technically precise. This may be a garbled summary.

6. `[CHECK]` **"'the strong might not oppress the weak' (a standard paraphrase of the Akkadian)"** — The text itself flags this as a paraphrase, which is honest. However, the specific Akkadian phrase in Hammurabi's epilogue is important enough to have established translations; readers might benefit from knowing this is a loose rendering of a formal text.

## Chapter 13 — The End: Cyrus and the Long Echo

1. `[CHECK]` **"The Cyrus Cylinder (a 23-centimeter clay barrel inscribed in Babylonian cuneiform, now in the British Museum)"** — The cylinder is ~23 cm long; this dimension is documented. The "clay barrel" description is accurate enough for popular use, though specialists call it a clay cylinder. Fine, but the dimensions in this context are the kind of specific detail that can propagate errors if wrong.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Hipparchus… used Babylonian eclipse records to calculate the precession of the equinoxes"** — The claim that Hipparchus used Babylonian records to discover precession is standard in the history of science literature, but the precise mechanism of transmission is debated. Some scholars argue Hipparchus derived precession primarily from his own observations compared to earlier Greek (Timocharis) data, with Babylonian records as a secondary check. Presenting it as straightforward transmission slightly overstates scholarly consensus.

3. `[SOFT]` **"Babylon itself continued to exist and in fact thrived as a provincial capital under Persian rule for the next two hundred years"** — "Thrived" is interpretive and arguably overstated; Babylon under Persian rule had significant periods of disruption, including Xerxes's reported destruction of the Esagila and the Marduk statue after a revolt. The narrative arc of "peaceful transition → thriving provincial capital" omits these later Persian-Babylonian tensions.

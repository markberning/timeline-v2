## Chapter 1 — The Coup

1. `[CHECK]` **"the Ashokavadana (a Buddhist hagiographic text, compiled several centuries after the events it describes) claims he offered rewards for the heads of Buddhist monks and demolished the 84,000 stupas Ashoka had built"** — The claim about "84,000 stupas" is a known Buddhist legendary number (Ashoka supposedly built one stupa for every village in India), not a real archaeological count. The narrative correctly attributes this to the Ashokavadana, but calling it a Buddhist "claim" rather than a legendary tradition slightly understates how mythologized this number is.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the Great Stupa at Sanchi — one of the most important Buddhist monuments in India — was enlarged during the Shunga period, and its magnificent stone gateways (the toranas, Chapter 5) were carved under Shunga patronage"** — Scholarly consensus on Shunga patronage of Sanchi is actually debated; some scholars argue the toranas were funded by guilds and merchants independently of royal Shunga support, with the royal attribution uncertain. The assertion of "Shunga patronage" may be too confident.

3. `[SOFT]` **"Patanjali's Mahabhashya (pah-TAHN-jah-leez mah-hah-BHAH-shah), a massive commentary on Sanskrit grammar composed around 150 BCE that incidentally preserves valuable historical information about the Shunga period, mentions the Greek siege of a city that was probably Saketa"** — The attribution of the Mahabhashya passage to a specific siege event near Pataliputra is a scholarly inference, not a direct statement in the text. The phrase "mentions the Greek siege of a city that was probably Saketa" presents an interpretation as stronger fact.

## Chapter 2 — The Greek Kings

1. `[CHECK]` **"The Chinese were watching. In 138 BCE, the Han dynasty emperor Wu Di (woo dee) sent an envoy named Zhang Qian (jahng chee-EN) westward to contact the Yuezhi"** — Zhang Qian's mission is placed in Chapter 4, not Chapter 2. This note appears in the wrong chapter (Chapter 4 is about Kushans). Not a factual error, just context — no flag here.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Menander's coins reflect the cultural synthesis. Some show Athena (the Greek goddess of wisdom) on the reverse. Others show the dharmachakra"** — Menander's coins with dharmachakra are well documented. Fine.

3. `[STRONG]` **"When Menander died, Buddhist tradition says his ashes were divided among the cities of his kingdom and enshrined in stupas — the same treatment given to the Buddha himself"** — This claim, while present in some Buddhist sources, is not universally attested and some scholars treat it as hagiographic embellishment rather than historical record. The narrative presents it without the hedging it deserves.

4. `[CHECK]` **"the Milindapanha records philosophical dialogues between King Menander and a Buddhist monk named Nagasena"** — The text and the philosophical exchange are well documented. But the characterization that the Milindapanha is "one of the earliest and most sophisticated attempts at cross-cultural philosophical dialogue in world literature" is a strong claim that could be contested (the Platonic dialogues predate it by centuries and also involve cross-cultural elements). Not hallucination per se, but worth noting.

5. `[CHECK]` **"Around 113 BCE, a Greek ambassador named Heliodorus (heh-lee-oh-DOR-us), sent by the Indo-Greek king Antialcidas (an-tee-AL-sih-das) of Taxila to the Shunga court"** — The Heliodorus Pillar and its inscription are well-documented. The date of 113 BCE comes from the inscription itself and is generally accepted. Fine.

## Chapter 3 — Invaders from the Steppe

1. `[STRONG]` **"In 58 BCE, an era was established (traditionally by the Indo-Scythian king Azes I, AH-zeez) that became the foundation of the Vikrama Samvat calendar"** — The connection between the Vikrama Samvat era and Azes I is a scholarly hypothesis, not established fact. The traditional Hindu account attributes the Vikrama era to the legendary king Vikramaditya of Ujjain, not to Azes. The Azes hypothesis is one scholarly theory but is not consensus, and presenting it as "traditionally by" Azes is misleading — "traditionally" in Hindu usage would actually point to Vikramaditya.

2. `[CHECK]` **"The Western Satraps lasted until around 400 CE, making them one of the longest-running dynasties of the post-Maurya period"** — The end date of the Western Satraps is debated — some sources give 395 CE (defeat by Chandragupta II of the Guptas), others 415 CE. The "around 400 CE" is a reasonable approximation.

3. `[SOFT]` **"Gondophares (gahn-DOH-fah-reez), who ruled parts of Gandhara and the Punjab from approximately 19 BCE to 45 CE"** — The reign dates of Gondophares are reconstructed from coins and inscription and vary between scholars. Some give 19–46 CE; others a narrower window. The approximate framing is appropriate but worth noting.

4. `[CHECK]` **"early Christian tradition identifies him as the Indian king visited by the Apostle Thomas, the disciple of Jesus, who according to the apocryphal Acts of Thomas traveled to India"** — The Acts of Thomas and the Thomas tradition are well documented. The narrative correctly identifies the Acts as apocryphal. Fine.

## Chapter 4 — The Kushan Empire

1. `[STRONG]` **"Kujula Kadphises (koo-JOO-lah KAHD-fih-seez), and he founded the Kushan Empire. Kujula conquered Gandhara, pushed into the Indian subcontinent, and began building an empire that would eventually stretch from the Aral Sea to the Ganges plain — roughly 1,500 miles (2,400 km) from north to south"** — The Aral Sea to Ganges plain extent is attributed to the empire at its peak, not Kujula's founding. Kujula himself is generally credited with conquering Gandhara; the full extent came under Kanishka. Presenting this extent as Kujula's is potentially misleading.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Kanishka I ruled from approximately 127 to 150 CE (though the dating is debated — the 'Kanishka era,' which appears on inscriptions and coins throughout the Kushan realm, is one of the most argued-about chronological anchors in Asian history)"** — The uncertainty is correctly flagged. Fine.

3. `[CHECK]` **"reportedly over 600 feet (180 m) tall, though this number is almost certainly exaggerated. Archaeological remains suggest a substantial but more modest structure"** — Good hedging. The 600-foot figure comes from Xuanzang and other pilgrims; the actual archaeological evidence suggests something much smaller. Fine.

4. `[CHECK]` **"Kanishka convened a Fourth Buddhist Council — the great assembly of monks that compiled extensive commentaries on the Buddhist scriptures. The council's location is debated (Kashmir or Gandhara)"** — The Fourth Council and its debate over location are well documented. Fine.

5. `[STRONG]` **"the council is traditionally associated with the formal codification of Mahayana (mah-hah-YAH-nah; 'Great Vehicle') Buddhism"** — This claim is contested. The Fourth Buddhist Council is traditionally a Sarvastivadin council (Theravada-adjacent), not a Mahayana council. The narrative's association of the Kanishka Council with Mahayana codification is disputed by some scholars, who argue Mahayana developed independently outside council-type structures. This may need hedging.

6. `[CHECK]` **"In 68 CE (a date that is traditional and approximate), Buddhist monks and merchants traveling through Kushan territory brought Buddhism to Han dynasty China. Emperor Ming of Han reportedly dreamed of a golden figure in the west, sent envoys to investigate, and the envoys returned with monks and scriptures"** — The Ming of Han dream story is well-known as a legendary account. The narrative correctly flags it as "probably legendary." Fine.

7. `[CHECK]` **"In 179 CE, the Kushan monk Lokaksema (loh-KAHK-sheh-mah), working in the Chinese capital Luoyang (loh-YAHNG), produced the first Chinese translations of Mahayana sutras"** — Lokaksema is a real historical figure and his translations are documented. The date and attribution are accurate.

8. `[SOFT]` **"the Lotus Sutra (formally the Saddharma Pundarika, the 'Sutra of the Lotus of the True Dharma') was composed during the early Kushan period, probably in the 1st or 2nd century CE"** — The dating and origin of the Lotus Sutra is debated, but the 1st–2nd century CE range is the mainstream scholarly estimate. Fine.

## Chapter 5 — Gandhara and Mathura

1. `[CHECK]` **"The hair is wavy, arranged in a Mediterranean style. The face is European: straight nose, narrow lips, sharply defined brow ridge"** — The characterization of Gandhara Buddha as "European-looking" is a common popular description but is debated by art historians who argue the Gandhara style is a local synthesis, not a copy of Greek anatomy. The description is atmospheric rather than strictly hallucinatory.

2. `[SOFT]` **"the earliest Gandhara and Mathura Buddha images are roughly contemporary (1st century BCE to 1st century CE), and neither can be securely dated early enough to claim clear priority"** — The dating is accurately described as uncertain. Fine.

## Chapter 6 — The Deccan Powers

1. `[CHECK]` **"Some guilds functioned essentially as banks, accepting deposits and paying interest, a financial practice more sophisticated than anything in the contemporary Roman world"** — The claim of superiority over Roman banking needs care. Roman banking (trapezitai, argentarii) was sophisticated. Indian guild-banking did involve interest-bearing deposits, but calling it categorically more sophisticated than Roman finance is a strong claim that some economic historians would dispute.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the Nasik inscription (NAH-sik; carved in a cave temple at Nasik in Maharashtra), which is one of ancient India's rare texts authored by a woman"** — The Gautami Balashri Nasik inscription and its attribution to Gautami are well documented. The characterization as "authored by a woman" is the mainstream reading (she dictated/commissioned it), though some would say "composed on her behalf" rather than "authored by."

## Chapter 7 — Rome, Trade, and Tamil Kings

1. `[STRONG]` **"The Roman historian Pliny the Elder (PLIN-ee; 23–79 CE) complained that Rome was spending 50 million sesterces annually on Indian goods"** — Pliny's figure is actually 100 million sesterces total for India, China, and Arabia combined (with India specifically around 55 million), depending on which passage of the Natural History is used. The narrative gives 50 million for India specifically, but also quotes Pliny as saying "100 million sesterces from our empire per year" in the next sentence for the combined total. The specific "50 million for India" figure may conflate two different Pliny passages.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Hoards of Roman gold coins have been found across southern India — in Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Andhra Pradesh — in quantities that are hard to explain as anything other than a massive, sustained trade deficit"** — Roman coin hoards in southern India are well documented archaeologically. The interpretation as a trade deficit is the standard reading. Fine.

3. `[CHECK]` **"the Roman cookbook attributed to Apicius (ah-PIH-see-us), the only surviving Roman culinary text"** — Calling the Apicius cookbook the "only surviving Roman culinary text" is slightly inaccurate — there are agricultural texts (Columella, Cato) that include recipes, and the attribution to "Apicius" is itself debated. But the Apicius as the primary surviving culinary reference is broadly correct.

4. `[SOFT]` **"Tiruvalluvar (tee-roo-VAL-loo-var; dates uncertain, probably around 50 CE)"** — The date of Tiruvalluvar is highly uncertain, ranging from 3rd century BCE to 8th century CE in various scholarly estimates. "Probably around 50 CE" is one estimate but on the earlier end of the scholarly range. The uncertainty should be more strongly hedged.

## Chapter 8 — Seeds of the Golden Age

1. `[CHECK]` **"Nagarjuna was born in the Satavahana realm (probably in modern Andhra Pradesh) around 150 CE"** — The birthplace and dates of Nagarjuna are uncertain and debated. "Around 150 CE" is a plausible estimate; some traditions place him earlier or later. The "probably in Andhra Pradesh" attribution is widely cited. Fine.

2. `[CHECK]` **"The Bakshali manuscript (BAHK-shah-lee; a fragmentary mathematical text found near Peshawar, carbon-dated with significant uncertainty to the early centuries CE)"** — The carbon dating of the Bakshali manuscript was published in 2017 and showed a range from 3rd to 10th centuries CE, which is wider than "early centuries CE." The narrative correctly notes "significant uncertainty," but the wording "early centuries CE" implies the earlier end of the range, when the dates span at least to the 10th century.

3. `[SOFT]` **"the Sushruta Samhita (SOOSH-roo-tah SAHM-hih-tah), compiled in the early centuries CE, is even more remarkable. It is a treatise on surgery that describes procedures including rhinoplasty (nose reconstruction, using a flap of skin from the forehead — a technique still called the 'Indian method' in modern plastic surgery)"** — The rhinoplasty claim is accurate and well documented. The "Indian method" designation is a real term in plastic surgery literature. Fine.

4. `[STRONG]` **"Around 275 CE, a minor chief named Sri Gupta (shree GOOP-tah) established a small kingdom in Magadha"** — The date of 275 CE for Sri Gupta's founding is uncertain and debated. Some scholars place him earlier (250s CE) or estimate his dates differently. Additionally, whether his kingdom was actually in Magadha (as opposed to somewhere in Bengal or eastern India) is debated — the Gupta origins are unclear. The confident "Magadha" attribution may be too definitive.

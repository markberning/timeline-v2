## Chapter 1 — The Forgotten Civilization

1. `[CHECK]` **"over 90 miles (150 km) of railway track on the pulverized remains"** — The 90-mile figure for track laid using Harappa bricks is suspiciously specific. Some sources say "100 miles," others are vaguer. The exact mileage may be embellished or invented.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Five million people"** — A population of five million for the Mature Harappan period is at the high end of scholarly estimates and is repeated as fact throughout the narrative. Some scholars put it considerably lower. The precision of a single round number presented without qualification is a flag.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Over a thousand settlements spread across 1.5 million square kilometers (580,000 square miles — roughly the area of Alaska)"** — The 1.5 million sq km figure appears in some sources but is contested; other estimates range from 800,000 to 1.25 million sq km. The number is stated flatly without hedging.

4. `[SOFT]` **"A standardized system of weights accurate to a tenth of a gram"** — This claim recurs (also in Ch 4). The "tenth of a gram" precision figure needs sourcing — it may be real but it sounds like it could be an exaggeration of the actual archaeological measurements.

## Chapter 2 — The Deep Roots

5. `[CHECK]` **"Some holes even show traces of a filling material — possibly beeswax or a plant-based compound"** — The beeswax filling claim for Mehrgarh dental work is sometimes cited but the original Coppa et al. (2006) study in *Nature* documented drilled teeth without confirmed fillings. The filling material may be conflated with a later finding or invented.

6. `[SOFT]` **"Copper smelting appeared around 5000 BCE — early enough to be among the first metalworking in South Asia"** — The 5000 BCE date for copper at Mehrgarh is plausible but worth checking; some sources place the earliest copper at Mehrgarh in Period II (5500-4800 BCE), others later.

## Chapter 3 — The Dress Rehearsal

7. `[CHECK]` **"a fortified settlement in what is now Sindh province in Pakistan. Founded around 2800 BCE"** — The founding date of 2800 BCE for Kot Diji is suspiciously precise for a pre-literate site dated by pottery typology and limited radiocarbon dates. Many sources give a broader range (3300-2600 BCE for the Kot Dijian phase).

8. `[CHECK]` **"walls about 260 feet (80 meters) long and rising 16 feet (5 meters) above the surrounding plain"** — The specific dimensions of the Kot Diji citadel walls (260 feet long, 16 feet high) are suspiciously precise and should be verified against excavation reports. These numbers may be approximations presented as measurements.

## Chapter 4 — The Urban Explosion

9. `[CHECK]` **"The smallest weights are accurate to 0.1 grams — a precision that's impressive even by modern standards"** — Repeated from Ch 1. The claim that Harappan weights were accurate to 0.1 grams is widely cited but the phrasing "impressive even by modern standards" oversells it — modern analytical balances measure to 0.0001 grams. The 0.1g figure itself may be accurate but needs sourcing against actual metrology studies (e.g., Kenoyer's work).

10. `[SOFT]` **"Mesopotamia's city-states occupied a region about the size of New Jersey"** — This comparison is memorable but suspiciously convenient. Mesopotamian city-state territory in the Early Dynastic period covered a substantial area of southern Iraq; whether it maps neatly to New Jersey's size feels like an AI-generated comparison that may not hold up.

## Chapter 5 — The Great Cities

11. `[CHECK]` **"at least 700, roughly one per city block"** — The "700 wells" figure for Mohenjo-daro is widely cited but originates from older excavation estimates. Given that only about a third of the site has been excavated, this number may be an extrapolation presented as a count.

12. `[CHECK]` **"peak population estimates range from 23,000 to 35,000"** — The population range for Harappa (23,000-35,000) is oddly specific, particularly the lower bound of 23,000. Population estimates for ancient cities are typically given in rounder numbers. This may be an AI-generated range rather than one drawn from published scholarship.

13. `[CHECK]` **"a large rectangular basin measuring about 120 by 72 feet (37 by 22 meters)"** — The Lothal dockyard dimensions of 37 by 22 meters are specific and should be verified. Some sources give different dimensions (e.g., 218 by 37 meters for the larger structure). The identification of this structure as a dockyard is itself debated — some archaeologists argue it was an irrigation tank.

14. `[STRONG]` **"the world's earliest known purpose-built harbor"** — This is a strong claim, and the identification of the Lothal basin as a dockyard/harbor is actively disputed by several archaeologists (including some Indian scholars). Presenting it as settled fact ("the world's earliest known purpose-built harbor") without noting the controversy is misleading.

15. `[CHECK]` **"Recent surveys suggest it covered over 865 acres (350 hectares)"** — The 350-hectare figure for Rakhigarhi is cited in some recent Indian archaeological publications but is contested. Some estimates are lower. The precision suggests a specific source but it may be an upper-bound estimate presented as the main figure.

16. `[CHECK]` **"In 2019, a study published in the journal Cell"** — The Rakhigarhi DNA study was published in *Cell* in 2019 (Shinde et al.). This attribution appears correct, but the summary should be verified — the study analyzed a single individual's genome, not "burials" (plural), which overstates the sample size.

## Chapter 6 — The Invisible Rulers

17. `[SOFT]` **"7.3 inches (18.5 cm) tall"** — The Priest-King bust dimensions are suspiciously precise. Most museum descriptions give "about 17.5 cm" — the 18.5 cm / 7.3 inches figure may be slightly off or invented.

## Chapter 7 — A World Connected

18. `[CHECK]` **"who reportedly boasted that ships from Meluhha docked at his capital"** — The attribution to Sargon of Akkad boasting about Meluhha ships is a common claim in popular histories but the actual cuneiform evidence is ambiguous. The text may refer to ships from Meluhha docking at Akkad generally during his reign, not a personal boast.

19. `[CHECK]` **"Long carnelian beads (some up to 5 inches, or 12 cm) required heating the raw orange-yellow stone to turn it deep red, then drilling from both ends with copper drills tipped with abrasive paste. The process took weeks per batch."** — The "weeks per batch" timeframe is commonly repeated but is an estimate based on experimental archaeology, not ancient records. The specific process description (heating to change color, drilling from both ends) is broadly correct but the confidence of "weeks" as a timeframe should be checked.

20. `[SOFT]` **"the only significant source of lapis lazuli in the ancient world"** — Badakhshan as the "only significant source" of lapis lazuli is the standard claim, but there are other known ancient sources (e.g., in the Chagai Hills of Balochistan, and possibly Siberia). "Primary" would be safer than "only significant."

## Chapter 8 — Gods Without Names

21. `[CHECK]` **"74 sacrificed attendants"** — The number 74 for attendants in the Royal Tombs of Ur is suspiciously specific. Woolley's excavation reports give varying numbers for different tombs — the most famous (PG 1237, the "Great Death Pit") had about 74 bodies, but the text implies this was a single burial rather than one specific tomb among several. The number is real but the context may be misleading.

## Chapter 9 — The Quiet Collapse

22. `[CHECK]` **"the fall of the Third Dynasty of Ur in 2004 BCE"** — The date 2004 BCE for the fall of Ur III is one specific chronological reckoning (the Middle Chronology). Other chronologies place it at 2006 or slightly different dates. Presenting it as a single precise year without noting chronological uncertainty is mildly misleading for a Bronze Age event.

## Chapter 10 — Two Worlds Meet

23. `[CHECK]` **"a collection of 1,028 hymns to Vedic deities, composed between roughly 1500 and 1200 BCE"** — The 1,028 hymn count for the Rigveda is correct and well-attested. However, the dating range of 1500-1200 BCE, while mainstream, is presented without the usual "roughly" or "approximately" qualifier on the endpoint — some scholars push composition as late as 1000 BCE.

24. `[SOFT]` **"Sanskrit is as closely related to ancient Greek as Italian is to Spanish"** — This is a vivid analogy but linguistically imprecise. The relationship between Sanskrit and Greek (both ancient IE branches that diverged perhaps 3000-4000 years before their attested forms) is not straightforwardly comparable to the Italian-Spanish relationship (both Romance languages diverging ~1500 years ago). The analogy may mislead more than it illuminates.

25. `[CHECK]` **"the Ghaggar-Hakra's gradual drying — its Himalayan tributaries were captured by other rivers through natural tectonic shifts"** — This river capture theory (Sutlej shifting to Indus, Yamuna shifting to Ganges) is stated as established fact across Chapters 1, 9, and 10. While it is a leading hypothesis, the mechanism and timing of these river avulsions are still debated. Some geomorphologists argue the Ghaggar-Hakra was never a major glacial-fed river in the Holocene.

Editorial evaluation as writing only: arc, momentum, stakes, structure, point. No fact-checking.

## Front Matter (Goal + three "A note on…" sections)

The framing apparatus is unusually strong and should largely be left alone — the "whose story this is" note ("these are not two stories but one") is the thesis of the whole book stated in one clean line, and the contested-numbers note earns trust before the reader hits a single disputed figure. One caution: the opening **Goal** paragraph is a single 200-word sentence-pile that front-loads the entire book's plot. It reads like a back-cover blurb crossed with a syllabus. A reader who hasn't started yet doesn't need WTO, COVID, and the demographic crisis in sentence one. Trim it to the hook ("the single fastest escape from mass poverty in human history, run by the same party that caused the famine") and let the chapters deliver the rest.

## Chapter 1 — Picking Up the Pieces

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** In post-Mao China legitimacy could no longer come from being Mao's heir — it had to come from results, and the 1979 bargain (prosperity for a monopoly on power) is set here.

**What works:**
- The opening question is a genuine grabber: "what do you do with a country of nearly a billion people that has just spent twenty-seven years being convulsed by its own government… where the only legitimating idea anyone in power has is the thought of a man who caused the worst famine in human history?" That is a thesis disguised as a question, and it pulls.
- Hua Guofeng is handled with real economy and wit: "He lasted about two years as the real leader and is the answer to a trivia question." The Two Whatevers reads as comedy and tragedy at once.
- The Democracy Wall close is a perfect chapter-ending: it names the limit ("the deal, never written but never since broken") and explicitly hands forward — "Everything in the next fourteen chapters is the working-out… of that bargain."

**What doesn't work:**
- The "practice is the sole criterion of truth" passage briefly slows into seminar mode. It recovers fast ("a precision-guided political weapon"), but the abstract-then-concrete rhythm repeats three times in two paragraphs. Minor — tighten one of the three restatements.

**Momentum map:** Strong open → slight dip in the epistemology paragraph → reaccelerates through the cat slogan → lands hard on the Democracy Wall bargain.

## Chapter 2 — The Household Responsibility System

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** China's largest and fastest escape from poverty was not engineered from Beijing — it was a crime committed by starving farmers and ratified after the fact.

**What works:**
- The Xiaogang scene is the best piece of pure narrative in the book: eighteen starving households, a secret contract signed in fingerprints "because several could not write," the pledge to raise each other's children if any were executed, and the payoff sentence — "The next harvest, on the same land with the same tools, was larger than the previous five combined." This is Tuchman-tier; it dramatizes an economic mechanism through one room of people.
- The mechanism is then stated plainly without killing the scene: "nothing was wrong with Chinese agriculture except the incentive." The chapter trusts the story to carry the idea.
- The Hu Yaobang / Zhao Ziyang close plants two names and an arc ("from the Xiaogang fingerprints to Zhao Ziyang under house arrest… is the arc of Chapters 2 through 5") — a textbook forward hook.

**What doesn't work:**
- One minor note: the township-and-village-enterprises paragraph, while important, is the one stretch where the chapter narrates rather than dramatizes, and it sits between two vivid passages. It's the necessary connective tissue; just keep it as tight as it currently is and don't let future edits expand it.

**Momentum map:** A sustained high — the strongest single chapter. Energy peaks at the Xiaogang harvest and never fully drops.

## Chapter 3 — Special Economic Zones and the Open Door

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Reform was run as a series of walled experiments precisely because the leadership did not trust it and could not admit, ideologically, what it was doing.

**What works:**
- The containment thesis is crisp and load-bearing: "Reform was run as a series of walled experiments precisely because the leadership did not trust it." It reframes the SEZs from triumph to nervous hedge — a genuine "so what."
- The site-selection logic ("The site selection was not about local resources; it was about adjacency to capital… Xiamen faced Taiwan; Zhuhai faced Macau") is the kind of concrete pattern that makes a reader feel they've understood something.
- The cross-series pacing comparison (Song centuries / Meiji forty years / Korea a generation / Shenzhen fifteen years "because it was not inventing the industrial economy — it was importing one that already existed, next door") is the book's comparative method at its sharpest.

**What doesn't work:**
- The closing paragraph does heavy thematic work (India-vs-China contrast, "socialism with Chinese characteristics," Western-expectation thesis) and ends on a hand-forward to Chapter 5. It's effective but slightly overloaded — three big ideas competing for the last word. Consider letting the India contrast land and cutting one of the restatements of "state-supervised capitalism + party monopoly," which the book will repeat many times.

**Momentum map:** Steady and confident throughout; mild thematic congestion in the final paragraph.

## Chapter 4 — The Urban Reforms and the First Strains

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The dual-track price system grew the economy and simultaneously built the corruption machine that filled Tiananmen Square.

**What works:**
- The chapter announces its own function and delivers on it: "This chapter is the machinery of that explosion." A reader always knows why they're reading.
- The arbitrage explanation is made visceral and political at once: "you doubled or tripled your money for doing nothing… it was the structural design of the transition handing a money-printing machine to the politically connected." The line "that grievance, more than abstract democracy, is what filled Tiananmen Square" is the chapter's payoff and a genuine reframe.
- The *River Elegy* / culture-fever passage gives the chapter an emotional and intellectual texture beyond economics, and the close ("All they needed was an occasion to gather, and in April 1989 one arrived in the form of a funeral") is a near-perfect cliff-hand into Chapter 5.

**What doesn't work:**
- The 1988 inflation paragraph is the densest stretch — price reform, panic buying, the leadership split, the "rectification" — and momentarily reads as a list of events rather than a story. It's recoverable: lead the paragraph with the human image (people running to convert cash into anything durable) before the policy mechanics, rather than after.

**Momentum map:** Builds steadily; brief encyclopedia-dump risk in the 1988 paragraph; finishes on one of the book's best transitions.

## Chapter 5 — Tiananmen 1989

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The 1979 bargain was challenged head-on and defended with the army, and every subsequent chapter is shaped by how it ended.

**What works:**
- Correctly identified and framed as "the hinge chapter of the book," and the chapter earns that billing.
- The contested-toll handling is a model of the series' method under maximum pressure: it gives the range, names who disputes it and why, and lands the one undisputed sentence — "the state used the army to kill an unknown but substantial number of unarmed citizens in Beijing to end the protest, and that it worked." Restraint creates more force than a number would.
- Zhao Ziyang's bullhorn — "We have come too late" — followed immediately by "He was purged within days and spent the remaining sixteen years of his life under house arrest" is the emotional spine of the whole Hu/Zhao arc paying off exactly where Chapter 2 promised it would.
- The closing turn is the best forward-hook in the book: "It looked, in 1990, as though China might freeze… That it did the opposite… was the work of an eighty-seven-year-old man with no office taking a train south."

**What doesn't work:**
- One minor structural note: the chapter carries a lot of "this is contested / the rule applies here" meta-commentary up front before the narrative begins. It's justified given the subject, but the first two paragraphs are throat-clearing before the story (the funeral) starts. Consider opening on Hu Yaobang's death and folding the contested-toll caveat in at the point of the death toll itself, where it has maximum narrative weight.

**Momentum map:** Slow, deliberate meta-opening → accelerates hard once the funeral begins → unbroken through June Fourth → lands on the strongest hand-forward in the book.

## Chapter 6 — The Southern Tour and the Capitalist Turn

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Deng's deepest achievement was not a reform but a succession system that let the regime survive its founder's death — and Chapter 11 will undo it.

**What works:**
- The Soviet-collapse framing gives the chapter genuine stakes: "the only thing that could save the Chinese Communist Party was to grow the economy so fast and so undeniably that prosperity, not ideology, became the basis of consent." The reader feels why the Southern Tour mattered, not just that it happened.
- The Southern Tour itself is dramatized as a political insurgency, not a trip: "an old man with no formal authority going over the heads of the entire central leadership by appealing directly to the regions getting rich."
- The 1994 tax reform is rescued from being a footnote by a sharp thesis line: "The boom is what people remember; the tax reform is what made the state that managed the boom." That is exactly the "so what" the series is good at.
- The Deng-death close ("a paramount leader died and the machine simply kept running… Chapter 11 is the story of the man who undid it") is a clean, ominous hand-forward.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the chapter has three potential closing beats (the *xiahai* boom, the tax reform, Deng's death), and the tax-reform paragraph interrupts the emotional momentum between the boom and Deng's death. Consider whether the tax-reform point lands harder before the *xiahai* paragraph, leaving the death of Deng as the uninterrupted final movement.

**Momentum map:** Strong stakes-setting open → high through the Southern Tour → slight structural eddy at the tax-reform paragraph → resolves on the quietly powerful Deng death.

## Chapter 7 — Joining the World

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** In 1997–2002 China fused itself structurally to the global economy — and the Western theory that this would liberalize it failed.

**What works:**
- The Hong Kong handover is given real emotional charge by tying it to the previous book: "the literal, ceremonial reversal of the opening humiliation of the entire previous book." The series-memory payoff works.
- The SOE layoffs passage is the chapter's best material — "one of the great untold mass dislocations of the era, absorbed with remarkably little organized resistance partly because… protest had been answered, definitively, in 1989" — and the callback to Chapter 5 gives a dry restructuring story a chill.
- The Falun Gong close is a strong, specific illustration of the failed-liberalization thesis rather than an abstract assertion.

**What doesn't work:**
- This is a three-events-in-one chapter (handover, Asian crisis, WTO) plus SOE layoffs plus Falun Gong, and it's the first chapter where the structure feels like a portmanteau rather than a single arc. The throughline ("economic results were the only thing standing between the party and the fate of its Soviet model") is asserted in the opening but doesn't tie the four sections together as tightly as the connective claim promises. Structural fix: pick the SOE/WTO double-punch as the spine (the party dismantling its own socialism to join the capitalist order) and demote the Asian crisis and handover to supporting beats rather than co-equal sections.
- The WTO paragraph carries the single most consequential economic claim in the book ("the single most consequential economic event in the book") but is wedged between SOE layoffs and Falun Gong; it deserves more room and a cleaner runway than it gets.

**Momentum map:** Even and competent but sectional — four discrete movements rather than one build; energy is highest in the SOE-layoffs paragraph and most diffuse around the Asian crisis.

## Chapter 8 — The Factory of the World

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The boom ran on 200–300 million migrant workers deliberately disenfranchised by the hukou system, and the same decade that built high-speed rail could not let a doctor tell the truth about a virus.

**What works:**
- The chapter refuses to let the boom be abstract: "The export boom did not run on robots; it ran on roughly 200 to 300 million people." The Foxconn-dormitory / rural-Sichuan detail makes the structural injustice concrete and human.
- The contested-claims discipline extends to the triumph itself ("the contested-claims rule applies even to the triumphs"), which is rare and trust-building — most popular history would just take the 800-million number.
- The closing antithesis is one of the best in the book: "The system could build a high-speed rail network in a decade. It could not let a doctor tell the truth about a virus, and the same decade contained both facts." It states the book's central paradox and seeds Chapter 15 in one stroke.

**What doesn't work:**
- The "costs" paragraph briefly becomes a list (inequality, environment, corruption, infant formula, SARS) moving fast through five major topics in a few sentences. SARS in particular — explicitly "the direct rehearsal for Chapter 15" — is too important to be a clause in a list. Structural fix: let SARS be its own short beat leading into the chapter close, rather than one item among the scandals.

**Momentum map:** Strong human open (migrants) → confident through the poverty-reduction reckoning → compresses into a fast cost-list → recovers fully on the closing antithesis.

## Chapter 9 — The Olympics and the Global Crisis

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** 2008 was the year China saved the world economy and bought its own future on credit — with the same check.

**What works:**
- The chapter's structural conceit is announced and delivered: "Compressed into twelve months are the triumph, the tragedy, the scandal, and the decision that mortgaged the future." Four-act compression gives the chapter unusual drive.
- The Sichuan earthquake is the emotional core and is handled with restraint and force: "a disproportionate number of the dead were schoolchildren, crushed when shoddily built schools collapsed while sturdier buildings nearby stood." "The earthquake is the moment the costs of the model acquired a body count the public could see and count" is a real thesis line.
- The closing sentence — "2008 is the year China saved the world economy and bought its own future on credit, and it did both with the same check" — is the single best closing line in the book and a perfect seed for Chapters 10 and 15.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the Olympics section, while well-written, is the weakest of the four acts because it's the most familiar and the least eventful as narrative — it's mood and meaning rather than story. It earns its place thematically; just be aware it's the low-energy quarter of an otherwise propulsive chapter, and don't let future edits lengthen it.

**Momentum map:** Measured Olympics open → sharp rise at the earthquake → sustained through the crash and stimulus → lands on the book's best closing line.

## Chapter 10 — The Disaffected Decade and the Bo Xilai Affair

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** The boom's costs came due, a brief semi-public square (Weibo) showed the demand for accountability never died, and a knife fight at the top produced Xi Jinping.

**What works:**
- "Not a movement in a square this time, but a knife fight at the top" sets the chapter's stakes immediately and distinguishes it from Chapter 5.
- The Bo Xilai affair is genuinely gripping as narrative — the police chief fleeing to a US consulate, the murdered British businessman, "a princeling's naked power grab" — and the close is one of the book's best dramatic-irony beats: "the party that chose him in 2012 believed it was choosing a dull, reliable compromise. It was the single greatest misreading in modern Chinese politics."
- The Wenzhou-rail-crash detail ("the authorities' attempt to literally bury a wrecked carriage while victims might still have been inside") is a perfect, awful image that does the work of a paragraph of analysis.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter front-loads three semi-discrete topics (the mood/ghost cities, the Weibo era, the Jasmine/Liu Xiaobo crackdown) before the Bo Xilai narrative, which is clearly the spine. The first two-thirds is mosaic; the engine doesn't start until "Into this came the succession crisis." Structural fix: open closer to the Bo Xilai drama (or at least foreshadow it in the lede) and weave the mood/Weibo/Liu Xiaobo material in as the conditions that made the succession crisis dangerous, rather than as a preamble the reader waits through.
- The Liu Xiaobo / Charter 08 paragraph is important and moving but is currently a cul-de-sac — it doesn't feed the Bo Xilai spine. Either connect it explicitly to the stakes of the transition or accept it as a deliberate set-piece and give it a cleaner landing.

**Momentum map:** Slow mosaic open (mood, Weibo, Jasmine, Liu Xiaobo) → energy spikes hard at "the succession crisis" → races through Bo Xilai to a superb ironic close.

## Chapter 11 — Xi Jinping Takes Power

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Xi used a real grievance (corruption) to dismantle the system that produced it, and reopened the two-thousand-year succession question Deng had solved.

**What works:**
- The chapter names its own method as the point: "a textbook case of using a real grievance to dismantle the system that produced it." The reader is given the lens before the events.
- The contested-claims handling of the anti-corruption campaign is the book's intellectual method at its best: "it was both at once and that the two functions were inseparable — every tiger removed was both a thief and an obstacle." That sentence refuses both propaganda and counter-propaganda.
- The series-spanning close is the most ambitious in the book and it lands: "The chapter that began the series, two thousand years and many books ago, was about a dynasty that fell because legitimacy and succession had no stable form… In 2018 the most powerful state on Earth chose, deliberately, to reopen that question." This is the payoff of the entire series' structure.

**What doesn't work:**
- The final paragraph appends the one-child-policy reversal after the soaring succession-question close, and it deflates the chapter's biggest moment. The "two thousand years… reopened that question" sentence is the natural end of the chapter; the one-child material, while needed as a Chapter 15 seed, reads as an afterthought tacked past the climax. Structural fix: move the one-child beat earlier (it fits naturally beside Document No. 9 as part of the era's policy turn) so the succession-question sentence can be the last word.

**Momentum map:** Strong lens-setting open → sustained through the anti-corruption and ideological turns → peaks on the series-spanning succession close → then a flat coda that should be relocated.

## Chapter 12 — The Belt and Road and the Tech Ambition

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** China's outward bid to reshape the global economy and its inward apparatus of social control are the same project seen from two ends — confident enough to lead the world, anxious enough to score every citizen.

**What works:**
- The organizing conceit is strong and stated cleanly: "a state confident enough to try to lead the world and anxious enough to want to see and score every one of its own citizens." It gives a two-topic chapter a single spine.
- The debt-trap-diplomacy passage is the contested-claims method working well on a Western myth, and the parallel move on social credit ("there was never… a single unified national score… that image is mostly a Western dystopian extrapolation") is brave and trust-building — it corrects the reader's likely prior rather than flattering it.
- The Xinjiang close has real moral weight and ties to the series: "This series has, across many books, described states absorbing and erasing peoples; this is that, documented in real time, by a state powerful enough to deny it to the world's face."

**What doesn't work:**
- The middle "genuine technological surge" paragraph (mobile payments, platform economy, Made in China 2025, the Moon probe) is the chapter's weakest stretch — it's a capability inventory, the closest the book comes to an encyclopedia dump. It moves through five distinct achievements in a few sentences without a narrative or a person. Structural fix: anchor it on one figure or moment (Jack Ma is named but not used — he is the natural human hook here, especially as a setup for his later humbling) so the surge is dramatized, not catalogued.
- The chapter covers an enormous amount (BRI, fintech, industrial policy, space, surveillance, social credit, Xinjiang) and the "two faces" frame, while elegant, is asked to carry more than it comfortably can. The Xinjiang material is strong enough that it competes with the chapter's stated thesis for the reader's memory; consider whether Xinjiang wants more room and the tech-inventory less.

**Momentum map:** Strong conceptual open → sharp on debt-trap → sags into the tech-capability inventory → recovers with full force on Xinjiang.

## Chapter 13 — Hong Kong and Taiwan

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The crushing of Hong Kong, intended to demonstrate control, destroyed the "one country, two systems" model that was supposed to win Taiwan peacefully.

**What works:**
- The central irony is set up early and paid off precisely: "the crushing of Hong Kong, intended to demonstrate control, destroyed the model that was supposed to win Taiwan peacefully." The whole chapter is built to deliver that one sentence and it does.
- The Hong Kong escalation is told as a genuine arc (Umbrella Movement → booksellers → 2019 → security law), each stage raising stakes, ending on "effectively finished twenty-seven years before its fifty-year guarantee expired" — a quietly devastating line.
- The Taiwan close correctly refuses a verdict and makes that refusal the point: "the one major thread this series cannot close, because it is not closed… how it resolves belongs to a history not yet written." This is the rare cliffhanger that is honest rather than evasive.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the Taiwan half has to compress a long backstory (1949, democratic transition, 1992 Consensus, Sunflower Movement, Tsai) into a few sentences, and it briefly accelerates into summary right before the strong close. It holds, but the Sunflower Movement — a strong potential scene (students occupying the legislature) — is reduced to a parenthetical when it could carry the Taiwan section's emotional turn the way Umbrella carries Hong Kong's.

**Momentum map:** Strong paired-stakes open → escalating, well-paced Hong Kong arc → faster Taiwan summary → lands on an honest, powerful non-ending.

## Chapter 14 — The Wolf Warriors and the US Rivalry

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** 2017–2022 is when both sides finally admitted engagement had failed and the relationship flipped from integration to open strategic rivalry.

**What works:**
- The chapter explicitly closes the book's longest-running argument: "Chapter by chapter this book has shown why that assumption failed. This chapter is about the period… when both sides finally admitted it had failed." It functions as a thesis-resolution chapter and announces itself as one.
- The chip war is given its proper weight as the deepest stake: "an attempt to freeze China a generation behind at the technological commanding height, and the single most serious external constraint on the Chinese model." That is a clear "so what."
- The Galwan close reactivates the book's central India–China comparison with real force: "the same contested frontier, the same two civilizations, as the 1962 war China fought while its own people starved… the great natural experiment in how to lift a civilization out of poverty." This is the comparative spine of the series paying off.

**What doesn't work:**
- This is the most list-like chapter in the book. The middle (Wolf-warrior diplomacy → Quad → AUKUS → South China Sea tribunal → Galwan) moves through five geopolitical items in quick succession with little scene or person; the Meng Wanzhou arrest is the only human anchor and it's confined to one sentence. Structural fix: Meng Wanzhou (a CFO and a founder's daughter arrested in an airport, two Canadians taken hostage in retaliation) is a ready-made scene that dramatizes "engagement is over" better than any abstraction — open or pivot the chapter on it rather than burying it mid-paragraph.
- "Two confrontations showed the new logic" introduces South China Sea and Galwan as paired examples, but they're unequal: Galwan carries the series-payoff and South China Sea is comparatively inert here. Consider demoting the tribunal ruling to a clause and giving Galwan the full runway it deserves.

**Momentum map:** Strong thesis-resolution open → energetic on the trade/chip war → flattens into a geopolitics inventory in the middle → spikes back up on the Galwan/India payoff.

## Chapter 15 — COVID and the New Era

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** COVID is the stress test of the entire bargain — a system that can mobilize like no other and cannot correct itself, because correction requires telling the leader he is wrong.

**What works:**
- The single-year inversion is the chapter's structural masterstroke and it's stated as such: "In the space of a single year the same policy was both the regime's proudest evidence of superiority and a self-inflicted catastrophe ended by street protest." The chapter is built on a hinge and the reader feels it turn.
- Li Wenliang is the right human anchor and is used well — silenced, forced to recant, dead in February 2020, "the closest thing to the Weibo moment of Chapter 10 the Xi era had allowed." A single doctor carries the chapter's first act, exactly as the book's method prescribes.
- The ending is the best closing movement in the book and the right one for the whole series: the Hu Jintao removal-from-stage image, the Evergrande/property/demographics turn, and the deliberate refusal of a verdict — "the one place this series, which has always known how its stories ended, has to stop and admit that no one does." This is a series-ending, not just a chapter-ending, and it earns the weight.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the final paragraph carries a great deal at once — the third term, the Hu Jintao image, Evergrande, youth unemployment, the population shrinking, the Qing callback, the 800-million summation, and the closing question. It lands, but it is dense enough that the Hu Jintao image (one of the most cinematic in the book) flashes past in a single clause. Consider giving that image its own short beat before the economic-reckoning pivot so it isn't competing with five other closing elements.

**Momentum map:** Measured Wuhan/Li Wenliang open → triumphant zero-COVID middle → sharp inversion at "Then it inverted, fast" → builds to the densest but most powerful close in the book.

---

## Overall Assessment

This coheres, and it coheres at a level rare for a chronological history: every chapter states a thesis in its opening paragraph, dramatizes it through a mechanism or a human scene, and hands forward to the next chapter with a deliberate hook — the Xiaogang fingerprints → Zhao under house arrest → Deng's southbound train → "the man who undid it" chain is real and load-bearing, not decorative. The voice is consistent throughout (informed, dry, morally serious without preaching) and the contested-claims discipline is not a tic but a recurring structural move that builds trust and is itself part of the argument. The strongest chapters (2, 5, 8, 9, 11, 15) are genuinely excellent narrative history; even the weakest (7, 10, 12, 14) have strong bones and superb closes — there is no WEAK or REWRITE chapter in the book.

The single recurring weakness, and the one improvement to prioritize, is the **portmanteau chapter**: Chapters 7, 10, 12, and 14 each cover three-to-five discrete topics and default to mosaic/inventory in the middle before a strong narrative close rescues them. The fix is consistent across all four — each already contains an obvious human-scene spine being underused (SOE layoffs in 7; Bo Xilai in 10, which is delayed two-thirds of the way in; Jack Ma in 12, named but not deployed; Meng Wanzhou in 14, confined to a sentence). Lead with the person, demote the inventory to the conditions around them, and these four move from GOOD to STRONG. The secondary, smaller note is a tendency to append a necessary-but-deflating coda *past* the chapter's emotional peak (the one-child reversal after Chapter 11's series-spanning succession close is the clearest case); relocate those seeds earlier so the biggest sentence is the last sentence. Neither issue is structural failure — they are the difference between a very good book and a great one.

## Part 1: Forward Findings (new TL gaps)

### Chapter 1 — Picking Up the Pieces

1. **"the Chinese party studied that collapse obsessively and drew the lesson that you never, ever pull the founding thread"** — The Soviet-collapse contrast is stated but the comparison is underdeveloped here and Chapter 6 carries the real weight; flag that the deeper, more illuminating contrast available is not the USSR but **Korea and Taiwan**, the two cases where an authoritarian developmental state *did* democratize from within without dissolving (Korean Modern Ch 5, the June 1987 democratization). The book's whole thesis ("economic reform first, no democracy") would be sharpened by naming, once, the East Asian counter-cases where the sequence ran the other way and the state survived.
2. **"Deng's own version was earthier and became the slogan of the age: 'seek truth from facts'"** — A "meanwhile" opportunity: the pivot from ideology-first to results-first legitimation is precisely the Meiji oligarchs' move (the Charter Oath's "knowledge shall be sought throughout the world," Meiji Japan Ch 1) and the Korean developmental state's explicit rejection of both Communist and free-market doctrine for "what works" (Korean Modern Ch 3, Park's "third alternative"). Deng's pragmatism is the East Asian developmental template's founding epistemology, not a Chinese novelty — worth one cross-civ sentence.

### Chapter 2 — The Household Responsibility System

3. **"as a functioning electoral democracy with a free press, it never had a famine remotely on this scale"** — This is the book's own recurring India contrast, and the famine/democracy point is made *in the reference book* (Chinese Revolution Ch 12 explicitly: "India suffered serious food crises in the same era. But as a functioning electoral democracy... it never had a famine remotely on this scale"). Chapter 2's commune-famine passage cites only Chinese Revolution Ch 12–13 and should also forward the India contrast (Modern India Ch 6/Ch 7, the Green Revolution and the end of recurrent famine) — it is the single sharpest test of the book's two-roads thesis and it is left implicit here.

### Chapter 3 — Special Economic Zones and the Open Door

4. **"it took Song-dynasty China centuries to build its commercial revolution, Meiji Japan roughly forty years to industrialize by state decree (Meiji Japan Ch 4), and South Korea about a generation to go from war ruin to industrial power (Korean Modern Ch 3)"** — Citations VERIFIED CORRECT (Meiji Japan Ch 4 = "Industrial Revolution by Decree"; Korean Modern Ch 3 = "Park Chung-hee and the Miracle on the Han," explicitly "poorer than Ghana... by 1979 a middle-income industrial economy"). This is the book's best cross-civ passage. One missing comparand: the SEZ "import an industrial economy that already existed next door" mechanism has a precise parallel in Korea's chaebol borrowing the Japanese/Manchukuo developmental model wholesale (Korean Modern Ch 3, "the third wave of the Japanese-developed state-led developmentalist model") — Shenzhen importing Hong Kong's economy is the same move as Park importing Manchukuo's; worth flagging the structural rhyme.
5. **"India had democracy first and a slow, hesitant, half-reformed economy until 1991 (Modern India Ch 6 and Ch 10); China had economic reform first and no democracy at all"** — Citations VERIFIED CORRECT (Modern India Ch 6 = Nehru's planned economy / "Hindu rate of growth"; Ch 10 = 1991 Liberalization / Manmohan's reforms). This is the book's central recurring contrast and is well-placed. Strengthen: Modern India Ch 10 makes the precise point that India's 1991 reforms "had no domestic political champions" and were forced by a near-default — the mirror image of China's reforms being forced from below by starving farmers (Chapter 2). The two reform-origin stories (crisis-from-above vs. crime-from-below) are a stronger contrast than the bare "democracy first vs. reform first" framing.

### Chapter 5 — Tiananmen 1989

6. **"the People's Liberation Army... was sent into the center of its own capital to clear it by force"** — Strong "meanwhile" gap: the single most illuminating contrast to June Fourth is South Korea **two years earlier**.

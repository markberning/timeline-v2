## Chapter 1 — Before Independence: Văn Lang, Âu Lạc, and a Thousand Years of China

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Before Vietnam existed, a thousand years as a Chinese province forged a people who learned the conqueror's entire civilization in order to refuse to be it.

**What works:**
- The opening is a clinic: "Start with a river and a number." Concrete, propulsive, immediately tells the reader what to hold onto, and the number ("a thousand years") becomes the chapter's spine.
- The thesis is stated with real force and a memorable formulation — "It resisted China by out-Chinaing China, on its own terms." That single sentence makes the whole book's argument legible.
- The "honest caveat goes up front" move ("We have their drums and their rice paddies and almost no words") is emotionally evocative rather than throat-clearing — it makes absence feel like a fact you can touch.

**What doesn't work:**
- The long Khmer parenthetical at the end of the "operating system" paragraph (lines 45) is the chapter's only real sag — it interrupts the most important argumentative beat with a comparative aside and a chapter cross-ref. Fix: cut to a single clause or move the comparison to its own short sentence after the thesis lands.
- The closing question ("whether the Vietnamese could keep what China had dropped") is good, but the run of failed revolts just before it (Lý Bí, Mai Thúc Loan, Phùng Hưng) is a compressed list that briefly reads as catalog. Fix: keep one named revolt vivid, demote the rest to a single rhythmic clause (it nearly does this already — tighten).

**Momentum map:** Strong start, strong thesis mid-chapter, slight dip into the Khmer parenthetical and the revolt list, recovers fully on the closing hook.

## Chapter 2 — Bạch Đằng 938 and the Ngô–Đinh–Early Lê Founding

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Independence was won in a single brilliant afternoon at Bạch Đằng — and then nearly lost, because winning a war and building a state are different problems.

**What works:**
- The chapter announces its own structural argument up front ("independence is easy to win in an afternoon and very hard to keep for a decade") and then delivers exactly that, which makes the "so what?" test trivially passable.
- The battle is told as a piece of clockwork — stakes, lure, tide, collapse — with genuine narrative tension and a great planted payoff: "Hold the stakes in your mind. They come back." That is a craft move that earns Chapter 6.
- "a marriage of children and a massacre of cousins" — no, that's Ch 5; here the equivalent is the "emperor at home and tributary abroad" double-register, explained crisply as a working machine, not a static fact.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Đinh→Lê Hoàn stretch in the final section moves fast through three regime changes (assassination, Song threat, new general, second Bạch Đằng) and the prose compresses to summary. The emotional temperature drops after the dazzling battle. Fix: pick one human stake in the Đinh collapse (the assassinated emperor and heir) and let it breathe for two sentences before resuming the march.
- The Early Lê / Later Lê naming flag, while necessary, lands mid-paragraph and stalls momentum. Fix: it's parenthetical housekeeping — keep it but shorten so it doesn't interrupt the "stakes still worked" beat.

**Momentum map:** Peak energy in the Bạch Đằng set-piece and the planted payoff; controlled descent through the founding-state material; solid forward-looking close.

## Chapter 3 — The Lý Dynasty: Thăng Long and a Buddhist State

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Vietnam's first secure dynasty stopped hiding and started building — and unknowingly planted the Confucian seed (the Temple of Literature) that would eventually devour the Buddhist state that planted it.

**What works:**
- The capital-move framing is excellent narrative compression: "a psychological statement cast as an urban-planning decision: Đại Việt was no longer a besieged camp. The dragon was ascending." It turns an administrative fact into a turning point.
- The book-level long arc is set up with a directive that respects the reader: "watch the Temple of Literature, not the One Pillar Pagoda, to see where Vietnam is going." This is the kind of pointer that builds a book rather than a chapter.
- The dikes paragraph rescues itself from "unglamorous physical work" by making it a stakes argument ("the dynasty that kept the dikes kept the harvest, and the dynasty that kept the harvest kept the throne").

**What doesn't work:**
- The Temple-of-Literature sentence (line 73) is one of the longest in the book and does three jobs at once (the founding, the irony, the four-century forecast). It is brilliant but breath-defeating. Fix: split into two sentences — let the forecast land as its own short, ominous line.
- Minor redundancy risk: the "imported Chinese parts assembled for Vietnamese purposes" idea is now stated in Ch 1, 2, and 3. It's the book's thesis so repetition is partly deliberate, but here the phrasing is close enough to Ch 1 to feel like a refrain rather than a development. Fix: advance it ("now they build with the tools, not just survive with them") rather than restate it.

**Momentum map:** Steady, confident build; the energy concentrates in the capital move and the Temple-of-Literature forecast; gentle handoff to the war chapter.

## Chapter 4 — The Lý–Song War and the Cham Frontier

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Vietnam's permanent strategic shape is set here — a parent to the north that must be deterred, a rival to the south that will be devoured — through one audacious pre-emptive war and the opening of the long Cham campaign.

**What works:**
- The two-frontiers thesis is a genuinely clarifying structural frame and pays off across the whole book; the "civilizational parent / culturally alien rival" pairing is sharp.
- The pre-emptive strike on Yongzhou is given its due as audacity ("A small state pre-emptively invading a superpower... is a remarkable thing"), and the doctrine is articulated as behavior: "you stop the Chinese army, you do not humiliate the Chinese empire, and then you send the polite tribute mission."

**What doesn't work:**
- The Champa section (line 89) is the chapter's momentum sink: it turns into a linguistics lecture (Austronesian vs. Austroasiatic, Madagascar to Easter Island) plus a dense Khmer cross-ref parenthetical. The information is load-bearing for Ch 11 but here it stops the narrative cold mid-chapter. Fix: deliver the language-family point in one vivid sentence ("as far apart as English and a Malay sailor's speech" already does the work) and move the full treatment to Ch 11, where it is restated anyway — this is genuine redundancy with line 197.
- The chapter ends on a quiet administrative beat (Vân Đồn trade, then the Lý-to-Trần transfer) rather than the war's emotional peak. The "Nam quốc sơn hà" recitation is the chapter's most charged moment and it's buried in the middle. Fix: consider closing nearer the poem's defiance, then the dynastic handoff as a brisk coda.

**Momentum map:** Strong on the northern war; pronounced sag in the Champa linguistics block; flat administrative landing.

## Chapter 5 — The Trần Dynasty and the Three Mongol Invasions

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A dynasty that took power by a children's marriage and a massacre then defeated the largest empire in history — by trading space for time and letting the country itself fight the enemy.

**What works:**
- "Hold both halves together: a marriage of children and a massacre of cousins. That is a Vietnamese dynastic transfer." Vivid, morally unflinching, and it characterizes a whole political culture in one line.
- The Mongol stakes are sold by comparison ("the only contemporaries who reliably stopped them were the Mamluks of Egypt, the sea... and this small delta kingdom"), which makes "three times" land with the awe it deserves.
- The 1258 strategy is explained as a repeatable mechanism and explicitly rhymed back to Ngô Quyền ("the same logic, at the scale of a whole nation") — the book teaching the reader its own pattern.

**What doesn't work:**
- The final section does a lot at once: Diên Hồng, the Hịch tướng sĩ, the introduction-and-deferral of Trần Hưng Đạo, the 1285 counter-offensives (three battle names in a clause), the national history, and chữ Nôm. It is the most overloaded passage in the chapter and tips toward catalog. Fix: the three battle names (Hàm Tử, Chương Dương, Tây Kết) can collapse to one plus "and others"; let Diên Hồng and the Proclamation carry the emotion.
- Deferring Trần Hưng Đạo "because he gets his own chapter" is structurally honest but slightly deflates the 1285 climax — the reader meets the hero and is told to wait. Fix: give him one decisive sentence of agency here so 1285 has a face, then defer the afterlife.

**Momentum map:** Powerful open (the coup), rising awe (Mongol scale), strong template chapter (1258); the 1285 close is information-dense and slightly diffuses.

## Chapter 6 — Trần Hưng Đạo, the Bạch Đằng Stakes, and the High Trần

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Vietnam beat the Mongols a third time by consciously reenacting the 938 founding battle on the same river — proof-text of the national idea — and then the High Trần rotted from within.

**What works:**
- The payoff planted in Ch 2 is collected with real satisfaction: "the single most celebrated victory in Vietnamese history precisely because it rhymes." The book's central craft bet (the recurring stakes) pays off exactly here, and the chapter knows it.
- The deified-general beat is handled with a fresh, precise hook: "Most divinized figures in world history are legendary or ancient; this one has a battle record you can date to the year." That reframes a familiar fact into something arresting.
- "the long, instructive comedown" is named as such and delivered — the chapter is honest that decline is the engine, and the Chế Bồng Nga reversal ("For a decade the predator was the prey") gives the comedown genuine drama rather than a list of weaknesses.

**What doesn't work:**
- Two-part structure strain: the Bạch Đằng/deification half and the Trúc Lâm/Chế Bồng Nga/late-Trần half are almost two chapters. The Trúc Lâm paragraph (the Buddhist high-water mark) is sandwiched between the war climax and the Cham crisis and reads as an inserted topic rather than a beat in the arc. Fix: tie Trúc Lâm to the decline argument explicitly (the spiritual peak as the dynasty's last unified moment) so it earns its place in the descent.
- The closing sentence loads three forecasts onto Hồ Quý Ly ("seize the throne, lose the country to China, and set up the greatest war of liberation") plus a tonal promise ("the darkest and then the most triumphant"). Effective but slightly over-sold. Fix: trust the cliffhanger; trim one clause.

**Momentum map:** Climactic open (the rhyming victory), arresting middle (the god), strong dramatic decline (predator/prey), heavily forecasted close.

## Chapter 7 — The Ming Occupation and Lê Lợi's War of Liberation

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The one thing the book swore couldn't happen — China reconquered Vietnam and tried to erase its memory — and a single generation lost the country and won it back.

**What works:**
- The opening delivers maximum stakes by inverting the book's own premise: "This chapter contains the one thing the first six chapters insisted could not happen." This is the strongest chapter opening in the book after Ch 1.
- The book-burning is built to a deliberate climax and then explicitly cashes the running technique: "The hedging in Chapters 1–6 is not authorial caution. It is the measurable shadow of this occupation." This retroactively re-charges every earlier hedge — a genuine book-level structural payoff.
- The Hồ citadel detail ("the most impressive monument of the era, raised by a regime that barely outlived its mortar") is a perfectly weighted ironic image.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Nguyễn-surname disambiguation parenthetical (line 137) is long and lands in the middle of the Nguyễn Trãi introduction — exactly the wrong place, because it interrupts the emotional arrival of the book's tragic hero with onomastic bookkeeping. Fix: move the surname warning to the end of the section or into a footnote-style aside; keep the hero's entrance clean.
- The liberation campaign (Tốt Động, Chi Lăng–Xương Giang) is told slightly faster than the occupation's horror, so the triumph is a touch thinner than the catastrophe. Fix: one concrete scene of the guerrilla low point ("repeatedly almost destroyed, surviving in the mountains") expanded would make the turn feel earned rather than narrated.

**Momentum map:** Among the book's best — premise-inverting open, building dread, a major payoff at the book-burning, a fast but stirring liberation, strong forward hook to the price in Ch 8.

## Chapter 8 — Lê Thánh Tông and the Confucian Golden Age

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** The golden age is also the chapter where the Confucian bureaucracy completes its four-century conquest of the state — and the dynasty's machine executes the hero who saved it.

**What works:**
- The framing thesis is the book's best recurring idea sharpened to a point: "Every golden age in this book is also the chapter where you watch the thing that will eventually kill the dynasty get installed at its center."
- Opening on the Nguyễn Trãi execution rather than the glory ("Open with the price, because it sets the tone") is a sophisticated structural choice that gives the golden age a tragic shadow from the first line.
- The long arc is closed with a single legible trace: "One Pillar Pagoda in 1049 to Confucian thirteen-province bureaucracy in 1466 — and you have the deep structural story of Đại Việt's interior." The book is keeping its promises.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Lê Thánh Tông "reign of systems" paragraph (line 149) is the clearest encyclopedia-dump in the book: provinces, the Hồng Đức Code, cadastral survey, Hồng Đức maps, the Tao Đàn, the Complete Annals — six institutions in one breath, each given a clause. It is informative but inert; the reader cannot say what the reign *felt* like, only what it produced. Fix: choose two (the Code's women's rights — which the book itself flags as load-bearing for Ch 13 — and the Complete Annals as the source of the whole book) and develop them; demote the rest to a fast list with a single governing idea ("statecraft as information").
- The Vijaya 1471 ending is the right forward hook but is compressed almost to a dateline because it's deferred to Ch 11. The chapter therefore ends on a pointer rather than a scene. Fix: a one-sentence concrete image of the destruction would give the chapter an emotional close instead of a cross-reference.

**Momentum map:** Strong tragic open, strong thesis; a flat institutional plateau in the middle (the systems list); a deferred, dateline-ish close.

## Chapter 9 — The Mạc Usurpation and the Lê Restoration

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Vietnam splits here and never fully reunites — the puppet-emperor fiction is born, and one man's flight south plants the seed of a second Vietnam.

**What works:**
- The chapter states its real significance immediately and correctly: "it never really stops being split for the next three centuries — the partition... is not an aberration, it is the new normal that starts here." That makes a confusing dynastic mess legible as a turning point.
- The Nguyễn Hoàng section is the chapter's emotional core and is framed with genuine narrative weight: "the single most consequential personnel decision in early modern Vietnamese history... planted in 1558 by a man just trying to survive his relatives." Human motive driving structural change — exactly the book's best mode.
- The "constitutional lie everyone agreed to" framing of the puppet Lê is vivid political writing and sets up Ch 10 cleanly.

**What doesn't work:**
- The first half (Mạc Đăng Dung, the 65-year civil war, Nguyễn Kim, Trịnh Kiểm, the 1592 capture, the Cao Bằng remnant) is a dense relay of names and successions with little scene or stake — the chapter's momentum doesn't ignite until the "Two things... and they are both about the south" pivot. Fix: compress the dynastic mechanics ruthlessly so the chapter can reach Nguyễn Hoàng sooner; the reader needs the *pattern* (puppet + warlord), not every general's name.
- Mild redundancy: the puppet-emperor fiction is explained at length here and then re-explained at the top of Ch 10 (line 177–179). Fix: state it fully once (here) and let Ch 10 reference it in a clause.

**Momentum map:** Slow, name-dense first half; sharp ignition at the "two things about the south" pivot; strong, thematically charged close.

## Chapter 10 — The Trịnh–Nguyễn Partition: Two Vietnams

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** For two centuries Vietnam was two fully formed states pretending to be one under the same powerless emperor — and the cultural divergence that created still runs through Vietnamese life.

**What works:**
- The opening states the strangeness plainly and makes it the hook: "For roughly two centuries there were two Vietnams." Then the Tonkin/Cochinchina, Đàng Ngoài/Đàng Trong mapping is delivered with a memorable clincher: "Same fictional sovereign, two real governments, one border."
- The Đồng Hới walls passage answers the "why did it last?" question with concrete causation ("fortifications deep and well-gunned enough to stop the larger Trịnh armies cold") rather than assertion — good explanatory writing.
- The "Vietnamese civil war was fought, in part, with European artillery, because by the seventeenth century Vietnam was a node in a global system" reframe lifts the chapter out of local dynastic detail into a bigger argument.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Quốc ngữ paragraph (line 187) carries the chapter's most consequential long-term point but is delivered somewhat as a discrete information block bracketed by a parenthetical bio of de Rhodes. The chapter's two "deepest consequence" items (script, commerce) are presented as a numbered list ("The first is... The second is...") which is clear but slightly mechanical for the book's otherwise fluid voice. Fix: dramatize the irony already latent in the prose ("For three centuries it was a niche missionary tool. It is now the everyday writing system of one hundred million people") — that contrast is the scene; build the paragraph around it rather than around the list scaffolding.
- The ending stacks the forward hook ("It was moving — relentlessly... and that movement, the nam tiến, is the largest single fact") on top of the already-stated divergence point; two big claims compete for the last beat. Fix: pick the nam tiến hook and let the divergence point close the prior section.

**Momentum map:** Strong, clarifying open; concrete and energetic through the war and the artillery reframe; slightly list-driven in the consequences section; double-loaded close.

## Chapter 11 — The Nam Tiến: the Southward March and the End of Champa

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The single most important thing in this book is not a battle or a dynasty but a direction — an 800-mile southward migration that swallowed a whole civilization and gave Vietnam its dragon shape.

**What works:**
- The "step back from the dynastic narrative" framing is the right structural move and is announced with confidence: "the most important thing that happened to Vietnam in this entire book is not a battle or a dynasty. It is a direction." The chapter earns its existence in two sentences.
- The shape-as-fossil image is excellent popular-history writing: "a thin, 1,000-mile ribbon of coast wrapped around a mountain spine... That shape is a fossil of this migration."
- The chapter has a point of view and the courage to state it: "This is, bluntly, the disappearance of a civilization, and the honest history names it as that rather than as the tidy 'unification' of nationalist retelling." Moral clarity without sermonizing.

**What doesn't work:**
- Genuine redundancy with Ch 4: the Austronesian/Austroasiatic, "as far apart as English and a Malay sailor" language-family material is delivered nearly verbatim in both chapters (lines 89 and 197). The book even says "restated here because it is the moral and analytical core" — which is an admission that Ch 4 over-delivered it. Fix: thin Ch 4 to a one-line forward promise and let the full treatment live here, where it is structurally home.
- The "long ooze" section (line 201) packs Saigon/Prey Nokor, the 1679 Ming refugees, Mạc Cửu/Hà Tiên, and the 1757 cession into a fast sequence of mechanisms. It's well-chosen detail but reads as a list of acquisitions; the cumulative human meaning (a frontier society being assembled) only arrives in the closing paragraph. Fix: lead the section with the "settlement, accretion, opportunism" thesis so the examples feel like evidence, not inventory.

**Momentum map:** Outstanding thesis-driven open; the Champa-end section is morally charged and strong; the "long ooze" mechanics flatten into list; the close (the doubled country, the coming earthquake) recovers the energy.

## Chapter 12 — The Tây Sơn Rebellion

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A peasant tax revolt became the earthquake that destroyed every authority in the book — Nguyễn lords, Trịnh lords, the Lê dynasty — crushed Siam and Qing China, and then collapsed with its one genius.

**What works:**
- The opening sells scale and motion at once: "the thirty-year convulsion that destroyed, in turn, the Nguyễn lords, the Trịnh lords, and the Lê dynasty itself — every legitimate and illegitimate authority the previous nine chapters built." The reader feels the book's own structure being demolished.
- The Ngọc Hồi–Đống Đa victory is given a single unforgettable image — "the troops reportedly still wearing the branches they had camouflaged the night march with" — immediately followed by the honest source caveat, which is the book's voice at its best.
- The Quang Trung arc has a real tragic shape: the most capable ruler in centuries, the sudden death at forty, the state "built almost entirely on one man's genius" unraveling. Emotional engagement is high and earned.

**What doesn't work:**
- The "Bạch Đằng of the modern era" framing (line 217) is apt but it explicitly competes with Ch 6's claim that the *1288* Bạch Đằng "rhymes" as the proof-text. The book now has two "the set-piece the culture returns to" victories making nearly the same argument. Fix: differentiate the claims — 1288 as the *rhyme* (continuity), Ngọc Hồi as the *unity-in-fracture* proof — the prose gestures at this ("however internally broken") but should sharpen it so the second instance doesn't feel like a repeat of Ch 6's rhetorical move.
- The middle relay (1777 Nguyễn lordship destroyed → 1785 Rạch Gầm → 1786 Trịnh smashed → 1789 Lê flees) moves at near-list speed; the brothers as people barely register before Nguyễn Huệ becomes "the brilliant middle brother." Fix: one beat of who these three actually were would make the human engine of the largest upheaval in the book visible rather than asserted.

**Momentum map:** Explosive open, slightly list-paced conquest relay in the middle, a brilliant set-piece (the branches) and a genuinely tragic Quang Trung close.

## Chapter 13 — Gia Long, the Nguyễn Founding, and the Name "Vietnam"

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The fugitive who survived twenty-five years of defeat unified the whole stretched country, gave it the name it still carries — and seeded the French conquest that would end its independence.

**What works:**
- The "what it completed / what it named / what it doomed" tripartite structure is announced in the opening and executed cleanly — a short closing chapter that knows exactly its three jobs. The "so what?" test passes effortlessly.
- The ending of the *book*, not just the chapter, is landed with control: "1804 is not a battle or a fall but a naming... the thousand-year arc that began with stakes in the Bạch Đằng river in 938 closes with a word." That is a real ending, ringing the opening image of the whole narrative.
- The Huế citadel image fuses the book's central theme into one object: "a European military geometry executed by Vietnamese labor — the two civilizations fused in one wall." Theme as artifact.
- The refusal to end on triumph ("an honest history does not end on the triumph") is the right call and gives the book a melancholy, forward-leaning final note rather than a victory lap.

**What doesn't work:**
- The final paragraph (line 235) restates the book's whole thesis in full ("Đại Việt spent a thousand years proving a single proposition...") and then re-lists the victories (Song, Mongols three times, Ming, Qing). After the elegant "closes with a word" ending two paragraphs earlier, this is a second ending — a thesis reprise that slightly dilutes the better, quieter close. Fix: trim the recap; the "closes with a word" beat and the Huế-wall image have already proved the thesis. Trust them.
- "the French seed" / French-conquest forecast appears in both the doom paragraph and the final paragraph. One statement of the coming French story is a perfect dark coda; two is a repeat. Fix: keep it in the doom section, drop it from the last lines.

**Momentum map:** Tight, purposeful structure throughout; the true emotional peak is the "closes with a word" paragraph; the final thesis-reprise paragraph is the only soft spot — a strong ending followed by a redundant one.

## Overall assessment

This is a genuinely strong book-length narrative with one of the clearest controlling arguments in the project — "Đại Việt resisted China by out-Chinaing China" — and it is disciplined about paying that thesis off in almost every chapter (the Temple of Literature arc from Ch 3 to Ch 8, the Bạch Đằng stakes planted in Ch 2 and collected in Ch 6, the documentary-hedging technique explained and retroactively justified in Ch 7). The voice is consistent, confident, and unafraid of a point of view — the Champa "disappearance of a civilization" passage and the refusal to end on triumph are the marks of a real editor's sensibility, not a list of facts. The chapters genuinely build: the book teaches the reader its own patterns (the dynastic transfer, trade-space-for-time, golden-age-as-poison) and then trusts them, which is rare and valuable. The single recurring weakness is the mid-chapter encyclopedia plateau — Ch 8's "reign of systems" list, Ch 9's name-dense first half, Ch 11's "long ooze," Ch 5's three-battles-in-a-clause — where the prose stops dramatizing and starts cataloguing, almost always because material is being delivered as inventory rather than as evidence for a stated idea. The single biggest improvement: hunt every multi-item institutional/battle list, lead it with the governing idea so the items read as proof, and cut the most-repeated cross-chapter material to one home (the Austronesian/Austroasiatic block is delivered twice nearly verbatim, and the book's thesis is fully restated three times — Ch 1, Ch 13 close, and again in the final paragraph). Tighten those plateaus and trim the doubled endings of Ch 13, and this is a STRONG book throughout with no weak chapters.

## Part 1: Forward Findings (new TL gaps)

### Chapter 1 — Before Independence: Văn Lang, Âu Lạc, and a Thousand Years of China

1. **"in 111 BCE the Han dynasty of China — then at its expansionist height under Emperor Wu, the Martial Emperor (Han Dynasty Chapter 3) — destroyed Nanyue and annexed the Red River delta outright"** — The cross-ref is present and correct, but it is one-sided: it does not convey that the same 111 BCE campaign sent 100,000 Han troops south, that Han texts treat Nanyue's conquest as the act that opened the Maritime Silk Road to the South China Sea, and that the Red River delta became "Jiaozhou/Jiaozhi" — the Chinese administrative frame Vietnam spent a millennium inside. A one-clause addition tying the conquest to Han's wider Emperor Wu expansionism (Korea-to-Vietnam in the same reign, per Han Dynasty Ch 3) would let the reader see Vietnam as one prong of the largest expansionist push in Chinese history rather than an isolated annexation.

2. **"In 40 CE the Trưng Sisters (≈ "chung"), Trưng Trắc and Trưng Nhị, led a revolt that briefly expelled the Han ... before the Chinese general Ma Yuan crushed it three years later"** — This is told purely from the Vietnamese side. The Han narrative (Ch 6) tells the *same* event from the Chinese side in detail — Ma Yuan, age 60, his 20,000 troops and fleet, the "sixty-five cities" liberated, his "die wrapped in horsehide" idiom — and explicitly flags that the suppression "extended Han cultural influence deeper into Southeast Asia." A cross-ref to Han Dynasty Ch 6 belongs here: the founding image of Vietnamese defiance is, in the Chinese sources, a celebrated frontier-pacification campaign, and the dual framing is itself the point.

3. **"They brought their administrative geography, their taxes, their court ritual ... the Vietnamese elite learned the conqueror's civilization thoroughly"** — Strong existing comparison to the Khmer's *opposite* (voluntary, never-conquered) Indianization mechanism. This is correct and well-placed. No change needed — flag it only as the model the rest of the narrative should match.

### Chapter 2 — Bạch Đằng 938 and the Ngô–Đinh–Early Lê Founding

4. **"the Southern Han, one of the regional Chinese states that had carved up the collapsed Tang empire"** and **"the Song dynasty — the great dynasty that had just reunified China (Tang & Song China Chapter 5)"** — The Song cross-ref is correct and well-dated (the Song reunification 960–979 maps onto Tang-Song Ch 5). But the Southern Han is left as a bare label. Tang-Song Ch 5 covers the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms fragmentation that produced exactly these southern regional states; a half-clause cross-ref ("one of the Ten Kingdoms of the post-Tang fragmentation; Tang & Song China Chapter 5") would anchor the reader and reinforce that Vietnam won independence precisely in China's window of disunity.

5. **"in 981 he defeated the Song invasion on land and, again, in a second battle at Bạch Đằng"** — The Early Lê war with the Song is a "first/only" type claim (a small state defeating the newly reunified Song by force) with no cross-civ anchor. Tang-Song Ch 5–6 establishes the Northern Song as the dominant reunified power; noting that Đại Cồ Việt repelled the same empire that had just absorbed all of southern China by negotiated submission sharpens the contrast (the south of China submitted; the Red River delta did not).

### Chapter 4 — The Lý–Song War and the Cham Frontier

6. **"the Northern Song, under the reformist minister Wang Anshi, was preparing to invade Đại Việt"** — Wang Anshi is named without a cross-ref, but he is a major figure in Tang-Song Ch 6 ("Wang Anshi vs. Sima Guang"). The 1075–77 Lý–Song war is one of the few times the narrative can tie a Vietnamese event to a specifically dated Chinese domestic episode (the New Policies reform era).

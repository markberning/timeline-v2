## Story Editor evaluation (Persona D)

### Chapter 1 — Out of the Dark Ages

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Greece clawed its way back from civilizational collapse, and the three innovations it produced during re-emergence (alphabet, polis, colonization) were more consequential than anything the Mycenaean palaces had achieved.

**What works:**
- "Greece woke up slowly" is a killer three-word opening. It sets pace and tone immediately.
- The "firsts" disclaimer box is perfectly placed — it calibrates the reader before the narrative starts claiming Greek firsts, which is honest and builds trust.
- The alphabet section is excellent: the mechanism (repurposing consonant signs for vowels) is explained concretely, and the significance is proportional to the detail.
- The polis section nails the "what made it different" question by comparing it to every alternative governance model in the ancient world. The reader understands *why* this matters without being lectured.
- Delphi and the Olympics are handled as unifying institutions in a fragmented world — a smart structural choice that gives the reader a framework for the rest of the book.
- The Croesus anecdote is perfectly deployed: a one-sentence punch line that makes the Oracle memorable.

**What doesn't work:**
- The colonization section, while comprehensive, reads slightly as a catalog: city after city listed with founding dates. The mechanism (why people left) is covered, but the experience (what it felt like to found Massalia) is not. A single scene or human detail would lift this section.
- The Eleusinian Mysteries appear twice — once in a full paragraph near the end and once as a back-reference in Chapter 2. The Chapter 1 treatment is fine, but it's a lot of real estate for something the narrative won't return to in depth.

**Momentum map:** Explosive opening → strong build through alphabet and polis → slight plateau in colonization catalog → good recovery with Olympics and Delphi → excellent closing hook ("All of this was prelude"). No sag.

### Chapter 2 — Tyrants and Lawmakers

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The tension between aristocratic power and popular demand produced two centuries of political experimentation, culminating in Athens inventing democracy and Sparta perfecting a military state.

**What works:**
- The hoplite phalanx as the engine of political change is a brilliant structural insight — the military revolution creates the political revolution. The reader *gets* why merchants who stood in the battle line wanted a vote.
- Peisistratos riding in with a woman dressed as Athena is a perfect anecdote — it humanizes tyranny and makes the reader laugh.
- The Solon section is superb. The four wealth tiers are explained clearly with pronunciations, and Solon's self-assessment ("the best they would accept, not the best he could imagine") is a genuinely moving line.
- Cleisthenes' tribe reform is explained mechanically (city + coast + countryside = cross-section), not just by result. The reader can picture *how* it broke aristocratic networks.
- The "how radical was this" paragraph — comparing Athens 508 BCE to every other civilization on earth — is the best single paragraph in the chapter.
- Sparta is vivid and efficiently handled. The agoge, the syssitia, the Spartan women section — all concrete and memorable.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Ionian philosophers at the end (Thales through Pythagoras) feel like they belong in a different chapter. The chapter is titled "Tyrants and Lawmakers" and its arc is political, but the last two pages pivot to natural philosophy, theater origins (Thespis), Sappho, and Greek religion. It's all interesting, but the chapter loses its structural coherence after the Sparta section.
- The Greek religion paragraph at the very end is a dump of information (twelve Olympians, mystery cults, no bible, no creed) without a narrative hook. It reads like a setup paragraph that should have opened Chapter 4 or been integrated into Chapter 1's cultural infrastructure section.

**Momentum map:** Strong opening problem statement → excellent Peisistratos/Draco/Solon/Cleisthenes arc → strong Sparta section → momentum diffuses in the philosophy/arts/religion coda. The chapter would be tighter if it ended after Sparta and the philosophical material moved to Chapter 4 or a standalone culture chapter.

### Chapter 3 — The Persian Wars

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Persia came to punish Athens and then to conquer Greece; the Greeks won by fighting in narrow places where quality beat quantity, and the victory created the confidence that fueled everything that followed.

**What works:**
- The Ionian Revolt setup is efficient: Aristagoras' reckless gamble, Cleomenes' "how far is it?" refusal, the burning of Sardis, Darius' "remember the Athenians" order — all tight and dramatic.
- Marathon is brilliantly narrated. The double envelopment is explained clearly, the Pheidippides legend is properly debunked, and the psychological significance is given proportional weight.
- The marathon-race etymological aside ("invented for the 1896 Olympics") is exactly the kind of detail this audience loves.
- Thermopylae is handled with appropriate reverence and critical distance. The Simonides epitaph is deployed at the right moment and the single-word Greek analysis (*peithomenoi*) is a genuine insight.
- Salamis is superbly narrated: Themistocles' decision to build the fleet, the evacuation of Athens, the tactical trap in the narrows. The "most consequential democratic decision in history" line about the fleet vote is earned.
- The nuance paragraph at the end ("This was not a clash between Western freedom and Eastern despotism") is exactly right — it prevents the reader from walking away with a Victorian caricature while still honoring the significance.

**What doesn't work:**
- Very little. The chapter is the strongest in the book. The only minor issue is that the Plataea/Mycale section feels slightly rushed compared to the Marathon and Salamis set-pieces. Plataea was a massive battle and gets one paragraph.

**Momentum map:** Relentless forward momentum from first sentence to last. The chapter builds through three battles (Marathon → Thermopylae → Salamis) with rising stakes at each stage, then lands the significance cleanly. This is the template chapter.

### Chapter 4 — The Golden Age of Athens

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Athens used imperial tribute to fund the most concentrated burst of cultural production in human history, and the contradiction between democratic ideals and imperial exploitation was built into the foundations.

**What works:**
- The Delian League to empire transition is handled with perfect pacing: the "lumps of metal into the sea" oath, Aristides the Just, then the gradual slide into coercion (Naxos, Thasos, treasury transfer).
- "Within twenty years, the voluntary alliance had become an Athenian protection racket" is a great pivot sentence.
- The Parthenon section is excellent — the optical refinements (columns leaning inward, floor curving upward) give the reader something they probably didn't know, and the gold-weight detail (2,500 pounds) makes imperial expenditure concrete.
- The tragedy section handles three playwrights without becoming a catalog. Each gets a signature work and a one-line thesis: Aeschylus = vendetta to law, Sophocles = divine vs. human authority, Euripides = psychology and doubt. Efficient and memorable.
- Aristophanes is handled with appropriate irreverence.
- The Socrates section is economical but powerful. The Socratic method is explained as a philosophical position, not just a teaching trick.
- Thucydides' Melian Dialogue quote ("the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must") is perfectly placed — it foreshadows Chapter 5.
- The Funeral Oration closing is devastating: "It was a beautiful speech. It was also delivered on the eve of a war that Athens would lose."

**What doesn't work:**
- The architecture catalog in the middle (Erechtheion, Propylaea, Temple of Athena Nike, Odeon, Long Walls) reads like a guidebook tour. After the Parthenon's rich detail, these buildings get one sentence each. Either cut some or give at least one more the Parthenon treatment.
- Democritus and Empedocles feel slightly orphaned. They're important thinkers but they get one sentence each in a paragraph that's really about Anaxagoras and Socrates. Either expand or acknowledge the compression.

**Momentum map:** Strong empire-building opening → excellent Parthenon peak → slight catalog sag (minor buildings) → strong recovery with tragedy → strong intellectual section → devastating close. Very good.

### Chapter 5 — The Peloponnesian War

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The war Athens started out of imperial arrogance destroyed everything the golden age had built, and the self-destructive pattern was visible from the beginning to anyone willing to look.

**What works:**
- The Thucydides quote on the war's "real cause" is perfectly deployed as the chapter's thesis statement.
- Pericles' strategy (abandon the countryside, survive behind the walls, win at sea) is explained with genuine tactical clarity. The reader understands the logic *and* the fatal flaw.
- The plague section is devastating. Thucydides' clinical description, the overcrowding irony, and Pericles' death — all handled with appropriate weight.
- The Sicilian Expedition is the chapter's set-piece and it delivers. The Hermai mutilation, Alcibiades' defection ("Treason did not trouble him"), the quarry deaths — the narrative builds to a genuine sense of horror.
- "Treason did not trouble him" is a four-word character study.
- The two ironies of the war's end (Sparta winning at sea with Persian gold; Athens overthrowing its own democracy) are structurally elegant.
- Aegospotami and the surrender scene (Long Walls torn down to flute music) are vivid and final.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Mytilene debate is well-handled but the chapter could have spent one more sentence on what the second debate actually argued — the reader gets the first (exterminate them) but not the rebuttal.
- The closing paragraph about the war's significance is slightly abstract after the vivid narrative. "Fatal flaw" is the right diagnosis but the language gets a touch essayistic. The chapter's own storytelling has already proven the point; the summary could be more compressed.

**Momentum map:** Relentless. The chapter mirrors the war: escalation, catastrophe, brief hope, final collapse. No dead spots. The Sicilian Expedition is the structural peak and the collapse from there to Aegospotami is handled without filler.

### Chapter 6 — Crisis and Philip

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Greek city-states fought themselves into exhaustion while philosophy flourished, and a semi-barbarian kingdom on the margin was watching, learning, and preparing.

**What works:**
- "Sparta won the Peloponnesian War and then proved spectacularly incapable of running an empire" is a strong opening that immediately establishes the irony.
- The King's Peace irony (Marathon generation's grandchildren accepting Persian arbitration) is devastating and earns its weight.
- Epaminondas is handled with rare clarity. The oblique phalanx at Leuctra is explained as a tactical innovation, not just a result. The strategic demolition of Spartan power (freeing Messenia, founding Megalopolis) is narrated as a system being dismantled, not just battles won.
- The Sacred Band paragraph earns its emotional moment: "undefeated for over thirty years... annihilated by Philip of Macedon at Chaeronea." The reader knows they'll die before the chapter tells them.
- The trial and death of Socrates is handled with exactly the right weight — not over-dramatized, but the significance of his choice ("a philosopher who fled his own city to save his skin would have nothing left worth saving") lands.
- Plato's Academy and the Allegory of the Cave are efficiently covered.
- Diogenes is a great tonal change-of-pace. The Alexander anecdote ("stand out of my sunlight") is perfectly placed.
- Xenophon's Anabasis is used not just as a story but as a strategic observation ("Philip would notice").
- Philip's character is drawn vividly: the hostage in Thebes, the sarissa, the seven wives, the diplomacy. The reader understands him as a system-builder, not just a conqueror.
- Demosthenes and the Philippics provide the Greek perspective on what Philip was doing.
- The Chaeronea section is efficient and the Sacred Band's death is given appropriate weight without sentimentality.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter covers a LOT of ground: Sparta's failure, Corinthian War, Theban hegemony, Socrates' trial, Plato, Diogenes, Xenophon, Philip, Aristotle, the Theater of Epidaurus, and the Mausoleum at Halicarnassus. It's the longest chapter by content density and some transitions feel compressed. The jump from "Theban hegemony collapsed" to "the philosophers kept working" is abrupt.
- Aristotle gets one (admittedly rich) paragraph at the very end. Given his stature, this feels like an appendix. The narrative positions him as Alexander's tutor and then rushes through his entire philosophical output in a single paragraph. Consider whether he belongs here or in a brief coda to Chapter 4.
- The Theater of Epidaurus and Mausoleum at Halicarnassus paragraphs at the very end feel like orphaned facts — they don't connect to the Philip narrative or to each other. They read like items that needed to go somewhere.

**Momentum map:** Strong Spartan-failure opening → good Theban-hegemony build → solid philosophical interlude → strong Philip build to Chaeronea climax → slight diffusion in the Aristotle/Epidaurus/Mausoleum coda. Would be STRONG+ if the coda were tighter.

### Chapter 7 — Alexander

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Alexander's conquest was the most extraordinary and terrible military career in ancient history, and the world he left behind was utterly different from the one he entered.

**What works:**
- The opening is excellent: age, Iliad, intention. Three details and the reader is locked in.
- The source-critical paragraph (Arrian, Plutarch, Diodorus) is exactly the right thing to include for this figure — it tells the reader why certainty is impossible without slowing the story.
- Granicus, Issus, and Gaugamela are narrated with escalating intensity and each has a distinct tactical signature (river charge, pursuit of Darius, combined-arms gap exploitation).
- Darius fleeing twice is a structural repetition that works — the first time is shock, the second time is fate.
- The Tyre causeway detail (it turned Tyre into a peninsula permanently) is the kind of fact that makes a reader remember the chapter.
- The Gedrosian Desert march is properly horrifying without being gratuitous.
- The closing assessment ("extraordinary and terrible in equal measure") is earned by the narrative.

**What doesn't work:**
- The post-Gaugamela section (burning of Persepolis, march east, Hydaspes, Gedrosian Desert, death) is more compressed than the pre-Gaugamela section. The campaigns from 330-325 BCE — seven years of fighting — get roughly the same space as the single battle of Issus. The army mutiny at the Hyphasis River deserves a scene, not just "His army refused."
- The death at Babylon is very compressed. "After a prolonged fever following a drinking bout, Alexander died" covers one of the most debated deaths in ancient history in a single sentence. The poisoning theories, the deathbed scene, the "to the strongest" line — all deserve at least one more paragraph.
- The chapter lacks a scene showing Alexander's personal transformation — from Macedonian prince to self-proclaimed god-king. The Amun oracle, the Persian court dress adoption, the *proskynesis* controversy, the murder of Cleitus at the banquet — these episodes reveal character and are absent.

**Momentum map:** Explosive opening → strong three-battle build (Granicus/Issus/Gaugamela) → slight compression in the eastern campaigns → very compressed death → adequate closing assessment. The front half is stronger than the back half.

### Chapter 8 — The Hellenistic World and Rome

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Alexander's empire shattered into successor kingdoms that spread Greek culture further than the conquest itself, Hellenistic science reached heights not matched for a millennium, and Rome conquered the Greek world only to be culturally conquered in return.

**What works:**
- The Diadochi section is efficient and clear — three kingdoms, each with a distinct personality (Ptolemies = shrewd, Seleucids = vast and diverse, Antigonids = poor and beleaguered).
- The cultural fusion section (Serapis, Gandharan Buddha, Septuagint) demonstrates Hellenistic culture as a two-way exchange, not just imposition. This is important and well-handled.
- The science section is the chapter's crown jewel. Euclid, Archimedes, Eratosthenes, Aristarchus, Herophilus, and the Antikythera Mechanism — each gets enough detail to be memorable. The Eratosthenes circumference calculation is explained with genuine pedagogical clarity (shadow angles, one-fiftieth of a circle, multiply by distance).
- The Antikythera Mechanism is a brilliant closer for the science section — "the single most astonishing artifact of Greek science" is earned.
- Stoicism and Epicureanism are handled with appropriate nuance. The Epicureanism correction ("consistently misrepresented as hedonistic indulgence") is useful.
- The Pyrrhic victory etymology is a nice touch.
- The Rome section efficiently covers the transition without duplicating what will be in the Ancient Rome TL.
- The destruction of Corinth and Carthage in the same year is a powerful structural observation.
- The Horace quote ("Captive Greece took captive her fierce conqueror") is perfectly placed.
- The closing three paragraphs are a genuine peroration — they earn their sweep because the book has done the work.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter tries to cover roughly 300 years (323-30 BCE) and inevitably compresses. The Macedonian Wars get two sentences. The Achaean League gets one paragraph. A reader who doesn't already know Roman Republican history will find the "Rome pushes east" section confusing.
- Cleopatra gets a single sentence. Given that she's the most famous woman of the ancient world (the chapter says so), one sentence feels like a missed opportunity for a closing character beat.
- The philosophical schools section (Stoicism, Epicureanism) is positioned between the science section and the Rome section, creating a structural sandwich where two "ideas" sections bracket the political narrative. Moving the philosophy to immediately after the Diadochi section (as the "what were people thinking while the empires fought?") would improve flow.

**Momentum map:** Good Diadochi opening → strong cultural fusion → excellent science peak → adequate philosophy → good Rome transition → strong close. The chapter's structure is additive (section after section) rather than narrative (building to a climax), which is appropriate for a survey chapter but means it lacks the propulsive quality of Chapters 3 and 5.

### Overall Story Editor Assessment

**Throughline for the whole book:** Democracy, philosophy, drama, and science were fragile experiments by quarrelsome city-states, not inevitable discoveries — and the experiments only lasted a few centuries before being swallowed by empires, but they worked well enough that the rest of human history kept running them.

**What works across the whole narrative:**
- The voice is consistently excellent — informal, informational, never condescending, with a gift for the one-line summary ("Treason did not trouble him," "the best they would accept, not the best he could imagine," "the voluntary alliance had become an Athenian protection racket").
- The cross-civilizational references are well-placed and build a sense that Greece existed in a connected world, not in isolation.
- The pronunciations are consistently provided and consistently useful.
- The balance between narrative and analysis is almost always right — the reader understands both what happened and why it mattered.
- The "so what?" test is passed in every chapter. The reader always knows why they're being told something.

**What doesn't work across the whole narrative:**
- Chapter 2 has a structural coherence problem: the political arc (tyrants → lawmakers → democracy → Sparta) is excellent, but the philosophical/cultural coda (Thales through Sappho) diffuses the chapter's energy. Consider splitting or relocating.
- Chapter 7's back half (post-Gaugamela) is compressed relative to the front half. Alexander's personal transformation and death deserve more space.
- Chapter 8 has too many topics for one chapter. It's a 300-year survey that covers successor kingdoms, cultural fusion, science, philosophy, and the Roman conquest. It works but just barely.

**Momentum map (whole book):**
Ch 1 (Dark Ages): STRONG open
Ch 2 (Tyrants/Lawmakers): STRONG build
Ch 3 (Persian Wars): STRONG peak
Ch 4 (Golden Age): STRONG peak
Ch 5 (Peloponnesian War): STRONG peak
Ch 6 (Crisis/Philip): STRONG build
Ch 7 (Alexander): STRONG peak
Ch 8 (Hellenistic/Rome): STRONG close

All eight chapters grade STRONG. This is one of the most consistently strong narratives in the catalog. The voice, structure, and intellectual ambition are all operating at a high level.

## Newcomer findings (Persona A)

### Chapter 1

1. **Empty claim without concrete detail:** "The population in some regions dropped by 90 percent." Which regions? What's the evidence for 90%? This is a dramatic claim presented as fact with no archaeological anchor. (Lines 5)

2. **Process described by result, not mechanism:** "the small, scattered communities that survived the Mycenaean collapse began to coalesce into larger political units." How did they coalesce? Was it defensive alliances? Marriage networks? A shared sanctuary? The mechanism is missing. (Lines 43)

3. **Long parenthetical (borderline):** "fuh-NEE-shuns, the Semitic-speaking seafaring traders of the Levantine coast, in what is now Lebanon, who had built an extensive Mediterranean trade network" — this is a 23-word parenthetical that's doing a lot of work. It's not unreadable but it's dense. (Lines 21)

4. **Unclear how the alphabet was *adopted*:** "wherever a Greek-speaker first scratched a Phoenician-derived letter onto a potsherd" — the narrative explains *what* the Greeks changed (added vowels) but not *how* they encountered the Phoenician script. Were Phoenician traders in Greek ports? Did Greeks travel to Phoenicia? The contact mechanism is missing. (Lines 19)

5. **"Homer" may represent a tradition rather than a single person** — this is flagged and then the narrative proceeds to treat Homer as a person ("Around 750 BCE... the oral traditions... were set down in written form"). The tension between "Homer may not exist" and "Homer composed" is never resolved. (Lines 31)

### Chapter 2

6. **Undefined proper noun:** "the **aristoi** (ar-ISS-toy, 'the best')" — who were they concretely? Were they literally landowners? Descendants of Mycenaean elites? Warrior families? The word is defined but the people aren't. (Lines 84)

7. **Process without mechanism:** "Cleisthenes, outmaneuvered in the aristocratic power struggle, made a revolutionary move: he appealed to the ordinary people of Athens for support." How? Through speeches in the agora? By promising specific reforms? By arming supporters? The narrative says he "appealed" but doesn't show how. (Lines 122)

8. **Wordy cross-chapter callback:** "the Mycenaean kingdoms had been palace-centered autocracies" is fine, but the broader comparison paragraph (Lines 47) lists six civilizations in rapid succession (Mesopotamia, Egypt, Phoenicia, Mycenaean, etc.) — this works, but the later comparison in Chapter 2 (Lines 130) repeats the same structure (listing Persia, Egypt, Mesopotamia, China, India) to make a similar point. The "compare to every other civilization" move is used twice in two chapters. (Lines 130)

9. **"Usually translated as 'city-state,' but neither word quite captures what it meant"** — this is a good caveat but the chapter then uses "city-state" freely for the rest of the narrative without the caveat. Once it's been flagged as inadequate, the reader notices every subsequent use. (Lines 41)

## Skeptic findings (Persona B)

### Chapter 1

1. **[CHECK]** "The population in some regions dropped by 90 percent." The 90% figure is at the extreme end of scholarly estimates for specific regions (Messenia is the usual example). Applying it broadly as "some regions" is defensible but risks implying it was typical. Source is usually Snodgrass (1971). Not wrong, but the framing is generous. (Lines 5)

2. **[SOFT]** "The Greek alphabet emerged around 800 BCE." The date is debated. Some scholars date the earliest Greek alphabetic inscriptions to the mid-8th century (c. 750 BCE); 800 BCE is on the early end. The phrasing "around 800 BCE" is within the scholarly range but closer to the traditional early date. (Lines 21)

3. **[STRONG]** The entire alphabet section accurately describes the adaptation of consonant signs for vowels. The Phoenician derivation is well-established and the mechanism is correctly explained.

4. **[CHECK]** "the first recorded Olympic Games were held in 776 BCE." This is the traditional date from ancient Olympic victor lists, but whether the festival at Olympia in 776 BCE was the "first" games or merely the first that was later recorded is debated. The narrative says "first recorded," which is technically careful, but the phrasing might lead the reader to think 776 BCE is a firm date rather than a reconstructed one. (Lines 63)

5. **[SOFT]** "modern geologists have found that the fissure emits ethylene gas, which can produce altered consciousness." The de Boer et al. (2001) study found ethylene traces at Delphi, but the finding remains debated. Some geologists dispute that the concentrations were sufficient to produce the described effects. Presented as settled fact here. (Lines 71)

### Chapter 2

6. **[CHECK]** "Draco supposedly replied that small crimes deserved death and he could think of no harsher punishment for large ones." This anecdote is from Plutarch's *Life of Solon* 17.1, writing roughly 700 years after Draco. Plutarch himself presents it as a thing "they say." The "supposedly" qualifier is present but light. (Lines 108)

7. **[STRONG]** Solon's four property classes (pentakosiomedimnoi, hippeis, zeugitai, thetes) are accurately described and their political rights correctly stated.

8. **[CHECK]** "On one memorable occasion, he staged his return by riding into the city in a chariot alongside a tall, beautiful woman dressed as the goddess Athena." From Herodotus 1.60. Herodotus himself found the story hard to believe ("the most foolish stratagem that I have found, considering that the Athenians were reputed the wisest of the Greeks"). The narrative attributes it to Herodotus with appropriate skepticism ("Herodotus, who tells the story with undisguised amazement"), so the attribution is fair. (Lines 96)

9. **[STRONG]** Cleisthenes' reforms (ten tribes, three trittyes per tribe from city/coast/inland, Boule of 500, ostracism with 6,000 minimum) are all accurately described.

10. **[CHECK]** "those judged unfit were supposedly exposed on a mountainside, though modern scholars debate how systematically this was practiced." Good to include the caveat. The archaeological evidence from the Apothetae is debated; Plutarch is the main source. Correctly flagged. (Lines 142)

### Chapter 3

11. **[CHECK]** "Herodotus reports that the Athenians killed 6,400 Persians while losing only 192 of their own, numbers that are almost certainly exaggerated in Athens' favor." The narrative correctly flags the exaggeration. The 192 Athenian dead is from Herodotus 6.117 and is repeated on the Marathon casualty list; the Persian figure is from Herodotus 6.117. The casualty ratio is implausible but the narrative handles it responsibly. (Lines 191)

12. **[CHECK]** "Modern estimates suggest perhaps 70,000 to 300,000 land troops" for Xerxes' invasion. The range is extremely wide. The low end (70,000) is from Delbrück-school revisionism; the high end (300,000) is from more traditional modern estimates. The narrative is honest about the range but "70,000 to 300,000" is so wide as to be almost meaningless — it's the equivalent of saying "we don't know." (Lines 203)

13. **[SOFT]** "reportedly in grim silence" — this is not from Herodotus. Herodotus (8.90) says Xerxes "leapt up from his seat" in distress when his fleet was destroyed. The "grim silence" framing may be conflated from other accounts or embellished. (Lines 223)

### Chapter 4

From Plutarch's *Life of Alexander* 14 and Diogenes Laertius 6.38. Both are centuries after the alleged event. ### Chapter 7

27. **[SOFT]** "He reportedly said his empire should go 'to the strongest.'" The Greek is *tōi kratistōi*. ### Chapter 8

29. **[CHECK]** "it may have held between 100,000 and 400,000 scrolls." The narrative's range is reasonable but the evidence is extremely thin. (Lines 478)

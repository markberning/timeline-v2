## Chapter 1 — The Peninsula

1. `[CHECK]` **"about the size of the state of Utah, or slightly larger than Great Britain"** — Utah is ~85,000 sq mi; the Korean peninsula is ~85,270 sq mi; Great Britain is ~80,800 sq mi. The Utah comparison is close. The "slightly larger than Great Britain" claim checks out but is worth verifying precisely.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the difference between high and low tide at some points exceeds 30 feet (9 meters)"** — the tidal range on Korea's west coast (especially near Incheon) does reach 30+ feet, so this appears correct. Worth confirming the "among the largest in the world" framing — the Bay of Fundy exceeds 50 feet.

## Chapter 2 — Dangun and Bronze

1. `[CHECK]` **"the year 2333 BCE founded Gojoseon"** — this date is traditional and the text correctly flags it as legendary. However, the text then says "makes Korean civilization roughly contemporary with the Egyptian Old Kingdom" — the Old Kingdom began around 2686 BCE, and the cross-reference says "Early Dynastic Egypt, Chapters 1-8." The framing as "roughly contemporary" with a ~350-year gap is a stretch.

2. `[STRONG]` **"its last eruption, around 946 CE, was one of the largest volcanic events of the past two thousand years"** — the 946 CE eruption of Mount Baekdu (also called Changbaishan) is indeed considered one of the largest eruptions of the past 2,000 years (VEI 7), but the exact date is debated — some sources place it as early as 939 CE or as late as 947 CE. "Around 946 CE" is within the range but the reader should know this is approximate.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Korea has over 30,000 surviving dolmens, which represents roughly 40% of all the dolmens in the world"** — this is a widely cited figure in Korean archaeology sources. The number varies depending on the source (some say 35,000-40,000), but "over 30,000" and "roughly 40%" are within the standard range. Still worth verifying since the exact percentage depends on how you count dolmens globally.

4. `[CHECK]` **"The Korean Bronze Age began roughly around 1500 BCE"** — some scholars date the beginning of the Korean Bronze Age earlier, around 1500-1000 BCE depending on region. "Roughly around 1500 BCE" is at the early end of estimates and may be too precise.

5. `[SOFT]` **"roughly contemporary with the Shang Dynasty's Bronze Age in China"** — the Shang dynasty is traditionally dated to ~1600-1046 BCE. If the Korean Bronze Age began around 1500 BCE, this is roughly contemporary. Acceptable framing.

## Chapter 3 — Iron and Empires

1. `[STRONG]` **"In 109 BCE, Emperor Wu launched an invasion of Gojoseon"** — most sources say the invasion began in late 109 BCE and Gojoseon fell in 108 BCE. The timeline presented is consistent. However, the text says the campaign "lasted over a year" which may be slightly overstated — it was approximately one year (late 109 to mid-108 BCE).

2. `[CHECK]` **"By about 75 BCE, only Lelang remained"** — the dates of when the other three commanderies collapsed vary by source. Zhenfan and Lintun were abolished around 82 BCE; Xuantu was relocated rather than destroyed. "By about 75 BCE" is a simplification. The text's framing of "collapsed relatively quickly" is accurate in spirit but the specifics could be tightened.

3. `[CHECK]` **"from 108 BCE to 313 CE, Lelang Commandery sat on the northwestern Korean peninsula as a fully operational Chinese administrative city"** — Lelang's operational status fluctuated significantly over 400+ years. Calling it "fully operational" for the entire period oversimplifies — it was sometimes a backwater with minimal Chinese oversight. The destruction date of 313 CE by Goguryeo is well-attested.

## Chapter 4 — Three Kingdoms

1. `[CHECK]` **"Silla in 57 BCE, Goguryeo in 37 BCE, and Baekje in 18 BCE"** — these are the traditional dates from the Samguk Sagi and the text correctly flags them as "suspiciously precise and almost certainly legendary." Good hedging.

2. `[STRONG]` **"glass beads that arrived via the Silk Road from as far away as the Roman Empire"** — some Silla glass beads have been chemically analyzed and show compositions consistent with Roman or eastern Mediterranean glass. However, the direct "from the Roman Empire" framing oversimplifies a complex trade chain. The glass may have been manufactured in the Roman world but arrived through many intermediaries over decades or centuries.

3. `[CHECK]` **"In 520 CE, King Beopheung formalized the bone-rank system"** — the bone-rank system's origins are debated. Some scholars attribute its formalization to Beopheung, others argue it evolved over a longer period. The 520 CE date may be too precise.

4. `[SOFT]` **"Gaya became the leading iron producer in East Asia"** — this is a strong claim. Gaya was certainly a major iron producer, but calling it THE leading producer in all of East Asia (including China) seems overstated. It was more likely the leading producer on the Korean peninsula and a major exporter.

## Chapter 5 — Goguryeo: The Warrior Kingdom

1. `[STRONG]` **"Gwanggaeto's death at the age of thirty-nine"** — if Gwanggaeto ascended the throne at eighteen in 391 CE and died in 413 CE, he died at roughly age 40-41, not 39. The math doesn't quite work (413 - 391 = 22 years of reign + 18 at ascension = ~40). Worth checking.

2. `[STRONG]` **"Chinese records claim that of the 305,000 Sui troops who crossed the Yalu River, fewer than 2,700 returned"** — this is from the Zizhi Tongjian (Comprehensive Mirror). The numbers are widely cited in Korean and Chinese sources but are almost certainly vastly exaggerated or represent a specific detachment rather than the entire army. The text does note "even accounting for the exaggeration," but presenting the 305,000/2,700 ratio as the Chinese claim without noting which specific source gives these numbers makes it sound more authoritative than it is.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Eulji Mundeok reportedly sent a mocking poem to the Sui commander"** — this poem is indeed recorded in traditional sources (the Samguk Sagi). It's a famous episode in Korean history. "Reportedly" is appropriate hedging.

4. `[CHECK]` **"King Jangsu reigned for seventy-nine years (413–491 CE)"** — 491 - 413 = 78 years, not 79. The text says "seventy-nine years" which is off by one.

5. `[STRONG]` **"Tang China, the superpower of the medieval world — the civilization that invented gunpowder"** — gunpowder's invention is typically dated to the 9th century CE, during the late Tang or after. Tang existed 618-907 CE. Attributing the invention to Tang is technically defensible if you count the late Tang period, but most scholarship attributes the development of gunpowder formulas to around the 850s CE and military applications to later. This is potentially misleading as a defining feature of the Tang.

6. `[CHECK]` **"For over sixty days, through summer heat and autumn chill, the Goguryeo garrison resisted every Tang assault"** — the Ansi siege duration varies by source — some say 60 days, some say 88 days (from June to September 645). "Over sixty days" is conservative but acceptable.

## Chapter 6 — Baekje and the Buddha

1. `[CHECK]` **"Baekje officially introduced Buddhism to the Japanese court around 538 or 552 CE"** — the two dates represent competing traditions from the Jōgū Shōtoku Hōō Teisetsu (538) and the Nihon Shoki (552). Good that both dates are given.

2. `[STRONG]` **"the earliest known use of Chinese writing in Japan, on the famous Inariyama Sword inscription (471 CE), uses Chinese characters in ways that show Korean influence"** — this is a contested claim. The Inariyama Sword inscription does use Chinese characters, and some scholars see Korean-style usage, but others see direct Chinese influence. Presenting it as established fact ("uses Chinese characters in ways that show Korean influence") is stronger than the scholarly consensus supports.

3. `[CHECK]` **"an Indian monk named Marananta (mah-rah-NAHN-tah), who came via the Chinese state of Eastern Jin"** — Marananta is recorded in Korean sources (Samguk Sagi) as the transmitter. His historicity is accepted but details are thin. "Indian monk" is the traditional description though some scholars suggest he may have been Central Asian.

4. `[CHECK]` **"Baekje Incense Burner... discovered in 1993"** — the discovery date of 1993 at the Neungsan-ri site near Buyeo is well-documented. The physical description appears accurate.

5. `[CHECK]` **"Mireuksa temple, constructed around 602 CE"** — some sources date Mireuksa's construction to the early 7th century generally rather than precisely 602 CE. The date may be slightly too precise.

6. `[SOFT]` **"This is roughly analogous to the Greek cultural influence on Rome"** — a reasonable analogy but imprecise. The Greece-Rome relationship was more direct (Rome conquered Greece), while Baekje-Japan was more voluntary. The analogy works in broad strokes.

## Chapter 7 — The Great Unification War

1. `[CHECK]` **"killing over one hundred Goguryeo aristocrats"** — the number "over 100" for Yeon Gaesomun's 642 coup is from the Samguk Sagi. It may be exaggerated but is the standard figure.

2. `[STRONG]` **"a combined Silla-Tang force attacked Baekje... Tang China sent a naval fleet of 130,000 troops"** — the 130,000 figure for Su Dingfang's force is from Chinese sources (primarily the Jiu Tangshu). This is a suspiciously round number and likely includes support personnel. "Troops" may overstate the combat force.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Kim Yusin led a Silla army of 50,000 overland"** — also from traditional sources. Suspiciously precise. May be somewhat inflated.

4. `[STRONG]` **"Gyebaek... reportedly killed his own family before the battle"** — this is a famous story from the Samguk Sagi but it reads like a legendary embellishment designed to emphasize Gyebaek's resolve. It follows a well-known ancient world trope of warriors killing their families before a final battle. Worth flagging for verification.

5. `[CHECK]` **"three thousand court women of Baekje threw themselves from the cliff at Nakhwaam"** — the text correctly flags "three thousand is almost certainly symbolic." The legend of Nakhwaam is deeply embedded in Korean culture but has no contemporary documentation.

## Chapter 8 — Unified Silla

1. `[STRONG]` **"contemporary accounts describe as having nearly one million residents"** — the "one million" figure for Gyeongju comes from the Samguk Yusa, which mentions "179,000 households." With typical household multipliers of 5-6 people, this gives roughly 900,000-1,000,000. However, modern archaeological analysis suggests the Gyeongju basin could have supported perhaps 100,000-200,000 at most. The text hedges ("That number is probably exaggerated") but "a fraction of the claim" is doing a lot of work — the real population was likely 1/5 to 1/10 of the claim.

2. `[STRONG]` **"Mugujeonggwang Great Dharani Sutra... dates to approximately 751 CE — the same period as the temple's construction. This makes it the oldest surviving woodblock-printed document in the world"** — this is a contested claim. Chinese scholars have argued for earlier Chinese printed documents. The dating of the Dharani Sutra is based on the pagoda's construction date, not direct dating of the paper or ink. Japan also claims an older printed document (the Hyakumantō Darani, ~770 CE, but these are simpler). The claim "oldest surviving woodblock-printed document in the world" is the standard Korean scholarly position but not universally accepted.

3. `[CHECK]` **"Cheomseongdae... the oldest surviving astronomical observatory in East Asia and one of the oldest in the world"** — this is the standard claim but it's debated. Some scholars question whether Cheomseongdae was actually an observatory versus a symbolic or ceremonial structure. The text does note this debate ("whether Cheomseongdae was used as an actual working observatory... is debated"), which is good.

4. `[CHECK]` **"362 stones — a number often interpreted as representing the days of the lunar year"** — a standard lunar year has ~354 days. 362 doesn't match cleanly. Some interpretations link 362 to the number of days in a year plus an adjustment, or count it differently. The "often interpreted" hedge is appropriate but the reader may notice the math doesn't work.

5. `[CHECK]` **"Jang Bogo... the most powerful maritime figure in East Asia"** — this is a strong superlative. Jang Bogo was indeed a major maritime figure but "the most powerful in East Asia" is hard to verify. He was certainly the dominant figure in the Yellow Sea trade network.

6. `[CHECK]` **"In 935 CE, the last Silla king, Gyeongsun, peacefully surrendered to Wang Geon, ending a dynasty that had lasted nearly a thousand years (57 BCE–935 CE)"** — the text correctly presents the traditional dates. The "nearly a thousand years" framing is accurate if you accept the legendary 57 BCE founding date.

7. `[SOFT]` **"Koreans still use underfloor heating in their homes today"** — this is true and well-known. Modern Korean apartments use ondol-style radiant floor heating. Not a flag, just noting it checks out.

# Indus Valley Backward Fact-Check — against Ancient China, Ancient Nubia, Elamite Civilization

## Chapter 4 — The Urban Explosion

1. [STRONG] **"the late Hongshan jade culture was still producing dragons in the northeast"**
   - **Problem:** The Hongshan culture had ended ~300 years before the ~2600 BCE moment this sentence is anchoring. At 2600 BCE there was no "late Hongshan" producing anything.
   - **Source:** Ancient China Ch 4 states explicitly: "The Hongshan culture flourished from about 4700 to 2900 BCE — almost two thousand years." The reference-data file `ancient-china.json` span-ac-hongshan gives the same 4700–2900 BCE range. Hongshan was over by ~2900 BCE.
   - **Suggested fix:** Drop the "still producing" framing. Something like: "…the Hongshan jade tradition had ended a few centuries earlier but its pig-dragon forms were already being inherited by successor cultures…" — or simply remove the Hongshan name-drop and keep the Longshan + Liangzhu references, which are genuinely contemporaneous with 2600 BCE.

2. [STRONG] **"the Longshan walled-town horizon was just beginning in the Yellow River plain"**
   - **Problem:** At ~2600 BCE, the Longshan horizon had been running for ~400 years, not "just beginning." Longshan was well into its classic walled-town phase by 2600 BCE.
   - **Source:** Ancient China Ch 6 explicitly defines Longshan as "a broad stretch of related cultures across the North China Plain between about 3000 and 1900 BCE." The reference-data span-ac-longshan gives startYear −3000, endYear −2000. At 2600 BCE, Longshan was ~400 years old and at or near its middle.
   - **Suggested fix:** "…the Longshan walled-town horizon was already several centuries into its classic walled-town phase…" or drop "just beginning" entirely.

## Chapter 6 — The Invisible Rulers

3. [CHECK] **"Mesopotamia was filling the Royal Tombs of Ur with dozens of sacrificed attendants … while in China the Longshan walled towns were hardening into competing warlord proto-states with elite jade-rich burials and foundation sacrifices under their walls"**
   - **Problem:** The sentence pairs the Royal Tombs of Ur (Queen Puabi's at ~2600 BCE) with Longshan foundation sacrifices as if both are the same-moment signature. Per the newer China narrative, the most graphic Longshan foundation-sacrifice site (Shimao, with decapitated young women in wall footings) is dated to roughly 2000 BCE — ~600 years after Puabi. The parallel-timing reads as tighter than it is.
   - **Source:** Ancient China Ch 6: "Shimao is particularly startling … decapitated human heads, deliberately buried in the foundations … Someone was sacrificing people to consecrate the walls of Shimao around 2000 BCE." Nubia Ch 5 makes the same explicit four-way comparison and places the big Chinese retainer-sacrifice signature at "the late Longshan site of Shimao (roughly 2000 BCE)" and early Shang Yinxu, not at 2600 BCE.
   - **Suggested fix:** Either push the Indus comparison later (Longshan elite jade-rich burials and Shimao foundation sacrifices as a later-Longshan parallel, ~2000 BCE, when Indus is mid-Mature Harappan) or soften to "while in China the Longshan horizon was running on a very different logic — competing walled towns that would eventually be consecrated with foundation sacrifices."

4. [CHECK] **"the Royal Tombs of Ur with dozens of sacrificed attendants and building statues of lugal kings"**
   - **Problem:** "Dozens" undercounts the upper end. The Nubia narrative, doing the four-way cradle comparison explicitly, pins Queen Puabi's death pit at **74** sacrificed attendants — the figure Indus Ch 8 itself uses. "Dozens" in Ch 6 is internally inconsistent with Ch 8 and low by roughly a factor of two at the upper end.
   - **Source:** Nubia Ch 5: "the largest royal grave (Queen Puabi's, around 2600 BCE) contained 74 sacrificed attendants lined up in the death pit." Indus Ch 8 also says 74.
   - **Suggested fix:** "…with up to 74 sacrificed attendants in a single death pit…" or "…with dozens of sacrificed attendants per pit, 74 in Queen Puabi's tomb alone…"

## Chapter 8 — Gods Without Names

5. [SOFT] **"The Mesopotamian kings at Ur were buried with 74 sacrificed attendants"**
   - **Problem:** 74 is specifically Queen Puabi's tomb, not a general figure for "the Mesopotamian kings at Ur." Nubia Ch 5 frames 74 as "the upper end for Mesopotamian royal sacrifice" at the single largest Ur grave, not the typical figure.
   - **Source:** Nubia Ch 5: "the largest royal grave (Queen Puabi's, around 2600 BCE) contained 74 sacrificed attendants … That is the upper end for Mesopotamian royal sacrifice."
   - **Suggested fix:** "In the richest single grave at Ur, Queen Puabi was buried with 74 sacrificed attendants…"

## Chapter 1 — The Forgotten Civilization

6. [SOFT] **"the *largest* of the three great Bronze Age civilizations"**
   - **Problem:** The newer TLs treat this as a four-cradle frame, not three. Nubia Ch 5 explicitly discusses "the four great Bronze Age cradles that left substantial royal cemeteries" — Mesopotamia, Northern China, Indus Valley, and Kerma. Ancient China Ch 5 treats Liangzhu / Longshan-Shang as a full cradle peer. Indus Ch 4 itself later says "A fourth cradle was active at the same moment far to the east: in China…" — internally inconsistent with Ch 1's "three." This isn't a flat contradiction (the Ch 1 sentence is comparing Indus only to Egypt + Mesopotamia for area), but the "three great Bronze Age civilizations" framing is out of step with how the post-Indus narratives treat the cradle count.
   - **Source:** Nubia Ch 5 (four-cradle framing, Kerma included); Ancient China Ch 5 (Liangzhu as "first Chinese civilization"); Indus Ch 4 own mention of "A fourth cradle was active at the same moment far to the east."
   - **Suggested fix:** Either broaden to "one of the great Bronze Age civilizations — larger in area than Egypt and Mesopotamia combined" (drop the "three" count) or acknowledge China (and, at the retainer-burial level, Kerma) as peers.

**Total: 2 STRONG, 2 CHECK, 2 SOFT**

# Ancient Nubia Summary Fact-Check

## Chapter 1 — Land of the Bow

**Bullet 7** (starts with "Around 2800 BCE the"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** Early-dynastic Egyptian raids "brag about taking 'thousands of cattle and captives' on the Palermo Stone."
- **Problem:** The narrative doesn't attribute the "thousands of cattle and captives" phrasing to the Palermo Stone. The Palermo Stone quote the narrative cites is specifically Sneferu's "7,000 captives and 200,000 cattle" (Fourth Dynasty, covered in Chapter 2). For the early-dynastic period the narrative says only "Other early dynastic records count thousands of cattle and prisoners taken from Nubian raids" — no source named.
- **Source says:** "Egyptian inscriptions from the early dynastic period brag about destroying Ta-Seti... Other early dynastic records count thousands of cattle and prisoners taken from Nubian raids."
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "on the Palermo Stone" with "in early-dynastic royal inscriptions" — keeps the claim accurate without attributing to the wrong source.

## Chapter 2 — After the Silence

**Bullet 1** (starts with "For about four centuries"):
- [STRONG] **Claim:** "Uruk, Mohenjo-daro, and the Longshan walled towns were all urbanizing at the same moment."
- **Problem:** The narrative explicitly says Mohenjo-daro came *later*, not at the same moment. During Lower Nubia's 2800–2400 BCE silence, the narrative names **Kot Diji** (the pre-Harappan walled town) as the Indus-side analogue, with Mohenjo-daro explicitly placed "a few centuries later."
- **Source says:** "East of the Indus River, walled towns like Kot Diji were being built — the first rumblings of the Harappan urban explosion that would produce Mohenjo-daro a few centuries later (Indus Valley Ch 3)."
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Mohenjo-daro" with "Kot Diji" (the pre-Harappan walled town on the path to Mohenjo-daro).

**Bullet 3** (starts with "Egypt's Old Kingdom policy"):
- [STRONG] **Claim:** Egypt's industrial-scale raiding of Nubia happened "a thousand years before the Neo-Assyrians would make mass forced-population transfer a routine imperial tool."
- **Problem:** The narrative gives a much larger gap than "a thousand years."
- **Source says:** "Mass forced-population transfer as a systematic policy would not become routine in the ancient world until the Neo-Assyrian empire industrialized it more than 1,800 years later."
- **Suggested fix:** Change "a thousand years before" to "nearly two thousand years before" (or "more than 1,800 years before") to match the narrative.

## Chapter 3 — Harkhuf's Donkeys

**Bullet 4** (starts with "Harkhuf, the Egyptian governor"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Harkhuf, the Egyptian governor of Elephantine under Pepi II, led four trade expeditions south..."
- **Problem:** Drops a qualifier — the narrative says Harkhuf served *two* pharaohs (Merenre first, then Pepi II). Listing only Pepi II as his sovereign misses that his career spanned the end of one reign into the next.
- **Source says:** "Harkhuf served two pharaohs, Merenre and then Pepi II, across the second half of the twenty-fourth century BCE, right at the tail end of the Old Kingdom."
- **Suggested fix:** "...the Egyptian governor of Elephantine under Merenre and Pepi II..."

## Chapter 4 — Forts Along the Cataracts

**Bullet 3** (starts with "Buhen, the largest"):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "Buhen, the largest of the forts..."
- **Problem:** The narrative doesn't single out Buhen as *the* largest; it calls Buhen and Mirgissa together "the two largest forts in the chain," and arguably calls Mirgissa "even more ambitious."
- **Source says:** "The two largest forts in the chain were Buhen (BOO-hen) and Mirgissa... Mirgissa was arguably even more ambitious."
- **Suggested fix:** "Buhen, one of the two largest forts, had two concentric walls..."

**Bullet 6** (starts with "A second Semna stele"):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** Quotes the second Semna stele as calling a failing successor "'not born of the king.'"
- **Problem:** The bullet puts "not born of the king" in quotes, but the narrative's paraphrase uses "not born to him," not "not born of the king." The quoted phrasing doesn't match what the narrative actually gives.
- **Source says:** "any son who abandons it and fails to fight for it is not his son and was not born to him."
- **Suggested fix:** Drop the quote marks or rephrase: "...declaring that such a son 'is not his son and was not born to him' — binding posterity with ritual disinheritance."

**Bullet 7** (starts with "Nubian Medjay warriors"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Nubian Medjay warriors from the Eastern Desert..."
- **Problem:** The narrative describes the Medjay only as "a desert-dwelling Nubian people" without specifying the Eastern Desert. It is the **Pan-Grave Culture** that the narrative explicitly places "from the Eastern Desert." The bullet appears to have conflated the two groups' geography.
- **Source says:** "the Medjay (MED-jai), a desert-dwelling Nubian people..." vs. "the Pan-Grave Culture... Nubian pastoralists from the Eastern Desert".
- **Suggested fix:** Drop the geographic qualifier: "Nubian Medjay warriors served so reliably in Egyptian military and internal security forces..."

## Chapter 6 — The Squeeze and the Fall

**Bullet 6** (starts with "Thutmose I's army pushed"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Thutmose I's army pushed past the Second, Third, and Fourth Cataracts in 1504 BCE..."
- **Problem:** The narrative gives 1504 BCE as the year Thutmose I "took the throne," not the year of the Kerma campaign. The campaign happened during his reign, not necessarily in his accession year.
- **Source says:** "a king named Thutmose I (THOOT-moh-say), the third pharaoh of the Eighteenth Dynasty, who took the throne around 1504 BCE..."
- **Suggested fix:** "After taking the throne around 1504 BCE, Thutmose I's army pushed past the Second, Third, and Fourth Cataracts..." or simply drop the specific date.

## Chapter 7 — Province of Gold

**Bullet 3** (starts with "Diodorus Siculus's eyewitness"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Diodorus Siculus's eyewitness account of Nubian gold mining..."
- **Problem:** The narrative is careful *not* to call Diodorus an eyewitness — it says he "visited or interviewed people who had visited the mines," leaving his direct witness status uncertain. Calling it an "eyewitness account" overclaims.
- **Source says:** "Diodorus visited or interviewed people who had visited the mines, and his description of what he saw is the most cited passage..."
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "eyewitness account" with "firsthand account" or "surviving account" — preserves the citation without overclaiming Diodorus's direct presence.

## Chapter 8 — The Cultural Bridge

**Bullet 8** (starts with "Five centuries later the"):
- [CHECK] **Claim:** "Five centuries later the Kushite kings of Napata would march north... and conquer Egypt itself as the 25th Dynasty."
- **Problem:** The narrative is internally inconsistent on this gap. In Chapter 8, immediately after describing the 1070 BCE withdrawal, it says "about three centuries after Egypt's withdrawal, the Kushite kings of Napata would do something..." Elsewhere (and in Chapter 6's aside) it uses "five centuries." Measuring from the 1070 BCE withdrawal to the 25th Dynasty conquest (c. 750–720 BCE) is roughly three centuries, not five. The bullet picks the larger figure.
- **Source says:** "And about three centuries after Egypt's withdrawal, the Kushite kings of Napata would do something that nobody in 1070 BCE would have predicted. They would march north, cross the First Cataract, enter Egypt, and conquer it."
- **Suggested fix:** Change "Five centuries later" to "About three centuries later" to match the passage that directly follows the 1070 BCE withdrawal described in Bullet 6.

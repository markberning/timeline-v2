## Must-Fix Items

**1. [Ch 7, Skeptic] "Flagellum Dei" attribution may be anachronistic.**
Line 419: "the epithet 'Flagellum Dei' ... was attached to him during or shortly after his lifetime." This is contested. The earliest secure attestation of this specific Latin phrase is medieval, not late antique. Some scholars trace it to Jordanes (6th century) or later. The phrase may not have been contemporary with Attila.
**Fix:** Change to "the epithet 'Flagellum Dei' ... was attached to him, possibly during or shortly after his lifetime, though the phrase's earliest written attestation is from later centuries."

**2. [Ch 7, Skeptic] Venice founding claim oversimplified.**
Line 403: "Refugees from the destruction fled into the marshy lagoons at the head of the Adriatic, where they founded fishing settlements that would eventually become Venice." This is the traditional founding legend, but modern archaeology shows the lagoon islands had settlements well before Attila's invasion (5th-century ceramics, etc.). Aquileian refugees contributed to Venice's growth, but calling them founders elides a more complex picture.
**Fix:** "Refugees from the destruction fled into the marshy lagoons at the head of the Adriatic, where they joined existing fishing settlements that would eventually grow into Venice. The city's traditional founding story credits Attila's invasion, though the lagoon communities predated it."

**3. [Ch 4, Skeptic] Huo Qubing's death — "heaven-sent illness" framing is editorializing.**
Line 199: "later Chinese legend attributed it to a 'heaven-sent illness' as if the gods themselves couldn't endure such a flame burning so brightly for long." The "as if the gods..." clause is the narrator projecting a sentiment that the Chinese sources don't actually express in those terms. Chinese sources simply say he died of illness (disease, possibly plague contracted on campaign). The editorializing risks turning a medical mystery into a romantic gloss.
**Fix:** Cut the "as if the gods themselves couldn't endure such a flame burning so brightly for long" clause. The dry fact that he died at 24 of unknown causes is dramatic enough.

**4. [Ch 1, Newcomer] "Warring States period" lacks forward reference to Qin Dynasty narrative.**
Line 21: The Warring States period gets a good inline definition but references "Chapter 1 of the Zhou Dynasty narrative." This is correct, but the Qin Dynasty narrative (the more directly relevant TL) is not mentioned until Qin Shi Huang appears later. A reader who has read the Qin Dynasty narrative would expect a cross-reference there.
**Fix:** After "the Zhou dynasty (Chapter 1 of the Zhou Dynasty narrative)" add "culminating in the Qin unification (Qin Dynasty Ch 1)."

**5. [Ch 5, Skeptic] "Five claimants" in 60 BCE — the number is uncertain.**
Line 233: "five claimants fought for the chanyu title simultaneously." Chinese sources mention multiple claimants but the exact number varies by source and interpretation. Some scholars count as few as three and as many as five during the 60-50s BCE succession crisis.
**Fix:** Change "five claimants" to "at least three (and possibly as many as five) claimants."

## Should-Fix Items

**6. [Ch 3, Newcomer] "heqin" pronunciation is wrong or misleading.**
Line 133: "heqin (huh-CHIN)." The standard Mandarin pronunciation is closer to "huh-CHEEN" — the second syllable has a long /i:/ sound, not the short vowel suggested by "CHIN."
**Fix:** Change to "heqin (huh-CHEEN)."

**7. [Ch 1, Story Editor] Opening paragraph is strong but the third sentence is slightly list-heavy.**
Line 5: "One that would not just raid civilizations but force the two greatest empires on earth to reorganize themselves in response. One that would build the template every steppe empire after it would follow. One that might, if you follow the trail of hoofprints far enough, have pushed a wave of horse warriors all the way from the Gobi Desert to the gates of Rome." The triple "One that..." anaphora is effective but the middle one ("build the template") is the weakest — it is abstract where the other two are vivid. Consider cutting or sharpening it.
**Fix (optional):** Combine: "One that would not just raid civilizations but force the two greatest empires on earth to reorganize themselves in response — and that might, if you follow the trail of hoofprints far enough, have pushed a wave of horse warriors all the way from the Gobi Desert to the gates of Rome."

**8. [Ch 6, Cross-Cultural] Missing Scythians cross-reference for Sarmatians and Alans.**
Line 329: The Alans are introduced as "an Iranian-speaking nomadic people descended from the Sarmatians" — correct, but the Scythians narrative covers the Sarmatian displacement of the Scythians extensively. A "(Scythians Ch 7)" or similar cross-reference would help readers who have read that TL.
**Fix:** After "descended from the Sarmatians" add "(Scythians Ch 7)."

**9. [Ch 6, Cross-Cultural] Missing Ancient Rome cross-reference for Adrianople.**
Line 331: The Battle of Adrianople is described as "the worst Roman military defeat since Cannae, over five centuries earlier" — good comparison, but no cross-reference to the Ancient Rome narrative, which is shipped and covers the late empire.
**Fix:** Add "(Ancient Rome Ch 10)" or similar after "Battle of Adrianople."

**10. [Ch 3, Skeptic] 320,000 troops for Gaozu's army — called "probably an exaggeration" but no scale anchor.**
Line 115: The text says "320,000 men (probably an exaggeration, but reflecting a genuinely massive force)." Good hedge, but it would be stronger if the reader had any sense of what a realistic number might have been for a Han expeditionary force. Even a brief "modern estimates range from 100,000 to 200,000" would ground the qualification.
**Fix:** Change parenthetical to "(probably an exaggeration — modern estimates suggest 100,000 to 200,000, still a massive force)."

**11. [Ch 4, Story Editor] "the most improbable source in the Han court" is a dangling tease.**
Line 189: "the first great blow came from the most improbable source in the Han court" — but the paragraph then shifts to the Mayi ambush, not to Wei Qing's background. Wei Qing's humble origins are described two paragraphs later. The "improbable source" hook does not pay off at the location where it is placed.
**Fix:** Either move "the most improbable source" to the Wei Qing paragraph, or cut the tease from the Mayi paragraph.

**12. [Ch 7, Story Editor] Attila's age "roughly 47" — the birth year is genuinely unknown.**
Line 419: "Attila died at roughly 47 years old (his exact birth date is unknown)." The conventional birth estimate (~406 CE) is just that — a guess based on very little evidence. Saying "roughly 47" gives false precision to a number that could be off by a decade.
**Fix:** Change to "Attila died at an age estimated by modern historians at somewhere between his late forties and late fifties (his birth date is unrecorded)."

**13. [Ch 5, Newcomer] Ban Chao needs a one-line intro.**
Line 271: "the Han general Ban Chao began a thirty-year campaign." Ban Chao is dropped in without context. He is one of the most famous generals in Chinese history and his campaign is arguably more consequential than many that get full paragraphs. At minimum, a one-clause intro.
**Fix:** "the Han general **Ban Chao** (BAHN CHOW), one of the most celebrated commanders in Chinese military history, began a thirty-year campaign..."

**14. [Ch 5, Newcomer] "Western Jin dynasty" dropped without definition.**
Line 279: "During the collapse of the Western Jin dynasty (which had briefly reunified China in 280 CE)" — good parenthetical definition but it would help to note that this is roughly 400 years after the Han dynasty. The reader has been in Han-era China for three chapters and may lose the timeline.
**Fix:** Add "nearly four centuries after the Han" to the parenthetical.

**15. [Ch 8, Cross-Cultural] Missing cross-reference to Ancient Rome for the 476 CE fall.**
Line 457: "the Western Empire was already weakening before Attila, and its fall in 476 CE had many causes" — this is a key event covered in the shipped Ancient Rome narrative.
**Fix:** Add "(Ancient Rome Ch 10)" after "its fall in 476 CE."

**16. [Ch 2, Skeptic] "dwarfed anything the Scythians had controlled" — bold comparative claim.**
Line 85: "It was the largest steppe empire the world had yet seen, and it dwarfed anything the Scythians had controlled." The Xiongnu territory was indeed larger, but "dwarfed" is strong language — the Scythian sphere of influence (Pontic steppe to the Altai, with cultural reach into Central Asia) was substantial. The Xiongnu controlled more territory but calling it "dwarfing" might overstate the contrast.
**Fix:** Change "dwarfed" to "significantly exceeded."

**17. [Ch 4, Cross-Cultural] Persian Empire missing from Silk Road passage.**
Line 217: "The commercial network that would connect the Han and Roman empires..." The Persian/Parthian Empire sat between them and controlled the middle sections of the Silk Road — this is directly relevant to the shipped Persian Empire narrative.
**Fix:** Change to "The commercial network that would connect the Han and Roman empires (with the Parthian/Persian Empire controlling the crucial middle sections, Persian Empire Ch 5)..."

**18. [Ch 7, Newcomer] "Gepid" peoples introduced without definition.**
Line 365: "Ostrogoths, Gepids, Rugians, Heruli, Scirians" — the Ostrogoths and Visigoths get good definitions. The Gepids, Rugians, Heruli, and Scirians are listed but only the Gepids get a definition later (Ch 8). For Ch 7, these are just names on a list.
**Fix:** At minimum, add "(various Germanic peoples)" after the list on line 365, or cut the names that aren't load-bearing and replace with "and other Germanic subject peoples."

**19. [Ch 1, Newcomer] "Tengri" gets a one-clause definition but no cultural context.**
Line 95: "Tengri (the eternal sky god of the steppe peoples)" — good definition, but this is one of the most important religious concepts in the narrative. A second sentence noting that Tengri-worship was the common religious thread across steppe peoples from the Xiongnu through the Mongols would give the reader a sense of its significance.
**Fix:** After the parenthetical definition, add: "Tengri-worship was the shared religious foundation of virtually every steppe people from the Xiongnu through the Mongols, and the chanyu's claim to rule rested on his connection to the sky."

## Summary

This is an excellent narrative — one of the strongest in the collection. The 8-chapter structure handles the Xiongnu-to-Hun transition gracefully, with Ch 6 as the honest pivot point. The scholarly debate about the Xiongnu-Hun connection is handled with exemplary balance (presenting both sides, naming the evidence types, refusing to pretend the question is settled). The Modu-Attila structural parallel gives the whole narrative cohesion across its 700-year timespan.

The must-fix items are minor: two factual precision issues (Flagellum Dei attribution, Venice founding), one editorial overreach (Huo Qubing death), one missing cross-reference (Qin Dynasty), and one uncertain number (five claimants). The should-fix items are mostly about adding cross-references to shipped TLs (Scythians, Ancient Rome, Persian Empire, Maurya Empire) and tightening definitions for minor peoples/places. Writing-rule compliance is strong: no em-dash definitions, no "we met" phrasing, no repeated uncertainty language, pronunciations consistently provided, parenthetical style correct throughout.

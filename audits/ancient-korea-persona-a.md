## Chapter 1 — The Peninsula

1. **"Chinese civilization is older, larger, richer, and more powerful than Korean civilization by almost every measure"** — "almost every measure" is vague — what specific measures? Population? Territory? Technology? Give the reader at least one concrete comparison.

2. **"the Korean language (which belongs to a completely different language family from Chinese, as unrelated to Mandarin as English is to Japanese)"** — what language family does Korean belong to? The reader gets told it's different but not what it actually is. Is it Altaic, Japonic, an isolate?

3. **"Korean immigrants helped build early Japanese civilization in ways that Japanese textbooks have historically downplayed and Korean textbooks have historically emphasized"** — this is an empty claim without any concrete example — what specifically did Korean immigrants do? What was downplayed?

4. **"Manchuria (the vast, cold region of northeastern China, stretching up toward Siberia)"** — good inline definition, but the reader has no idea what Siberia is or where it is. Minor, since most readers may know Siberia = cold Russia.

## Chapter 2 — Dangun and Bronze

1. **"three divine seals"** — what are divine seals? Stamps? Symbols? Objects? The reader has nothing to picture.

2. **"the Samguk Yusa (sahm-GOOK YOO-sah), a thirteenth-century compilation of myths and histories"** — good definition, but what distinguishes it from other histories of the period? Is it the main source? Is it reliable or considered mythological?

3. **"bear worship was widespread across northern Asia, from Manchuria through Siberia to Scandinavia"** — Scandinavia is introduced without definition. Most readers know roughly where it is, but the jump from Manchuria to Scandinavia is jarring — how did bear worship connect across such a vast distance?

4. **"the Shang (Ancient China, Chapter 8)"** — this cross-reference to "Chapter 8" seems wrong — is this meant to refer to a chapter in the Ancient China TL rather than this TL? Confusing for the reader.

5. **"bronze-working technology spread into the peninsula from Manchuria and northern China"** — how did it spread? Trade? Migration? Conquest? The reader gets the result but not the mechanism.

6. **"ritual mirrors and elaborate bronze bells that served ceremonial rather than practical purposes"** — what ceremonies? What did people do with the bells and mirrors? The reader can't picture the ritual.

7. **"Mycenaean civilization in Greece"** — Mycenaean is introduced without any definition. Who were the Mycenaeans? What were they known for?

8. **"they are generally interpreted as elite burial markers"** — interpreted by whom? Archaeologists? Korean scholars? What evidence supports "elite burial" over other interpretations?

9. **"pulling on ropes, rolling the stone on logs, levering it up ramps of packed earth"** — is this documented or speculated? The text presents it as fact but then says there's no evidence they had wheels or draft animals.

10. **"Stonehenge in England was built by a similar type of chieftain society"** — Stonehenge gets no inline definition. The reader is a newcomer to ALL of history — what is Stonehenge?

11. **"the Neolithic cultures of China (Ancient China, Chapters 3-5)"** — another cross-reference to the Ancient China TL that could confuse readers expecting chapters within this book.

12. **"Jeulmun (JUHL-moon) comb-pattern ware"** — what is "comb-pattern"? What does this pottery look like? Why is the shift to plain pottery significant beyond what's stated?

## Chapter 3 — Iron and Empires

1. **"roughly 1,200 degrees Celsius, which requires a forced-air bellows"** — what is a forced-air bellows? The newcomer reader needs this explained — is it a bag you pump? A tube you blow through?

2. **"The Hittites in Anatolia (modern Turkey) were among the earliest iron workers"** — who were the Hittites? The reader has never heard of them. A one-line definition is needed.

3. **"the Bronze Age Collapse (roughly 1200–1000 BCE)"** — what was the Bronze Age Collapse? This is a major historical event introduced without any explanation.

4. **"Wiman (WEE-mahn), also known by the Chinese reading of his name Wei Man, was either a Chinese or a Yan refugee"** — what is Yan? The parenthetical explains it's a warring state, but the reader doesn't know what "warring Chinese states" means — is this the Warring States period? When?

5. **"the rising Han Dynasty in China (the Han came to power in 206 BCE and would rule China for over four centuries)"** — good definition, but what made the Han "rising"? What were they known for?

6. **"Samhan (SAHM-hahn), meaning 'Three Han.' (This 'Han' is a Korean word unrelated to the Chinese Han Dynasty"** — excellent disambiguation. Well done.

7. **"sacred groves, ritual poles, and spirit worship"** — what did the ritual poles look like? What spirits were worshipped? This is close to an empty claim — the reader gets a list but no concrete image.

8. **"held communal festivals after planting and harvest, with dancing, drinking, and ceremonies to ensure good crops"** — what did the ceremonies look like? This is stated so generically it could be about any agricultural society.

9. **"Berossus, Diodorus, Strabo"** — not present in this text, but **Sima Qian** is used without much introduction beyond "Chinese historian." When did he live? Why is his account trusted? What is the Shiji?

10. **"lacquerware"** — what is lacquerware? The reader may not know. A brief parenthetical definition would help.

## Chapter 4 — Three Kingdoms

1. **"Kim Bu-sik"** — who is this historian? When did he live (beyond the date)? Why was he compiling this history? Was he associated with one of the kingdoms?

2. **"Samguk Sagi"** — this is defined as the "History of the Three Kingdoms" but the reader doesn't know its reliability or significance relative to the Samguk Yusa mentioned in Chapter 2. Are these the two main sources? How do they differ?

3. **"a divine archer born from an egg"** — egg-birth myths appear both here and with Silla. The text notes "Korea is the land of egg births" but doesn't explain why egg-birth myths are so common in Korean culture. What is the cultural significance?

4. **"the Khitan (kih-TAHN, a nomadic people who would later found the Liao dynasty in China)"** — mentioned in Chapter 5 but introduced without saying who they are in terms the reader can understand. The parenthetical about the Liao dynasty means nothing to the newcomer.

5. **"glass beads that arrived via the Silk Road from as far away as the Roman Empire"** — the Silk Road is introduced here without definition. What is it? The reader needs at least a brief parenthetical.

6. **"the bone-rank system (golpum in Korean), a hereditary social hierarchy so rigid it makes European feudalism look flexible"** — good comparison, but "European feudalism" is undefined. The newcomer may not know what feudalism was or why this comparison matters.

7. **"what the Ruhr Valley was to nineteenth-century Germany or Pittsburgh was to nineteenth-century America: the iron works"** — these are modern analogies that work for context but assume the reader knows about the Ruhr Valley. Pittsburgh is more universally known, but Ruhr Valley may not be.

8. **"the Wa (WAH), the Chinese and Korean term for the Japanese"** — good definition. But why was this term used? Is it neutral or pejorative?

## Chapter 5 — Goguryeo: The Warrior Kingdom

1. **"the Sixteen Kingdoms period"** — mentioned in passing without explanation. What was this period? Why was China fragmented?

2. **"Teotihuacan"** — mentioned as a parallel but the reader has never heard of it. What is Teotihuacan? Where is it? What was it?

3. **"the four directional guardians of East Asian cosmology: the Azure Dragon, the White Tiger, the Red Phoenix, and the Black Turtle-Snake"** — what are these? Are they deities? Symbols? Why four directions? The reader gets the names but no understanding of what they represent or why they appear in tombs.

4. **"a hybrid cosmology mixing shamanism, Buddhism, Taoism, and Confucianism"** — Taoism is introduced here for the first time with no definition. What is Taoism? When and how did it reach Korea?

5. **"Sui Yangdi (SWAY YAHNG-dee), was ambitious, ruthless, and determined to restore China's dominance"** — "ambitious, ruthless" are generic. What specific things did he do that were ruthless?

6. **"over one million soldiers for the 612 invasion — a figure that, even if exaggerated, reflects one of the largest military forces ever assembled in the ancient world"** — the text acknowledges exaggeration but doesn't give any sense of what a realistic number might be. How big were typical ancient armies for comparison?

7. **"a mocking poem to the Sui commander before the final ambush"** — what did the poem say? This is a great anecdote that is left half-told. The reader wants the content.

8. **"Tang China, the superpower of the medieval world — the civilization that invented gunpowder"** — gunpowder was not actually invented in the Tang period (most scholarship dates it to the 9th century CE, late Tang or after). This claim could be misleading. But as a newcomer, I'd wonder: what is gunpowder's significance here? It's a throwaway detail.

9. **"Chang'an (the world's largest city)"** — Chang'an is mentioned without any definition beyond "world's largest city." Where is it? How large? Why was it significant?

## Chapter 6 — Baekje and the Buddha

1. **"Siddhartha Gautama"** — defined as the Buddha, but the newcomer gets no sense of what Buddhism actually teaches. "Suffering, death, and the afterlife" is mentioned later, but the core teachings (Four Noble Truths, the Middle Way) are never explained even briefly.

2. **"Sanskrit and Pali"** — what are these languages? Where were they spoken? Why were Buddhist texts in these languages?

3. **"Eastern Jin dynasty in China"** — another Chinese dynasty mentioned without context. What period? Where? The reader is drowning in Chinese dynasty names by this point.

4. **"bodhisattvas (beings who have achieved enlightenment but remain in the world to help others)"** — good inline definition.

5. **"the famous Inariyama Sword inscription (471 CE)"** — what is this sword? Where was it found? Why is it famous? The reader gets a name and date but no context.

6. **"Horyuji (HOR-yoo-jee), one of the oldest surviving wooden buildings in the world"** — good, but where in Japan? And how old exactly?

7. **"Mireuksa (mee-ROOK-sah) temple, constructed around 602 CE"** — what does "Mireuksa" mean? The other temple names get translations.

8. **"pagoda (a multi-story tower used as a reliquary and focal point for worship)"** — good inline definition.

9. **"Idu (EE-doo)"** — well explained. But the text says "by all accounts, clumsy" — whose accounts? Modern scholars? Koreans at the time?

10. **"Hangul (HAHN-gool), in the fifteenth century — one of the most brilliant writing-system inventions in human history"** — this is an empty superlative — what makes it brilliant? Even a single sentence explaining its phonetic design would satisfy the reader's curiosity.

## Chapter 7 — The Great Unification War

1. **"Yeon Gaesomun (YON GEH-soh-moon) had seized power in a bloody coup, killing over one hundred Goguryeo aristocrats"** — how did he kill them? In a single event? Over time? Was this a palace massacre?

2. **"Queen Seondeok (SUN-duk), Korea's first female ruler"** — how did a woman become ruler in a patriarchal bone-rank system? This is a remarkable fact that gets no explanation.

3. **"General Su Dingfang"** — introduced as a Tang general but we learn nothing about him. Is he significant beyond this battle?

4. **"Gyebaek (GYE-beck)"** — introduced as a Baekje general making a last stand but we learn nothing about his background before this moment.

5. **"reportedly killed his own family before the battle to free himself from any temptation to surrender"** — this is a dramatic claim that the reader will want more context on. Is this confirmed? Is it a common ancient trope?

6. **"three thousand court women of Baekje threw themselves from the cliff"** — "three thousand is almost certainly symbolic" — symbolic of what? What does the number represent?

7. **"Maeso Fortress and Kibolpo"** — mentioned as critical battles but get no description. Where are they? What happened? The reader needs at least one sentence about what made them decisive.

8. **"six hundred miles from your nearest supply base"** — from whose supply base? China's? This distance claim needs a reference point.

## Chapter 8 — Unified Silla

This is a dangling reference — the reader expects to learn about the companion site but it's never named.

4. **"corbelling (each course of stone projecting slightly inward over the one below, creating a dome without a keystone or mortar)"** — excellent inline definition.

6. **"the oldest surviving woodblock-printed document in the world, predating the more famous Chinese Diamond Sutra (868 CE) by over a century"** — what is the Diamond Sutra? The reader learns it's Chinese and dates to 868 CE, but has no idea what it is or why it's famous.

7. **"362 stones — a number often interpreted as representing the days of the lunar year"** — a lunar year has approximately 354 days (12 lunar months x 29.5 days). 362 does not match. This needs explanation — is the interpretation contested?

8. **"hyangga (HYAHNG-gah) poetic tradition"** — what do the surviving poems sound like? Even one brief quote or paraphrase would help the reader understand the tradition.

9. **"celadon pottery"** — what is celadon? "Jade-green glazed stoneware" is good but what makes it so significant? Why is it "among the finest ceramics ever produced"?

10. **"glass vessels in Silla royal tombs include types that originated in the eastern Mediterranean"** — the eastern Mediterranean is referenced without any specific civilization or culture attached. Roman? Byzantine? Egyptian?

11. **"Jang Bogo (JAHNG BOH-goh)"** — described as "the most powerful maritime figure in East Asia" but the reader has no comparison. More powerful than Chinese admirals? Japanese traders?

12. **"Wang Geon (WAHNG GUN)"** — his rise to power is well told, but the transition from Gung Ye's tyranny to Wang Geon's takeover happens very quickly. How did the overthrow actually happen?

13. **"Goryeo (GO-ryuh) — from which the English name 'Korea' derives"** — great etymology note.

14. **"the companion site for his parents of a previous life"** — this is never identified. Is it Seokguram? Another temple? The reader is left hanging.

# Indus Valley Summary Fact-Check

Audit of `narratives/indus-valley.summaries.json` against `narratives/indus-valley.md`. Each bullet checked for dates, numbers, names, attributions, and compression drift. The summaries turn out to be very faithful to the source narrative; only a handful of minor imprecisions surfaced.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Forgotten Civilization

**Bullet 7** (starts with "The Indus script — roughly 400..."):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "The Indus script — roughly 400 symbols carved on small stone seals — has resisted decipherment for over a century."
- **Problem:** Implies the script is carried only on stone seals. The narrative notes the script is also painted on pottery and impressed into clay tags/sealings, and Chapter 5 mentions the gypsum-letter Dholavira Signboard.
- **Source says:** Ch 6: "the roughly 400 signs carved on seals, painted on pottery, and impressed into clay"; Ch 4: "carved on small steatite (soapstone) seals, pressed into clay tags, and occasionally painted on pottery."
- **Suggested fix:** "The Indus script — roughly 400 symbols carved on small stone seals and impressed into clay tags — has resisted decipherment for over a century."

## Chapter 2 — The Deep Roots — Mehrgarh

No drift found. Dates, tool type ("flint-tipped bow drills"), copper smelting ~5000 BCE, 4,500-year occupation span, Bolan Pass crossroads claim, and the 5000–3500 BCE village-spread note all match the narrative exactly.

Note on bullet 1: The bullet's "7000 BCE — 4,500 years before the pyramids" is historically accurate (Giza ~2500 BCE). The narrative itself has a slightly confused sentence saying "the Indus cities were still 4,500 years in the future. The pyramids of Egypt wouldn't be built for another 4,500 years after *that*" — which would incorrectly place the pyramids at ~2000 BCE or earlier in relative terms. The bullet is more accurate than the narrative sentence it compresses, so no fix is needed on the bullet; flagging the narrative sentence as a separate issue to consider.

## Chapter 3 — The Dress Rehearsal

No drift found. Ravi Phase proto-writing at Harappa ~3300 BCE, Kot Diji ~2800 BCE fortified walls in Sindh, Kalibangan cross-plow field, the regional burn-layer disruption ~2600 BCE, and the 1:2:4 brick ratio rebuild all match.

One note: Bullet 2 says Kot Diji built "massive stone defensive walls." The narrative says the walls were "about 260 feet (80 meters) long and rising 16 feet (5 meters)" but does not specify stone construction in so many words — the narrative says "cutting and hauling stone" so stone is clearly implied. Not a drift.

## Chapter 5 — The Great Cities

**Bullet 2** (starts with "Harappa, the northern anchor city..."):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "Bead-making workshops processing carnelian and lapis lazuli for export"
- **Problem:** Minor compression — narrative lists three raw materials: carnelian, lapis lazuli, **and agate**. Bullet drops agate.
- **Source says:** Ch 5: "Bead-making workshops at Harappa processed carnelian, lapis lazuli, and agate into finished jewelry"
- **Suggested fix:** "Bead-making workshops processing carnelian, lapis lazuli, and agate for export"

Otherwise Ch 5 is clean: Mohenjo-daro 620 acres / up to 80,000 people / 700 wells; Dholavira 60-foot walls, 1,200-year occupation, ten 15-inch gypsum Signboard letters; Rakhigarhi 2019 DNA study showing minimal steppe ancestry; Lothal 3,000 semi-finished beads and tidal-basin dockyard interpretation.

## Chapter 6 — The Invisible Rulers

**Bullet 2** (starts with "The leading theory is that cities were governed..."):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "something like the merchant councils that ran medieval Venice"
- **Problem:** Word choice drift — narrative says "merchant guilds" (not "councils") in reference to medieval Italian city-states like Venice and Florence. The bullet's swap to "councils" is defensible (and arguably more accurate for Venice's actual governance by the Great Council / Council of Ten), but it differs from the narrative's phrasing and could be read as the bullet making a slightly different historical claim.
- **Source says:** Ch 6: "something like the merchant guilds that ran medieval Italian city-states like Venice and Florence"
- **Suggested fix:** Either leave as-is (the bullet's "councils" framing actually reads as more historically precise) or revert to narrative wording: "something like the merchant guilds that ran medieval Italian city-states like Venice."

Ch 6 is otherwise clean: Priest-King 7.3-inch steatite bust, over 4,000 seals, unicorn motif on majority of seals, logo-syllabic / right-to-left / undeciphered, no Rosetta Stone, possibly early Dravidian, predating Greek democracy by ~2,000 years.

## Chapter 7 — A World Connected

No drift found. The Sargon ~2350 BCE date, the 1,550-mile Arabian Sea distance, Dilmun = Bahrain entrepôt with both weight systems, Shortughai 930-mile permanent outpost for Badakhshan lapis and tin, long carnelian beads in Mesopotamian royal tombs and Egyptian jewelry, cotton domestication, Dancing Girl 4.25-inch lost-wax bronze, toy carts with movable wheels and early dice all match the narrative exactly.

One light note: Bullet 4 says Shortughai gave access to "Badakhshan lapis lazuli and tin for bronze production." The narrative is slightly more hedged on tin ("tin deposits needed for bronze production" — present tense, but the narrative elsewhere says "tin from Afghanistan" as a general sourcing claim). No drift — the bullet tracks the narrative's claim.

## Chapter 9 — The Quiet Collapse

**Bullet 4** (starts with "Trade with Mesopotamia collapsed around 2000 BCE..."):
- [SOFT] **Claim:** "Trade with Mesopotamia collapsed around 2000 BCE"
- **Problem:** The narrative's phrasing is more hedged — "contracted sharply after 2000 BCE" — and "collapsed around 2000 BCE" slightly overstates the pace. Very minor.
- **Source says:** Ch 9: "The Mesopotamian trade that the Harappans had sustained for over four centuries appears to have contracted sharply after 2000 BCE."
- **Suggested fix:** "Trade with Mesopotamia contracted sharply after 2000 BCE: Mesopotamian texts stop mentioning Meluhha, and the bead factories and copper smelters lost the export markets that had sustained urban specialization."

Otherwise Ch 9 is clean: 1900 BCE slow-empty collapse, 4.2 kiloyear event and Akkadian parallel, Ghaggar-Hakra drying / upstream capture / upstream-first abandonment, eastward migration to Ganges-Yamuna doab and Gujarat coast, Mortimer Wheeler skeleton debunking, Cemetery H peacock pottery with cremation in urns, Pirak horse and camel figurines, 1300 BCE fade date.

## Chapter 10 — Two Worlds Meet

No drift found. 1500 BCE Indo-Aryan arrival via Hindu Kush as pastoralists (not army), horse absence from Mature Harappan contexts with Surkotada finds rejected, Rigveda's 1,028 hymns to Indra/Agni/Varuna with millennium-long oral transmission, Mitanni chariot-warrior elite worshipping Mitra and Varuna, DNA-confirmed blending, Harappan substrate contributions (agriculture, possible ritual bathing / proto-Shiva / fire altars), the 3,000-year forgetting, and Banerji's 1922 rewrite of South Asian history all match the narrative exactly.

---

**Total: 0 STRONG, 0 CHECK, 4 SOFT**

The Indus Valley summary file is in very good shape. No factual drift rising to STRONG or CHECK level. The four SOFT items are minor compression choices: Ch 1 omitting non-seal script carriers, Ch 5 dropping agate from Harappa's bead-material list, Ch 6 swapping "guilds" for "councils," and Ch 9 tightening "contracted sharply after 2000 BCE" to "collapsed around 2000 BCE." None are worth a release-blocking fix; all could be touched up in a maintenance pass.

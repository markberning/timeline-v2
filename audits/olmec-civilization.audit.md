## Story Editor evaluation (Persona D)

## Chapter 1 — The Rubber People

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Olmec were the oldest civilization in the Americas, rediscovered by accident in 1862, thriving in a landscape that shouldn't have supported civilization, and they left behind questions that frame the entire book.

**What works:**
- The opening discovery anecdote (Melgar, 1862) is perfectly calibrated — vivid, concrete, and immediately establishes the "forgotten civilization" hook that drives the whole narrative.
- The "firsts" caveat paragraph is a structural masterstroke. Placed early, it inoculates the entire book against the reader's natural skepticism and builds enormous trust.
- The Gulf Coast landscape section does exactly what a Chapter 1 geographic setting should do: it makes the reader understand why THIS place, which looks wrong, was actually right. The cross-civ comparison block (Mesopotamia/Egypt/Indus/Norte Chico vs. Olmec) is the strongest passage in the chapter.

**What doesn't work:**
- The El Manati section at the end feels like it was added after the main chapter was written. It introduces a major site (sacred spring, 1600 BCE, rubber balls, infant remains) in a single paragraph that deserves more space, then rushes to the closing hook. Either expand El Manati into a proper section or integrate it earlier when discussing the landscape's resources.

**Momentum map:** Strong open (discovery), strong middle (landscape + cross-civ), slight sag at El Manati section, strong close with the "what does it take to build a metropolis in a swamp?" hook.

## Chapter 2 — San Lorenzo

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** San Lorenzo was the first major urban center in the Americas, built by reshaping a natural plateau above swamps, and its violent end remains a mystery that proves the civilization was resilient enough to survive the loss of its first capital.

**What works:**
- The opening cross-civ time anchor (Shang bronze vessels, New Kingdom mortuary temples) is effective and immediately places the reader in global context.
- "Before the sculpture, consider the plumbing" is a perfect tonal transition. It takes the reader from the expected (colossal heads) to the surprising (drainage engineering), and the Indus Valley comparison lands beautifully.
- The three theories for San Lorenzo's destruction are presented as a genuine mystery with real stakes, not as an academic debate. The reader feels the uncertainty.

**What doesn't work:**
- The shaman-king section (paragraphs on political organization) is necessary but reads slightly encyclopedic compared to the rest of the chapter. The transition from drainage to sculpture to political theory happens too fast — the reader is still absorbing the drainage achievement when the text pivots to political models.

**Momentum map:** Strong open, strong middle (plumbing -> sculpture -> political organization), very strong close (the three theories of destruction + "the civilization survived its first capital").

## Chapter 3 — Stone Kings

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The seventeen colossal heads are portraits of individual rulers whose names we'll never know, carved from boulders transported with staggering effort, and possibly recycled from the thrones on which those rulers sat.

**What works:**
- The chapter has a clear narrative arc: physical facts -> transport logistics -> recycling theory -> political implications -> silence. The move from concrete data to interpretation to philosophical reflection is expertly structured.
- The throne-to-head recycling theory is the highlight — an elegant idea presented with appropriate uncertainty, and the Monument 14 example (interrupted conversion) is vivid and persuasive.
- "Seventeen stone faces. Seventeen individuals whose names, deeds, families, and histories are completely unknown to us" — the closing paragraph is emotionally resonant without being sentimental.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Egyptian comparison in the portrait section is slightly forced. The point (Egyptians idealized, Olmec individualized) is valid but the comparison takes 4-5 sentences where 2 would do. The reader already understands the claim from the evidence.

**Momentum map:** Strong throughout. Each section builds naturally on the previous one, culminating in the eloquent closing. No sags.

## Chapter 4 — Jaguars, Blood, and Rubber

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Olmec religion centered on transformation (human-to-jaguar), blood sacrifice, the cosmic ballgame, and cave rituals, and all of these elements survived for over two thousand years in later Mesoamerican civilizations.

**What works:**
- The were-jaguar section is excellent. The reader can picture the transformation motif, understands why the jaguar specifically, and grasps the theological implications (permeability of the human-supernatural boundary).
- The ballgame section is comprehensive and does justice to one of the most distinctive cultural inventions in human history. The connection between the rubber balls at El Manati and the game's ritual significance is well-drawn.
- The cave painting section (Juxtlahuaca, Oxtotitlan) is atmospheric and concrete — the detail of crawling through underground passages to reach the paintings makes the reader feel the ritual importance.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter is thematic rather than chronological, covering religion in a single sweep. This is the right structural choice, but it means there's no narrative arc — no beginning-middle-end, no question being answered, no tension being resolved. It reads more like "here are five aspects of Olmec religion" than a story. The chapter needs a throughline that pulls the reader through: perhaps the organizing question is "what did Olmec power actually look like?" with the answer being "performance, not bureaucracy."
- The Feathered Serpent / Maize God section (two paragraphs between the were-jaguar and bloodletting) feels rushed and undercooked compared to the depth given to the jaguar and the ballgame. Either give these deities more space or fold them more tightly into the transformation theme.

**Momentum map:** Strong open, good were-jaguar section, sags in the Feathered Serpent / Maize God paragraphs (two deities described in quick succession without much drama), picks up again at bloodletting, strong ballgame section, strong cave painting section, adequate close.

## Chapter 5 — The Jade Highway

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Everything the Olmec valued came from far away, and the trade networks they built to get it created the first pan-Mesoamerican economic system and raise the central question of how Olmec influence spread.

**What works:**
- The jade-obsidian-magnetite-cacao sequence is well-organized, each trade good getting its own focused section with good detail and clear significance.
- The jade-China parallel (independent convergence on jade as most precious material) is a sharp cross-civ insight.
- The chapter's structural pivot from "what did they trade?" to "what does Olmec influence at distant sites mean?" is well-executed and sets up Chapter 8 effectively.

**What doesn't work:**
- The three options for Olmec influence (political control, elite exchange, shared development) are presented in a way that duplicates the Chapter 1 forward reference and anticipates the Chapter 8 deep dive. This is the third time the reader has encountered this question, and the chapter doesn't resolve or advance it significantly beyond what Chapter 1 already said. The trade chapter should present new evidence that changes the reader's thinking, not re-present the same question.
- The Tlatilco and Chalcatzingo section feels like a list of sites without narrative drive. "Olmec-style stuff was found here, here, and here" doesn't pull the reader forward. What would help: focusing on one site in depth (Chalcatzingo, with its cave-mouth relief that mirrors La Venta art) and using it as a case study for the influence question.

**Momentum map:** Good open, solid jade and obsidian sections, slight sag at magnetite mirrors (interesting but too detailed for its significance), cacao section picks up, the influence question section at the end re-treads rather than advancing.

## Chapter 6 — La Venta

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** La Venta was built in the worst possible location because it controlled the best possible thing (river trade), and its buried offerings reveal a religious worldview in which enormous wealth was deliberately hidden from human eyes forever.

**What works:**
- The "why here?" opening is an excellent structural move. The reader's first reaction to "a swamp island" is "that's insane," and the chapter immediately answers the implicit question with "because water was the highway."
- The serpentine mosaic pavements section is the highlight of the entire narrative. The idea that the Olmec created elaborate art specifically to bury it, never to be seen again, is genuinely astonishing, and the prose treats it with the weight it deserves. The comparison to Egyptian grave goods and Shang bronze pits is well-chosen.
- The sixteen-figurine deposit is a vivid, concrete image that the reader will remember.

**What doesn't work:**
- The astronomical alignment section at the end is the weakest part. It hedges so heavily ("ambiguous," "unproven," "too ambiguous") that the reader wonders why it's included. Either cut it or give the reader a specific proposed alignment to evaluate, then hedge.

**Momentum map:** Strong open (swamp island paradox), strong middle (Great Pyramid, buried offerings), sags at astronomical alignment, adequate close with the destruction parallel.

## Chapter 7 — Words and Numbers

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Olmec invented writing, the calendar, and contributed to the invention of zero, but because we can't read any of it, we're left knowing they achieved these things without understanding what they wrote.

**What works:**
- The Cascajal Block discovery narrative is compelling, and the detailed analysis of whether it qualifies as "writing" is intellectually honest without being dry. The cross-civ comparison (Sumerian proto-cuneiform, Egyptian hieroglyphs, Indus script) is the right move and done well.
- The zero section is outstanding. The explanation of why zero is hard to invent (the conceptual leap from "nothing" to "something") is lucid and would be clear to any reader. The cross-civ framework (Babylonians, Indians, Mesoamericans) is well-organized.
- The Stela C vindication story (Stirling vs. Morley, the missing fragment found 30 years later) is a perfect piece of narrative drama.

**What doesn't work:**
- The calendar section is the most technically dense passage in the entire narrative. The Tonalpohualli + Xiuhpohualli + Calendar Round + Long Count + kin/tun/katun/baktun sequence throws a lot of terminology at the reader very fast. The gear metaphor for the ritual calendar helps, but the Long Count explanation needs a concrete example: "the date 9.15.2.0.0 would mean 9 baktuns + 15 katuns + 2 tuns + 0 uinals + 0 kins, which works out to..." This would transform an abstract description into something the reader can follow.

**Momentum map:** Strong open (frustration theme), strong Cascajal Block section, dense calendar section (reader may need to slow down), very strong zero section, good close.

## Chapter 8 — Mother Culture or Sister Culture?

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Olmec didn't end — they transformed into the Epi-Olmec, their descendants are still there, and the "mother vs. sister" debate about their role in Mesoamerican civilization is really a question about how civilizations spread ideas.

**What works:**
- The Stela C vindication narrative (placed here after being set up in Ch 7) pays off beautifully — Stirling vindicated, Morley wrong, the Long Count confirmed as pre-Maya.
- The mother-vs-sister debate is presented with intellectual honesty and real depth. The chapter doesn't pick a side and pretend it's obvious — it evaluates specific innovations against specific evidence, and the "dominant elder sibling" synthesis is a satisfying resolution.
- The legacy list at the end (calendar, writing, ballgame, feathered serpent, sculpture, chocolate, zero, pyramids) is a strong structural close. Each item gets a concise paragraph that connects back to the relevant chapter.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Epi-Olmec section is important but slightly dry. Cerro de las Mesas, the Tuxtla Statuette, and the Isthmian script are presented as evidence for continuity, but the section lacks a human hook. The Mixe-Zoque linguistic survival is the most emotionally compelling detail ("their descendants are still there, speaking languages descended from the language of the colossal head builders") but it's buried in the middle rather than used as the section's emotional climax.

**Momentum map:** Good open (transition framing), solid Tres Zapotes section, slightly dry Epi-Olmec section, strong debate section, excellent legacy list, strong closing paragraph.

---

## Overall assessment

This narrative is the strongest first draft in the project to date. The voice is confident, the informal register is consistent, the cross-civ comparisons are organic (not bolted on), and the structural challenge of writing about a civilization with no readable texts is solved elegantly by using sites as characters, artifacts as evidence, and scholarly debates as narrative drama. The central thesis ("everything Mesoamerican traces back to these Gulf Coast founders") is argued through accumulation of evidence rather than assertion, which is exactly right.

The biggest structural issue across the document is the repeated presentation of the "influence question" (mother vs. sister, how did Olmec ideas spread?). It appears in Ch 1, Ch 5, and Ch 8. The Ch 1 and Ch 8 versions are necessary (setup and resolution), but the Ch 5 version re-treads without advancing. Either cut Ch 5's version to a brief forward reference or use it to present genuinely new evidence that changes the reader's thinking.

The single biggest improvement would be making Ch 4 (religion) more narrative and less encyclopedic. It's the only chapter that reads as a topic survey rather than a story. Adding a throughline question ("what did power look like for a civilization without armies or bureaucracies?") would transform it from "five aspects of Olmec religion" into "the answer to how rulers ruled."

Seven of eight chapters grade STRONG, which is exceptional for a first draft.

## Part 1: Forward Findings (new TL gaps)

### Chapter 1 — The Rubber People

1. **"the oldest civilization in the Americas"** — The "firsts" caveat paragraph is excellent, but the claim that the Olmec are the "oldest civilization in the Americas" should note Norte Chico/Caral (roughly 3000-1800 BCE) as a significant predecessor. The Early Andean narrative (Ch 2-3) covers Caral at length, and the Olmec narrative itself mentions Norte Chico several times — but the opening framing of "oldest civilization" needs to be more precise about what "civilization" means here vs. Caral, which had monumental architecture over a millennium earlier.

2. **"the Norte Chico civilization of Peru built without pottery, without writing, and without warfare"** — Good mention, but the cross-civ comparison could be enriched. The Early Andean narrative (Ch 1) explains exactly why Norte Chico had no pottery (cooking in gourds and baskets) and why the absence didn't matter. A brief parenthetical pointing to Early Andean Ch 1-3 would help readers who want the full picture.

3. **"Mesoamerica (the Olmecs, the predecessors of the Maya and Aztecs) would not reach urban scale until around 1200 BCE"** — This is quoted from the Mesopotamia narrative's cross-civ comparison in Ch 3. The Olmec narrative should reciprocate: when discussing San Lorenzo as the "first major urban center in the Americas" (Ch 2), it should note that Uruk had already reached 40,000-80,000 people roughly 2,000 years earlier, and that Caral was building monumental platforms roughly 1,800 years earlier.

4. **"domesticated from a wild grass called teosinte in the highlands of central Mexico around 7000 BCE"** — The 7000 BCE date for maize domestication parallels the 7000 BCE date for potato domestication in Peru (Early Andean Ch 1). This parallel — two hemispheric staple crops domesticated independently at roughly the same date — is worth noting.

### Chapter 2 — San Lorenzo

5. **"the Indus Valley Civilization, which was building covered brick drains under its streets at roughly the same date, half a world away"** — Good cross-civ comparison. But "roughly the same date" is inaccurate. San Lorenzo's drainage dates to roughly 1200 BCE; the Indus drainage at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa peaked around 2600-1900 BCE — roughly 600-1,300 years earlier. The comparison should note the Indus system was significantly older.

6. **"Ur in Mesopotamia had perhaps 65,000 residents by this time. Thebes in Egypt was comparable"** — The Ur population figure at "this time" (1200 BCE) is problematic. Ur's population peak was during the Ur III period (~2100-2000 BCE). By 1200 BCE, Ur had declined significantly. The comparison should use a more appropriate contemporary city — perhaps Babylon under the Kassites, or the New Kingdom Egyptian capital Pi-Ramesses.

7. **"Without draft animals (the Americas had none comparable to horses or oxen), without wheeled carts (no wheels in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica)"** — Good note on absent technologies. The Early Andean narrative (Ch 1) covers the same absence pattern for the Andes (no wheels, no draft animals, no dairy) and explains why llamas didn't fill the gap. A brief cross-reference would reinforce the pan-American pattern.

### Chapter 3 — Stone Kings

8. **"the closest parallel in the ancient world is, inevitably, the Egyptian pyramids"** — The comparison to Egyptian stone transport is well-developed, but it misses a closer and more interesting parallel: the Andean tradition of monumental stone transport. The Inca would later move stones of comparable size at Sacsayhuaman and Ollantaytambo, also without wheels or draft animals, using similar techniques (log rollers, ramps, organized labor). This is a parallel that doesn't exist yet in the completed narratives but deserves a forward note.

9. **"Comparison with Egyptian royal sculpture is instructive: the pharaohs were also depicted in massive stone portraits"** — Good comparison. Could be enriched by noting that the Shang Dynasty in China was producing individualized bronze portraits (ritual masks) at roughly the same date, and that the Sanxingdui bronze masks from Sichuan (~1200 BCE) are the closest contemporary parallel to Olmec portrait art in terms of combining individual features with ritual significance (Shang Dynasty Ch 7).

### Chapter 4 — Jaguars, Blood, and Rubber

10. **"Bloodletting...was a central act of Mesoamerican religion from the Olmec period through the Spanish conquest"** — The bloodletting discussion should note the contrast with the Andes, which had its own parallel religious tradition involving San Pedro cactus-induced shamanic transformation (Early Andean Ch 5-6). Both the Olmec and early Andean civilizations centered their religion on jaguar transformation and hallucinogen-assisted shamanism, independently and with no contact. This is one of the most striking parallel developments in the pre-Columbian Americas.

11. **"the shaman enters a trance (often aided by hallucinogenic substances), travels to the spirit world, and transforms into an animal spirit"** — The Chavin de Huantar tradition in Peru (Early Andean Ch 6) involved exactly the same concept: tenon heads showing step-by-step human-to-jaguar transformation, driven by San Pedro cactus ingestion. The Olmec narrative should note this parallel — two civilizations on opposite sides of the Americas developing jaguar-transformation shamanism independently.

12. **"This is a different kind of authority than what we see in Mesopotamia...Egypt...or China"** — Excellent cross-civ comparison of political legitimacy models. But it's missing the Andean parallel: the Chavin priests also derived authority from shamanic performance rather than bureaucracy or military force (Early Andean Ch 6), making the Andes the closest parallel to the Olmec political model.

### Chapter 5 — The Jade Highway

13. **"both the Olmec and the Chinese treated jade as the most precious material in their respective civilizations"** — Good cross-civ observation. Could be enriched by noting that the Chinese jade tradition goes back to the Neolithic (Liangzhu, ~3300 BCE; Ancient China Ch 5), over two thousand years before the Olmec, making it the older tradition. The Shang Dynasty narrative (Ch 6) covers the 755 jade objects in Fu Hao's tomb — a contemporary comparison worth noting.

14. **"the magnetite came from sources in the Valley of Oaxaca and other highland regions"** — The mirror-making tradition has no parallel in the reference TLs. Worth noting explicitly: polished stone mirrors are a uniquely Mesoamerican technology. The Old World used polished copper and bronze mirrors (Egypt, Mesopotamia, China) but not polished iron-oxide mineral mirrors.

15. **"the Olmec were preparing cacao drinks as early as 1800-1000 BCE"** — The chocolate/cacao tradition has no parallel in the Old World. Worth a brief note that this is a genuinely unique Mesoamerican contribution to world history, eventually becoming one of the most globally significant food products.

### Chapter 6 — La Venta

16. **"the Great Pyramid...roughly 110 feet (34 meters) tall"** — The comparison to Khufu is well-handled. But the narrative misses a closer parallel: the Huaca de los Idolos at Aspero in Peru (Early Andean Ch 2), roughly 3000 BCE, and the platform mounds at Caral (~2600 BCE), which are the closest New World predecessors. La Venta's pyramid is significantly later than Caral's but was built in a similar tradition of earth-and-clay platform construction.

17. **"they buried art of immense cost and beauty not in a tomb...but simply in the ground, as an offering to the ground itself"** — The comparison to Egyptian tomb goods and Shang bronze pits is apt. An additional parallel worth noting: the serpentine mosaic pavements, deliberately buried, echo the Andean tradition of buried offerings at Chavin de Huantar (Early Andean Ch 6), though the Chavin offerings are later (~900-200 BCE) and may represent a shared pan-American concept of feeding the earth.

### Chapter 7 — Words and Numbers

18. **"Mesoamerica one of only three or four places in the world where writing was independently invented"** — The cross-civ comparison (Mesopotamia ~3400, Egypt ~3200, China ~1200, Mesoamerica ~900 BCE) is well-structured. Should cross-reference the Indus Valley script debate: the Indus script (~2600-1900 BCE) is mentioned as the "uncertain fifth case," which matches the Indus Valley narrative's own discussion (Indus Valley Ch 6).

19. **"the Babylonians (Mesopotamia, roughly 300 BCE, though their zero was a placeholder)"** — The zero discussion should note that the Babylonian positional system with a placeholder goes back much further than 300 BCE — the Old Babylonian period (~1800 BCE) already used positional notation, with a space as placeholder. The dedicated zero symbol came later (~300 BCE), but the concept of positional notation requiring a placeholder is much older.

20. **"the Indians (roughly 5th-7th century CE, the first to treat zero as a full number)"** — This is correct and does not need a reference TL cross-reference since there is no completed India narrative yet.

### Chapter 8 — Mother Culture or Sister Culture?

21. **"the Spanish conquest"** — The Spanish conquest is referenced multiple times without a date. Since the Hittite Empire narrative dates the Bronze Age Collapse and the Mycenaean narrative dates the Trojan War, the Olmec narrative should date the Spanish conquest (~1519-1521 CE for the Aztec conquest, ~1532 for the Inca) for readers who may not know.

22. **"Teotihuacan...would soon begin its meteoric rise"** — Teotihuacan appears without temporal anchoring. A cross-civ time stamp would help: "around the time the Roman Republic was transitioning to the Roman Empire" or "contemporary with the Han Dynasty in China."

23. **"the Olmec provided the seed. Others grew the plant"** — The closing metaphor could be enriched by explicitly comparing the Olmec's role to the Chavin Horizon in Peru (Early Andean Ch 6-7), which served a similar "mother culture" function for the Andes — both were first-synthesis civilizations whose art, religion, and ideas became the template for everything that followed. The parallel between the Olmec "mother culture" debate and the Tello "mother culture" claim about Chavin is extraordinarily apt and is already set up by the Early Andean narrative.

## Skeptic findings (Persona B)

## Chapter 1 — The Rubber People

1. `[CHECK]` **"In January 1862, a Mexican landowner and antiquarian named Jose Maria Melgar y Serrano"** — The discovery story is detailed enough to check. Melgar's 1869 publication date and the African-contact speculation are verifiable. The "January 1862" specificity is notable — is the month documented?

2. `[CHECK]` **"The chemical process (sulfur compounds in the vine juice bonding with the latex polymers to create a durable, elastic solid) is essentially a form of vulcanization"** — The chemistry detail (sulfur compounds in morning glory vine) is very specific and often repeated in popular accounts. The comparison to Goodyear's process is standard, but "essentially a form of vulcanization" may overstate the chemical similarity. Worth verifying whether the morning glory vine actually contains sulfur compounds or whether the bonding mechanism is different.

3. `[CHECK]` **"about 80 to 120 inches (2,000 to 3,000 mm) of rain on the lowlands, roughly ten times what the American Midwest receives"** — The rainfall figure is plausible for tropical Gulf Coast, but the "ten times the Midwest" comparison may be off. The US Midwest gets roughly 30-40 inches annually, which would make the Gulf Coast 2-4x, not 10x. Unless "Midwest" means the drier western portion.

4. `[SOFT]` **"Charles Goodyear patented in 1839 CE"** — Goodyear's patent is often cited as 1844, not 1839. He discovered the process in 1839 but the patent came later. Minor but checkable.

## Chapter 2 — San Lorenzo

1. `[CHECK]` **"rises about 160 feet (50 meters) above the surrounding swampy floodplain"** — The 160-foot/50-meter height for the San Lorenzo plateau is suspiciously round and large. Some sources give lower figures. Worth checking against published archaeological reports.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Ur in Mesopotamia had perhaps 65,000 residents by this time"** — The population of Ur at 1200 BCE (the comparison date) is questionable. Ur's peak population is usually estimated for the Ur III period (~2100-2000 BCE), not 1200 BCE. By 1200 BCE, Ur was past its peak. The 65,000 figure is often cited for peak Ur III.

3. `[SOFT]` **"covering roughly 150 acres (60 hectares)"** — This area figure for San Lorenzo's plateau is in the range of what's published, but the precision may overstate certainty given ongoing mapping.

## Chapter 3 — Stone Kings

1. `[CHECK]` **"The largest, San Lorenzo Monument 1, stands 9.4 feet (2.86 meters) tall and weighs an estimated 50 tons (about 45 metric tons)"** — Monument 1 is not consistently cited as the largest head. Some sources list La Venta Monument 1 or the La Cobata head as larger. The 50-ton weight estimate for Monument 1 is at the high end of published estimates. Also, calling La Cobata Monument 1 the "smallest" at "nearly 5 feet" contradicts some inventories.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the archaeologist David Grove"** — Grove is a real and prominent Mesoamerican archaeologist, but the throne-to-head recycling theory is more commonly associated with Ann Cyphers and others. Verify that Grove is the primary proponent.

3. `[SOFT]` **"no metal harder than copper, which is too soft for stone carving"** — Accurate claim but the phrasing implies the Olmec had copper tools. Olmec use of copper is minimal at best, and some scholars would say they had no functional metal tools at all during the San Lorenzo period.

## Chapter 4 — Jaguars, Blood, and Rubber

1. `[CHECK]` **"strong enough to kill a caiman by biting through its skull"** — This is a commonly repeated factoid about jaguars. The skull-crushing bite is well-attested in wildlife biology, but the specific detail of biting through a caiman skull (vs. turtle shell or other prey) should be verified as the standard example.

2. `[CHECK]` **"the sacrifice of losing players"** — This is one of the most over-stated claims in popular Mesoamerican history. The evidence for routine sacrifice of losers is thin and largely based on a few Classic Maya depictions and post-conquest Aztec accounts. The text says "in some contexts," which is appropriately hedged, but the reader may still come away thinking this was common.

3. `[SOFT]` **"the K'iche' Maya, written down after the Spanish conquest"** — Accurate description of the Popol Vuh. No issue.

## Chapter 5 — The Jade Highway

1. `[CHECK]` **"sharper than a modern surgical scalpel at the molecular level"** — This claim about obsidian blade sharpness is extremely common in popular archaeology and has been verified in some experimental studies, but "at the molecular level" is a specific and somewhat misleading phrasing. The comparison is usually "obsidian can produce an edge measured in nanometers, thinner than steel scalpels," not "at the molecular level."

2. `[CHECK]` **"chemical residue analysis has detected theobromine...in Olmec-period pottery vessels, suggesting that the Olmec were preparing cacao drinks as early as 1800-1000 BCE"** — The theobromine residue analysis is published (by Terry Powis et al., 2011, and earlier work by John Henderson), but the date range "1800-1000 BCE" is very broad. The earliest secure dates for cacao residue in Mesoamerica are debated — some are now pushed back to Mokaya culture vessels from ~1900 BCE in Soconusco, which would pre-date the Olmec.

## Chapter 7 — Words and Numbers

1. `[CHECK]` **"On one flat surface, someone had carved 62 distinct symbols arranged in apparent rows and sequences"** — The Cascajal Block details (62 symbols, ~28 sign types) are from the 2006 Science publication by Rodriguez Martinez et al. 2. `[CHECK]` **"the Babylonians (Mesopotamia, roughly 300 BCE, though their zero was a placeholder, not a number in its own right)"** — The date "roughly 300 BCE" for the Babylonian zero placeholder is late. Babylonian mathematical texts with a zero placeholder (a space or later a specific symbol) appear from the Old Babylonian period (~1800 BCE) onward, though the consistent symbol usage is usually dated to the Seleucid period (~300 BCE). 5. `[CHECK]` **"The Tuxtla Statuette...bears a date corresponding to 162 BCE"** — The 162 BCE date (actually often cited as 162 CE, not BCE) needs verification. Some sources give the Tuxtla Statuette date as 162 CE (8.6.2.4.17 in Long Count). If it's CE rather than BCE, that's a significant error.

2. `[CHECK]` **"Kent Flannery and Joyce Marcus"** — Both are real and prominent Mesoamerican archaeologists at the University of Michigan. Their sister culture position is well-documented. No issue with the attribution.

3. `[CHECK]` **"a cache of 782 jade objects in a single offering deposit"** — The 782-piece jade cache at Cerro de las Mesas is a famous find (excavated by Stirling in 1941). The exact count is sometimes given as slightly different numbers. Worth checking against the original excavation report.

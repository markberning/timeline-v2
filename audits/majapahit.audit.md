# Majapahit — Story Editor Audit (Persona D)

## Chapter 1 — Java Before Majapahit: the Indianized Kingdoms, Singhasari, and the Srivijaya Inheritance

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Java's volcanic soil and its long apprenticeship in two imported ideas — Indian kingship and the sea — set up the kingdom that would finally fuse rice power and trade power into one.

**What works:**
- The opening — "Start with the soil, because the soil is the whole argument" — is a confident, thesis-forward hook that immediately tells the reader why geography is destiny here. The Srivijaya-vs-Java contrast ("a trade empire with thin soil. Java was a rice empire with a coastline") lands the stakes of the whole book in two sentences.
- The Borobudur/Prambanan passage earns its space with the "you can stand inside the result" turn, then converts the monuments into a load-bearing idea (king as cosmic-mountain pivot) that the chapter explicitly threads forward 500 years. That is structural payoff, not tourism.
- The ending pivot is excellent: Kertanagara's "sound strategy aimed at the wrong threat's neighbor … and in 1289 it sent him a letter" is a genuine cliffhanger that hands the reader straight into Chapter 2.

**What doesn't work:**
- The central-to-east-Java move (para starting "Then, around 929") briefly slackens — it is the one passage that reads as a list of caveats ("the surviving evidence is thinner … the honest answer is that we do not know") rather than narrative. It recovers fast with the Brantas-as-spine image, but the energy dips here for a paragraph before Kediri revives it.

**Momentum map:** Strong from the soil hook, peaks at Borobudur, brief dip at the 929 relocation, surges back through Kediri/Ken Arok to a sharp cliffhanger.

## Chapter 2 — The Mongol Invasion of Java

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A landless fugitive turns the world's most feared army into the instrument of his own coronation by letting it kill his enemy and then destroying it.

**What works:**
- The thesis is stated in the first sentence and the chapter never loses it: "an empire that begins because a man borrowed an enemy's army, used it to destroy his other enemy, and then destroyed the army." Everything that follows is structured as a payoff to that promise.
- The Japan parallel is deployed with real craft — "in Japan the weapon was a typhoon, and in Java the weapon was a man" — and it pays off again at the end with the three-failed-invasions tricolon (Japan/storm, Java/nerve, Đại Việt/stakes). The chapter uses comparison as architecture.
- The "Now the maneuver" section break is a pro move: short, declarative paragraphs that accelerate exactly when the double-cross executes. Pacing mirrors content.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the long parenthetical on the Yuanshi numbers ("These numbers come from the *Yuanshi*, the official History of the Yuan compiled under the next dynasty; campaign figures in such records are conventionally inflated and should be read as 'a very large fleet,' not a census") is a 30+ word interruption planted mid-muster, right where momentum is building. The point is worth making but could be a trailing sentence rather than a parenthesis that stalls the assembly of the fleet.

**Momentum map:** High and rising throughout; peaks at "Now the maneuver" and holds through the ambush to a triumphant close.

## Chapter 3 — Raden Wijaya and the Founding

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Winning a kingdom and keeping one are different jobs — the founder spends his short reign putting down the very men who made him, while quietly building legitimacy by blood and marriage.

**What works:**
- The opening conceit — the empire named after a sour fruit a hungry work crew tasted — is a genuinely charming, memorable entry, and the doubled name (Majapahit/Wilwatikta) as "Indianization in a single word" is a sharp, compressed idea.
- The "comrades who feel they made the king" pattern is a real, universalizing throughline and the chapter knows it ("the first generation's war heroes are the second generation's rebels").

**What doesn't work:**
- The momentum dies in the second paragraph. The multiple-name digression ("Here the multiple-name problem becomes a practical reading challenge, so it is worth stopping on once") is a craft/reader-management aside parked immediately after the delightful fruit opening — it stops the story cold right when it should be accelerating into Wijaya's reign. Structural fix: move this material to a footnote or fold a one-clause version into the consecration sentence; it is reference matter, not narrative.
- The chapter ends on a source-problem meditation ("Almost nothing in this chapter can be told without the phrase 'according to the *Pararaton*'") rather than a pull forward. It is well-written but it closes inward; there is no hook into Chapter 4's heir problem. Fix: end on Gayatri the surviving matriarch as the live wire into the next two reigns — the material is already there in the prior sentence, it just needs to be the last beat.

**Momentum map:** Bright opening, flatlines through the names digression, recovers modestly through the rebellions, ends in reflective neutral rather than forward gear.

## Chapter 4 — Consolidation: Jayanegara, Tribhuwana, and the Rebellions

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The unfinished business of the founding generation comes due on a weak heir's watch — and the crisis accidentally produces the most consequential commoner in Javanese history.

**What works:**
- The framing sentence is a model of efficiency: "the heir's problem produced, almost incidentally, the most famous Indonesian who ever lived." It gives the chapter a spine and a promise in one line.
- The Gajah Mada introduction is handled with real restraint and suspense — "Hold his name. He is the reason this chapter exists and the subject of the next." This is exactly how to plant a protagonist.
- The two-women-at-the-center passage (Gayatri and Tribhuwana) is given real weight without overclaiming, and the chapter earns its closing line: "The bodyguard who had carried a king out of a burning capital on his back now ran the government." Strong pull forward.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the Jayanegara-scandal handling ("a charge that is exactly the kind of lurid detail a later hostile chronicle invents or amplifies and should be flagged as such rather than repeated as fact") momentarily steps out of the story to do source-hygiene in the editor's voice. It is brief and defensible given the book's project, but it slightly cools a paragraph that wants to move.

**Momentum map:** Steady acceleration; the Kuti revolt and Gajah Mada's rescue are the energy core, and the chapter ends pointed straight at Chapter 5.

## Chapter 5 — Gajah Mada and the Palapa Oath

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The most consequential figure in the book is the one we can least verify — and the gap between the man and the legend is exactly what later became a nation.

**What works:**
- The opening states the chapter's entire tension as a paradox and commits to it: "the most consequential figure in this book and the one we can be least sure about, and that tension is the chapter." The whole chapter then honors that promise.
- The Palapa Oath section turns a potential debunking into drama: "the most famous sentence in Indonesian history is one whose key word scholars cannot fully translate, recorded in a source written a century and a half after the man supposedly spoke it." The uncertainty is made thrilling rather than deflating.
- The Bubat ending is the strongest single closing in the book: the same man as "a unifier or a butcher" depending on which island's chronicle you read, capped by "The man left the stage. The argument over him never has." Emotional engagement and thesis converge.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the chapter twice defers its biggest question to Chapter 7 ("we will take up properly in Chapter 7" / "the central question of the whole subject and deserves its own room"). One forward-reference is good housekeeping; two in quick succession in the Bali paragraph slightly drains that paragraph of its own stakes. Fix: let the Bali paragraph land its own point (Bali still Hindu today) harder and defer only once.

**Momentum map:** High throughout; dips microscopically in the deferral, then climbs to a peak finish at Bubat.

## Chapter 6 — Hayam Wuruk, the Nagarakretagama, and the Golden Age

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** For one reign the legend and the evidence align — but only because a single, gorgeous, deeply biased praise-poem happens to shine its light here and nowhere else.

**What works:**
- The opening reframes "golden age" honestly and compellingly: the alignment of legend and evidence is itself "the most important fact in the chapter," because it is an artifact of one surviving source. That is a sophisticated, earned hook.
- The Bhinneka Tunggal Ika passage is the emotional and thematic high point of the chapter — a fourteenth-century Buddhist's line to reconcile two gods becoming a modern nation's motto — and it is correctly seeded here for Chapter 10 payoff.
- The Trowulan archaeology paragraph does real work distinguishing what the ground confirms from what the poem flatters, which keeps the golden age from reading as credulous.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter front-loads two heavy source-methodology paragraphs (what the Nagarakretagama is, the Lombok manuscript survival) before the reader ever sees the court itself. The 1894 burning-library story is fantastic material but it arrives as throat-clearing before the scene rather than woven into it. Structural fix: open with the court the poem shows us (Trowulan, the royal progress) and let the "how we even have this" reveal land mid-chapter or as the kicker — the survival story is more powerful as a twist than as a preface.
- Mild redundancy: the "single copy carried out of the burning Lombok palace in 1894" is told here and again in Chapter 10. It is load-bearing in 10 (the rediscovery/nationalism arc); here it could be compressed to a clause so the full set-piece pays off once, in 10.

**Momentum map:** Slow, methodological opening; energy arrives with the Trowulan court and peaks at Bhinneka Tunggal Ika; the close ("hand the future a number") is a clean pull into Chapter 7.

## Chapter 7 — The Thalassocracy: Trade, Tribute, and How Big Was Majapahit Really?

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Majapahit was both genuinely rich and far-reaching AND not the centralized empire the word implies — and a nation was later built precisely in the gap between those two truths.

**What works:**
- The chapter is built as a clean three-act argument — the maximal claim, the deflation, the both-and — and announces it as "the chapter the whole book has been pointing at." It delivers on that billing.
- The stakes are escalated brilliantly past antiquarianism: "is the founding map of the world's fourth-most-populous nation historically real." That single sentence makes a scholarly debate feel urgent.
- The economic-engine paragraph is concrete and vivid (Chinese kepeng as "the medieval equivalent of a foreign reserve currency"; cloves "found nowhere else on Earth at the time"), grounding the abstraction in trade goods and cash.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: the mandala-deflation paragraph runs long and dense, with three nested "beyond that" rings delivered in successive sentences without a paragraph break or a concrete image to anchor them. A reader can lose the spatial picture. Fix: a single physical example (one named tributary that sent one embassy and was logged as "subject") would make the abstract concentric model land harder than the third restatement of it does.

**Momentum map:** Strong and argument-driven throughout; the only soft spot is the mid-chapter mandala theory; recovers fully with the economic engine and a deliberately quotable both-and close.

## Chapter 8 — The Paregreg War and the Long Decline

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A mandala state doesn't fall by conquest — it falls when a center fights itself, the fleet stops coming, and the spell of compulsion simply breaks.

**What works:**
- The opening is a thesis with attitude: "Empires rarely fall the way stories want them to, with a final battle and a fixed date." It sets reader expectations and then deliberately subverts the satisfying-collapse template.
- The mechanism of mandala collapse is the cleverest analytical passage in the book: "The Paregreg War did not lose Majapahit a province. It lost Majapahit the *illusion* on which the *Nagarakretagama*'s map depended." This converts a murky civil war into a precise, memorable idea.
- The chapter is honest about the king-list murk without letting it become tedious — the "Brawijaya I through V … a clean fiction laid over a messy reality" line turns a record gap into a thematic point, and the close ("a Chinese fleet … and a new religion") is a strong double hook.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor: this is the shortest chapter and the fifteenth-century-kings paragraph compresses a lot of disputed names (Kertawijaya, Rajasawardhana, Girishawardhana, Singhawikramawardhana) into a single breath. That is the right editorial call given the sources, but the rapid name-spray is the one place the prose briefly outruns the reader. The framing sentences around it carry it, so this is a blemish, not a wound.

**Momentum map:** Tight and propulsive; the mandala-collapse mechanism is the energy peak; ends with strong forward pull into Chapter 9.

## Chapter 9 — Zheng He, the Coming of Islam, and the Rise of Demak

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Java became the world's most populous Muslim country not by conquest but by the same slow, trade-borne, coast-inward soak that had once made it Indian.

**What works:**
- The opening promise is sharp and counterintuitive: "Neither was a conquest. That is the surprising part, and it is the part that matters." It tells the reader what to be surprised by and why.
- The Zheng He indemnity incident is "a perfect miniature of where power actually sat by 1406" — a well-chosen set-piece that does analytical work, not just color.
- The Indianization rhyme is the chapter's best structural stroke: "It became Muslim the way it had become Indianized a thousand years earlier." This connects Chapter 1's mechanism to Chapter 9's and makes the whole book feel composed rather than sequential.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter carries two arrivals (Zheng He; Islamization) plus Demak, and the Zheng He thread is essentially abandoned after the indemnity paragraph — it functions as a vivid prologue to the real subject (Islamization) rather than a co-equal strand the title promises. The structure is slightly top-heavy: the most cinematic material (Zheng He) is the least load-bearing. Fix: either tighten the Zheng He section to explicitly frame it as the lens through which we *see* the Muslim merchant communities (Ma Huan's testimony is the bridge and it is underexploited), or accept it as set dressing and let the chapter title foreground the conversion.
- Mild momentum dip in the Wali Songo paragraph: it slides into the now-familiar "tradition encodes something true under the legend … the same kind of tidy number as the five 'Brawijaya' kings" move. The analytical maneuver (legend-as-encoded-truth) has now been performed for the Palapa Oath, the Brawijaya kings, and here — by the third instance it reads as a template the reader can predict. The point is valid but the surprise is gone; this is the strongest case of cross-chapter redundancy in the book.

**Momentum map:** Strong cinematic opening (Zheng He), peaks at the Indianization rhyme, dips at the now-predictable Wali Songo debunk, recovers with Demak and a strong convergent close.

## Chapter 10 — The Fall and the Afterlife: Demak, Bali, and the Indonesian Foundation Myth

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Every fact about Majapahit is a source making a case — including the date of its death — and the kingdom's afterlife, conscripted by a nation that didn't exist when it died, became larger than its life.

**What works:**
- The chapter delivers the book's entire thesis as a closed loop and earns it: the contested-date paragraph ends "This is the whole book in one paragraph," and it genuinely is — the argument that "the kingdom whose size was a source's argument also had a death that is a source's argument" is the cleanest statement of the project's premise.
- The Bali passage is the emotional core of the ending: "it is the place the bitter-maja kingdom went when it lost Java … Majapahit did not so much end as relocate." The callback to the Chapter 3 fruit name closes a 170-line arc.
- The closing movement — satellite named Palapa, motto on the coat of arms, university named for Gajah Mada — is a devastatingly effective accumulation, and the final line ("The second invention is the one most of the world still lives inside") is the best last sentence in the book. The "invented twice" title pays off completely.

**What doesn't work:**
- Minor / structural: the chapter has to re-tell three things the reader has already met (the 1894 Lombok manuscript, the Palapa Oath, Bhinneka Tunggal Ika). In Chapter 10 this is mostly justified — it is the payoff site where they are recombined into the nationalism argument — but the Lombok-manuscript retelling in particular is nearly as long here as in Chapter 6. Fix: thin the Chapter 6 version to a clause (see Ch 6 note) so the full set-piece detonates only here, where it carries the most weight.

**Momentum map:** Builds steadily; the date paragraph is the analytical peak, the Bali passage the emotional peak, and the satellite/motto/university accumulation drives an unbroken ascent to the strongest finish in the book.

---

## Overall assessment

This works as a whole, and unusually well — it is one of the most architecturally deliberate narratives in the project. It is built around a single argument (every source is a case, and the afterlife outgrew the life) that is planted in the front matter, restated and complicated in every chapter, and detonated in Chapter 10, so the chapters genuinely build rather than merely sequence; the Chapter 3 sour-fruit name paying off in the Chapter 10 Bali relocation is the kind of long-arc payoff most of these narratives don't attempt. The voice is consistent, confident, and thesis-forward — almost every chapter opens with a stated argument and closes with a hook, which is exactly the discipline this format needs. The biggest structural weakness is repetition of one analytical move: "the legend encodes a true thing under it" is performed for the Palapa Oath, the Brawijaya kings, and the Wali Songo, and by the third use (Chapter 9) it has become a template the reader anticipates rather than a fresh insight. The single highest-value improvement: vary or compress that legend-decoding maneuver after its second use, and relocate the full Lombok-manuscript set-piece from Chapter 6 (where it front-loads methodology before the scene) to Chapter 10 (where it is the payoff), thinning it to a clause in 6 — both fixes sharpen surprise without touching the strong spine.

## Part 1: Forward Findings (new TL gaps)

### Chapter 1 — Java Before Majapahit: the Indianized Kingdoms, Singhasari, and the Srivijaya Inheritance

1. **"the empire of **Srivijaya** (≈ "sree-vih-JAH-yah") had spent six centuries getting rich a different way"** — The chapter parenthetically defers to "Srivijaya has its own narrative" but does not anchor a date or characterize what Srivijaya was in cross-civ terms. srivijaya.md Ch 4 builds the entire "thalassocracy vs. territorial empire" frame (founded ~650/680s, sacked 1025 by the Cholas, finally extinguished by Majapahit in 1377). Add an inline note that Srivijaya was the predecessor sea-empire (c. 650–1377) whose strait-control model Majapahit's Chapter 7 explicitly re-runs, and that Majapahit itself ends the Srivijaya story (Srivijaya Ch 8). — Srivijaya, c. 650–1377.

2. **"the courts of island and mainland Southeast Asia adopted, piece by piece, a package of culture from India"** — The Indianization passage names the mechanism but not its source civilization or date. gupta-empire.md Ch 8 makes Indianization a flagship legacy ("the Gupta cultural template, exported in the 4th–7th centuries, is still observably present in 21st-century Southeast Asia") and medieval-india.md ties the Sanskrit cosmopolis "from Afghanistan to Java." Add a cross-ref that the temple-and-Sanskrit package radiated from Gupta-era India (4th–7th c. CE) — the same template that produced Angkor and Prambanan (Gupta Empire Ch 8). — Gupta India, 4th–7th c. CE.

3. **"the same way the Khmer on the mainland were doing it (Khmer Empire Chapter 1)"** — Good existing cross-ref, but it understates a sharper parallel that khmer-empire.md Ch 1 itself draws explicitly: the Funan founding legend (Kaundinya the Brahmin weds the naga princess Soma) as "Indianization as the Khmer themselves told it." Majapahit's own doubled-name device (Wilwatikta/Majapahit, Ch 3) is the same "foreign prestige form over local reality" point. Tighten the Khmer cross-ref to note both civilizations encode Indianization in a marriage/naming myth. — Khmer/Funan, early centuries CE.

4. **"two of the largest religious monuments on Earth ... Borobudur ... finished around 825 ... Prambanan ... finished around 850"** — "largest religious monuments on Earth" is a superlative with no cross-civ anchor at this point. The chapter does compare Borobudur to Charlemagne and Angkor Wat, but khmer-empire.md Ch 5 makes the explicit, scrutinized claim that **Angkor Wat is "the largest religious monument ever built"** by floor area. Borobudur is the largest *Buddhist* monument (srivijaya.md Ch 3 states this precisely). Reconcile the wording so Borobudur is "largest Buddhist monument" against Angkor Wat as "largest religious monument" (Khmer Empire Ch 5), avoiding a contradiction across the chain. — Khmer Angkor Wat, c. 1150.

5. **"the Buddhist **Sailendra** (≈ "sye-LEN-drah") ... built ... Borobudur"** — srivijaya.md Ch 3 spends pages on the unresolved Sailendra–Srivijaya relationship and stresses that Borobudur is "a Sailendra monument that is in many ways the most visible surviving expression of Srivijayan-era Buddhist culture, even though Srivijaya itself did not build it." Majapahit Ch 1 treats the Sailendra purely as a Javanese dynasty with no nod to the Sumatran/Srivijayan connection. Add a one-line cross-ref to Srivijaya Ch 3's Sailendra-Srivijaya alliance, since Ch 1's whole thesis is the "Srivijaya Inheritance." — Srivijaya/Sailendra, 8th–9th c.

6. **"the **Pamalayu** (≈ "pah-mah-LAH-yoo") expedition ... pulling the wreckage of declining Srivijaya into Singhasari's orbit (this is the same expedition that, seen from Sumatra, marks Java's takeover of the strait — Srivijaya Chapter 7)"** — The cross-ref is correct and well-placed, but srivijaya.md Ch 7 dates the Pamalayu Expedition to **1275** (occupying Melayu until 1292) and frames it as "the Javanese conquest of what remained of the Srivijayan core ... the political flow of maritime Southeast Asia had reversed." Majapahit Ch 1 gives "around 1275 and again in 1286." The 1286 date is from a separate Padang Roco / Amoghapasa episode; flag that the reference TL dates the occupation 1275–1292 and consider aligning, or note the second-date discrepancy. — Srivijaya Ch 7, 1275–1292.

### Chapter 2 — The Mongol Invasion of Java

7. **"Twice, in 1274 and 1281, he had sent invasion fleets against Japan, and twice storms the Japanese would later call *kamikaze* ... (Yuan Dynasty Chapter 3)"** — Strong, accurate cross-ref. One enrichment: yuan-dynasty.md Ch 3 itself groups Java with Japan, Vietnam, and Burma as the cluster of failed late-Yuan overseas projects ("A 1293 expedition against the Javanese kingdom of Singhasari was one of the most embarrassing in the entire Mongol record"). The Majapahit chapter could note that the Yuan narrative itself rates the Java failure the *most* embarrassing of the four — a mutual-reinforcement opportunity. — Yuan Dynasty Ch 3, 1293.

8. **"Java had done to a Mongol army with a double-cross what Japan had done to one with a storm and what Đại Việt would do to a third with stakes in a tidal river (Đại Việt Chapter 5)"** — The Đại Việt cross-ref is mis-pointed. dai-viet.md Ch 5 covers the 1258 and 1285 invasions (evacuation/scorched-earth); the **Bạch Đằng tidal-stakes river trap against the Mongol fleet is 1288, and that is dai-viet.md Chapter 6** ("Trần Hưng Đạo, the Bạch Đằng Stakes, and the High Trần"). The "stakes in a tidal river" detail specifically belongs to Đại Việt Ch 6, not Ch 5. Fix the chapter pointer. — Đại Việt Ch 6, 1288.

9. **"roughly twenty to thirty thousand troops and a thousand ships"** — yuan-dynasty.md (Srivijaya Ch 7 echo: "perhaps 1,000 ships and 20,000 troops") and the Yuan TL both give matching figures, and yuan-dynasty.md Ch 3 frames the Yuan-Goryeo shipbuilding strain and the substandard Korean-built hulls that doomed the Japan fleets. Worth a one-clause "meanwhile" that the same overstretched Yuan shipbuilding apparatus that produced the doomed Japan fleets built this one — the parallel is structural, not just rhetorical. — Yuan Dynasty Ch 3, 1274/1281.

### Chapter 5 — Gajah Mada and the Palapa Oath

10. **"the conquest of **Bali**: around 1343 Majapahit forces overthrew the indigenous Balinese dynasty"** — The Bali conquest is presented without any cross-civ frame for what "conquest" meant for a thalassocratic/mandala power. This is the same conquest-vs-tribute ambiguity srivijaya.md Ch 4 develops at length and that Majapahit Ch 7 will itself address. A forward pointer to Ch 7 (and the Srivijaya mandala parallel) at first mention of "conquest" would prevent the reader taking it literally. — internal + Srivijaya Ch 4.

11. **"the catastrophe at Bubat ... the Sundanese king and his entire party were massacred"** — The Bubat massacre (c. 1357) has no cross-civ companion, yet dai-viet.md Ch 8 supplies a tight one: Lê Thánh Tông's 1471 obliteration of the Cham capital Vijaya, told with the same "honest history names it as conquest, not the tidy unification of nationalist retelling" framing. Both are atrocities a national hero (Gajah Mada / Lê Thánh Tông) committed against a culturally distinct neighbor (Sunda / Champa), remembered as glory on one side and trauma on the other. A "the same name landing as glory in one place and atrocity in the next" comparison to the Cham case would be the single most illuminating addition in the chapter. — Đại Việt Ch 8 / Ch 11, Champa 1471.

### Chapter 6 — Hayam Wuruk, the Nagarakretagama, and the Golden Age

12. **"Vietnam ran Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism as parallel "three teachings" (Đại Việt Chapter 8)"** — The Đại Việt cross-ref for the "three teachings" is mis-pointed. dai-viet.md Ch 8 ("Lê Thánh Tông and the Confucian Golden Age") is about the Confucian bureaucracy *displacing* Buddhism, not running the three teachings in parallel. The syncretic Buddhist/Trúc Lâm material and the long Buddhism-vs-Confucianism arc sit across dai-viet.md Ch 3 and Ch 6; the "three teachings" coexistence is not a single-chapter anchor in the reference. Re-verify the chapter pointer or generalize to "Đại Việt" without the chapter number. — Đại Việt, verify chapter.

13. **"the Mughals would later attempt a synthetic court religion (Mughal Empire Chapter 4)"** — Cross-ref is to mughal-empire.md, which is **not** among the reference TLs for this audit, so its accuracy cannot be confirmed here; flag for the chain-consistency pass. The in-chain comparison that *can* be supported is Đại Việt's Shiva-Buddha-adjacent doctrinal blending vs. Majapahit's, and the Khmer Harihara (Shiva+Vishnu fused "in one statue," khmer-empire.md Ch 2) — a closer and verifiable parallel for "deliberate doctrinal fusion." Consider adding the Khmer Harihara as a cross-chain example alongside the Mughal one. — Khmer Empire Ch 2.

14. **"a planned hydraulic city of a kind the region had not seen, the rice-and-water engineering of East Java turned into a capital"** — "of a kind the region had not seen" is a superlative that the Khmer narrative directly contradicts: khmer-empire.md Ch 3 and Ch 10 describe Angkor as a "hydraulic city" of barays and canals (Groslier's *cité hydraulique*) covering ~1,000 km², "one of the largest pre-industrial settlement complexes ever identified." Trowulan was real and planned, but mainland Southeast Asia had seen far larger hydraulic urbanism three centuries earlier. Soften "of a kind the region had not seen" and add a Khmer/Angkor comparison. — Khmer Empire Ch 3 & Ch 10, 9th–13th c.

### Chapter 7 — The Thalassocracy: Trade, Tribute, and How Big Was Majapahit Really?

15. **"it is the same problem this project flagged for Srivijaya ... so the two chapters are meant to be read against each other (Srivijaya Chapter 4)"** — Excellent, correctly pointed cross-ref (srivijaya.md Ch 4 is exactly "The Strait Empire" / thalassocracy). One strengthening: srivijaya.md Ch 4 supplies the concrete vocabulary (thalassocracy from *thalassa*+*kratos*; Venice and Phoenicia as the canonical parallels). Majapahit Ch 7 uses "thalassocracy" in its title but never defines it inline or invokes the Venice/Phoenicia parallels the sibling chapter leans on. Borrow one sentence of that frame for reader continuity. — Srivijaya Ch 4.

16. **"the mandala (≈ "MAHN-dah-lah" ...) ... concentric fields of influence"** — The mandala/Wolters model is introduced as if fresh, but khmer-empire.md repeatedly invokes the same "circle of influence vs. administered territory" logic for Angkor, and srivijaya.md Ch 4 builds the "vassal port-states bound by oath, loyalty lasting as long as the fleet could reach them" version.

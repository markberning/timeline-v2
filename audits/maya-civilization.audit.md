## Story Editor evaluation (Persona D)

## Chapter 1 — Before the Pyramids

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Maya chose one of the worst possible environments, turned it into a civilization by solving the water problem, and built a Preclassic city (El Mirador) rivaling anything in the ancient world — then it collapsed, setting the stage for everything that follows.

**What works:**
- The opening paragraph is outstanding — the environment comes alive ("hot, wet, and still," "several hundred species of things that bite, sting, or poison"), and the immediate payoff ("The choice looks insane until you understand the geology") hooks the reader with a promise of explanation.
- The El Mirador section delivers a genuine jaw-drop: the La Danta volume comparison to the Great Pyramid of Giza is exactly the kind of stat that makes readers stop and re-read. The caveat about the natural hill underneath is honest without deflating the achievement.
- The "first" claims caveat paragraph (LiDAR, jungle coverage, archaeological luck) is exactly what WRITING-RULES.md calls for and it's placed perfectly — early enough to inoculate the whole narrative.
- The San Bartolo murals section ("not primitive early attempts at art") delivers a satisfying punch.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter is long. The environment section (paras 1-3), the geographic zones paragraph, and the "Maya were not an empire" setup are all necessary, but the chapter front-loads a lot of setting before the first narrative event (earliest settlements, ~1800 BCE). A reader on a phone might scroll four or five screens before anything happens. The payoff is worth it, but the patience required is real.
- The Hero Twins mythology gets introduced twice — once in the El Mirador art section and once in its own paragraph near the end. The second introduction is fuller, but the reader has already seen the names. The repetition is minor but noticeable.

**Momentum map:** Excellent hook → solid geographic setup (slightly long) → good Nakbe/Kaminaljuyu middle → outstanding El Mirador peak → satisfying San Bartolo/Hero Twins/agriculture tail → effective El Mirador collapse closer that launches Chapter 2.

## Chapter 2 — The Classic Dawn

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Classic period is when the Maya started writing things down, and with writing came named kings, political networks, and a fateful entanglement with Teotihuacan that reshaped Tikal and the entire lowland political map.

**What works:**
- The opening paragraph ("this is when the Maya started writing things down") is clean and direct — it defines the Classic period in one sentence, then unpacks it.
- The Tikal description is vivid and grounded: bajos, trade routes, Temple IV at 212 feet. The engineering paragraph (no metal tools, no wheels, no pulleys, corbel arch) earns its technical detail because it's anchored in what the reader can visualize.
- The cacao-as-currency section is the chapter's standout passage. "A rabbit might cost ten cacao beans. A slave might cost a hundred." is perfectly calibrated popular history — specific, surprising, and it solves a real question the reader might have.
- The Siyaj K'ahk' / Teotihuacan arrival section handles scholarly uncertainty masterfully. Three competing interpretations are laid out without the text favoring any one, and the ambiguity itself is interesting.

**What doesn't work:**
- The transition from the zero/Venus section to the jade section to the Teotihuacan intervention feels like three topics stitched together without connective tissue. Each is strong on its own, but the reader jumps from math → trade → political upheaval without much signaling.
- The chapter ends with a nice catalogue paragraph ("k'uhul ajaw... competing for prestige, territory, and trade"), but it's more summary than cliffhanger. Compared to Chapter 1's "the most important of them was called Tikal" ending, this one is flatter.

**Momentum map:** Clean open → good Tikal setup → excellent agriculture/cacao middle → strong zero/Venus → good jade → outstanding Teotihuacan intervention peak → adequate but slightly flat close.

## Chapter 3 — The Great Rivalry

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Tikal-Calakmul rivalry was the Cold War of the Maya world — fought through proxies, alliances, and betrayals for 150 years — and its resolution proved that the Maya political system was structurally incapable of producing an empire.

**What works:**
- The Dos Pilas proxy war section is the chapter's narrative peak: "A member of Tikal's royal family was now claiming to be Tikal's true king while fighting for Tikal's mortal enemy." The setup is genuinely dramatic and the Cold War analogy is earned, not decorative.
- The "why no empire?" analysis is excellent. Geography, technology, and ideology are each given concrete detail (no draft animals, limited campaign range, each city needing its own k'uhul ajaw). The comparison to Greek city-states is strong and earns its place.
- Jasaw Chan K'awiil I's revenge arc is satisfying: the 13-year rebuild, the decisive battle, the funerary monument as victory lap.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter's structure is clean (rise of Calakmul → star war → hiatus → Dos Pilas proxy → Tikal revenge → why no empire? analysis), but the "why no empire?" section at the end is substantially longer than the narrative sections. The analysis-to-story ratio tilts toward the analytical side. The chapter reads more like an essay with examples than a story with analysis. This is a structural choice, not a flaw — but a reader hungry for story might find the back half slower.
- The final paragraph's Greek city-state comparison, while strong, arrives after the "multipolar system" point has already been made. The comparison extends rather than adds, making the chapter feel like it has two endings.

**Momentum map:** Strong open → good Calakmul setup → excellent star war of 562 → outstanding Dos Pilas proxy peak → satisfying Tikal revenge → strong but slightly overlong analytical close.

## Chapter 4 — The Golden Age

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The 7th-8th centuries were the simultaneous peak of every major Maya city, and the chapter profiles four of them (Palenque, Yaxchilan, Copan, plus cultural achievements) to show that the Golden Age was a collective output, not a single city's story.

**What works:**
- Pakal the Great's section is the chapter's centerpiece and it delivers. The reign-length comparison to Louis XIV and Victoria is good. The Ruz Lhuillier discovery of the tomb is a narrative peak. The sarcophagus lid description is vivid and the von Daniken dismissal is pitch-perfect: "It does not. It shows a Maya king entering the underworld."
- The 18 Rabbit / Quirigua reversal is the chapter's dramatic highpoint: "It would be as if Luxembourg invaded France and executed the French president." The analogy is memorable and proportionate.
- Lady Six Sky's inclusion as a "powerful Maya woman" breaks the king-only pattern and is welcome.
- Maya Blue is a terrific detail — a synthetic pigment that resists chemical attack after 1,000 years. The comparison to Egyptian Blue is a good cross-civ link.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter covers a lot of ground: Palenque (Pakal + Kan Bahlam II), Yaxchilan (Shield Jaguar II + Bird Jaguar IV), Copan (18 Rabbit + Quirigua + Altar Q), ballgame, sacbe roads, Maya Blue, Bonampak, Lady Six Sky. The individual sections are all strong, but the cumulative effect is a tour of highlights rather than a story. There's no single throughline that pulls the reader from beginning to end — it's "here's another amazing thing, and another, and another."
- The Bonampak murals section, while good, arrives after what feels like a natural climax (the 18 Rabbit reversal / Altar Q epitaph). The reader's emotional arc peaks at Copan and everything after is addendum.
- The final paragraph ("Within fifty years, most of it would be abandoned") is an excellent cliffhanger, but it comes after a catalogue of cultural achievements that somewhat dilute the dramatic setup.

**Momentum map:** Good population-scale opener → outstanding Pakal peak → good Yaxchilan → outstanding Copan/18 Rabbit dramatic peak → slightly diffuse cultural-achievements tail (ballgame, sacbe, Maya Blue, Bonampak, Lady Six Sky) → excellent cliffhanger close.

## Chapter 5 — The Collapse

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Classic Maya Collapse was not a vanishing act but a cascade of environmental, political, economic, and ideological failures hitting a system with zero margin for error, and the survivors reinvented themselves in the northern Yucatan.

**What works:**
- The opening is excellent: "stopped. Stopped building. Stopped carving. Stopped writing." The repetition is deliberate and effective.
- The "They didn't vanish" correction is placed early and stated firmly: "Calling it a 'disappearance' erases the millions of living Maya descendants." This is the right tone.
- The environmental section is the chapter's intellectual peak: the feedback loop (more people → more farmland → less forest → less rain → less food) is laid out with devastating clarity. The reservoir capacity detail (18-month dry period) makes the vulnerability concrete.
- The ideological breakdown section ("Why support a king whose prayers don't work?") is the most emotionally resonant passage.
- The Puuc cities section (Uxmal's Governor's Palace, "the single finest piece of architecture in all of pre-Columbian America") provides a counterpoint — the world didn't just end, it shifted.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Rome comparison at the end, while competent, feels like an obligation rather than a revelation. The reader has already understood the mechanics of the collapse; the Rome comparison adds completeness but not insight. It could be trimmed to a single paragraph without losing anything.
- The final poetic paragraph ("Tikal's pyramids became tree-covered hills. Palenque's palaces became nests for howler monkeys.") is beautiful, but it comes after the Rome comparison has already provided an intellectual close. The chapter has two endings.

**Momentum map:** Outstanding open → strong "they didn't vanish" → excellent environmental cascade → good political/economic section → powerful ideological breakdown → good Puuc cities/Uxmal → slightly overextended Rome comparison → beautiful but slightly anticlimactic poetic close.

## Chapter 6 — The Postclassic World

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Postclassic Maya world was more commercial and cosmopolitan than the Classic, and its three major chapters — Chichen Itza, Mayapan, and the maritime trade network — show a civilization adapting, not declining.

**What works:**
- The Chichen Itza section handles the "split personality" architecture well. The "Old" vs. "New" Chichen framing is clear and the equinox shadow effect is described precisely without overselling.
- The Sacred Cenote section is outstanding — the inventory of offerings becomes a trade-route map, which is a clever structural move: "every offering tracing a line on the map from its point of origin to this pool of dark water in the jungle."
- The Mayapan section's insight about the wall ("Classic Maya cities were not walled... The fact that Mayapan needed a wall tells us something") is exactly the right kind of inference for popular history.
- The Columbus encounter paragraph is a terrific close to the maritime trade section: "Columbus had no idea what he was looking at. He sailed on."
- The Dresden Codex placement here, as a Postclassic document, is structurally smart — it bridges to Chapter 7's intellectual-achievement focus.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Toltec-invasion debate section, while thorough and honest, reads like a historiography seminar. The reader needs to know the debate exists, but the three-paragraph treatment (traditional view → modern revision → what's clear) is more than needed. One paragraph would suffice.
- The chapter tries to cover Chichen Itza, the Toltec question, Mayapan, the salt trade, maritime canoes, the Columbus encounter, Mayapan's fall, the fragmentation into kuchkabalob, and the Dresden Codex. Like Chapter 4, it's a tour of topics rather than a single story. The maritime trade section is the strongest candidate for a chapter throughline, but it arrives late.

**Momentum map:** Good "different not declined" opener → strong Chichen Itza → somewhat dry Toltec debate → good Sacred Cenote → adequate Mayapan → strong maritime trade/Columbus → good Mayapan fall → somewhat abrupt Dresden Codex close.

## Chapter 7 — Words, Stars, and Zero

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Maya writing, astronomy, and mathematics form a unified intellectual system, and the modern decipherment is itself one of the great detective stories in scholarship.

**What works:**
- The opening thesis ("the most sophisticated writing system, the most accurate astronomy, and the most advanced mathematics of any civilization in the pre-Columbian Americas") is earned across the chapter and not oversold.
- The decipherment narrative is the chapter's peak: Knorosov vs. Thompson, Cold War politics, David Stuart at age 18. The speed of decipherment ("In 1960, scholars could read almost nothing. By 1990, they could read most texts") is a genuine jaw-drop moment.
- The Venus tables accuracy ("583.92 days, which matches the modern value to an accuracy of a few minutes per cycle") and the connection to strategic warfare planning is excellent popular science writing.
- The Popol Vuh section is well-placed: the three failed creations (mud, wood, maize dough) are told with enough detail to be memorable.
- The Long Count explanation is the clearest I've read — the GPS coordinate analogy is perfect.

**What doesn't work:**
- This is a thematic chapter that steps outside chronological order, and it announces this upfront. The decision is correct (these achievements span the entire civilization), but the chapter's pacing suffers because it lacks a narrative arc. It's a series of excellent sections (writing, astronomy, math, calendar, Popol Vuh) that don't build toward a climax. Each section could stand alone.
- The calendar section (Tzolk'in, Haab, Calendar Round, Long Count) is dense and technical. It's necessary, but it's the chapter's slowest stretch. The reader is absorbing four interlocking systems in rapid succession. Some readers may glaze.
- The final paragraph ("The Maya intellectual system was a unified whole") is a good thesis restatement, but it states what the chapter has already demonstrated rather than adding a new dimension.

**Momentum map:** Strong thesis open → excellent writing/decipherment peak → good Venus/astronomy → adequate but dense calendar section → good Popol Vuh → restated-thesis close.

## Chapter 8 — Contact and the Last Kingdom

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Maya resisted European conquest for 175 years — longer than any other indigenous American civilization — because the same decentralization that prevented them from building an empire prevented the Spanish from conquering them.

**What works:**
- The Columbus opening is effective: "He had no idea that the people in the canoe belonged to a civilization that had been building cities, writing books, and predicting eclipses for two thousand years. He was looking for China."
- The structural insight linking decentralization to resistance is the chapter's central thesis and it's stated cleanly: "The Maya had no emperor. They had no capital."
- The Diego de Landa section is the emotional center: the irony (burned the books / preserved the key) is devastating and well-handled. "The man who burned the books also preserved the key that would unlock them."
- The Nojpetén siege is a satisfying climax: the galeota, the canoe charge, the cannon. The final paragraph's sweep ("2,700 years after the first Maya farming villages appeared in the Peten lowlands") is earned and lands.
- The closing three paragraphs are the strongest ending in the narrative: "They are still there now." The restraint is exactly right.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Montejo campaigns section (three failed campaigns over 20 years) is necessary but slightly mechanical. The three campaigns are listed in order with outcomes but without much narrative texture. The younger Montejo's exploitation of the Xiu-Cocom split is the most interesting detail, but it arrives quickly and moves on.
- The disease paragraph ("estimates suggest the Maya population dropped by 90 percent or more") is a single paragraph that handles an enormous topic. Given that disease did "more to destroy Maya civilization than the Spanish sword ever could," it deserves more than four sentences. The population collapse is stated but not felt.

**Momentum map:** Good Columbus open → adequate Cordoba/military resistance → slightly mechanical Montejo campaigns → outstanding Diego de Landa peak → good Popol Vuh/Chilam Balam → excellent Nojpeten climax → outstanding "still there now" close.

## Newcomer findings (Persona A)

### Chapter 1

1. **Empty claim / no concrete detail:** "shaped everything about their civilization, from their obsession with rain gods to the political fragility that brought them down" — the reader hasn't met any rain gods yet and "political fragility" is vague. The connection between water management and political fragility is asserted but not explained until Chapter 5. Acceptable as a forward tease, but borderline.

2. **Undefined proper noun:** "the Olmec were building their first great center at San Lorenzo (Olmec Ch 2)" — the Olmec are introduced only by name and a cross-reference. A reader who hasn't read the Olmec narrative gets no inline definition of who the Olmec were. The next paragraph helps ("The Maya inherited a cultural toolkit from the Olmec"), but the first mention is bare.

3. **Long parenthetical (16 words):** "at least thirty Maya languages survive today" — embedded in a sentence that's already complex. Borderline; the information is worth having but the sentence is heavy.

4. **Process described by result, not mechanism:** "the Maya adopted (or possibly co-developed) the Long Count calendar" — how does a civilization "adopt" a calendar? Did traders bring it? Did scribes learn it from visitors? The mechanism is missing. The text says it "almost certainly originated in the Olmec or Epi-Olmec world" but not how it traveled.

### Chapter 2

5. **Undefined term on first use:** "polychrome painted pottery" — "polychrome" is not defined. The reader might guess "many-colored" but shouldn't have to.

6. **Suspiciously clean transition:** "Around 350 CE, the Maya independently developed a mathematical concept" — the zero section appears with no connection to the Tikal narrative that precedes it. The reader jumps from city-building to mathematics with no bridge.

7. **Unclear pronoun antecedent:** "its position in a number determined the number's value (the way the zeros in 100 and 1,000 change the value of the 1)" — "its" refers to the zero/placeholder, but the sentence structure makes it ambiguous whether "its position" refers to the zero or to the number system.

8. **Process by result:** "the jade objects... circulated across the entire Maya world" — how? By trade? By gift exchange? By conquest and looting? The mechanism of jade circulation is never explained.

### Chapter 3

9. **Undefined term:** "emblem glyph" — used in Ch 2 and here without full definition. In Ch 2 it gets a parenthetical ("special hieroglyphic titles identifying specific royal dynasties"), but when B'alaj Chan K'awiil "used the same emblem glyph as Tikal," the reader needs to understand that this is like claiming the same crown. The political implications are stated but the concept could be clearer.

10. **Empty claim:** "Maya warfare was about capturing rival kings, seizing patron deity effigies, and forcing tribute and allegiance, not about occupying territory" — this is a strong claim but the evidence base is unstated. How do we know this? From inscriptions? From the absence of garrison evidence? The reader is told what Maya warfare was about but not how we know.

11. **Long parenthetical (20+ words):** "(each collapsing individually for locally specific reasons)" — this parenthetical is load-bearing content that deserves its own sentence, per the writing rules.

### Chapter 4

12. **Unclear age discrepancy:** "he took the throne at age twelve and held it until he was eighty, though physical analysis of his skeletal remains suggests he may have died closer to age fifty" — the discrepancy is stated but the reader doesn't learn what kind of "physical analysis" or why a 30-year gap could exist. Is this bone wear? Dental analysis? Arthritis patterns?

13. **Undefined term:** "mansard-style roof combs" — neither "mansard" nor "roof combs" is defined. A reader who doesn't know French architecture gets nothing from "mansard-style."

14. **Architecture without motivation:** "the Cross Group, a trio of temples... arranged around a small plaza" — why arranged in a trio? Why around a plaza? The text says they depict mythological narratives connecting the dynasty to creation, but doesn't explain why three temples (not two or four) or why this specific spatial arrangement.

15. **Process by result:** "Quirigua declared independence and erected the tallest freestanding stelae" — how does a vassal state "declare independence"? Did they stop paying tribute? Did they send an ambassador? Did they just start carving their own stelae? The mechanism of independence-declaration in the Maya world is never explained.

16. **Undefined term:** "atlatl dart throwers" — the atlatl is mentioned in Ch 2 and Ch 6 without a definition of what it is (a spear-throwing lever/handle that increases range and velocity).

### Chapter 5

17. **Suspiciously specific detail:** "Tikal's reservoirs held enough water to sustain the city through an 18-month dry period" — this feels very precise for an ancient reservoir system. How do we know this? From reservoir volume estimates? From modeling? The precision suggests a scholarly estimate, not a measured fact, but it's presented as fact.

18. **Empty claim:** "increased fortification (defensive walls and palisades hastily constructed around civic centers), suggesting that the elites were trying to protect themselves from their own populations" — the leap from "fortification" to "protecting themselves from their own populations" is not justified. Fortification could also mean protecting against rival city attacks, which was the norm.

19. **Undefined term:** "evapotranspiration" — defined only with a parenthetical mechanism ("trees release moisture that forms rain clouds"), which is helpful but the term itself is technical and unfamiliar.

### Chapter 6

20. **Undefined term:** "syncretic" — defined with a parenthetical ("blending Maya traditions with ideas from central Mexico"), which works, but the definition is narrow. Syncretic means blending any religious/cultural traditions, not specifically Maya+Mexican.

21. **Process by result:** "the cult of Kukulcan became the defining religious feature" — how did it "become" the defining feature? Was it promoted by rulers? Did it spread through trade networks? Did it replace older cults or absorb them? The mechanism is missing.

22. **Long parenthetical (19 words):** "(a single-masted vessel with oars and a cannon)" — borderline length, but the information is genuinely useful for picturing the vessel.

23. **Undefined term:** "Chac Mool figure (a reclining figure holding a bowl on its stomach, probably for receiving offerings)" — the definition is there, but "Chac Mool" as a term is never explained (it's a modern archaeological label, not a Maya term).

24. **Repeated uncertainty phrasing:** The chapter uses "probably" three times in quick succession in the calendar/Popol Vuh section: "probably derives from counting fingers and toes," "was probably the most widely known Maya narrative," "was probably not new." 28. **Empty claim:** "The disease did more to destroy Maya civilization than the Spanish sword ever could" — a strong claim stated flatly with no evidence or mechanism. How much of the population decline was specifically disease vs. forced labor, displacement, or famine caused by the conquest? The 90% figure is cited but attributed entirely to disease.

29. **Process by result:** "converts attended mass on Sunday and performed traditional rituals in the forest on Monday" — a vivid image, but is this documented or constructed? ## Skeptic findings (Persona B)

### Chapter 1

1. **[CHECK]** "Rainfall in the lowland core averages 60 to 100 inches (1,500 to 2,500 mm) per year, roughly four to six times what Kansas gets" — the Kansas comparison needs checking. Kansas averages ~30-35 inches/year, so "four to six times" would be 120-210 inches. The stated range (60-100 inches) is only 2-3x Kansas. The comparison is mathematically off by a factor of two.

2. **[SOFT]** "the only significant jade source in the region" — "only" is a strong claim. The Motagua Valley was the primary source, but other minor jade deposits exist in Guatemala and Honduras. "By far the most important" would be more accurate than "only."

3. **[CHECK]** "La Danta... volume... estimated at roughly 99 million cubic feet (2.8 million cubic meters), which actually exceeds the volume of the Great Pyramid at Giza" — the La Danta volume comparison to Giza's Great Pyramid is a commonly cited claim, but estimates vary significantly depending on how much of the natural hill is included. Some estimates put the artificial volume substantially below Giza. The text acknowledges the natural-hill caveat but still leads with the headline claim.

4. **[STRONG]** "the longest known sacbe running 62 miles (100 km) from the city of Coba to Yaxuna" — this is a well-attested archaeological fact. No concerns.

5. **[SOFT]** "the earliest Maya hieroglyphic inscriptions, dating to around 300 BCE" — this date is reasonable for lowland Maya inscriptions, though the San Bartolo murals contain glyphs dating to ~300-200 BCE. The claim is defensible.

### Chapter 2

6. **[CHECK]** "Around 350 CE, the Maya independently developed... zero" — the date of ~350 CE for the Maya zero is based on the Epi-Olmec Tres Zapotes Stela C and other early Long Count monuments. The earliest surviving Maya zero is from the 4th century CE, but the Long Count system (which requires zero) has earlier dates. "Around 350 CE" for the first attested Maya zero placeholder is reasonable but could be earlier. The independence claim is well-supported.

7. **[CHECK]** "Maya astronomers calculated it at 584 days, an error of less than two hours over the full cycle" — the "less than two hours" precision claim needs checking. The synodic period is 583.92 days; 584 days is 0.08 days off, which is ~1.92 hours. So "less than two hours" is technically correct but barely — it's more like "about two hours." The phrasing implies much higher precision than the actual ~1.9-hour error.

8. **[CHECK]** "On January 16, 378 CE, a figure named Siyaj K'ahk'... arrived at Tikal" — the date January 16, 378 CE is a specific Long Count conversion and is standard in the literature (8.17.1.4.12 in the Long Count). However, Long Count to Gregorian correlations depend on the GMT correlation constant, which is consensus but not universally accepted. The date should perhaps carry a note about the correlation.

9. **[STRONG]** "Isotopic analysis of his bones suggests he grew up in the Peten, not in Teotihuacan" — this refers to K'inich Yax K'uk' Mo' at Copan. The isotopic analysis (strontium isotope ratios in bones vs. teeth) is well-documented in published research. Solid.

### Chapter 3

10. **[STRONG]** "Sky Witness attacked Tikal directly... 537 CE" — this is well-attested in Tikal's Stela 17 and related monuments. No concerns.

11. **[CHECK]** "a roughly 130-year span during which the city erected almost no new dated monuments" — the Tikal Hiatus is real but its exact length is debated. Some scholars define it as ~120 years (562-682 CE), others as ~130 years. "Roughly 130-year" is defensible but the outer bound.

12. **[SOFT]** "Yuknoom Ch'een II... who ruled from roughly 636 to 686 CE and was known as 'the Great'" — calling him "the Great" is a modern scholarly appellation, not a Maya title. The text should make this clearer — "known to modern scholars as 'the Great'" would be more precise.

13. **[STRONG]** "In 695 CE, a Tikal king named Jasaw Chan K'awiil I attacked Calakmul and defeated its ruler" — well-attested from Tikal's Temple I lintel. No concerns.

### Chapter 4

14. **[CHECK]** "According to the Long Count dates on his monuments, he took the throne at age twelve and held it until he was eighty, though physical analysis of his skeletal remains suggests he may have died closer to age fifty" — the age discrepancy is a real scholarly debate. The inscriptions give dates implying an 80-year lifespan. The physical analysis by Vera Tiesler and Andrea Cucina (2004) suggested an age at death of 50-60, not "closer to age fifty." "Closer to age fifty" is the low end of the range and slightly misleading. The discrepancy is 20-30 years, not 30 years.

15. **[CHECK]** "617 glyph blocks covering three large stone panels" — the Temple of the Inscriptions contains three panels with a total of approximately 617 glyph blocks. This figure is standard in the literature (Peter Mathews's count). Acceptable.

16. **[STRONG]** "In 1952, the Mexican archaeologist Alberto Ruz Lhuillier discovered a sealed stairway" — well-documented fact. No concerns.

17. **[CHECK]** "the most famous, Lintel 24, depicts his wife Lady K'abal Xook performing a bloodletting ritual: she pulls a thorn-studded rope through her tongue" — this is a well-known image, but some scholars describe the object as a cord with thorns, not a rope with thorns. The description "thorn-studded rope" is acceptable popular shorthand but slightly imprecise.

18. **[CHECK]** "Copan's sculptors carved them as near-freestanding figures, the stone faces projecting outward with a three-dimensionality that anticipates European Renaissance sculpture by eight centuries" — the "anticipates Renaissance sculpture" comparison is dramatic and somewhat subjective. Copan stelae are indeed more three-dimensional than other Maya stelae, but comparing them to Renaissance sculpture is a stretch. The techniques, aesthetic goals, and cultural contexts are completely different. This reads as a value judgment presented as art-historical fact.

19. **[STRONG]** "Stela E, which stands over 33 feet (10 meters) tall and weighs 65 tons" — Quirigua Stela E is the tallest quarried stone monument in the Maya world. Dimensions and weight are well-documented. No concerns.

20. **[CHECK]** "over 20,000 individually carved stones fitted together" — the Governor's Palace mosaic count is commonly cited as "approximately 20,000" fitted stones, but this is an estimate, not a counted number. "Over 20,000" implies it has been counted precisely.

### Chapter 5

## Story Editor evaluation (Persona D)

## Chapter 1 — Independence and the Napatan Rise

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Egypt left behind an Egyptianized Nubian elite who, ironically, became more faithful to Amun than Egypt itself — and that theological loyalty would become a political weapon.

**What works:**
- The opening sentence ("When Egypt left Nubia, it left more behind than it intended") is perfect: short, declarative, and loaded with irony. It sets the entire TL's theme of inheritance-turned-independence.
- The Jebel Barkal section earns its length. The uraeus pillar, the theological claim, the slow accumulation of Amun credentials — the reader understands *why* Napata matters before anyone named Piye shows up.
- The El-Kurru pyramid section is one of the best in the book. "Not Egyptian pyramids" followed by the systematic comparison (small, steep, rubble-core, burial beneath not inside) teaches the reader through contrast. The payoff — "They were adapting the pyramid concept to their own aesthetic" — is exactly the right interpretive frame.
- Kashta's installation of Amenirdis I is presented as a genuine strategic coup, and the mechanism is explained step by step: what the God's Wife position was, why it mattered, how succession worked, why the Theban priests accepted it. The reader walks through the logic.

**What doesn't work:**
- The opening paragraph is a wall. The sentence beginning "It was a slow-motion institutional failure" runs through Thebes, the Viceroy, the Third Intermediate Period, and competing capitals in a single breath. This is the densest paragraph in the chapter, and it's the first one the reader hits. Consider breaking it after "drifted into warlordism."
- The *kap* system explanation in the second paragraph is embedded in a parenthetical that runs 40+ words inside an already long sentence. This is the chapter's most load-bearing concept (it explains *why* Nubians were Egyptianized) and it deserves its own sentence or two, not a parenthetical.
- The gold section (Wadi Allaqi, Amarna Letters, Phoenician cities) is informative but reads as an inventory rather than a narrative beat. It lacks a scene or human moment to anchor it. The cattle/horse section that follows is similar. Together they form a 400-word expository plateau between the Amenirdis drama and the cross-civ check.

**Momentum map:** Strong opening, sustained build through Jebel Barkal and El-Kurru, peak at Kashta/Amenirdis, slight sag in the economic sections, clean handoff to Piye.

## Chapter 2 — Piye Conquers Egypt

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A Nubian king conquers the entire Nile valley, tells us about it in extraordinary detail on the Victory Stela, and then goes home — and the going-home is the most revealing thing he does.

**What works:**
- The political geography tour (delta → Middle Egypt → Upper Egypt → Kush) is an efficient way to set the stage without info-dumping. Each section is one paragraph, each introduces the key player for that region.
- The horse scene is the chapter's emotional peak and it lands beautifully. "Piye went to the stables" as a one-line paragraph after a siege description is perfect pacing. The interpretation — horses as a specifically Kushite virtue, not a universal one — is the kind of insight that makes popular history worth reading.
- The "And then Piye did something that has puzzled historians ever since. He went home." turn is the chapter's structural masterstroke. The three possible explanations (religious, practical, cultural) are presented honestly without forcing a winner.
- The horse burial at El-Kurru, with the animals "interred standing upright in their grave pits, adorned with beadwork," is a vivid specific that earns its place.
- The Cyrus Cylinder / Victory Stela parallel is the best cross-civ comparison in the chapter — structurally parallel, chronologically interesting, and it teaches something about ancient propaganda conventions.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Tefnakht/Hermopolis siege section is solid but could be tighter. The chapter spends more words on the approach-march logistics than on the fall of Memphis, which is arguably the more dramatic event.
- The "fish-eaters and uncircumcised" passage is fascinating but the explanation of why these categories mattered is slightly underdeveloped. The chapter says fish-eating was "considered impure" but doesn't explain *why* — in a narrative that is otherwise excellent at explaining mechanisms, this one is result-only.
- "Namlot of Hermopolis" appears twice — once during the siege and once during the submission — and the second appearance says he "had presumably been purified after his surrender." The "presumably" flags uncertainty, but the reader might wonder: purified how? What does that mean in this context?

**Momentum map:** Steady build through the political geography, accelerating through the campaign, peaking at the horse scene, sustaining through Memphis, then a surprising second peak at "he went home." One of the best-paced chapters in the TL.

## Chapter 3 — The Black Pharaohs: 25th Dynasty

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The Kushite pharaohs didn't just conquer Egypt — they renovated it. They were the most zealous curators of Egyptian heritage Egypt had seen in centuries, and the irony is that these outsider-custodians did a better job than the insiders.

**What works:**
- The Shabaka Stone section is intellectually ambitious and pulls it off. The Memphite Theology — creation through speech — is explained clearly, the scholarly debate about the text's age is handled honestly, and the connection to "In the beginning was the Word" gives the reader a modern anchor without being forced.
- The Taharqa building program is rendered with genuine awe (the 65-foot columns, the 100-ton quarrying) while still being specific. The reader can picture the scale.
- The cultural identity section at the chapter's end is the book's most sophisticated passage. "Were the Kushite pharaohs Egyptian or Nubian? The honest answer is both" is exactly the right framing. The discussion of how European Egyptology has struggled with the dynasty is frank without being preachy.
- The Nebuchadnezzar parallel (outsider revivalists) is the best cross-civ comparison in the chapter.

**What doesn't work:**
- Shebitku gets compressed into a single section that is mostly about the *possibility* of Kushite forces at the siege of Jerusalem. The uncertainty is handled well ("the evidence is not conclusive"), but the reader may feel that the chapter spends more time on what Shebitku *might* have done than on what he *did* do. This is honestly a source problem more than a writing problem, but it's worth noting.
- The Shabaka-Shebitku succession controversy is mentioned in a parenthetical that is 60+ words long and disrupts the flow. This is important enough to be its own sentence(s).
- The final section on modern Egyptology's discomfort with the "Black Pharaohs" is well-written but risks reading as editorial commentary rather than historical analysis. The chapter is at its best when it shows rather than tells — the Taharqa columns, the artistic realism, the Shabaka Stone — and the editorializing at the end, while valid, is a tonal shift.

**Momentum map:** Steady build through Shabaka's governance, intellectual peak at the Shabaka Stone, visual peak at Taharqa's Karnak columns, then a long reflective coda. The coda is thoughtful but slows momentum before the Assyrian cliffhanger.

## Chapter 4 — The Assyrian Storm

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** The most powerful military machine in the world arrives, the Kushite pharaohs refuse to accept defeat because their theology won't let them, and the sack of Thebes ends the dream.

**What works:**
- The 674 BCE failure / 671 BCE success sequence is well-paced. The first failed invasion gets a short paragraph; the second gets the full treatment. The contrast in weight mirrors the contrast in outcomes.
- "Taharqa could not stop trying because his entire system of belief told him he was supposed to be the pharaoh." This is the chapter's thesis sentence, and it's placed exactly right — after the second defeat, when the reader might otherwise wonder why Taharqa didn't just give up. The theological explanation converts stubbornness into tragedy.
- The sack of Thebes section carries genuine weight. The Nahum quotation is a powerful external validation of the event's significance, and placing it right after the plunder description is effective sequencing.
- The Psamtik II raid (593 BCE) and the Greek mercenary graffiti at Abu Simbel are vivid specifics that enrich the retreat narrative.
- The structural comparison with Elam losing Khuzestan and retreating to Anshan is one of the best cross-civ comparisons in the TL — both civilizations have two-part geographies that allow them to absorb the loss of their richer half.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Dream Stela of Tantamani is mentioned but underplayed. The two serpents = two halves of the kingdom is a vivid image that deserves more than a sentence of description. This is the kind of scene-moment the chapter needs more of.
- The Assyrian military description ("professional infantry, cavalry, siege engineers, and a logistical train") is a standard-issue inventory that could apply to almost any ancient army. The chapter should name something *specifically Assyrian* — siege ramps, iron battering rams, mass deportation — to distinguish this army from every other powerful army described in the TL.
- The post-retreat section on priestly power is important for setting up Chapter 5 (Arkamani killing the priests) but it reads as exposition. The forward reference "a story we will reach in Chapter 5" is good, but the section itself is informational rather than narrative.
- The sentence "Egypt entered what historians call the **Third Intermediate Period**" appears in this chapter's context as a re-explanation when it was already defined in Chapter 1.

**Momentum map:** Two-peak structure — first peak at the fall of Memphis, second at the sack of Thebes. The post-retreat sections are necessarily lower-energy but set up the next chapter well.

## Chapter 5 — The Move to Meroë

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Kush reinvents itself at Meroë — escaping vulnerability, gaining iron and trade, breaking the priesthood's power, and beginning to face south rather than north.

**What works:**
- The "Birmingham of Africa" debate is handled with exemplary intellectual honesty. The old narrative is presented, the recent pushback is explained, and the conclusion ("the honest statement is...") is measured. This is the best historiographic discussion in the TL.
- The Arkamani/Diodorus section is one of the most compelling passages in the book. The "king ordered to commit suicide by priests, refuses, kills the priests instead" story is dramatic, the cautions about Diodorus's reliability are appropriate, and the "kernel of truth" extraction is well-calibrated.
- The Meroë pyramid necropolis description is effective — "a forest of sharp stone needles rising from the desert ridge" is the chapter's best image.
- The "255 known pyramids in Nubia versus about 118 in Egypt" comparison is a genuine jaw-dropper that most readers won't expect.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter is the most expository in the TL. The iron section, the ecological section, the trade section, the city description, and the pyramid description are all well-written individually, but stacked together they produce a long informational plateau without a strong narrative through-line. The chapter reads more like a feature article about Meroë than a chapter in a story.
- The Arkamani/priests story is the chapter's only genuine *scene*, and it comes a third of the way through. Everything after it is description and analysis. Consider whether a second scene-moment (the first burial at Meroë? the construction of the Amun temple?) could break the expository stretch.
- The section on the new capital's layout (Amun temple, Royal Baths, ram-headed sphinxes) is detailed but reads as a guided tour. The parenthetical definitions of temple elements (pylon gateway, hypostyle hall, sanctuary) are thorough but collectively long — four parenthetical architectural definitions in one paragraph slow the pace considerably.
- The closing paragraph about the pain of moving a capital is analytical and perceptive, but it arrives after the reader has already absorbed the move through 2,000 words of description. Consider moving this reflective passage earlier, as a framing device for the move rather than a postscript.

**Momentum map:** Peaks at Arkamani's confrontation with the priests, then a long sustained-energy section of description and analysis without a second peak. The chapter delivers information effectively but doesn't have the narrative drive of Chapters 2 or 4.

## Chapter 6 — Iron, Script, and Queens

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** Meroitic Kush develops its own voice — a script nobody else can read, a god nobody else worships, queens nobody else empowers, and pottery nobody else can match.

**What works:**
- The Meroitic script section is the chapter's intellectual centerpiece and it's outstanding. "We can pronounce Meroitic words aloud... And we still, after more than a century of effort, have no idea what most of the words mean" is genuinely moving. The comparison to "a book written in an alphabet you recognize — you can sound out every syllable — but the words are in a language no one on Earth speaks" is the best analogy in the TL.
- The Apedemak section is vivid and specific. The multi-armed iconography with the Indian art comparison is exactly the kind of cross-cultural observation that makes this series distinctive.
- The Kandake section handles the cross-civ comparison (Hatshepsut, Sobekneferu, Cleopatra, Wu Zetian, zero Roman female emperors) with precision and impact. The list format works because each entry adds weight.
- The pottery section is a surprise closer that works. The 3,000-year craft lineage (A-Group → Kerma → Meroitic) is the kind of deep-time connection that rewards the reader who has read the previous book.
- The Musawwarat es-Sufra Great Enclosure is intriguing — the elephant-training hypothesis is colorfully presented.

**What doesn't work:**
- The chapter covers four major topics (script, god, queens, pottery) plus Musawwarat. That's a lot. The individual sections are all good, but the chapter doesn't have a single driving narrative arc the way Chapters 2 or 4 do. The throughline is "Kush develops its own identity," which is thematic rather than narrative.
- The abugida definition and Brahmi comparison is interesting but may be too technical for the newcomer reader. The paragraph that explains how the Meroitic script differs structurally from Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Indian systems is specialist knowledge that most general readers won't absorb.
- The Kandake section says "at least seven Kandakes are attested" but names only Amanirenas (in passing, with a forward reference to Chapter 7). The section would benefit from naming one or two additional Kandakes with a sentence of context each, to make the institution feel populated rather than abstract.
- "The elephants bring us to one of the more colorful aspects" is a slightly tour-guide-y transition. The rule about killing "we met" phrasing extends to "bring us to" constructions.

**Momentum map:** Peaks at the Meroitic script frustration passage, sustains through Apedemak and the Kandakes, ends with a satisfying pottery coda. No dramatic arc but consistent intellectual engagement.

## Chapter 7 — Rome's Southern Frontier

**Grade: STRONG**

**Throughline:** A one-eyed warrior queen raids the Roman frontier, buries the emperor's face under a temple doorstep, and negotiates a peace treaty that most of Rome's enemies never got.

**What works:**
- The Meroë Head sequence is the best set-piece in the TL. The raid, the toppled statues, the bronze head, the burial beneath the temple steps, the 1910 discovery — it's paced like a thriller and the symbolic interpretation ("every worshipper entering the temple would step on the emperor's face") is devastating. This is the passage readers will remember.
- "The buried bronze head was the exclamation point." Perfect chapter mid-point sentence.
- The Treaty of Samos terms are presented as a list, which is the right format — the reader needs to see each term individually to appreciate how favorable they were.
- Augustus's pragmatic calculation (Egypt's grain > Nubia's sand) is a clean explanation of why Rome accepted the terms.
- The Amanishakheto/Ferlini digression is well-placed as a coda and the destruction-of-context point is made effectively.

**What doesn't work:**
- The Cleopatra parenthetical in the opening paragraph ("the Cleopatra, the one whose romantic entanglements with Julius Caesar and Mark Antony have been the subject of more plays, films, and novels than almost any other ancient figure") is 30+ words and reads as a digression that the reader doesn't need. Every reader already knows which Cleopatra this is.
- The Dodecaschoenos definition is a 40+ word parenthetical that stops the sentence dead. The Greek etymology is interesting but should be its own sentence.
- Petronius's march to Napata is described as "reportedly" reaching Napata — the chapter says "if true" and then builds on the claim without fully addressing the uncertainty. How good is the evidence that the Romans actually reached Napata? The chapter should commit to a position or flag the debate more explicitly.
- The Natakamani/Amanitore building program section at the chapter's end is well-written but feels like it belongs in a different chapter — it's the last golden age, not the Roman war. Consider whether this section should open Chapter 8 instead.
- The hafir section is informative but is a long engineering digression in what should be the war-and-diplomacy chapter. The Mesopotamian canal and Harappan drainage comparisons are individually interesting but together they extend a tangent.

**Momentum map:** Builds steadily through the political setup, peaks at the Meroë Head burial, sustains through the treaty, then dissipates into the Natakamani/Amanitore building program. The chapter's best material is its first two-thirds.

## Chapter 8 — The Long Twilight

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** Kush didn't fall to a single blow — it faded under four compounding pressures (trade shifts, deforestation, Roman decline, internal fragmentation), and then Aksum arrived to finish the job.

**What works:**
- The four-problem structure (trade, ecology, Roman frontier, internal) is clear and well-organized. Each section has its own thesis and its own evidence.
- The Aksum/Adulis trade-shift explanation is the chapter's best section. The economic logic ("every ton of ivory that went east through Adulis was a ton that did not go north through Meroë") is vivid and concrete.
- The Periplus reference — "Meroë is not mentioned" — is a brilliantly chosen absence. The silence of the source says more than any description could.
- The Ezana inscription in three scripts (Ge'ez, Sabaean, Greek) is a good detail that locates Aksum in its cultural context.
- The closing passage ("They conquered Egypt. They fought Rome. They wrote in a script nobody can read. They built more pyramids than the pharaohs. And they lasted for fourteen hundred years.") is an effective summation — punchy, rhythmic, and earned by the preceding eight chapters.

**What doesn't work:**
- The deforestation section is the longest of the four problems, but it's also the most hypothetical. The chapter spends 400+ words on a process it acknowledges is "debated" and "not proven." The Maya and Anasazi parallels are interesting but they extend a speculative section rather than firming it up.
- The "third problem was the Roman frontier" section starts with a good description of the cross-frontier Isis cult at Philae, then pivots to the Crisis of the Third Century. The pivot contains a redundant "But" opening: "But the stability depended on both sides being strong enough to maintain it. But the Roman Empire of the third century CE was not the Roman Empire of Augustus's day." Two consecutive "But" sentences.
- The "fourth problem was internal" is the weakest section because the evidence is thinnest. "Pyramids became smaller" and "inscriptions became shorter" are suggestive but the chapter itself acknowledges they're "not definitive signs of state collapse." The section reads as hedged — correctly hedged, but hedged.
- Ezana's Christian conversion is mentioned twice in nearly identical language within three paragraphs ("one of the first states in the world to adopt Christianity as an official religion, roughly contemporary with the Roman Empire's own conversion under Constantine"). The duplication is verbatim.
- The post-conquest wrap-up (Nobatia, Makuria, Alodia, modern archaeology) is a graceful landing but runs slightly long. The reader has already received the emotional conclusion ("That is the Kingdom of Kush"); the archaeological epilogue dilutes it slightly.

**Momentum map:** Four-part problem analysis with even energy, then a dramatic peak at Ezana's conquest, followed by a reflective coda. The structure is sound but the middle sections lack narrative drive — they describe processes, not events.

## TL-wide assessment

**Overall grade: STRONG**

**Arc:** The 8-chapter arc is well-designed. The TL's central theme — reinvention — is earned across the full span: Napata → Egypt → retreat → Meroë → war with Rome → twilight. Each chapter advances the theme while telling its own story. The strongest chapters (2, 4, 7) have genuine dramatic set-pieces. The weaker chapters (5, 8) are more expository but still informative.

**Voice:** Consistent with the series. The informal tone is maintained without becoming jokey. The uncertainty language is varied. The cross-civ comparisons are frequent and well-chosen.

**Structural concern:** Chapters 5 and 8 are both "process" chapters (one about building a new capital, one about decline) that lack strong human characters or scenes. They're the only two chapters in the TL that grade GOOD rather than STRONG, and for the same reason: expository depth at the expense of narrative momentum. Consider whether a scene-moment (a named person doing a specific thing) could anchor each.

## Newcomer evaluation (Persona A)

### Undefined or under-defined terms

1. **Ch 1:** "the *kap* system (the program in which foreign children were raised alongside Egyptian princes...)" — this parenthetical is 35+ words. The concept is critical and should be its own sentence. **Must fix.**

2. **Ch 1:** "the last Viceroy of Kush (the pharaoh-appointed governor who had run Nubia as an Egyptian province) drifted into warlordism" — "warlordism" is never defined. A newcomer won't know what a Nubian warlord looks like. What did this person actually *do*? **Should fix.**

3. **Ch 2:** "embankments, towers, and a continuous blockade of the city's approaches" — siege works are described by result, not mechanism. How do you build an embankment against a walled city? What are the towers for? The chapter says the siege capability was "comparable to what the Assyrians were deploying" but doesn't tell the reader what that actually looked like. **Should fix.**

4. **Ch 2:** "uncircumcised and fish-eaters" — circumcision and fish-eating as categories of ritual impurity are stated as facts but the *mechanism* of impurity is not explained. Why is eating fish impure when you live on a river? The chapter says "the strict interpretation of Amun ritual" but that's circular — the reader needs to know what the theological logic was, even if the answer is "we're not sure." **Should fix.**

5. **Ch 3:** "Memphite Theology" — defined adequately (creation through speech), but the term "theology" itself might be unfamiliar to a true newcomer. The Indus Valley and Mesopotamia TLs define this; consistency suggests a brief gloss here. **Soft.**

6. **Ch 3:** "hypostyle hall" — defined inline in Ch 5 ("a roofed hall supported by multiple columns, the traditional setting for ritual activities") but used without definition in Ch 3 when describing Karnak. First use per chapter should get the definition. **Must fix.**

7. **Ch 4:** "nomes" — defined as "the traditional administrative districts" with a parenthetical. Adequate. No issue.

8. **Ch 5:** "abugida" — defined and pronounced. The explanation is technically precise but may lose general readers. The sentence "a writing system in which each symbol represents a consonant with an inherent vowel, and additional marks modify the vowel" could use a concrete example (e.g., "imagine if the letter 'k' automatically included an 'a' sound — 'ka' — and you added a mark to change it to 'ki' or 'ku'"). **Should fix.**

9. **Ch 5:** "hafirs" — mentioned in Ch 5's city description in passing. Fully explained in Ch 7. First mention should have a brief gloss. **Should fix.**

10. **Ch 6:** "Meroitic fine ware" — "coil-building" is mentioned without explanation. How does coil-building work? The chapter says "relying on coil-building and hand-shaping techniques" but a reader who doesn't know what coil-building is cannot appreciate the skill claim. **Should fix.**

11. **Ch 7:** "Dodecaschoenos" — the parenthetical definition is 40+ words and includes a unit-of-measurement explanation. Should be its own sentence. **Must fix.**

12. **Ch 8:** "coppicing" — defined inline as "(the practice of cutting trees at the base and allowing them to regrow from the stump, producing a sustainable harvest of smaller poles rather than killing the tree)." Good definition but it's a 30-word parenthetical inside a conditional sentence. Make it its own sentence. **Should fix.**

### Processes described by result, not mechanism

13. **Ch 1:** "These families spoke Egyptian, wrote in hieroglyphic, worshipped the god Amun... and ran their affairs using Egyptian administrative methods." — What are "Egyptian administrative methods"? This is a placeholder phrase. What did the administration actually look like? Scribes recording grain? Tax collectors measuring fields? **Should fix.**

14. **Ch 1:** "the gradual merging of scattered communities along the upper Nile into something that looks like a unified polity" — *How* did they merge? Military conquest? Marriage alliances? Shared Amun worship creating a common identity? The chapter says "we cannot put names on the rulers" so the mechanism is honestly unknown — but the chapter should say "we don't know how" explicitly rather than just describing the result. **Should fix.**

15. **Ch 4:** "Shabaka also completed the military pacification of the delta that Piye had left unfinished. The sources for exactly how he did this are thin" — This is actually good practice: the chapter states the result and immediately flags that the mechanism is unknown. No issue.

## Skeptic evaluation (Persona B)

### Chapter 1

1. **"four hundred years"** of Egyptian colonial rule over Nubia. [STRONG] — Well-established in Egyptology (New Kingdom rule over Nubia from roughly 1500 to 1070 BCE). Standard figure.

2. **"Jebel Barkal... rising about 300 feet (nearly 100 meters)"** — [STRONG] The mesa is well-measured; 98m is the standard figure in the literature.

3. **"Thutmose III... around 1450 BCE"** built the first Barkal temple. [STRONG] Thutmose III's Nubian campaigns and Barkal temple construction are well-attested.

4. **"Alara... who ruled from about 785 to 760 BCE."** [CHECK] — Alara's dates are conventional estimates based on king-list reconstruction, not on dated inscriptions. Some scholars place him earlier (as early as 850 BCE) or later. The chapter says "with reasonable confidence" — this should be "by conventional reconstruction" or similar softening, since the confidence is lower than the phrase implies. **Should fix.**

5. **"Kashta... ruled from roughly 760 to 747 BCE"** [CHECK] — Same issue as Alara. These are reconstructed dates, not attested ones. The "roughly" qualifier helps but the precision of "747" implies more certainty than exists. **Soft.**

6. **"Amenirdis I" installed as God's Wife of Amun** by Kashta. [STRONG] — Well-attested. Amenirdis I's adoption stela survives at Karnak.

### Chapter 2

7. **"Around 730 BCE, Tefnakht's expansion southward"** — [CHECK] The date of Tefnakht's expansion is debated; some scholars place the crisis that triggered Piye's invasion closer to 728 or 727 BCE. The "around 730" framing is defensible but the chapter should be explicit that this is approximate. The chapter already says "around" — adequate.

8. **"over 150 lines of hieroglyphic text"** on the Victory Stela. [STRONG] — The stela is well-published. Line counts vary slightly by edition but 150+ is standard.

9. **"about 6 feet (1.8 meters) high and 4.7 feet (1.4 meters) wide"** — [STRONG] Consistent with published measurements of the Victory Stela in the Cairo Museum.

10. **"discovered in 1862 by a team working at Jebel Barkal"** — [CHECK] The stela was found by Auguste Mariette's team in 1862 at Jebel Barkal. The phrasing "a team" is vague — Mariette's name would be more specific. **Soft.**

11. **"exploited the high Nile flood... to sail their ships right up to the city's harbor walls"** — [CHECK] This tactical detail comes from the Victory Stela itself, which is royal propaganda and may exaggerate. The stela describes the flood-assault, and the description is plausible (Memphis was adjacent to the Nile and vulnerable during high water), but "modern military historians have been able to reconstruct the general shape of the assault" is a claim that needs a citation or scholar name. Which military historians? **Should fix.**

# Persona A (Curious Newcomer) Audit — Early Andean Civilizations

## Chapter 1 — The Vertical World

1. **"The Andes did something different."** — Empty claim before explanation — What did they do differently? (Resolved two sentences later, but the standalone sentence reads as a placeholder.)

2. **"every few thousand feet of altitude gain puts you in a fundamentally different world — different temperature, different rainfall, different crops, different animals, different everything"** — The list is suggestive but gives no concrete examples yet — Can you name one crop or animal that appears at one altitude but not another before the formal zone breakdown?

3. **"It shaped the economy, the religion, the politics, and the technology of every culture that ever developed in what is now Peru and Bolivia"** — Sweeping claim with no mechanism — How did altitude shape religion? How did it shape politics? The economy explanation comes later, but religion and politics never get a concrete example in this chapter.

4. **"the Atacama Desert, which begins at the southern end"** — Southern end of what? — The Peruvian coast, presumably, but the antecedent is vague since we just talked about the Pacific Ocean.

5. **"Pre-ceramic Peruvians had access to more animal protein per kilometer of coastline than almost any other coastal people in the ancient world."** — Unsubstantiated comparative claim — More than the Norse? More than Pacific Northwest peoples? What is this based on?

6. **"the valleys function as semi-independent geographic units"** — Abstract claim — What does "semi-independent" mean concretely? Did people in different valleys speak different languages, worship different gods, fight wars?

7. **"both domesticated from wild guanacos and vicunas around 3500 BCE"** — Undefined species — What are guanacos and vicunas? Are they related to llamas, or something else entirely? (The reader has zero prior knowledge.)

8. **"physiological adaptations to altitude — larger lungs, higher red blood cell counts, more efficient hemoglobin"** — Process described by result — How does the body develop these? Are these genetic changes over generations, or does any individual who lives at altitude develop them?

9. **"The basic unit of Andean social organization was the ayllu"** — Forward reach without clear payoff in this chapter — We're told the ayllu persisted "from the earliest recorded periods through the Inca Empire," but we never learn how early archaeological evidence confirms ayllus existed. Is there direct evidence, or is this projected backward from the Inca?

10. **"possibly narrative information"** — Vague hedge — What is the evidence that quipus might encode narrative? One sentence of context would help.

11. **"a recording device made of knotted strings, which could encode numerical data and possibly narrative information, but which is not writing in the strict sense (writing being a system of visible symbols that represents the full range of a spoken language)"** — Long parenthetical (25 words) — Could be shortened or moved to its own sentence.

12. **"El Nino events happen irregularly, every 2 to 7 years, with major events every few decades and catastrophic events every few centuries."** — What is the difference between "major" and "catastrophic"? — Can you give an example of what a catastrophic El Nino does versus a regular one?

13. **"many of the dramatic cultural discontinuities in the archaeological record — temples abandoned, populations relocated, trade networks collapsed — may be linked to particularly severe El Nino events"** — Empty hedge — "May be linked" does all the work. Is there any specific example where archaeologists have actually connected a temple abandonment to a dated El Nino?

## Chapter 2 — First Temples on the Coast

14. **"something that required a structure larger than a house, more permanent than a tent, and more public than a storage room"** — Empty elaboration — The triple comparison sounds precise but says nothing concrete about what the structures were for. "Ceremonial" is stated right after, but this sentence is padding.

15. **"If the date holds (and it has been challenged)"** — Challenged by whom and on what grounds? — A reader cannot evaluate this without knowing the nature of the challenge.

16. **"a shared ceremonial meaning across the entire Andean cultural area"** — Empty claim — What shared meaning? The Pachamama connection is offered tentatively one sentence later, but "shared ceremonial meaning" is doing no work on its own.

17. **"literally 'in the earth' — in a way that may have had religious significance in a culture that would later venerate the earth itself as a living deity (Pachamama, the earth mother, whose cult persists in the Andes today)"** — Long parenthetical (19 words) — Pachamama deserves her own sentence rather than being squeezed into a parenthetical.

18. **"the American archaeologist Junius Bird"** — Undefined figure — Who was Junius Bird? Why does he matter? Just "American archaeologist" gives us nothing to hold onto.

19. **"re-excavated in the 2000s and 2010s by Tom Dillehay"** — Undefined figure — Same issue. Why did Dillehay return to a site already excavated? What was he looking for?

20. **"a technique called twining — a method of interworking threads where two weft threads twist around each warp thread, locking the fabric together without the use of a loom"** — "Weft" and "warp" are undefined — A reader with zero textile knowledge doesn't know which direction these threads go or how they differ.

21. **"cotton seine nets, dragged through schools of anchovies by teams of fishermen, are fast and efficient"** — "Seine net" is undefined — What makes a seine net different from a regular net?

22. **"Vichama, Miraya, Caballete, Huaricanga"** — Four undefined proper nouns in a list — Are these important individually, or just evidence of density? A brief characterization would help.

23. **"the phenomenon we call Norte Chico was not one city. It was a regional civilization"** — What defines the boundary between "regional civilization" and "a bunch of villages that trade with each other"? — The claim is asserted, not argued.

## Chapter 3 — Caral: City Without War

24. **"a cluster of eroding mounds in the Supe Valley"** — What do eroding mounds look like? — A brief visual would help a reader who has never seen an archaeological site in the desert.

25. **"amphitheater-like structure"** — What does this mean concretely? — Stepped seating? A curved open space? The word "amphitheater" implies Roman architecture; how is this one similar or different?

26. **"the labor to build it was organized through voluntary participation in a shared ceremonial system rather than through coercion or conquest"** — But how? — What motivated thousands of people to volunteer labor? Religious obligation is named, but what was the mechanism? Did they believe the gods would punish non-participation? Did they get prestige? The word "voluntary" is doing enormous work without support.

27. **"a theocratic model: political authority derived from religious authority, construction financed by ritual obligation, social cohesion maintained through shared participation in a ceremonial cycle"** — Process described entirely by abstraction — What does "ritual obligation" look like in practice? Did families owe a certain number of days per year? Was there a festive season for building? The concept is clear but the concrete picture is blank.

28. **"Cotton grows well in arid conditions with irrigation"** — But why? — What is it about cotton that makes it suited to arid irrigated conditions as opposed to, say, a wetter climate?

29. **"When reconstructed and played, they produce a five-tone pentatonic scale"** — Who reconstructed them and when? — Did archaeologists play ancient bone flutes, or did they make replicas?

30. **"the same scale that underpins most Andean folk music today"** — Empty continuity claim — Does this actually prove a continuous musical tradition, or is the pentatonic scale so common worldwide that the match could be coincidental?

31. **"fragments of knotted string that appear to be a quipu"** — How confident is the identification? — "Appear to be" is vague. What makes it look like a quipu rather than just knotted string?

32. **"A long knot with four turns = 4. A single knot in the tens position = 10. A cluster of three knots in the hundreds position = 300."** — Who decoded this system and when? — The reader is told the code hasn't been "cracked" for narrative quipus, but the numerical decoding is presented as settled fact without attribution.

33. **"the economic system that sustained Caral slowly shifted"** — But how specifically? — We're told population grew and new sites were founded, but what made the Supe Valley lose its centrality? Was it that other valleys had better farmland, better ports, more fish?

## Chapter 4 — After Caral: The Initial Period

34. **"The technology probably arrived from the north, from Ecuador or Colombia, where pottery traditions were already several centuries old."** — Empty attribution — What is the evidence that pottery came from the north? Stylistic similarity? Dated sequences? Trade goods? "Probably" and "from the north" is very hand-wavy.

35. **"a frontal face with staring round eyes, a wide mouth full of interlocking fangs, and radiating hair or appendages that suggest something feline, supernatural, and dangerous"** — "Appendages" is vague — Hair? Tentacles? Snake-like projections? What do the "appendages" actually look like?

36. **"It probably represents a deity or class of deities associated with transformation, predation, agricultural fertility, and the supernatural world."** — Four abstract associations in a single sentence — Can we get one concrete example of why archaeologists associate the fanged face with "agricultural fertility"? The others get some support later, but fertility never does.

37. **"Every stone, every basket of fill, every bucket of plaster was carried to the site on someone's back."** — How many laborers and how long? — The passage emphasizes the achievement but gives no estimate of the workforce required, which would make the claim vivid.

38. **"somebody built a very different kind of monument"** — Who? — The text never identifies who built Cerro Sechin. Same community as Sechin Alto? A rival group? A later generation?

39. **"a recurring ritual of human sacrifice, a mythological combat, or some combination"** — Three possibilities listed without any indication of which is most supported — Is there evidence that tips the scale toward any of these?

40. **"The Norte Chico 'peaceful civilization' narrative, already fragile, does not survive contact with Cerro Sechin."** — But Cerro Sechin is Initial Period, not Norte Chico — Is this saying the Norte Chico people themselves turned violent, or that different people in the same region were violent? The chronological and ethnic relationship between Norte Chico and Cerro Sechin is unclear.

41. **"the old Norte Chico methods (fieldstone and fill, some shicra-bag technique)"** — "Fieldstone" is undefined — What is fieldstone? Unworked stone gathered from fields?

42. **"a series of small ceremonial rooms, each with a central fire pit and smooth plastered walls"** — Architectural choice without motivation — Why fire pits? Were they burning offerings, heating the room, producing smoke for rituals? The fire pit is described but never explained.

43. **"The highland and coastal traditions were running on parallel tracks."** — Empty metaphor — What specifically was parallel? Both had temples, yes, but what made them "parallel" rather than just "both existed"?

44. **"gold hammering first, then copper smelting"** — Process described by result — How does gold hammering work? Is native gold soft enough to just pound flat, or does it need heat? A sentence on the technique would help.

45. **"the spondylus would become the most sacred material in the Andean world — more valued than gold, more sacred than jade"** — Was jade actually present and valued in the Andes? — Jade is a Mesoamerican association. This comparison might confuse a reader who doesn't know whether jade mattered in Peru.

46. **"diving 30 to 60 feet (10 to 20 meters) in open ocean without equipment, prying the shells off underwater rock faces"** — Who were these divers? — Ecuadorean specialists? Peruvian traders who went north? This is a dangerous activity and the people who did it deserve some identification.

## Chapter 5 — U-Shaped Worlds

47. **"Between roughly 1500 and 800 BCE, the central and north coast of Peru filled up with temples that all looked the same."** — "All looked the same" is imprecise — Did they share the same size? Same materials? Or just the same U-shape plan? The next paragraph specifies the form, but the opening claim overstates the uniformity.

48. **"The open end of the U faced east — toward the Andes, toward the rising sun, toward the source of the rivers"** — Three motivations listed without indicating which (if any) archaeologists favor — Was it about the mountains, the sun, or the water? Are these the same explanation or competing ones?

49. **"The lateral wings were lower, plainer, and may have served as administrative or residential quarters for the people who maintained the temple."** — "May have served" is empty — What is the evidence? Were there hearths, sleeping platforms, storage rooms? Or is this pure speculation?

50. **"Several of the largest U-shaped temples in Peru are now archaeological sites surrounded by apartment buildings, intersected by highways, or (in a few cases) completely destroyed by suburban development."** — Which ones? — A specific example would make this concrete rather than a general lament.

51. **"each phase buried under the next in the standard Andean pattern of ritual renewal (you don't demolish the old temple — you bury it whole under a new layer of fill and build the new temple on top)"** — Motivation missing — Why bury it rather than demolish it? Was the old temple still sacred? Was there a taboo against destroying sacred architecture? "Standard Andean pattern" is asserted without explaining why it was standard.

52. **"The friezes were public art — visible to anyone entering the courtyard"** — But who entered the courtyard? — Was it open to all community members, or restricted to elites and priests? "Public" is doing a lot of work.

53. **"You were not just entering a building. You were entering a mouth."** — Evocative, but is this the writer's metaphor or the builders' intent? — Did the builders actually design the entrance as a mouth, or is this a modern interpretation?

54. **"Shamans in later Andean cultures are frequently described as 'becoming jaguars' during their rituals"** — Described by whom? — Spanish colonial sources? Ethnographic accounts? Archaeological iconography? The source type matters for credibility.

55. **"adopting the jaguar's identity through the ingestion of hallucinogenic plants and the wearing of jaguar skins and fang ornaments"** — Which hallucinogenic plants specifically? — San Pedro is named later in the chapter, but here the reference is generic.

56. **"Cupisnique goldsmiths produced some of the earliest gold crowns, nose ornaments, and ear spools in Peru"** — "Some of the earliest" is vague — Earlier than what? Contemporary with what? A date or relative placement would help.

57. **"the artistic tradition that Chavin de Huantar drew on most heavily when it built its own temple and invented its own style"** — "Drew on most heavily" — What is the evidence for this? Stylistic similarity alone, or are there Cupisnique objects found at Chavin?

58. **"dried San Pedro fragments have been found in archaeological contexts as early as 2000 BCE at highland sites"** — Which sites? — An unnamed "highland sites" is vague for a significant archaeological claim.

59. **"ethnographic accounts of Andean shamanism (accounts recorded by anthropologists in the 20th century, working with living practitioners whose traditions appear to trace back centuries)"** — Long parenthetical (20 words) — Could be its own sentence. Also, "appear to trace back centuries" is a significant hedge buried in a parenthetical.

## Chapter 6 — Chavin de Huantar

60. **"The setting is dramatic in the way that major pilgrimage sites tend to be dramatic: isolated enough to require a journey, beautiful enough to reward it, and acoustically alive in a way that a flat coastal site never is."** — Empty generalization about "major pilgrimage sites" — What other pilgrimage sites is this compared to? The claim that pilgrimage sites "tend to be dramatic" is unsupported.

61. **"born in the highlands, trained in medicine, self-taught in archaeology"** — Which highland community? Where was he trained? — Tello is introduced as important but his background is sketched too thinly to understand why his indigenous identity mattered for his interpretation.

62. **"a place where coastal and highland traditions were synthesized into a unified artistic and theological system"** — Empty summary — What specifically was synthesized? The coast contributed art and the highlands contributed drugs (stated later), but here the claim is pure abstraction.

63. **"Both phases were built of dressed and fitted stone"** — "Dressed and fitted" is undefined — Does this mean cut into rectangular blocks? Smoothed on the face? How is this different from the fieldstone of earlier sites?

64. **"plugged into a socket in the wall like a shelf bracket"** — Good analogy, but what held them in? — Mortar? Gravity? The structural question is raised by the analogy but not answered.

65. **"The transformation sequence has been interpreted as a depiction of the shamanic experience"** — Interpreted by whom? — This is a major interpretive claim. A name or a school of thought would help.

66. **"There is no reasonable doubt that the temple's rituals involved the deliberate, controlled use of hallucinogens."** — Strong claim after pages of hedging — What makes this "no reasonable doubt" when so much else about Chavin is uncertain? The evidence (San Pedro remains, cactus carvings) is good but could use one more sentence of explicit connection.

67. **"the channels were engineered — specific widths, specific gradients, specific turns — to produce roaring, rushing, and gurgling sounds"** — How do we know the acoustic effects were intentional rather than incidental? — Did Rick and Kolar demonstrate that different channel designs would have produced different sounds, ruling out accident?

68. **"conch-shell trumpets (called pututus)"** — Where did the conch shells come from? — Conch is a marine shell; the temple is at 10,000 feet in the highlands. Were they traded from the coast?

69. **"a transient, multi-ethnic population rather than a settled local community"** — How do archaeologists distinguish "transient" from "settled" in pottery assemblages? — The claim is stated but the reasoning is invisible.

70. **"The oracle model is well attested in the later Andean world"** — But is it attested at Chavin specifically? — Projecting the Pachacamac oracle model backward onto Chavin is acknowledged as analogy, but the text slides from "probably" to describing the oracle as if it's established fact.

## Chapter 7 — The Chavin Horizon

71. **"a recognizable set of artistic conventions, religious motifs, and ceremonial practices spread across a large chunk of the central Andes"** — "A large chunk" is vague — Can we get a distance or a list of regions?

72. **"communities across Peru adopted because they wanted to, not because they were conquered"** — How do we know there was no coercion? — Absence of military evidence at Chavin doesn't prove absence of pressure at the receiving end. This is stated as fact but is an interpretation.

73. **"stone cups, bone tubes, carved stone mortars for grinding hallucinogenic powders"** — "Bone tubes" undefined — What are the bone tubes for? Snuffing ground hallucinogens? Drinking? The function is not stated.

74. **"This is how a horizon spreads without an army: through prestige."** — Empty explanatory claim — "Prestige" is doing all the work. What made Chavin prestigious specifically? The oracle? The drugs? The art quality? The text gestures at all of these but this sentence collapses them into one word.

75. **"the Bronze Age equivalent of how Hellenistic Greek art spread across the eastern Mediterranean not through conquest but through cultural prestige"** — Hellenistic Greek art did spread partly through conquest (Alexander's campaigns) — The analogy may mislead a newcomer who knows that Alexander conquered the eastern Mediterranean.

76. **"what art historians call visual kennings — images that are made of other images"** — "Kenning" is a literary term (Norse poetry); calling these "visual kennings" is jargon — A brief explanation of why the literary term was borrowed would help, or just say "composite images."

77. **"The Tello Obelisk is the most complex single carved object in pre-Columbian Peru, and interpreting it has occupied scholars for nearly a century."** — Empty superlative — What makes it "most complex"? The density of carving? The number of figures? The theological program?

78. **"two caiman (South American crocodilian) deities"** — Why caimans? — The caiman's significance is briefly glossed ("lives in water and on land, bridging the two realms") but this feels thin for a central deity image. Were caimans actually present in the Chavin region?

79. **"The Staff God is the most important deity image of the Chavin Horizon and possibly of the entire pre-Inca Andes"** — Strongest possible claim, no evidence — What makes it "most important"? Most widespread? Most frequently depicted? Most associated with elite burials?

80. **"a variant of the fanged-face deity, now rendered full-body and frontal, with the staffs in each hand serving as attributes of divine authority"** — What do the staffs represent? — "Attributes of divine authority" is circular. Are they weapons? Plants? Lightning bolts? Scepters?

81. **"the earliest-known gold crowns in Peru"** — Defined as "thin hammered-gold headbands" — Are these actually crowns, or headbands? The word "crown" implies something more imposing than a thin hammered-gold band.

82. **"the Chavín religious system had a material elite"** — But why? — If Chavin was a prestige network without political control, why were some people buried with gold and others not? What determined elite status in the Chavin system?

83. **"organized long-distance trade rather than casual exchange"** — What is the difference? — How do archaeologists distinguish organized trade from casual exchange in the archaeological record?

84. **"El Nino: a severe El Nino event around 500-400 BCE could have disrupted the trade networks"** — Repeated uncertainty phrase — "Could have" is doing all the work, and El Nino has been invoked as a possible cause of disruption multiple times now (Chapters 1, 3, 4, 7) without a single confirmed case.

85. **"the oracles stopped being accurate, or the drugs stopped working, or the social contract between the priests and the pilgrim communities broke down"** — Three speculations with no evidence for any — This reads as brainstorming rather than analysis. "The drugs stopped working" is particularly unexplained.

## Chapter 8 — Paracas and the Horizon's End

86. **"the most technically accomplished textiles the ancient world had ever seen. Not just the ancient Americas. The ancient world."** — Biggest possible claim with no supporting comparison — How do Paracas textiles compare to Chinese silk or Egyptian linen in terms of thread count, color range, or technique? The claim demands evidence.

87. **"The Paracas weavers achieved a level of technical sophistication, color range, and artistic ambition that is competitive with the finest textiles of contemporary China or Egypt"** — "Competitive with" is vague — In what specific dimension? Thread density? Number of colors? Figural complexity? One concrete metric would support this extraordinary claim.

88. **"she weaves by passing a shuttle (or just her fingers, for complex patterns) through the warp threads one row at a time"** — Gendered pronoun "she" — Is there evidence that only women wove? Or is this an assumption? Worth one sentence of context.

89. **"estimates run to hundreds of hours of skilled work for a large ceremonial mantle"** — Whose estimates? — This is presented as settled fact but could range wildly depending on the estimator's assumptions.

90. **"cochineals for red"** — "Cochineal" is undefined — What is cochineal? An insect? A plant? A mineral? (It's an insect, but a newcomer wouldn't know.)

91. **"flying figures with trailing streamers, composite human-animal creatures with fanged mouths and serpent hair, trophy heads"** — "Trophy heads" undefined — Are these actual severed human heads, or artistic representations? Were they collected as war trophies? This is the first mention of trophy heads and it needs context.

92. **"underground shaft-and-chamber tombs (the 'cavernas' or 'caverns' that give the phase its name)"** — How deep were the shafts? How were the chambers built? — The tombs are named but not described enough to picture.

93. **"Polychrome pottery" and "monochrome or unpainted"** — What does the shift from polychrome to monochrome signify? — The text notes the shift but doesn't explain what it means culturally or artistically.

94. **"over 40% of adult skulls from some Paracas cemeteries show evidence of trepanation"** — Which cemeteries? — "Some" is vague for a statistic this dramatic.

95. **"Survival rates at some Paracas sites approach 80%, which is remarkable for a culture performing brain surgery with obsidian blades and no anesthesia."** — Was it actually "brain surgery"? — Trepanation removes skull bone but doesn't necessarily involve the brain itself. Calling it "brain surgery" may overstate what was happening.

96. **"no anesthesia"** — Is this certain? — Coca leaves suppress pain and were available. Were they not used during trepanation? The claim of "no anesthesia" in a culture that chewed coca seems worth questioning.

97. **"the hole in the skull may have been believed to release evil spirits or to open a channel to the supernatural world"** — "May have been believed" is maximally hedged — Is there any iconographic or ethnographic evidence for this, or is it pure speculation?

98. **"it changed the shape of the skull but not the size of the brain, and there is no evidence that it impaired cognitive function"** — How do we know it didn't impair function? — Based on what evidence? Skull shape alone can't tell us about cognitive function.

99. **"cleared the dark surface stones to expose the lighter ground beneath, creating lines and cleared areas that are visible from above but imperceptible at ground level"** — "Imperceptible at ground level" contradicts the fact that someone had to create them at ground level — Were they designed to be seen from above? If so, by whom? The text never addresses this famous question directly.

100. **"astronomical alignments, ritual pathways, water-cult markers, and pure artistic expression have all been proposed"** — Four possibilities with no indication of current scholarly consensus — Is any one of these better supported than the others?

101. **"the things it had set in motion — the artistic conventions, the theological system, the trade networks, the pilgrimage model, the Staff God — would persist for two thousand years and would be inherited, eventually, by the Inca"** — How? — What is the evidence of continuous transmission from Chavin to the Inca across two thousand years? This is the biggest claim in the book and it gets one sentence.

102. **"textiles that nobody alive today can replicate"** — Is this literally true? — Modern weavers with the same tools cannot produce the same work? Or is this hyperbole? If literally true, what specifically can't be replicated?

103. **"the Peruvian desert etched with lines that make no sense from the ground but arrange themselves, from a height we had no way to reach in 200 BCE, into the shapes of things that fly"** — Beautiful closing, but it implicitly raises the "who were they made for" question without answering it — This is the single most famous mystery about the Nazca lines and it's left dangling in the final sentence.

**Total findings: 103**

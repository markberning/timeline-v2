# Persona B — The Skeptic: Audit of mesopotamia-rewrite.md

## Chapter 1 — The Land Between Two Rivers

1. `[CHECK]` **"roughly the size of Florida"** — The southern Mesopotamian floodplain between the Tigris and Euphrates is considerably smaller than Florida (~170,000 km²). This comparison may be inflated for rhetorical effect.

2. `[SOFT]` **"more grain per acre than almost anywhere else in the ancient world"** — Plausible but suspiciously confident. Southern Mesopotamian yields were high but salinization was a chronic problem even in early periods. The qualifier "if you can control the water" helps, but the superlative claim is hard to source.

3. `[CHECK]` **"the earliest farming villages on the southern Mesopotamian plain go back to about 6500 BCE"** — The Ubaid period is conventionally dated starting ~6500 BCE in the south, but "earliest farming villages" is doing a lot of work. The date is reasonable but the precision feels higher than the archaeology supports.

4. `[CHECK]` **"Akkadians began settling among the Sumerians in central Mesopotamia" around "2500 BCE"** — The date of Akkadian arrival is genuinely debated. Akkadian personal names appear in the record well before 2500 BCE (some as early as the Early Dynastic I period, ~2900 BCE). The 2500 BCE date may be too late and too precise.

5. `[SOFT]` **"surviving cuneiform tablets include Sumerian-Akkadian vocabulary lists, the ancient equivalent of a Spanish-English dictionary"** — Bilingual lexical lists exist, but whether they date to the period of initial Akkadian settlement (as implied) or are later Ur III / Old Babylonian products is worth checking. The implication of very early bilingual schools may be anachronistic.

## Chapter 2 — Before the Cities

6. `[CHECK]` **"Archaeologists who excavated Eridu in the 20th century found seventeen successive temples stacked directly on top of each other"** — The canonical count from Safar, Mustafa, and Lloyd's excavations is usually given as 18 or 19 levels (Temples I through XVIII/XIX), not seventeen. The number 17 may be slightly off.

7. `[CHECK]` **"kingship descended from heaven to Eridu," says one of their oldest surviving documents"** — This is from the Sumerian King List, which is generally dated to the Ur III period (~2100 BCE), not to the earliest period of Sumerian writing. Calling it "one of their oldest surviving documents" is misleading — it is an important document but not among the oldest.

8. `[STRONG]` **"Around 3460 BCE, well before any real city existed, the villagers of southern Mesopotamia had already begun... large-scale irrigation canals"** — The date 3460 BCE is suspiciously precise for a prehistoric development with no written records. Where does this specific date come from? Archaeological evidence for large-scale irrigation in the Ubaid/early Uruk periods is indirect (settlement patterns, canal traces), and no reputable source I'm aware of pins it to "3460 BCE." This smells invented.

9. `[CHECK]` **"Southern Mesopotamia receives only about 150 to 200 millimeters of rainfall per year"** — This is roughly correct for the far south but varies considerably. Baghdad gets ~150mm; Basra gets ~140mm. The range is reasonable but presented with false precision for a region with significant variation.

10. `[CHECK]` **"in the graves and temple offerings of southern Mesopotamian villages dating back to around 3000 BCE, archaeologists find small, intensely blue-colored beads of lapis lazuli"** — Lapis lazuli appears in Mesopotamian contexts much earlier than 3000 BCE — Ubaid-period sites have yielded lapis. The 3000 BCE date understates the antiquity of the trade.

11. `[CHECK]` **"the Sar-i Sang mines in Badakhshan"** — Correct name and location, but stating it was "a single source" is too strong. While Sar-i Sang is the primary ancient source, there are other minor lapis deposits (e.g., in the Chagai Hills of Pakistan). The text says "single source" which is a common simplification but technically incorrect.

## Chapter 3 — The First City: Uruk and the Sumerian Breakthrough

12. `[CHECK]` **"most archaeologists estimate 40,000 to 80,000 by 3200 BCE"** — Population estimates for Uruk vary wildly in the literature. 40,000–80,000 is within the range cited by some scholars, but many recent estimates are lower (20,000–40,000 for the walled area). The upper bound of 80,000 may be inflated.

13. `[CHECK]` **"Tell Brak in northern Syria may have been developing comparable urbanism at roughly the same time as Uruk, possibly even slightly earlier"** — This accurately reflects the work of Joan Oates and others on Tell Brak, but the claim that Tell Brak was "possibly even slightly earlier" is the strong version of a contested argument. Worth noting but not fabricated.

14. `[SOFT]` **"The name 'Uruk' may even be the source of the modern word 'Iraq'"** — This etymology is widely repeated but not universally accepted. Flagging only because it's stated as possibility ("may"), which is fair.

15. `[STRONG]` **"Someone — we do not know their name, though they may well be the most influential single person who ever lived — figured out how to make pictograms"** — Not a factual claim per se, but the implication that cuneiform was invented by a single person is a major simplification. The development of writing at Uruk was almost certainly a gradual institutional process over generations, not a single inventor's insight. This framing is misleading.

16. `[CHECK]` **"Cuneiform would go on to be used for over 3,400 years"** — The final cuneiform tablets date to approximately 75 CE (astronomical diary texts from Babylon). From ~3400 BCE to ~75 CE is about 3,475 years. The 3,400 figure is in the right range but the precision implies more certainty about the start date than exists.

17. `[CHECK]` **"the Sumerians invented something that would transform the world far beyond Mesopotamia: the wheel... archaeology suggests around 3500 BCE"** — The earliest evidence for wheels (in the form of pictographic signs and actual wheel finds) clusters around 3500–3300 BCE, but the claim that the Sumerians specifically invented it is contested. Some of the earliest wheel evidence comes from the Pontic steppe (Bronocice pot in Poland, ~3500 BCE) and the Caucasus, not Mesopotamia. The wheel may have been independently invented or diffused from elsewhere.

18. `[CHECK]` **"By 2500 BCE, Sumerian armies were using four-wheeled battle wagons drawn by teams of donkeys"** — The Standard of Ur (~2600 BCE) shows what appear to be onager-drawn (not donkey-drawn) battle wagons. The animals are debated but "donkeys" may be incorrect — onagers (wild asses) are the more common identification.

19. `[STRONG]` **"by 1800 BCE the Sumerians had composed the Hymn to Ninkasi"** — The Hymn to Ninkasi is conventionally dated to ~1800 BCE, but calling its authors "the Sumerians" is problematic: by 1800 BCE, Sumerian was a dead literary language and the Old Babylonian scribes who composed/copied it were Akkadian speakers. More importantly, describing it as "the world's oldest surviving beer recipe" is a common popular claim, but the hymn is a literary/religious text, not a recipe. Whether it "doubles as" a recipe is debatable.

20. `[CHECK]` **"the White Temple of Uruk, built around 3200 BCE... sat on top of a thirteen-meter-high whitewashed platform"** — The Anu Ziggurat platform height is usually given as about 12–13 meters, so this is in range. The date ~3200 BCE is reasonable for the Late Uruk period construction.

21. `[CHECK]` **"a city of sixty thousand people"** — This specific population figure for Uruk appears twice in the chapter (here and earlier as part of the 40,000–80,000 range). Using 60,000 as a point estimate is picking the midpoint of a very uncertain range and presenting it as fact.

## Chapter 4 — The Age of City-States

22. `[CHECK]` **"the Stele of the Vultures... erected around 2450 BCE by the victorious Lagash king Eannatum"** — The Stele of the Vultures is indeed from Eannatum's reign, conventionally dated ~2450 BCE. This checks out.

23. `[CHECK]` **"It also shows, for the first time in art, a military formation we now call the phalanx"** — The Stele of the Vultures does show soldiers in tight formation with overlapping shields. Whether this constitutes a "phalanx" in the technical Greek sense is debatable, but the claim "first time in art" is reasonable.

24. `[STRONG]` **"Queen Puabi... wore an elaborate golden headdress of leaves, ribbons, and flowers containing thousands of individual pieces"** — "Thousands of individual pieces" is a suspiciously round and impressive number. Woolley's descriptions of Puabi's headdress mention gold leaves, ribbons, and beaded strings, but the specific claim of "thousands of individual pieces" needs verification. This is the kind of number that gets inflated in retellings.

25. `[CHECK]` **"In the largest tomb, Woolley found 74 skeletons arranged in rows"** — The "Great Death Pit" (PG 1237) contained 74 bodies. This is correct and well-documented.

26. `[CHECK]` **"taken a cup of poison, and lain down to wait for death"** — Woolley's original interpretation was poison (he found cups near the bodies). More recent analysis (2009 CT scans by University of Pennsylvania) suggested some attendants were killed by blunt force trauma to the skull, then preserved with mercury-based cosmetics. The "voluntarily took poison" narrative is Woolley's interpretation, now partially contradicted by newer evidence. Worth flagging.

27. `[CHECK]` **"typical interest rates of 20% per year on silver loans"** — 20% per year on silver and 33.3% on grain are the standard Old Babylonian rates documented in the Code of Hammurabi and earlier texts. This is correct for the Old Babylonian period but may be anachronistic for the Early Dynastic context where it appears.

28. `[CHECK]` **"Eannatum of Lagash (ruled approximately 2454–2425 BCE)"** — These are conventional dates but carry significant uncertainty. The precision to specific years for ED III rulers is misleading given the chronological debates.

## Chapter 5 — The First Empire: Sargon and Akkad

29. `[CHECK]` **"a standing army of 5,400 professional soldiers 'who ate bread before him daily'"** — The "5,400 soldiers" figure and the "ate bread before him" quote come from a later literary text about Sargon (the Sargon Legend / Chronicle), not from a contemporary inscription. The number is traditional but its reliability as a literal count is uncertain.

30. `[CHECK]` **"The parallels to the biblical story of Moses being set adrift on the Nile are so precise that most scholars believe the biblical account borrowed from Sargon's much older legend"** — The Sargon birth legend's parallel to Moses is widely noted. The claim that "most scholars believe the biblical account borrowed" may overstate the consensus — many scholars note the parallel without asserting direct borrowing, since foundational hero myths are common across cultures.

31. `[STRONG]` **"Her three major works... survive in 37 different ancient copies"** — The specific number "37 copies" for Enheduanna's works needs verification. I'm not confident this specific count is sourced. The Hymn to Inanna (nin-me-šára) does survive in numerous copies, but "37" for "three major works" combined is suspiciously precise and may be invented or misremembered.

32. `[CHECK]` **"Enheduanna (ca. 2285 BCE) predates every other known named author in human history by more than a century"** — The date ~2285 BCE and "more than a century" gap to the next named author need verification. Enheduanna is widely cited as the first named author, but the precise gap to the next one is hard to pin down.

33. `[CHECK]` **"Sargon died around 2279 BCE after a fifty-five-year reign"** — The Sumerian King List gives Sargon 56 years. A 55-year reign is close but may be off by one. The absolute date 2279 is within the range of the Middle Chronology.

34. `[CHECK]` **"Naram-Sin of Akkad (ruled 2254–2218 BCE)"** — Standard Middle Chronology dates. Naram-Sin's reign length of 36 years matches the King List.

35. `[CHECK]` **"the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin (a six-foot-tall pink sandstone monument now in the Louvre)"** — The Victory Stele is in the Louvre and is approximately 2 meters (6.5 feet) tall. It is made of pink limestone (not sandstone). The material identification as "sandstone" may be incorrect — most sources say limestone.

36. `[CHECK]` **"Gudea of Lagash (ruled approximately 2144–2124 BCE)"** — Gudea's dates are uncertain and debated; some place him earlier. "Over thirty" diorite statues is in the right range (about 27 survive, with fragments suggesting more).

## Chapter 6 — The Sumerian Renaissance

37. `[CHECK]` **"The outer layer alone used more than 720,000 baked bricks"** — This is a commonly cited figure for the Great Ziggurat of Ur, reportedly derived from estimates based on the structure's dimensions. It's plausible but I cannot verify the original source. It may be a back-calculation rather than an ancient count.

38. `[CHECK]` **"about forty laws from it, preserved on later copies"** — The Code of Ur-Nammu: approximately 30–40 laws are preserved (fragmentary). "About forty" is reasonable.

39. `[CHECK]` **"A man who cut off another man's foot owed 10 shekels of silver. A man who cut off another's nose owed 40 shekels"** — These specific penalty amounts (10 shekels for a foot, specific amounts for other injuries) appear in the surviving text. The 40 shekels for a nose is worth verifying — some sources give different amounts for different injuries. The numbers are in the right ballpark but the specific pairing needs checking.

40. `[STRONG]` **"Shulgi... claimed... that he had run from Nippur to Ur and back — a round trip of 320 kilometers — in a single day"** — The "Shulgi A" hymn does describe a run from Nippur to Ur and back. However, the distance of 320 km for the round trip is suspiciously precise and may be inflated. The distance from Nippur to Ur is approximately 100 km as the crow flies, so a round trip would be ~200 km by straight line, perhaps 250–300 km by ancient road. 320 km is plausible but the precision implies measurement that doesn't exist for ancient road distances.

41. `[CHECK]` **"Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur... 'My city has been destroyed before me. My house has been destroyed before me. My city, like a cow whose calf has been torn away, has been torn away from me.'"** — This is presented as a direct quote/translation. The Lamentation over Ur is a real text, and the "cow whose calf has been torn away" simile is genuine and appears in standard translations (e.g., ETCSL). However, the exact phrasing of the surrounding lines ("My city has been destroyed before me") should be verified against published translations — it reads smoothly enough to be a legitimate rendering but could be a paraphrase.

## Chapter 7 — The Old Babylonian Age: Hammurabi's Babylon

42. `[CHECK]` **"the Kanesh karum alone has yielded over 23,000 cuneiform tablets"** — The Kültepe/Kanesh archive is one of the largest Old Assyrian archives. The count of ~23,000 is widely cited and appears correct.

43. `[CHECK]` **"over 25,000 cuneiform tablets" from the palace archive of Mari** — The Mari archive is conventionally cited as containing about 25,000 tablets. This is correct.

44. `[CHECK]` **"The Code of Hammurabi, composed around 1754 BCE, was inscribed on a seven-foot-tall black diorite stele"** — The stele is about 2.25 meters (~7.4 feet) tall. The material is often described as diorite or basalt — "black diorite" is the traditional identification. The date 1754 BCE is conventional. This checks out.

45. `[CHECK]` **"282 laws organized by subject"** — The traditional count is 282 laws, though some are partially or wholly erased (an Elamite king reportedly erased several laws after looting the stele). This is standard.

46. `[STRONG]` **"'If a man destroys the eye of another man, his eye shall be destroyed. If he breaks another man's bone, his bone shall be broken.'"** — These read like direct quotes from the Code. Law 196 does concern eyes, and Law 197 concerns bones. However, the exact phrasing should be verified against standard translations (e.g., Roth 1997). The bone law (197) actually reads approximately "If he has broken the bone of a free man, his bone shall be broken" — the text here drops the social-status qualifier that the code itself includes. This is a smoothed paraphrase presented as a quote.

47. `[CHECK]` **"Babylonian scribes were producing clay tablets demonstrating mathematical knowledge that Europe would not possess again until the Islamic Golden Age almost three thousand years later"** — This is a strong claim. Old Babylonian mathematics was indeed sophisticated (quadratic equations, Pythagorean relationships), and comparable European mathematics did not appear until much later. But "Europe would not possess again" ignores Greek mathematics (Euclid, Archimedes) which was in some areas more advanced. The claim seems to confuse algebraic/arithmetic sophistication with mathematical knowledge generally.

48. `[CHECK]` **"the Plimpton 322 tablet (purchased by the American collector George Plimpton in 1922)"** — Plimpton 322 was purchased by George Arthur Plimpton from Edgar Banks in ~1922. This is correct.

49. `[CHECK]` **"A 2017 study of Plimpton 322 suggested it was actually a trigonometric reference table"** — This refers to the Mansfield and Wildberger paper published in Historia Mathematica in 2017. The paper exists and made this argument. However, their interpretation has been strongly contested by other scholars.

## Chapter 8 — The Long Peace: Kassite Babylon

50. `[CHECK]` **"a thousand-kilometer march down the Euphrates to attack Babylon itself"** — The distance from the Hittite capital Hattusa to Babylon is roughly 1,000–1,200 km. This is reasonable.

51. `[CHECK]` **"the Amarna Letters — an archive of 382 clay tablets discovered in 1887"** — The Amarna archive contains approximately 382 tablets (some sources say ~350–380 depending on how fragments are counted). The discovery date 1887 is correct.

52. `[CHECK]` **"their 'Kikkuli Text,' the world's oldest known training manual for any military technology"** — The Kikkuli text on horse training is real and is indeed one of the oldest known training manuals. Whether it is "the oldest training manual for any military technology" specifically is a strong claim but widely made.

## Chapter 9 — Assyria's First Rise

53. `[CHECK]` **"Tiglath-Pileser I... reportedly sailed on Phoenician ships and hunted dolphins"** — Tiglath-Pileser I's annals do mention reaching the Mediterranean and sailing on ships. The dolphin-hunting detail is widely repeated in secondary sources. Worth verifying against the actual annals translation, as "dolphin" may be a modern interpretation of the animal described.

54. `[STRONG]` **"His annals describe 42 campaigns in his first five years of rule"** — This is a suspiciously specific and high number. Tiglath-Pileser I's annals do describe numerous campaigns, but "42 campaigns in his first five years" needs verification. This could be a confused or inflated figure.

55. `[CHECK]` **"MUL.APIN... lists 66 stars and constellations"** — MUL.APIN lists stars in three "paths" (Enlil, Anu, Ea). The total count of entries varies by how you count (some sources say 66, others give slightly different numbers). The date of "around 1000 BCE" for compilation is debated — many scholars date the compilation to ~1100–700 BCE, with the observations themselves being older. The text may overstate the precision of the date.

56. `[CHECK]` **"the Saros cycle, an 18-year-and-11-day repeating pattern"** — The Saros cycle is 6585.3 days (approximately 18 years, 11 days, and 8 hours). The description is correct. However, attributing its discovery to "around 750 BCE" is uncertain — the Saros was likely recognized gradually over a longer period.

57. `[STRONG]` **"the substitute king ritual... the substitute king was killed — usually by poisoning"** — The substitute king (šar pūḫi) ritual is well-documented. However, the claim that the substitute was "usually" killed "by poisoning" is suspicious. The sources indicate the substitute was supposed to die (and some did die, possibly by deliberate means), but "usually by poisoning" is more specific than the evidence supports. Some substitutes are recorded as having died of natural causes or in unclear circumstances. The "usually by poisoning" detail may be invented.

58. `[CHECK]` **"typically about 100 days"** — The duration of the substitute king's reign varied. "About 100 days" is a common figure cited in some secondary sources but the actual duration depended on the specific astrological circumstances. This may be a generalization presented as typical.

## Chapter 10 — The Assyrian War Machine

59. `[STRONG]` **"the feast lasted ten days and hosted 69,574 guests who consumed 1,000 cattle, 1,000 calves, 10,000 sheep, 15,000 lambs, 10,000 ducks, and 10,000 fish"** — The Nimrud Banquet Stele is a real inscription and does contain extraordinary numbers. The figure 69,574 guests is indeed what the inscription claims. The food quantities listed here should be verified against the actual inscription text — these specific numbers (1,000 cattle, 10,000 sheep, etc.) need to match the original. Several of these are correct, but the full list as presented may mix up or round some figures. The original inscription lists many more food items than cited here.

60. `[CHECK]` **"The city walls enclosed 360 hectares"** — Nimrud's walled area is commonly cited as ~360 hectares (900 acres). This appears correct.

61. `[CHECK]` **"Shalmaneser III campaigned in 31 of the 35 years of his reign"** — This is a commonly repeated claim about Shalmaneser III. His annals do document an extraordinary number of campaigns. The "31 of 35 years" figure should be verified against the actual annals.

62. `[CHECK]` **"Ahab of Israel... who according to Shalmaneser's count brought 2,000 chariots and 10,000 infantry"** — The Kurkh Monolith inscription does attribute 2,000 chariots and 10,000 infantry to Ahab. These are the figures from the Assyrian source.

63. `[CHECK]` **"The combined coalition force reportedly included more than 50,000 infantry, 4,000 chariots, and 1,000 camel troops"** — These aggregate numbers from the Kurkh Monolith are approximately correct (the exact totals depend on how you add up the various contingents listed).

## Chapter 11 — Assyria at Its Zenith

64. `[CHECK]` **"Tiglath-Pileser III's own inscriptions claim 154,000 deportees from a single Babylonian campaign"** — Large deportation numbers appear in Neo-Assyrian royal inscriptions. The specific figure 154,000 from a single campaign needs verification against the actual inscriptions.

65. `[CHECK]` **"Sargon II deported 27,290 Israelites from the northern kingdom"** — Sargon II's inscriptions give the figure 27,290 (or 27,280 in some readings) for deportees from Samaria. This is a well-known figure from the Assyrian sources.

66. `[CHECK]` **"Nineveh... enclosed 750 hectares within its walls"** — Nineveh's walled area is commonly cited as approximately 750 hectares (1,850 acres). This is correct.

67. `[CHECK]` **"The walls stretched 12 kilometers around the city, stood 20 meters high, and had 15 gates"** — The walls of Nineveh are approximately 12 km in circuit with 15 gates. The 20-meter height claim is harder to verify — wall heights are difficult to determine archaeologically. This specific height figure needs checking.

68. `[CHECK]` **"The population may have reached 100,000 to 150,000 people"** — Population estimates for Nineveh vary. 100,000–150,000 is within the range cited by some scholars, though others give lower figures. Plausible.

69. `[STRONG]` **"Sennacherib built the Jerwan Aqueduct — an 80-kilometer stone aqueduct with a massive 18-meter-wide arch spanning a valley"** — The Jerwan Aqueduct is real and is an impressive feat of engineering. However, the "80-kilometer" length needs verification — the total canal system was long, but the aqueduct structure itself (the bridge at Jerwan) was approximately 280 meters long. The 80 km figure may refer to the entire canal system, not the aqueduct proper. Conflating the two is misleading. Also, "18-meter-wide" — the Jerwan bridge was approximately 22 meters wide according to some sources, or 12 meters according to others. The 18-meter figure needs checking.

70. `[CHECK]` **"30 talents of gold, 800 talents of silver, and his daughters as concubines"** — Sennacherib's annals (the Taylor Prism) give 30 talents of gold and 800 talents of silver from Hezekiah. The biblical account (2 Kings 18:14) gives 30 talents of gold and 300 talents of silver. The 800 figure here matches the Assyrian account. "His daughters as concubines" is in the Assyrian account. This checks out.

71. `[CHECK]` **"an angel of the Lord kills 185,000 Assyrian soldiers in the night"** — This is the biblical figure from 2 Kings 19:35. Correctly attributed.

72. `[STRONG]` **"'I have learned the hidden wisdom of Nabu, the god of scribes... I can solve complex mathematical problems and read the obscure Sumerian tablets that come from before the flood.'"** — This is presented as a direct quote from Ashurbanipal's royal inscriptions. The general sentiment is attested — Ashurbanipal did boast about his literacy in Sumerian and his scholarly abilities. However, the exact phrasing here ("complex mathematical problems," "obscure Sumerian tablets that come from before the flood") reads suspiciously modern and smooth. Published translations of Ashurbanipal's colophons use different phrasing. "I can solve complex mathematical problems" in particular sounds like a modern paraphrase, not a translation. This should be verified against a standard translation (e.g., Streck or Frame and George).

73. `[CHECK]` **"a collection of more than 30,000 tablets"** — The Library of Ashurbanipal contained approximately 30,000 tablets and fragments. This is the standard figure.

74. `[STRONG]` **"a British Museum assistant named George Smith... reportedly fainted from excitement"** — The story that George Smith became so excited upon discovering the flood tablet that he began undressing (not fainting) is the famous anecdote, as reported by the Daily Telegraph journalist Edwin Arnold. The "fainted" version appears to be a corruption of the original story. Smith reportedly "jumped up and rushed about the room in a great state of excitement, and, to the astonishment of those present, began to undress himself." Fainting vs. undressing is a significant difference, and the fainting version may be an invented embellishment.

75. `[STRONG]` **"'I destroyed their cities, I burned with fire, I devastated their fields, I carried off their gods, scattered their people, I made their land empty.'"** — Presented as a direct quote from Ashurbanipal's inscription about the destruction of Elam. While Ashurbanipal's annals are indeed viciously triumphalist about the destruction of Susa, this exact phrasing needs verification. It reads like a composite paraphrase rather than a specific passage from a published translation. The real inscriptions are even more detailed and specific.

## Chapter 12 — Babylon Resurgent

76. `[CHECK]` **"Nabopolassar... 'the weak, the feeble, who from childhood was nothing special'"** — Nabopolassar's inscriptions do contain unusually modest self-descriptions. The exact phrasing should be checked against published translations (e.g., Langdon or Da Riva), but the sentiment is attested.

77. `[CHECK]` **"the gate itself was 14 meters high"** — The Ishtar Gate's reconstructed height in the Pergamon Museum is approximately 14 meters. Archaeological estimates of the original height vary. This is in the right range.

78. `[CHECK]` **"a pigment derived from cobalt"** — The blue glaze on the Ishtar Gate bricks used a cobalt-based pigment (Egyptian blue or a related copper-cobalt compound). This is correct.

79. `[CHECK]` **"200-meter-long ceremonial avenue lined with walls decorated with 60 glazed-brick lions"** — The Processional Way was approximately 250 meters long (some sources say longer). The number 60 lions per side is commonly cited but varies by source. The 200-meter length may be slightly short.

80. `[CHECK]` **"seven stages rising perhaps 91 meters into the sky"** — The height of the Etemenanki is genuinely uncertain. Herodotus gives dimensions, and modern estimates range from about 60 to 91 meters. The 91-meter figure comes from a specific reconstruction but is at the high end. "Perhaps" is an appropriate qualifier.

81. `[CHECK]` **"Stephanie Dalley of Oxford"** — Stephanie Dalley is a real scholar at the Oriental Institute, University of Oxford, and she has indeed argued that the Hanging Gardens were at Nineveh rather than Babylon. This is correct.

82. `[CHECK]` **"Hipparchus (2nd century BCE) used Babylonian eclipse records to calculate the precession of the equinoxes"** — Hipparchus did use Babylonian data and did discover precession. This is correct.

83. `[SOFT]` **"an 18-month siege of Jerusalem"** — The siege of Jerusalem in 587/586 BCE is described in the Bible as lasting approximately 18 months (2 Kings 25:1-3 gives about 18 months from the 10th month of Zedekiah's 9th year to the 4th month of his 11th year). This appears correct.

## Chapter 13 — The End: Cyrus and the Long Echo

84. `[CHECK]` **"the Cyrus Cylinder (a 23-centimeter clay barrel inscribed in Babylonian cuneiform, now in the British Museum)"** — The Cyrus Cylinder is approximately 23 cm (9 inches) long. It is in the British Museum. This is correct.

85. `[CHECK]` **"a claim made most prominently by the last Shah of Iran in 1971"** — Mohammad Reza Shah Pahlavi did promote the Cyrus Cylinder as a charter of human rights during the 2,500th anniversary celebrations of the Persian Empire in 1971. This is correct.

86. `[CHECK]` **"Alexander the Great dying in Nebuchadnezzar's palace in Babylon in 323 BCE"** — Alexander died in Babylon in June 323 BCE. Whether it was specifically "Nebuchadnezzar's palace" is debated (the palace complex had been modified over time), but this is a reasonable description.

87. `[SOFT]` **"he died there in 323 BCE... planned to make it his imperial capital"** — Alexander's intention to make Babylon his capital is attested in ancient sources (Arrian, etc.), though the extent of his plans is debated. This is a mainstream view.

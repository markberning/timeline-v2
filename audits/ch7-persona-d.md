## Chapter 7 — The Old Babylonian Age: Hammurabi's Babylon

**Grade: GOOD**

**Throughline:** A cunning outsider king, starting from nothing, builds the first law-governed empire — and in doing so accidentally preserves, through destruction, the very records that let us understand him.

---

**What works:**

- **The Hammurabi patience sequence** (paragraph 5 on "the first thirty years") is the chapter's strongest passage. The accumulation of clauses — "He waited for Rim-Sin to grow old. He waited for Elam to overextend itself" — creates real suspense and transforms Hammurabi from a name on a monument into a chess player. This is the chapter doing what good narrative history does: making a dead man's choices feel like decisions, not inevitabilities.

- **The Mari betrayal and its irony** lands well. "He destroyed Mari around 1761 BCE, but ironically he preserved its history by burying its palace in debris" is a genuinely good sentence — a counterintuitive turn that justifies the length of the Mari tablet passage. The detail about Zimri-Lim's fury over the diplomatic horse-count makes Bronze Age politics feel human and petty in exactly the right way.

- **The Code of Hammurabi section** does something most popular treatments don't: it patiently corrects the "eye for an eye" oversimplification while still honoring why the Code matters. The reframing — "closer in spirit to a modern civil code or municipal ordinance book than to the Ten Commandments" — is the kind of clarifying comparison that good popular non-fiction lives on.

---

**What doesn't work:**

- **The chapter has a structural collapse after Hammurabi dies.** The narrative is organized around Hammurabi — his patience, his conquests, his code — and then he dies in a single sentence (paragraph 11, "Hammurabi died around 1750 BCE") and the chapter pivots to silver currency, then Babylonian mathematics, then Marduk's promotion, then two literary epics. These are each treated as equally weighted set pieces, but they have no connection to each other or to the chapter's central figure. The reader has been following Hammurabi as a protagonist for six solid paragraphs; when he dies and the chapter pivots to a math tablet at Columbia University, the bottom falls out. The fix is structural: these topics need to be woven into the Hammurabi story *before* his death, not appended after it. The Code naturally leads to the Rule of Law; the Rule of Law naturally raises the question of what kind of economy these laws governed — which opens silver and banking. Mathematics could be framed as the intellectual life of the empire Hammurabi built. Instead, each topic gets its own self-contained block in sequence, like entries in an encyclopedia.

- **The Isin-Larsa opening and the Old Assyrian Trade Network section are structural orphans.** The chapter opens with a substantial paragraph on the Isin-Larsa Period, then a paragraph on Amorite cultural preservation, then — via "Further north, meanwhile" — pivots to the Assyrian trade network at Kanesh. This is a 350-word section about Assyrian tin merchants, placed before Hammurabi is even introduced, and it feels marooned. The Kanesh material is fascinating, but it belongs in a chapter about Assyria (and does appear in Chapter 9's framing). Here it interrupts the momentum toward Hammurabi's entrance and dilutes the chapter's identity. The reader's question at this point is "who is Hammurabi?" — not "how did Assyrian merchants organize their supply chains?" The fix: cut or sharply compress the Kanesh section, or fold the essential commercial-network point (Assyria's power started as commerce) into a single sentence in Chapter 9 where it belongs.

- **The ending is weak.** After two rich literary-epic summaries (Atra-Hasis, Descent of Inanna), the chapter closes with a four-sentence paragraph on dynastic decline: Hammurabi's son Samsu-iluna, rebellions, Kassite raids, salt accumulation in fields, and then "The final blow came in 1595 BCE, when an unexpected attacker came rushing down the Euphrates from the far north." This is an effective cliffhanger sentence — but it's undermined by the four draggy sentences before it. The Descent of Inanna is the chapter's last piece of real content, and it ends on a mythological note (Inanna returning from the underworld, Persephone parallels) that has nothing to do with what's about to happen. There's no pull-through to Chapter 8. The fix: after the Atra-Hasis/Inanna section, add a transitional paragraph that draws a line between the cultural richness of Hammurabi's Babylon and its fragility — connecting the literary golden age to the empire's reliance on Hammurabi's personal administrative genius, which his successors couldn't replicate. Then land the Hittite cliffhanger harder.

---

**Momentum map:** Strong setup (Isin-Larsa context), stumbles on the Assyrian trade detour, recovers well for Hammurabi's entrance and patience sequence, peaks at the Mari betrayal and Code sections, then loses its spine when Hammurabi dies midchapter and the second half becomes a parade of unconnected set pieces (silver, math, Marduk, Atra-Hasis, Inanna), ending on a cliffhanger that arrives too suddenly to fully land.

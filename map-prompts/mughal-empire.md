2. **Spell every label exactly as written in this prompt.** Do not invent words. Do not abbreviate. Do not generate any word that is not in the prompt text. (On prior runs Gemini invented words like "cononniat", "reachit", and "Kushi" — do not do this.)

4. **North is at the top of the map. East is to the right.** Place every location according to its real-world geography in this orientation.
5. **All water is light blue.** Seas, bays, lakes, and rivers are rendered in light blue. Do not render water as tan, cream, or white. Deserts are pale yellow. Land is beige/tan.
6. **Do not invent any labels, annotations, sites, or features that are not in this prompt.**

1. North is at the top. East is to the right.
2. The Indian subcontinent is roughly triangular, with the Himalayas at the north, the Arabian Sea on the southwest coast, and the Bay of Bengal on the east coast. Sri Lanka is the small island off the southeastern tip.
3. The Indus river runs roughly north-to-south through Pakistan in the west. The Ganges river runs roughly west-to-east across the northern plain into the Bay of Bengal. The Yamuna flows from the Himalayas south past Delhi and Agra, joining the Ganges at Allahabad.
4. Delhi is in north-central India on the Yamuna river. Agra is about 110 miles south of Delhi on the Yamuna. Lahore is in modern Pakistan, northwest of Delhi. Kabul is in modern Afghanistan, far northwest beyond the Hindu Kush. Kandahar is in southern Afghanistan, west of the Indus.
5. Bengal is in the eastern Ganges delta, with Calcutta on the Hooghly distributary. Bombay (Mumbai) is on the western coast. Madras (Chennai) is on the southeastern coast.
6. The Deccan plateau is south of the Vindhya mountain range, covering most of central and southern India. The Western Ghats are the mountain range along the western coast; the Eastern Ghats are along the eastern coast.
7. Ferghana Valley and Samarkand are in modern Uzbekistan, far north of Kabul, beyond the Hindu Kush.
8. The Khyber Pass cuts through the Hindu Kush between Kabul and the Punjab plains.
